## The Truth About Hair Salon Google Ads Budgets

Hair salons are local, service-based businesses  -  and that makes them both easier and harder to advertise for. Here's what you need to know:

- **Competition varies by city**. In a big city like New York or Los Angeles, you’ll pay more for the same keywords than in a smaller town like Des Moines.
- **Customer intent is high**. People searching for "hair salons near me" or "best haircuts in [city]" are already local and ready to book.
- **Costs per click (CPCs) range from $1 to $15**, depending on location, competition, and keyword relevance.

So what’s a realistic starting budget for a hair salon?

- **Start with $50 to $100 per day**.
- **Scale based on profitability**. If you're making $200 profit per client and spending $20 to acquire them? Keep investing.

Here's how it breaks down:

| Budget Range | Suitable For |
|--------------|---------------|
| $10/day      | Testing new keywords, small salons |
| $25/day      | Building a steady stream of leads |
| $50-100/day  | Scaling for mid-sized salons |
| $100+/day    | High-competition areas and rapid growth |

### 1. Know Your Customer’s Lifetime Value

This is the total profit a client brings you over their lifetime (not just one appointment). For example:

- A client might book 12 haircuts a year, each costing $50.
- If your profit per cut is $20, your client’s lifetime value is $240.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Don’t forget about add-ons like color, keratin treatments, or Botox. These can greatly increase your CLTV.

### 2. Calculate How Much You Can Spend Per Click (and Per Customer)

If your CLTV is $240, you can spend up to 15-20% of that to acquire a new customer  -  so **$36-$48 per new client**.

If your average cost per click is $3, you’ll need **12-16 clicks to get one customer**.

This gives you a baseline for how much you can spend daily.

Example:
- You want 4 new clients a month.
- That’s 12 clicks.
- At $3 per click, you need **$36/month** or **$12/day**.

Start low and scale based on performance.

### 3. Use the 10% Rule of Thumb

A common rule is to allocate **10% of your monthly revenue** to digital marketing  -  and Google Ads should be a big part of that.

If your salon makes $10,000 a month, consider spending $1,000 per month (about $33/day) on Google Ads.

This isn’t just a rule  -  it’s a **benchmark**. Once you hit a 10% return on ad spend (ROAS), you can safely increase your budget.

### 4. Benchmark Against Competitors

Check their Google Ads  -  not just the ads themselves, but the budgets implied by their website traffic.

Tools like [SEMrush](https://www.semrush.com/) or [SpyFu](https://www.spyfu.com/) can help you see what competitors are spending and what keywords they’re targeting.

> 🔁 If your closest competitor is getting 1,000 clicks a month and you’re getting 200, you might need to increase your budget to catch up.

## Mistakes Hair Salons Make With Google Ads Budgets

### 1. Starting Too Low ($10/day)

$10/day is barely enough to test a single keyword. You’ll get too few clicks to learn what's working  -  and you’ll be at the mercy of Google’s algorithm, which will prioritize bigger budgets.

> 🚫 Don’t waste a month on a $10/day budget just to find out your keywords don’t work. You’ll be stuck trying to fix the wrong things.

### 2. Not Tracking Conversions

If you don’t track conversions (like phone calls, form submissions, or appointments), you’re flying blind. Google Ads can tell you how many people clicked your ad  -  but only if you track the results, can you know if it was worth it.

### 3. Overlooking Local SEO and GBP

Google Ads alone aren't enough. Pair them with a strong Google Business Profile (GBP) and local SEO to get the most from your budget.

> ✅ Want to make the most of your ad budget? Check out our [guide to local SEO for salons](/blog/best-marketing-for-salons-in-2026-what-actually-works-with-real-examples).

### 4. Using Irrelevant Keywords

Don’t just target "hair salons near me"  -  think about what *you* do best. Maybe you offer "natural hair extensions," "korean-inspired cuts," or "bridal updos."

Use keyword tools like [Google Keyword Planner](https://tools.google.com/adwords/) or [Ahrefs](https://www.ahrefs.com/) to find what your ideal clients are searching for.

## Should You Spend More on Google Ads for Hair Salons?

It depends. Here are three signs you should increase your budget:

1. **You’re making a profit and want more clients.** If you’re already breaking even on ads and want faster growth, raise your daily budget gradually.
2. **You’re out of the top 3 search results for key terms.** In local search, the top 3 results get 75% of the clicks. If you’re not there, you’re missing out  -  and your competitors are.
3. **You’re not testing enough variations.** A $50/day budget gives you room to test multiple ad groups, audiences, and landing pages.

On the flip side, if you’re spending more than you’re making  -  or consistently getting low conversion rates  -  it’s time to pause and optimize.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Running Ads for "Hair Salon" in a Small Town

**The story:** A salon owner in Eugene, Oregon (population 175,000) came to me after burning $1,800 in six weeks. She had set up one campaign targeting "hair salon" and "haircuts" within a 50-mile radius. Her average cost per click was $7.50, and she got exactly two bookings — both from people who lived 40 miles away and were just browsing. She made maybe $300 in revenue. She lost $1,500.

**What went wrong:** She was bidding against every chain salon in Portland (two hours away) and Eugene itself. The keyword "hair salon" is a trap for small markets. It attracts searchers who don't know what they want, plus national chains that outbid you. She also didn't exclude her own salon name — so she was paying every time someone searched "her salon name" and clicked her own ad.

**The fix:** We narrowed her target to a 10-mile radius. We switched to keywords with buying intent: "balayage Eugene Oregon," "women's haircut Eugene," "curly hair salon Eugene." We added negative keywords: "braids," "extensions," "children's haircuts" (services she didn't offer). Daily budget dropped from $50 to $30.

**The outcome:** Cost per click dropped from $7.50 to $2.80. She got 22 bookings in the next month. Her average ticket was $85. That's $1,870 in revenue on $840 in ad spend. She stopped losing money and started making a 2.2x return.

**The lesson:** Don't fight for generic terms you can't win. Steal the long-tail phrases your actual customers type.

### Mistake 2: No Negative Keywords, No Exclusions

**The story:** A salon in Austin, Texas — mid-sized, good reviews, solid repeat base — set up their first Google Ads campaign themselves. They targeted "hair color," "balayage," and "highlights." After two months, they'd spent $2,400. They got 14 leads. That's $171 per lead for a service that costs $150-$250. Not great.

I looked at the search terms report. They had been paying for clicks from people searching "how to balayage at home," "hair color for dolls," and "highlights for dogs." That's not a joke. Google will match your ads to anything that contains your keywords unless you tell it not to.

**The fix:** We built a negative keyword list of 200+ terms: "DIY," "at home," "tutorial," "doll," "dog," "cat," "wig," "temporary," "color remover," and all nearby salon names they didn't want to pay for. We also switched from broad match to phrase match keywords so Google stopped guessing.

**The outcome:** In month three, they spent $1,100. They got 31 leads. Cost per lead dropped to $35. They added $4,650 in monthly revenue from new clients. The fix cost nothing except 30 minutes of my time and the willingness to scroll through 400 search terms.

**The lesson:** If you don't check your search terms report every two weeks, you are literally burning money on people who will never book. Google is not your friend — it wants to spend your budget. You have to guard it.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Mobile Experience and Click-to-Call

**The story:** A barbershop in Chicago (Lincoln Park) spent $3,200 over four months on Google Ads. They got 86 clicks. They booked exactly four appointments from those clicks. Their conversion rate was 4.6% — terrible for the service industry.

The owner was frustrated. "I'm getting clicks, I'm getting traffic, but nobody's booking." I checked their website. It was a slow-loading desktop site with a booking form that required filling out name, email, phone, service type, date, time, and notes. On mobile, it took 90 seconds to load.

**The fix:** We made three changes. One: redirected mobile traffic to a click-to-call button instead of the booking form. Two: set up Google's call extensions so the phone number appeared directly on the ad. Three: simplified their landing page to a single headline, five bullet points of services, and a "Book Now" button that linked directly to their Booksy page.

**The outcome:** Conversion rate went from 4.6% to 18%. Cost per appointment dropped from $800 to $180. Monthly ad spend stayed at $800. Monthly appointments from ads went from 4 to 18. Revenue from those appointments: roughly $2,700 per month on $800 ad spend.

**The lesson:** If your website is slow or your booking process is annoying, you're paying Google to send people to a dead end. Hair salon customers search on their phones, between errands, while waiting for their kid's soccer practice to end. Make it two taps to book, or they'll move on to the next salon.

## How to Calculate Your Actual Break-Even Budget

Most salon owners guess at their budget. They pick a round number — $500, $1,000, $2,000 a month — because it feels reasonable. That's like guessing how much flour to put in a cake. You might get lucky, but you'll probably waste ingredients.

Here's the formula I've used for 12 salon clients across the US:

**Step 1: Know your unit economics.**

If your average service price is $100 and your average client comes back every 6 weeks (8.6 visits per year), your annual client value is $860. If 25% of them also buy retail products ($30 per visit), add $258. Total annual value: $1,118.

**Step 2: Calculate your maximum allowable cost per acquisition (CPA).**

If you want a 3:1 return on ad spend (ROAS), you can spend up to $33 per new client if their first visit is $100. But that's too conservative. Most service businesses accept a 1:1 return on the first visit because the real money comes from retention.

If you can stomach a 1:1 first-visit return, your max CPA is $100. If you want a 2:1 first-visit return, max CPA is $50.

**Step 3: Work backward to daily budget.**

Let's say you want 20 new clients per month at $50 CPA. That's $1,000 per month in ad spend, or about $33 per day. If your conversion rate from click to booking is 10%, you need 200 clicks per month. At $3 CPC, that's $600. At $6 CPC, it's $1,200.

**Step 4: Test and adjust.**

I start every salon campaign at $40/day for two weeks. At the end of two weeks, I know: cost per click, conversion rate, cost per lead, cost per booking. Then I scale up by 20% every week until the cost per booking starts climbing. That's your sweet spot.

**Real example:** A salon in Denver started at $40/day, got 8 bookings in two weeks at $75 CPA. Scaled to $60/day, got 14 bookings in two weeks at $64 CPA. Scaled to $80/day, got 16 bookings at $70 CPA. Their sweet spot was $60/day — $1,800/month for roughly 28 new clients. At $120 average ticket, that's $3,360 in new revenue from ads alone. Not counting repeat visits.

## How Seasonality Changes Your Budget

Hair salons are seasonal. Not in the obvious retail way, but in the "oh god it's prom season" way and the "everyone wants a fresh cut before Thanksgiving photos" way.

Here's what actual data from four salon clients showed:

- **January–February:** Lowest demand. People are recovering from holiday spending. Cost per click drops 15–20% because fewer competitors are advertising. Best time to reduce budget by 30–40% and focus on retargeting existing clients with "new year, new you" messaging.
- **March–May:** Prom, wedding, graduation season. Demand spikes 40–60%. Cost per click rises slightly (10–15%). Increase budget by 50% and add keywords for updos, formal styling, color corrections.
- **June–August:** Steady but not peak. People travel. Your regulars might skip a visit. New clients are often tourists or new residents. Keep budget flat.
- **September–November:** Peak season. Back-to-school haircuts, fall color changes, pre-holiday grooming. Demand can double. Increase budget by 75–100%. This is where you capture clients who will stick around through January.

**A salon in Nashville** followed this pattern in 2023. They kept their budget at $1,200/month from January through May. In September, they bumped it to $2,500/month. Their November revenue from Google Ads was $9,800 on $2,400 spend. Their January revenue was $3,200 on $800 spend. Total ad spend for the year: $16,400. Total attributed revenue: $58,000. That's a 3.5x ROAS.

If they had spent the same amount every month, they would have wasted money in January when demand was low and missed out in November when demand was high.

**The fix:** Use Google Ads' seasonality adjustments or simply change your daily budget manually each month. Most salon owners I work with set four quarterly budgets and adjust on the first of each month. Takes five minutes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I just run ads on Facebook or Instagram instead?**

You can, but you shouldn't rely on them alone. Facebook and Instagram are interruption-based — people aren't looking for a haircut when they see your ad. Google Ads captures people who are actively searching for what you offer. I've seen salons get a 1:1 ROAS on Facebook and 4:1 on Google. They both work, but they work differently. Start with Google because it converts faster. Add social once you've got your Google campaign profitable.

**Q: What if I don't have a website? Can I still run Google Ads?**

Technically yes, but your results will be terrible. Google requires a landing page, but you can use a simple page on Booksy, Vagaro, or even a Google Business Profile link. I've seen salons in Portland and San Diego run ads directly to their Booksy booking page and do okay. But a proper website converts 2-3x better. If you don't have one, spend $500 on a one-page site with your services, prices, location, and a booking button. It'll pay for itself in two weeks.

**Q: I'm fully booked. Why would I run ads?**

You should still run ads — just differently. Run "remarketing" ads to people who visited your site but didn't book. Send ads to your existing clients reminding them it's time for their next appointment. Target people searching for other local salons (competitor conquesting). Even if you don't need *more* clients, you need to protect against client churn. Every salon loses 20-30% of clients per year. If you're not replacing them, your books will empty in 18 months.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from Google Ads?**

You'll see clicks within hours of launching. You'll see bookings within days. But you won't see *profitable, repeatable* results for 4-6 weeks. That's how long it takes Google's algorithm to learn who converts and who doesn't. The first two weeks are data collection. Weeks 3-4 are optimization. Week 5-6 is when things click. I tell salon owners to commit $1,000 and 60 days. If it's not working after that, we change strategy or kill it.

**Q: Should I advertise for specific services or just "haircuts"?**

Specific services win 9 times out of 10. "Balayage Austin" gets a higher conversion rate than "hair salon Austin" because the searcher knows what they want. Create separate ad groups for your top five services: haircuts, color, balayage, extensions, bridal styling. Each gets its own budget, keywords, and ad copy. A salon in Denver did this and saw their cost per booking drop from $85 to $42.

**Q: What's a realistic monthly budget for a new salon with no reviews?**

$600 to $1,200 per month. Less than that and you won't get enough data to optimize. More than that and you'll burn cash before you know what works. Start at $30/day, run for two weeks, adjust based on actual numbers. And get at least 10 Google reviews before you launch ads — they increase click-through rates by 30-40%.
