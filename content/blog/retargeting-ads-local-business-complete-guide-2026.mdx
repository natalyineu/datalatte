## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do retargeting ads work for local businesses?
Retargeting ads use cookies or user data to show targeted ads to people who visited your website but didn’t book an appointment or make a purchase. For example, if 97% of your site visitors leave without booking, these ads remind them of your services and encourage them to return.

### Are retargeting ads cost-effective for small businesses?
Yes, with an average ROI of 20%, retargeting campaigns often outperform other ads. They focus on users already familiar with your business, reducing wasted spend compared to broad campaigns.

### How can I target customers who abandoned their cart?
Use pixel tracking or UTM parameters to identify cart abandoners (40% average rate). Then, serve them personalized ads with discounts or reminders to complete their purchase.

### How long should a retargeting campaign run?
Most campaigns run 30–60 days, depending on user behavior. Analyze performance weekly and adjust targeting or creatives if engagement drops below 15% of monthly ad spend.

## Step 5: Segment Your Retargeting Audiences for Better Results

Not all lost customers are the same. A first-time visitor who browsed your menu for 30 seconds is different from a repeat customer who hasn’t booked in three months. Segment your retargeting lists based on behavior to serve the right message at the right time.

- **Website visitors who viewed a service page but didn’t book** → Show them a “Still thinking about us?” ad with a limited-time offer (e.g., 15% off their first haircut at your salon).
- **Cart abandoners** → Remind them exactly what they left behind. For a fitness studio, that might be a “Reserve your spot now – class filling fast!” ad with a countdown timer.
- **Past customers who haven’t returned in 60+ days** → Use a “We miss you!” campaign with a loyalty discount. Coffee shops can offer a free pastry with any drink purchase to re-engage lapsed regulars.

Segmentation boosts click-through rates by up to 50% compared to blanket retargeting. Start with just two or three segments – you can always refine later.

## Step 6: Use Frequency Caps to Avoid Ad Fatigue

Retargeting works because it keeps your business top-of-mind – but too many ads can backfire. If a potential customer sees the same “Book now!” ad ten times in a day, they’ll feel stalked rather than welcomed. Set a frequency cap of 3–5 impressions per person per week across all platforms.

For example, a pet groomer in Toronto found that capping Facebook ads at 4 impressions per week reduced negative feedback by 60% while maintaining a 22% conversion rate. Test different caps for your business: start with 3, then increase to 5 if engagement stays high. Monitor your ad relevance score (Facebook) or quality score (Google) – a drop signals it’s time to refresh creative or lower frequency.

## Step 7: Leverage Seasonal Offers and Urgency

Local businesses thrive on timely, location-based promotions. Tie your retargeting ads to upcoming events, holidays, or local weather patterns to create urgency.

- **Hair salon before Valentine’s Day** → “Book your Valentine’s Day look – only 3 slots left this week!”
- **Coffee shop during a cold snap** → “Warm up with our new peppermint mocha. Order ahead and skip the line.”
- **Fitness studio at the start of a new month** → “New month, new you – first session free if you book by Friday.”

Urgency works because it taps into the fear of missing out (FOMO). Use countdown timers in your ad copy or offer codes that expire in 72 hours. A UK-based yoga studio increased retargeting conversions by 35% simply by adding “Ends Sunday” to their ad headlines.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Retargeting Everyone Who Walked In

A hair salon owner in Nashville called me after she’d spent $1,200 on retargeting over three months and booked exactly four new appointments. She was about to declare retargeting dead. I asked who she was targeting. “Everyone who visited our website,” she said. That included people who landed on her “Careers” page, accidentally clicked an ad, or searched for “Nashville hair salon near me” and bounced in three seconds.

The fix: segment your audience by intent. Someone who spent 45 seconds on your booking page is worth a different ad than someone who landed on your blog about winter hair care. In her case, we built two audiences in Google Ads: “visited booking page but didn’t book” and “spent >60 seconds on services page.” We spent the same $400/month but only showed ads to the first group — a smaller, hotter audience. The creative changed too: instead of a generic “Come back!” we showed a photo of the stylist they’d browsed with a “She’s still available Thursday” headline.

Outcome: $3,800 in new bookings over the next two months from those retargeted visitors. Cost per booking dropped from $300 to $42. She stopped firing her ad budget at the entire parking lot and started talking to the people who actually had their hand on the door.

### Mistake #2: Using the Same Creative for Everyone

A coffee shop owner in Austin ran a retargeting campaign for six weeks. His ad was a photo of a latte with “Try our new seasonal drink!” He spent $800. He got 14 clicks. Zero cups sold. He texted me a screenshot: “What am I doing wrong?”

I asked who saw the ad. “People who visited our site,” he said. “And what did they do there?” “Most of them signed up for our newsletter to get a free drink on their birthday.” So he was showing a “buy a drink” ad to people who already wanted a free drink. Of course they ignored it.

We split the audience: newsletter sign-ups got an ad offering double loyalty points on their second visit. People who browsed the menu page got a reminder of their favorite pastry. People who clicked the “Catering” link got a corporate coffee service pitch. Same budget ($500/month), three ad sets. Revenue from retargeted visits went from $0 to $2,100 in the first month. The difference wasn’t better targeting — it was smarter messaging to a specific intent.

### Mistake #3: Stopping After One Click

A pet groomer in Portland set up a retargeting ad on Facebook. A customer clicked. The groomer watched the notification pop up. Then she waited for the phone to ring. It didn’t. She assumed retargeting was a scam.

Here’s the part most guides skip: a click is not a conversion. It’s a nudge. That customer likely got distracted by their dog chewing a shoe. They need one or two more touchpoints. We added a second retargeting ad for people who clicked the first ad but didn’t book — a simple video tour of the grooming station, showing how calm the dogs are. Then a third ad for people who watched >50% of that video: a limited-time “Book this week, get a free nail trim.”

The groomer spent $350 total. Within two weeks, 11 new appointments booked. Average value per appointment: $65. That’s $715 in revenue on a $350 spend. Not a moonshot, but profitable. The mistake was expecting a single ad to do the job of a sequence. Your customer isn’t ignoring you — they’re just busy.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Offline Retargeting

A fitness studio in Chicago ran retargeting ads for months. They tracked website visits, clicks, and bookings. But they ignored the 60% of customers who walked in the door, did a free trial, and never came back. No website visit. No pixel. Retargeting couldn’t touch them.

We fixed it by collecting emails at the front desk — “Join our monthly challenge list” — and using Mailchimp’s retargeting integration with Facebook. Anyone who took a free trial but didn’t sign up within 14 days got a Facebook ad showing before-and-after photos of current members. The ad linked to a landing page with a “Second trial – this time with a coach.” Cost per lead: $8. Conversion rate from that audience: 22%. The studio added $4,300 in new memberships over three months from people they would have otherwise lost.

## How to Set Up Retargeting Without Burning Your Budget (or Your Patience)

Let’s get practical. You don’t need a $5,000/month programmatic buy. You need a $300–$500 experiment with a clear structure. Here’s what I’ve seen work for local businesses across Denver, Austin, and Nashville.

**Start with one platform.** If you’re a hair salon or pet groomer, Booksy or Yelp might already have your customer data. Booksy’s retargeting tool lets you show ads to people who browsed your services but didn’t book. Square’s Marketing suite does the same for retail businesses (a gift shop in Denver used it to retarget people who added items to cart but didn’t check out — recovered $1,900 in abandoned carts over four weeks). If you use Mailchimp, you can sync your email list to Facebook or Google and serve ads to existing customers who haven’t visited in 90 days. That’s a warm audience that already trusts you.

**Set a frequency cap.** I’ve seen a coffee shop in Los Angeles show the same ad 47 times to one person in a week. That’s not retargeting — it’s harassment. Limit to 2–3 impressions per user per day. Anything above that trains people to ignore your brand.

**Budget by audience size.** A common rule: if your audience is 500 people, you can run a solid campaign on $300–$400/month. If it’s 5,000, you’ll need $800–$1,200 to get meaningful reach. Don’t set a daily budget that exhausts your audience in two hours — spread it across the day.

**Use UTM links.** I know, it’s boring. But without them, you’ll have no idea which ad drove the booking. Google’s Campaign URL Builder is free. Tag every retargeting ad with source, medium, and campaign name. Then check your analytics to see which creative actually works. One salon owner in San Diego thought her “Spring Special” ad was killing it until she saw that 80% of bookings from retargeting actually came from a boring “Same-day appointments available” ad she’d forgotten to pause.

**Track offline conversions.** If someone sees your ad and calls you three days later, you need a system to tie that phone call back to the ad. Google Ads can track calls if you use a forwarding number. Facebook has offline event tracking — you upload a spreadsheet of bookings and it matches them to ad clicks. It’s not perfect, but it’s better than guessing.

## The One Metric That Actually Matters for Local Retargeting

Agency people love talking about ROAS (Return on Ad Spend). But for a small business, ROAS can be misleading if you don’t account for customer lifetime value. Let me give you a real scenario from a dog daycare in Denver.

They spent $600 on retargeting ads. The campaign directly generated $1,200 in new bookings. ROAS: 2x. Most guides would call that a win. But here’s the catch: those new customers stayed an average of 8 months, spending $180/month. That’s $1,440 per customer — not $1,200 total. The real return was $5,760 from the $600 spend. That’s a 9.6x ROAS.

The metric you should care about is **cost per retained customer** — the total ad spend divided by the number of customers who book at least three times after the retargeting ad. If that number is under $50, you’re winning. If it’s over $100, either your targeting is too broad or your ad creative isn’t convincing enough.

How to calculate it: run your retargeting campaign for 60 days. Export the list of people who clicked an ad and eventually booked. Then look at your booking system to see how many of those customers returned in the next three months. Divide total ad spend by the number of customers who came back at least once. That’s your real cost.

We did this for a yoga studio in Austin. Their cost per retained customer was $34. They immediately doubled their retargeting budget to $800/month and saw retention rates hold steady. If their number had been $120, we would have pulled the campaign and tried something else. Don’t let vanity metrics fool you.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Won’t retargeting creep people out? They’ll think I’m following them.**
Some will. That’s why you set a frequency cap and keep the messaging helpful, not desperate. A customer who visited your booking page and then sees an ad saying “We saved a spot for you tomorrow” feels recognized, not stalked. A customer who sees your ad 15 times a day feels violated. Keep it to 2–3 per day. Also, exclude anyone who already booked. There’s nothing creepier than showing a “Come back!” ad to someone who just left.

**Q: I tried retargeting on Facebook and got zero results. What did I do wrong?**
Probably one of these three: your audience was too small (under 200 people Facebook won’t optimize), your ad was just a logo and a generic offer, or you didn’t give it enough time. I’ve seen campaigns that spent $200 in a week with no bookings — that’s not enough time to learn. Run at least 30 days with consistent creative. If after 30 days you have less than 10 clicks per day, your audience is too small. Expand to lookalike audiences of past customers.

**Q: How much should I spend per month on retargeting?**
For a local business with a single location and under 2,000 website visitors per month, start at $300–$500. For a multi-location or high-ticket service (like a dental practice or landscaping company), $800–$1,500. I’ve never seen a local business benefit from spending over $2,000/month on retargeting unless they have a massive customer list. That money is better spent on acquisition.

**Q: Which platform works best for a hair salon or pet groomer?**
Booksy does retargeting natively for appointments. Yelp Ads let you retarget people who viewed your profile. For most small service businesses, Facebook + Instagram is still the most flexible and gives you the best audience segmentation. Google Display Network is good for people actively searching, but it’s harder to get creative. I’d start with Facebook, test for two months, then add Google if results justify it.

**Q: Can I do this myself or do I need an agency?**
You can do it yourself if you’re comfortable with Facebook Ads Manager, Google Ads, or your booking platform’s tools. The setup takes an afternoon. The real work is monitoring, testing different headlines, and not giving up after three days. If you’re the owner and you’re already working 60 hours a week, paying someone $300/month to manage it might be smarter than burning out. I’ve seen owners do it themselves and succeed. I’ve also seen them blow $2,000 in three weeks because they forgot to add an exclusions list. Your call.

**Q: How do I measure if it’s working?**
Look at booked appointments, not clicks or impressions. Track the last click attribution: did they click a retargeting ad and then book within 7 days? That’s the simplest model. More accurately, use a 30-day click window. If your cost per acquired customer is lower than your average profit per customer, it’s working. If not, change the creative or the offer before you change the audience.
