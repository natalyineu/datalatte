Not all businesses are created equal, and some may find it more challenging to showcase prices than others. For instance, pet groomers might struggle to display prices due to the complexity of their services and varying client needs. However, even in such cases, making an effort to provide transparent pricing can make a significant difference.

### 1. Advantages of Showing Prices on Your Website

Showing prices on your website has several benefits, including:

* Increased conversion rates: By providing clear and transparent pricing information, you can help visitors feel more confident in their purchasing decisions, leading to higher conversion rates.
* Reduced bounce rates: When visitors can easily find the information they need, they are more likely to stay on your site and explore your services further.
* Improved customer trust: Transparency builds trust, which is essential for local businesses that rely heavily on word-of-mouth and repeat customers.

### 2. Types of Pricing Pages

Not all pricing pages are created equal. Here are a few common types:

* Simple pricing: A basic page with a list of services and their corresponding prices.
* Tiered pricing: A page that offers different levels of service or packages with varying prices.
* Custom pricing: A page that allows customers to request a quote or customize their services.

<Callout type="tip">When creating a pricing page, make sure to highlight any promotions, discounts, or special offers you're currently running.</Callout>

### 3. Best Practices for Creating a Pricing Page

To create an effective pricing page, follow these best practices:

* Keep it simple: Avoid clutter and make sure the most important information is easy to find.
* Use clear headings and formatting: Organize your pricing information with headings, subheadings, and bullet points.
* Use visuals: Include images or videos to help illustrate your services and make them more relatable.
* Make it mobile-friendly: Ensure your pricing page is easily accessible and readable on mobile devices.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

After a decade of watching small businesses fumble their pricing pages, I can tell you with some confidence: the problem isn't whether to show prices. It's how you show them. Here are the mistakes I've watched burn real money, and what actually worked.

**Mistake #1: Hiding Prices Until Someone "Calls for a Quote"**

I consulted for a hair salon in Austin, Texas — we'll call it Luxe Cuts. They had a beautiful website. Great photos. Their service menu listed every treatment with a description like "Custom balayage — contact us for pricing."

Their owner told me proudly that this approach "builds a relationship" before talking money. I told her it was costing her about $2,400 a month in lost bookings.

Here's what was actually happening: Luxe Cuts was paying $1,800/month on Google Ads targeting "balayage Austin" and "color correction Austin." Their ads took people to a landing page that required a form fill to get pricing. Of 312 ad clicks in one month, 7 people submitted the form. Average booking value: $180. Revenue from ads: $1,260. Ad spend: $1,800. They were losing $540 a month on ads alone.

The fix: We moved pricing onto every service page. A $240 balayage got listed as $240. A $85 haircut got listed as $85. We added one line: "Prices reflect base services. See your stylist for custom add-ons."

Within 60 days, their organic conversion rate (visitor to booking) went from 1.2% to 4.8%. Ad conversions jumped to 14.2%. That $1,800 in ad spend started generating $4,300 in bookings. The owner had been terrified that showing prices would scare people off. What scared people off was the work of asking.

**Mistake #2: Showing Prices Without Context**

A dog grooming and boarding business in Nashville called Bark & Stay had their prices listed — but only in a downloadable PDF at the bottom of their "Services" page.

I talked to the owner, who was frustrated that people kept calling to ask "how much is a bath and nail trim for my golden retriever?" — even though it was in the PDF.

The problem: People don't download PDFs. Nobody does. It's 2024. You're asking someone to leave your website, open a separate app, scroll through a document, and then come back. That's three steps too many.

Bark & Stay's PDF had 47 service combinations with different sizes (extra small, small, medium, large, extra large, giant). A customer with a 70-pound lab would see "Large: $65-85" and call to ask "is my dog large or extra large?" That's not a pricing problem. That's a clarity problem.

The fix: I built them a simple interactive table on their services page using a free tool (not naming it because they don't pay me). Three rows: "Under 30 lbs," "30-60 lbs," "60+ lbs." Base prices listed clearly. One sentence: "Not sure which size your dog is? Most golden retrievers and labs fall in the 30-60 lb range."

Phone calls asking about pricing dropped by 80%. Online bookings increased 35% in six weeks. The owner later told me she'd spent $1,200 the previous year on a "custom booking software" that didn't solve this problem because the problem was layout, not software.

**Mistake #3: Using "Call for Pricing" on High-End Services Only**

This one kills me because it's so common. A portrait photography studio in Portland, Oregon had clear pricing for their mini-sessions ($395) and standard shoots ($895). But their "elopement package" said "Starting at $3,500 — call for details."

The owner explained: "Elopements are custom. I need to understand what they want before I quote."

What actually happened: They ran Google Ads for "Portland elopement photographer" at $3.80/click. Their landing page showed that custom package — beautiful photos, but no price. Over four months, they spent $2,280 on those ads. They booked exactly two elopements from that campaign. Total revenue from those two bookings: $8,200. Ad spend per booking: $1,140.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: When you hide a price, the customer assumption is always higher than the actual price. The studio was charging $3,500 to $6,500 for elopements. Their average customer, when surveyed, assumed they'd have to pay $8,000-$12,000. Most people didn't call because they assumed they couldn't afford it.

The fix: We put a band on the elopement page: "Most couples invest $3,500-$5,500 for a full elopement day. Book a free 15-minute call, and I'll build a custom quote that fits your exact plan." Three sentences. No hiding. No games.

In the next 90 days, they booked nine elopements from organic traffic alone. The only difference was that people knew whether they were in the right price range before they picked up the phone.

**Mistake #4: Listing Prices That Don't Match Reality**

A mobile pet grooming van in Denver — happy to call them Pooch Mobile — listed "Bath and Brush: $55." That was the price for a well-behaved 15-pound dog. The problem: Pooch Mobile's average customer had a 65-pound labradoodle with tangled fur that took an extra 20 minutes to untangle.

Customers would see $55 on the website, book online, and get a $75-85 invoice after the service. The owner thought this was fine — "I explain it before I start."

Reviews started mentioning "bait and switch" and "pricing was different from website." Within six months, their Google rating dropped from 4.8 to 4.1. New customer bookings fell 22%.

The fix: We listed a range: "Small dogs (under 25 lbs): $55-65. Medium dogs (25-50 lbs): $65-80. Large dogs (50+ lbs): $75-95. Heavy shedding or matting may add $10-20 — we'll confirm before starting."

Within a month, their review mentions of pricing went from negative to neutral. Within three, their rating recovered to 4.5. The owner lost exactly zero customers who canceled after hearing the real price. She'd just been losing customers who felt tricked.

## How to Handle Price Changes Without Losing Customers

If you've been running a local business for more than a year, you've already experienced this: your costs go up. Rent increases. Your supplier raises prices. Your best stylist asks for a raise. So you need to raise your prices, and suddenly you're worried about your website.

I worked with a boutique fitness studio in Chicago called Northside Cycle that hadn't changed their pricing in 18 months. Their costs had gone up 14% across rent, utilities, and instructor pay. They needed to raise class packs from $180 for 10 classes to $200. The owner asked me, "Do I just change the website and hope nobody notices?"

No. Do not do that. I've watched businesses lose $5,000 in a weekend because they changed a price without telling anyone.

Here's what actually works:

Northside Cycle sent an email through Mailchimp (they were already paying $59/month for it) with the subject line: "Why our classes are going up $2." The email opened with: "We've never wanted to be the studio that nickel-and-dimes you. So here's exactly where the increase is going." Then they listed three bullet points: instructor pay increase (+$30/class), new bike maintenance schedule, and higher-quality mats.

They posted the same explanation on their website three weeks before the change went live. They announced the price increase during the last 30 seconds of every class for two weeks. They gave current members a 30-day window to buy their next pack at the old price.

The result: 62% of their active members bought one more pack during the grace period. That was about $9,600 in cash flow they wouldn't have had. Only 4 members out of 260 canceled. Those 4 were people who were already thinking about leaving.

The lesson isn't "don't raise prices." The lesson is "don't raise them silently." Your customers are smarter than you think. They know prices go up. They just want to know you're not pocketing the difference.

If you're using Booksy or Square Appointments, you can schedule price changes in advance and email affected clients automatically. Use that. It's free and it saves you the Friday night panic of realizing you broke your booking flow.

## What to Do When Your Competitors Don't Show Prices

This is the objection I hear most often: "But my competitor in [city] doesn't show prices, and they're doing fine." Fine. Let's talk about what "doing fine" actually means.

I ran an audit for a massage therapy practice in San Diego. The owner, Sarah, had four direct competitors within a two-mile radius. Two showed full pricing. One showed a range ("$80-120 per hour"). One showed nothing — "call for rates."

Sarah was showing full pricing: $95 for a 60-minute Swedish massage, $130 for deep tissue, $175 for a 90-minute hot stone. She was worried she was losing customers to the "call for rates" competitor because her prices were "visible and easy to criticize."

So I spent $47 on a mystery shopping exercise. I asked a friend in San Diego to call each competitor and ask for pricing as a new customer. The "call for rates" business quoted $105 for a 60-minute Swedish. That's $10 more than Sarah. When my friend asked why, the receptionist said, "Our therapists are more experienced." No specifics. No credentials. Just vibes.

The other "range" competitor quoted $85-110 depending on the therapist. They couldn't explain the difference on the phone. Two out of three people who call a massage place and hear "it depends" just hang up and book somewhere else.

Sarah kept her prices visible. She added one line under each service: "All therapists are licensed with minimum 3 years experience." She started running a small Google Ads campaign targeting "massage [her neighborhood]" with her exact prices in the ad copy.

Within three months, her booking rate from Google was 22% higher than the competitor who hid their prices. Not because she was cheaper. Because she was easier.

The uncomfortable truth: When you hide prices, you don't look exclusive. You look like you're afraid someone will think you're too expensive. And customers can smell fear.

## How to Use Prices in Your Google Ads and Social Media

If you're running ads — and if you're not, you should be thinking about it — your prices belong in the ad copy itself. Not the landing page. The ad.

I tested this with a yoga studio in Denver. Two ad groups, same budget, same targeting, same landing page. One ad group used generic headlines: "Yoga Classes in Denver | Book Now." The other used: "Yoga Classes Starting at $15 | Drop-In Rates Available."

The pricing ad had a 47% higher click-through rate. The cost per booking was $8.40 instead of $14.20. Same landing page. Same booking system. The only difference was the words in the blue link.

Here's the logic: When you put a price in your ad, you filter out people who can't afford you. You also attract people who can. The people who click on a "$15 yoga class" ad are telling you they're ready to spend $15 on a yoga class. The people who click on "yoga classes Denver" are telling you they might be curious about yoga. Those are not the same person.

The same principle applies to Yelp and Instagram. A pet groomer in Nashville started putting "Bath & Brush: $45+ | Full Groom: $75+" in their Instagram bio. They got DMs from people saying "finally, a groomer that shows prices" — which is sad because it's 2024 and this is still rare. They booked 12 new clients in two weeks. Those clients had an average lifetime value of $340.

If you're on Yelp, use the price range field. It's one click. Nobody clicks on the business that shows "$$$$" and thinks "this is reasonable." And nobody clicks on the business with no price listed and thinks "this person is confident in their work."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What if my prices are higher than my competitors? Won't showing them scare people away?**

It will scare away people who can't afford you. That's not a loss — that's a filter. I'd rather have 50 people who can afford my services book than 500 people who can't and waste my time asking for discounts. A client in Austin raised her prices by 30% and lost 15% of her inquiries. Her revenue went up because the people who stayed booked more and complained less. Show your prices. Let the price-sensitive customers self-select out. Your time is worth something.

**Q: Should I list prices if I offer custom quotes for every client?**

If every job is genuinely different, use a range. "Most clients invest $2,000-$4,000 for a full kitchen refresh. Free consultation to build an exact quote." That gives people a framework without locking you into a number. If you're doing custom quotes because you're afraid to commit to a price, that's a you problem, not a customer problem.

**Q: What if I change my prices seasonally (holiday rates, summer specials)?**

Update your website the day the new prices take effect. Not the week before. Not the week after. If you're using Square or Booksy, you can schedule price changes in advance. Do that. If you have a recurring client base, email them a week before with the specific change and a "get it at the old price" deadline. Treat them like adults and they'll act like adults.

**Q: Won't competitors see my prices and undercut me?**

They already know your prices. They can call and ask. They can send a friend. They can look at your Booksy or Square listing. Hiding your prices from competitors is like hiding your storefront from other stores on the same street. It doesn't work and it makes you look insecure. Focus on being better, not cheaper.

**Q: I'm a service that changes price based on travel distance. What do I do?**

Put a base price on your website. "In-shop service: $60. Mobile service: $80 + $10 per mile outside [zip code]." Or use a ZIP code calculator — there are free plugins for WordPress and Wix that let people enter their ZIP and get a real price. It's 30 minutes of setup. It will save you 50 phone calls a month.

**Q: Should I show prices for add-ons and upgrades?**

Yes, if you want people to buy them. A nail salon in Denver started listing "Gel upgrade: $15 | Nail art per finger: $5" on their service menu. Add-on rate went from 8% to 22% in one month. People don't buy what they don't know exists. If your add-ons are hidden behind "ask your technician," they don't exist.

## Closing

Here's what I learned in ten years of watching small businesses overcomplicate this: pricing transparency is not a strategy. It's a baseline. Your customers are making decisions with their phones in one hand and their wallets in the other. If you make them work to find out what something costs, they will go to someone who doesn't. I once watched a salon in Brooklyn lose a $6,000 wedding party booking because the bride didn't want to "inquire about pricing" for seven people. She just went to the place that had a menu.

You've got a business to run. Stop making people guess. Put your prices where they can see them, update them when they change, and trust that the right customers will choose you anyway.
