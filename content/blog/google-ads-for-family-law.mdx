**Cost of Google Ads for Family Law Attorneys**

Google Ads is a pay-per-click (PPC) platform, which means you only pay for ads that are clicked. The average cost-per-click (CPC) for Google Ads in the family law niche is around $50-$70 per click. However, this can vary depending on your location, ad targeting, and ad copy.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Too Broad a Geography

**The story:** A solo practitioner in Austin, Texas — let's call her Sarah — came to me after burning $1,800 in three weeks with zero consultations booked. She'd set her location targeting to "Austin, Texas" with a 30-mile radius. Austin is a spread-out city. She was showing up for someone in Round Rock who needed a real estate attorney, someone in Buda looking for a criminal defense lawyer, and someone in Westlake Hills Googling "how to file for divorce without a lawyer." Her click-through rate was fine. Her conversion rate was zero.

**What went wrong:** Google's location targeting is not location *intent*. A 30-mile radius in a metro area brings in people who are physically near Austin but searching for other types of law in other cities. Sarah also had "people in or regularly in your targeted locations" selected, which means Google included anyone who had ever passed through Austin on I-35. She was paying for clicks from San Antonio commuters.

**The fix:** We narrowed her radius to 10 miles. We switched to "presence" targeting — only show ads to people physically in Austin when they search. We added negative keywords: "pro bono," "free consultation," "criminal defense," "real estate," "traffic ticket." We also excluded zip codes that were primarily commercial or university zones where people weren't going to hire a family law attorney long-term.

**The outcome:** Her ad spend dropped to $700/month. Her cost-per-click went from $62 to $48 because the clicks were more relevant. She booked three consultations in the first two weeks. One of those turned into a $4,200 retainer. She spent $1,150 total to get that client. Compare that to the $1,800 she burned getting nothing.

### Mistake #2: Using the Same Ad Copy for Everyone

**The story:** A family law firm in Nashville — three attorneys, one marketing assistant — was running six ads that all said essentially the same thing: "Experienced family law attorneys. Call today for a free consultation." They had one ad group called "Family Law" and they were bidding on "divorce lawyer," "child custody attorney," "alimony lawyer," and "prenuptial agreement lawyer" all in the same group. Their quality score was 4 out of 10. They were paying $72 per click for some keywords that should have cost $35.

**What went wrong:** Google rewards relevance. When you put "prenuptial agreement" in the same ad group as "divorce lawyer," Google doesn't know which ad to show. It shows the generic one. The person searching for a prenup sees "Experienced family law attorneys" and thinks, "This isn't specific to me." They don't click. Or if they do, they land on a page about divorce, get confused, and bounce. Either way, your quality score tanks and your CPC climbs.

**The fix:** We built six separate ad groups: Divorce (filed by wife), Divorce (filed by husband), Child Custody, Child Support, Alimony, Prenuptial Agreements. Each had its own two ads, its own landing page, its own keyword list. The divorce ads mentioned "navigating the end of your marriage." The child custody ads said "protecting your time with your kids." The prenup ads said "planning your future with clarity." Every ad had a specific headline that matched the search intent.

**The outcome:** Quality scores went from 4 to 8. CPC dropped from $72 to $44 on average. One child custody ad had a 12% click-through rate — for legal services, anything above 5% is good. The firm booked 11 consultations in the first month, up from 4 the month before. They attributed $18,000 in signed retainers to the campaign within 60 days.

### Mistake #3: Not Tracking Phone Calls Properly

**The story:** A family law attorney in Portland, Oregon was spending $2,400/month on Google Ads. He told me he was "getting a lot of calls." I asked how many. He didn't know. He asked how many turned into clients. He didn't know that either. His office manager was writing down first names and phone numbers on sticky notes. Half the sticky notes fell off the desk. The ones that didn't had no record of whether the call came from Google, his Yelp listing, or someone walking past his office.

**What went wrong:** Google Ads for family law is a call-heavy business. People don't fill out a form when they're in the middle of a divorce — they pick up the phone. But if you're not using call tracking, you have no idea which keywords are driving calls, which ads are driving calls, or whether those calls are turning into revenue. This attorney was optimizing his campaigns based on click data only. He had paused his best-performing keyword because it had a low click-through rate. The problem? That keyword was generating 80% of his incoming calls. He just didn't know it.

**The fix:** We set up call tracking through CallRail (Google Ads integrates with it natively). We created a separate phone number for each ad group. We set up call recording so we could listen to the first 30 seconds of every call and determine if it was a qualified lead, a wrong number, or a vendor call. We also set up Google Ads call extensions with a dedicated forwarding number so we could see which ads triggered calls versus clicks.

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, we found that the keyword "child custody lawyer Portland" was generating calls that converted 40% of the time — but it had a 1.2% CTR. The attorney had been about to pause it. We shifted 60% of the budget to that keyword and keywords like it. His cost-per-lead dropped from $180 using clicks to $94 using calls. His monthly ad spend stayed the same, but he went from 4-6 leads per month to 11-14. Three of those turned into $5,000+ retainers in the first 30 days.

## How to Structure Your Campaigns for Maximum ROI

I built a lot of campaigns at GroupM and BBDO. Most of the strategies I learned there were designed for million-dollar budgets. For small law firms, you need something leaner. Here's the exact campaign structure I use for family law attorneys running $1,000-$3,000/month in Google Ads.

**One campaign, three ad groups.** Not ten. Not twenty. Three.

- Ad Group 1: "I Need a Divorce" — keywords like "divorce lawyer [city]," "file for divorce," "how to get a divorce in [state]." These are high-intent searches. Someone typing "how to get a divorce in Texas" is doing research, but they're close to acting. Bid aggressively here. Expect $50-$70 CPC.

- Ad Group 2: "I Have a Specific Problem" — keywords like "child custody lawyer [city]," "alimony attorney," "child support modification," "father's rights lawyer." These searchers are further along. They have a specific issue. They're more likely to convert because they know what they need. CPCs here may be $40-$55 depending on competition.

- Ad Group 3: "I'm Just Starting to Think About It" — keywords like "should I get a divorce," "signs of a failing marriage," "legal separation vs divorce," "how much does a divorce cost." These are informational searches. CPCs are lower — $25-$40 — because the competition is less aggressive. These people won't call today, but if you get in front of them now, they'll remember you in three months when they're ready. Keep bids low. Use this ad group as your awareness layer.

**Use phrase match and exact match only.** Broad match is a budget killer. I've seen broad match on "divorce lawyer" trigger ads for "divorce party ideas" and "how to cancel a wedding." That's real. It happened at a client in Chicago. They spent $300 in one weekend on clicks from people planning weddings. Not divorcing. Weddings. Don't use broad match for legal services.

**Set a negative keyword list from day one.** Add these before you launch:
- "free" — unless you offer free consultations, but even then, people searching for "free legal advice" are not your clients
- "pro bono"
- "cheap"
- "low cost"
- "criminal"
- "real estate"
- "traffic"
- "personal injury"
- "DUI"
- Ask your state bar association for common searches that aren't family law. Add those too.

**Set a bid cap.** Don't let Google auto-bid without limits. I had a client in Denver whose max CPC hit $140 for one click because Google's "maximize conversions" strategy decided that one click was worth it. It wasn't. The person bounced in 11 seconds. Set a hard cap at 1.5x your target CPC. If you want to pay $50 per click, cap it at $75. Adjust manually as you collect data.

## Retargeting Strategy for High-Value Legal Services

Most family law attorneys stop at search ads. They think retargeting is for e-commerce and SaaS companies. But retargeting works for legal services because of the timeline. Someone searching for "divorce lawyer" today might call tomorrow, or they might call in 8 weeks after they've gathered documents, talked to their spouse, and worked up the courage.

Here's the retargeting setup I use with family law clients. It costs about $200-$300/month extra and it consistently brings in $3,000-$5,000 in signed retainers per quarter.

**Step 1: Build your audiences in Google Ads.**

- "Search ad clickers" — anyone who clicked your ad in the last 30 days. These people showed intent but didn't convert. Show them a display ad that reinforces trust: "Still deciding? We're here when you're ready. 20+ years of family law experience in [city]."
- "Website visitors" — anyone who visited your site but didn't call. Use Google Analytics to create an audience of visitors who spent more than 30 seconds on your services page or your "About" page. Show them a different ad: "Here's what our clients say" with a testimonial quote and a photo of you or your team.
- "Callers who didn't book" — if you're using call tracking, you can create an audience of people who called but never scheduled a consultation. This requires a bit of data stitching, but it's worth it. Show them an ad with a direct call to action: "Free 30-minute consultation. No pressure. Just answers." Use a different phone number so you know the retargeting ad drove the call.

**Step 2: Use YouTube for top-of-funnel awareness.**

Family law is emotional. People search for comfort as much as they search for advice. Create a 60-second video titled "3 Things No One Tells You About Filing for Divorce" or "What to Expect in Your First Custody Hearing." Run it as a YouTube in-stream ad targeting people in your metro area who have searched for "divorce" or "family law" in the last 7 days. The cost is about $0.10-$0.15 per view. Budget $100/month. It won't drive immediate calls, but it builds trust. I had a client in Detroit who ran this for 90 days and three separate callers mentioned "that video on YouTube" during their consultation.

**Step 3: Exclude converters.**

If someone calls and books a consultation, or if they fill out a contact form, add them to an exclusion list. Do not keep showing ads to someone who is already working with you. It wastes money and it annoys your clients. I set up a Google Ads conversion tracker for calls over 60 seconds and form submissions, and I exclude anyone who triggered either in the last 90 days.

The full retargeting package — display ads + YouTube + audience exclusions — costs about $250/month on top of your search budget. In my experience, it delivers a 3:1 to 5:1 ROAS within 60-90 days. Not overnight. But legal services are a long game. Patience pays.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need to spend $50-$70 per click? That seems insane. Can I get cheaper clicks?**

Yes, you can get cheaper clicks if you target informational keywords like "how much does a divorce cost" instead of "divorce attorney [city]." But those cheaper clicks convert at 1-2%, while the expensive ones convert at 8-12%. The math works out in favor of the expensive clicks. I've tested this at three different firms. The $50 clicks bring in clients for $400-$600 per lead. The $20 clicks bring in tire-kickers who cost $200 in ad spend and never book a consultation. Cheap clicks aren't a bargain if they don't turn into revenue.

**Q: How long does it take to see results? I need clients now, not in six months.**

If you set up the campaign correctly — specific ad groups, proper negative keywords, call tracking — you can see phone calls within 24-48 hours. The question is whether those calls convert. In my experience, the first 7-10 days are a testing period. You'll get some wrong numbers, some people asking about other areas of law, and maybe one or two legitimate leads. By week three, you'll have enough data to optimize. By week six, you should see consistent, qualified leads. If you don't, something is wrong with your landing page, your ad copy, or your bid strategy. Don't wait six months. If you're not seeing results by week six, reach out to someone who knows what they're doing.

**Q: Should I run both search and display ads, or just search?**

Start with search only. Display ads for legal services have a low click-through rate — usually 0.1% to 0.3% — and they attract a lot of accidental clicks. I've seen display campaigns for family law spend $500 and generate exactly zero qualified calls. Add display retargeting after 30 days of search data, but don't start there. Search ads capture intent. Display ads try to create it. For family law, you want to capture people who already know they need help.

**Q: What about Google's "Local Services Ads" for lawyers? Should I use those?**

Yes, if you're eligible. Google Local Services Ads (LSAs) for family law are available in most major US markets now. They show above regular search ads, you pay per lead instead of per click, and Google screens your licensing and insurance. The cost per lead is typically $50-$100, which is often cheaper than traditional search ads. The catch: you have to respond to leads quickly (within 24 hours) and maintain a high rating. If you're consistent, LSAs can be a strong addition to your search campaigns. I've seen firms in Dallas and Phoenix get 60% of their Google leads from LSAs alone.

**Q: I'm a solo attorney. Can I manage Google Ads myself, or do I need to hire someone?**

You *can* manage it yourself, but you'll make mistakes. Every attorney I've worked with who tried to DIY their campaigns made at least two of the three mistakes I outlined above. The learning curve is steep and the cost of mistakes is high — you can burn through $1,000 in a weekend on broad match keywords before you realize what happened. If your budget is under $1,000/month, consider a freelancer who specializes in legal ads. Budget $500-$1,000/month for management. If your budget is over $2,000/month, hire an agency or a dedicated specialist who can do full campaign management, call tracking, and landing page optimization. Going cheap on management is how you end up with a quality score of 4 and a $72 CPC.

**Q: Can I target people based on marital status or income level in Google Ads?**

Not directly. Google's demographic targeting doesn't include marital status or household income for legal services. You can target by age, gender, and parental status, but the accuracy is limited and I've seen it miss the mark. A better approach is to use keyword intent as your targeting. Someone searching for "divorce lawyer [city]" has self-identified as needing your service. You don't need to know their income. You need to know they're searching. Let keyword intent do the targeting for you.
