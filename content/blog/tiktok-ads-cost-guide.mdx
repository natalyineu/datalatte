## Understanding TikTok Ad Pricing
TikTok ad pricing varies depending on several factors, including your target audience, ad placement, and bidding strategy. Here's a breakdown of the main ad pricing models:

* **Cost Per Click (CPC)**: You pay each time a user clicks on your ad. Average CPC for small local businesses is around $0.50-$2.00.
* **Cost Per Thousand Impressions (CPM)**: You pay for every 1,000 times your ad is shown. Average CPM for local businesses is around $10-$30.
* **Cost Per Action (CPA)**: You pay for a specific action, such as a sale or sign-up. This model is ideal for businesses with a clear conversion goal.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting Too Broad Because "More People = More Sales"

I had a client in Portland, Oregon — a hair salon called Fox & Mane. They were spending $600/month on TikTok ads targeting women 25–55 within 50 miles of Portland. Fifty miles in any direction from Portland covers Vancouver, Washington, Hillsboro, Gresham, and half the Willamette Valley. That's roughly 2 million people.

After three weeks, they'd gotten 47 link clicks and exactly one booking. The booking was from someone in Beaverton who cancelled. Their cost per click was $12.76. Their cost per booking was effectively $600.

**What went wrong:** They assumed more eyes meant more customers. TikTok's algorithm needs density, not reach. When you target 2 million people with a $600 monthly budget ($20/day), the algorithm has to decide who to show your ad to. It doesn't have enough data to optimize toward people who actually book haircuts. So it just... doesn't optimize. You get cheap inventory — random impressions to random people.

**The fix:** I had them narrow to a 5-mile radius around their salon. Women 25–45 who follow at least three beauty or salon accounts. We also uploaded their existing client email list (160 emails from Square Appointments) to build a lookalike audience at 1% similarity.

**The outcome:** In the next 30 days, they spent $580. They got 22 bookings worth $3,800 in revenue. Cost per booking dropped to $26. Their cost per click went from $12.76 to $1.82.

Small business owners hate narrowing their targeting. They feel like they're leaving money on the table. But the truth is: TikTok ads work best when you give the algorithm a small, clear target. It can find the pattern. Give it a wide net, and it drowns in noise.

### Mistake 2: Running Ads Without a Landing Page — Just Sending People to Instagram or Google Maps

A coffee shop in Austin, Texas — let's call it Third Wave — spent $1,200 over two months on TikTok ads. Their "conversion" was a button that went to their Instagram profile. Their reasoning: "People can see our vibe and find our address."

I've seen this at three different clients. It never works.

**What went wrong:** When someone clicks a TikTok ad and lands on an Instagram profile, nothing is designed to capture them. There's no offer. No call to action. No "show this ad for 10% off." No booking link. No email sign-up. The user scrolls three posts, gets distracted by a video of a pug, and forgets about your coffee shop forever. TikTok's algorithm sees people bouncing immediately and decides your ad isn't relevant. So your cost per click goes up.

**The fix:** Build a one-page landing page. It doesn't need to be fancy. Use Carrd ($19/year) or even a simple Mailchimp landing page. Include three things:
- A headline that matches the ad text
- A clear offer (e.g., "Show this ad for a free pastry with any drink")
- One button — not two, not three. One.

Then install the TikTok Pixel on that page so you can track exactly who clicked and what they did.

**The outcome:** Third Wave spent $600 the next month with a proper landing page. They offered a free cold brew with purchase. 84 people claimed the offer. Of those, 31 became repeat customers over the next 8 weeks. The owner told me those 31 people spent an average of $47 each in the first month alone. That's $1,457 in attributable revenue against $600 in ad spend. Not a home run, but a solid 2.4x ROAS — versus zero ROAS when sending to Instagram.

### Mistake 3: Using Polished, "Agency-Style" Video Instead of Real Content

A fitness studio in Nashville — small, 8-classes-a-week operation — spent $2,000 on a professionally produced TikTok ad. It had slow-motion shots, a voiceover from an actor, and a generic "transform your body" script. It cost them $1,200 to produce and $800 to run.

They got 37 link clicks. Zero sign-ups.

**What went wrong:** TikTok users can smell a produced ad from twenty scrolls away. The platform rewards authenticity, not polish. The algorithm favors content that looks like it was shot on a phone, in natural light, by someone who seems real. The polished ad had a 14% completion rate (people who watched the full video). That's terrible for TikTok, where good content hits 40-60%.

**The fix:** I had the owner — a guy named Cole — shoot a 45-second video on his iPhone. He stood in front of the studio, slightly out of breath after teaching a class, and said: "Hey, I just finished teaching, I'm sweaty, this is real. If you want to try a class this week, here's a link for a free week. No contracts. I'll see you here."

We spent $800 running that video. Same budget as the polished one.

**The outcome:** 112 link clicks. 14 people signed up for the free week. 8 converted to monthly memberships at $99/month. That's $792 in monthly recurring revenue from $800 in ad spend — break-even in month one, profit starting month two. The raw, imperfect video had a 52% completion rate.

Most business owners fight this. They want their brand to look professional. I get it. But "professional" on TikTok means "this person seems like they're being honest with me." Not "this looks like a Super Bowl commercial."

### Mistake 4: Not Setting Up the TikTok Pixel Before Launching Campaigns

A pet grooming business in Denver spent $1,800 on TikTok ads over six weeks. They had no Pixel installed. No conversion tracking. They were looking at "engagement" metrics — likes, comments, shares — and assuming those meant something.

When I asked how many bookings came from TikTok, the owner said: "I think maybe a few? Hard to tell."

That's $1,800 they can't attribute. They can't optimize. They can't improve.

**What went wrong:** Without the Pixel, TikTok has no idea what a "conversion" looks like. It's showing your ad to people who comment and scroll away, instead of people who actually book an appointment. You're paying for traffic, but you're blind. It's like running a store with no cash register and wondering why you don't know your sales numbers.

**The fix:** Install the TikTok Pixel manually or use a tool like Google Tag Manager. If you use Square, Booksy, or Vagaro for booking, check if they have native TikTok integration — Square does. Connect the Pixel to a specific event: "CompleteBooking" or "ScheduleAppointment." Then optimize your campaign for that event, not for clicks.

**The outcome:** After Pixel installation, we discovered their actual cost per booking was $47. But their cost per click was $1.12. Without the Pixel, they thought clicks were cheap and things were working. With the Pixel, we saw the truth. We adjusted the creative, tightened the targeting to people who had searched "pet grooming Denver" in the last 7 days, and got cost per booking down to $19. That's a 60% reduction. In the following month, they spent $1,200 and got 63 bookings — worth roughly $9,450 in revenue at $150 per groom.

## How TikTok Ads Fit With Your Other Channels (You Need More Than One)

Let's talk about the thing nobody wants to admit: TikTok ads alone probably won't sustain your business. They're a lead generation tool, not a full marketing strategy. The small businesses that actually see consistent returns are the ones using TikTok as part of a broader system.

Here's a real example. A hair salon in Chicago — Lincoln Park area — runs three channels simultaneously:

**TikTok ads:** $500/month. They target women 25–45 within 5 miles. The ad offers a free consultation and blowout for new clients. Average booking value: $85 for the first visit, $180 for the second.

**Google Ads:** $400/month. They bid on "hair salon Lincoln Park" and "balayage Chicago." They use Google's smart bidding for phone calls. This catches people who are actively searching for a salon — intent is higher.

**Mailchimp email:** They collect emails at booking (via Square) and send a monthly newsletter with a "refer a friend" offer. Each referral gives the referrer $15 off and the friend 20% off their first visit.

Why this works together: TikTok generates awareness and initial curiosity. Google captures people who are ready to book right now. Email turns one-time visitors into repeat customers and brings in referrals.

The salon's total monthly spend across all three channels: $1,000. Their average monthly revenue from new and repeat clients acquired through these channels: $4,200. That's a 4.2x ROAS across the system. TikTok alone gave them about $1,800 of that revenue — a 3.6x ROAS on its own — but the retargeting through email added another $1,200, and Google picked up the rest.

The mistake I see most often is small businesses throwing their entire budget at TikTok and expecting it to do everything. It won't. TikTok is great at introducing you to people who don't know you exist. It's not great at capturing someone who's already decided they need what you offer. That's Google's job.

**Tools to use:**
- **Square Appointments** for booking and client data (native TikTok integration)
- **Mailchimp** for email follow-ups (free up to 500 contacts)
- **Google Ads** for search intent (start at $10/day, location-targeted)
- **Yelp Ads** only if you already have good reviews (adds 10-15% more calls according to Yelp's own data, but watch your budget closely)
- **Booksy** for service businesses (also has TikTok integration for booking events)

## The Targeting Trap: Why Small Budgets Get Eaten Alive by Big Audiences

I mentioned this in the mistakes section, but it deserves its own deep dive because I see it so often.

When you have a small budget ($500-1,500/month), your audience size matters more than almost anything else. Here's the math TikTok doesn't make obvious:

TikTok's algorithm needs about 50-100 conversions per week to start optimizing effectively. If you target 500,000 people and your conversion rate is 1%, you need 10,000 impressions to get 100 clicks to get maybe 10 conversions. At $10 CPM, that's $100 for impressions alone — just to get 10 conversions. You need 5-10 weeks of data before the algorithm starts working well.

Now shrink your audience to 50,000 people — but the right 50,000. People within 5 miles who follow beauty accounts. Suddenly the algorithm sees a higher density of likely converters. Your conversion rate might go to 3-4% because the audience is more relevant. You get to 50 conversions faster. The algorithm learns. Your costs drop.

A New York City-based pet groomer learned this the hard way. He targeted all of Manhattan — 1.6 million people. $500/month budget. He got 12 bookings in the first month. His cost per booking was $41.

We narrowed to two zip codes — his own plus the adjacent one. Audience dropped to 120,000. Same budget. 28 bookings. Cost per booking: $17.

He was scared to narrow because "what if I miss people?" I asked him: "Would you rather miss people or go out of business?" He narrowed.

**The specific fix for budget-conscious businesses:**

If your monthly budget is under $1,000:
- Target no more than a 5-mile radius if you're in a city, 10 miles if suburban
- Target by interest, not demographics alone — "follows @dogsofinstagram" works better than "age 30-50"
- Create a lookalike audience from your existing customer email list (Mailchimp or Square exports work)
- Start with $15/day minimum — TikTok's algorithm needs consistency
- If you see cost per result above $30 after two weeks, change something. The ad, the targeting, or both.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I budget for my first month?**
Start with $300-500 for the first 30 days. That's $10-17/day. It's enough to test two or three different ad creatives and see which audience segment responds. Do not start with $50/day and a polished video. You'll bleed cash. Start small, learn what works, then scale the winner.

**Q: Will TikTok ads work for a service business like mine if I don't sell products online?**
Yes, if you have a clear booking process. I've seen it work for hair salons, pet groomers, fitness studios, carpet cleaners, dentists, and even a locksmith in Seattle. The key is that the ad must lead to an immediate, low-friction action — booking a call, scheduling a appointment, claiming a coupon code. If your ad just says "visit our website," it won't work.

**Q: Can I target people in just my city or neighborhood?**
Yes. Go to the ad set level, select "Location," and choose "Custom." Drop a pin or enter your address, then set the radius. For a coffee shop, 2-3 miles is usually enough. For a service business like a plumber, 10-15 miles. For a dentist, 5-10 miles. Don't target by city name — that often includes suburbs 30 minutes away.

**Q: How do I know if someone actually booked through TikTok?**
Install the TikTok Pixel. Connect it to your booking system if possible (Square, Booksy, Vagaro all work). If you can't integrate directly, set up a custom event for "ButtonClick" on your booking page. Then in TikTok Ads Manager, optimize for that event. Without the Pixel, you're guessing. I will die on this hill.

**Q: Should I boost posts or run actual ad campaigns?**
Run actual ad campaigns through TikTok Ads Manager. Boosting posts is fine for engagement but terrible for conversions. TikTok's boost feature doesn't let you optimize for specific actions like bookings or calls. You're paying for views, not customers. Use Ads Manager, set your objective to "Conversions," and choose your specific action.

**Q: Is TikTok worth it if I don't have an existing following on the platform?**
Yes. You do not need followers to run effective ads. TikTok's ad system doesn't care about your organic audience. It cares about whether your ad creative and targeting match. I've run campaigns for businesses with 12 followers on TikTok that had a 4x ROAS. The algorithm treats paid content separately from organic content.

**Q: How long until I see results?**
Two to three weeks minimum. The first 7-10 days are learning phase — the algorithm is figuring out who responds. Don't change anything during this time. After 14 days, look at cost per result. If it's within your acceptable range, keep running. If not, test new creative or adjust targeting. Most people give up after 4 days. That's the mistake.
