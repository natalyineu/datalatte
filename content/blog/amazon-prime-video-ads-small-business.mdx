In early 2024, Amazon made a move that changed the CTV advertising landscape: it added ads to Prime Video. Every Amazon Prime subscriber — all 200 million of them globally — now sees ads unless they pay an extra monthly fee to opt out. Most don't.

The result: Amazon instantly became one of the largest ad-supported streaming platforms in the world, combining the reach of Netflix with the targeting depth of Amazon's purchase data.

For advertisers, this is genuinely significant. Amazon knows what people actually buy, not just what they search for or express interest in. Prime Video ads let you reach a massive streaming audience with purchase intent targeting that no other streaming platform can match.

The question for small businesses: is this accessible at your budget level, and does the targeting advantage justify the premium CPMs?

## What Prime Video Advertising Is

Amazon Prime Video advertising runs as standard pre-roll and mid-roll video ads within Prime Video content. The ads appear in a similar format to traditional TV commercial breaks — non-skippable, appearing at natural content breaks.

What distinguishes it from Hulu, Peacock, or Roku: the **targeting data**. Amazon can target Prime Video viewers based on:

- **Purchase history:** People who've bought pet food, fitness equipment, beauty products, or home improvement items in the last 30/60/90 days
- **Product category interest:** Shoppers who regularly browse your category on Amazon
- **Prime Video viewing history:** Audiences targeted by the genres and shows they watch
- **Amazon Audience segments:** Income, household composition, life stage
- **Location:** Geographic targeting by DMA, zip code, and state

No other streaming platform has Amazon's purchase data depth. For consumer brands that sell categories Amazon carries, this targeting precision is a genuine competitive advantage.

## Who Can Actually Access Prime Video Ads

This is where the "small business opportunity" narrative requires nuance. Amazon Prime Video advertising is primarily accessed through:

**Amazon DSP (Demand-Side Platform):** Amazon's programmatic platform. Historically required a $50,000/month minimum spend through Amazon directly. This makes it inaccessible for most small businesses.

**Amazon's Managed Service:** Direct campaign management for brands spending $10,000–50,000/month. Still a significant minimum.

**Third-party DSPs:** The Trade Desk, DV360, and other programmatic platforms carry Prime Video inventory with lower minimums. If you access via a DSP, you can start with $3,000–5,000 per campaign — more accessible but still substantial.

**Amazon's Expanding Self-Serve Access:** Amazon has been gradually opening Prime Video inventory to smaller advertisers through its self-serve Amazon Ads platform, primarily bundled with Sponsored Products and Amazon streaming TV campaigns. As of 2026, limited self-serve access to streaming TV inventory (which includes Prime Video) is available through Amazon Ads at lower minimums.

**The honest bottom line:** Prime Video advertising is not yet truly accessible to a single-location local business with a $500–2,000/month budget. It's realistically a $3,000+ per campaign channel, most accessible to product brands already advertising on Amazon's ecosystem.

Prime Video's CPMs are among the highest in streaming — $30–50 for standard placements, up to $60–80 for premium purchase-targeted audience segments. You're paying for the targeting quality, not just the reach.

Impression volume at $5,000/month: approximately 100,000–165,000 unique household impressions. For a premium brand, that's reasonable. For a budget-constrained local business, there are more efficient awareness channels.

## When Prime Video Ads Are Worth It for a Smaller Business

Despite the high minimums and CPMs, there are scenarios where Prime Video's specific capabilities justify the investment:

**You're an Amazon seller with strong review ratings.** The combination of Prime Video awareness ads + Amazon Sponsored Products creates a powerful full-funnel: someone sees your video ad in Prime Video, then searches Amazon, finds your product in Sponsored listings with strong reviews, and converts. The purchase intent data makes the retargeting loop extremely tight.

**You have a regional or national product brand.** A specialty coffee brand selling direct-to-consumer across the US has a different ROI equation than a single café. National reach combined with coffee-buyer targeting is compelling at $5,000+/month.

**Your product category is one Amazon carries.** Purchase intent targeting is most powerful when Amazon has your category's buyers clearly identified in their data. Pet products, health supplements, fitness equipment, beauty — these categories have deep Amazon purchase history for targeting.

**You're testing CTV broadly and want data.** Running a parallel Prime Video test alongside Roku and Hulu gives you the only apples-to-apples comparison of how purchase data targeting performs versus demographic targeting. The insights are valuable even if the channel doesn't scale for you.

<Callout type="tip">
If you're an Amazon seller, Prime Video advertising is worth testing even at higher CPMs than alternatives. The closed-loop attribution (you can track whether Prime Video viewers subsequently purchase your product on Amazon) is cleaner than any other streaming platform's attribution. You get real ROAS data, not just impression and website visit proxies.
</Callout>

## The Prime Video vs Hulu vs Roku Decision for Small Business

If your budget is $2,000–3,000/month and you want streaming TV presence:

**Start with Roku** (most accessible, lowest minimums, good reach) or **Hulu** (best self-serve platform, strong demographic targeting). These platforms are genuinely designed for self-serve advertisers at lower budgets.

**Add Prime Video** when:
- Your monthly streaming TV budget exceeds $5,000
- You're an Amazon seller wanting closed-loop attribution
- You're specifically trying to reach your Amazon category buyers on their TV

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can Prime Video be bought without going through Amazon DSP?

Yes — through third-party DSPs like The Trade Desk. Some agencies with programmatic capabilities can access Prime Video inventory with lower per-campaign minimums ($3,000–5,000) than Amazon's own direct minimums. Amazon is also gradually expanding self-serve access through its Amazon Ads platform.

### What's the creative format for Prime Video ads?

Standard video: 15 or 30 seconds, MP4 format, 1920×1080 resolution, stereo audio. Non-skippable. Same technical specs as other premium streaming platforms. Your existing Hulu or Roku creative typically runs on Prime Video without modification.

### Does Prime Video advertising work if I don't sell on Amazon?

Yes, but you lose the closed-loop attribution advantage. You can still reach Prime Video viewers with demographic and interest targeting — the purchase data targeting requires your brand to have an Amazon presence. For non-Amazon brands, Prime Video's targeting advantage is reduced (though still competitive with other streaming platforms).

### When will Prime Video ads be truly accessible to small local businesses?

Amazon's trajectory is to keep expanding self-serve access over time. Based on how Google and Meta evolved their platforms, it's reasonable to expect meaningful self-serve Prime Video access at lower minimums within the next 1–2 years. For now, the minimum spend requirements make it a channel for product brands with meaningful marketing budgets rather than single-location local service businesses.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Treating Prime Video Ads Like Facebook — Broad Targeting With No Intent Filter**

I had a coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas reach out last spring. He'd heard about Prime Video ads from a YouTube guru and decided to go all-in. He spent $2,000 in two weeks targeting "people who like coffee" across the Austin metro. His video was nice. His offer was solid. His results: 187,000 impressions, 14 clicks to his website, and zero store visits he could attribute.

The problem wasn't the platform. It was how he used it.

Prime Video ads are not Facebook. You aren't paying for retargeting or interest-based audiences. You're paying for Amazon's purchase data — and if you don't use that data, you're paying a premium for what's essentially a TV commercial with bad targeting.

**The fix:** Stop targeting vague interests. Use Amazon's purchase history targeting. Instead of "coffee lovers," target people who bought coffee beans, espresso machines, or pour-over kits on Amazon in the last 90 days. Then layer on a 15-mile radius around your shop. That's the actual power of this platform.

**What happened next:** We restructured his campaign. Reduced the target to people with verified coffee-related purchases, added a "PRIME15" coupon code unique to the ad, and dropped the daily cap to $50. After three weeks: $1,050 spent, 22 coupon redemptions, $780 in tracked revenue from in-store purchases, and 6 new email signups via the landing page. Not a home run, but a 74% return on ad spend. On the first test, he was at zero.

**Mistake #2: Ignoring Attribution Entirely**

A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon spent $1,500 on Prime Video ads over two months. She had a great ad — before-and-after transformations, local music, clear call to action. But she had no way to track who saw it. No promo code. No landing page. No QR code. No unique phone number.

She assumed the campaign was working because foot traffic went up. But October is always busy for salons. She couldn't say if her ad did anything. She canceled the campaign out of frustration.

**The fix:** You need *some* attribution bridge. For local service businesses, the easiest option is a unique phone number through a service like CallRail or even Google Voice. Or a custom landing page on your website with a URL that only appears in the Prime Video ad (like yoursalon.com/prime). Or a QR code that links to your Booksy booking page.

**What happened next:** We set up a call tracking number and a simple landing page with a free consultation offer. She tested again for six weeks with a $400/month budget. The call tracking recorded 14 calls, 8 of which converted into appointments. The landing page captured 5 more booking requests. Total revenue from tracked conversions: $2,340. Campaign cost: $600. That's a 290% return. She only knew because she measured.

**Mistake #3: Overlooking Creative Fatigue**

A pet grooming business in Nashville ran one video for eight weeks straight. Same ad. Same offer. Every time someone in their 20-mile radius watched Prime Video, they saw the same golden retriever getting a wash. The first two weeks performed well — 4.1% click-through rate to their booking page. By week four, it dropped to 0.8%. By week six, the business owner told me they felt like they were pouring money down a drain.

**The fix:** CTV audiences experience fatigue faster than social media because there's no skip button and no feed to scroll past. You need at least three ad variants for a two-month campaign, rotated every two weeks. Change the opening shot, the offer, or the music. You don't need new production each time — just recut existing footage with different text overlays or a different opening line.

**What happened next:** We made three versions of the same ad: one focusing on dog breeds, one on quick turnaround time, one on a "first grooming free" offer. Rotated them every 10 days. Same budget, same targeting. Click-through rate stabilized at 2.6%. More importantly, the booking rate from clicks went up — people who responded to specific offers converted at higher rates than people who saw a generic ad. The business spent $1,200 over eight weeks and generated $3,800 in bookings. Not life-changing, but profitable enough that they kept it running as a retargeting channel.

**Mistake #4: Scaling Before You Understand Your Baseline**

A boutique clothing store in Chicago ran a one-week test on Prime Video ads. They saw a 40% lift in website traffic and got 12 online orders. The owner was thrilled and immediately scaled from $500/week to $3,000/week.

Two weeks later, the store had spent $6,000. CPMs had jumped from $31 to $48. The click-through rate dropped. The orders fell to 9 total across the two weeks. The owner had no idea why.

**The fix:** Prime Video ads bid in a real-time auction. When you increase your budget, the algorithm doesn't just show more ads — it expands your audience into less relevant pools. CPMs climb, and your cost per acquisition rises. The right way to scale is 15-20% per week, while monitoring frequency caps and cost per action.

**What happened next:** We dropped the budget back to $500, let the campaign stabilize for a week, then increased to $750, then $1,000. We added a frequency cap of two impressions per household per week. At $1,000/week, CPMs sat at $34 and the store averaged 14 orders per week — roughly consistent with the original performance. The owner's panic was avoidable. It was just scaling too fast.

## How to Test Prime Video Ads Without Burning Your Budget

Most guides will tell you to start with a minimum of $3,000/month for Prime Video campaigns. That's true if you go through the self-serve Amazon Ads console — the platform enforces a $100/day minimum for Prime Video placements.

But there's a workaround. Managed service partners (including agencies like DataLatte) can run campaigns on your behalf with lower minimums. I've set up tests for as little as $300/month. You won't get massive reach, but you'll get enough data to decide if Prime Video works for your business.

Here's the test structure I recommend for a small business:

- **Budget:** $600 over 60 days ($300/month)
- **Creative:** One 15-second video, one 30-second variant
- **Targeting:** Purchase-based (people who bought a relevant product on Amazon in the last 90 days) + location radius (15 miles around your business)
- **Tracking:** A unique URL and a promo code specific to this campaign
- **Conversion goals:** Measurable actions — coupon redemptions, form fills, phone calls

I ran this exact setup for a yoga studio in Denver. $300/month, targeting people who had purchased yoga mats, blocks, or leggings on Amazon within a 20-mile radius. The ad featured a "first month free" offer. We tracked redemptions through a custom QR code leading to a Mailchimp signup form.

Results after 60 days: $600 spent, 83,000 impressions, 211 clicks to the form, 38 signups, and 12 of those signed up for paid memberships within 30 days. That's $2,160 in membership revenue — a 360% ROAS. The studio owner now runs Prime Video ads as a consistent top-of-funnel channel, alongside Google Ads for search and Booksy for booking.

The key: we started small, kept the offer simple, and tracked everything. If the results had been flat, the studio would have been out $600 instead of $3,000.

## What Prime Video Ads Can't Do (And Why That Matters for Your Business)

I've talked a lot about the targeting power of Amazon's purchase data. It's real. But it's also incomplete. Here's what Prime Video ads won't give you:

**Hyperlocal targeting.** Amazon's location targeting works at the DMA (Designated Market Area) level, plus a custom radius. But the minimum radius is usually around 15-20 miles in practice. If you're a coffee shop in downtown Chicago, you'll reach people in Naperville and Evanston. Some of those people might drive in. Most won't. For businesses that rely on a 2-mile customer radius — dry cleaners, sandwich shops, bungalow salons — Prime Video ads are too broad.

**Demographic layering.** Amazon won't let you target by income, education, marital status, or home ownership. Those layers are available on Facebook and even Google Display. If your business relies on a specific demographic — say, new parents or homeowners — Prime Video's purchase data might not overlap with your audience as cleanly as you'd hope.

**Real-time retargeting.** You can retarget Prime Video viewers if you've installed the Amazon Ads pixel on your website. But the retargeting window is longer and less precise than Facebook's. For a local business running a two-week promotion, that's a problem.

**Granular attribution.** Amazon gives you view-through and click-through data, but it doesn't tie directly to in-store purchases unless you use a promo code or QR code. The platform also doesn't integrate natively with standard small business tools like Square, Lightspeed, or Yelp.

A bakery in Brooklyn spent $1,500 on Prime Video ads to promote a new gluten-free product line. They targeted people who had purchased gluten-free products on Amazon. The campaign got 120,000 impressions. But the bakery couldn't track who actually came in, and the product's launch fell flat. The issue wasn't the ad — it was that Prime Video couldn't tell them if their message reached the right sub-audience. A combination of Prime Video for awareness and Yelp Ads for local search would have been more effective, at a lower total cost.

The bottom line: Prime Video ads are a reach tool with targeting advantages for *product* businesses. For local service businesses, they work best as part of a mix, not the only channel.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I target only people within 10 miles of my shop?**

No. Amazon's minimum radius is typically 15-25 miles, and it's based on DMA boundaries, not zip code. If you're a single-location business, you'll serve ads to people who live too far away to visit. Mitigate this by using a strong local offer on the creative and tracking redemptions so you know how many actually came from within your real service area.

**Q: What's the actual minimum spend if I go direct through Amazon?**

The self-serve Amazon Ads console requires a $100/day minimum for Prime Video placements. That's $3,000 per month minimum. If that's too high, work with a managed service or agency that can run campaigns on a lower budget. I've run Prime Video tests for $500/month through agency-managed accounts.

**Q: I run a hair salon with a $1,500 monthly marketing budget. Should I use Prime Video?**

Not as your only channel. At $1,500, I'd allocate $250-400 to Prime Video for awareness, $800 to Google Ads for "hair salon near me" searches, and the rest to Booksy ads (which is where salon customers actually book). Prime Video alone won't generate enough conversions at this budget level to justify the full $3,000 minimum. But a small test can be worth it if you track carefully.

**Q: How do I measure whether someone came to my store because of the Prime Video ad?**

Three reliable methods: a unique promo code printed on the ad graphic, a custom URL that redirects to your booking page, or a call tracking number. Do not rely on "I felt like foot traffic was up." Use one of these methods for at least 60 days before making any decisions.

**Q: Is Amazon just trying to sell me a more expensive version of what I can do on Facebook?**

Not exactly. Facebook lets you target interests and behaviors. Amazon lets you target actual purchases. Those are different signals. For a business selling products — say, a local pet store selling dog food — Amazon's data is more powerful because you're reaching people who have already proven they'll spend money on dog products. For a service business like a plumber, Facebook is probably better because Amazon doesn't know your customers' home repair purchase history.

**Q: What kind of video works best? Do I need to hire a videographer?**

You need a 15-30 second video with a clear offer and a reason to act within the ad. You don't need a Hollywood production. I've seen a florist in Nashville film an iPhone ad of her arranging a bouquet and it outperformed a professionally produced spot because it felt authentic. What doesn't work: shaky footage, no audio, or a talking head that doesn't show the product. If you have $300, hire a freelance videographer for a half-day shoot. If you have $0, use Canva or CapCut to edit existing photos into a slideshow with text and music. The offer matters more than the video quality.

**Q: How quickly should I expect results?**

You need at least 30 days of consistent spend to gather enough data. In the first two weeks, you'll see impression counts and maybe a few redemptions. By week four, you'll see a clearer picture of your cost per action. Make a decision after 60 days or $1,000 spent — whichever comes first. Anything less and you're guessing.
