## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Buying an Email List (Because "Everyone in Austin Drinks Coffee")

**The story:** A coffee shop in Austin, TX — let's call it "Brew & Beats" — spent $1,200 on a list of 8,000 email addresses they bought from a third-party broker. The owner figured: "Austin loves coffee, these people live near me, this is a shortcut to growth." They uploaded the list to Klaviyo and sent a welcome offer: 20% off any drink.

**What happened:** Klaviyo immediately flagged the list as high-risk. Within 12 hours, 62% of the emails bounced. Another 18% marked the message as spam. The remaining 20%? Zero sales. Klaviyo's algorithm throttled their sending reputation for the next 30 days, meaning even their legitimate subscribers had emails landing in Promotions or spam folders. Their organic open rate dropped from 38% to 11%. It took four months and a manual IP warm-up to recover.

**What to do instead:** Zero-list start. Put a clipboard on your counter with a sign: "Get a free drink on your birthday — just leave your email." Use Klaviyo's pop-up form on your WiFi landing page. Collect 50–100 emails per week organically. Then email those people. One bakery in Portland, OR did exactly this — they collected 340 emails in six weeks using just a counter form and a free pastry offer. Their first campaign generated $2,100 in revenue from repeat visits by people who actually wanted to hear from them.

**The fix:** Buy a domain that matches your business, warm it up slowly (start with 50 sends/day), and only send to people who opted in through your own channels. The $1,200 you would have spent on a list? Put it toward a Google Ads campaign targeted to your zip code instead.

**Outcome:** The Austin shop scrapped the purchased list. They spent $1,200 on Google Local Ads instead, collected 280 opted-in emails over two months, and saw $3,400 in attributed revenue from their first three Klaviyo campaigns. They also stopped getting spam complaints from strangers.

### Mistake 2: Sending Only Sales Emails (No Context, No Rhythm)

**The story:** A specialty coffee roaster in Denver, CO set up Klaviyo and sent 11 campaigns in their first month. Every single one was a discount offer. "15% off single-origin beans." "Buy two bags, get one free." "Flash sale: 20% off cold brew concentrate." Their open rates started at 22%, dropped to 9% by the third week, and by the end of the month, 14% of their list had unsubscribed.

**The problem:** People don't want to be sold to every time they open an email. They want to know when you got a new single-origin from Guatemala. They want the story behind your seasonal syrup. They want a recipe for a maple latte they can make at home. The Denver roaster was treating their email list like a coupon app.

**What to do instead:** Use a 60/30/10 split. Sixty percent of your emails should be content: origin stories, brewing guides, behind-the-scenes at the roastery, staff picks. Thirty percent should be gentle reminders: new seasonal drinks, menu changes, events, loyalty point summaries. Ten percent should be direct offers — and those offers should be exclusive to email subscribers, not posted on Instagram.

One shop in Nashville uses Klaviyo's flow builder to send a weekly "Coffee Note" — 150 words about a single coffee they're roasting, a quick brewing tip, and a low-key call to action ("Grab a bag before it sells out"). Their open rate has held at 48% for 18 months. They send a discount offer once per month, and that single email drives 22% of their online sales.

**The fix:** Plan a month of content before you send a single email. Use a calendar. Monday: origin story. Wednesday: staff pick. Friday: recipe. Repeat. Only insert an offer when you have something actual to offer — not because you feel like you need to "send something."

**Outcome:** The Denver roaster rebuilt their strategy. After three months of the 60/30/10 model, their open rate climbed back to 34%, unsubscribes dropped to under 1% per campaign, and their monthly revenue from Klaviyo rose from $1,100 to $4,600. They also noticed something: customers who clicked on a content email were 3x more likely to purchase in the next campaign.

### Mistake 3: Not Segmenting (Because "Everyone Drinks Coffee, Right?")

**The story:** A bakery + coffee shop in Portland, OR had 2,400 email subscribers. They sent every campaign to every subscriber. They promoted a new vegan banana bread. The problem? 900 of their subscribers only come in for espresso drinks and have never bought food. Another 600 are wholesale accounts who order beans for their own shops. Those 1,500 people did not care about banana bread. They ignored the email. Worse, the wholesale accounts started flagging the messages as irrelevant. Open rates for the entire list fell from 33% to 19% in two months.

**The fix:** Klaviyo's segmentation is its best feature. Split your list by purchase behavior. People who bought beans in the last 30 days get bean-related emails. People who only buy drinks get drink promotions. Wholesale accounts get a separate flow entirely.

One shop in Chicago set up three segments: "Bean Buyers" (purchased whole bean or ground coffee in last 60 days), "Cafe Regulars" (purchased a drink in last 14 days), and "Lapsed Visitors" (no purchase in 90 days). Each segment got a different email. Bean Buyers got a single-origin spotlight. Cafe Regulars got a loyalty reward reminder. Lapsed Visitors got a "We miss you" with 20% off a drink. The lapsed segment alone recovered $1,800 in revenue in the first month — from people who had otherwise been silently ignored.

**Outcome:** The Portland bakery segmented by purchase category. They set up a flow for food buyers, a flow for drink-only customers, and a flow for wholesale. Total list size stayed the same, but email revenue per recipient went from $0.42 to $1.19. Their open rate climbed back to 31%. They also found that wholesale customers engaged more when they received their own dedicated monthly newsletter with pricing updates and seasonal bean offerings.

Here's 10% off your first booking."

A coffee shop in NYC with an online espresso bean store set this up. They saw an 18% conversion rate on the first email alone. The second email (with the 10% discount) recovered another 9%. Combined, they recovered 27% of abandoned carts, adding $3,200 in monthly revenue from people who would have just walked away.

**Outcome:** The Nashville pet groomer set up a two-email flow via Klaviyo's Shopify integration. They recovered 31% of abandoned bookings in the first 30 days, worth $1,300. That flow alone generated $540 in new bookings from people who thought their pet might be anxious.

## Integrating Klaviyo With Google Ads (For When Organic Isn't Enough)

Klaviyo's integration with Google Ads is one of the most underused tools I've seen in small businesses. Here's the setup that actually works.

**The setup:** In Klaviyo, create a segment of people who opened any email in the last 14 days but did not click. Export that list to Google Ads as a custom audience. Then build a "Retargeting Lite" campaign — small budget ($10–$15/day), a single ad with a clear offer ("First drink free when you join our loyalty program"), and a landing page that captures their email if they aren't already subscribed.

A coffee shop in Miami tried this. They were spending $500/month on Google Ads with generic keywords ("coffee shop Miami") and getting terrible ROI — about $1.20 returned for every $1 spent. They switched to retargeting their email openers. Their cost per conversion dropped 64%. They were spending $200/month and generating $1,100 in tracked revenue from new customer visits.

**Why it works:** Email openers are already warmed up. They know your name. They just needed a nudge. Google Ads retargeting is that nudge. The key is keeping the audience narrow — only people who have shown interest in the last two weeks. Anything older than that is noise.

**Another application:** Use Klaviyo to create a segment of customers who purchased once in the last 90 days but not in the last 30. Push that list to Google Ads as a "Reactivate" audience. One Denver barbershop did this — sent a $5-off haircut offer to that segment via paid search. They saw a 22% conversion rate and added $800 in repeat revenue in the first month.

**Budget note:** Don't spend more than $300/month on this until you've proven the loop works. Start with $100/month, track the UTM parameters through Klaviyo, and scale only when you see a clear $3 return per $1 spent.

## Using Klaviyo With Your POS System (Square, Toast, Shopify POS)

Most small business owners I talk to think Klaviyo is just for online stores. They have a coffee shop with a Square terminal and think they're excluded. You're not.

**Square integration:** Square connects directly to Klaviyo. Every in-person transaction creates a customer profile with purchase data. You can then trigger emails based on in-store behavior. Someone bought a bag of beans in person? Send them a brewing guide two days later. Someone bought a drip coffee every morning for two weeks and then stopped? That's a lapsed customer flow.

A coffee shop in Austin (different one, I promise) set up a simple post-purchase flow for in-store customers. Anyone who spent $10 or more in one transaction got an email the next day saying "Thanks for stopping by — here's a recipe for the drink you ordered." Their repeat visit rate within 14 days went from 12% to 27%. The email cost them nothing but 30 minutes of setup time.

**Toast integration:** Toast works similarly for restaurants and cafes. One pizza-and-coffee spot in Chicago used Toast + Klaviyo to create a "Third Visit" flow. After a customer's third in-store purchase, they received an automated email with a free pastry offer. The owner told me they recovered $2,300 in revenue from lapsed customers in the first quarter — customers who had made three visits and then disappeared for months.

**What most guides skip:** Import your historical transaction data. Square and Toast both allow bulk exports of customer purchase history. Upload that CSV to Klaviyo as a custom event. Now you have purchase data going back months or years. Segment customers who bought in Q1 but not Q2. Send them a re-engagement offer. One bakery in Brooklyn did this with three years of Square data and recovered $4,100 in revenue from "dead" customers — people who hadn't visited in over a year but responded to a personal "We miss you" email.

**The setup pain:** Yes, connecting Square to Klaviyo takes 10 minutes. But the CSV import takes longer — you need to format the columns correctly (email, purchase date, total spent, product name). Most people skip this step because it's tedious. That's exactly why you should do it. The bakeries and salons that do the tedious work are the ones who see results.

## When To Use SMS (And When To Shut Up)

Klaviyo's SMS feature is powerful, but it's also easy to abuse. I've seen a fitness studio in Nashville send 14 SMS messages in one month. Their opt-out rate hit 22%. They lost more customers than they gained.

**The rule:** SMS is for time-sensitive, high-value messages only. Not "new blog post." Not "we're having a sale." Not "happy Monday." The only acceptable SMS use cases for a small service business are:

1. Appointment reminders (24 hours and 1 hour before)
2. "We have fresh donuts today" (only if you have a predictable schedule and customers opted in for this)
3. Last-minute cancellations ("We just got a 2 PM slot open — first to book gets it")
4. Exclusive VIP offers (once per quarter, max)

**The setup:** Use Klaviyo's SMS consent checkbox on your signup forms. Do not import SMS numbers from your POS unless customers explicitly opted in. The fine on SMS violations under TCPA starts at $500 per message sent without consent.

**What actually works:** A nail salon in Denver added an SMS flow for appointment confirmations only. They also set up a weekly "Friday Cans" message (they're next to a brewery) — a single text saying "We have two appointments left today, and the brewery next door has a new IPA." That weekly text generated $400/month in last-minute bookings from people who were already nearby.

**What doesn't work:** Sending an SMS blast to your entire list because you want to clear out day-old pastries. That's what your email list is for. One pet groomer in Chicago sent an SMS at 9 PM on a Saturday promoting a "Sunday walk-in special." Three people replied "stop," one replied "who is this?" and zero appointments were booked. They also got a warning from their SMS provider about send times.

**Threshold:** Keep SMS to no more than 2–4 messages per month per subscriber. Anything above that and you're training your audience to ignore you. Or worse — to report you as spam.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn't Mailchimp cheaper for a small business?**

Mailchimp's free tier covers 500 contacts with limited features. Klaviyo's free tier covers 250 contacts with more robust automation. If you have fewer than 250 contacts, Klaviyo is cheaper because you're paying $0. If you have 1,000 contacts, Klaviyo will cost you around $45/month; Mailchimp will cost $59/month for similar functionality. But the real cost is not the monthly subscription — it's what you lose in revenue by using Mailchimp's weaker segmentation. Mailchimp's "segments" are based on tags and basic groups. Klaviyo's segments use actual purchase behavior, browse history, and event data. I've run parallel tests for clients. Using Klaviyo's segmentation, one bakery generated 2.3x more revenue per email than the same content sent through Mailchimp. Do the math.

**Q: I own a hair salon, not a coffee shop. Does any of this apply?**

Almost everything. A salon has appointment slots (booked or empty), a product line (shampoo, styling tools), and repeat customers (color appointments every 6 weeks). The same principles apply: segment by service type, send reminders for re-book dates, use abandoned cart flows for online product orders. One salon in Portland used Klaviyo to create a "Color Touch-Up" flow — automated emails sent at week 5 after a color service. Their re-booking rate for color clients went from 41% to 68%. The only difference is the product names in your campaigns.

**Q: How often should I email my list?**

Weekly is the sweet spot for most small service businesses. Bi-weekly is fine if you have less content to share. Monthly is too infrequent — people will forget who you are and mark you as spam when you finally show up. Once you fall below once per month, your deliverability drops because engagement signals go stale. One fitness studio in NYC emailed once per week with a schedule update and a client spotlight. Their open rate held at 39% for two years. When they tried emailing three times per week for a month to promote a new class schedule, unsubscribes jumped 4x and revenue per email dropped.

**Q: Do I need a separate domain for sending emails?**

If you're sending from a new domain with no sending history, yes. Set up a subdomain like **mail.yourcoffeeshop.com** and warm it up gradually. Start with your most engaged subscribers (people who opened in the last 7 days) and send to 50–100 people per day for the first week. Then increase by 100 per week. This prevents your emails from landing in spam. One pet groomer in Nashville skipped this step, sent their first campaign to 2,000 people from their main domain, and got blocked by Google on the first send. It took three weeks to get unblocked.

**Q: What's the one email that will make me the most money?**

A post-purchase thank-you with a related offer. Someone buys a bag of your house blend? Send them an email the next day: "Thanks for your order — here's a pour-over guide for that coffee, and use code POUR10 for 10% off a ceramic dripper." The purchase is still top of mind. The offer is relevant. One coffee shop in San Diego generated $2,400 in add-on sales from this single automated flow over three months. The email cost them nothing. The only investment was writing the copy.

**Q: What about CAN-SPAM and GDPR — do I need a lawyer?**

No, but you need to follow the rules. Every email must include your physical business address and an unsubscribe link. You must honor unsubscribes within 10 business days. If you're sending to people in the UK or EU, you need explicit consent (a checked checkbox, not a pre-checked one). You do not need a lawyer to set this up — Klaviyo's default templates include the required fields. I've seen someone get fined $16,000 for sending to a list of 400 people who never opted in. The fine came from a single complaint. Follow the rules.
