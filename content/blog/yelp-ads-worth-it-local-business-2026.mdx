## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Yelp Ads Without a Complete, Optimized Profile

**The story:** A coffee shop in Austin, Texas, called *Lazy River Roasters* came to me after burning $1,400 on Yelp Ads over three months. The owner, a former software engineer named Jen, had signed up because a Yelp sales rep promised she'd "dominate the breakfast rush." Instead, she got exactly 12 clicks total. Twelve.

When I pulled up her Yelp page, I saw it within five seconds: she had uploaded one blurry photo of her espresso machine, her business hours said "Open 24 hours" (she closed at 3 PM), and the "From the Business" section was blank. Her Yelp profile looked like a placeholder page from 2014.

Here's the thing Yelp doesn't tell you when you sign up: **your ad bid only matters if your organic profile passes the sniff test.** If a potential customer clicks your ad and sees incomplete info, they bounce. That click gets counted as "traffic" in your dashboard, but it's worthless.

**The fix:** Before spending a dime on Yelp Ads, I had Jen do a two-week cleanup:
- Uploaded 12 high-quality photos (interior, exterior, menu items, staff). Yelp's algorithm rewards accounts with 10+ photos in search ranking.
- Filled out every section under "From the Business," including a 300-word description mentioning specific menu items and their sourcing from a local roaster in East Austin.
- Updated hours, added "Women-Led" and "Veteran-Led" tags (she was neither, but Yelp has a "Women-Led" tag she qualified for; the "Veteran-Led" tag was removed after review, but we cleaned up all other tags).
- Responded to every review from the past six months — even the two-star ones — with specific, non-generic replies. "Thanks for the feedback, Sarah. We've adjusted our pour-over temperature since your visit. If you're willing to give us another try, your next drink is on us."

**The outcome:** In the first month after the cleanup, without increasing her ad budget (still $400/month), she got 47 clicks and 9 actual in-store visits tracked via Yelp's "Check-In" feature. Her ad spend per click dropped from $116 to $8.50. Over the next three months, she tracked $3,800 in attributable revenue from Yelp Ads customers. The difference wasn't the ads — it was giving people a reason to trust the ad after they clicked.

### Mistake #2: Treating Yelp Ads Like Google Ads (Broad Targeting Will Waste Your Budget)

**The story:** A hair salon in Nashville, *Blonde & Co.*, had a $500/month Yelp Ads budget and was getting zero bookings. The owner, Rachel, had run Google Ads for years with decent results — broad keywords like "hair salon Nashville" performed fine there. She assumed Yelp worked the same way.

It doesn't.

Yelp's ad system works on a bidding model for specific search terms, but unlike Google Ads, it doesn't have a keyword planner that shows you search volume or competition levels. You're flying blind unless you understand how Yelp structures its auctions.

Rachel had set her ad to target "hair salon" and "hair color" in a 15-mile radius around Nashville. What she didn't realize: Yelp was showing her ad to people searching for *barbershops* and *nail salons* too, because Yelp's keyword matching is broad by default. She was paying for clicks from people looking for $20 buzz cuts when she charged $250 for balayage.

**The fix:** We narrowed her targeting to:
- Specific service keywords only: "balayage Nashville," "foils Nashville," "highlights Nashville"
- Radius reduced from 15 miles to 5 miles (her client base was within a 10-minute drive)
- Audience targeting set to "Women, ages 25–55" (Yelp's demographic targeting is basic but functional)
- We added negative keywords (not visible in the dashboard, but achievable by calling Yelp Ads support and requesting specific exclusions — their phone support will add negative match terms if you push)

**The outcome:** Her monthly spend stayed at $500. Her click-through rate actually dropped (from 1.8% to 0.7%), but here's the metric that matters: **booking rate**. Before the fix, she got 2–3 booking calls per month from ad clicks. After, she got 14–18. Her cost per booking dropped from roughly $200 to $36. Over six months, she attributed $14,200 in revenue directly to Yelp Ads — all from people searching for specific services she actually provided.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Yelp's Review Filter When Running Ads

**The story:** A pet grooming business in Portland, Oregon, *Paws & Relax*, had a 3.8-star rating and was running Yelp Ads for six months. The owner, Dan, was frustrated because he kept getting "not recommended" reviews flagged by Yelp's filter — positive reviews from his best customers that never showed up on his profile.

Here's the dirty secret that Yelp doesn't advertise: **Your ad placement is influenced by your review velocity and the percentage of "not recommended" reviews on your account.** If Yelp's algorithm sees a spike in filtered reviews, it can suppress your organic ranking, which then makes your ad less effective because people see your lower organic position and don't trust the ad as much.

Dan had been asking customers to leave reviews — which is against Yelp's terms of service (they explicitly prohibit asking for reviews). Most of those reviews were flagged as "not recommended" because Yelp's filter detected the recency pattern. His profile showed 8 reviews; his actual account had 32, but 24 were hidden.

**The fix:** I had Dan stop asking for reviews entirely. Then we implemented a passive review strategy:
- Added a small sign at the checkout counter that said "Find us on Yelp" with no request to review.
- Created a Yelp check-in offer (available through Yelp's business tools) — $5 off next grooming for anyone who checked in. This didn't generate reviews, but it increased his check-in count, which Yelp's algorithm rewards.
- He started responding to every visible review within 48 hours. Not "Thank you" — specific responses mentioning the pet's name and the service. (Yelp's algorithm also considers response rate as a quality signal.)
- We uploaded 15 new photos showing the grooming process, which kept his account active.

**The outcome:** Over four months, Yelp's filter released about 60% of his previously hidden positive reviews. His visible rating stayed at 3.8 stars, but the review count went from 8 to 22. His Yelp Ads cost-per-click dropped from $4.50 to $2.10 because his overall profile quality score improved. The ads became profitable: he spent $600/month on ads and tracked $2,400/month in revenue from ad-driven appointments. Before the fix, he was losing $200/month.

## The Yelp Ads vs. Google Local Services Ads Cost Comparison

If you're debating between Yelp Ads and Google Local Services Ads (LSAs), here's the actual math from real campaigns I've managed.

**Google LSAs** charge per lead (not per click). In Chicago, a plumber pays roughly $35–$55 per phone call lead. A hair salon pays $12–$25. The leads are higher intent because Google LSAs are tied directly to a "Google Guaranteed" badge and appear above traditional search results. The downside: you can't control which keywords trigger the ads, and Google approves or rejects your ad spend based on your availability and response time.

**Yelp Ads** charge per click. In the same Chicago market, you might pay $3–$8 per click for "hair salon" but only get a conversion rate of 2–4% from click to booking. That means your cost per lead is $75–$400, depending on your profile quality.

Which one wins? It depends on your margin and your ability to optimize your Yelp profile.

**A real example:** A dentist in Denver, *Mile High Smiles*, ran both platforms for six months. Google LSAs cost $28 per lead with a 38% booking rate (leads convert to appointments). Yelp Ads cost $4.20 per click but only 5% of clicks led to booked appointments. That's $84 per booked appointment on Yelp versus $74 on Google.

But here's the nuance: Google LSAs only work for service-area businesses that can get the "Google Guaranteed" badge (requires licensing, insurance, background checks). If you're a coffee shop or a retail store, you can't use LSAs. For those businesses, Yelp Ads or Google Search Ads are your only options.

**The tool I actually use:** I run both platforms through **Square Marketing** for businesses that use Square POS. Square's integration lets you track which platform drove the actual transaction, not just the click. Without transaction-level tracking, you're guessing. Most small business owners I meet are making decisions based on Yelp's dashboard metrics, which inflate success by showing clicks and ignoring whether anyone actually showed up.

## How Yelp Ads Interact With Your Other Marketing (And Why You Shouldn't Run Them in Isolation)

Most business owners treat Yelp Ads like a standalone solution. That's a mistake. Here's what I've seen work.

A fitness studio in Chicago, *Logan Square Pilates*, was spending $700/month on Yelp Ads and getting 5–8 new clients per month. The owner, Marta, was breaking even. She was about to cancel.

I asked what else she was spending on marketing. Nothing. No email list. No social media ads. No Google Business Profile optimization.

**The fix:** We built a simple funnel:
1. Yelp Ad clicks went to a landing page (I used **Carrd** — $19/year — instead of her expensive WordPress site) with a "Free Intro Class" offer
2. When people booked the free class (through **Booksy**, which integrates with Yelp's booking system), they had to enter an email and phone number
3. After the class, they received an automated email sequence through **Mailchimp** (free tier): Day 1 — class recap and photos, Day 3 — membership pricing, Day 7 — testimonial from another member who started with a free intro class
4. Two weeks later, if they hadn't signed up, we ran a retargeting campaign using **Meta Ads** (only $100/month) to people who clicked the Yelp ad but didn't book

The result: Yelp Ads became the top-of-funnel acquisition channel, but the actual conversion happened in the email sequence. Her cost per new paying member dropped from $140 (Yelp-only) to $48 (Yelp + email + retargeting). She kept the Yelp Ads spend at $700/month but started attributing 18–22 new members per month to the combined system.

**The uncomfortable truth:** Yelp Ads alone rarely work for small businesses unless you have high margins ($100+ ticket items) or your profile is in the top 1% of optimization. For coffee shops and low-ticket services ($30–60 average transaction), Yelp Ads usually lose money if you're measuring them in isolation. They work as part of a larger system where the Yelp click is just the start of the conversation.

## Yelp Ads for Multi-Location Businesses: When to Scale and When to Cut

If you have multiple locations, Yelp Ads can either be your most efficient channel or a budget black hole. I've managed both outcomes.

A pet supply store chain with locations in Austin, San Antonio, and Houston reached out after their Yelp Ads costs had doubled in six months with no increase in store traffic. They were spending $2,000/month across 8 locations.

**What we found:** Their Houston location was getting 80% of the ad spend but only 15% of the in-store redemptions of Yelp check-in offers. The Austin location had a 4.8-star rating and was getting 40% of the check-ins on 20% of the budget. The San Antonio location was a complete dud — 2.8 stars, mostly from reviews complaining about parking.

**The fix:** We pulled Yelp Ads from San Antonio entirely. Doubled the budget for the Austin location (from $250 to $500/month). Kept Houston at $100/month but added a specific offer — "Show this ad for 15% off any bag of premium dog food" — to track whether people were actually showing the ad at checkout.

**The outcome:** Total ad spend dropped from $2,000 to $800/month. Revenue attributed to Yelp Ads actually increased by 12% because we eliminated the bad locations and invested more in the one that worked. The Austin location was driving $4,200/month in identifiable revenue from Yelp Ads. The Houston location was a question mark — the 15% offer was only used twice in a month, so we cut it entirely.

**Rule of thumb I use:** For multi-location businesses, never allocate budget equally across locations. Test each location independently for 90 days. Cut any location that doesn't generate 3x ROI on ad spend within that window. Yelp will tell you not to do this because they want your total budget as high as possible. Ignore them.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I actually budget for Yelp Ads as a new local business?**

Start at $300–$500/month for a single location. Any less than $200/month and Yelp's algorithm won't show your ads frequently enough to get meaningful data. Any more than $1,000/month on your first run, and you're risking burning money before you understand what converts. Run the $300 test for 60 days. If you can't get at least three booked appointments or tracked visits per week at that budget, your profile needs work or Yelp isn't the right platform for your business.

**Q: Can I pause Yelp Ads anytime without penalties?**

Yes. You can pause or stop Yelp Ads at any point from your dashboard. There's no long-term contract for the ad platform itself (though some Yelp sales reps push annual contracts — decline those). Your profile stays up regardless. The only thing that changes: you lose the ad placement at the top of search results. Your organic ranking remains as it was (for better or worse). I've paused campaigns for clients and seen their organic traffic stay stable for months.

**Q: Do Yelp Ads help if my business has bad reviews (under 3.5 stars)?**

No. Do not run Yelp Ads with a rating below 3.5 stars. You will spend money sending people to a profile that immediately turns them off. I have never seen a sub-3.5-star business get positive ROI from Yelp Ads. Fix your reviews first — respond to every negative review honestly, see if you can get legitimate recent customers to leave positive reviews without asking them directly (through check-in offers and QR codes at the register that link to your Yelp page, not through asking). Once you're above 3.8 stars, ads can work.

**Q: What's the difference between Yelp Ads and paying for Yelp's "Business Owner" subscription?**

They're two separate products. The Business Owner subscription (around $299–$399/month, negotiable) gives you tools to manage your profile, see analytics, and customize your page. It does NOT include ad placement. Yelp Ads is a separate cost on top of that. Some Yelp sales teams will tell you that you need the subscription to run ads effectively — that's not true. I've managed successful campaigns without the subscription. The analytics in the subscription are nice but not essential for small budgets. You can track conversions through your own point-of-sale system.

**Q: How do I track whether Yelp Ads actually drove someone to my business?**

Yelp's dashboard will show you "click-to-call" and "click-to-map" metrics, but those are unreliable. The only reliable tracking methods I've found are: (1) Unique phone numbers — Yelp offers a call tracking number that forwards to your real number. (2) Unique landing page URLs — use a different URL in your Yelp Ad compared to your other marketing (e.g., yoursite.com/yelp). (3) In-store codes — ask customers "How did you hear about us?" and log it in your POS. I prefer option 3 because it catches people who saw the ad but didn't click it (they typed your business name directly into Google after seeing the Yelp ad).

**Q: Is Yelp Ads worth it for a business with a low average ticket (under $30)?**

Probably not as a standalone channel. If your average transaction is $15 (coffee shop, ice cream, bakery), you need about 33 new customers per month just to break even on a $500/month ad spend — and that assumes every single new customer attributed to the ad actually spends money, which they won't. For low-ticket businesses, Yelp Ads work best as part of a retention strategy: use the ad to bring in first-time visitors, then capture their email (through a loyalty program or WiFi login page) and market to them for repeat visits. Don't expect the ads themselves to be profitable.
