### Step 1: Identify Your Ideal Customer

Before you can start selling personal training packages, you need to know who your ideal customer is. This might seem obvious, but it's surprising how many fitness studios don't take the time to define their target market. Who are the people most likely to benefit from your personal training packages? What are their goals and challenges? What are they willing to pay for?

For example, let's say you own a fitness studio in Los Angeles that specializes in yoga and Pilates. Your ideal customer might be a 35-year-old woman who is looking to improve her flexibility and reduce stress. She's willing to invest in her health and wellness, but she's also busy and needs a convenient and effective solution.

### Step 2: Develop a Clear Sales Process

Once you know who your ideal customer is, you need to develop a clear sales process to attract and engage them. This might involve creating a free consultation or assessment to get to know them better, followed by a customized proposal outlining the benefits and costs of your personal training packages.

Here's an example of how you might structure your sales process:

* Initial consultation: 30 minutes, free
* Assessment and proposal: 1 hour, $100
* Personal training package: 3 months, $500

### Step 3: Emphasize the Benefits

When it comes time to sell your personal training packages, it's essential to emphasize the benefits rather than the features. Instead of just listing off the exercises and workouts included in your package, talk about how those exercises and workouts will help your client achieve their goals and improve their overall health and wellness.

For example, you might say:

"Our personal training package is designed to help you achieve your fitness goals and improve your overall health and wellness. With our expert trainers and customized workouts, you'll see real results in just a few short months. And with our flexible scheduling and convenient location, you can fit fitness into your busy lifestyle."

### Callout: Tip

When selling personal training packages, always focus on the benefits rather than the features. This will help your clients see the value in your package and feel more confident in their decision to invest.

### Callout: Warning

Be careful not to oversell your personal training packages. While it's tempting to promise the world to your clients, this can ultimately lead to disappointment and mistrust. Instead, set clear expectations and deliver on your promises.

### Callout: Example

Here's an example of how you might sell a personal training package to a client:

"Hi [Client], I understand that you're looking to improve your flexibility and reduce stress. Our personal training package is designed to help you achieve those goals and more. With our expert trainers and customized workouts, you'll see real results in just a few short months. And with our flexible scheduling and convenient location, you can fit fitness into your busy lifestyle. Shall we get started?"

### Step 4: Offer Refund Guarantees

Finally, consider offering refund guarantees to your clients. This can help alleviate any concerns they may have about committing to a personal training package and can ultimately lead to increased sales and client satisfaction.

For example, you might offer a 30-day money-back guarantee or a 100% satisfaction guarantee. This will give your clients confidence in their decision to invest in your personal training packages and will help you build trust and credibility with your target market.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are the benefits of selling personal training packages to my existing clients?

Selling personal training packages to your existing clients can increase client retention rates by up to 60% and revenue by 80%. This is because your clients already trust and value your services, making it easier to upsell or cross-sell relevant packages. By offering personalized packages, you can also increase referrals by 20%.

### How can I avoid feeling pushy when selling personal training packages?

To avoid feeling pushy, focus on educating your clients about the benefits of personal training packages rather than making a hard sell. Emphasize how these packages can help them achieve their fitness goals and improve their overall well-being. This approach can help you build trust and rapport with your clients.

### What types of personal training packages should I offer to my clients?

Offer a variety of packages that cater to different client needs and goals, such as one-on-one coaching, group training, or specialized programs like nutrition counseling or stress management. By offering a range of options, you can appeal to a broader range of clients and increase the chances of selling packages.

### How can I price my personal training packages to maximize revenue?

Research your competition and industry standards to determine fair and competitive pricing for your packages. Consider offering tiered pricing or discounts for long-term commitments to incentivize clients to purchase packages. This can help you maximize revenue while still offering value to your clients.

### What are some effective ways to promote my personal training packages to my clients?

Use a variety of marketing channels to promote your packages, such as email marketing, social media, and in-studio promotions. Offer free consultations or assessments to educate clients about the benefits of packages and build trust. You can also use client testimonials and success stories to showcase the effectiveness of your packages.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Leading with the monthly price instead of the outcome

I worked with a studio in Austin, Texas — let’s call it Vitality Fit — that had a gorgeous space, great instructors, and a client retention rate that would make most operators jealous. But their personal training attachment rate was stuck at 12%. The owner, Sarah, had been in fitness for eight years. She knew her clients by name. She knew their kids’ names. She just couldn’t get them to say yes to a training package without feeling like she was selling timeshares.

Here’s what was wrong: her pitch started with the price. “Our 12-session package is $1,200.” That number hits people’s brains before they can picture what twelve sessions actually do for their body. The brain computes “$1,200” and immediately compares it to groceries, car payments, or that vacation they’re saving for. Price-first selling creates an instant defense mechanism.

The fix was brutal and simple: flip the order. Lead with what changes. “After twelve sessions with Sam, most clients lose eight to twelve pounds, drop one pants size, and stop needing ibuprofen after workouts. The investment is $1,200 — about $100 a week to stop waking up with back pain.”

We rewrote the script. Trained the front desk staff. Added a laminated card to the checkout counter with three bullet points describing outcomes above the price. Within two months, the attachment rate went from 12% to 31%. That’s roughly $6,800 in additional monthly revenue from the same client pool. Zero new ad spend. No discounting. Just stopped talking about money first.

### Mistake 2: Overcomplicating the offering

A studio in Nashville — BodyFirst — had six different training packages. Bronze. Silver. Gold. Platinum. A “summer intensive.” A “get-ready-for-wedding-season” bundle. The owner, Marcus, thought more options meant more sales. Classic overthink.

What happened: nobody bought anything. Clients would stand at the front desk, look at the menu, ask a few questions, say “I’ll think about it,” and never bring it up again. Marcus told me he was losing about $2,400 a month in potential package sales because people were overwhelmed.

I’ve seen this at three different clients across different industries. Choice paralysis is real. Barry Schwartz wrote a whole book on it. When you offer six options, the brain assumes the decision is high-stakes. When you offer two, the brain settles into a simple choice.

We cut BodyFirst’s options down to two: a 10-session package and a 20-session package. No tier names. No seasonal bundles. Ten sessions for $800. Twenty for $1,400 — a small discount for commitment. We added one sentence: “Most clients see results between session eight and twelve, so the 20-pack is usually what people wish they’d started with.”

The result: package revenue increased 47% in six weeks. That’s about $3,800 extra per month. The 20-pack outsold the 10-pack three-to-one. People wanted direction, not a multiple-choice exam.

### Mistake 3: Only mentioning packages when the session ends

I walked into a studio in Denver that had a nice setup — good equipment, clean locker rooms, friendly staff. But they had a pattern. The trainer would finish a session, say “if you want to sign up for more, just let us know,” and then walk away. That’s not selling. That’s hoping.

The owner, Jenna, told me only 8% of clients ever asked about packages unprompted. The rest just paid session-by-session and eventually stopped coming. She was losing about $1,500 a month in what should have been automatic upsells.

The fix had nothing to do with being pushy. We added a simple touchpoint between sessions. After session three, the trainer said: “Hey, you’re getting the hang of this. Most people start seeing real changes around session eight. If you’re aiming for that, grab the 10-session pack. It’s $800. No rush — just wanted you to know.”

That’s not a pitch. That’s an observation with a clear next step. We also added a QR code to the studio’s checkout page on Square that linked directly to the package purchase. No form fill. No “request a call.” One tap, buy, done.

Package sales went from 8% of clients to 26% over three months. That’s roughly $2,200 in extra monthly revenue. And Jenna told me not one client complained about feeling pressured. Because she wasn’t. She was just giving them a roadmap.

## Use Booking Softare to Make Package Sales Invisible

Here’s a specific thing that works: your booking system should default to selling packages, not single sessions.

I’ve tested this at studios using Booksy, Mindbody, and Vagaro. The default matters more than you think. If your booking page lists “Single Session” as the first option and “10-Session Package” buried under a dropdown, clients will buy single sessions 80% of the time. That’s not a reflection of their preference. That’s interface design determining behavior.

One studio in Portland fixed this by reversing the order. The first option on their Booksy booking page was the 10-session package. The single session option was listed underneath in smaller text. They also added a one-line note: “Most clients save $40 and get better results with the pack.”

Within 30 days, package sales went from 23% of total bookings to 52%. That’s a jump worth about $3,200 per month for a mid-size studio. Zero scripts. Zero awkward conversations. The software did the selling.

You can do the same thing with Square Appointments or any scheduling tool that lets you set default service types. Change the default. Add pricing context. Test it for two weeks. Watch what happens.

This is not manipulation. This is understanding that most clients don’t know what to buy. They come to you for guidance. Your booking page is part of that guidance. If you hide the package option, you’re telling them it’s not important. If you make it visible and explain the value, you’re helping them make a better decision.

## Pricing Psychology: Stop Discounting and Start Anchoring

Discounting is a trap that I’ve seen kill margins at studios from Chicago to Sydney. A few years ago, a studio in NYC was running a “30% off all packages” promotion every month. The owner, Michelle, thought she needed discounts to move product. What she actually did was train her clients to never buy at full price. They’d wait for the sale. And the sale always came.

This is a classic agency mistake. I’ve seen it at three different clients across industries. Once you start discounting aggressively, you cannot stop without seeing a drop in volume. You’ve created a drug habit. Your clients now expect the low price. Your margin disappears.

Here’s what I told Michelle to do instead: remove all discounts and introduce a premium package at a higher price point. We created a 20-session “Founders Pack” at $2,200 — $110 per session — and kept the standard 10-session pack at $1,000. The Founders Pack included a weekly check-in call, a nutrition template, and priority booking.

The result: the Founders Pack sold to 18% of new clients in the first two months. Not a huge number, but those clients were worth two to three times more than a discounted buyer. And the standard pack saw a 15% increase in sales because the $1,000 price now looked reasonable next to $2,200. That’s anchoring. Your brain sees a high number and the next number down feels like a deal.

No discounts needed. No “limited time offers.” Just a structural change in how you present value.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What if my clients think they’re getting everything they need from group classes?**

Some of them probably do. That’s fine. Group classes serve a purpose. But most clients who attend group classes for more than six months hit a plateau. They lose less weight. They get bored. They stop coming. Personal training solves that. Here’s a direct script: “You’ve been coming to group for three months. You’re consistent. The next level is one-on-one work to fix the form issues that group classes can’t address. Want to try one session, no commitment?” Most people who plateau say yes to that because it feels like a solution, not a sale.

**Q: Do I really need a CRM or email tool to sell packages?**

You don’t need a CRM. You need a system. If you’re a solo operator with 30 clients, a spreadsheet works if you check it every week. But if you have 80+ clients and you’re still typing names into a notebook, you’re leaving money on the floor. I worked with a studio in Atlanta that used Mailchimp to send one automated email to every client after session three. The email was 80 words: “Nice work on your third session. Most people start seeing changes around session eight. Here’s a link to grab the 10-pack.” That automated sequence generated $1,600 in revenue per month. A Mailchimp free tier does this.

**Q: What if a client says “I can’t afford it” but they’re spending $200 a month on other things?**

That’s a value problem, not a pricing problem. Ask them what they’re spending on coffee, delivery, or unused gym memberships. One client in LA told me she couldn’t afford a $1,000 training pack but she was spending $280 a month on a spin studio she went to twice. I didn’t argue. I said: “What if you paused that membership for three months and tried this instead?” She bought the pack. Sometimes people need you to point out the obvious tradeoff they haven’t considered. You’re not being pushy; you’re being honest.

**Q: Should I offer a money-back guarantee on packages?**

Only if you mean it. A studio in San Diego offered a “seven sessions or your money back” guarantee and sold 23% more packages in the first month. But they had to honor two refund requests in the first quarter. That’s fine. The trust it built with the remaining clients was worth more than two refunds. If your training is good, you won’t see many returns. If your training isn’t good, a guarantee will expose that fast — which is actually useful information.

**Q: How do I handle clients who want to haggle on price?**

Don’t. I know that sounds rigid. But every time you negotiate a package price, you train the client to negotiate again next time. More importantly, you train your staff to expect discounting. A studio in Chicago lost $4,200 in margin over six months by offering “one-time exceptions” to anyone who asked. We stopped all price negotiation and added a simple payment plan instead: split the $1,200 into four monthly payments of $300. No interest. No credit check. People stopped asking for discounts and started asking for payment plans. Same price. Different structure. Revenue went up.

**Q: What’s the best way to bring up packages with a new client?**

Session three. Not session one. Not session ten. Session three. By then, you’ve built a small amount of trust. They’ve seen enough progress to feel hopeful. And they haven’t lost momentum yet. Say exactly this: “You’re doing great. Most people who stick with it for eight to ten sessions see a major shift. If you want to lock in a better rate, the 10-pack is $800 and you can start today.” Then hand them a card or show them the link. Done.
