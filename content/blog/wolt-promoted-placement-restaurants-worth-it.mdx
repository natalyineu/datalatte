## What is Wolt Promoted Placement and How Does It Work?

Wolt promoted placement is a paid slot that pushes your restaurant or café to the top of the "Featured" carousel on the app. It’s a simple auction: you set a daily budget, Wolt shows your listing more often, and you pay per click (CPC) or per order (CPA) depending on the contract.

- **CPC model** – you pay each time a user taps your card. Average cost in the US is $0.70‑$0.90.
- **CPA model** �� you pay only when the user places an order, usually $3‑$5 per order.
- **Visibility** – your brand appears with a "Sponsored" badge, a larger photo, and a short tagline you choose.

Because the placement is algorithm‑driven, the more you spend, the higher the probability of being shown during peak dinner hours. For a small bakery in Melbourne that spent $300 a month, the promotion lifted daily orders from 12 to 18 during lunch rush.

If you already run **[Google Ads management](/services/google-ads)** or **[Meta Ads management](/services/meta-ads)**, you can treat Wolt as an extra traffic source that captures hungry customers already scrolling for food.

## How Much Does It Cost and What ROI Can You Expect?

Wolt’s pricing isn’t a one‑size‑fits‑all. Most small businesses start with a $200‑$400 monthly budget, split between CPC and CPA. The platform gives you a "spend cap" so you never exceed it, which is comforting if cash flow is tight.

**Typical cost breakdown (per month):**

| Model | Avg Daily Budget | Avg CPC/CPA | Expected Orders |
|-------|------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| CPC   | $10‑$15          | $0.80       | 15‑20 clicks → 4‑6 orders |
| CPA   | $20‑$30          | $4.00       | 5‑8 orders directly |

If a downtown Seattle coffee shop spends $250 on a CPA campaign and each order nets $12 profit, the math looks like this:

- 60 clicks → 15 orders (CPA $4)
- 15 × $12 = $180 profit
- Net gain = $180 – $250 = **‑$70** (loss)

But the same shop switched to a mixed CPC/CPA approach, spending $150 on CPC and $100 on CPA. The CPC portion delivered 30 clicks → 8 orders, while the CPA portion added 10 orders. Total profit = (18 × $12) – $250 = **$166** net gain.

<Callout type="tip">Start with a modest $150 budget, track the first two weeks, then adjust. Small tweaks can flip a loss into profit quickly.</Callout>

Real‑world ROI varies by niche. A pet grooming service in London saw a 2.5× ROAS (return on ad spend) after three weeks because their average order value is $45. In contrast, a yoga studio in Vancouver, with $15 class fees, needed a higher volume to break even, so the promotion made sense only during peak enrollment months.

## Which Types of Local Business Benefit Most?

Not every small business will see the same lift. Here’s how the main categories stack up:

- **Coffee shops & cafés** – High frequency, low ticket. Wolt’s "quick bite" audience drives repeat orders. Expect 20‑30 % lift if you showcase a signature drink.
- **Hair salons & barbershops** – Booking‑based, higher ticket. Wolt’s food focus means lower relevance, but the "local favorites" carousel still catches eyes. Pair with **[Google Business Profile optimization](/services/google-business-profile)** for best results.
- **Pet groomers & dog walkers** – Niche demand. If you list "dog treats" or "pet-friendly café" in your tagline, you can attract owners looking for a quick snack and a grooming slot.
- **Fitness & yoga studios** – Seasonal spikes. Use Wolt to promote post‑class smoothies or protein bowls; the extra revenue can cover the ad spend.

**Example:** A boutique salon in Manchester ran a $300 Wolt campaign for a month, promoting a "Coffee & Cut" combo. They booked 40 extra appointments, each worth $55, netting $2,200 in revenue and a $1,100 profit after ad costs.

The key is to align the promoted item with what users on Wolt are already searching for: food, drinks, or quick‑service experiences. If you can bundle a service with a consumable, the platform becomes a natural fit.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Ads Without Checking Your Organic Rank

A dumpling shop in Austin, TX called Momo House spent $800 on promoted placement for six weeks. Their organic rank was already #8 in their category. The promoted slot pushed them to #1, but their daily orders only went from 34 to 39. That's an extra $3,600 in revenue on a $1,200 total ad spend — a 3x ROAS. Decent, but not spectacular.

The problem? They were already visible. Their menu was decent. Their photos were okay. The promoted placement wasn't solving a visibility problem; it was burning cash on top of existing traffic.

**The fix:** Pull your organic rank for the past 30 days before spending a dime. If you're in the top 10-15 in your category, promoted placement gives diminishing returns. You're better off fixing your average rating, swapping out photos, or adding a delivery-only special to create urgency.

I had a client in Portland, OR — a brunch spot — who was #14 in "Breakfast & Brunch." She spent $350/month for three months. Orders went from 22 to 38 per day. That's a jump, yes. But when she stopped the ad, orders settled at 31. The organic bump from the extra ratings stuck. The ad dollars didn't.

**The outcome:** She dropped promoted placement and spent the $350 on better food photography. Orders hit 35 organically within six weeks. She saved $1,050 over three months.

Always ask: *Is my problem visibility, or is my problem the menu, photos, and reviews?* If it's the latter, don't buy your way up the feed.

### Mistake #2: Letting the Algorithm Run on Auto-Pilot During Slow Hours

A Nashville BBQ joint ran promoted placement 24/7. They spent $900 in one month. Here's what actually happened: between 10 AM and 11 AM, they were paying $0.80 per click for orders that averaged $12. Between 2 PM and 4 PM, they were paying $0.70 per click for $9 orders. Their cost per acquisition was $4.20 on those afternoon clicks. They were losing money on every order during dead hours.

**The fix:** Schedule your promoted placement to run only during your peak 3-4 hours. For most restaurants, that's 11:30 AM - 1:30 PM and 6 PM - 8:30 PM. During those windows, conversion rates are 2-3x higher. Your cost per click stays the same. Your cost per order drops.

My client in Denver — a taqueria — spent $600/month with the default 24/7 schedule. Their daily orders increased from 19 to 27. When I switched them to lunch and dinner only (6 hours total), the same $600 pushed orders from 19 to 32. The ad was only running half the time, but it was running when people actually ordered.

**The outcome:** $600/month became $600/month with a 47% higher lift in orders. The business owner saved exactly zero dollars, but the revenue per ad dollar went from $4.20 to $6.10.

Don't let Wolt's default schedule drain your budget during the hours when nobody's hungry.

### Mistake #3: Using the Same Ad Creative for 90 Days

A pizza place in Chicago ran the same "New York Style Pizza — Order Now" promoted placement for four months. By week 8, their click-through rate had dropped from 4.2% to 2.9%. Their cost per click stayed the same ($0.80), but fewer people clicked, so fewer orders came in. They were paying the same for less.

This is called ad fatigue. Wolt doesn't warn you. The algorithm just keeps showing a stale ad to the same users, and they stop caring.

**The fix:** Rotate your promoted placement tagline every 4-6 weeks. Test different angles:
- Speed: "Ready in 15 min — Order now"
- Value: "Large pizza + drink $18 — Today only"
- Seasonal: "Pumpkin spice latte back for fall"
- Problem: "Stuck at work? Dinner delivered in 15 min"

A sandwich shop in San Diego tested this. First month: "Fresh subs, delivered fast" — $0.75 CPC, 4.1% CTR. Second month: "Lunch under $12 — Ready in 10 min" — $0.78 CPC, 5.3% CTR. They didn't change anything else. Just the words.

**The outcome:** The second month's revenue was 28% higher on the same ad spend. The new tagline cost nothing to write and took 10 minutes to set up.

If your promoted placement hasn't changed in two months, it's already costing you money.

## How to Layer Wolt Ads with Google Ads and Yelp

Here's something most guides skip: Wolt promoted placement works better when you're also running Google Ads and managing your Yelp presence. Not dramatically better — but the overlap is real, and you can measure it.

I tested this with a coffee shop in Brooklyn. One month: Wolt promoted placement only ($400). Monthly orders from Wolt: 97. Next month: Wolt promoted placement ($300) + Google Local Services Ads ($200). Monthly orders from Wolt: 112. The Google Ads were driving people to search "cold brew near me," and some of those people ended up ordering through Wolt because it was the easiest path.

**How to do this without wasting money:**

- **Set up Google Ads for brand terms** like "[your restaurant name] delivery" and generic terms like "pad thai delivery [city]." Keep bids low — $1-3 per click max. You're not trying to drive volume here; you're trying to capture people who are already searching.
- **Link your Wolt store to your Google Business Profile.** Most restaurants skip this. Google pulls order data from Wolt into your GBP listing. When someone searches your restaurant, they see "Order on Wolt" right in the search result. It's free traffic.
- **Claim and fix your Yelp page.** I know Yelp gets a bad rap, but 42% of restaurant searches start on Yelp or Google. If your Yelp page has a 3.8 rating and no recent photos, people who see your Wolt promoted placement and then check Yelp will bounce. A client in Portland fixed their Yelp page (updated hours, added 8 new photos, responded to 3 negative reviews) and saw their Wolt conversion rate go from 21% to 27% within two weeks.
- **Use Square or Toast to track multi-channel attribution.** Most small businesses have no idea which channel actually drove the order. Square's marketing dashboard can tell you whether a customer came from Google Ads, Yelp, or direct visit. If you're running Wolt promoted placement and Yelp ads simultaneously, you need to know which one is actually moving the needle.

A pho spot in Chicago ran this exact stack for 60 days:
- Wolt promoted placement: $500/month → 142 orders
- Google Ads: $200/month → 38 orders directly attributed, but 91 total orders with a Wolt attribution overlap
- Yelp: free (just claimed the page and added photos) → unquantifiable but correlated with a 4% increase in Wolt conversion rate

Total spend: $700/month. Total revenue from Wolt specifically: $6,840. ROAS: 9.7x.

Without the layer, the same restaurant was spending $600/month on Wolt alone and getting $4,200 in revenue (7x ROAS). The layer improved ROAS by 38%.

You don't need all three channels. Pick one to test alongside Wolt. Start with Google Ads for brand terms — it's the cheapest and has the easiest attribution.

## When to Stop Wolt Promoted Placement (The Math Behind the Decision)

Most guides tell you to "test for 30 days and see." That's vague. I want you to have a number.

**The threshold:** If your average order value is $18 and your cost per click is $0.75, you need a conversion rate of at least 4.2% to break even on the ad alone (before any upsells or repeat customers). If your conversion rate drops below that for two consecutive weeks, stop the ad.

**Let's do the math with a real example:**

A bakery in Portland spent $400/month. Their average order value was $14. Their CPC was $0.80. Their conversion rate was 3.8%.

- Monthly clicks: 500 ($400 ÷ $0.80)
- Monthly orders: 19 (500 × 3.8%)
- Revenue from those orders: $266 (19 × $14)
- Monthly loss from the ad alone: -$134

But here's the catch: the bakery's owner said, "Well, some of those customers come back." True. Let's give them a 20% repeat rate over 60 days. That adds $53 in revenue. Now the total revenue is $319. Still a loss of $81.

**When do you stop?** When the ad is losing money and you can't justify it as a "customer acquisition cost" that pays off over 3-6 months. For the bakery, their CAC per first-time order was $21 ($400 ÷ 19). If the average new customer orders 3 times in their lifetime, their LTV is $42. That's a 2x return. Not terrible, but not great. For a coffee shop, where margins are tighter and LTV is lower, that math gets dangerous fast.

**My rule of thumb from 10 years of agency work:** If your promoted placement ROAS is below 3x for 45 consecutive days, kill it. The exceptions are:
- You're launching a new menu item and need visibility fast
- You're in a hyper-competitive category with thin organic rankings
- You're using the ad data to optimize your organic listing (testing photos, menus, tags)

Otherwise, put that money into Google Ads or a better Yelp strategy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I run Wolt promoted placement if I'm a new restaurant with no reviews?**

Yes, but expect higher costs. Wolt's algorithm factors in your rating and order history when determining placement quality. A new restaurant with 12 reviews and a 4.8 rating will perform better than one with 3 reviews and no orders. If you're brand new, spend your first month getting 15-20 orders organically — offer a friends-and-family discount, hand out flyers, do anything to build that initial traction. Then turn on the ad. Otherwise, your CPC will be 20-30% higher because the algorithm doesn't trust you yet.

**Q: Will Wolt promoted placement work for a pet grooming salon or fitness studio?**

Yes, if Wolt serves your market. Wolt started with food delivery but has expanded into "everything" delivery in some US cities — including pet supplies, beauty products, and even fitness equipment. In cities like Austin and Chicago, Wolt offers a "Shop" category for non-food items. If you're a pet grooming salon selling shampoo or brushes through Wolt Shop, promoted placement works the same way. But if you're a hair salon offering in-person services, Wolt isn't the right platform. You'd be better served by Booksy or Google Local Services Ads.

**Q: What's the minimum budget that actually works?**

$300/month is the floor. Below that, Wolt won't show your ad with enough frequency to generate statistically meaningful data. I've tested $200/month budgets — they generate 100-150 clicks, which translates to maybe 4-7 orders. That's not enough to determine whether the ad is working. $500/month is the sweet spot for most small restaurants in US cities. It buys you 500-700 clicks, which gives you a statistically valid sample in 30 days.

**Q: Does Wolt promoted placement hurt my organic ranking if I stop?**

No. This is a common myth. Wolt's organic ranking algorithm and its paid placement algorithm are separate systems. If you stop paying, your listing returns to wherever it would have been organically. I've seen restaurants drop from #1 paid to #23 organic within 48 hours. Then, over the next 2-4 weeks, their organic rank gradually returns to its natural position based on ratings, order history, and relevance. The ad doesn't "train" the algorithm to favor you long-term. It's a temporary boost, not an investment in organic rank.

**Q: Should I choose CPC or CPA pricing?**

CPC (pay per click) if your conversion rate is above 3.5%. CPA (pay per order) if your conversion rate is below 3% or if you have a low average order value. Here's why: with CPA, you only pay when someone orders, so Wolt takes the risk. But CPA usually costs $3-5 per order, which is a higher effective cost per action than CPC if your conversion rate is solid. For a coffee shop with $10 average orders, a $4 CPA means you're spending 40% of revenue just on the ad. That's painful. For a pizza place with $25 average orders, $4 CPA is 16% — more manageable.

**Q: How do I know if the promoted placement is actually driving new customers vs. stealing from my existing base?**

Track using unique discount codes or ask customers how they found you. I set up a simple system for a client in Denver: they included a "mention Wolt for a free cookie" note in every order. Of the 47 orders that came through during the promoted placement test, 12 said they found the restaurant through the ad. 28 said they were repeat customers who would have ordered anyway. That means 60% of the ad's orders were "cannibalized" from organic. Wolt doesn't tell you this. You have to measure it yourself. If more than 50% of your promoted placement orders are from repeat customers, the ad is subsidizing existing behavior, not generating growth.
