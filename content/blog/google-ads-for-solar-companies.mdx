## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting Too Broadly Because "More Clicks = More Leads"

A solar company in Austin, Texas came to me after burning $2,500 in two weeks on Google Ads. They had zero signed contracts. Zero. The owner, Marcus, was ready to swear off paid ads forever.

When I looked at his account, here's what I found: He had targeted the entire state of Texas. He was showing his ads to homeowners in El Paso, Amarillo, and Beaumont — places his installation team couldn't even reach. His budget was spread so thin that his daily spend in Austin proper was maybe $12. Meanwhile, his ads were triggering for people searching "solar panel DIY kit" and "how solar panels work diagram" because he hadn't added a single negative keyword.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the campaign to target only five specific zip codes within a 20-mile radius of his shop. We added over 200 negative keywords (anything containing "DIY," "how to," "kit," "science project," "school," "rental," "costco"). We set a hard location radius and excluded areas where his team didn't install.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, his cost per lead dropped from $87 to $34. His conversion rate on those leads went from 0% to 22%. He signed two contracts worth $18,400 total in the first month after the fix. His ad spend those three weeks was $1,100.

### Mistake 2: Using "Solar Panels" as Your Only Keyword

A Denver-based solar company was spending $4,200 a month on Google Ads and getting an average of 40 click-throughs per day. Sounds good until I saw their search terms report. Their single keyword — "solar panels" — was generating clicks from people looking to *buy* solar panels for a boondocking RV setup, a shed, or a tiny house. These weren't homeowners with a roof that needed 28 panels and a permit.

The owner, Sarah, had been told by a previous agency that "broad match keywords will capture all the traffic." They did. But 76% of the clicks were from people who had no intention of installing a residential system. She had wasted about $3,200 over the previous month on clicks from people who could never become customers.

**The fix:** We replaced the broad match keywords with phrase match and exact match variations. "Solar panel installation Denver," "residential solar panels [zip code]," "home solar system quote," and the kicker — "solar panel financing Colorado." We also added a sitelink extension that went directly to a "Check Your Roof's Compatibility" page, which filtered out the looky-loos before they cost her a click.

**The outcome:** Total impressions dropped by 60%, but click-through rate to her contact form went up 340%. Monthly ad spend dropped to $2,600 because we weren't burning money on irrelevant traffic. Cost per qualified lead went from $104 to $38. She closed $52,000 in contracts over the next 90 days.

### Mistake 3: Not Using Call Tracking (And Wasting $1,800/Month)

The other 11 were organic listings, Yelp, or past customers calling a different line that happened to forward to the same phone. James had been attributing $1,800 per month in ad spend to calls that had nothing to do with his ads.

More importantly, the 4 ad-generated calls were from homeowners who had done their research and were ready to schedule a consultation. But James's receptionist was routing those calls to voicemail because she was "busy with walk-ins." Four warm leads, every week, hitting voicemail.

**The fix:** We installed call tracking with call recording on every ad extension. We created a separate phone number for the ad campaign that forwarded directly to James's cell phone during business hours. We wrote a 30-second call script that his receptionist could use to qualify leads before transferring.

**The outcome:** Response time to ad-generated calls dropped from 4 hours (when they called back from voicemail) to 30 seconds. His close rate on ad leads went from 8% to 31%. Monthly ad spend stayed at $1,500, but monthly revenue from ad leads went from $3,600 to $11,400. The call tracking tool paid for itself in the first day.

## What to Track After the Click (That Most Agencies Ignore)

Most guides stop at "set up your campaign and track conversions." Here's what they skip, and why it matters for your bank account.

Google Ads will happily report that you got 50 clicks and 3 conversions for $500. That sounds good. But here's the real question: what happened to those 3 "conversions"?

I worked with a company in Chicago that was celebrating 40 conversions in a month from their Google Ads campaign. It took me about 15 minutes of digging to realize that 35 of those "conversions" were form submissions on a "download our solar savings guide" page. Those people had no interest in buying panels. They wanted a PDF. The agency running the account had set up a conversion tracker on *any* form submission, so the owners were looking at a dashboard that said "40 leads this month" when in reality they had 5 real leads and 35 PDF requests.

**Set up conversion tracking that actually tracks real leads.** If you're using Google Ads directly, create separate conversion actions for:
- Phone calls that last at least 60 seconds (ignore the hang-ups)
- Form submissions on the "get a quote" page (not the blog subscribe form)
- WhatsApp or text message inquiries (yes, people text now)
- "Book a consultation" calendar appointments

Use a tool like CallRail or WhatConverts ($30-$50/month) to track which keywords and ads are driving actual conversations, not just button clicks. I've seen solar companies in Phoenix realize that the keyword "solar panel maintenance" was costing them $45 per click but never led to a sale, while "solar panel installation Phoenix cost" was $12 per click and closed at 17%.

**Track the full journey, not the first click.** Most solar installations take 2-4 weeks from initial inquiry to contract signing. A homeowner might click your ad on Monday, visit your site, leave, come back via an organic search on Wednesday, call you on Friday, and sign a contract the following Tuesday. If you look only at first-click attribution, your Google Ads campaign looks like it did all the work. That's fine if you're reporting to a boss. It's dangerous if you're spending your own money, because you'll over-invest in search ads and under-invest in retargeting, direct mail, or referrals.

**Track cost per booked appointment, not cost per lead.** A $35 lead that schedules a consultation is worth more than a $20 lead that fills out a form and never picks up the phone. If you're using a scheduling tool like Booksy or Calendly, integrate it with your Google Ads conversion tracking. Measure the cost to get someone to block 30 minutes on their calendar to talk to you. That number — cost per booked appointment — is the only number that directly predicts revenue.

## Should You Run Google Local Service Ads or Search Ads First?

This is the question I get most from solar companies, and the answer depends on your city and your budget.

**Local Service Ads (LSAs)** are the ads that appear at the very top of Google search results with a green checkmark that says "Google Guaranteed." You pay per lead, not per click. You can set a weekly budget, and Google sends you leads via phone call or text message. If the lead is invalid (wrong number, spam, outside your service area), Google refunds you.

I had a client in Phoenix who spent $3,000 on LSAs in a month and got 22 leads. Of those, 3 were invalid and got refunded. Of the remaining 19, 6 turned into installations worth $38,000 total. The cost per lead was $136, but the cost per installation was $500 — a 76x return on ad spend.

**The catch:** LSAs require a Google Screened badge, which means you have to pass background checks, license verification, and insurance verification. If you're a legitimate solar company, this takes 2-4 weeks to set up. If you're not fully licensed and insured, you can't run LSAs. Period.

**Search Ads** give you more control. You choose the keywords, the ad copy, the landing page, the bidding strategy. You can run them immediately without waiting for background checks. You can target specific zip codes, exclude certain neighborhoods, and test multiple headlines and descriptions.

**My recommendation:** Start with Search Ads if you have less than $2,000/month budget and you want to test different messaging. Switch to LSAs if you have more budget, you've passed the verification process, and you want a simpler lead flow. The best-performing solar campaigns I've managed used both: LSAs to catch the highest-intent searchers, Search Ads to capture people who are still researching but may convert later.

One note: if you run both campaigns simultaneously, make sure you're not double-counting leads. Use unique phone numbers for each campaign. I've seen a company in Seattle pay for the same lead twice because their LSA call and their Search Ad call both routed to the same number.

## The Seasonality Question: When to Spend and When to Save

Solar installations follow a predictable seasonal pattern in most US markets. Understanding this can save you thousands of dollars in wasted ad spend and double your revenue during peak months.

**Spring (March-May):** This is the peak season. Homeowners are coming out of winter, starting to think about home improvement projects, and looking at their energy bills going up as they run AC. Interest in solar peaks in April. Increase your budget by 30-50% during these months. Expect higher cost per click (more competition), but higher conversion rates too.

**Summer (June-August):** Good season, but you'll face more competition from other solar companies. Your cost per click may rise by 15-25%. Focus on "avoid high summer bills" messaging. If you're in Arizona, Texas, or Florida, this is your second peak.

**Fall (September-November):** Interest drops as people focus on holidays and year-end expenses. Many solar companies cut their budgets here. That's a mistake. This is the time to run remarketing campaigns to people who visited your site during spring and summer but didn't convert. Your cost per click will be 20-30% lower because competitors have pulled back. I ran a campaign for a company in Denver that generated 12 leads in November at $28 per lead — the cheapest leads they got all year — because we were the only solar advertiser still running in their area.

**Winter (December-February):** The dead zone in most markets. Search volume for "solar panel installation" drops by 40-60% in snowy states. Don't stop ads entirely — maintain a small budget ($500-800/month) to capture the few people who are actively searching. These are often the most motivated buyers (they're researching before spring installation). But don't throw money at broad keywords. This is the time to pause campaigns that are losing money and test new ad copy for spring.

A company in New York followed this pattern: they spent $8,000 on ads from March to May, generated $96,000 in revenue. They spent $4,000 from June to August, generated $38,000. They spent $1,200 from September to November, generated $14,000. They spent $600 from December to February, generated $4,200. Total ad spend: $13,800. Total revenue: $152,200. The owner saved $4,200 by not running full-budget ads in winter and reinvested that savings into spring campaigns.

**The uncomfortable truth is** that most solar companies run the same ad budget year-round because someone told them "consistency is key." That's the kind of advice that sounds smart in a blog post and costs you money in real life. Match your spend to your market's season, not to a calendar.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I budget for Google Ads as a solar company?**

Start with $1,500-2,000 per month for a single metro area. Anything less than $1,000 and you won't get enough data to know what works. Anything more than $5,000 without proven conversion data and you're gambling. I've seen solar companies spend $500/month and get zero leads because their budget was too small to win any auctions. I've seen companies spend $8,000/month on the wrong keywords and burn $8,000. Start small, prove the model, then scale.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from Google Ads for solar?**

You'll see clicks and impressions within hours of launching. You'll see leads within 1-2 weeks if your targeting and landing page are correct. You'll see signed contracts within 3-4 weeks. If you're not getting leads after two weeks and $1,500 spent, something is broken — either your location targeting, your keyword list, or your landing page. Don't wait three months to figure this out.

**Q: Can competitors click my ads to drain my budget?**

It happens, but it's rare and Google has systems to catch it. Google's invalid click team monitors for patterns — multiple clicks from the same IP address, rapid clicking, clicks that don't convert. They automatically refund those clicks in most cases. The bigger risk isn't competitor clicking — it's your own ad showing to people who searched for "solar energy disadvantages" or "solar panels are a scam." Add those as negative keywords and the problem solves itself.

**Q: Do I need a professional website or can I use a landing page?**

You need either a fast-loading landing page or a website with a clear path to conversion. The key requirement: it needs to load in under three seconds on a phone. Google will penalize slow pages with lower ad rankings. You don't need a $10,000 custom website. A $400 landing page built on a platform like Unbounce or Leadpages that loads fast and has a clear call-to-action will outperform a $12,000 agency website that takes six seconds to load.

**Q: Should I use broad match keywords for solar?**

No. Broad match will show your ads for searches you don't want — "solar panel science project," "solar panels for chicken coop," "can I build my own solar panels." Use phrase match and exact match only for the first 90 days. Once you have at least 500 clicks and 20 conversions, you can experiment with broad match on a separate campaign with strict negative keywords. But starting with broad match is how you spend $3,000 and get calls from people who want to power a garden shed.

**Q: What's the most important metric to track?**

Cost per booked appointment. Not clicks, not impressions, not even cost per lead. A "lead" is a person who fills out a form or calls. A "booked appointment" is a person who agreed to let you come to their house and give a quote. If you're paying $50 per booked appointment and you close 30% of those appointments at an average sale of $10,000, you're spending $167 to make $10,000. That works. Track everything back to that number.
