**Understanding Negative Keywords**

Negative keywords are specific words or phrases that you don't want your ads to show for. Think of them as a filter that helps you target the right audience and avoid irrelevant clicks. For example, if you own a coffee shop, you might want to exclude negative keywords like "tea", "hotel", or "wedding venue".

**The Top 5 Negative Keywords for Local Businesses**

Here are the top 5 negative keywords to exclude in your Google Ads campaigns, tailored to local businesses like yours:

1. **Broad keywords**: Exclude broad keywords like "coffee" or "salon" if you're catering to a specific niche or service.
2. **Competitor names**: Block competitor names to prevent your ads from showing next to theirs.
3. **Irrelevant services**: Exclude services that don't align with your business, such as "wedding planning" or "event catering".
4. **Cities or locations**: Block cities or locations that aren't relevant to your business, especially if you serve a specific area.
5. **Time-sensitive keywords**: Exclude time-sensitive keywords like "hourly rate" or "rush service" if you don't offer them.

**## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are negative keywords in Google Ads?

Negative keywords are specific words or phrases that you can add to your Google Ads campaigns to exclude irrelevant searches and prevent your ads from showing up for them. This can help you save up to 40% of your budget by avoiding wasteful clicks. For example, if you sell coffee makers, you might add "free" or "used" as negative keywords to exclude people searching for those terms.

### How do I find the right negative keywords for my Google Ads campaign?

You can find negative keywords by analyzing your search term reports in Google Ads, looking for keywords that are driving low-quality traffic or high costs. You can also use tools like Google Keyword Planner or third-party keyword research tools to identify relevant negative keywords. For a coffee maker business, you might find that keywords like "coffee beans" or "coffee shop" are not relevant and should be added as negative keywords.

### Can I add negative keywords to an existing Google Ads campaign?

Yes, you can add negative keywords to an existing Google Ads campaign at any time. To do this, go to your campaign settings, click on "Keywords," and then click on the "Negative keywords" tab. From there, you can add new negative keywords or edit existing ones. Adding negative keywords to an existing campaign can help you save money and improve your campaign's performance right away.

### How many negative keywords can I add to a Google Ads campaign?

There is no limit to the number of negative keywords you can add to a Google Ads campaign. However, it's generally recommended to start with a small list of 10-20 negative keywords and gradually add more as you analyze your campaign's performance. Adding too many negative keywords at once can make it harder to manage your campaign and may lead to missed opportunities.

### Will adding negative keywords affect my Google Ads ad visibility?

Adding negative keywords can actually help improve your ad visibility by preventing your ads from showing up for irrelevant searches. This can help you appear more frequently for searches that are relevant to your business, which can lead to more clicks and conversions. By carefully selecting and adding negative keywords, you can optimize your ad visibility and improve your campaign's performance.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Adding Every Obvious Negative and Killing Your Volume

A dog groomer in Portland, Oregon called me after she’d “optimized” her own Google Ads for three months. She’d added “free,” “cheap,” “DIY,” and “how to groom my dog at home” as negative keywords. Smart, right? Except she’d also added everything else she could think of: “cat,” “horse,” “mobile,” “clip,” “brush.” Her reasoning was logical — she wanted only people looking for full-service professional grooming.

The problem? She was using broad match for her negatives. In Google Ads, broad match negative keywords can block more than you expect. “Clip” blocked “nail clipping.” “Brush” blocked “de-shedding brush treatment.” After I pulled her search term report, I found she was missing 63% of the actual queries that converted because her negatives were too aggressive. Her cost per lead had climbed to $28, and she was getting 12 leads a week from a $1,500 monthly budget.

**The fix:** We switched all negative keywords to phrase match and exact match. I showed her how to use the search term report to separate “block everything containing this word” from “block only this exact phrase.” We kept “free,” “DIY,” and “how to groom” as exact match negatives. We removed “clip,” “brush,” and “mobile” entirely.

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, her weekly leads jumped to 31. Her cost per lead dropped to $11. On that $1,500 monthly budget, she went from $672 per lead to $387 per lead. She stopped wasting roughly $500/month on irrelevant clicks that never converted anyway.

The lesson: negative keywords are a scalpel, not a sledgehammer. If you block too broadly, you starve your campaign of the exact traffic that converts.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Competitor Names and Brand Confusion

A hair salon owner in Austin, Texas was frustrated. She spent $2,000/month on Google Ads, but her phone wasn’t ringing. She knew her service was good — her Yelp reviews were solid, and her Booksy booking calendar was full from referrals. But ads? Nothing.

I looked at her search term report. The top-spending term was “blow dry bar Austin.” Okay, fine. But right under it: “drybar Austin,” “Drybar prices,” “is Drybar worth it.” She was paying $4.50 per click for people looking for a national chain that does blowouts only. She doesn’t do blowouts. She’s a full-service salon. Every single click was a waste.

Over 90 days, she had spent $1,860 on clicks from people who wanted a Drybar experience. Not one of those clicks resulted in a booking. Not one.

**The fix:** We added “Drybar,” “dry bar,” “blow dry,” and “blowout” as negative keywords. Then we added every competitor name within a five-mile radius — both exact and phrase match. We also went through her review snippets on Yelp and Google Business Profile to catch phrases her customers used that didn’t match her actual services.

**The outcome:** Her click-through rate initially dropped by 12%, but her conversion rate jumped from 1.8% to 4.9%. Her cost per booking went from $48 to $19. She reallocated the wasted $1,860 into a localized ad set targeting people searching for “color specialist Austin” and “balayage consultation.” That switch alone generated $3,800 in incremental revenue over the next two months.

### Mistake #3: Never Updating Your Negative Keyword List After Launch

A yoga studio owner in Denver set up her Google Ads campaign in January. She added negatives like “yoga pants,” “yoga mat,” “yoga teacher training,” and “online yoga.” Solid start. Then she didn’t touch the list for eight months.

By August, Google’s broad match had evolved. Her ads were showing for “yoga for weight loss,” “hot yoga for beginners,” and “yoga studios with childcare.” None of those were intentional. “Yoga for weight loss” drove clicks from people who wanted a quick fix, not her slow-flow community classes. “Hot yoga for beginners” got people who expected a 105-degree room (she doesn’t offer hot yoga). “Childcare” queries came from parents who were price-sensitive and never booked.

She had spent $3,400 over those eight months on clicks that never converted. Her cost per acquisition was $62, and she was averaging only 8 new clients per month from ads.

**The fix:** We set a recurring monthly calendar reminder to review the search term report. Every 30 days, she exports her top 200 search terms, flags non-converting queries, and adds them as negative keywords. We also built a “negative keyword watchlist” — a shared Google Sheet with categories like “product searches,” “DIY searches,” “competitor names,” and “off-service requests.” Each month, she reviews and adds 5–10 new negatives.

**The outcome:** After two months of consistent pruning, her CPA dropped to $31. Monthly new clients went from 8 to 17. She reclaimed $1,200 of her annual budget that had been leaking to irrelevant clicks. Most importantly, she stopped feeling like she was “burning money” every month.

## How to Build a Negative Keyword List That Actually Works

Most guides tell you to “think about what people might search for that isn’t relevant.” That’s useless advice. Here’s a three-step process I’ve used for clients in Chicago, Nashville, and New York.

**Step 1: Run a 30-day search term report and categorize every query.**

Go to Keywords > Search Terms in your Google Ads account. Set the date range to the last 30 days. Export everything. Now sort by cost. Anything that spent more than $10 and generated zero conversions goes into a “candidate” column. Be honest: if a query got 200 clicks and zero conversions, you have a problem.

For a pet groomer in Nashville, the top wasted query was “mobile dog grooming Nashville.” She doesn’t do mobile grooming. That single query had cost her $340 over 90 days. One negative keyword later, the waste stopped.

**Step 2: Use your competitors’ review data.**

Open Yelp, Google Maps, and any review platform your competitors use. Search for your own service category in your city. Look at the negative reviews. What do customers complain about? “They charged extra for nail clipping.” “They didn’t offer organic treats.” “The wait was two hours.” Those complaints tell you exactly what your customers don’t want — and what you shouldn’t be advertising for.

A coffee shop in Portland used this trick. They noticed a competitor’s one-star reviews complained about “no vegan milk options.” They added “vegan” as a negative keyword because they don’t advertise vegan options. Saved $120/month in irrelevant clicks from people who wanted oat milk cappuccinos (which they didn’t serve).

**Step 3: Add negative keywords for every service you don’t offer.**

This sounds obvious, but most small business owners only think of what they *do* offer. If you’re a hair salon that doesn’t do extensions, add “hair extensions,” “tape-in extensions,” “sew-in extensions,” and “clip-in extensions” as negatives. If you’re a fitness studio that doesn’t do personal training, add “personal trainer,” “one-on-one training,” and “private session.”

A fitness studio in Chicago with a $2,500 monthly budget was spending 18% of it on “personal training Chicago.” They didn’t offer it. After blocking that phrase and its variants, they saved $450/month.

## Why Negative Keywords Are Even More Important for Local Service Ads

If you’re using Local Service Ads (LSAs) — the “Google Guaranteed” ads that show at the very top of search results — you might think negative keywords don’t apply. They do. Indirectly.

LSAs don’t let you add negative keywords in the traditional sense. But they do use your service categories and business profile to determine what you show for. Here’s where it gets expensive.

A plumber in Denver had his LSA set to “plumber.” Google showed him for “emergency plumber,” “drain cleaning,” “water heater repair,” and “toilet installation.” He only does residential plumbing repairs — no emergencies, no drain cleaning. Yet he was paying $35 per lead for calls about burst pipes at 2 a.m. (He doesn’t do emergency calls.)

**The fix:** He adjusted his service categories in the LSA dashboard to only include the specific services he offers. He also updated his business description to say “scheduled residential plumbing repairs only — no emergency calls.” Over six weeks, his lead quality improved. His cost per booked job dropped from $87 to $41.

If you’re running LSAs, regularly review your lead details. If you get a lead that doesn’t match your actual services, note the category Google used. Then either remove that category or update your profile to clarify.

## The Role of Negative Keywords in Remarketing and Display Campaigns

Remarketing and display ads come with their own waste problem. You’ve probably seen this: you run a remarketing ad for your coffee shop in Austin. Someone clicks, doesn’t buy. Your ad follows them around the internet for two weeks.

But here’s what’s happening: your remarketing campaign might also be showing to people who visited your site accidentally — maybe they clicked a broken link, visited once six months ago, or are a competitor checking you out.

**The fix:** Exclude visitors who didn’t spend meaningful time on your site. Set a minimum session duration (I recommend 30 seconds) before someone enters your remarketing pool. Also exclude people who visited your pricing page but didn’t book — those are price shoppers, not buyers.

A pet groomer in NYC used this approach. She had a $1,200/month display campaign that was showing to anyone who had visited her site in the last 30 days. After adding a 30-second minimum session duration and excluding people who visited more than three times without booking, her cost per new client dropped from $45 to $28. Her total wasted spend dropped by roughly $320/month.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I update my negative keyword list?**

Every 30 days minimum. If your campaign is new (less than 90 days old), check every 15 days. The search term report is your single best source of truth. Set a recurring calendar reminder. I use the first Monday of every month.

**Q: Can too many negative keywords hurt my campaign?**

Yes. Blocking too broadly — especially with broad match negatives — can choke off relevant traffic. A hair salon in Chicago once blocked “blow dry” because they didn’t offer blowout services. But “blow dry styling” and “blow dry with curl” were actual services they offered. The fix: use phrase match for negatives. Exact match for the specific things you never want. Broad match for negatives only when you’re 100% sure no relevant query contains that word.

**Q: What about competitor names? Should I add them as negatives?**

Depends. If you’re a local business in a small city, adding competitor names as negatives will likely save you money. But if you’re in a competitive market with high search volume, sometimes competitor names can capture brand-new customers who are comparison shopping. Test it: add competitor names for 30 days, then compare conversion rates. If your CPA drops, keep them. If it rises, remove them.

**Q: Do negative keywords work for branded campaigns?**

Yes, but differently. If you run a branded campaign (your business name + city), add negatives for common misspellings and abbreviations that aren’t yours. A coffee shop in Portland added “Stumptown” and “Stumptown coffee” as negatives because customers kept searching for the larger chain by accident. Saved $80/month.

**Q: Should I use negative keywords for locations I don’t serve?**

Absolutely. If you’re in Austin and only serve Austin proper, add “Round Rock,” “Cedar Park,” “Pflugerville,” “Kyle,” “Buda,” “Georgetown,” and any neighboring city. These people will click your ad, realize you’re too far, and never convert. A dog groomer in Austin saved $210/month by adding 12 neighboring city names as location-based negative keywords.

**Q: What’s the fastest way to find negative keywords?**

Run your search term report. Sort by cost descending. Look at the top 50 terms. If any have zero conversions and a cost higher than $10, add them immediately. Then sort by conversions ascending. Look at terms with 1–5 conversions over 30 days. If the cost per conversion is 3x your target CPA, add them as negatives. This two-step process takes about 15 minutes and usually finds 80% of the waste.
