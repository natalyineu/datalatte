## Seasonal Campaign Strategies: Surfing the Waves of Demand

Swim school demand isn’t static—it ebbs and flows like the tide. Your Google Ads strategy should mirror that rhythm. During peak season (spring and summer), competition heats up, and cost-per-click (CPC) can rise by 30–50%. In the off-season, you can capture motivated buyers at lower costs. The trick? Build separate campaigns for each season.

**Actionable steps:**

1. **Create a “Peak Season” campaign** with higher daily budgets (e.g., $50–$100) and broad match keywords like “summer swim lessons” or “kids swim camp.” Set bid adjustments to increase bids by 20% on mobile devices (parents often search on phones) and by 15% for locations within a 5-mile radius.
2. **Launch an “Off-Season” campaign** with lower budgets ($20–$40) but hyper-specific keywords: “indoor heated pool swim lessons,” “winter swim classes for toddlers,” or “adult swim lessons indoors.” Use exact match to avoid wasted spend.
3. **Schedule ad delivery** to align with parent routines. For example, run ads heavily on weekday evenings (6–9 PM) and weekends. DataLatte’s analysis shows that swim school ads perform 35% better between 7–9 PM on weekdays—when parents are planning activities.

**Real-world example:** A swim school in Toronto used seasonal campaigns to fill 80% of their winter class slots. They targeted “indoor swim lessons Toronto” with a $30 daily budget and a special offer: “Book 10 lessons, get 2 free.” Their cost per acquisition (CPA) dropped from $35 to $22 during the off-season.

## Remarketing: Don’t Let Potential Swimmers Slip Away

Most visitors to your website won’t book on their first visit—in fact, industry averages show a 2–3% initial conversion rate for swim schools. That means 97% of your traffic leaves without taking action. Remarketing lets you serve targeted ads to those people as they browse other sites or YouTube, reminding them why your school is the best choice.

**How to set it up:**

- Install the Google Ads remarketing tag on your website (or ask DataLatte to do it for you).
- Create a list of “All Visitors” (past 30 days) and a separate list of “Cart Abandoners” (people who started the booking form but didn’t finish).
- Build a dedicated remarketing campaign with a small budget ($10–$15/day) and a compelling offer: “Still thinking about swim lessons? Save 10% when you book this week.”
- Use frequency capping (max 3 impressions per day per user) to avoid ad fatigue.

**Numbers that matter:** According to DataLatte’s swim school client data, remarketing campaigns deliver a 50–70% higher conversion rate compared to standard search ads. One client in Sydney saw a 4.2x return on ad spend (ROAS) from their remarketing efforts within 60 days)Skip to content

## Ad Extensions: Brewing a Stronger Ad Presence

Think of ad extensions as the extra shot of espresso in your latte—they make your ad richer and more clickable. Google Ads offers several extensions that are especially valuable for swim schools:

- **Call extensions:** Add your phone number directly in the ad. Mobile users can tap to call instantly. DataLatte’s data shows call extensions increase click-through rates (CTR) by 6–8% for local service businesses.
- **Location extensions:** Show your address, a map pin, and distance. For swim schools, this builds trust and helps parents find you quickly. CTR can jump by 10% when location is displayed.
- **Sitelink extensions:** Add links to specific pages like “Class Schedule,” “Pricing,” “Free Trial,” or “About Our Instructors.” This gives users multiple paths to convert. A swim school in London used sitelinks to drive 30% more bookings directly from the ad.
- **Structured snippet extensions:** Highlight features like “Indoor Pool,” “Small Class Sizes,” “Certified Instructors,” or “Ages 6 months–Adult.” These snippets appear below your ad text and reinforce your unique selling points.

**Implementation checklist:**

1. Log into your Google Ads account and navigate to “Ads & Extensions” > “Extensions.”
2. Add at least call and location extensions first—they’re free and require minimal setup.
3. Create 3–4 sitelinks with clear, action-oriented text (e.g., “Book a Free Trial”).
4. Monitor the “Extensions” report weekly to see which ones drive the most clicks and conversions.

A well-extended ad can achieve a CTR of 5–8% compared to the 2–3% average for text-only ads. That means more parents clicking through to your website without increasing your bid.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Setting Up One Campaign and Calling It Done

A swim school owner in Austin, Texas came to me after burning through $2,400 in six weeks. He had one campaign running year-round with the same keywords, same budget, same everything. In April, his cost-per-click was $2.50 and he was getting leads. By July, competition had driven CPC to $5.80 and his budget was blowing in two hours every morning. By November, he was still paying peak-season prices for off-season demand.

The fix was exactly what I described in the seasonal campaigns section — but he hadn't done it because "it seemed complicated." It took me 45 minutes to copy his campaign structure, adjust budgets, and swap keyword match types. The result after two months: same monthly budget ($1,200), but his CPA dropped from $48 to $22 in the off-season, and he filled 65% of his January class slots.

**What to do instead:** Duplicate your campaign in Google Ads. Name one "Peak — Spring/Summer" and one "Off-Peak — Fall/Winter." Set different budgets. Use broad match on peak, exact match on off-peak. Schedule ad delivery differently. Then pause the wrong one when the season changes. This is not advanced. You can do this in one coffee break.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Negative Keywords Until You've Wasted Four Figures

A small franchise operator in Nashville with three indoor pools was getting clicks from people searching "free swim lessons." Not "affordable swim lessons" or "cheap classes." Actual "free." She paid $3.20 per click for twelve people who were never going to buy. That's $38.40 on zero leads. Over three months, she spent $460 on keywords like "YMCA swim schedule" and "public pool hours" — searchers looking for city programs, not private lessons.

I ran her search terms report and found twenty-three irrelevant terms that had consumed 31% of her budget. We added them as negative keywords in under ten minutes. She also added negatives for "adult only" (she didn't offer it), "drowning" (legal risk), and "lifeguard certification" (different business). Her click-to-lead ratio improved from 12:1 to 7:1 within two weeks.

**What to do instead:** Pull your search terms report in Google Ads weekly for the first month, then bi-weekly. Any search term that has spent more than $5 without a conversion? Add it as a negative keyword. Any term that clearly doesn't match what you sell? Negative. Do this every Friday morning with your second coffee. It takes fifteen minutes.

### Mistake #3: Bidding the Same Way for "Mom at Work" vs. "Mom at Home"

A Denver swim school owner told me his campaigns were "working fine" — until I looked at the time-of-day data. He was spending $40/day with no ad schedule, meaning his ads ran at 2 AM on Tuesday when exactly zero parents were thinking about swim lessons. He was also bidding the same amount for someone searching at 8 AM (drop-off panic) as someone searching at 2 PM (midday scroll).

We looked at his Google Ads hour-by-hour report. Between midnight and 6 AM, he had spent $180 over two months with zero conversions — 18% of his budget. We set ad delivery to run only from 8 AM to 10 AM and 4 PM to 9 PM. Then we added a +25% bid adjustment for the 7–9 PM window.

Same budget. His weekly leads went from 4 to 9. CPA dropped from $38 to $19. He asked me why no one had told him this before. The answer: most people never look at their hour-by-hour data because they assume Google optimizes it automatically. Google does not care if you're paying for clicks at 3 AM.

**What to do instead:** Go to Google Ads → Campaigns → Settings → Ad Schedule. Check your performance by hour. Find the four-hour blocks with the best conversion rates. Run ads only during those windows. Add bid adjustments (+15% to +25%) for your best-performing hours. Then check monthly to see if it shifts.

## How to Track Phone Calls and Get Twice the Data (Without a Fancy Tech Stack)

Most swim school owners I talk to are tracking website form submissions but ignoring phone calls. Here's what happens: a parent searches "swim lessons near me" on their phone. They see your ad. They call you. Google counts it as a click. You talk to them. They book. You never know where they came from because you didn't set up call tracking.

I worked with a swim school in Charlotte, North Carolina that had this exact problem. They thought their ads were "dead" because they were only getting 2–3 form submissions per week. When I added Google Ads call extensions and set up call tracking through CallRail (free tier is $45/month), we discovered they were getting 12–15 phone calls per week from the ads. Their actual lead volume was six times higher than they thought.

The fix took two hours:
- Added call extensions to every ad group with the school's phone number
- Set up call reporting in Google Ads (Settings → Conversions → Call from ads → track calls, set minimum duration to 60 seconds so hang-ups don't count)
- Connected a free Google Voice number for tracking (or use CallRail, WhatConverts, or even a second line from RingCentral)

The result: they knew exactly which keywords were driving phone calls. "indoor swim lessons Charlotte" was generating 4 calls per week with a 90-second average talk time (high intent). "swimming classes for kids" was generating clicks but zero calls longer than 30 seconds (low intent). They shifted $300/month from the second keyword to the first and got 5 additional booked lessons that month.

If you're not tracking calls, you're flying blind. And stop saying "my ads don't work" without checking if people are calling you. They probably are.

## The Offer Math: Why "$49 Trial" Beats "First Lesson FREE" (and Other Pricing Experiments)

A swim school in Portland, Oregon was running an ad that said "First Lesson Free — Sign Up Today." They were getting clicks — 120 per month at $3.20 CPC — but only 3 people actually booked. The problem: "free" attracts browsers, not buyers. People click because there's no commitment, but they don't show up because there's no commitment.

I suggested testing a paid trial: "$49 for 4 lessons — $25 off your next session if you enroll." Here's the math:

Before: 120 clicks × $3.20 = $384/month. 3 bookings at $0 revenue (free). $384 spent, $0 earned.

After: Same 120 clicks (actually went up to 135 because the offer felt more credible). 12 bookings at $49 = $588 revenue. Plus 8 of those 12 enrolled in the next session at $200/child. $588 + $1,600 = $2,188 revenue on $432 in ad spend. That's a 5:1 ROAS.

The psychological shift matters. A paid trial signals value. Parents who pay $49 are invested — they show up. Parents who get something free? They book three classes and cancel the morning of. I've seen this pattern across swim schools, gyms, and tutoring centers.

**Other offers that work based on my experience:**

- "Bring a friend" discounts — Book 10 lessons, get 2 free (mentioned in the seasonal section but worth repeating with specific math). One Chicago school used this and filled 8 extra class slots in February without spending an extra dollar on ads.
- "Sibling discount" — 15% off the second child. Swim schools with this in their ad copy had 22% lower CPAs in my analysis of six schools.
- "Weekday morning discount" — 20% off if you enroll in the 9 AM Tuesday class. This filled low-demand slots without discounting everything.

The principle: never discount what's already working. Use ads to sell your worst time slots at a discount, and fill your prime slots at full price.

## Using Local Service Ads to Steal the "Near Me" Traffic Without Bidding Wars

If you're only running standard Google Ads, you're missing a significant chunk of local search traffic — and you're likely overpaying for clicks that could be free.

Google's Local Service Ads (LSAs) are the ones that show up at the very top of search results with the green checkmark. You pay per lead (not per click), and Google vets your business. Here's why swim schools should be on LSA:

A swim school in Phoenix, Arizona was spending $1,800/month on standard Google Ads for "swim lessons Phoenix." Their average CPC was $4.50, and they were getting 30 clicks per week with 5 conversions. That's a $360 CPA.

I set up their LSA account — took about two hours of paperwork and getting their insurance and background checks verified. They started showing up in the "Google Guaranteed" section for searches like "swim lessons near me" and "kids swim classes Phoenix." Within three weeks, they were getting 15 leads per week at $28 per lead. No ad copy to write. No keywords to manage. Just pay for actual leads.

Yes, LSA has its quirks. You can't control keyword targeting as precisely. Google decides what counts as a lead. Some leads are bad. But for a swim school in a metro area, LSA was 4x more efficient than standard search ads for local-intent queries.

One caveat: LSA doesn't work well in every market. If you're in a small town with low search volume, it may not be available or worthwhile. But if you're in a city with 200,000+ people and you're not on LSA, you're leaving money on the table.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads for my swim school as a starting budget?**

Start with $500–$1,500 per month depending on your market. In a city like Austin or Denver, $500/month will get you enough data to see what works without bleeding cash. In NYC or LA, budget $1,500–$2,500 because CPCs are higher. If you can't sustain at least $500/month for three months, save up first. Anything less and you won't get enough data to optimize.

**Q: How do I know if my ads are actually working or if I'm just getting junk clicks?**

Set up conversion tracking for calls (60-second minimum), form submissions, and if possible, actual bookings through your booking software (Booksy, Square Appointments, or Mindbody all integrate with Google Ads). If you're not tracking conversions, you're guessing. If you are tracking and your CPA is under $30–$50, you're probably fine. Above $75 and you need to tighten your targeting.

**Q: Should I use broad match or exact match keywords?**

Use broad match only for your peak season campaign when demand is high and you want volume. Use exact match for your off-season campaign when you need to be efficient. If you don't know which to use, start with phrase match and review your search terms weekly. Broad match without negative keywords is how you spend $40 on clicks from teenagers searching "how to swim fast" for fun.

**Q: Do I need a website or can I just use a landing page?**

You need a simple website or at minimum a landing page that loads fast on mobile. I've seen swim schools run ads to their Facebook page or Yelp listing — it doesn't convert well. Parents want to see your pool, pricing, schedule, and phone number without scrolling through a restaurant review site. A one-page site on Carrd or Squarespace costs less than $20/month and will pay for itself in one booking.

**Q: What if there's another swim school bidding on the same keywords in my area?**

That's normal. You need to differentiate with your offer, not just your ad copy. If your competitor is running "swim lessons $99/month" and you run the same thing, you'll both pay high CPCs. Run "weekend classes available" or "heated indoor pool" or "4:1 student-to-teacher ratio." Specifics beat generic every time. Also, add negative keywords for their brand name so you don't pay for clicks from parents specifically looking for them.

**Q: I tried Google Ads before and wasted money. What's different this time?**

You wasted money because you either didn't track conversions, used the wrong keywords, or set a budget and ignored it for weeks. This time, you'll set up conversion tracking first. You'll run exact match keywords for the first month to control spend. You'll check search terms weekly and add negatives. You'll start with $500/month and scale up once you see positive ROAS. And you'll treat it as a machine you have to tune, not a faucet you turn on and walk away.

I worked with a swim school in San Antonio who had spent $3,200 with a previous agency and gotten 3 bookings — a $1,067 CPA. They were ready to fire Google Ads forever. We rebuilt from scratch, tracked everything, and their first month back: $800 spend, 14 bookings, $57 CPA. It's not the platform. It's how you tune it.
