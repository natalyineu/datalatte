But there's a better way. By building your own loyalty programme, you can reward your delivery customers without giving up a cut of your hard-earned cash. In this article, we'll show you how to create a retention strategy that drives repeat business and keeps your customers coming back for more.

**Designing Your Loyalty Programme**

Before you start building your programme, it's essential to understand your customers' needs and preferences. What motivates them to order from your restaurant? What do they value most in a loyalty programme? Conducting customer surveys or focus groups can help you gather valuable insights and inform your programme's design.

A well-designed loyalty programme should be simple, easy to understand, and rewarding. Consider offering points or rewards for repeat orders, referrals, or specific menu items. You can also create tiered programmes with increasing rewards for loyal customers.

<Callout type="tip">Use a points-based system to reward customers for repeat orders. For example, every 10th order earns a free meal or dessert.</Callout>

**Implementing Your Loyalty Programme**

Once you've designed your programme, it's time to implement it. This may involve creating a mobile app, integrating with your existing POS system, or using a loyalty programme provider. Consider the following options:

*   Mobile apps like Swagbucks or Ibotta that allow customers to earn and redeem rewards
*   Loyalty programme providers like Belly or Thanx that integrate with your POS system
*   Custom-built solutions that integrate with your existing technology stack

<Callout type="warning">Avoid using third-party platforms that charge high commission rates or take a cut of your revenue. Instead, opt for a custom-built solution or a provider with transparent fees.</Callout>

**Measuring Success**

To determine the effectiveness of your loyalty programme, you'll need to track key metrics like customer retention, order frequency, and revenue growth. Consider using data analytics tools to monitor programme participation, redemption rates, and customer lifetime value.

## Promoting Your Loyalty Programme to Delivery Customers

You’ve built a great loyalty programme – but if your delivery customers don’t know about it, it won’t drive repeat orders. Start by leveraging your existing touchpoints. Add a QR code to every delivery bag or box that links directly to your loyalty sign-up page. Include a one-page flyer explaining the programme, with a simple headline like “Earn a free meal on your 10th delivery.” According to a study by Annex Cloud, 61% of customers are more likely to join a loyalty programme if they see a clear, immediate benefit. Also, send an email to your existing delivery customers announcing the programme, with a special bonus for signing up – for example, double points on their next order. Use SMS marketing for a quick reminder: “Hey [Name], your next delivery could be free! Join our loyalty club now.” For coffee shops, consider a “stamp card” digital version – every fifth delivery gets a free latte. Track sign-up rates per channel to see what works best.

## Creative Reward Ideas That Keep Customers Coming Back

Beyond simple points, think about rewards that feel personal and timely. For example, offer a “Birthday Bonus” – a free dessert or drink during the customer’s birthday month, redeemable only on delivery orders. This can increase order frequency by 15-20% during that period. Another idea: “Free Delivery Friday” – after a customer places five delivery orders, they unlock free delivery for the next month. Delivery fees are a top reason customers abandon carts, so waiving them is a powerful incentive.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating delivery customers like they don't exist

A pizza place in Portland, Oregon called Slice House had a problem. They were doing $12,000 a month on DoorDash alone. Owner Marcus was happy with the volume until he ran the numbers. After DoorDash's 25% commission, marketing fees, and chargebacks, he was pocketing about $3,200 on that channel. Meanwhile, his direct orders through his own site were growing at 3% a year.

We pulled his delivery data. Out of 1,400 unique delivery customers over six months, exactly 112 had ordered directly from him. Not 112 *more* times — 112 total. The rest went back to DoorDash every time because they didn't know he had his own ordering system.

**What he did instead:** He stapled a small card inside every delivery bag: "Order direct on our site and get a free garlic knot on your second order. Code: KNOTNOW." Simple. No app download. No points program yet. Just a reason to visit his site once.

**The outcome:** Within 90 days, 340 of those delivery-only customers placed a direct order. Average order value on direct orders was $38 versus $31 on DoorDash. He saved $7.75 per order in commission fees. Total savings per month: roughly $2,600. That's $31,200 a year he was leaving on the table because he assumed delivery customers only wanted convenience.

The fix isn't a loyalty program. The fix is *visibility*. You can't reward people who don't know you exist outside the app.

### Mistake #2: Rewarding the wrong behavior

A coffee shop in Nashville called Forth & Co. launched a punch card program. Buy 10 drinks, get one free. Classic. They printed 2,000 cards. Six months later, they'd handed out 1,800 and redeemed 37.

Why? Because their average customer came in 2.3 times a week. A frequent customer would hit 10 drinks in about a month. But the reward — a free drip coffee — was worth $3.50. Those customers were already spending $45 a month. A $3.50 reward didn't change their behavior. It just annoyed them when they lost the card.

**What they did instead:** Scrapped the punch card entirely. Switched to a points system tied to *spend*, not visits. 100 points per dollar. 1,000 points = $5 off any item. But the real change was in what they tracked: they gave double points for afternoon orders (2–4 PM) when traffic dropped 40%. They promoted it via a simple Square Loyalty integration and an Instagram story every Tuesday.

**The outcome:** Afternoon ticket counts went up 22% in the first month. Average spend per loyalty member increased from $47/month to $62/month. The $5 reward cost them $1.30 in margin per redemption. Net gain per member: $13.70/month. Across 400 active members, that's $5,480 in additional monthly revenue.

The lesson: giving a discount for behavior that's already happening isn't a loyalty program. It's a tax on your best customers.

### Mistake #3: Making the program harder to use than the delivery app

A barber shop in Austin called Eastside Cuts tried to build a custom loyalty app. Spent $4,200 on a developer through a referral. The app had a login, points tracker, booking integration, and a rewards catalog. It also took three minutes to load, required a password reset on first use, and asked for location permissions before showing the menu.

Out of 2,100 clients in their database, 87 downloaded the app. Sixteen used it more than once.

**What they did instead:** Text-to-join. They put a sign at the register: "Text 'CUTS' to [number] and get $5 off your next visit. No app. No login." They used a simple SMS tool (Text-Em-All, $19/month). Every text reply got an automated response with their booking link. After the visit, they sent a follow-up: "Thanks, Marcus. You've earned 50 points. Reply 'REWARDS' to see what you can trade them for."

**The outcome:** 340 subscribers in two weeks. Redemption rate on the initial $5 offer: 58%. Average second visit within 30 days: 71% versus 34% for non-text customers. Monthly spend per text subscriber: $58 versus $41 for the general list.

Your customers already have an app problem. Don't make another one.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring the silent majority who never complain

A hair salon in Chicago called North Avenue Beauty ran a tiered loyalty program. Bronze, Silver, Gold. Spend $500 to reach Gold and get 15% off products. They thought it was working because their Gold members were happy.

But when I looked at their churn data, something stood out. Customers who spent between $200 and $400 in a year — just shy of Silver — had a 47% churn rate. They were coming consistently, then disappearing. No complaints. No unsubscribes. They just stopped booking.

**What they did instead:** They set up an automated email sequence triggered when a customer hit $180 in annual spend. The email didn't say "You're almost at Silver!" It said: "Hey, we noticed you've been coming in regularly. We'd love to see you more. Here's $20 off your next color service." No points. No tiers. Just a nudge and a real discount.

**The outcome:** Of the 230 customers who received that email, 89 booked within two weeks. Of those, 44 came back a third time within 60 days. The $20 discount cost them about $8 in margin per redemption. The lifetime value of those recovered customers averaged $340. They recovered roughly $30,000 in otherwise lost revenue over the next year.

Your loyalty program isn't for your best customers. It's for the ones who are about to leave.

## Why Yelp Reviews Are Your Second-Best Loyalty Tool

Most loyalty guides skip this part because it doesn't sound sexy. But here's what actually happened when I tested this with a diner in Denver called The Universal.

They had a 4.2-star Yelp rating with 87 reviews. Not bad. But every time a customer paid with a card, the receipt printed a request: "Leave us a review on Yelp!" Generic. They got maybe one review per month.

**What we changed:** We linked the review request to a loyalty reward. After a customer's third visit, the system (via a simple Square + Yelp integration) sent a text: "You've been here three times. If you enjoyed it, leave a Yelp review and we'll add 100 bonus points to your account. If something wasn't right, reply to this text and tell us directly."

**Why this works:** You're not asking strangers for reviews. You're asking people who've already proven they like you. The bonus points are cheap (100 points = $1 value in their system). The direct feedback channel catches complaints before they hit Yelp.

**The numbers:** In six months, they got 43 new Yelp reviews, average rating 4.6. Their Yelp page went from "You might also like" to showing up in the top three results for "Denver diner." Estimated value of that organic visibility: roughly $1,200/month in free advertising. Cost of the bonus points redeemed: $43.

The other side: they got 17 complaint texts via the same system. They resolved 14 of them before the customer left the parking lot. Zero new one-star reviews during that period.

**How to set this up without a developer:** Use a tool like LoyaltyLoop or Wavetransit. Connect it to your POS (Square, Toast, Clover). Set a trigger for visit count. The email/text sends automatically. Test it on yourself first — most owners launch this, send a broken link, and wonder why nobody responds.

## The $0 Cost Referral System That Actually Works

Referral programs sound great in theory. But most small businesses set them up wrong. They offer "refer a friend, get $10" and then wonder why nobody participates. The problem is usually one of two things: the reward isn't worth the effort, or the process is too complicated.

A dog groomer in Austin called Bark & Bubble tried the standard approach. Printed referral cards, put them in a bowl at checkout. After three months, they'd given out 400 cards and gotten back 12. That's a 3% redemption rate.

**What actually worked:** They stopped asking customers to do anything. Instead, when a customer booked a grooming appointment through Booksy, the confirmation email included a line: "Bring a friend's dog to their first appointment and we'll add a nail trim (value $15) to your next visit. No code needed — just tell us their name."

**Why this is different:** No card to remember. No code to enter. No awkward conversation where a customer has to explain a referral program. The existing customer just mentions a name during checkout. The system (Booksy + a manual spreadsheet they later automated with Zapier) tracks it.

**The outcome:** 41% of new clients in the next quarter came from referrals. Average referral customer lifetime value: $280. Cost per acquisition: $0 in upfront spend, $15 in nail trim costs when the referring customer redeemed. Net ROI: roughly 18:1.

A fitness studio in Nashville did something similar but with a twist. Instead of rewarding the referrer, they gave the new person their first class free and the referrer a shoutout on their Instagram story. Cost: $0. Result: 28 new members in 60 days.

The common thread: make it frictionless. If a customer has to remember a code, print a card, or download an app, they won't do it. The best referral system is the one they don't have to think about.

## Stop Treating Email Like a Newsletter. It's a Revenue Channel.

I see this constantly. Small businesses collect emails, then send a monthly "Here's what's happening this month" email that gets a 12% open rate and zero clicks. That's not a channel. That's a waste of bandwidth.

A yoga studio in San Francisco called Forward Fold had 1,400 email subscribers. They were sending a weekly newsletter with class schedules and a quote from a guru. Open rate hovered around 14%. No one was booking from it.

**What they changed:** They segmented their list by booking behavior using their studio management software (Momence). Three segments: active (booked in last 30 days), lapsed (30-90 days), and ghost (90+ days).

For active members: "Book your next class and bring a friend for free. Expires in 48 hours." Open rate: 38%. Booking rate: 22%.

For lapsed members: "We miss you. Here's a free class credit. No strings attached." Open rate: 31%. Reactivation rate: 15%.

For ghost members: "We're clearing our list. Click here to stay subscribed, or we'll remove you in 7 days." Open rate: 9%. Unsubscribes: 4%. But their list cleaned up by 30%, and engagement on subsequent sends doubled.

**The revenue impact:** In the first month, they generated $3,800 in additional bookings from the active segment alone. The lapsed segment brought back 42 people who hadn't visited in 45+ days. Cost: $0 in ad spend, $0 in design fees (plain text emails with a button).

**How to do this with Mailchimp or Klaviyo:** Set up a simple automation based on days since last purchase. Offer a time-sensitive, specific reward. Not "10% off your next visit" — that's weak. "Your next class is on us if you book by Friday" is strong. Test it on 100 people first. If it works, scale it.

Most business owners overthink email. They want pretty templates and clever subject lines. The only thing that matters is whether the email makes someone take an action they wouldn't have taken otherwise.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Won't this cost more than the delivery fees I'm saving?**

Only if you set it up wrong. A text-to-join system costs $19/month. A Square Loyalty integration is free with your account. The rewards you give out should be funded by the commission you're no longer paying. If you save $2,000/month in DoorDash fees and spend $400 on rewards and $30 on software, you're still ahead by $1,570. The math only breaks if you give away more than you save. Don't do that.

**Q: My customers are already on DoorDash. Why would they switch to my site?**

They won't unless you give them a reason. That reason isn't "support local" — that's a guilt trip. The reason is speed, price, or a bonus they can't get on the app. A free side. A slightly lower price. Faster pickup. I've seen "Order direct and skip the $3.50 service fee" work better than any values-based messaging.

**Q: What if I only have 10 regulars? Is a loyalty program worth it?**

Ten loyal customers who spend $50/month is $6,000/year. If you lose them because you're not tracking their behavior, that's real money. But don't build a tiered program for 10 people. Text them individually. Remember their order. A loyalty program at that scale is a relationship, not a system. You can scale it when you hit 50+ regulars.

**Q: Isn't this just another marketing thing I have to manage?**

Yes, but poorly managed is better than nonexistent. Start with one channel. Text messages. Or email. Not both. Spend 30 minutes a week on it. If you see results, add the next channel. If you don't, stop. Most owners overcomplicate this because they think they need a full strategy. You need one automated email and one sign-up method. That's it.

**Q: How do I know if this is actually working?**

Track three numbers: reorder rate, average order value, and churn rate. If your loyalty program doesn't improve at least two of those in 90 days, it's not working. Don't track email opens or app downloads. Those are vanity metrics. Track whether people are spending more money, more often, over a longer period.

**Q: Do I need a separate app for this?**

No. I've watched business owners spend $5,000 on a custom app that nobody downloads. Use what you already have. Square. Toast. Mailchimp. Booksy. Your phone's notes app. The technology is not the barrier. The barrier is whether you consistently ask customers to join and consistently deliver value once they do.
