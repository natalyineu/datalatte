And they make purchase decisions in ways that are completely different from Western tourists.

## How Chinese tourists find local businesses (it's not Google)

The single most important thing to understand: **Chinese tourists use different platforms than Western tourists**. They're not on TripAdvisor or Yelp. They're not using Google Maps as their primary discovery tool. They're using:

**Xiaohongshu (RED):** The primary platform for travel inspiration and local business discovery. Before leaving China, most tourists create a saved list of places to visit based on Xiaohongshu posts from friends and influencers. If your business isn't on Xiaohongshu, you're not on their pre-trip list.

**Douyin:** China's version of TikTok (same company, separate app). Short video content about restaurants, cafés, and experiences drives significant discovery — a viral Douyin video of someone's meal at your restaurant can drive bookings for weeks.

**Mafengwo:** The "TripAdvisor of China" — a dedicated travel review platform. Less important than Xiaohongshu for local small businesses but worth having a presence on for restaurants and accommodation.

**WeChat:** Used for peer recommendations via group chats and Moments. If one tourist has a great experience at your café and shares it on WeChat, their entire travel group will know about you within minutes.

**Baidu Maps:** The Chinese equivalent of Google Maps, used for navigation once tourists are on the ground. Claiming your Baidu Maps listing ensures you appear when they're physically nearby and searching.

## The 5 signals that tell a Chinese tourist "this place welcomes us"

Beyond which apps you're on, physical signals inside and outside your business communicate cultural fluency and comfort. Chinese tourists specifically look for these:

**1. WeChat Pay and Alipay logos.** Displaying these at your register is the single highest-impact thing you can do. Many Chinese tourists carry minimal cash and prefer not to use foreign credit cards. Accepting mobile payments removes a major friction point and signals clear cultural awareness. Both Stripe and Airwallex support WeChat Pay and Alipay for Australian and UK businesses.

**2. Chinese-language menu or signage.** Even a simple laminated Chinese menu insert — professionally translated, not Google Translated — shows effort and care. A QR code that links to a Chinese menu page works equally well. For a café, just having your signature items written in Chinese on your chalkboard can make a tangible difference.

**3. Xiaohongshu QR code at the counter.** Chinese tourists are conditioned to scan QR codes. A small card with your Xiaohongshu QR code next to the register invites them to follow you and leave a review — and they often do.

**4. A "photo spot."** Chinese tourists actively seek out 打卡点 (dǎ kǎ diǎn) — visually distinctive spots worth photographing. A neon sign, a beautiful window display, an unusual product presentation. If your business has one, you'll appear in tourist posts automatically.

**5. Staff who speak even basic Mandarin.** A single staff member who can say "你好" (nǐ hǎo), "谢谢" (xièxiè), and "欢迎" (huānyíng) — hello, thank you, welcome — creates a disproportionately positive impression. It signals warmth and cultural effort, not just transaction efficiency.

<Callout type="example">A fish and chip shop near the Great Ocean Road in Victoria added WeChat Pay and a small Chinese-language sign in their window. Within one summer season, Chinese tourists became their second largest customer group — up from near zero — without any additional paid advertising.</Callout>

## Claiming your Baidu Maps listing

When a Chinese tourist is walking around your city and searching nearby on their phone, Baidu Maps is likely their navigation app. Claiming your listing ensures you appear in those searches.

**How to claim your Baidu Maps listing:**
1. Go to map.baidu.com and search your business name
2. If your business appears, click "Claim business" (认领商家)
3. If it doesn't appear, click "Add a place" (添加地点)
4. Verify via phone — you'll need a Chinese phone number, OR use a Baidu account and submit via the online verification form (slower but works for overseas businesses)

Alternatively, use a verified Baidu partner service — many digital marketing agencies in Australia and the UK offer Baidu listing verification for $100–$200 one-time.

**What to add to your listing:**
- Business name in English AND Chinese
- Address (in English — Baidu supports international addresses)
- Opening hours
- Phone number
- Photos: minimum 5 high-quality images

<Callout type="warning">Baidu Maps information often lags behind Google Maps. If your business has moved, changed hours, or has incorrect information on Baidu, Chinese tourists using it for navigation will arrive at the wrong location or wrong time. Check your listing quarterly.</Callout>

## Handling Chinese tourist reviews

When Chinese tourists post reviews on Xiaohongshu, WeChat Moments, or Mafengwo, those reviews are visible to thousands of their connections — and they persist. A single positive review from a Chinese tourist can drive referral visits for months.

**How to encourage reviews:**
- Train staff to mention Xiaohongshu: "We'd love it if you shared your experience on 小红书 (Xiaohongshu)" — many tourists will be delighted that you know the platform
- Display a QR code linking to your Xiaohongshu page with Chinese text: "扫码关注我们" (Scan to follow us)
- Respond to every Chinese-language review — even a brief "谢谢你的好评！欢迎再次光临！" (Thank you for your kind review! Please visit again!) shows genuine engagement

**Translation tools for responding to reviews:**
If you don't have a Chinese speaker on staff, DeepL (not Google Translate) produces much more natural Chinese. For critical communications — formal responses to negative reviews — invest in a human translator.

## Building a seasonal campaign around Chinese travel peaks

Chinese tourist traffic follows predictable seasonal patterns tied to Chinese public holidays:

- **Chinese New Year (Jan/Feb):** 7-day holiday, massive outbound travel surge
- **Golden Week (Oct 1–7):** National Day holiday, second largest outbound travel period
- **Summer school holidays (July–Aug):** Family travel peak, higher spend on experiences
- **May Day Golden Week (May 1–5):** Growing in importance as a travel period

Plan promotions and Xiaohongshu content 4–6 weeks before these windows. A café that posts "🧧 Spring Festival Special — Free red bean pastry with every coffee order in February" will appear in searches from tourists planning their trip while still in China.

## FAQ

**Do I need to speak Chinese to market to Chinese tourists?**
No — but having one bilingual staff member, or access to a freelance translator for your key materials, makes a significant difference. The platforms (Xiaohongshu, WeChat) require Chinese-language content to be effective.

**Which city in Australia gets the most Chinese tourists?**
Sydney receives the most Chinese visitors, followed by Melbourne and the Gold Coast. However, many regional areas near natural attractions (Great Barrier Reef, Great Ocean Road, Blue Mountains) also receive significant Chinese tourism. Check Tourism Australia's regional data for your area.

**Are Chinese tourists price-sensitive?**
Less so than many business owners assume, especially for quality food and beauty experiences. What they're more sensitive to is perceived value — a high price for an exceptional, photogenic, authentic experience is well-received. A high price for a mediocre experience gets reported in detail on Xiaohongshu.

**Should I hire a Chinese-speaking staff member just for tourist season?**
If you're in a high-traffic tourist area and experiencing significant Chinese visitor traffic, a part-time bilingual staff member during peak seasons (Chinese New Year, Golden Week) has an immediate positive ROI. Even 10–15 hours per week during these periods can transform the customer experience for this segment.

**What if I get a negative review in Chinese and can't read it?**
Copy the text into DeepL for a translation, then respond in Chinese using DeepL to draft your response (and have a Chinese speaker review it if possible). Leaving a Chinese negative review unresponded is worse than an imperfect response — it signals that you didn't care enough to engage.
