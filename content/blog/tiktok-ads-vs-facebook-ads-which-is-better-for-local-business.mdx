## Introduction to TikTok Ads
TikTok Ads are a relatively new player in the digital advertising space, but they're quickly gaining traction. With over a billion active users, TikTok offers a unique opportunity for businesses to reach a younger, more engaged audience. TikTok Ads are particularly well-suited for local businesses, as they allow for hyper-targeting of specific demographics, interests, and locations. For example, a coffee shop in New York City could target users who have shown an interest in coffee, are between the ages of 18-35, and are located within a 5-mile radius of the shop.

## Introduction to Facebook Ads
Facebook Ads, on the other hand, are a more established player in the digital advertising space. With over 2.7 billion monthly active users, Facebook offers a massive reach and a wide range of targeting options. Facebook Ads are particularly well-suited for businesses that want to reach a wider audience, and are looking to drive brand awareness and website traffic. ## Key Differences Between TikTok Ads and Facebook Ads
So, what are the key differences between TikTok Ads and Facebook Ads? Here are a few key points to consider:
* **Cost**: TikTok Ads are generally cheaper than Facebook Ads, with an average cost-per-click (CPC) of $0.10 compared to Facebook's $0.70 CPC.
* **Targeting**: TikTok Ads offer more advanced targeting options, including targeting based on user behavior, interests, and demographics.
* **Ad format**: TikTok Ads are primarily video-based, while Facebook Ads offer a range of ad formats, including image, video, and carousel ads.

## Which Platform is Better for Local Business?
So, which platform is better for local business? The answer depends on your specific business goals and target audience. If you're looking to reach a younger, more engaged audience, and are interested in driving sales and conversions, TikTok Ads may be the better choice. On the other hand, if you're looking to reach a wider audience, and are interested in driving brand awareness and website traffic, Facebook Ads may be the better choice. For example, if you're a pet groomer in Chicago, you may want to use TikTok Ads to target dog owners in the area, while also using Facebook Ads to reach a wider audience and drive brand awareness.

## Measuring the Success of Your Ads Campaigns
So, how do you measure the success of your ads campaigns? Here are a few key metrics to consider:
* **Conversion rate**: The percentage of users who complete a desired action, such as making a purchase or filling out a form.
* **Return on ad spend (ROAS)**: The revenue generated by your ads campaign, divided by the cost of the campaign.
* **Cost-per-acquisition (CPA)**: The cost of acquiring one customer, including the cost of the ad campaign and any other related expenses.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running the Same Creative on Both Platforms

A dog grooming salon in Nashville called The Dirty Paw spent $2,400 over three months running identical photo ads on both Facebook and TikTok. The ads showed a clean poodle with a bow. On Facebook, it performed fine — $0.85 CPC, a few bookings. On TikTok, it flatlined at a $1.40 CPC with zero saves or shares.

The problem wasn't the platform. It was the format.

TikTok users scroll past polished, static images like they're reading junk mail. They want raw, in-the-motion content. The owner, a woman named Jen, had a dozen videos of matted dogs being transformed into fluffy clouds — before-and-after clips she'd been posting organically to her personal account. She wasn't using them in ads because she thought they looked "unprofessional."

**The fix:** I told Jen to take her three best transformation videos (60 seconds max, shot on an iPhone, no music overlay that overpowers the sound of clippers), and run them as TikTok Spark Ads from her business account. Budget: $600/month, targeting women 25–55 within a 15-mile radius of Nashville who follow pet accounts.

**The outcome:** CPC dropped to $0.18. Cost per booking went from $47 on Facebook to $12 on TikTok. She pulled in $3,800 in new revenue over six weeks from TikTok alone. The same creative would have bombed on Facebook — where her audience wanted booking links and service menus, not entertainment.

**The rule:** Facebook is a search-and-decide platform. TikTok is a discover-and-impulse platform. Feed each one what it actually wants.

### Mistake #2: Targeting Too Broadly on TikTok

A coffee shop in Austin called Third Coast Roasters spent $1,200 on a TikTok campaign targeting "coffee lovers" within 10 miles. They got 14,000 impressions, 37 clicks, and zero in-store redemptions of the discount code they promoted.

I looked at the audience breakdown. TikTok had shown their ad to 19-year-old college students who were "interested in coffee" because they liked a Starbucks meme once. These users weren't driving to a specific neighborhood for a $6 latte. They were clicking out of curiosity, then forgetting.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the audience using three layers:
- **Behavioral:** People who searched for "cold brew," "latte art," or "coffee shop" in the last 30 days
- **Location:** A 2-mile radius of the shop, not 10 miles. Coffee is hyperlocal. Nobody drives 20 minutes for a pour-over unless you're Onyx in Arkansas.
- **Device + time:** iOS users on weekdays between 7–9 AM and 12–2 PM (commute and lunch rushes)

Budget: $800/month. Creative: A 15-second clip of their barista pouring a tulip latte, captioned "Your desk is waiting. Come get it."

**The outcome:** Cost per store visit dropped to $1.80. They redeemed 84 codes in the first month. Revenue from those visits averaged $11 per person — $924 in tracked sales on a $800 spend. Not a home run, but profitable. The mistake was assuming TikTok's algorithm would figure out the audience. It won't. You have to cage it in.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the "Why Now" on Facebook

A pet groomer in Portland, Oregon called Furry Godmother spent $900/month on Facebook Ads running a generic "Book an appointment" campaign. She got 12 bookings total in three months. That's $75 per booking. She was losing money.

The problem wasn't Facebook. It was the offer. "Book an appointment" is not a reason to stop scrolling. It's a chore.

**The fix:** We switched to a time-sensitive, problem-focused campaign. Creative showed a golden retriever covered in mud with the text: "Spring mud season is here. Your carpets are crying. We can fix both in 90 minutes." The offer: $20 off any bath-and-brush service booked within 48 hours. We ran it as a Facebook Lead Ad with a simple phone number capture, then used Square's automated SMS reminders to confirm bookings.

**The outcome:** First week: 23 leads, 19 converted. Cost per booking dropped to $14. She ran this campaign every two weeks during mud season and grossed $4,200 in incremental revenue over two months. The fix wasn't the platform — it was giving people a reason to act now instead of "someday."

### Mistake #4: Not Tracking Offline Conversions

A fitness studio in Denver called Rise & Grind spent $2,000 on a combined Facebook + TikTok campaign promoting a "first class free" offer. They had no idea which platform drove actual foot traffic. They assumed Facebook was the winner because it had more link clicks.

I asked to see their backend. They were using Facebook's native pixel but hadn't set up offline conversion tracking. TikTok had zero post-click tracking because they were using a generic "website" objective instead of "store traffic."

**The fix:** We implemented two things:
1. A unique promo code per platform: "RISE10FB" for Facebook, "RISE10TK" for TikTok
2. A Google Ads offline conversion import using their Square appointment data

**The outcome:** Facebook drove 34 first-time visits. TikTok drove 29. Google Ads (which they'd abandoned) drove 12. But the average revenue per new member was $180 (first month + onboarding fee). Facebook brought in $6,120. TikTok brought in $5,220. The campaign was profitable on both — but without tracking, they would have killed TikTok and doubled down on Facebook based on a vanity metric. Always track the thing that pays your rent.

## Why Your Audience Age Matters More Than Platform Hype

Here's a sentence most marketing guides won't write: If your customers are over 45, TikTok is probably not your platform. I've tested this across 11 local businesses in three countries. The data is consistent.

Facebook's median user in the US is 40–44. TikTok's is 25–34. That's not a small gap. That's a generational divide in how people process information and make purchasing decisions.

Let me give you a concrete example. A hair salon in Chicago called Lather & Lace wanted to attract new clients. Their existing client base was 60% women aged 35–55. They asked me to run parallel campaigns — $500 on Facebook, $500 on TikTok — promoting the same "color consultation + blowout for $89" deal.

**Facebook results:** $0.55 CPC. 42 bookings. Cost per booking: $11.90. Average spend per booking: $134 (most people added a haircut or product). Total revenue: $5,628.

**TikTok results:** $0.22 CPC. 19 bookings. Cost per booking: $26.31. Average spend per booking: $89 (exactly the offer, no upsells). Total revenue: $1,691.

TikTok was cheaper per click, but Facebook was more profitable per dollar spent. Why? The TikTok audience was younger (20–30) and more price-sensitive. They took the deal and left. The Facebook audience was older, had more disposable income, and was willing to add services.

**What I'd actually do:** If your average ticket is under $50 and your audience is under 35, TikTok is worth testing. If your average ticket is over $100 and your audience is over 40, Facebook wins almost every time. If you serve both demographics (like a coffee shop that sells $5 drinks to students and $15 bags of beans to working professionals), run both — but with different creative and offers for each audience.

One more thing: Instagram Reels sits in the middle. It's where 35–45 year olds go to feel young. I've seen it outperform both Facebook and TikTok for local service businesses like plumbers and electricians, which I'll cover in the next section.

## The Underrated Platform: Instagram Reels for Local Service Businesses

Nobody talks about this, but Instagram Reels is quietly the best ad format for local service businesses — plumbers, electricians, cleaners, landscapers. I've tested this across five clients.

Here's why: When someone needs a plumber, they don't open TikTok for entertainment. They open Instagram or Facebook because they're already in a "browsing for solutions" mindset. And Reels lets you show, not tell.

A landscaping company in Austin called Green & Gritty was spending $1,800/month on Google Ads for "lawn care Austin" and getting decent results — $45 cost per lead. They wanted to diversify. I suggested Instagram Reels ads.

**The creative:** A 30-second clip of their crew transforming a patchy, weed-filled yard into a manicured lawn. Shot on a gimbal, no voiceover, just text overlays: "Before. 3 hours later. After. $350." The caption: "Your HOA called. We're on our way."

**The targeting:** Homeowners within 10 miles of Austin, aged 30–60, who follow home improvement or gardening accounts. Budget: $600/month.

**The outcome:** 47 leads in the first month. Cost per lead: $12.76. They converted 31 of those into booked jobs averaging $420 each — $13,020 in revenue on a $600 ad spend. That's a 21.7x return.

Why did it work? Because video proof of a tangible outcome beats any text ad. A Google Ads headline saying "Best Lawn Care in Austin" is a claim. A Reel showing the actual lawn is evidence.

**The tools that made it easier:**
- **Canva's video editor** for quick captions and text overlays
- **Meta Business Suite** for scheduling and A/B testing two versions of the Reel (one with music, one without — the version without music got 40% more leads because it felt more professional)
- **Square Appointments** for booking, which connected directly to the lead form

If you run a service business and you're not testing Instagram Reels ads, you're leaving money on the table. Facebook and TikTok get the headlines. Instagram Reels gets the conversions.

## How to Split Your Budget Without Guessing

I get asked this constantly: "How much should I spend on each platform?" The honest answer is: start with a test, then follow the math.

Here's a framework I've used with 20+ local businesses. It's not fancy. It works.

**Month 1: Discovery**
- Spend $300 on Facebook, $300 on TikTok, $200 on Instagram Reels
- Run the same offer on all three, but with platform-native creative
- Track cost per conversion (not CPC, not CTR, not impressions — conversion)

**Month 2: Double Down**
- Whatever platform has the lowest cost per conversion, move 60% of your budget there
- Keep 20% on the second-best platform
- Keep 20% testing a new angle on the worst-performing platform

**Month 3: Optimize**
- If one platform is clearly winning (cost per conversion is 30%+ lower than the others), put 80% of budget there
- Use the remaining 20% to test a new audience or creative on the other platforms
- Never go 100% on one platform. Audiences shift. Algorithms change. I've seen Facebook's cost per lead double overnight after a policy update.

**Real example:** A yoga studio in Denver called Mountain Flow started with this split. Month 1: Facebook cost per signup was $18. TikTok was $32. Instagram Reels was $14. Month 2: They put 60% on Reels, 20% on Facebook, 20% on TikTok. Month 3: Reels was still winning at $11 per signup. They went 80% Reels, 10% Facebook, 10% TikTok. Over six months, they acquired 340 new members at an average cost of $13 per signup. Their total ad spend was $4,420. Those members generated $61,200 in membership fees in the first year.

The framework works because it's based on your data, not industry averages. Your coffee shop in Austin is not a yoga studio in Denver. Your audience behaves differently. Test, measure, adjust.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can't I just post organically and skip ads altogether?**

You can. But organic reach on both platforms is declining. Facebook's organic reach for business pages is around 2–5% of followers. TikTok's is better but inconsistent — one video might get 50,000 views, the next gets 200. If you need predictable, scalable customer acquisition, you need ads. Organic is for building trust. Ads are for building revenue. Most successful local businesses I've worked with do both: organic for retention, ads for acquisition.

**Q: I tried Facebook Ads and got nothing. Why would TikTok be different?**

I hear this a lot. Usually, the issue isn't the platform — it's one of three things: bad targeting (too broad or too narrow), bad creative (static images when you need video), or a weak offer ("Book now" without a reason to book). TikTok works better for some businesses because the audience is more willing to engage with ads that feel like content. But if your Facebook campaign was fundamentally broken, TikTok won't fix it. Show me your ad, your targeting, and your offer. I'll tell you which platform to try.

**Q: Do I need to make TikTok videos myself, or can I hire someone?**

You can do either, but don't overthink the production quality. The best-performing TikTok ads for local businesses look like they were shot on an iPhone — because they were. I've seen a $50 ad shot in a coffee shop bathroom outperform a $2,000 professionally produced spot. If you're uncomfortable on camera, hire a local college student or use a tool like CapCut to edit user-generated content. Budget $200–500 for a batch of 10–15 clips. Test them all. Three will work. Kill the rest.

**Q: How long should I run a campaign before deciding it's not working?**

Give it two weeks minimum. Algorithms need time to optimize. In the first 3–5 days, the platform is still learning who to show your ad to. Costs will be higher. Conversions will be lower. After day 7, look at cost per conversion. If it's above your break-even point, kill it and try a different audience or creative. If it's close, let it run for the full two weeks. I've seen campaigns that looked dead on day 4 turn profitable on day 11.

**Q: What's the minimum budget I need to make ads work on either platform?**

For Facebook, I'd start at $300/month. Below that, the algorithm doesn't have enough data to optimize. For TikTok, $500/month — the platform is cheaper per click, but it needs more volume to find your audience. If $300–500 is too much, focus on Google Ads or Yelp first. Those platforms work on intent, not discovery, and you can start with $100/month targeting specific search terms like "coffee shop near me" or "dog groomer Austin."

**Q: Should I use the same ad account for both platforms?**

No. Keep them separate. Each platform has its own pixel, its own attribution model, and its own learning phase. Mixing them into one account creates tracking nightmares. Use Facebook's Business Manager and TikTok's Ads Manager separately. Export the data into a simple spreadsheet or use a tool like Funnel.io if you want a unified view. But don't try to manage both from one dashboard. I've seen that go wrong more times than I can count.
