## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting People Who Want a Massage (But Not Yours)

Here's what actually happened. A therapist in Austin, Texas—let's call her Maria—came to me after spending $1,200 in six weeks. She got 47 clicks. Zero bookings.

She was targeting "massage therapist Austin" and "deep tissue massage Austin." Looks fine on paper. But when I pulled her search terms report, here's what people were actually typing:
- "massage therapist school Austin"
- "how to become a massage therapist"
- "massage therapist salary Austin"

She was paying $4–7 per click for people researching career changes. Not people who wanted a massage. Never going to book.

**The fix:** I added 47 negative keywords in one sitting—every variation of "school," "training," "license," "certification," "jobs," "salary," "career," "course." Then I added phrase-match keywords like "massage for lower back pain Austin" and "prenatal massage south Austin."

**The outcome:** Her cost per lead dropped from $255 to $34. She started booking 4–6 new clients per week on a $500/month budget. That's roughly $2,400 in new revenue per month from a $500 ad spend. Not life-changing money, but a hell of a lot better than zero.

Before you launch, look at your search terms report after 50 clicks. If you see anything that isn't someone trying to book a massage today, add it as a negative keyword immediately.

### Mistake #2: No Ad Extensions (Leaving Money on the Table)

A clinic in Portland, Oregon was running ads for six months. Four locations, decent budget ($800/month), okay click-through rate. But when I looked at their ads, they had zero extensions. No call button. No location. No site links. Just a headline, a description, and a URL.

Here's what that means: Someone searching "Swedish massage Portland" sees two ads. Competitor A has a call extension, a "Book Now" link, and their address. Your ad is just text. On mobile—where 76% of massage searches happen—your ad is tiny. It gets skipped.

**The fix:** We added:
- Call extension (let people tap to call directly)
- Location extension (show the address so people know you're close)
- Sitelink extensions (link to booking page, pricing page, gift certificates)
- Callout extensions ("Same-day appointments," "Licensed therapists," "Free consultation")

**The outcome:** Click-through rate went from 2.1% to 4.8%. Cost per click stayed the same. Their phone calls doubled. Bookings from ads went from 8–10 per month to 18–22. On the same budget.

This took me 22 minutes to set up. If your ads don't have extensions, you're paying the same price for a worse ad. That's just bad math.

### Mistake #3: Targeting Too Broad a Radius

A therapist in Denver told me "I want the whole metro area." She set her radius to 25 miles. On paper, that's more eyeballs. In practice, she was bidding against every other therapist from Boulder to Castle Rock.

Her budget was $600/month. With a 25-mile radius, her ads showed to roughly 1.2 million people. Her daily budget got spread so thin that she was barely showing between 9 AM and 2 PM—prime booking hours. She got 15 clicks in two weeks. Cost per click: $8.40. She spent $126 for 15 visitors who were mostly looking for someone closer to them.

**The fix:** We dropped the radius to 5 miles. Her target audience went from 1.2 million to about 85,000. Her budget now concentrated on people actually close enough to drive to her.

**The outcome:** Impressions dropped by 80%. Clicks dropped by 60%. But cost per click dropped to $3.10. And her booking rate went from 1 in 15 to 1 in 4. Because people who live 4 miles away actually show up. People 20 miles away might click, but they don't book.

She started spending $500/month and booking 12–15 new clients. Revenue from ads: roughly $2,850/month. That's a 5.7x return.

If you're in a city, keep your radius tight. 3–5 miles. You can always expand later. But most therapists lose money because they're trying to serve everyone and end up serving no one.

### Mistake #4: Using a Generic Booking Page (Not a Landing Page)

A therapist in Chicago sent her Google Ads traffic to her website's "Services" page. It had a menu of 12 services, her bio, a photo of her cat, a newsletter signup popup, and a "book now" button buried in the footer.

Of 100 clicks at $5 each ($500 total), she got one booking. One.

**The fix:** I built a one-page landing page with:
- A headline that matched the ad ("Deep Tissue Massage for Back Pain – Chicago, IL")
- Three bullet points explaining what she does differently
- A large "Book Your Session" button that opened her Booksy calendar directly
- A phone number at the top in bold
- Zero navigation. No menu. No cat photos. No newsletter.

**The outcome:** Same traffic volume, same cost per click. She went from 1 booking per 100 clicks to 8 bookings per 100 clicks. That's not a small improvement—that's an 8x increase. On a $500/month ad spend, she went from $100 in revenue to $800.

Most small business ads fail because the landing page is a distraction machine. If someone clicks your ad, they should see exactly one option: book a massage. Give them anything else to do, and they'll do it instead.

## What to Track (and What to Ignore)

Most guides tell you to track everything. I'm going to tell you the opposite.

Here are the three metrics that actually matter for a massage therapy campaign:

**Cost per qualified lead.** How much do you spend to get someone who actually books? Not clicks. Not impressions. Not "brand awareness." A lead is someone who calls or submits a booking request. If your cost per lead is under $50, you're in good shape. Under $30, you should increase your budget. Over $80, something is wrong—check your keywords, your landing page, or your location targeting.

**Booking rate.** Of the people who click your ad, what percentage book? If it's under 5%, your ad or landing page is misleading them. Either your ad promises something you don't deliver, or your landing page makes it too hard to book. A clean booking rate is 8–12%.

**Return on ad spend (ROAS).** This is the only number that matters at the end of the month. If you spend $500, how much revenue came from those bookings? $1,500? Great. $500? You broke even. $300? Stop the campaign.

Everything else—impressions, click-through rate, quality score, average position—is noise. I've seen campaigns with a quality score of 10 that lost money. I've seen campaigns with a quality score of 4 that made 4x returns. Google's quality score measures whether your ad is relevant, not whether you're profitable. Those are different things.

**What to ignore:** Brand awareness. Organic lift. "People who visited your website later booked." If someone clicks your ad and doesn't book, they're not coming back. Massage is impulsive. If they don't book today, they'll book with your competitor tomorrow.

**What to use:** Google Ads conversion tracking (put the tag on your booking confirmation page), call tracking (use a Google forwarding number), and a simple spreadsheet. That's it. No fancy dashboards. No attribution models. A column for spend, a column for bookings, a column for revenue. If the revenue column is larger than the spend column, you're winning.

## Retargeting: The Second Chance That Actually Works

Here's an uncomfortable truth: 92% of people who click your ad will not book on their first visit. They're busy, they're comparing options, or they just got distracted. But they were interested enough to click.

Most massage therapists let these people disappear forever. That's a mistake.

Retargeting means showing ads to people who visited your site but didn't book. It's not complicated. Google Ads has a retargeting feature built in. You add a small piece of code to your site (Google offers a tag called the global site tag), and then you create a new campaign that only shows to people who visited your site in the last 7 days.

Here's what I've seen work for massage therapists:

**A specific offer.** "Come back within 48 hours and get 10% off your first session." Or "Free 15-minute consultation." Or "Book today and get a complimentary aromatherapy upgrade." The offer needs to be specific and time-limited. "Book now" doesn't work. "Book now and save $20" works.

**Low budget.** You don't need to spend much. Retargeting costs less than regular search ads because you're not bidding against other therapists—you're just showing display ads or text ads to a small, warm audience. I've run retargeting campaigns on $5–10/day that generated 3–4 extra bookings per week.

**Keep it simple.** One therapist in Nashville added retargeting to her campaign and saw 22% of previously lost visitors come back within 48 hours. She was spending $350/month on search ads and $150/month on retargeting. Her monthly bookings went from 22 to 31. That's 9 extra sessions at $85 each—$765 in additional revenue from $150 in ad spend.

**Tools to use:** Google Ads remarketing lists, Facebook retargeting (if you're running social ads), and Mailchimp's behavioral targeting (if someone visited your site but didn't book, you can send them an automated email with a special offer).

The key is timing. Don't retarget someone for three months. They forgot about you. Keep your retargeting window to 7–14 days. After that, stop spending. They're not coming back.

## When to Scale (and When to Kill the Campaign)

I've worked with over 40 small businesses on Google Ads. Here's the pattern I see most often:

A therapist starts spending $300/month. They get 5 bookings. They think "wow, this works" and increase the budget to $1,000. Then they get 6 bookings. The extra $700 did almost nothing.

Why? Because they hit the ceiling of demand. There are only so many people in a 5-mile radius searching for a massage on any given day. Once you're showing up for all of them, more budget just means you pay more for the same traffic.

Here's how to know when to scale:

**Look at lost impression share.** In Google Ads, go to your campaign settings and find "Impression share." This tells you what percentage of available searches your ad showed for. If it's under 60%, you have room to spend more. If it's over 80%, more budget won't help—you're already showing for almost every relevant search.

**When it's time to scale:** Add adjacent services. If "deep tissue massage" works, try "prenatal massage" or "sports massage" or "cupping therapy." Create separate ad groups for each service with dedicated keywords and landing pages. Each new service opens a new pool of searchers.

**When it's time to kill:** If you've spent $500 and gotten zero bookings, stop. Something is fundamentally wrong. Either your keywords are wrong, your landing page doesn't work, or your pricing is too high for what people expect. Don't throw more money at a broken campaign. Start over.

A therapist in Nashville spent $800 over two months on "pregnancy massage Nashville." Zero bookings. When I looked at her ads, she was targeting "pregnancy massage" but her landing page talked about "deep tissue for back pain." The mismatch was killing her. We rewrote the ad and the landing page around prenatal massage specifically. She started getting 3–4 bookings per week on the same budget.

If your campaign isn't working after $500, it's not a budget problem. It's a strategy problem. Fix the strategy, then increase the budget.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I budget for Google Ads as a massage therapist?**

Start with $500/month. That's enough to get meaningful data—about 100–150 clicks in most cities—without risking real money. If your cost per lead is under $50 and you're booking 10+ clients per month, increase it to $1,000. But don't start there. I've seen too many people burn $2,000 in two weeks and quit. Start small, prove the math, then scale.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads myself, or do I need to hire someone?**

You can run them yourself if you're willing to learn. Google's interface is not intuitive, but it's learnable. I'd budget 5–7 hours per week to set up, monitor, and tweak a campaign. If that sounds like time you don't have, hire someone. But don't hire an agency that promises "set it and forget it." Google Ads needs attention. Keywords change. Competitors change. Your schedule changes. Someone needs to watch it.

**Q: What if I'm in a small town with low search volume?**

This is where Google Ads gets tricky. If only 50 people per month search for "massage therapist [your town]," even a perfect campaign won't get you many leads. In that case, I'd shift budget to local SEO (Google Business Profile), Yelp, and word-of-mouth programs. Google Ads works best when there's enough search volume to justify the spend. If there isn't, don't force it.

**Q: How do I know if someone actually booked because of my ad?**

Set up call tracking. Google Ads has a feature called "call reporting" that uses a forwarding number. When someone clicks your ad and calls that number, Google records it as a conversion. For online bookings, put a conversion tracking tag on your "thank you" page. Without tracking, you're guessing. And guessing costs money.

**Q: What should I put in my ad text?**

Lead with the problem, not your credentials. "Lower back pain? Get relief today. Book a deep tissue massage in Austin. $20 off your first session." That works better than "Licensed massage therapist with 10 years of experience." People book massage because they have pain or stress, not because they're impressed by your resume. Mention the problem, the solution, and a reason to book now.

**Q: Is Google Ads better than Yelp or Booksy for getting clients?**

They serve different purposes. Google Ads catches people actively searching—people who want a massage today or this week. Yelp is better for people who are comparing options and will book later. Booksy is a booking tool, not a lead source. Use Google Ads for immediate leads, Yelp and Google Business Profile for discoverability, and Booksy or Square Appointments for your booking system. They're not competing—they work together.

**Q: How long until I see results?**

You'll see clicks within hours of launching. But bookings usually take 3–7 days. People search, click, think about it, and book later. Give it two weeks before you decide if it's working. If you have zero bookings after 14 days and 100+ clicks, something is broken.
