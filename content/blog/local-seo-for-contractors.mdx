## Google Business Profile: Your First SEO Win

Homeowners type queries like *"plumber near me"* into Google when they need help. For contractors, Google Business Profile (GBP) is the fastest way to dominate these searches. A complete GBP listing shows up in both search results and Google Maps, giving you 2x visibility.

Start with these steps:
- **Claim your profile**: Search for your business in Google and click "Own this business."
- **Add high-res photos**: Include before/after project shots and team photos.
- **Post updates weekly**: Share promotions, completed jobs, or team shoutouts.

Example: A roofing company in Austin, TX, increased their GBP clicks by 150% after adding 10+ project photos and live chat hours.

<Callout type="tip">Use [Google Business Profile optimization](/services/google-business-profile) services to automate updates and track performance.</Callout>

## Keyword Targeting for Contractors

Homeowners search using location + service. Target keywords like:
- *Electrician in San Francisco*
- *Affordable plumbing services near me*
- *Top-rated HVAC repair [City Name]*

Use these methods:
1. **Google Keyword Planner** for search volume
2. **AnswerThePublic** to find "how to" and "vs" queries
3. **Local directories** like Yelp and Angie’s List for review-based terms

Avoid broad terms like "plumber." Add location modifiers to steal traffic from big brands.

<Callout type="warning">Don’t ignore "near me" searches. 46% of Google searches are local, but only 20% of businesses optimize for them.</Callout>

## On-Page SEO for Local Visibility

Every page on your site needs local relevance. For a plumbing company in Chicago:
- **Title tags**: "Chicago Plumbing Services | Fast Emergency Repairs"
- **Meta descriptions**: "Fix leaks, clogs, and pipe issues in Chicago. 24/7 emergency plumber with 5-star reviews."
- **Local schema markup**: Add address and service area data to help Google understand your location.

Create city-specific pages if you serve multiple areas. A landscaping business covering Austin, Dallas, and Houston could build separate landing pages for each.

A painting contractor in Denver saw their search ranking jump 40% after getting 15+ backlinks from local business directories and blogs.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Using a P.O. Box or Virtual Address on Google Business Profile

A roofer in Nashville, TN, called me after his Google listing got suspended. He'd been using a UPS mailbox as his business address because he worked from a home office and didn't want clients showing up. Google flagged it within three months, his listing disappeared, and he lost an estimated $12,000 in booked jobs during the 6-week reinstatement process.

**What actually happened:** Google's algorithm cross-references your address with its database of commercial mail centers. A UPS Store, Regus, or WeWork address is an immediate red flag.

**The fix:** If you work from home, you can still list your home address. Google allows service-area businesses to hide their street address from the public. Go to your GBP dashboard → Info → "Clear address" under the address field. Then set your service radius (typically 30-50 miles for contractors). Your listing will still show up in maps for "plumber near me" searches—it just won't display your front door.

**The outcome:** That Nashville roofer reactivated his listing with his actual home address hidden. Within 60 days, his call volume returned to previous levels. He added a note in his profile: "I'll text you my location before arrival." Six months later, he had 47 new booked jobs directly attributed to his GBP listing.

**One more thing:** If you're worried about safety, get a separate phone number for the business (Google Voice or RingCentral work fine) and use that on your profile. Never list your personal cell.

### Mistake #2: Stuffing Keywords into Your Business Name

A plumber in Denver renamed his business to "AAA Denver Emergency Plumbing & Drain Cleaning Service 24/7." He thought adding every keyword would help him rank for everything. Instead, Google flagged his profile for "keyword stuffing" (violating their spam policy), and his ranking dropped from #3 to page 4. He spent two months climbing back.

**The real cost:** He told me he was spending $1,800/month on Google Ads to compensate for the lost organic traffic. After fixing the name and getting his ranking back, he cut that ad spend to $600/month.

**What to do instead:** Use your actual registered business name. If your legal name is "Smith Plumbing LLC," that's what goes on your profile. You can include one main service keyword after your name in the "description" field, but not in the name field.

For example:
- Wrong: Denver's Best Roofer Roof Installation Repair
- Right: Mile High Roofing LLC

**Why this works:** Google's algorithm understands context. A profile with a clean name and a thorough description (mentioning "roof installation," "roof repair," and "Denver area") will outrank a spammy name every time. I've tested this with three different clients. Clean names convert better too—homeowners trust "Oak Street Plumbing" more than "Affordable Plumbing Drain Sewer Water Heater."

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Negative Reviews (or Responding Defensively)

A general contractor in Austin, TX, had 4.2 stars across 38 reviews. One homeowner posted a 1-star review about a delayed bathroom renovation. The contractor replied: "This customer is unreasonable. We finished on time and she changed her mind about tile. Don't believe everything you read."

That reply sat there for 18 months. Every time someone researched him, they saw a defensive owner arguing with a client. His quote-to-close rate dropped from 35% to 22% during that period—directly costing him roughly $3,800/month in lost revenue based on his average job size of $4,200.

**The fix:** Respond to every review—good and bad—within 48 hours. For negative reviews, follow this structure:
1. Thank them for the feedback
2. Apologize for their specific experience
3. Offer a fix in private (never negotiate in public comments)

His response after the fix: "Hi Sarah, thank you for sharing your experience. I'm sorry the timeline didn't meet your expectations for the bathroom project. I'd like to make this right—please email me directly at [email] so we can discuss a solution."

**The outcome:** He kept the original review but added 12 more positive reviews over the next 4 months. His average rating stayed at 4.0. More importantly, his close rate returned to 34%. He told me the only metric that changed was how he handled the reply.

### Mistake #4: Buying Reviews

I'm putting this one last because it's the most dangerous. A landscaper in Portland, OR, paid a service $500 for "verified Google reviews." The reviews were from fake accounts with generic names like "Jennifer R." that had no review history. Google caught it within three weeks. He got a permanent suspension on his GBP listing—no reinstatement process, no appeal.

**The cost:**
- Lost listing (which drove 60% of his new leads)
- $0 refund from the review service (they disappeared)
- Estimated $24,000 in lost revenue over the next 8 months before he built a new listing from scratch

**What actually works:** Ask real customers in person or via email. A simple link works: "If you're happy with how the kitchen turned out, would you mind leaving a quick review?" Use Google's own free review link generator in your GBP dashboard. Send it the same day you finish the job, while the result is still fresh.

**Better approach:** Set up an automated text 24 hours after job completion. "Hey [Name], we finished your [service] yesterday. We'd love your feedback: [link]. If anything isn't perfect, please text me directly at [number] so I can fix it first." This catches issues before they become 1-star reviews and catches happy customers when they're most likely to leave 5 stars.

## Why Reviews Matter More Than You Think

Let me put a number on this. A study from BrightLocal (the people who actually track this data every year) found that 74% of homeowners check reviews before hiring a contractor. That's 3 out of 4 potential customers.

But here's what most guides skip: **the quantity matters almost as much as the rating.**

A contractor with 10 reviews at 5.0 stars will lose to a contractor with 50 reviews at 4.5 stars. Google's algorithm weighs both the number of reviews and the recency. A business with 5 reviews from 2021 looks dormant. A business with 3 new reviews this month looks active and trusted.

**The target numbers:**
- Minimum 25 reviews before you'll see any meaningful ranking boost
- 3-5 new reviews per month to maintain momentum
- Respond to every single one, even the 5-stars (a simple "Thanks, glad you're happy with the deck!")

**One tool that helps:** Use a service like Podium or Birdeye to send automated review requests via text. They cost around $200-400/month, but I've seen them generate 8-12 additional reviews per month for contractors who were previously getting 1-2. At that rate, the ROI is obvious.

## Local Service Ads: Google's Paid Shortcut

Google Business Profile is free. Local Service Ads (LSAs) cost money but come with the "Google Guaranteed" badge. For contractors, this is often worth the price.

**How they work:** LSAs appear at the very top of search results, above the map pack and above regular ads. You pay per lead (phone call or message), not per click. Google screens your business and gives you that green "Google Guaranteed" checkmark.

**The catch:** LSAs aren't available for every trade or every city. You need to check if your category and location are supported. As of 2024, they're most common for plumbers, electricians, HVAC, roofers, and landscapers in major metro areas.

**Real numbers from a client:**
An electrician in Chicago spent $900/month on LSAs for 4 months. He received 47 leads from them. Of those, 22 turned into jobs. His average job value was $850. That's $18,700 in revenue from a $3,600 spend. His conversion rate on LSA leads (47%) was significantly higher than his Google Ads conversion rate (28%).

**Why LSAs convert better:** Homeowners see the "Google Guaranteed" badge and feel safer calling. They also know you've been vetted—Google checks your license, insurance, and background before approving you.

**The downside:** You can't control which keywords trigger your ad. Google matches you to searches it thinks are relevant. I've had clients get charged for leads that were outside their service area or for services they don't offer. You can dispute these charges through the LSA dashboard, but it takes time.

**My advice:** Start with a $300-500/month budget for LSAs if you're in a supported area. Monitor the lead quality for 60 days. If you're getting 3+ leads per week and converting over 30%, scale up. If not, redirect that money to local SEO and review generation.

## Using Your Website to Convert Local Traffic

Your GBP listing gets people to your business. Your website closes the deal. Most contractor websites are terrible at this.

**Common problems I see:**
- No phone number visible without scrolling
- Generic stock photos (that family smiling in a bathroom? I've seen that photo on 12 different contractor sites)
- No before/after photos specific to your recent projects
- No clear call-to-action on the homepage

**One change that moved the needle:**
A roofer in Charlotte, NC, had a website built by his cousin in 2019. It had a flashy intro video and 14 menu items. His bounce rate was 76%. I helped him strip it down: a headline ("Charlotte Roof Repair & Replacement | Free Estimates"), his phone number in the top right corner, 6 before/after photos from jobs he'd completed in the last 90 days, and one button: "Get Your Free Estimate."

Within 30 days, his bounce rate dropped to 52%. His form submissions went from 8/month to 23/month. He didn't change anything else—not his ad spend, not his GBP listing, not his pricing.

**The specific elements that work for contractors:**
1. Above-the-fold phone number (bigger font than you think)
2. Service area in the headline ("Serving Austin and Surrounding Counties")
3. Real photos (your jobs, your truck, your team—not a stock photo from a database)
4. Third-party review snippets (screenshots from Google or Yelp, not testimonials you wrote)
5. Quick load time (Google penalizes slow sites, and homeowners on mobile will bounce under 3 seconds)

**A tool worth testing:** Use Google Optimize (free) or a simple A/B test with your website platform. Test your current headline against something direct. For a plumber, test: "Plumbing Services in Denver" vs. "Pipe Burst? We're There in 60 Minutes." The specific, urgent headline often wins.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it take to see results from local SEO?**

Six to twelve weeks, minimum. If someone promises you page one rankings in two weeks, they're either lying or using black-hat tactics that will get you banned. Real local SEO takes time because Google has to crawl your optimized profile, index your reviews, and trust your location data. I've seen businesses wait 8 weeks for their first ranking movement, then jump from page 3 to page 1 in 10 days. The key is consistency—don't pause your efforts after 4 weeks.

**Q: Can't I just pay Google for ads instead of doing SEO?**

You can, but you shouldn't. I recommend a hybrid approach. SEO is the foundation—it's free traffic that compounds over time. Ads are the accelerator—they get you immediate leads while your SEO builds. If you only do ads, you're renting your traffic. If Google increases ad costs or your budget runs out, you disappear. SEO takes longer but becomes an asset you own.

**Q: Do I need a separate website for each city I serve?**

No, and I'd argue against it. One site with individual service pages for each city works better. Create a page like "roofing-denver.html" and "roofing-boulder.html" on the same domain. Each page should have unique content, local photos, and local testimonials. Google sees a single domain as more authoritative than multiple small domains. I've helped a painter in Oregon cover 4 cities with one site, and he ranks for "painter [city name]" in all four.

**Q: My competitor has 100 reviews and I have 12. Am I hopeless?**

No. Review quantity matters, but recency matters more. If your competitor's last review was 14 months ago and you're getting 3-4 new reviews per month, you'll eventually outrank them. Google wants to show active businesses. Focus on generating new reviews consistently. Aim for 3 per month minimum, and make sure you're responding to every single one.

**Q: Should I build links to my site for local SEO?**

Only if they're local and relevant. A link from the local Chamber of Commerce website or a local news article about a project you completed is valuable. A link from a random blog about "top plumbing tips" in another state is worthless. Don't waste money on link-building services that promise 50 links for $200. Those are usually from spammy directories and can hurt your ranking.

**Q: What do I do if a competitor leaves fake bad reviews on my profile?**

Report them to Google. Go to the review, click the three dots, and select "Flag as inappropriate." Choose "Conflict of interest" and explain that you believe it's a competitor. Google does investigate these, though it can take 2-3 weeks. In the meantime, respond to the review professionally: "We don't have a record of service for this name. Please contact us directly so we can sort out the confusion." This shows future customers that you're handling it gracefully.
