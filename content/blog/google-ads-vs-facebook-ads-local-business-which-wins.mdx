Google Ads costs $1.50 per click in 2026, while Meta’s $8.20 per click makes it harder to justify for low-margin businesses. But here’s the catch: Google’s cost rises when competing with chains, while Meta’s budget can swing wildly based on trending hashtags.

**Example:** A 5-chair hair salon in Toronto spent $300 on Google Ads for "emergency haircuts near me" and got 22 walk-ins. The same budget on Meta only brought 8 appointments — but 5 came from 20+ miles away, adding $100 in wasted spend.

**Example:** A fitness studio in Denver used Meta to target "women aged 25–35 who liked plant-based recipes" and saw a 12% conversion rate for a prenatal yoga trial.

But here’s the catch: Meta’s local targeting is only as good as your data. If your business has under 100 customers, Google’s automated location-based ads will outperform.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Facebook-Only with No Location Targeting (Austin Coffee Shop)

A three-location coffee roaster in Austin spent $1,800/month on Facebook Ads targeting “coffee drinkers” within 50 miles. After three months, they had 14,000 link clicks and maybe 60 new customers. The owner told me, “I got likes from people in San Antonio. That’s two hours away. No one drives two hours for a latte.”

**What went wrong:** They didn’t set a hard radius. Facebook defaults to “optimize for reach,” which means it shows your ad to whoever it can find cheapest—often people nowhere near you. On top of that, they ran a single image ad of their espresso machine, which looks great on Instagram but doesn’t trigger purchase intent.

**The fix:** We rebuilt their account from scratch. Switched to Google Ads with location targeting set to a 3-mile radius around each cafe. Added “coffee near me” and “best espresso in Austin” keywords. Budget: $500/month total. Facebook stayed at $300/month but with a 5-mile radius and a “show your receipt for 20% off” offer that actually drove foot traffic.

**Outcome:** First month on Google: 48 tracked visits from ad clicks, average ticket $9. That’s $432 in directly attributable revenue on $500 spend. Facebook dropped to 18 visits, but those 18 people brought friends because of the receipt offer. Combined monthly revenue from ads went from roughly $540 (undercounted) to $1,260 (tracked). They cut total ad spend by $1,000/month and saw a 133% increase in tracked revenue.

### Mistake #2: Running Both Platforms with the Same Budget Split (Portland Hair Salon)

A five-chair salon in Portland’s Hawthorne district put $600 into Google Ads and $600 into Facebook Ads every month for eight months. The owner assumed since both platforms charge per click, they should spend the same. She told me, “I figured if one didn’t work, I’d know. But both seemed fine, so I kept it.”

**What went wrong:** She wasn’t looking at the right metric. Google Ads was giving her clicks from people already searching for “balayage Portland Portland” or “haircut near me.” Those people showed up within 24 hours. Facebook was giving her clicks—but mostly from people who clicked because the picture of a balayage looked nice, then forgot about it. Her conversion rate was 2.1% on Google and 0.4% on Facebook. But since both campaigns had similar click-through rates, she assumed they performed equally.

**The fix:** I gave her a spreadsheet that tracked cost-per-booking instead of cost-per-click. After three weeks of data, we saw Google cost-per-booking was $12. Facebook was $47. We dropped Facebook to $200/month (strictly for retargeting people who visited her site) and moved the remaining $1,000 to Google. Added call extensions and a booking link that went directly to her Booksy page.

**Outcome:** Bookings increased from an average of 28 per month to 54 per month over the next quarter. Monthly ad spend stayed the same ($1,200). Her cost-per-booking dropped from $42 to $22. The owner said she “felt stupid for not looking at this sooner.” I told her most agency people miss it too—they’re paid on ad spend, not bookings.

### Mistake #3: No Offline Conversion Tracking (Chicago Pet Groomer)

A pet grooming business in Logan Square, Chicago, spent $900/month on Google Ads for “dog grooming near me” and “cat grooming Chicago.” Their dashboard showed 0 conversions. The owner was about to cancel when I asked, “How do people book?” Answer: Phone calls and walk-ins. Their ad was designed to drive phone calls and store visits, but they had zero call tracking set up.

**What went wrong:** Google Ads counts a conversion only if someone clicks a specific button or fills a form. If your business runs on inbound calls, you need call tracking. This groomer was paying for clicks that led to actual appointments, but Google was recording them as “bounced” because nobody clicked a button.

**The fix:** Implemented a simple free Google forwarding number for one week, then upgraded to a $29/month call tracking tool (CallRail). Created a Google Ads conversion action for phone calls over 60 seconds. Also set up offline conversion tracking by importing appointment data from their Square booking system.

**Outcome:** In week one, they tracked 17 inbound calls from ads. Sixteen turned into bookings (94% call-to-booking rate). Average booking value: $65. That’s $1,040 in revenue from $225 in ad spend that week—a 362% return on ad spend (ROAS). The owner went from “these ads don’t work” to “how do I double my budget?” The hard cost: $29 for the call tracking tool and one hour of setup.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Peak Demand Windows (Nashville Fitness Studio)

A boutique fitness studio in Nashville’s 12South neighborhood ran Google Ads for “yoga classes Nashville” and “HIIT classes near me” at the same bid level, 24/7, 365 days a year. They spent $1,500/month. In December and January, they got flooded with leads but couldn’t service the calls fast enough. In August, they got crickets.

**What went wrong:** They didn’t use ad scheduling or bid adjustments. The owner was paying the same cost-per-click at 2 AM on a Tuesday (when nobody searches for fitness classes) as she did at 5 PM on a Monday (when everyone does). Worse, during January’s New Year’s rush, a ton of competitors bid up keywords, so her $1.50 cap clicks turned into $4.50 clicks, and she ran out of budget by the 15th of the month.

**The fix:** Built a schedule that increased bids by 40% from 4 PM to 8 PM Monday through Thursday (peak booking time). Added a 50% bid increase for January and February. Lowered bids to $0.30 off-peak hours (midnight to 6 AM) and added negative keywords like “youtube yoga” and “free HIIT workouts.” Created separate ad groups for different services (yoga, HIIT, barre) with dedicated budgets.

**Outcome:** Within 60 days, cost-per-lead dropped from $38 to $19. January budget lasted the full month for the first time in three years. Total leads increased by 28% without spending an extra dollar. The studio owner said, “So you're telling me I was paying $4.50 for a 2 AM click that went to a sleeping person's phone?” Yes. That’s exactly what was happening.

## When Facebook Ads Actually Beat Google (It's Rare, But It Happens)

I’m not anti-Facebook. I’ve just seen too many small business owners pour money into Meta because they think search ads are complicated. But there are three scenarios where Facebook Ads will outperform Google Ads for a local business.

**Scenario 1: You sell something visually irresistible.** If you own a bakery that makes croissants that look like they’re glowing, or a boutique that sells handmade leather bags, Facebook’s image-heavy format works. People don’t search for “artisan croissant near me” unless they already know what they want. Facebook lets you show them the product and create the desire. A bakery in Williamsburg, Brooklyn, spent $400 on a Facebook campaign showing a single high-res shot of a raspberry danish. They had 22 pre-orders within 48 hours, average order $28. That’s $616 in revenue—154% ROAS in two days. Google couldn't have done that.

**Scenario 2: You run time-bound events or promotions.** If you’re hosting a live music night at your coffee shop or offering a 24-hour flash discount on haircuts, Facebook’s reach is faster than Google’s. Google can take a day or two to ramp up. Facebook can start driving traffic within an hour. A Nashville bar ran a $250 Facebook campaign for a Thursday night trivia event. The ad went live at 10 AM, and by 4 PM they had 60 RSVPs via the event page. Google Ads would have required someone to search “trivia night Nashville Thursday”—and most attendees didn’t.

**Scenario 3: Your service is purely local and highly seasonal.** Think Christmas tree lots, pumpkin patches, or Fourth of July fireworks stands. Facebook lets you target by zip code and interest (e.g., “Christmas” or “holiday traditions”), while Google might not have enough search volume to justify a campaign. A Christmas tree lot in Denver ran Facebook Ads targeting parents within 5 miles who liked “Christmas” or “holiday decorating.” $300 turned into 54 tree sales at $65 each—$3,510 in revenue. Google Ads for “Christmas tree Denver” couldn't get that volume in the same timeframe.

The catch: Facebook’s cost-per-click in 2026 is $8.20 on average. You cannot compete there unless your product has high margins or you’re running short, high-urgency campaigns. If your average ticket is under $30, stay on Google.

## How to Track the Stuff That Actually Matters

Most small business owners track clicks and impressions. Those are vanity metrics. Clicks don’t pay your rent. Walk-ins and phone calls do.

**If you’re a coffee shop or restaurant:** Use Square’s marketing dashboard to connect ad spend to sales. Square’s Ads Manager app lets you link Google Ads conversions to specific menu items sold. A diner in Portland used this and discovered that people who clicked their “pancakes near me” ad spent 27% more than average diners because they added coffee and sides. That data changed their bid strategy—they started bidding 20% higher on pancake keywords because the return justified it.

**If you’re a salon or barber:** Booksy and Vagaro both have built-in ad integrations with Google and Facebook. You can track exactly how many bookings came from each ad. Set up a unique phone number for each platform using CallRail or Google’s free call forwarding. A Chicago barber found that 60% of his Google Ads calls came from people searching “barber open now” between 6 PM and 8 PM. He increased bids during that window and saw a 40% lift in bookings.

**If you’re a fitness studio:** Use Mindbody’s integration with Google Ads to track class sign-ups. Set up a free Google tag to track “add to cart” events on class packages. A yoga studio in Denver used this to identify that people who clicked an ad for “hot yoga Denver” were 3x more likely to purchase a 10-class pack than people who clicked an ad for “yoga for beginners.” They paused the beginner ad and allocated all budget to hot yoga. Monthly revenue jumped from $2,100 to $4,400.

**If you’re a pet groomer or service business:** Use online booking platforms that generate unique URLs for each ad campaign. Most platforms (MoeGo, PetExec, Gingr) let you create a custom link like `yourname.com/google-ads` vs `yourname.com/facebook-ads`. Track those separately in your CRM. A dog groomer in Nashville discovered that 85% of her Facebook leads never showed up for their appointment—they just booked online. Meanwhile, 95% of her Google Ads leads showed. She moved all budget to Google. No brainer.

The uncomfortable truth: Most small businesses spend 90% of their time creating ads and 10% of their time tracking results. It should be the opposite. The ad craft matters, but the tracking matters more. You can have a mediocre ad with great tracking and make money. You can have a perfect ad with no tracking and go broke.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I run Google Ads without hiring someone?**
Yes, but only if you’re willing to spend 30 minutes per week checking search term reports, adding negative keywords, and adjusting bids. I’ve seen business owners set it and forget it, then come back three months later to find they spent $1,500 on clicks from people searching for “free dog grooming guides.” Google does not care about your budget. You have to babysit it. If you can’t spare 30 minutes a week, hire someone or stick to Facebook, which requires less maintenance.

**Q: Should I run both platforms at the same time if I only have $500/month?**
No. With $500, you need to pick one platform and maximize it. Running both spreads your budget so thin that neither gets enough data to optimize. Start with Google if you have search intent (people looking for what you sell). Start with Facebook if you have a visually distinctive product or a time-bound event. Once one platform shows consistent positive ROAS (at least 200%), then consider adding the other.

**Q: What’s the minimum budget for Google Ads to get meaningful data?**
For a local service business, I wouldn’t start below $300/month. Below that, Google won’t show your ad frequently enough to accumulate data. You’ll get maybe 30 clicks in a month, and you won’t know if those clicks came from bad targeting or bad luck. $300/month gives you about 200 clicks at $1.50 CPC. That’s enough to see a trend after 30 days. If you can’t commit $300, save your money for three months and run a $900 campaign.

**Q: How do I know if my ads are actually driving foot traffic, not just clicks?**
Three ways. One: set up location extensions on Google Ads. Two: install a free WiFi analytics tool like Zenreach or Euclid that tracks when people who clicked your ad walk into your store. Three: ask customers how they heard about you and log it in your POS system. Most clients I’ve worked with are surprised to learn their Facebook ads drove 10 clicks but zero foot traffic, while their Google Ads drove 5 clicks and 4 walk-ins. You need offline data to tell the difference.

**Q: Are Yelp Ads worth the money for local businesses?**
Rarely, but sometimes. Yelp Ads work for salons, barbers, and restaurants because Yelp users are already in a purchase-intent mindset. The cost-per-lead is often $25–$40. Compare that to Google’s $12–$22. For a coffee shop or pet groomer, I’d skip Yelp. For a high-ticket service (wedding photographer, dentist, cosmetic surgeon), Yelp could make sense because your margins are bigger. But always test with $100 first. If you don’t get at least three booked appointments in two weeks, pause it.

**Q: I tried Google Ads before and wasted money. What should I do differently?**
The most common reason small businesses waste money on Google Ads is bad keyword selection. They bid on broad terms like “coffee shop” instead of “organic coffee near me” or “espresso [neighborhood].” This gets them clicks from people who are browsing, not buying. Start with exact match keywords only. Use the "skag" structure (single keyword ad groups) so you control exactly which search query triggers your ad. And for God’s sake, add negative keywords. Add “free,” “how to,” “jobs,” “salon” (if you’re a barber), “DIY,” and “reviews.” Those five saved one of my clients $400 in one month.
