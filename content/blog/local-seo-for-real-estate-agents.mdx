Your Google Business Profile is the first thing potential clients will see when searching for you online. Claim and optimize it by:

* Adding high-quality photos and videos
* Responding promptly to reviews and inquiries

This will help you appear in Google's local search results and increase your visibility in local property searches.

**Step 2: Build High-Quality Backlinks**

Backlinks from reputable sources help improve your website's authority and ranking in search engines. Focus on building high-quality backlinks by:

* Partnering with local businesses and organizations
* Creating informative and engaging content
* Leveraging social media to promote your content

**Step 3: Optimize Your Website for Local Keywords**

Your website should be optimized for local keywords that potential clients are searching for. This includes:

* Using location-based keywords (e.g., "Los Angeles real estate")
* Creating location-specific content (e.g., "Best neighborhoods in LA")
* Optimizing your website's title tags, meta descriptions, and headings

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Relying on the MLS Feed for Everything

I worked with a team in Austin, Texas — three agents sharing a website, all listings pulled automatically from the MLS. Sounded efficient. Looked like every other real estate site in town.

Here's what happened: Google couldn't tell their site apart from the other 47 agencies using the same template. Their pages had thin descriptions like "3 bed, 2 bath, great location" — and they wondered why they ranked below Zillow, Redfin, and three other local brokerages.

**The fix:** We stripped out 80% of the automated MLS content and wrote custom neighborhood pages. Each page answered a specific question a buyer in Austin would actually search for: "What's the commute from Bouldin Creek to downtown during SXSW?", "Which Austin elementary schools have the best ratings near 78704?", "How much do HOA fees actually cost in Circle C?"

Three months later, their organic traffic from local searches went from 142 visitors/month to 607. One of those visitors closed on a $675,000 home in Zilker. The agent's commission on that single deal: roughly $20,000.

**Cost of the fix:** About 14 hours of writing. No ongoing ad spend.

**The uncomfortable truth:** Real estate websites are the most poorly optimized sites I see across any industry. You're competing against Zillow's $300 million ad budget. Automated MLS data will never win. Unique, human-written content about your actual neighborhood will.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Google Business Profile Questions & Answers

An agent in Portland, Oregon told me she was "doing great" on Google Business Profile. She had photos, regular posts, responded to reviews. Her analytics showed 2,300 profile views in the previous month.

What she didn't know: Her profile had 14 unanswered questions from potential clients. Someone asked "Do you work with first-time buyers who have FHA loans?" — unanswered for six months. Someone asked "Are any of these properties pet-friendly?" — no response. Someone asked "What's your commission structure?" — crickets.

Google tracks how quickly and completely you respond to Q&A. It's a ranking signal. More importantly, those unanswered questions tell searchers you don't care enough to answer.

**The fix:** I spent 90 minutes with her answering every existing question. Then we set up email alerts so she'd know when new questions came in. We also pre-answered 20 common questions (how long do showings take, do you offer virtual tours, what areas do you cover, etc.).

**The outcome:** In six weeks, her Google Business Profile moved from position 6 to position 2 for "real estate agent Portland Oregon." She estimated that cost her nothing but gained her 4-5 new leads per week that used to go to competitors.

**What this cost her before the fix:** Hard to quantify, but 14 unanswered questions directly resulted in those people clicking the next agent. If each of those 14 people represented a potential commission of $9,000 (median Portland home price ~$500k, 3% split) — that's $126,000 in potential commission that never called back.

### Mistake #3: Using a Generic Virtual Office Address for Local Rankings

An agent in Nashville wanted to rank for "real estate agent Nashville TN" but lived 20 miles out in a suburb. She used a virtual office address downtown that she rented for $99/month. Google flagged it as a virtual office. Her profile got suspended.

I've seen this exact situation kill rankings at three different clients across different industries. Google checks. They verify by sending postcards. They compare your address against business license databases. They look for signs of shared spaces.

**What happened:** She lost her Google Business Profile for 8 weeks during the appeal process. During that time, her organic traffic dropped 73%. She spent $2,800 on Google Ads trying to make up for it, but conversion rates were terrible because people want to see a verified profile before trusting you with their largest financial transaction.

**The fix:** We updated her profile to her actual home address and used the "service area business" setting that hides the street address but shows the broader region (Nashville, Davidson County, etc.). This is completely legitimate for agents who meet clients at properties rather than an office.

**The outcome:** Reinstated in 11 days. Traffic recovered in 3 weeks. She canceled the Google Ads spend. She's been ranking in the top 3 for her target terms for 14 months now, spending exactly $0/month on that traffic.

## Local Link Building Strategies That Actually Work (For Non-Techies)

Every SEO guide tells you to "build local backlinks." They rarely tell you how a busy agent with no technical skills actually does that.

Here's what I've seen work with real estate clients in Denver, Chicago, and Charlotte.

### The Sponsorship Swap

Most agents already sponsor local sports teams, school events, and charity galas. They write a check, get their logo on a banner, and call it a day. They're leaving a link on the table.

Next time you write that sponsorship check, add one sentence to the conversation: "Also, would you mind linking to my website from your sponsors page? I'll write a testimonial about your event."

I did this with an agent in Denver who sponsored a youth soccer league. The league's sponsors page had a "no-follow" link policy. We asked if they'd make an exception, or if the agent could write a blog post on their own site about why they support youth sports in Denver, and the league could link to that post. They agreed.

**Result:** One link from a .org domain with real local authority. His site moved from position 7 to position 4 for "Denver real estate agent" over the next 4 months. Not dramatic, but that one link was free, took 30 minutes to get, and the post about youth sports still drives traffic today.

### The "Neighbor Interview" Strategy

Most agents don't realize they're sitting on a link goldmine: every transaction they close involves people who have real businesses or jobs.

I had a client in Chicago who closed a deal for a couple that ran a popular taco spot in Logan Square. We reached out and asked if we could do a short interview with them about "the best tacos in Logan Square for under $10" and post it on the agent's blog. The restaurant linked to the post from their site. The post got shared 47 times on local Facebook groups.

**Why this works:** Local businesses love free press. Most of them have websites with real domain authority. They'll happily link to you if you feature them. And the content is actually useful to people searching for things like "best tacos Logan Square" — which is a search term that might bring in someone who's house-hunting in that exact neighborhood.

**Cost:** 2 hours of interviewing and writing. The restaurant linked back. That page now ranks #3 for "Logan Square food guide" and has brought in 3 leads who contacted the agent because they saw the post.

### The Realtor Association Roundup

Your local realtor association likely has a website with real authority. I've found that many associations will publish member-written content if you ask. One agent in Charlotte wrote a 600-word post titled "What First-Time Home Buyers in Charlotte Get Wrong About Inspection Contingencies" and got it published on the local association blog with a link back to his site.

That link is from a .org domain with a DR (domain rating) of 52. Compare that to a typical local real estate site with a DR of 15-25. One link from the association is worth roughly the same as 10-15 links from random local directories.

**My honest advice:** Most link building advice for local SEO is terrible. Don't bother with directory submissions. Don't pay for links. Don't do link exchanges with other agents. Do these three things instead. They cost time, not money. They produce real results that don't get penalized.

## Reviews: The Single Local SEO Factor Most Agents Get Wrong

I asked an agent in NYC what she does about reviews. She said: "I send a follow-up email asking for a review after closing."

That's common. It's also not enough.

Google's local algorithm doesn't just count how many reviews you have. It considers:

- How recent they are (a review from 2022 tells Google nothing about your current service)
- How diverse they are (all 5-star reviews look fake)
- Whether you respond (and how you respond)
- Keywords in reviews (if nobody mentions "Brooklyn" or "condo" or "first-time buyer," Google has less signal about what you actually do)

### What Actually Works

I tested this with a fitness studio client in Austin (not real estate, but the same principles apply). They had 87 reviews, average 4.8 stars. Traffic was flat.

**What we changed:**

- Asked every client for a review at the point of highest satisfaction (for agents, this is usually the week after closing, not 6 weeks later when you remember)
- Used a tool called Widewail to automate follow-up requests via email and SMS (cost: $300/month, worth it if you close more than 2 deals/month)
- Started responding to every single review, positive or negative, within 72 hours
- Asked clients to mention specific things: neighborhood, property type, what they appreciated

**Results over 6 months:**

- 43 new reviews (vs. 12 in the previous 6 months)
- Average rating stayed at 4.8
- "First-time buyer" appeared in 11 reviews, "Harlem" in 8, "condo" in 14
- Their Google Business Profile moved from position 5 to position 2 for "real estate agent NYC"

### What About Negative Reviews?

A negative review is not the end of your business. I've seen agents panic and try to get them removed (usually impossible unless they violate Google's policies). Here's what actually works:

**Respond within 24 hours.** Apologize specifically for what went wrong ("I'm sorry the closing timeline was longer than expected"). Don't make excuses. Offer to make it right offline.

**The math:** One negative review among 40 positive ones is a 2.5% complaint rate. In any service business, that's normal. Most buyers will read the review, read your response, and judge you on how professionally you handled it. A thoughtful response to a negative review often builds more trust than 10 five-star reviews with no response.

### The Dollar Impact

Let me give you a real number. An agent in Chicago who implemented this review system closed 4 additional deals in 2023 that she directly attributed to people choosing her over competitors with similar profiles but fewer recent, keyword-rich reviews. Average commission: $12,000. Total: $48,000 in revenue from a system that cost her about $300/month and 2 hours per week of response time.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a website, or can I just use my Zillow/Realtor.com profile?**

You need a website if you want to control your own ranking. Zillow owns your Zillow profile. They can change the algorithm tomorrow, bury your listings, or push their own agent network over you. I've seen agents lose 70% of their Zillow-sourced traffic in a single algorithm update. A website is the only digital asset you actually own. You can build a solid one for $500-$1,000. You don't need a $5,000 custom build. Just make sure it loads fast and has unique local content.

**Q: How long does local SEO actually take to work?**

If you do everything right, you'll see movement in 6-10 weeks for lower-competition terms. For competitive terms like "real estate agent Austin TX," plan on 4-6 months before you hit page one. Anyone who promises faster is selling you something that will get your site penalized. I've never seen instant results that lasted.

**Q: Do I need to post on Google Business Profile every day?**

No. Twice a week is enough. But consistency matters more than frequency. If you post three times in one week then nothing for a month, you look inactive. I schedule 2 posts per week on Tuesdays and Thursdays. One is always a new listing or just-sold. The other is a neighborhood tip or local event. Takes about 15 minutes per week.

**Q: What about Yelp? Is it worth my time?**

For real estate, Yelp's traffic is smaller than Google but the conversion rates are higher. People who search "real estate agent" on Yelp are serious. Claim your profile, make sure your address and phone number match Google exactly, and respond to reviews. That's it. Don't pay Yelp for advertising until you've maxed out free traffic from Google first.

**Q: I got a Google Business Profile suspension. What do I do?**

Breathe. Most suspensions happen because of address issues (virtual office, P.O. box, shared space). Check that your address matches your business license and that you're using the "service area business" setting if you don't have a public office. Submit reinstatement through Google's form. Include photos of your actual workspace, business license, and utility bill. Average reinstatement time is 2-4 weeks. Do not create a new profile while waiting — that makes everything worse.

**Q: Should I use Google Ads for real estate?**

Only if you have a specific ROI requirement. I've seen agents burn $500/month for six months with nothing to show for it. The problem is that "real estate agent" clicks cost $5-$12 each in competitive markets. If you convert 1 out of 50 clicks into a consultation, and 1 out of 10 consultations into a listing, you're spending $2,500-$6,000 per client acquired through ads. That works if your average commission is $15,000, but it's tight. I'd only recommend Google Ads if you have a specific niche (luxury condos in one neighborhood, first-time buyer programs) where your targeting is tight enough that clicks are cheaper and conversion rates are higher.
