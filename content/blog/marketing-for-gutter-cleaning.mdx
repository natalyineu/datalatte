Gutter cleaning services need a solid marketing plan to stay ahead of the competition and maintain a steady flow of customers. Here are some actionable marketing strategies to help you fill your schedule every season:

### 1. Develop a Seasonal Marketing Calendar

Create a marketing calendar that aligns with the changing seasons. For gutter cleaning services, the peak season is usually spring and fall. Develop a content strategy that highlights the importance of gutter cleaning during these periods.

### 2. Leverage Local SEO

Optimize your website and online presence for local search engine optimization (SEO). This includes claiming and optimizing your Google My Business listing, creating location-specific content, and building high-quality backlinks from local sources.

### 3. Run Targeted Ads

Run targeted ads on Google Ads and social media platforms to reach homeowners in your area. Use location targeting and specific keywords to ensure your ads are seen by potential customers.

### 4. Offer Incentives and Discounts

Offer incentives and discounts to new and existing customers to encourage repeat business and referrals. This could include referral programs, loyalty rewards, or limited-time discounts.

### 5. Partner with Home Services Providers

Partner with other home services providers, such as lawn care or pressure washing services, to expand your reach and offer bundled services.

### 6. Utilize Email Marketing

Build an email list and send targeted campaigns to homeowners in your area. Use email marketing automation tools to personalize your messages and increase engagement.

Callout: **Warning**: Be cautious when using email marketing automation tools, as they can lead to spam complaints and negatively impact your email deliverability.

### 7. Invest in Social Media Management

Invest in social media management services to maintain a consistent online presence and engage with potential customers. Use social media listening tools to monitor conversations about gutter cleaning services and respond promptly to customer inquiries.

### 8. Monitor and Optimize

Monitor your marketing performance regularly and make data-driven decisions to optimize your strategy. Use analytics tools to track your website traffic, ad performance, and customer engagement.

Callout: **Tip**: Use Google My Business insights to track your website traffic, calls, and messages from your Google My Business listing.

### 9. Provide Exceptional Customer Service

Provide exceptional customer service to build trust and loyalty with your customers. Respond promptly to customer inquiries, and use customer feedback to improve your services.

Callout: **Example**: Consider offering a satisfaction guarantee or a maintenance program to demonstrate your commitment to customer satisfaction.

### 10. Stay Up-to-Date with Industry Trends

Stay up-to-date with the latest industry trends, technologies, and best practices. Attend workshops, conferences, and webinars to stay ahead of the competition and improve your marketing skills.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: The Door Hanger Delusion

Mike ran a gutter cleaning company in Austin, Texas. He printed 10,000 door hangers with a discount offer and hired two college kids to hang them on every door in three zip codes. Total investment: $2,400.

He got 12 calls. Gross revenue: $1,800. Net loss: $600 plus two weekends of his life.

Here's what Mike didn't know: door hangers work when you target homes with visible gutter problems — leaves poking out, sagging sections, rust stains. Blanket coverage in a neighborhood where most roofs were cleaned last month is just expensive litter.

The fix: Mike stopped buying door hangers and started running Google Local Services Ads — the ones with the green checkmark that say "Google Guaranteed." He targeted specific neighborhoods where homes had mature trees and hadn't had gutter service in 18+ months (data he got by cross-referencing his own customer list with property tax records for an afternoon).

**The outcome:** Mike spent $500 on Local Services Ads in one month and booked $3,800 in cleanings. He also learned that his door hangers were paying two kids to tell people his company exists, but the Google ads were finding people already searching for "gutter cleaning near me." Different intent, different results.

### Mistake 2: The Website That Sounded Like a Press Release

Sarah's gutter business in Nashville had a website that said things like "We provide comprehensive gutter maintenance solutions utilizing industry-leading practices." She paid a web designer $3,200 for that copy.

She wondered why leads were flat.

Here's what actually happened: homeowners searching for "gutter cleaning Nashville" landed on her site, read two sentences, and left. They didn't want a solution provider. They wanted a price, a time slot, and proof she wouldn't steal their lawn ornaments.

The fix: Sarah rewrote her homepage using real customer language. Instead of "comprehensive gutter maintenance," she wrote "We clean the gunk out of your gutters so water doesn't ruin your foundation. $150 flat rate. We show up on time or you get $50 off." She added photos of her actual crew (not stock photos), a calendar showing available same-week slots, and a text-to-book button.

**The outcome:** Her conversion rate went from 2% to 11% in six weeks. No new traffic — just better words. The $3,200 redesign was a waste. The $0 rewrite made her an extra $2,200 that fall.

### Mistake 3: The "Set It and Forget It" Ad Account

A gutter company in Chicago hired a marketing agency that set up one Google Ads campaign and never touched it again. The agency kept charging $1,500/month. The business owner assumed it was working because "the agency seemed professional."

Nine months later, they'd spent $13,500 on ads. The account was running the same $5 broad-match keywords for "gutter cleaning" that triggered ads for people in Milwaukee, Indianapolis, and Des Moines. Their actual phone calls? Seven. Total.

The fix: The owner fired the agency, took control of the Google Ads account, tightened the radius to 15 miles, switched to phrase-match keywords, added negative keywords (no "DIY," "free," "how to clean"), and set up call tracking. He also paused ads during January and February when nobody in Chicago is thinking about gutters.

**The outcome:** His ad spend dropped to $400/month, and his call volume increased because the ads were finally shown to the right people. First month after the fix: $3,100 in booked jobs from $400 in spend. The mistake was trusting someone else to care about his money as much as he did.

## Why Your Google Business Profile Is Your Best (Free) Employee

Most gutter cleaning companies treat their Google Business Profile like a digital business card. They fill in the basics, upload one photo, and wonder why they're not showing up in the "local 3-pack" on search results.

Here's what actually moves the needle.

A gutter cleaning company in Portland, Oregon, had 12 Google reviews in four years. They showed up on page two of local search results, which is basically invisible. Their competitor had 87 reviews and was booking three weeks out.

The fix took one Saturday morning: the owner asked every customer from the past two years for a review. He texted them a direct link, kept the request short ("If you were happy, a 5-star review helps me feed my kids"), and offered nothing in exchange (Google forbids incentives, and honestly, you don't need them — happy customers will leave reviews if you make it easy).

Within 30 days, they had 46 new reviews. Their profile started showing in the local 3-pack for "gutter cleaning Portland." Phone calls went from 3–4 per week to 15–18.

**The financial part:** $0 spent. The owner invested a Saturday morning and a $20 lunch for his wife while he worked. His September revenue increased by $4,700 compared to the same month the year before.

The other thing that worked: posting updates directly on the Google Business profile once a week. Photos of gutters before and after. A short video of a crew member explaining why fall cleaning matters. A post saying "We have openings this Thursday" with a phone number. Google rewards active profiles with higher visibility.

If you have a Google Business Profile and haven't posted anything in six months, you're leaving money on the table. That's not a theory. I've seen it happen at three separate service businesses.

## The Email Campaign That Picks Up When the Rain Starts

Gutter cleaning is seasonal. You know that. But most small business owners treat seasonality like a weather forecast they can't do anything about. "Spring is busy, fall is busy, winter is dead, summer is slow." That's the narrative.

It doesn't have to be.

I worked with a gutter company in Denver that had a customer list of 340 people — names, email addresses, dates of service from the past three years. They never contacted anyone after the job was done. They assumed people would call back when they needed gutters cleaned again.

The problem: people forget. Your customer might need their gutters cleared in October, but in October they're thinking about Halloween decorations and football. They're not thinking about you until the leaves are piled up and water is dripping down their siding.

The fix was a three-email sequence timed to the season, sent using Mailchimp (free for the first 500 contacts):

**Email 1 (September 1):** "The leaves in Denver are about to fall. Here's why cleaning your gutters now costs less than fixing your foundation later." Included a photo of a water-damaged fascia board from an actual local job.

**Email 2 (September 15):** "We're booking October cleanings now. Current lead time is 10 days. Book by Friday and get $25 off." Simple. Specific. A deadline.

**Email 3 (October 1):** "We have three openings this week for customers who need gutters cleared before the first big snow." Scarcity. Action-oriented.

The company sent these emails to 340 people. Open rate was 41% (Mailchimp's average for home services is about 22%). They booked 28 cleanings directly from those emails.

**Revenue from the campaign:** $4,200. Cost: $0 (Mailchimp free tier) plus maybe 90 minutes of writing and scheduling the emails.

The owner told me later, "I spent $800 last year on Facebook ads that got me two jobs. This email thing got me 28 jobs for zero dollars. I feel like an idiot for not doing it sooner."

He's not an idiot. Most small business owners don't realize that the most profitable customer is the one who already paid you. You just have to remind them you exist.

Tools that work for this: Mailchimp (free tier), Square (if you take card payments, you already have customer emails), or Booksy (for service scheduling and reminders).

## Paying for Leads: The Two Channels That Actually Pay Off

I've watched small business owners throw money at Facebook ads, Instagram followers, billboards, radio spots, and sponsored content on local news sites. Most of it doesn't work for gutter cleaning.

Here's what does.

**Google Local Services Ads** — not regular Google Ads, specifically the "Google Guaranteed" ads with the green checkmark. These show up at the very top of search results above everything else. You pay per lead, not per click. Google screens your business first, which means customers trust the results more.

A gutter company in Charlotte, North Carolina, spent $600 on Local Services Ads in October. They got 34 leads. They converted 22 of those into booked jobs. Average job value: $185. Total revenue: $4,070. Cost per lead: $17.60. That's a 6.8x return.

The downside: Google takes a cut, and you have to pass a background check and maintain a minimum rating. That's not a downside — that's quality control that protects you from competitors who work out of unmarked vans.

**Nextdoor** — not Facebook, not Yelp. Nextdoor. Homeowners on Nextdoor are actively looking for local service recommendations. They trust neighbors. They post questions like "Anyone have a gutter guy they like?" and the thread gets 40 replies.

A business owner in Raleigh, North Carolina, created a Nextdoor business page and simply responded to recommendation requests. He never paid for an ad. He'd write something like: "I clean gutters in this neighborhood. Happy to give a quote. Here are three before-and-after photos from a job I did on Maple Street last week."

He got 15 jobs in six weeks from Nextdoor responses. Total time investment: about 20 minutes per week.

**What about Yelp?** I'll be honest: Yelp ads work for a small number of categories (plumbers, locksmiths, tow trucks) but I've rarely seen them pay off for gutter cleaning. The cost per lead tends to be $40–60, and the conversion rate is lower because people on Yelp are often price-shopping five companies at once.

**What about Facebook ads?** I'd skip them unless you have a very specific targeting strategy — think "homeowners within 10 miles, aged 45–65, who follow local real estate pages." Even then, test with $100 before scaling. Facebook is great for getting likes. It's mediocre for getting gutter cleanings.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads as a new gutter cleaning business?**

Start with $500/month on Google Local Services Ads. Not regular search ads — Local Services. If you don't get at least 15 leads at $30 or less per lead in the first 30 days, your targeting is wrong. Adjust the radius, check your service area settings, and make sure your availability is accurate. Do not scale above $500 until you have a system for tracking which leads actually book.

**Q: Do I need a website, or can I just use my Google Business Profile?**

You can generate leads with nothing but a well-optimized Google Business Profile and a phone number. I've seen it happen. But you'll convert more leads if you have a simple one-page website with your price, your service area, your phone number, and a booking calendar. Don't spend $5,000 on a website. $500 with a template from Squarespace or Carrd is fine. The website isn't the product. The gutter cleaning is the product.

**Q: Should I offer discounts for first-time customers?**

Not a deep one. A small discount ($20 off) to overcome hesitation is fine. A "50% off first cleaning" attracts tire-kickers and bargain hunters who will leave you a 3-star review because you were five minutes late. I've watched businesses train their customers to only book when there's a coupon. Don't build a discount-dependent customer base.

**Q: How do I get more Google reviews without sounding desperate?**

Ask when the customer is happiest — right after you finish the job and they see clean gutters. Hand them a card with a QR code that goes directly to your Google review page. Say "If you're happy with the work, a review helps me out more than you know." Do not offer a discount in exchange. That violates Google's policy and it cheapens the ask. A direct, one-time request works. Automated follow-up texts asking for reviews work too, but use a tool like Podium or Broadly — manually texting 50 customers is not scalable.

**Q: Is seasonal marketing enough, or should I advertise year-round?**

Advertise seasonally, but stay visible year-round. In slow months (January, February, August), keep your Google Business Profile active. Post photos. Reply to reviews. Update your service hours. Don't run paid ads when nobody is buying. Instead, use slow months to collect reviews, update your website, and plan your fall campaigns. I've never seen a gutter company profit from January Google Ads. The search volume isn't there, and the people searching are price-comparing months in advance — low conversion, high annoyance.

**Q: What's the one marketing tool I should buy before anything else?**

A call tracking service. If you don't know which ads are generating phone calls and which are generating nothing, you're flying blind. CallRail costs $30–50/month. Set it up before you spend a single dollar on ads. I've watched businesses waste $10,000 on ads that were driving zero calls because the tracking was broken and the agency blamed "attribution issues." Call tracking removes the guesswork. Buy it first.
