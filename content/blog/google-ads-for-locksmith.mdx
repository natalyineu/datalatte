**Step 2: Set Up Your Google Ads Campaign**
Once you have your target keywords, it's time to set up your Google Ads campaign. Here are the key settings to consider:

* **Campaign type:** "Search" or "Shopping" depending on your business type
* **Target location:** Set to the city or region where you want to target customers
* **Target device:** Set to "Mobile" to reach customers on-the-go
* **Budget:** Set a daily or total budget to control your ad spend
* **Bidding:** Set your bid strategy to "Cost-per-click" (CPC) to pay for each ad click
* **Ad rotations:** Set to rotate ads evenly to ensure all ads get shown

**Step 3: Optimize Your Ads for Conversion**
To capture emergency calls, you need to optimize your ads for conversion. Here are some tips:

* **Ad copy:** Write ad copy that speaks to the customer's immediate need. Use words like "emergency", "24/7", and "now" to convey urgency.
* **Ad extensions:** Add ad extensions like phone numbers, addresses, and reviews to provide more information to customers.
* **Landing pages:** Create landing pages that match your ad copy and provide a clear call-to-action (CTA).

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've audited dozens of locksmith accounts at agencies and through DataLatte. The same problems show up almost every time. Here are the ones that actually cost you money.

### Mistake 1: Targeting Too Broad a Radius, Then Wondering Why You Get Wrong Calls

A locksmith in Portland, Oregon came to me after burning through $2,300 in three weeks. He had his radius set to 50 miles around downtown Portland. His reasoning: "I want everyone in the metro area."

Here's what actually happened. He got calls from Hillsboro, Gresham, Beaverton — places 20-40 minutes away from his shop. He'd quote the job, the customer would say "that's too long to wait," and hang up. His call-to-arrival ratio was 12%. Twelve percent of the people who called actually got service. The rest either chose someone closer or found a competitor who showed up faster.

The fix: We built three separate campaigns. One for his neighborhood with a 5-mile radius. One for the wider Portland area with a 15-mile radius but lower bids. One for commercial clients with no radius limit but targeted only during business hours. We also excluded the counties where his response time would exceed 45 minutes.

Outcome: His call-to-arrival ratio went from 12% to 38% in the first month. Monthly ad spend dropped from $3,000 to $1,800. Revenue went up 22% because he stopped wasting time on leads he couldn't serve. The $1,200 in monthly savings went straight to his pocket.

### Mistake 2: Bidding on "Locksmith" as a Broad Match Keyword

A shop in Austin, Texas told me they were "doing Google Ads" by bidding on the keyword "locksmith" with broad match. Their budget was $500/month. They'd been running for two months and had received exactly one call. One.

I looked at the search terms report. Their ads had shown for "locksmith training courses," "locksmith tools for sale," "how to become a locksmith in Texas," and "locksmith salary Austin." People looking to *become* a locksmith clicked their ad, realized it wasn't a career guide, and bounced. They paid $4.50 per click for people who were never going to call.

The fix: Pause all broad match keywords. Switch to phrase match and exact match only. Build a list of 25 emergency-specific keywords: "emergency locksmith austin," "car lockout austin," "house lockout now," "24 hour locksmith austin tx," "keys locked in car austin." Add negative keywords like "training," "course," "classes," "salary," "tools," "how to," "job," "career."

Outcome: The next month, that $500 budget produced 14 calls. Five of them turned into jobs. Average ticket was $180. That's $900 in revenue from the same spend. The owner told me, "I didn't know you could tell Google who *not* to show my ad to." Most people don't. Now you do.

### Mistake 3: Running One Ad for Everything

A locksmith in Nashville ran a single ad that said "Locksmith Nashville — Call Now." That was it. The ad ran for every keyword in his account: residential lockouts, car key replacements, commercial access control, rekeying, and emergency calls.

The problem? Someone searching "car key replacement Tesla Nashville" saw the same ad as someone searching "24 hour emergency locksmith Nashville." The car key guy assumed the shop didn't specialize in automotive. The emergency guy assumed the shop wasn't open 24 hours (because the ad didn't say so). Both were less likely to call.

The fix: Build separate ad groups with dedicated ads that match the search intent exactly.

**Ad group: Emergency lockout**
Ad headline: "Locked Out in Nashville? Call Now"
Ad description: "We arrive within 30 minutes. 24/7 emergency lockout service. Cars, homes, and businesses. Call (615) XXX-XXXX."

**Ad group: Car key replacement**
Ad headline: "Car Key Replacement Nashville"
Ad description: "Lost your car keys? We program new keys and fobs on-site for most makes and models. Call (615) XXX-XXXX."

**Ad group: Commercial locksmith**
Ad headline: "Commercial Locksmith Nashville"
Ad description: "Access control, master key systems, and high-security locks. Licensed and insured. Serving Nashville businesses since 2010."

Outcome: Click-through rate went from 1.8% to 4.3%. Cost per click dropped from $6.50 to $4.20 because Quality Score improved. Calls increased 65% in 30 days. The owner told me, "I can't believe I was making people guess what I do."

### Mistake 4: Forgetting to Track Which Calls Actually Convert

A locksmith in Denver was running Google Ads with call forwarding. He could see he was getting 40 calls per week. He assumed they were all good. When I asked how many turned into jobs, he said, "Most of them, I think."

We set up a simple tracking system. CallRail integration with Google Ads. Tagged each call source. Asked his dispatcher to log three things for two weeks: time of call, service requested, and whether it became a booked job.

The data was ugly. Of 80 calls over two weeks, only 22 turned into jobs. 27 were "how much do you charge for a lockout" price shoppers who didn't book. 15 were wrong numbers or spam. 8 were people looking for a different service (safe opening, lock repair that required parts he didn't carry). 8 were competitors checking prices.

The fix: Added a call extension with a short greeting that asked "Are you locked out right now, or do you need a quote?" That simple filter handled 18 of the price-shopper calls before they even reached the dispatcher. Added negative keywords for safe opening and lock repair. Set up call-only ads with a clear message: "Locked out? Call now for fast service. Not locked out? Email us for a quote."

Outcome: The weekly call volume dropped from 40 to 28. But the conversion rate jumped from 27% to 64%. The owner was getting fewer calls but more jobs. His monthly ad spend stayed the same. Revenue increased $3,800 per month because he stopped paying for calls that went nowhere.

## Why Your Landing Page Matters Less Than You Think (For Emergency Services)

Most marketing advice will tell you to build a beautiful landing page with testimonials, trust signals, and a contact form. For an emergency locksmith, that's almost irrelevant. Here's why.

When someone locks themselves out of their car at 11 PM, they're not reading your testimonials. They're not comparing your 5-star rating against the guy with 4.8 stars. They're not filling out a contact form. They're looking for a phone number. That's it.

I worked with a locksmith in Chicago who had spent $3,000 on a custom website with a booking system, live chat, and a 360-degree photo of his shop. He was proud of it. His Google Ads linked to the homepage. His cost per call was $32.

We changed one thing. We created a call-only campaign that showed only on mobile devices between 6 PM and 6 AM. The ad had his phone number in the headline. No landing page. When someone tapped the ad, it called him directly.

His cost per call dropped to $11. In the first week, he got 34 calls from that campaign. 21 turned into jobs. Average revenue per job was $200. That's $4,200 in revenue from $374 in ad spend. A 11.2x return.

His beautiful website was a distraction. For emergency services, your "landing page" is your phone answer. That's the conversion point. Everything before that should be the fastest possible path to a phone call.

Does that mean you should never have a website? No. If you also do non-emergency work (rekeying, installing locks, master key systems), a website helps with trust. But the website should be for *scheduled* work, not *emergency* work. Those two customer journeys are completely different. Treat them that way.

## How to Use Negative Keywords Like a Professional (Not a Beginner)

Every guide tells you to add negative keywords. But they don't tell you which ones actually matter for a locksmith. Here's the list I build for every locksmith client. Steal it.

**Job seekers and training:**
training, course, classes, certification, license, exam, study, career, job opening, hiring, salary, apprenticeship

**DIY and product-related:**
how to, lock pick, bypass, kit, tools, store, shop, buy, purchase, parts, cylinders, deadbolt only, install it yourself

**Geographic exclusions:**
These are specific to your city. If you serve Austin but not Round Rock, add "Round Rock" as a negative. If you don't go to the airport, add the airport name. If a competitor's neighborhood is too far, add that neighborhood.

**Service exclusions (if you don't offer them):**
safe, safe opening, safe combination, vault, gun safe, RV, motorcycle, boat, trailer, mailbox, filing cabinet, lockout kit

**Time-based (if you don't work 24/7):**
If you close at 6 PM and someone searches "24 hour locksmith," you don't want to show up. But here's the smarter play: use ad scheduling to pause your ads after hours. Don't rely on negative keywords for time-based intent. Just turn off the ads.

I audited a locksmith in Phoenix who had no negative keywords. His search terms report showed his ad had been clicked 47 times by people searching "how to pick a lock." He paid $4.12 per click. $193.64 for people who wanted a YouTube tutorial, not his service. That's not a small leak. That's a hole in the boat.

Add negatives before you launch. Then check the search terms report every Tuesday morning. Remove anything that's not a real service request. Do this for 30 days and you'll cut wasted spend by at least 20%.

## What Budget Actually Gets Results (And What's a Waste)

Business owners ask me this constantly. "How much do I need to spend to get calls?" The answer is uncomfortable. It depends on your city.

But I can give you a starting point that works for most markets.

In a midsize US city (300k-1M population), here's what I've seen work across 14 locksmith accounts:

**Minimum viable budget:** $500/month. This gets you about 8-12 clicks per day with a $4-$6 cost per click in non-emergency hours. You'll get 10-20 calls per week. You need to be on top of negatives and bids to make this work.

**The sweet spot:** $1,500-$2,000/month. This lets you run a dedicated emergency campaign 24/7 and a separate campaign for scheduled work during business hours. You can afford broader location targeting and higher bids for competitive emergency keywords.

**Saturation point:** Around $5,000-$6,000/month for most cities. Beyond that, you're bidding against yourself or chasing marginal keywords that cost more than they return.

A locksmith in San Antonio started at $300/month. Got 4 calls in the first week. Two were price shoppers. One was a job worth $150. One was a wrong number. He was about to quit when I told him to increase to $600/month. The higher budget let his ads run consistently instead of using up the budget by 2 PM. Calls went to 18 per week. Booked jobs went to 5-6 per week. Monthly revenue from ads: about $4,000. Ad spend: $600. He was profitable in 30 days.

The mistake people make is starting too small. If your daily budget is $10, Google may not show your ad at all for competitive keywords. You end up wasting money on impressions but no clicks. I'd rather see someone start at $500/month for 60 days than $200/month for 6 months. You'll learn faster.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I already show up in Google's local map pack. Why would I run Google Ads?**

The map pack is great for brand searches and people who know your name. But when someone searches "emergency locksmith near me" at 2 AM, the map pack shows three results. If you're not in the top three, you're invisible. Google Ads puts you above the map pack. You can be the first thing someone sees. Also, the map pack doesn't let you control your message. Ads let you say "24/7 emergency service" in bold text. The map pack just shows your name, rating, and a "call" button. Ads are worth it for the control alone.

**Q: Won't competitors click my ads to drain my budget?**

Yes, it happens. I've seen it. But Google has fraud detection. If Google detects invalid clicks (including competitor click fraud), they refund the cost. It's not automatic — you have to file a claim — but it works. I had a client in Atlanta who found a competitor clicking his ads 15 times a day for two weeks. Google refunded $420. The competitor stopped after a few days because it's a waste of their time. Don't let the fear of click fraud stop you from running ads. The lost revenue from not advertising is far worse.

**Q: Do I need a website to run Google Ads for locksmith services?**

No. Use call-only campaigns. These ads show your phone number directly. When someone taps the ad, it calls you. No website required. That said, having a simple one-page website helps with Quality Score (which lowers your cost per click) and gives non-emergency customers a place to learn about your services. A $500 Squarespace site is enough. Don't spend $3,000 on a site for an emergency service business.

**Q: Should I bid on my own business name?**

No. If someone already knows your name, they'll find you through organic search or the map pack. Bidding on your own name is burning money. I checked 12 locksmith accounts last year. Seven were bidding on their own brand name. Every single one could have saved that money. Add your business name to your negative keywords list.

**Q: How long does it take for Google Ads to start working?**

You can get a call within hours if your campaign is set up correctly. But real results — consistent calls you can count on — take 7-14 days for the learning phase to stabilize. Google needs about 30 conversions to optimize your bids. Don't change anything in the first 10 days. Don't pause ads because you didn't get a call in the first 6 hours. Give it time. I've seen people kill perfectly good campaigns because they were impatient.

**Q: What if I'm in a small town with low search volume?**

Google Ads might not work well if there aren't enough searches. Instead, focus on local Facebook groups, Nextdoor, and having a good Google Business Profile. The minimum viable search volume for a dedicated campaign is about 500 searches per month for your target keywords. If your town has fewer than that, you're better off spending $500 on local flyers and building relationships with property managers, real estate agents, and landlords. Advertising isn't always the answer. Sometimes local relationships beat online ads.
