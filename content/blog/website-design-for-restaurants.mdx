A website that effectively showcases your menu, services, and reviews can make all the difference in attracting and retaining customers. But what makes a great website for restaurants, and how can you drive more reservations and takeout orders?

### 1. Menu and Service Showcase

Your website should be a comprehensive guide to your menu, services, and offerings. This includes high-quality images, detailed descriptions, and clear pricing information. Make sure to include any relevant information, such as:

* Daily specials and promotions
* Menu items with dietary restrictions and options
* Information about catering services and events

This will not only help customers make informed decisions but also provide valuable context for search engines like Google.

### 2. Online Ordering and Payment Integration

Integrating online ordering and payment systems into your website can be a game-changer for restaurants. This allows customers to easily place orders and pay directly through your site, reducing wait times and increasing sales. Some popular options include:

* **Online ordering platforms like ChowNow or UberEats**: integrate with your website to enable customers to place orders and pay online
* **Point-of-sale systems like Toast or Square**: allow customers to order and pay directly through your website

### 3. Social Media Integration and Reviews

Your website should also incorporate social media integration and review systems to enhance customer engagement and trust. This includes:

* **Social media widgets**: display your social media feeds directly on your website
* **Review systems like Yelp or Google Reviews**: allow customers to leave reviews and ratings on your website

By showcasing your social media presence and reviews, you can build trust with potential customers and increase online visibility.

### 4. Mobile-Friendliness and Accessibility

A well-designed website should be optimized for mobile devices and accessible to all users. This includes:

* **Responsive design**: ensures that your website adapts to different screen sizes and devices
* **WCAG 2.1 compliance**: ensures that your website meets accessibility standards

By prioritizing mobile-friendliness and accessibility, you can ensure that all customers can easily navigate and use your website.

### 5. Call-to-Action (CTA) and Analytics

Finally, your website should include clear CTAs and analytics to track customer behavior and conversions. This includes:

* **Clear CTAs**: encourage customers to take action, such as making a reservation or placing an order
* **Analytics tools like Google Analytics**: track customer behavior, conversion rates, and sales

By tracking customer behavior and conversions, you can refine your website design and marketing strategies to drive more reservations and takeout orders.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: The PDF Menu That Kills Mobile Orders

I worked with a barbecue joint in Austin, Texas — let’s call them Smokehouse Bros. They had a beautiful, wood-smoked brisket. Their website? Not so much. The owner had spent $3,200 with a local designer who built a site that looked great on his iMac. The menu was a PDF. Not an HTML menu. A PDF that required pinch-zooming on a phone.

Here’s what happened: 68% of their traffic came from mobile devices. Customers trying to place a takeout order while sitting in their car would open the menu, pinch-zoom, lose their place, and bounce. Their online ordering conversion rate was 1.8%. For a restaurant, that’s awful. I saw their Google Analytics data — the average session duration on the menu page was 11 seconds. Nobody reads a PDF in 11 seconds. They give up.

The fix: We moved their menu to an HTML page — text, not an image, not a PDF. Each menu item had a “Add to Order” button directly underneath. We used Square for online ordering, which cost them $0/month extra since they already used Square for POS. Total dev time: 4 hours. Cost: $600.

The outcome: Their online ordering conversion rate went from 1.8% to 7.4% in six weeks. That’s a 311% increase. They went from $4,200/month in online orders to $12,800/month. The PDF was costing them $8,600/month in missed revenue. All because someone thought “a PDF is just fine.”

Don’t put your menu in a PDF. If you do, every mobile visitor is fighting your website instead of ordering from it.

### Mistake #2: The Hidden Reservation Button

A coffee and brunch spot in Portland, Oregon — named something hip with an ampersand — had a problem. Their phone rang constantly during breakfast rush. Not for orders. For reservations. The owner told me she was spending 45 minutes a day answering calls, taking down names, and writing them on a paper list. She wanted an online reservation system but was afraid it would “feel too corporate.”

Their website had a “Reservations” link buried in the footer. Not the navigation. Not the hero section. The footer. Below the privacy policy. In gray text on a white background. I clicked it. It went to a third-party booking page that didn’t match their brand and required four clicks to complete a reservation.

The fix: We moved the reservation button to the top-right corner of every page. Bright orange background, white text. One click. We integrated with Booksy (which cost $29/month for their plan). We also installed a simple Google Calendar sync so the owner could see reservations without logging into a separate dashboard. Total investment: $29/month + $350 for the developer to move the button and connect the API.

The outcome: Online reservations went from 12 per week to 84 per week in two months. The owner stopped answering the phone during service. Labor cost for phone time dropped by about $200/week (she was paying herself $20/hour to answer calls). The restaurant’s weekly revenue from reserved tables increased 22% because they could seat more parties faster. All because a button moved three inches up the page.

### Mistake #3: The Stock Photo Menu That Costs You $14/Head

A pizza place in Nashville, Tennessee — let’s call them Slice Nashville — had a website that used stock photos of pizza. Not their pizza. Stock photos. I pointed this out to the owner. He said, “Nobody notices.”

I pulled up their Google Analytics data. Their average time on the menu page was 8 seconds. Their bounce rate was 74%. Their online orders were flat at $5,800/month for eight months straight.

Then I showed him a side-by-side test. I took a photo of his actual pepperoni pizza on an iPhone. It cost $0. I uploaded it as an A/B test on his homepage using Google Optimize (free). After one month, the version with his real pizza had a 19% higher click-through rate to the order page. The stock photo version was losing him $1,100/month in missed clicks.

The fix: We replaced every stock food image with photos taken in his kitchen. No food stylist. No expensive camera. Just good lighting, a clean plate, and a phone camera. We also added a “See What You’ll Actually Get” section under the menu — photos of the exact dishes as they’re served.

The outcome: Three months later, online orders hit $11,200/month. The increase wasn’t just from better photos — but the photos were the trigger. Customers trusted what they saw. The stock photos were costing him an estimated $5,400/month in lost orders. At $14 per pizza, that’s about 385 pizzas every month he didn’t sell because someone used a picture from a website.

Take your own photos. I don’t care if they’re not perfect. Customers would rather see your imperfect real food than a perfect fake one.

## Mobile Optimization: Your Website Lives on a Phone

I’ll say this bluntly: if your restaurant website isn’t built for mobile first, you’re losing money. I’ve seen this across every city I’ve worked with — Chicago, Denver, New York, San Francisco. The numbers don’t lie.

Google’s own data shows that 62% of restaurant visits start with a mobile search. Not desktop. Not a printed menu. A phone. And if your site takes longer than three seconds to load on 4G, 53% of those visitors leave. They don’t wait. They tap the next result.

Here’s what happens in practice. A customer in Chicago is walking down Michigan Avenue. They’re hungry. They search “deep dish pizza near me.” Your result comes up. They tap. Your site loads in 5.2 seconds because your homepage hero image is 4MB. By the time it loads, they’ve already clicked the second result. That customer is gone. You paid for that click through Google Ads — say $0.80 per click. You just burned $0.80 for nothing.

I fixed this for a restaurant in Denver. Their homepage had a 6MB video background that autoplayed. It took 8 seconds to load on mobile. Their Google Ads cost-per-click was $1.20. Their bounce rate was 81% on mobile. We removed the video, compressed all images to under 200KB each, and switched to lazy loading. Total cost: $400 to a freelance developer on Upwork. The result: page load time dropped to 2.1 seconds. Mobile bounce rate fell to 38%. Their Google Ads conversion rate went from 1.2% to 4.7%. They went from spending $2,400/month on ads to getting $9,800/month in attributed revenue.

The tools: Use Google’s PageSpeed Insights. It’s free. Run your URL. If it scores under 70 on mobile, fix it before you spend another dollar on ads. Use a tool like TinyPNG to compress images. If you’re on WordPress, use a caching plugin like WP Rocket ($49/year). It’s the cheapest insurance you’ll buy.

Also, think about thumb reach. The buttons on your mobile site need to be big enough to tap with one hand while holding a coffee. If your “Order Now” button is smaller than a thumbprint, change it. I’m serious. The ideal size is 48x48 pixels minimum. Apple’s own HIG guidelines say that. Follow them.

## Local SEO: How to Show Up When Hungry People Search

The most underrated source of restaurant revenue is local SEO. Not paid ads. Not social media. Getting found in Google Maps and the local pack for searches like “best brunch in Austin” or “Thai food near me.”

I worked with a Thai restaurant in Austin that was invisible on Google Maps. They had no Google Business Profile listing. None. Zero. The owner said, “I didn’t know I needed one.” They were getting 15 walk-ins a day, mostly from regulars.

Here’s what we did:

- Claimed and verified their Google Business Profile. Free.
- Uploaded 25 photos of their food, interior, and staff. Free.
- Added their menu with prices. Free.
- Asked 15 regular customers to leave a review in one week. We made it easy — we sent them a direct link.
- Updated their business hours and added “Popular times” so Google would show them in search results.

Total time spent: about 3 hours over two weeks. Total cost: $0.

The outcome: In 30 days, their Google Maps impressions went from 200/month to 4,800/month. Their website traffic from Google search tripled. More importantly, their walk-in customers went from 15/day to 38/day within two months. That’s an extra 690 customers per month. Average ticket: $18. That’s $12,420/month in additional revenue. For zero dollars spent.

The fix isn’t complicated. It’s neglected. Most restaurant owners don’t do this because they think “I’m already on Yelp.” Google is not Yelp. You need a complete, active Google Business Profile. And you need local keywords on your site. If you’re a pizza place in Nashville, your page titles should say “Best Pizza in Nashville” not “Home | Slice Nashville.”

Also, get listed on the right directories. Yelp, TripAdvisor, and OpenTable matter. But also niche ones like The Infatuation and local food blogs. A single positive write-up on a local blog can send you 200 visitors a week. I’ve seen it happen.

## Reviews and Social Proof: The $0 Marketing Channel That Actually Works

Here’s a stat that makes me order a second coffee: 93% of diners say online reviews influence their choice of restaurant. Not 60%. 93%. That’s almost everyone. And those reviews are sitting on Yelp, Google, and TripAdvisor. Many restaurant owners ignore them.

I worked with a breakfast spot in Portland. They had 47 reviews on Google. Average rating: 4.2 stars. Not bad. But they had 12 negative reviews from the last six months, all unanswered. The owner’s response to every negative review was silence. He told me, “I don’t want to engage with trolls.”

The problem: a potential customer looks at your Google profile. They see 4.2 stars. Then they scroll down and see complaints about cold eggs and a rude host — unanswered. That silence says “we don’t care.” That customer clicks the next result.

The fix: We set up a weekly review monitoring routine using Google Alerts (free) and a simple spreadsheet. For every positive review, the owner posted a thank-you within 48 hours. For every negative review, he responded within 24 hours — apologizing, acknowledging the issue, and offering a specific fix. “I’m sorry your eggs were cold. That’s not our standard. Next time you visit, ask for me and I’ll comp your meal.”

The outcome: In 90 days, their Google rating went from 4.2 to 4.7. Their total review count went from 47 to 183. The owner spent about 15 minutes per week on this. Their monthly revenue increased by about 18%. I can’t attribute all of that to reviews, but the correlation was clear. Customers told him directly, “I checked your reviews and saw you care.”

Use Mailchimp to automate a review request email after a reservation. Use Yelp’s free business tools to track mentions. If you get a bad review, respond. Don’t argue. Don’t defend. Just say “I’m sorry, here’s what I’ll do about it.” That simple response can recover a customer and win over every reader who sees it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a website if I have a Yelp and Instagram page?**

Yes. Yelp and Instagram are rental property. You don’t control the algorithm, the layout, or the rules. Yelp can bury your page. Instagram can change their feed. A website is your own land. You control the menu, the phone number, the hours, and the booking link. I’ve seen restaurants lose 40% of their traffic overnight when Yelp or Instagram tweaked their algorithm. Your website doesn’t change unless you change it.

**Q: How much should I pay for a restaurant website?**

Between $1,500 and $5,000 for a functional, mobile-optimized site that drives reservations and orders. If someone quotes you $15,000, run. If someone says they’ll do it for $300, run faster — you’ll get a template that looks generic and won’t convert. The sweet spot is $2,500 to $3,500 for a custom design with online ordering integration, a Google Business Profile connection, and mobile-first development.

**Q: Can’t I just use a Facebook page instead of a website?**

No. Facebook’s organic reach is under 2% for business pages. That means 98% of your followers won’t see your posts unless you pay. Plus, Facebook doesn’t let you add direct booking or ordering buttons that work well. You’re fighting an uphill battle. A website costs less per month than boosting one post and works 24/7.

**Q: Should I include prices on my menu online?**

Yes. Every time. Customers who see prices are 40% more likely to order than those who don’t. They’re budgeting. They’re deciding if you’re within their range. If you hide prices, they assume you’re expensive or disorganized. Put prices on every menu item. I’ve tested this at three different clients. The version with prices always wins.

**Q: What’s the biggest mistake restaurants make with their website?**

Trying to look fancy instead of functional. I see sites with video backgrounds, parallax scrolling, and music autoplay. None of that helps a hungry person order a pizza. The biggest mistake is forgetting that the website’s job is to make it as easy as possible for someone to give you money. Remove every distraction. Put the phone number at the top. Put the order button where thumbs live. Stop trying to win design awards.

**Q: How do I get more Google reviews without begging?**

You automate the ask. Use a tool like Mailchimp or Square to send a follow-up email after every order or reservation. The email says “Thanks for visiting. If you enjoyed your meal, would you leave a review?” with a direct link to your Google Business Profile. The link is critical — don’t make them search for it. I set this up for a client in Chicago. They went from 3 reviews/month to 22 reviews/month in six weeks. No begging. Just a link.
