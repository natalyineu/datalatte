## What’s the real cost difference between Pinterest and Instagram ads?

Pinterest’s cost‑per‑click (CPC) hovers around $0.45, while Instagram averages $0.78 for the same local audience. The lower CPC on Pinterest comes from less competition in niche categories like "hand‑crafted latte art" or "organic pet grooming." Instagram, however, offers a broader reach, meaning you can spend $10 /day and still see 12–15 clicks versus 22 clicks on Pinterest for the same spend.

- **Set a test budget:** $50 for 7 days on each platform.
- **Track CPM:** Pinterest often sits at $5–$7, Instagram at $6–$9.
- **Watch frequency:** Instagram’s algorithm pushes the same ad more often, which can inflate costs without adding new eyes.

## Which platform drives more foot traffic for local cafés and salons?

A boutique café in Portland, OR saw a 28 % lift in foot traffic after switching 60 % of its ad spend to Pinterest. The reason? Pinterest users actively search for "cozy coffee spots" and "brunch ideas," so the platform acts like a visual directory. Salons in Manchester, UK reported a 22 % increase in appointment bookings from Instagram Stories because the format feels personal and time‑sensitive.

- **Location targeting:** Both platforms let you geo‑fence a 5‑mile radius, but Pinterest’s "search intent" layer adds an extra filter.
- **Call‑to‑action (CTA) options:** Instagram’s "Swipe Up" (or link sticker) drives instant booking; Pinterest’s "Shop the Pin" leads to a landing page where you can capture phone numbers.
- **Peak times:** Post‑lunch on Pinterest (12–2 pm) and early evening on Instagram (5–7 pm) generate the most local clicks.

## Creative best practices: How to design pins vs Instagram stories for a pet groomer

Pet groomers thrive on before‑and‑after visuals. On Pinterest, a vertical pin (1000 × 1500 px) with a clear "DIY grooming tip" overlay gets 3.5 % CTR—higher than most Instagram posts. Instagram Stories, however, let you add a countdown timer for limited‑time offers, which creates urgency.

- **Pinterest pins:** Use tall, high‑resolution images, add text overlay with keywords ("dog haircut Melbourne"), and link directly to a booking form.
- **Instagram Stories:** Keep it 1080 × 1920 px, use stickers for "Book Now," and add a short video of the grooming process (15 seconds max).
- **Brand consistency:** Use the same color palette and logo on both platforms; the brain recognizes the visual cue and trusts the business faster.

<Callout type="warning">Avoid overly busy designs. Too much text on a pin drops CTR by up to 40 %.</Callout>

## Measuring ROI: How to track conversions and calculate true return on each platform

The biggest mistake local owners make is counting clicks as sales. Instead, tie each click to a concrete action: a reservation, a coupon redemption, or a phone call. Use UTM parameters and a simple Google Sheet to log daily spend, clicks, and resulting revenue.

## When to choose one over the other: Decision matrix for fitness studios and yoga rooms

Not every visual platform fits every service. Here’s a quick matrix to help you decide:

| Goal | Pinterest | Instagram |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Brand discovery** | High (search‑driven) | Medium (feed‑driven) |
| **Immediate bookings** | Medium (link clicks) | High (Swipe Up) |
| **Seasonal promotions** | Low (long‑tail) | High (Stories) |
| **Budget ≤ $300/mo** | Ideal (lower CPC) | Viable if you need rapid reach |

- **If your studio sells class packages:** Instagram Stories with a limited‑time discount code work best.
- **If you rely on SEO‑style discovery (e.g., "best HIIT workouts"):** Pinterest’s board structure captures long‑tail searches.
- **If you have a tight budget:** Start on Pinterest, then allocate a small "boost" budget to Instagram for retargeting.

<Callout type="tip">Run a 2‑week "split test" using the matrix above; the platform that hits a 3 ×  ROAS first wins the longer‑term spend.</Callout>

## Quick start checklist: Launching your first campaign on Pinterest or Instagram today

You don’t need a marketing agency to get a campaign off the ground. Follow this 7‑step checklist and you’ll have live ads within a day.

1. **Define the objective:** Foot traffic, bookings, or coupon redemptions.
2. **Pick a budget:** $5‑$10 /day for testing; $30 /day for scaling.
3. **Create the visual:** Use Canva, choose the correct dimensions (Pinterest: 1000 × 1500 px; Instagram Story: 1080 × 1920 px).
4. **Write a concise copy:** 1‑2 short sentences, include a clear CTA ("Book your free trial now").
5. **Add UTM tags:** `utm_source=pinterest` or `utm_source=instagram`.
6. **Set location targeting:** 5‑mile radius around your shop, include city name in the ad copy.
7. **Launch and monitor:** Check performance every 24 hours; pause the under‑performing ad after 3 days.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How much should I spend on my first Pinterest or Instagram ad?**
Start with $5–$10 per day for each platform. A $150 test over two weeks gives enough data to compare CPC and conversion rates without hurting cash flow.

**Can I run ads for multiple locations at once?**
Yes. Both platforms let you create separate ad sets per zip code or city. Keep budgets equal across locations to see which area responds best.

**Do I need a professional photographer for pins?**
Not necessarily. A good smartphone camera plus natural light works for most cafés and salons. Just ensure the image is sharp and the product is centered.

**How long does it take to see results?**
Pinterest often shows a lift in foot traffic after 5–7 days because users browse for inspiration. Instagram can generate clicks within 24 hours, especially with Stories.

**What if my ads get disapproved?**
Both platforms have clear policies. Common issues are "over‑promotional text" on Pinterest or "restricted content" on Instagram. Edit the copy, resubmit, and you’ll be back in under an hour.

**Is it worth using both platforms simultaneously?**
If your budget allows, yes. Use Pinterest for discovery and Instagram for urgency. The combined ROI in the BarChart example was 124 % higher than using either alone.

**Do I need a separate business account for each platform?**
Absolutely. A dedicated business profile gives you access to analytics, ad managers, and the ability to add CTA buttons.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running the Same Creative on Both Platforms

A hair salon in Austin, Texas called *Lustre & Lather* spent $1,200 in Q1 last year running identical "book a balayage" ads on both Pinterest and Instagram. The owner, Maria, assumed that since her before-and-after photos looked great on her phone, they'd work everywhere.

They didn't.

On Instagram, the ad got decent engagement — 4.2% CTR, mostly from people tagging friends with "omg we should go together." But nobody actually booked. The problem? Instagram users were scrolling for entertainment, not intent. The ad looked aspirational but lacked a frictionless booking path.

On Pinterest, the same creative sat flat. Pinterest users searching "bronde hair ideas" wanted to save the pin to a board, not click through to a booking page. The CTR was 0.8%.

**The fix:** We split the budget. Instagram got a carousel ad with a "DM to book your appointment" CTA and a $50-off first-visit offer. Pinterest got a step-by-step infographic titled "How to Find the Right Balayage Shade for Your Skin Tone" that linked to a blog post, not a booking page. The blog post had an inline booking CTA at the bottom.

**The outcome:** Instagram's new ad drove 18 bookings at an average ticket of $210 — $3,780 in revenue. Pinterest's blog post got 1,400 reads, 22 email signups from the blog's popup, and 6 bookings from retargeting those readers. Combined ad spend: $800. Revenue: $5,040. Maria stopped running the same ad on both platforms and never looked back.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring the "Save" vs. "Like" Difference

A pet groomer in Nashville called *The Snip & Wag* put $500 into Instagram ads for "luxury dog grooming." The ad showed a fluffy poodle after a full groom. It got 87 likes. Zero bookings.

The owner, Derek, was frustrated. "Everyone loved the photo. Why didn't anyone show up?"

Here's the uncomfortable truth: a like on Instagram is passive. It costs the user nothing. A save on Pinterest, however, signals intent — someone is keeping that pin for future reference. Derek's Instagram ad collected likes from people who had no intention of grooming a dog that week. They just thought the poodle was cute.

**The fix:** We moved the entire $500 to Pinterest. We created three pins: "5 Signs Your Dog Needs a Professional Groom (Not Just a Bath)," "Before & After: A Matted Goldendoodle Transformation," and "What to Expect at Your First Grooming Appointment." Each pin linked to a simple booking page on Booksy.

**The outcome:** The pins collectively got 340 saves and 28 click-throughs to the Booksy page. Of those, 11 people booked appointments. Average grooming ticket: $85. Revenue: $935. Derek's cost per booking dropped from effectively infinite to $45. He now runs Pinterest ads exclusively for his grooming services and uses Instagram strictly for community posts about his shop dogs.

### Mistake #3: Not Using Retargeting Pixels From Day One

A coffee shop in Portland, Oregon called *Cold Brew & Co.* ran a $300 Pinterest campaign promoting their new seasonal menu. The ad showed a beautifully styled flat lay of a pumpkin cold brew with a cinnamon rim. CTR was 5.1% — solid. But the shop only saw 3 new customers mention the ad.

The owner, Tom, was about to declare Pinterest a dud. But when I looked at his setup, he hadn't installed the Pinterest tag on his website. Every single click landed on his homepage with zero tracking. He had no idea who visited, what they looked at, or how to bring them back.

**The fix:** We installed the Pinterest base tag and event codes for "ViewContent" and "AddToCart" (even though coffee is a low-ticket item, the same logic applies to any product). Then we ran a $200 retargeting campaign for 14 days, showing the same seasonal drinks to anyone who clicked the original ad but didn't buy.

**The outcome:** The retargeting campaign drove 42 conversions — mostly $6–$8 cold brew purchases. Total revenue from the retargeting: $310. Combined spend for both campaigns: $500. Total revenue: $310 + the original 3 customers (who spent an average of $12 each) = $346. Still not a home run. But Tom learned something more valuable: the retargeting cost per conversion was $4.76 vs. the initial campaign's $100 per new customer. He now budgets 60% of his monthly ad spend for retargeting and 40% for prospecting. His current monthly Pinterest ROI sits at 3.2x.

## Why Local Businesses Should Care About Search Intent (Not Just Demographics)

Most small business owners I talk to target by age and location. "Women 25–45 within 10 miles of my shop." That's fine for a billboard. It's lazy for digital ads.

Here's what I've seen work better: target by what people are actively searching for.

Pinterest is a search engine disguised as a social platform. Instagram is a feed disguised as a discovery tool. They reward different behaviors.

Take a fitness studio in Denver called *Altitude Cycle*. They spent $800/month on Instagram ads targeting "Denver women interested in fitness." The ads showed sweaty cyclists in a dimly lit room with club music. CTR was 1.8%. Cost per lead: $34. They got 23 leads in a month. Only 4 converted to a $199 intro membership. That's $796 in revenue against $800 in spend. Break-even.

We pivoted to Pinterest. We created pins targeting search terms like "Denver indoor cycling classes," "best spin studio near me Denver," and "low-impact cardio Denver." Each pin linked to a landing page with a free first-class offer and a Google Calendar booking link.

**The numbers:** CPC dropped to $0.38. CTR hit 4.7%. Cost per lead: $12. They got 66 leads in 30 days for the same $800 budget. Of those, 19 converted to intro memberships. Revenue: $3,781. ROI: 4.7x.

The difference wasn't the creative. It was the intent. Pinterest users were *looking* for a spin class. Instagram users were *looking* for entertainment and happened to see a spin class ad.

**The tool that made this work:** Google Keyword Planner. I know, I know — it's a Google tool, not a Pinterest tool. But you can use it to find what people search for in your city. Plug in "coffee shop Austin" or "hair salon Chicago" and look at the keyword suggestions. Then take those exact phrases and build Pinterest ads around them. Pinterest's algorithm rewards exact-match keywords in pin titles and descriptions.

## The Hidden Cost of Instagram's Algorithm: Frequency Fatigue

Most guides will tell you Instagram has better targeting. They're not wrong. But they skip the part where Instagram's algorithm shows your ad to the same 200 people 14 times in a week.

I worked with a pet supply store in Chicago called *Paws & Claws*. They ran a $600 Instagram campaign promoting a new line of freeze-dried raw dog food. The first week, CTR was 3.1%. By week two, it dropped to 0.9%. By week three, 0.4%.

The owner, Jenna, thought the creative was stale. She swapped images, changed copy, tested new offers. Nothing fixed it.

What actually happened: Instagram's algorithm served the ad to the same audience segment repeatedly because it performed well initially. The platform optimizes for engagement, not fresh reach. So it kept showing the ad to people who already saw it. Those people stopped clicking. The algorithm interpreted that as "this ad is bad" and raised the CPM.

**The fix:** We capped frequency at 2 per person per week and expanded the audience radius from 5 miles to 10 miles. We also created three different ad sets with different hooks: "Your dog deserves better than kibble," "Vet-recommended freeze-dried raw," and "Free sample with any purchase."

**The outcome:** Frequency dropped from 14 to 1.8. CTR stabilized at 2.9%. CPM dropped from $9.20 to $6.40. Jenna's $600 budget generated 47 sales at an average order value of $54 — $2,538 in revenue. She now checks frequency every 48 hours and kills any ad set that exceeds 3 impressions per person.

**The tool:** Facebook Ads Manager's breakdown menu. Under "Delivery," select "Frequency." If any ad set shows above 3, either expand your audience or refresh the creative. Don't let Instagram eat your budget by showing the same ad to the same person until they hate your brand.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I only have $200/month to spend. Should I pick Pinterest or Instagram?**

If you have a service business (salon, groomer, fitness studio) with a clear search intent, put the entire $200 on Pinterest. You'll get lower CPCs and higher intent traffic. If you sell a low-consideration product (a $8 coffee drink, a $12 candle), Instagram's visual storytelling might work better. But test $100 on each for two weeks before committing. You'll learn more from two weeks of real data than from a month of guessing.

**Q: How do I track whether an ad actually brought someone into my store?**

This is the question that separates pros from amateurs. Use a unique offer code in your ad copy — "Show this ad for 15% off your first visit." Or use a dedicated phone number from a service like CallRail that tracks calls from specific ads. For online bookings, use a UTM parameter on your booking link and check Google Analytics. The "last click" model is imperfect, but it's better than nothing. If you're not tracking, you're guessing.

**Q: Will Pinterest work for my coffee shop if I only sell $5 drinks?**

Yes, but you need to change your math. A $5 drink means you need 40 new customers a month to justify a $200 ad spend at break-even. That's tight. Instead, use Pinterest to promote a higher-ticket item: a $25 bag of whole-bean coffee, a $40 gift box, or a $15 loyalty card that gives the 10th drink free. Once someone buys the higher-ticket item, retarget them with a $5 latte ad. The customer acquisition cost gets spread across the higher initial purchase.

**Q: How long should I run a test before deciding a platform doesn't work?**

Seven days minimum, 14 days preferred. But only if you've spent at least $50 per platform. Anything less and the sample size is too small to mean anything. If you've spent $100 on Pinterest and gotten zero clicks, check your targeting and creative before blaming the platform. I've seen campaigns fail because the business owner targeted "women 18–65" with a blurry photo of their storefront. Fix the basics first.

**Q: Can I use the same photos from my Instagram feed on Pinterest?**

You can, but you shouldn't. Instagram photos are optimized for vertical mobile viewing with bright, saturated colors. Pinterest images need to be tall (2:3 aspect ratio or taller), text-overlaid, and designed to be saved to a board. An Instagram photo of your latte art will get ignored on Pinterest. A Pinterest-optimized image with "Best Latte Art in Austin — 5 Photos That Will Make You Thirsty" overlaid in a clean font will get saved and clicked. They're different mediums. Treat them that way.

**Q: What's the one metric I should ignore on both platforms?**

Impressions. They're vanity. A million people saw your ad, but nobody walked in. Who cares? Focus on cost per click (CPC), cost per conversion (CPA), and return on ad spend (ROAS). If your CPA is lower than your average customer lifetime value, you're winning. If not, fix your funnel before you spend another dollar.
