## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Ignoring Negative Reviews (Then Wondering Why Leads Dried Up)

A roofer in Denver—let's call him Dave—had been in business for 12 years. Solid work. Good crew. But his Google Business Profile had seven unanswered 1-star reviews dating back to 2023. Three of them were from the same storm season when his guys were overbooked and showed up late. Two were from people who'd never even hired him (wrong address, confused with another company). He ignored all of them.

**What happened:** A homeowner in Aurora searching "roof inspection Denver" saw Dave's 3.8-star rating with multiple complaints about "no-shows." She picked the roofer with 4.7 stars who responded to every review. Dave lost a $9,500 roof replacement job. Then another. Then another. He told me six months later he'd lost roughly $23,000 in potential contracts because potential clients saw those reviews and clicked away.

**The fix:** I had Dave respond to every single review in one sitting. For legitimate complaints, he apologized publicly, explained what happened (overbooked by a storm surge), and offered a discount on future work. For the mistaken reviews, he politely clarified that it was a different company. Then he started responding to every new review within 48 hours.

**The outcome:** His rating climbed to 4.3 stars over three months. More importantly, his profile's response rate hit 100%. Google's local algorithm favors businesses that engage with reviews. His phone started ringing again. He told me his July lead volume was up 40% from the year before—and he credited review management, not anything else he'd changed.

### Mistake #2: Using the Wrong Business Name Across Directories

A roofer in Nashville—family business, three generations—had been "Nashville Roofing Pros" on their website and Google Business Profile for eight years. But when I ran a citation audit, I found them listed as "Nashville Roofing Company" on Yelp, "Nashville Roofing Pros LLC" on Angi, and just "Roofing Pros" on a local chamber of commerce directory.

Google's local algorithm cares about NAP consistency—Name, Address, Phone. When your business name changes by even one word across directories, Google gets confused. It treats your listings as separate businesses, splits your authority, and ranks you lower.

**What happened:** This roofer was stuck at position 7 for "roof repair Nashville" for six months. They were spending $1,200/month on Google Ads just to compensate for their terrible organic ranking. Meanwhile, a competitor with a consistent name across all directories was ranking #3 organically with zero ad spend.

**The fix:** We did a full citation cleanup. I used Moz Local and manually checked 30+ directories. We standardized the name to exactly what was on their Google Business Profile—"Nashville Roofing Pros"—and updated every single listing. It took about 10 hours of work.

**The outcome:** Within eight weeks, they moved from position 7 to position 3 for "roof repair Nashville." They reduced their Google Ads spend from $1,200/month to $400/month—saving $9,600 per year—because organic leads replaced paid ones. That's $9,600 back in their pocket for one weekend of listing cleanup.

## How to Build Local Citations That Actually Drive Calls

Most guides tell you to "get listed on directories" and call it a day. Here's what they don't tell you: not all citations are equal, and some can actually hurt you.

I've audited roofing companies that were listed on 85 directories—but 60 of them were spam sites that Google ignores or penalizes. Quality matters more than quantity.

**What actually works:**

- **Google Business Profile** — Non-negotiable. If this isn't optimized, nothing else matters.
- **Bing Places** — Yes, people use Bing. Especially older homeowners in their 50s and 60s who own houses and need roof repairs.
- **Apple Maps** — iPhone users searching "roof repair near me" pull from Apple's database. If you're not listed, you don't exist to them.
- **Yelp** — I know business owners hate Yelp. I get it. But homeowners check it anyway. Claim your page, fill it out completely, and respond to reviews. You don't have to pay them.
- **Angi (formerly Angie's List)** — Homeowners with urgent roof leaks will pay for a "Angi certified" badge because they think it means quality. It's worth the $200/year listing fee.
- **HomeAdvisor** — Similar to Angi. The lead quality varies, but the citation value for local SEO is real.
- **Better Business Bureau** — Older homeowners trust the BBB. An A+ rating with accredited status signals credibility. The $400-$600 annual fee is worth it if you serve clients over 55.
- **Local chamber of commerce** — Google uses chamber listings as a trust signal. The $200-$400 membership fee is cheaper than running one bad ad campaign.

**What to skip:**
- Foursquare (mostly dead)
- Hotfrog (spam directory)
- Any site that asks for payment but has zero traffic
- "Premium" directory packages that promise first-page rankings (they're lying)

A roofer in Chicago did exactly this—focused on the eight high-quality citations, skipped the rest—and saw his GBP impressions go from 400/month to 2,100/month in 10 weeks. He told me his phone rang 18 times in the third month. Eighteen calls from people actively looking for a roofer. He booked $19,500 in work from those calls.

## Using Google Posts to Capture Storm Season Urgency

Most roofing companies set up their Google Business Profile once and forget about it. That's a missed opportunity, especially during storm season.

Google Posts are the little updates that appear on your GBP listing—under your business info, above your reviews. They're free, they show up in search results, and they expire after seven days. Most roofers ignore them.

**Here's what I've seen work:**

- **Post before storms hit.** A roofer in Austin, Texas, started posting "Free roof inspections" every Monday during monsoon season. He wrote: "Leaks starting? We'll check your roof and give you an honest assessment. No pressure. Free." He posted it every Monday for eight weeks. His GBP clicks went from 20/week to 85/week.

- **Post photos of completed work.** Not stock photos. Real photos of roofs you've replaced, gutters you've installed, skylights you've added. One roofer in Phoenix posted a time-lapse video of a full roof replacement—15 seconds, shot on his phone. It got 1,200 views in three days. Three people called him specifically because they saw the video.

- **Post storm damage photos.** After a hailstorm, post photos of damaged shingles with text like: "Hail damage in northeast Denver? We're doing free inspections today and tomorrow." This catches people right when they're scared about their roof.

- **Post seasonal tips.** "Summer heat stressing your shingles? Here's what to look for." This keeps your profile active even when you're not running promotions.

**The tool:** Google Business Profile dashboard. Free. Takes five minutes per post. Set a recurring calendar reminder for Monday mornings.

A roofer in Oklahoma City told me he generates about 8-10 calls per week from Google Posts alone. No ads. No extra spend. Just consistent posting. He estimated that's worth about $4,000-$5,000 in booked revenue per month.

## Why "Near Me" Searches Matter More Than You Think

Here's something most local SEO guides don't tell you: "near me" searches for roofers spike by 200-300% within 48 hours of a major storm.

A roofer in Houston tracked his phone calls after a thunderstorm in May 2025. Normally, he gets 12 calls per week. In the three days after that storm, he got 47 calls. Forty-seven. Of those, 34 were from people searching "roof repair near me" or "emergency roofer near me."

**The key to capturing "near me" traffic:**

1. **Your ZIP code service area matters.** Set your GBP service area to cover the specific ZIP codes where you actually work. Not "all of Texas." The specific ZIP codes. Google uses proximity as a ranking signal for "near me" searches.

2. **Include "near me" keywords naturally in your website copy.** Not keyword stuffing. But if you're writing a blog post about "how to spot storm damage," include a sentence like: "If you're searching for a roofer near me in Houston, here's what to check before you call."

3. **Make sure your website loads fast on mobile.** 80% of "near me" searches happen on phones. If your site takes more than three seconds to load, people bounce. A roofer in Atlanta cut his mobile load time from 5.2 seconds to 2.1 seconds and saw his "near me" click-through rate increase by 34%.

4. **Get reviews that mention "near me" or your neighborhood.** A review that says "Best roofer in the Heights neighborhood" signals relevance for local searches in that area. Encourage customers to mention their neighborhood or city in reviews.

A roofer in Denver who optimized for "near me" searches told me he went from 3-4 leads per week to 12-15 per week during storm season. His revenue during July-September went from $38,000 to $94,000 year-over-year. Same crew. Same pricing. Just better visibility for the right searches.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does local SEO take to work for a roofing business?**

If you fix your Google Business Profile today—correct categories, complete info, 15 photos, active reviews—you can see results in 2-4 weeks. Full citation cleanup (Moz Local, correcting inconsistencies) takes 8-12 weeks to show ranking improvements. For competitive cities like Austin or Nashville, expect 3-6 months to crack the top 3 local results. Don't believe anyone who promises "first page in 48 hours." That's not how Google works.

**Q: Should I pay for Yelp ads?**

Probably not. Yelp ads work well for restaurants and dentists. For roofers? I've seen mixed results. One roofer in Portland spent $500/month on Yelp ads for three months and got exactly two leads—both from people who wanted quotes under $1,000 for jobs that cost $8,000+. He canceled and put that $500 into Google Posts and citation cleanup instead. His organic leads tripled. Yelp's organic listing (the free one) is worth having. The ads? Test with $100/month first, track every call with a unique phone number, and cancel if you don't see a 4x return.

**Q: Do I need a website, or is Google Business Profile enough?**

GBP alone will get you some calls. But a website gives you control. You can post detailed case studies, before/after photos, service area pages, and seasonal content that Google indexes for broader searches. A roofer in Chicago with just a GBP was getting 15 calls/month. He added a simple 5-page website with his GBP info duplicated, wrote two blog posts about storm damage, and started getting 35 calls/month within 8 weeks. The website cost $1,500 to build. The extra 20 calls per month generated about $60,000 in additional revenue over the next year. Yes, get a website.

**Q: How many reviews do I need to rank in the top 3?**

There's no magic number. I've seen roofers with 12 reviews rank above roofers with 85 reviews. What matters: recency (new reviews every week), response rate (respond to every one), and quality (detailed reviews with photos or mentions of specific services). A roofer in Nashville got 8 new reviews in 10 days by simply asking every customer to leave one. He went from position 5 to position 2. Focus on getting 3-5 new reviews per month consistently. That beats a burst of 30 reviews followed by silence.

**Q: Should I use a different phone number for SEO tracking?**

Yes. Set up a Google Voice number or a service like CallRail that tracks which calls come from your GBP, which from your website, and which from citations. A roofer in Austin thought his GBP was driving most of his calls. When he added call tracking, he discovered that 60% of his calls actually came from his Yelp listing—which he'd been ignoring. He started responding to Yelp reviews and posting photos there. His call volume went up 25% in six weeks. Cost of call tracking: $30/month. Value of the insight: thousands.

**Q: Does Google Guarantee (Local Services Ads) work for roofers?**

Sometimes. Google Guarantee shows your listing at the very top of search results with a checkmark badge. It's pay-per-lead, not pay-per-click. A roofer in Dallas spent $800 on Google Guarantee in one month and got 14 leads—but 8 of them were low quality (wrong service, wrong location, or people just price shopping). He booked 2 jobs worth $6,800 total. His cost per booked job was $400. That's actually decent. But if you're in a competitive market like NYC or LA, the cost per lead can hit $80-$120. Test with $300/month for 60 days. Track the lead quality. If more than 50% are junk, pull the budget and put it into citation cleanup instead.
