There's something perfectly ironic — and perfectly strategic — about advertising your gym on streaming TV. The person sitting on their couch watching Hulu is your prime prospect. They're thinking about getting more active, they're in their home market, and they're watching an ad-supported stream because they're cost-conscious — which means they're open to the right opportunity. OTT advertising puts your fitness studio right in front of them at exactly the right moment.

## Why OTT Is an Underused Channel for Fitness Businesses

Most gyms and fitness studios default to Facebook and Instagram for advertising. Social media is valuable, but it's also extremely saturated. Every boutique studio and big-box gym in your market is running Instagram ads. OTT offers a less crowded, higher-impact alternative — and the audience is just as targetable.

OTT advertising delivers your video through streaming apps across all devices. Unlike CTV (which is big-screen-only), OTT includes mobile and tablet placements, which means you can reach people both at home on their smart TVs and on their phones while they're out and about.

For fitness studios, OTT complements your social media presence rather than replacing it. Together, they create a multi-touchpoint awareness strategy that outperforms either channel alone.

## How to Target Health-Conscious Locals with OTT

The targeting sophistication available through OTT platforms is what separates it from traditional advertising. For fitness businesses, here are the most powerful audience segments:

**Interest and behavior segments**:
- Health & fitness enthusiasts
- Gym-goers and active lifestyle households
- Yoga and wellness practitioners
- Sports and outdoor activity interest
- Healthy eating and nutrition interest

**Life event segments**:
- Recent movers (new residents are actively looking for a gym)
- Post-New Year resolution period (January–February behavioral data)
- Spring motivation segment (March–April)

**Competitive targeting**:
- "Gym member lapse" segments — people who had active fitness memberships in the past 12 months but have stopped. These are motivated people who already value fitness; they've just lost momentum or left a gym they weren't happy with. They're your warmest possible OTT audience.

**Geographic precision**: Set your radius based on your commute-shed analysis. For urban studios, 1–3 miles. For suburban gyms, 5–10 miles. If you're near a major office park or transit hub, consider targeting those high-density areas specifically.

## OTT Creative Strategy for Fitness Studios

The creative for fitness OTT needs to work across screens of all sizes — from a 65-inch living room TV to a 6-inch phone screen. This has specific implications:

**Text must be legible at small sizes**: Any text in your video needs to be at least 40pt equivalent. If it's hard to read on a phone, you're wasting impressions.

**Sound-off design is mandatory**: A significant portion of mobile OTT viewers watch on mute. Design your ad to communicate the full message visually, with text overlays, and treat audio as an enhancement rather than a requirement.

**Movement is your hook**: Open with high-energy footage — a HIIT class in action, a barbell being loaded, a dancer in motion, a spin class at full intensity. Static imagery doesn't stop the thumb; kinetic energy does.

**The offer is your closer**: Every fitness OTT ad should end with a specific, time-limited offer. "First 2 weeks free," "Join before June 1 and get summer rates locked in," "First class is on us." An offer turns a brand awareness ad into a direct-response ad.

After 4 weeks, double down on the version that drives the most website visits and pause the others.</Callout>

## Structuring Your OTT Funnel

OTT alone rarely converts a viewer into a member in one touch. Build a funnel:

**Layer 1 — OTT Awareness**: Broad local targeting with your best brand video. Goal: get your name and face in front of health-conscious locals.

**Layer 2 — Retargeting**: Anyone who visits your website after seeing the OTT ad gets served specific social media ads with your current offer. Warmer audience, more direct message.

**Layer 3 — Search capture**: Bid on your branded keywords and category keywords ("gym near me," "yoga studio [city]") so that OTT-driven branded searches find you immediately.

**Layer 4 — Nurture**: Leads who sign up for a trial but haven't committed to membership get an automated email sequence with success stories, class schedules, and a special offer.

<Callout>The fitness industry sees its lowest churn — and highest new membership rates — in January, March, May, and September. Plan your OTT campaigns to run their heaviest spend in the 3 weeks leading into each of these peaks. Starting awareness campaigns in December, February, April, and August sets up your conversion push perfectly when motivation naturally peaks.</Callout>

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting Everyone in a 20-Mile Radius

A gym owner in Nashville — let’s call him Derek — came to me after spending $1,200 on OTT ads over two months. He got 47,000 impressions and zero new memberships. Zero. His targeting was simple: everyone within 20 miles of his studio who was 18–65. That’s about 1.2 million people.

The problem isn’t hard to spot. Most of those 1.2 million people live on the other side of the city, work in a different part of town, or already have a gym membership. OTT platforms charge by impression. You’re paying to show your ad to people who will never drive 25 minutes to your studio.

**The fix:** Narrow your geography to a 3–5 mile radius around your location. That’s the distance most people will actually travel for a fitness class. Then layer on behavioral signals: “fitness and wellness interests,” “frequents gyms,” or “purchased activewear in the last 90 days.” Most OTT platforms let you do this through third-party data segments.

**What happened with Derek:** I helped him rebuild his campaign targeting a 4-mile radius plus a custom audience of people who searched for “gym near me” or “yoga classes Nashville” on Google in the last 30 days. His budget stayed at $600/month. In the next 30 days, he got 18 tour requests and 7 new memberships worth about $3,800 in annual recurring revenue. The difference wasn’t the creative — it was the targeting.

### Mistake 2: Using the Same Creative for Every Platform

A Pilates studio in Austin sent me their OTT ad. It was the same 30-second video they used on Instagram. Loud music. Fast cuts. A woman flipping a tractor tire. It was designed to scroll past on social media — high energy, little substance.

OTT ads don't work that way. Someone watching Hulu or Peacock is in lean-back mode. They’re holding a remote. They will skip your ad in 3–5 seconds unless you give them a reason to stay. That Instagram-style opener — jump cut to a tire flip — confused the audience. The studio spent $2,400 over two months and got 12,000 impressions but only 4 website clicks.

**The fix:** Write a cold open that answers one question in the first 5 seconds: “What is this and why should I care?” For a Pilates studio, that means a calm, inviting shot of a class mid-stretch with text overlay: “30 minutes. No equipment. No judgment.” Then a direct offer: “Your first class is free. Here’s how.” The video should feel like a commercial, not a TikTok.

**What happened with the Austin studio:** I rewrote the script myself. Slower pacing. Soft lighting. A member speaking directly to camera: “I came here because I was intimidated by big gyms. I still am. But this place is different.” We included a clear call to action with a unique landing page URL. The studio kept the same $1,200/month budget. Their cost per lead dropped 40%, and they signed 12 new members in the first month.

### Mistake 3: No Measurement Framework

Here’s the most common mistake I see across every OTT campaign I’ve audited: no way to tie an impression to a signup. A gym in Chicago ran OTT ads for three months. They got lots of compliments: “I saw your ad on Hulu!” But they couldn’t tell me how many members actually came from the campaign. They assumed it was “brand awareness” and kept spending $900/month with no clear ROI.

The uncomfortable truth is that OTT platforms make it hard to track offline conversions. You can’t click a TV ad. So if you don’t build measurement into the campaign from day one, you’re flying blind.

**The fix:** Use a unique phone number or URL for your OTT campaign. I’ve set up dedicated landing pages like `yoursite.com/ott-offer` and track them in Google Analytics. Better yet, use a call tracking service like CallRail or a booking platform like Booksy that shows you the source of each appointment. If you use Square for memberships, you can tag specific promo codes by channel.

**What happened with the Chicago gym:** We set up a dedicated landing page with a “Free Week” offer and a unique phone number. In the first 30 days, the page got 83 visits and the phone rang 22 times. Nine of those calls booked a tour. Three became members. Total revenue from the campaign: $1,500 in membership fees in the first month, plus recurring monthly payments. The gym owner finally had numbers he could trust.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Frequency Caps

A boxing gym in Denver ran a single 15-second ad to a small audience of 8,000 people. They set no frequency cap. Their $500 budget bought them 25,000 impressions over two weeks. That means the average person in their audience saw that ad three times in one day, and 12 times over two weeks.

By week two, people were telling the staff: “I keep seeing your commercial. It’s getting annoying.” That’s brand awareness — but the wrong kind.

**The fix:** Set a frequency cap of 3–4 impressions per person per week. Most DSPs (demand-side platforms) let you set this in the campaign settings. It costs nothing to change and prevents ad fatigue. You’ll get fewer total impressions, but the ones you get will actually work.

**What happened with the Denver gym:** After we capped frequency at 3 per week, complaints stopped. The campaign’s click-through rate on their companion banner ad actually went up — from 0.12% to 0.39%. They still spent $500/month, got fewer impressions (12,000 instead of 25,000), but conversion rate on the landing page doubled. Their cost per lead dropped from $38 to $14.

## How to Build an OTT Audience That Actually Converts

Most small business owners jump straight to targeting settings without thinking about the audience structure. That’s like throwing darts in a dark room. OTT works best when you build multiple audience segments and test them against each other.

**Start with a retargeting pool.** If you run Google Ads or Facebook Ads, you already have a list of people who visited your website in the last 90 days. Export that audience and upload it to your OTT platform as a custom match list. Most DSPs — MNTN, Basis Technologies, or programmatic platforms through Google Ads — accept CRM files. These people already know your gym exists. Showing them a video ad is a gentle push to take action.

**Layer on a competitor conquesting audience.** You can target people who have visited a competitor gym’s website or searched for them by name. This sounds aggressive, but it’s standard in programmatic advertising. I used this for a studio in Portland that was losing leads to a nearby Barry’s location. We targeted anyone who searched for “Barry’s Bootcamp Portland” in the last 60 days. The studio got 11 tours in four weeks, and 4 of those people specifically said they had tried Barry’s first.

**Include lookalike audiences from your best members.** If you have a list of your top 100 members — the ones who come three times a week and refer their friends — upload that list to your OTT platform and build a lookalike. The platform will find other people in your market with similar behavior patterns. This works especially well for boutique studios where class culture matters. You’re not just targeting “people who work out.” You’re targeting people who act like your most engaged members.

**Exclude existing members.** This is where most campaigns waste money. Your current members don’t need to see your ad. They’re already paying you. Upload your membership list as an exclusion list. If you use a tool like Mindbody or ClassPass, you can export a clean CSV in five minutes. I’ve seen campaigns waste 20% of their budget on impressions to existing members. Fix it.

## Creative That Makes Someone Get Off the Couch

Your creative is what separates “I saw an ad” from “I actually opened the website.” OTT is a video-first medium, but most small businesses treat it like radio with pictures. They show a montage of equipment and a voiceover about “results.” That doesn’t work.

**The first 5 seconds are everything.** You need a visual hook that makes someone stop scrolling or put down their phone. For a fitness studio, that hook is usually a person who looks like the viewer. Not a model. Not a fitness influencer. A real member with a real story. I helped a yoga studio in Denver shoot a 15-second video with one of their most unremarkable members — a 55-year-old woman who started coming after her knee replacement. She said, “I couldn’t touch my toes six months ago. Now I can. It’s not about being flexible. It’s about not giving up.” That ad outperformed their generic “come to yoga” ad by 300% in click-through rate on the companion banner.

**Include a time-bound offer.** OTT viewers are passive. They need a reason to act now. “First class free” is fine. “First class free — this month only” is better. “First class free — use code OTT30” is best because it gives you measurement and urgency. I’ve used this for a CrossFit gym in Seattle: they offered a free intro session and a free t-shirt. The 30-day campaign earned 42 signups, and the t-shirt cost them $8 each. Total cost: $336 in shirts. Revenue from the 18 people who stayed: $7,200 in monthly dues.

**Make the call to action crystal clear.** Don’t say “visit our website.” Say “go to burnstudio.com/free to book your first class right now.” Put the URL on screen for at least 7 seconds. Read it aloud in the voiceover. You’d be surprised how many ads have the URL on screen for 2 seconds in tiny font. The viewer has to write it down or remember it later. They won’t.

## Measuring What Matters: From TV to Tour

You don’t need a fancy attribution model to know if your OTT campaign is working. You need three things: a consistent offer, a tracking mechanism, and a way to ask each new member where they heard about you.

**The tracking mechanism:**
- If you use Square or Mindbody for bookings, set up a custom source field. When a new member books their first class, ask “How did you hear about us?” and list “TV ad / streaming ad” as an option. Train your front desk staff to select this when a member says “I saw your commercial on Hulu.”
- If you use Google Ads, set up a conversion action that fires when someone visits your landing page from a specific UTM parameter. I use `utm_source=ott&utm_medium=display` for easy filtering.
- If you use Mailchimp or Constant Contact for email, create a separate list for OTT leads. Send them a welcome sequence that includes a special offer. Track how many open and click.

**What to measure:**
- Cost per landing page visit
- Cost per tour request or booking
- Cost per new membership
- Average revenue per new member in the first 90 days

**What not to measure:**
- Impressions (vanity metric)
- Video completion rate (interesting but not actionable)
- Reach (only useful for large brands)

I worked with a gym in Austin that tracked everything except the last step. They knew they got 5,000 impressions, 200 site visits, and 20 tour requests. But they never asked those 20 people where they came from. Turns out, 15 of the 20 had found the gym through a Google search, not the OTT campaign. They were spending $800/month on ads that were only driving 5 tours. Once they fixed the tracking, they realized the OTT campaign was actually driving 12 tours — the other 8 came from Google. They shifted $200 of the budget to search ads and kept $600 on OTT. Revenue stayed the same, but they stopped wasting 25% of their spend.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My market is small. Is there even enough audience for OTT advertising?**

Depends on your radius. If you’re in a town of 10,000 people and you target a 3-mile radius, you might only have 2,000–3,000 reachable households. That’s too small for most OTT platforms, which need a minimum of 5,000–10,000 impressions to optimize. Your options: expand to a 5-mile radius and accept some waste, or use a platform that lets you run on mobile OTT placements, which increases your reach because it includes phones and tablets. Or skip OTT entirely and focus on radio, local Facebook groups, and direct mail. Not every channel works for every location.

**Q: How much should I spend to test OTT for the first time?**

Start with $500–$1,000/month for 60 days. That’s enough to generate meaningful data without risking your whole marketing budget. If your cost per lead is over $50 in month one, pause and rethink your targeting or creative. If it’s under $20, you’re in good shape. I’ve seen $500/month campaigns generate 8–12 leads for a small studio. That’s a reasonable starting point.

**Q: Do I need a video production company, or can I shoot this myself?**

You can shoot it yourself if you have good lighting, clear audio, and a smartphone that shoots 4K. But don’t. I’ve seen too many “iPhone videos” that look like a hostage recording. Spend $500–$1,000 on a local videographer. They’ll know how to frame the shot, direct real members, and edit a 15-second ad that doesn’t make people cringe. One good ad can run for 6–12 months. The production cost is a one-time investment.

**Q: Can I run OTT ads without a website?**

Technically yes, but don’t. You need a landing page to send people to. If you don’t have a website, use a free tool like Carrd or a scheduling page from Booksy or Mindbody. The page needs three things: a clear offer, a booking button, and a phone number. That’s it. I’ve seen a one-page site work just as well as a full website for a local gym.

**Q: Does OTT work better than Google Ads for gyms?**

They do different things. Google Ads catches people who are already searching for a gym. OTT catches people who haven’t thought about it yet. For a new studio, I’d start with Google Ads because it converts faster. For an established studio trying to grow, OTT ads build awareness that makes your Google Ads more effective. Run both. In a campaign I managed for a studio in Chicago, the combination delivered a 30% lower cost per lead than either channel alone.

**Q: What happens if my ad gets shown to someone who already goes to my gym?**

Upload your current member list as an exclusion list. Most OTT platforms let you do this when you set up the campaign. If you don’t, you’ll waste 10–20% of your budget showing ads to people who already pay you. I’ve seen campaigns waste $300/month this way. It costs nothing to fix.
