**Setting Up Your Google Ads Account**

To get started, you'll need to create a Google Ads account and set up your campaign. This involves choosing your ad group, setting a budget, and selecting your target audience. Sounds easy, right? But there's a lot to consider.

*   **Ad group**: This is where you'll create ads for specific services or locations. For example, if you're a coffee shop, you might create one ad group for your coffee shop in downtown and another for your coffee shop in the suburbs.
*   **Budget**: You'll need to set a budget for your campaign, which can be daily or total. Be careful not to overspend, as this can lead to burnout and decreased ROI.
*   **Target audience**: This is where you'll select who you want to reach with your ads. You can target by location, demographics, interests, and more.

<Callout type="tip">When setting up your Google Ads account, make sure to take advantage of Google's free ad extensions, such as location extensions and call extensions. These can help increase the visibility and reach of your ads.</Callout>

**Optimizing Your Google Ads Campaign**

Once you've set up your account, it's time to optimize your campaign. This involves tracking your performance, adjusting your targeting, and creating high-performing ads.

*   **Tracking performance**: This means monitoring your ad metrics, such as CPC, CTR, and conversion rates. This will help you understand what's working and what's not, so you can make data-driven decisions.
*   **Adjusting targeting**: Based on your performance data, you may need to adjust your targeting to improve ad relevance and effectiveness.
*   **Creating high-performing ads**: This involves writing compelling ad copy, selecting relevant images, and structuring your ads for maximum impact.

<Callout type="warning">Don't get too caught up in optimizing your campaign – sometimes, it's best to simplify and focus on getting the basics right.</Callout>

**Measuring ROI and Making Data-Driven Decisions**

To truly understand the effectiveness of your Google Ads campaign, you need to measure your ROI. This involves tracking your revenue, expenses, and profit margins to see whether your campaign is yielding a positive return.

*   **Tracking revenue**: This means monitoring your sales, bookings, or other revenue streams to see how your campaign is impacting your bottom line.
*   **Tracking expenses**: This involves monitoring your ad spend, as well as other expenses associated with your campaign, such as creative development and copywriting.
*   **Tracking profit margins**: This means monitoring your revenue minus your expenses to see whether your campaign is yielding a positive return.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched small business owners throw away anywhere from $500 to $5,000 on Google Ads before they call someone who actually knows what they're doing. Here are the four mistakes I see most often — and what to do instead.

### Mistake 1: Targeting Too Wide a Radius

**The story:** A pet groomer in Austin, Texas, came to me after spending $1,200 over three months. She was targeting a 30-mile radius around her mobile van's home base. She got 847 clicks, three bookings. That's $400 per booking. She was ready to quit Google Ads entirely.

**What went wrong:** She was competing with every other pet groomer within a 30-mile radius of downtown Austin. That's hundreds of businesses. Her ad was showing to people in Round Rock, Pflugerville, and even San Marcos — places her van would take 45 minutes to reach. Nobody drives 45 minutes to get their dog's nails trimmed.

**The fix:** We narrowed her targeting to a 5-mile radius around the neighborhoods where she already had two or more existing clients. Then we excluded zip codes where response times would exceed 20 minutes. We added location bid adjustments (+25% for the core neighborhood, -50% for the outer ring).

**The outcome:** In the next 30 days, she spent $380 and got 14 confirmed bookings. Cost per booking dropped from $400 to $27. She added two more vans within six months.

**What to do:** Open Google Ads, go to Locations, and click "Advanced search." Enter your service area manually by zip code. Remove the "People in or regularly in your targeted locations" option and switch to "People in your targeted locations." That alone will cut wasted spend by 30-40%.

### Mistake 2: Using Broad Match Keywords Without Negative Keywords

**The story:** A mobile mechanic in Nashville, Tennessee, was ecstatic about his Google Ads performance — 1,200 clicks in two weeks, $680 spent. But when I asked how many bookings he got, he said "only four, but it'll pick up." It never picked up. He spent $2,100 over two months and got 11 bookings total.

**What went wrong:** He used broad match for keywords like "car repair," "mobile mechanic," and "truck service." Google showed his ad for searches like "how to become a mobile mechanic" (a training query), "free car repair estimate" (not buying), "mobile mechanic salary" (job seeker), and "DIY car repair" (not hiring anyone). He was paying $0.50 to $1.20 per click for people who would never book a service.

**The fix:** We built a negative keyword list with 47 terms: "how to," "training," "course," "school," "job," "salary," "free," "DIY," "manual," "parts," "insurance," "rental," "review." Then we switched the core keywords to phrase match and exact match only. For "mobile mechanic Nashville" exact match, his cost per click dropped to $0.35.

**The outcome:** $340 spent in the next 30 days. 18 bookings. Cost per booking: $19. He texted me a photo of his first $1,200 engine repair job. "Worth every penny," he wrote.

**What to do:** Before you launch, go to Tools > Keyword Planner and run a search terms report from any similar campaign you've run. Export the terms. Anything that isn't a direct service inquiry goes on your negative list. Update it weekly for the first month.

### Mistake 3: Not Tracking Phone Calls Properly

**The story:** A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon, was convinced Google Ads didn't work. She spent $900 over six weeks, tracked 12 website bookings, and felt ripped off. She was about to cancel when I asked about phone calls. "Oh, I get a few calls," she said. "But I don't know where they come from."

**What went wrong:** She had call extensions enabled but never set up call tracking or forwarding. Google was showing her ad with her real business phone number. Every call went straight to her salon. She had no way to know which calls came from Google Ads, Yelp, or her sign on the street. She was getting an estimated 8-12 calls per week from Google Ads — and ignoring them in her ROI calculations.

**The fix:** We set up Google Forwarding Numbers through the Call Extensions settings. Cost: about $1 per unique number per month. Every click-to-call from her ad routed through a forwarding number that Google tracks. We also added call-only ads for mobile devices, targeting people actively searching on their phones.

**The outcome:** In 60 days, she had 47 tracked calls, 31 of which led to appointments. Average appointment value: $85. That's $2,635 in tracked revenue from calls alone — plus the 12 website bookings she was already tracking. Total return on $900 ad spend: roughly 3x. "I was two days away from canceling," she told me.

**What to do:** In your Google Ads account, go to Ads & Extensions > Extensions > Call Extension. Enable "Use a Google forwarding number." Set up conversion tracking for calls lasting longer than 60 seconds. Without this, you're flying blind on at least 40% of your leads.

### Mistake 4: Running Ads 24/7 Without Considering When People Actually Book

**The story:** A mobile dog grooming business in Denver ran ads 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. They spent $1,800 over two months. Owner said "I'm getting clicks at 3 AM. Is that good?" No, that's bad. 3 AM clicks cost $0.60 each and never convert. Nobody books a dog groomer at 3 AM.

**What went wrong:** They set the campaign to run "All day" by default. Google happily spent their budget on overnight clicks from insomniacs scrolling their phones. No ad scheduling, no bid adjustments for peak hours. About 22% of their total spend came between 10 PM and 7 AM — essentially zero return on that $396.

**The fix:** We checked Google Ads' built-in "Hour of day" report (Reports > Predefined > Time). For their business, 80% of bookings came between 8 AM and 11 AM (people booking same-day) and 4 PM to 7 PM (people booking for next day). We scheduled ads to run 7 AM to 9 PM only. Then we added bid adjustments: +40% for 8-11 AM and 4-7 PM, -30% for 7-8 AM and 7-9 PM.

**The outcome:** Same $900/month budget. But now 100% of spend happens during high-intent hours. Bookings increased from 22 per month to 38. Cost per booking dropped from $41 to $24. The owner stopped checking ads at midnight.

**What to do:** Go to Campaign Settings > Ad Schedule. Start with 7 AM to 9 PM, seven days a week. Run for two weeks, then pull the Hour of Day report. Adjust your schedule based on actual conversion data. Most service businesses see 60-70% of conversions within a 6-hour window.

## Tracking the Right Conversions (Because Google Reports Liars)

I need to say something uncomfortable: Google Ads' default conversion tracking is designed to make you feel good, not to tell you the truth.

Here's what I see at DataLatte: Business owners set up conversion tracking, Google says "You got 47 conversions this month!" and they're thrilled. But when we dig in, 12 of those "conversions" were people who clicked their website link, then clicked the phone number, then hung up without booking. Google counted a call as a conversion even if it lasted 12 seconds. That's not a conversion. That's a misclick.

**What actually matters for mobile service businesses:**

**Phone calls lasting 60+ seconds.** You can set this in Google Ads under Conversions > Call from Ads. I set the minimum duration to 60 seconds. Anything shorter is a wrong number, a hang-up, or someone asking about prices they'll never pay. One of my clients, a plumbing company in Chicago, had 73 "conversions" in their default report. When we applied the 60-second filter, they had 19. Those 19 calls generated $8,400 in revenue. The other 54 calls? $0.

**Form submissions that include a phone number.** If someone fills out your contact form but leaves the phone field blank, odds are low they'll actually book. Set up conversion tracking to only count form submissions where the phone number field has at least 10 digits. A carpet cleaner in Phoenix tried this and saw his conversion rate drop from 8% to 3%. But his actual booking rate went from 12% to 67% because he was only following up with serious leads.

**Direction requests.** If someone clicks "Get Directions" from your Google Business Profile ad, that's a high-intent signal. They're ready to come to you — or ready for you to come to them. Set this as a secondary conversion action. One of my nail salon clients in NYC saw 22 direction requests in a month. Eighteen of those turned into appointments within 48 hours. That's an 82% close rate from a conversion type most people ignore.

**How to set this up properly:**

- **Google Ads:** Go to Conversions > New Conversion Action. Choose "Phone calls" and set minimum duration to 60 seconds. Then add "Website" > "Import" > "Google Analytics" to track form submissions with the phone field requirement.
- **Google Analytics 4:** Create an event for "phone_click" and "form_submit_with_phone." Import these as secondary conversions in Google Ads.
- **Call tracking software:** If you're spending more than $1,000/month, consider CallRail or WhatConverts. They cost $30-50/month but tell you exactly which keyword and ad generated each call. One landscaper in Austin used CallRail, discovered his "lawn mowing Austin" keyword was generating $1,200/month in bookings, and tripled his bid on it. His spend went up $200/month. His revenue went up $3,600/month.

**The truth:** Most tracking setups are wrong. If you're looking at Google's default reporting and feeling good, I'd bet 30-50% of what you're seeing is noise. Fix the tracking before you spend another dollar on ads.

## Budget Strategy That Actually Works for Mobile Service Businesses

I'll keep this short because most guides make it complicated.

**The minimum viable budget:** $500/month. I know some guides say $200. Those guides are lying. At $200/month, Google shows your ad to maybe 8 people per day. Three of them click. One calls. That's not enough data to optimize anything. At $500/month, you get 25-40 clicks per day, 3-5 calls, and enough data to start making decisions within two weeks.

**How to structure that $500:**

- **$350** on a Search campaign for high-intent keywords (exact match and phrase match only)
- **$100** on a Performance Max campaign with location targeting and a call extension
- **$50** on remarketing to people who visited your site but didn't book

**Why Performance Max gets $100:** It's Google's AI-driven campaign type that shows your ad across Search, Display, YouTube, and Discover. For a mobile service business, it works surprisingly well if you give it good location and audience signals. A pet sitter in Denver put $150/month into Performance Max, gave it a list of 12 neighborhoods, and got 9 bookings in the first two weeks. Cost per booking: $17.

**Why remarketing gets $50:** Most people who visit your site won't book on the first visit. They're comparing you to two other services. A $50/month remarketing campaign keeps your name in front of them. A mobile car detailer in Nashville spent $45/month on remarketing, got 4 bookings worth a total of $1,200. That's a 27x return.

**When to scale:** Double your budget only when you're hitting at least 5 confirmed bookings per week and you have capacity to handle more. I've seen too many businesses jump from $500 to $2,000/month and watch their cost per booking triple because they saturated their local market. Scale gradually — 20-30% increases every two weeks.

**What to cut immediately:** Display Network campaigns for general awareness. If you're running Display ads to people who "might be interested in pet grooming," you're paying for impressions on cooking blogs and news sites. Nobody sees a banner ad for a mobile dog groomer and thinks "yes, I need that right now." Cut Display to zero until you've exhausted Search.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I run Google Ads with only $300/month?**

Yes, but you're limiting yourself. At $300/month, you're getting roughly 10-15 clicks per day. That's one or two calls. You'll need 60-90 days to gather enough data to optimize. If you can't do $500, I'd rather see you put $300 into a well-targeted Facebook ad campaign for your specific neighborhood and wait to launch Google Ads when you have $500. One coffee roaster in Portland did exactly that — ran Facebook to his 5-mile radius for three months, built a small customer base, then launched Google Ads at $500/month with 47 initial customer reviews on his Google Business Profile. His cost per click was 40% lower because the profile had social proof.

**Q: How do I compete with bigger companies that have unlimited budgets?**

You don't compete on budget. You compete on relevance. A big plumbing company in Chicago might bid $8 per click on "plumber Chicago." But they're running a generic ad that says "Emergency Plumber — Call Now." You can bid $4 per click on "plumber Lincoln Park" with an ad that says "Serving Lincoln Park for 8 years. Same-day service, no overtime fees." Your quality score will be higher because your ad is more relevant to that search. Google rewards relevance with lower costs. I've seen small businesses get top placement at 40% lower cost than their big competitors because they were hyper-specific.

**Q: How do I know if Google Ads is actually working?**

Look at three numbers: tracked phone calls (60+ seconds), form submissions with phone numbers, and direction requests. If those three metrics are going up month over month, Google Ads is working. If your "conversions" number is going up but those three specific metrics are flat, something is wrong with your tracking. I had a client in Austin who thought Google Ads was failing because his website bookings were low. We added call tracking and discovered he was getting 11 calls per week that he never counted. His Google Ads was working fine — his tracking was broken.

**Q: How much time does it take to manage a Google Ads campaign?**

First month: 3-4 hours to set up properly, including keyword research, negative keywords, location targeting, ad copy, and conversion tracking. After that: 30 minutes per week to review search terms (add negatives), check cost per conversion, and update ad copy if performance drops. If you're spending more than $1,500/month, hire someone part-time or use a freelancer. The time you spend managing ads is time you could spend actually doing the service work that pays the bills.

**Q: Should I run ads on Yelp instead of Google?**

I've tested both extensively. Here's my honest take: Yelp works for businesses with 30+ reviews and a rating above 4 stars. Below that, your Yelp ads will produce clicks at $2-4 each and very few conversions. Google Ads works at any review count because it prioritizes relevance and location. If you have 10 reviews on Yelp at 3.8 stars, skip Yelp ads and focus on Google. If you have 50+ reviews at 4.5 stars, Yelp ads can be a solid secondary channel — but Google should still get 70% of your budget.

**Q: What happens if I stop running ads?**

Your leads drop immediately. Google Ads has no lasting effect — it's like a faucet. Turn it off, the water stops. But here's the thing: if you've been running ads for 6+ months and built up Google Business Profile reviews and organic traffic, you'll keep getting some leads from people searching your business name directly. Not enough to sustain growth, but enough to bridge gaps between campaigns. One thing I recommend: capture every ad lead's email and phone number so you can market to them directly if you pause ads. Build your own list, don't rely entirely on Google.
