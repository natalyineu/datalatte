CTV. OTT. Linear TV. FAST. AVOD. SVOD. The streaming advertising world is drowning in acronyms — and most explanations make them sound more complicated than they are.

Here's the plain-English version: CTV and OTT describe the same content from different angles. OTT is how the content is delivered. CTV is the device it's watched on. Understanding the distinction matters because buying CTV inventory and buying OTT inventory can be different processes with different targeting options and CPMs.

If you're a local business considering streaming TV advertising, this guide tells you exactly what you're actually buying.

## OTT: The Delivery Method

**OTT (Over-the-Top)** refers to video content delivered over the internet, bypassing traditional cable or satellite infrastructure. The "over the top" refers to going over the top of the cable box.

Any streaming content — Netflix, Hulu, Peacock, YouTube, Tubi, Pluto TV — is OTT content. It doesn't matter what device you watch it on. Someone watching Hulu on their phone is watching OTT. Someone watching Hulu on their living room TV is also watching OTT. Same delivery method, different device.

OTT advertising means your ad is served within internet-delivered streaming content — as opposed to a traditional TV commercial break on a cable broadcast.

## CTV: The Device

**CTV (Connected TV)** refers specifically to the television set connected to the internet — whether that's a smart TV with built-in streaming, a TV connected to a streaming stick (Roku, Fire TV, Chromecast), or a gaming console used for streaming.

CTV is a subset of OTT. All CTV viewing is OTT, but not all OTT viewing is CTV. When someone watches Peacock on their iPad, that's OTT but not CTV. When they watch Peacock on their 65-inch LG smart TV, that's both OTT and CTV.

<Callout type="tip">
The distinction matters for advertisers because CTV ads appear on the living room TV screen — full-screen, lean-back, non-skippable in most cases. OTT on mobile or desktop has different viewing dynamics (smaller screen, more multitasking, sometimes skippable). CPMs for CTV placements are higher because the viewing environment commands more attention.
</Callout>

## The Other Acronyms You'll Encounter

**AVOD (Advertising-Based Video On Demand):** Streaming services that are free to watch but supported by ads. Tubi, Pluto TV, Peacock's free tier, the Hulu with Ads plan. This is where advertisers buy inventory.

**SVOD (Subscription Video On Demand):** Streaming services paid by subscription with no ads — original Netflix model, Disney+ base tier, Apple TV+. No advertising inventory.

**FAST (Free Ad-Supported Streaming Television):** A specific type of AVOD featuring live linear channels (like traditional TV channels) delivered via streaming. Pluto TV's channel grid is FAST. Tubi's live channels are FAST.

**Linear TV:** Traditional broadcast or cable television. Scheduled programming, you watch what's on now. No on-demand. Still significant reach but declining as audiences shift to streaming.

## CTV vs OTT: What You're Actually Buying When You Advertise

When an ad platform says "CTV advertising," they typically mean: your video ad delivered to a television screen, within streaming content, often non-skippable.

When they say "OTT advertising," they typically mean the broader category: your video ad delivered within internet-streamed content across all devices (TV, mobile, desktop, tablet).

**In practice:**
- Buying CTV = targeting television screens specifically, higher CPM, maximum impact per impression
- Buying OTT = buying across all streaming devices, lower average CPM, broader reach
- Most platforms let you buy both under a single campaign and break out performance by device

For local businesses, the most common practical recommendation is to **buy CTV when you want maximum brand impact and can afford the premium CPM**. Buy OTT (across all devices) when you want to maximise reach within a budget.

## How to Buy CTV and OTT Advertising

**Programmatic DSPs:** The Trade Desk, DV360, Amazon DSP, StackAdapt — access inventory across multiple streaming platforms simultaneously. Best for targeting flexibility and cross-platform reach measurement.

**Platform-direct (self-serve):** Roku's OneView, Hulu's Ad Manager, Amazon's self-serve streaming TV — buy directly from individual platforms. Simpler but limits you to one platform per campaign.

**Agency with CTV specialisation:** For local businesses without programmatic infrastructure, an agency handling CTV buys across platforms is often the most efficient route.

**Minimum budgets:**
- Platform self-serve (Roku, Hulu): $500–1,000 per campaign
- Programmatic via DSP: $2,000–5,000 per campaign
- Managed service through agencies: $5,000–10,000+ per campaign

<Callout type="warning">
Don't let vendors sell you "OTT advertising" without clarifying the device mix. An "OTT campaign" that's 80% mobile and tablet is very different from one that's 80% CTV. The viewing experience, attention level, and CPM are all different. Always ask for the device breakdown in your reporting.
</Callout>

## What the Measurement Looks Like

CTV/OTT measurement is more complex than social or search advertising because the path from ad exposure to purchase is longer and harder to track directly.

**Standard metrics:**
- Impressions (how many times your ad was served)
- Reach (unique households or devices)
- Video completion rate (VCR) — typically 85–95% for non-skippable CTV
- Frequency (average exposures per unique household)

**Attribution metrics:**
- Website visits attributed to ad exposure (pixel-based or probabilistic matching)
- Brand lift studies (survey-based measurement of awareness and consideration)
- Store visit attribution (through location intelligence providers)
- Second-screen behaviour (people searching your brand name on their phone while watching the TV ad)

The cleanest metric for local businesses: **brand search lift** — track Google searches for your business name during and after a CTV/OTT campaign flight. Meaningful lift is the most direct signal the campaign is working.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is YouTube CTV advertising?

Yes — when viewed on a connected TV. YouTube on a smart TV or streaming device is CTV inventory. YouTube on mobile or desktop is OTT but not CTV. YouTube's advertising platform lets you target by device type, so you can run campaigns specifically on TV screens.

### What's the minimum I need to spend to test CTV or OTT?

Platform self-serve options (Roku, Hulu) allow campaigns starting at $500–1,000. To generate statistically meaningful data for a local business in one DMA, $1,500–3,000 over 4 weeks is a more practical test. Below $500, the reach is too thin to evaluate meaningfully.

### Should a single-location local business bother with CTV/OTT?

It depends on your category and budget. For businesses where brand image matters and where TV advertising would traditionally make sense (restaurant, gym, spa, home services), CTV/OTT is worth testing once Google and Meta are running efficiently. It's an upper-funnel awareness channel — don't expect direct trackable ROI equivalent to search advertising.

### What's the difference between CTV and Streaming TV in Google Ads?

Google Ads calls it "YouTube and TV screens" or "Connected TV" targeting. Functionally, this targets YouTube content viewed on television screens and content on partner streaming services. It's accessing CTV inventory through Google's ecosystem rather than a separate platform or DSP.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting the DMA Instead of the Block

I worked with a pet grooming business in Denver that spent $800 a month on CTV ads for six months. The owner was frustrated because they'd gotten exactly two appointments she could trace back to the campaign. Two.

Here's what went wrong: She bought inventory through a reseller who targeted the entire Denver Designated Market Area (DMA). That's roughly 3 million households. Her shop was in the Highlands neighborhood, and most of her clients came from a 2-mile radius. She was paying to show ads to people in Aurora, Littleton, and Boulder — none of whom would drive 30 minutes for a $55 grooming.

The fix was aggressive geographic narrowing. We rebuilt the campaign using household-level targeting through a managed DSP, restricting delivery to zip codes 80211, 80212, and parts of 80220 — about 48,000 households total. We also added a frequency cap at 3 per week so we weren't annoying the same 48 homes.

The outcome: Cost per appointment dropped from $400 to $28. She started spending $300 a month instead of $800 and filled 10–12 new recurring appointments per month within 60 days. That worked out to roughly $3,600 in recurring monthly revenue from a $300 ad spend.

### Mistake 2: Treating a 30-Second CTV Spot Like a TV Commercial

A coffee roastery in Austin — think wholesale beans plus a retail counter — ran a 30-second CTV ad that opened with slow drone footage of their roastery, cut to smiling baristas, and ended with their logo. It was beautiful. It generated zero measurable response.

The problem was the ad was designed for passive consumption, not action. Traditional TV commercials work because you're supposed to remember the brand when you're in the grocery aisle three days later. CTV viewers are sitting 6 feet from a screen with a phone in their hand. If you don't give them something to do right now, they scroll past.

We rewrote the creative to 15 seconds. First 3 seconds: "Your morning coffee shouldn't be boring." Next 5 seconds: free latte with any bean purchase, show the QR code. Final 7 seconds: "Swipe up on the remote or search 'Austin Roast Co' on your phone." We added a unique promo code visible at the end of every ad break.

The outcome: In the first month, they redeemed 47 promo codes averaging $12 per transaction. That's $564 in immediate tracked revenue from a $500 ad budget. More importantly, their cost-per-acquisition (CPA) hit $10.64 on a product with high repeat purchase potential. The 30-second version drove exactly zero redemptions.

### Mistake 3: Not Measuring Anything and Hoping for the Best

A fitness studio in Nashville spent $1,200 on a CTV campaign through their Facebook Ads rep who said they could "just throw it in." The rep set up the campaign, the ads ran for 30 days, and the studio owner got a report showing "impressions" and "reach." That was it. No clue if anyone walked through the door.

I see this constantly. Agencies and platforms love reporting impressions because the numbers look big. 50,000 impressions sounds impressive. It tells you nothing about whether someone bought a class pack.

The fix was adding measurable conversion points. We set up a unique landing page on their Square site with a specific URL — studio-name.com/freetrial — that only appeared in the CTV ad. We also created a promo code ("NASHVILLE20") that viewers could enter when booking through Booksy. Finally, we added a phone number with a call tracking extension through Google Ads so we could see which calls came from the campaign.

The outcome: Out of $1,200 in spend, 34 people booked a free trial class through the unique URL. 19 of those converted to paid memberships averaging $89/month. That's $1,691 in recurring monthly revenue from one campaign. The studio owner now runs CTV ads every month and tracks everything through Square and Booksy analytics.

## How to Actually Track Whether Your CTV Ads Are Working (Without a CMO)

Most streaming ad platforms offer targeting. Very few offer clean attribution for a single-location business. Here's what actually works for the small business owners I've worked with.

**UTM Parameters Are Not Optional.** Every single CTV ad you run should send traffic to a URL with UTM parameters that distinguish it from your other marketing channels. If you're advertising a $10-off service, the link should look something like yourwebsite.com/offer?utm_source=ctv&utm_medium=streaming&utm_campaign=spring2025. Mailchimp and Square both read UTM data natively. Google Analytics will show you exactly how many people visited that page, how long they stayed, and whether they took action.

**Unique Promo Codes Work Better Than You Think.** When I ran ads for a hair salon in Portland, we used a distinct code for each streaming platform — "PODX10" for Hulu, "TUBI20" for Tubi, "PLUTO15" for Pluto TV. The salon's booking system (they used Booksy) let us track which codes were redeemed. Hulu drove the most redemptions at a $22 CPA. Tubi was cheaper per impression but had lower conversion rates. Without the codes, they'd have no idea which platform was working.

**Phone Call Tracking Is Worth the Setup Cost.** If your business gets calls from CTV ads, install a call tracking number through Google Ads (around $5/month per number) or a dedicated service like CallRail. I had a carpet cleaning company in Chicago that ran CTV ads and attributed 40% of their new phone calls to the campaign — but only after they started tracking calls. Before that, they assumed the phone was ringing from their Google Business Profile.

**Compare Cost Per Acquisition, Not Cost Per Impression.** I don't care if your CPM is $18 or $35. I care how much it costs to get a booking. A $35 CPM that generates a $15 CPA on a $90 service is fantastic. A $15 CPM that generates zero bookings is a waste. Set your tracking up before you launch, not after.

## Which Streaming Platform Makes Sense for Your Budget

Not all streaming inventory is the same. Here's how the platforms actually break down for small business budgets.

**Hulu (AVOD tier):** CPMs run $25–40 depending on market and time of year. Hulu offers zip code targeting but requires a minimum daily spend of around $500 at most programmatic partners. For a single-location business, that's usually too high unless you're in a dense urban area. I've seen it work for a dentist in NYC who had a $2,000 monthly budget and needed to fill a specific zip code. For a coffee shop in Cleveland? Probably not.

**Pluto TV and Tubi (FAST platforms):** These are free, ad-supported streaming services. CPMs run $12–20. They accept smaller budgets — $300–500 minimum monthly spend is common through managed DSPs. The audience skews older (35–55) and the inventory is more linear-like than Hulu. I ran a campaign for a restaurant supply store in Minneapolis through Tubi. Budget was $500/month. They got 40,000 impressions and saw a 10% lift in customers mentioning the ad at checkout. Not trackable to the dollar, but the owner could hear people saying "I saw you on Tubi."

**YouTube on TV Screens:** This is technically CTV (because it's on a TV) but YouTube's targeting is more advanced than traditional streaming. You can target by search history, YouTube channel subscriptions, and life events — "people who watched home renovation videos last month." CPMs are lower ($10–15) but the inventory includes user-generated content, not premium shows. A handyman in Phoenix ran YouTube CTV ads for $400/month, targeted to "homeowners in 85014 who watched plumbing tutorials." He booked 8 new jobs at an average of $350 each. $2,800 in revenue from $400 in spend.

**Local News Streaming Apps:** Many local TV stations now stream their broadcasts through apps like Very Local or their own branded apps. CPMs are $18–25. Targeting is usually by DMA only, not zip code. This works best if you serve an entire metro area — a moving company, a real estate agent, or a home services franchise. I tested this for a painting company in Portland that covered the whole metro. $600/month in spend generated 12 leads, 5 of which converted at an average job value of $2,200. That's $11,000 in revenue from $600 in spend.

The practical advice: If your budget is under $500/month, start with YouTube CTV or a FAST platform like Tubi. If you're at $1,000+ and need neighborhood-level targeting, go through a managed DSP that can restrict delivery by zip code. Hulu makes sense if you have the budget and want premium environment. It doesn't make sense if you have $300 and want to reach three specific streets.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I run CTV ads if I only have $300 a month?**

Yes, but you need to pick the right platform. YouTube CTV and Tubi accept lower minimums through self-serve or managed DSPs. Avoid Hulu at that budget. Focus on one zip code or one neighborhood. Track everything with a unique URL or promo code. You'll get smaller reach but higher relevance.

**Q: Do CTV ads work for service businesses like plumbers or electricians?**

They can, but the creative needs to match. Don't show a generic "we fix pipes" ad. Show a specific problem — a flooded basement — and a specific solution. A plumber in Denver ran CTV ads on Tubi with a "free inspection" offer. He tracked calls through a unique phone number. $600 in spend generated 18 phone calls, 7 of which turned into jobs averaging $450 each. $3,150 in revenue. It works if you treat it like direct response, not brand awareness.

**Q: Will my ads run on Netflix or Disney+?**

No. Netflix and Disney+ are subscription-based (SVOD) and do not serve ads on their base tiers. Netflix recently launched an ad-supported tier, but access is currently limited to large advertisers with six-figure commitments. This may change as the platforms open up, but for 2024, you cannot buy ads on Netflix or Disney+ as a small business.

**Q: How is this different from running a Facebook video ad?**

Facebook video ads run in the Facebook app on a phone. CTV ads run on a television screen. The viewing context is completely different. Someone on Facebook is scrolling and can tap to skip in 2 seconds. Someone on CTV is leaning back watching a show. CTV ads are unskippable for 15–30 seconds in many placements. The cost is higher but the attention is higher. Both can work, but they serve different parts of the funnel. Facebook is better for remarketing and retargeting. CTV is better for reaching new households.

**Q: Can I target by zip code or do I have to buy the whole city?**

Zip code targeting is available on most DSPs, but the platform matters. Tubi and Pluto TV offer zip-level targeting through programmatic partners. YouTube CTV offers radius targeting around a location. Hulu offers DMA-level targeting but some partners can overlay zip code restrictions. If your DSP says you can only target the entire metro area, find a different partner.

**Q: Is CTV advertising just TV advertising on a different screen?**

No. Traditional TV buys are done by daypart and network. You buy a 30-second spot at 6:30 PM on the local news. CTV is programmatic — you buy specific households based on data. Traditional TV cannot target "households that searched for pet grooming last week." CTV can. Traditional TV cannot stop showing ads after someone visits your website. CTV can (through frequency caps and retargeting). They're fundamentally different buying mechanisms that happen to use the same screen.
