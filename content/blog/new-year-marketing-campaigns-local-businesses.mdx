## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating New Year Traffic Like Any Other Traffic

A yoga studio owner in Austin, Texas came to me in January last year. She had cranked up her Google Ads budget from $400/month to $1,200/month on January 2, expecting the resolution crowd to convert like her regular warm leads. By January 15, she had burned through $600 with a 2.1% conversion rate. Her usual rate is 8%.

Here's what happened: she was bidding on "yoga classes Austin" and "beginner yoga" — same keywords that work in March. But January searches are different. People searching "New Year yoga resolutions Austin" or "how to start yoga in 2024" are browsing, not buying. They're three weeks away from booking anything.

**The fix:** I had her build a separate campaign targeting resolution-adjacent keywords with lower bids and a lead magnet — a free "7-Day Resolution Starter" class pass. Budget: $500/month. The ad copy explicitly said "No commitment, just show up." Conversion rate jumped to 6.5%, and 42% of those free pass holders converted to a paid membership within 30 days.

**The outcome:** She spent $500, generated 87 free pass sign-ups, and 37 of those became paying members at $89/month. That's $3,293 in monthly recurring revenue from a campaign that would have otherwise been a $600 loss.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring the People Who Already Know You

A pet groomer in Portland, Oregon was running a "New Year, New Do" promotion on Instagram. She got 14 bookings. Then she sent the exact same offer to her email list of 340 past clients — no discount, just a "we have openings in January" note with a photo of a poodle she did last week. She got 52 bookings in three days.

Think about that. Her entire Instagram campaign cost her $200 in ads and netted 14 bookings. A single email to people who already trusted her cost $0 and netted 52.

I've seen this at three different clients. Small business owners obsess over acquisition in January while their existing customer list sits there, completely ignored. Retention is 5x cheaper than acquisition. And in January, when everyone is fighting for the same resolution crowd, your warm list is your moat.

**The fix:** Send a "Welcome to the New Year" email to anyone who booked with you in the last 12 months. No big discount, just a friendly heads-up that you're booking for February now. Include a direct booking link. That's it.

**The outcome:** The pet groomer filled her calendar through March without spending another dollar on ads. She estimates $4,200 in additional revenue from that single email that took her 12 minutes to write.

### Mistake #3: Setting Up a Campaign and Walking Away

A hair salon owner in Denver spent $800 on a New Year Google Ads campaign in early January. She set it up on January 3, checked it once on January 10, and let it run until February 1. The campaign spent $800 and generated 14 calls. She booked 6 of them. That's a $133 cost per booked appointment.

I looked at the search terms report. She was paying for clicks from people searching "haircuts near me" in a different part of Denver — 8 miles away. Also, her "New Year hair transformation" ad was showing for "hair donation" queries. She was paying $2.50 per click for people who wanted to donate their hair to charity.

**The fix:** Set a 15-minute calendar reminder every Monday in January to check your search terms report. Add negative keywords for irrelevant terms. Adjust your radius targeting. Look at what time of day your ads are getting clicks vs. conversions — they're rarely the same.

**The outcome:** After two weeks of weekly adjustments, her cost per booked appointment dropped from $133 to $38. Same budget, better targeting. She booked an additional 11 appointments in the second half of January that would have gone to competitors.

### Mistake #4: Forgetting That January Is a Low-Trust Month

A coffee shop owner in Nashville spent $600 on Facebook ads promoting a "New Year Loyalty Card" — buy 10 coffees, get one free. The ad reached 12,000 people. She got 37 new sign-ups. That's $16 per new loyalty card member.

The problem? January is when every business is pushing a promotion. Consumers are skeptical. They've been bombarded with "New Year, New You" messaging since December 26. They're tired of being sold to.

**The fix:** Instead of a promotion, run a "We survived the holiday rush — here's what we learned" email or social post. Show a behind-the-scenes photo of your team on December 26. Talk about the 400 pounds of coffee beans you went through. Be human. Then, at the end, casually mention that loyalty cards are available if anyone wants one.

**The outcome:** The coffee shop owner posted a photo of her team holding a whiteboard that said "416 lb of coffee brewed in December. See you in January." It got 2,300 organic reach, 84 comments, and 29 people asked about the loyalty card without any ad spend.

## How to Structure Your Google Ads for January Behavior

Most local businesses set up one campaign and hope for the best. January requires a different structure because the searcher is different.

The resolution crowd searches differently than the regular customer. A fitness studio in Chicago might normally bid on "HIIT classes Chicago" and get $12 cost per conversion. In January, "HIIT classes Chicago" gets more searches but also more competition — bigger gyms with bigger budgets are buying those same keywords at 2x the price.

**What actually works:** Create three separate ad groups:

**Ad Group 1: The Browsers.** Keywords like "New Year fitness classes Chicago," "what gym should I join," "best workout for beginners." These people aren't buying today. Bid lower — $1-$2 per click. Send them to a blog post or a no-obligation free class sign-up. Accept that most won't convert immediately. Track them with a pixel and retarget them in two weeks.

**Ad Group 2: The Decision-Makers.** Keywords like "sign up for fitness classes Chicago," "gym membership Chicago prices," "HIIT class schedule." These people are actively comparing. Bid higher — $3-$5 per click. Send them to a booking page with clear pricing, class times, and a "Reserve Your Spot" button. Your ad copy should say "Limited January availability" — scarcity works in January because they're afraid of missing out.

**Ad Group 3: The Location-Specific.** Keywords like "fitness studio Lincoln Park," "gym near Wrigleyville." These people want convenience. Bid at $2.50-$3.50. Your ad copy should mention the neighborhood. "Lincoln Park's highest-rated HIIT studio. 2 blocks from the Brown Line."

I set this up for a fitness studio owner in Chicago in January last year. Her old campaign: one group, $700 spent, 14 conversions, $50 per conversion. The new three-group structure: same $700 budget, 31 conversions, $22.58 per conversion. The Browsers group only converted 4% initially, but retargeting those people in February brought in another 9 conversions at $15 each.

The uncomfortable truth is that most Google Ads guides tell you to consolidate your campaigns for efficiency. In January, the opposite is true. You need to separate the browsers from the buyers because they require completely different messaging, bids, and landing pages.

## Using Offline Signals to Fill Your Calendar (Not Just Online)

Most small business owners focus entirely on online marketing — ads, SEO, social media. They forget that their existing operations generate the strongest signals for what to market next.

I worked with a massage therapist in NYC who runs her own studio. She was frustrated that January bookings were slow despite her "New Year relaxation package" ad campaign. I asked her one question: "What are your clients actually asking for when they come in?"

She thought about it. "Well, a lot of them have been complaining about neck and shoulder pain from sitting at their desks more. And a few have mentioned they want gift certificates to give their friends."

She had been advertising a relaxation package that no one was asking for. Her existing clients were giving her the exact campaign brief — she just hadn't listened.

**The fix:** She ran a specific "Desk Worker Relief" package — 60 minutes focused on neck, shoulders, and upper back. Priced at $95 (her standard rate was $120 for a full-body massage). She didn't run ads. She sent a one-paragraph email to her list of 220 past clients: "I've been hearing a lot of you mention desk-related tension. Here's a 60-minute session focused on exactly that. A few January slots are open."

She got 18 bookings in 48 hours. The email took her 10 minutes.

**The tool:** She used Booksy for scheduling and Mailchimp for the email. Total monthly cost: $15. The campaign generated $1,710 in revenue.

The lesson: your clients are telling you what to market. Every question they ask, every complaint they make, every offhand comment — that's market research you're already paying for. You just have to write it down and act on it.

For a pet groomer, that might mean noticing that 40% of your December clients asked about "dematting services" because of winter coat issues. Market your dematting service in January, not your standard "New Year, New Do" package.

For a coffee shop, it might mean noticing that 6 people a day ask if you have a punch card system. Instead of buying a fancy loyalty app, print 500 punch cards and put them by the register.

The tools don't have to be expensive. Square has free email marketing for the first 1,000 contacts. Mailchimp is free up to 500 contacts. Booksy and Mindbody both have built-in email features. Yelp Business is free to claim and update. The barrier isn't cost — it's paying attention to what your customers are already telling you.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is it too late to start a New Year campaign if I haven't done anything yet?**

No. Late January and early February are actually a sweet spot. The big brands with unlimited budgets blast their campaigns on January 1-7 and then pull back. By January 15, CPCs on Google Ads typically drop 20-35% because the big spenders have moved on. You can target people who are still resolution-motivated but haven't found a solution yet. I've seen campaigns that started January 18 outperform early-January campaigns because the audience is more intent-driven. Start now, keep it running through Valentine's Day.

**Q: Should I offer a discount or will that cheapen my brand?**

Discounts cheapen your brand if you frame them as discounts. If you frame them as "we have limited January availability and want to fill specific time slots," that's not a discount — that's efficiency. A massage therapist I work with in San Francisco offers $10 off for any booking between 2-4 PM on weekdays in January. That's not "we're cheap" — that's "our slowest hours are a deal." She fills slots that would otherwise be empty. Same goes for pet groomers offering Tuesday/Wednesday-only appointments. Don't run a blanket 20% off. Target specific inventory that's going to waste.

**Q: Is Google Ads or Facebook better for New Year local campaigns?**

Google Ads, by a wide margin. In January, people are searching for specific services — they have intent. Facebook interrupts their scrolling with an ad that says "hey, you should do something." One is demand capture (Google), the other is demand creation (Facebook). For local businesses in January, demand capture is more reliable because the search volume is already there. Facebook works better as a retargeting channel for people who visited your website but didn't book. Run Google Ads for acquisition, run Facebook retargeting for the people who clicked but didn't convert.

**Q: I have a small budget — $300-$500/month. Is that even worth it?**

Yes, but only if you're surgical about it. A $300 budget spread across 20 keywords is useless. A $300 budget focused on 3-5 high-intent keywords with a tight radius (2-3 miles) will work. I had a hair salon in Austin with a $400/month budget. We focused on "women's haircut [neighborhood]" and "balayage [neighborhood]" — two keywords. She got 12 bookings in January, $96 average ticket, $1,152 in revenue. At $400 ad spend, that's a 2.88x ROAS. Not amazing, but profitable. The key is extreme specificity: narrow keywords, narrow radius, narrow audience.

**Q: Do I need to hire someone to manage this, or can I do it myself?**

You can do it yourself if you have 2-3 hours per week to dedicate to it. Set up the campaign, check it every Monday for 15 minutes (search terms, negative keywords, ad schedule performance), and adjust. What kills DIY campaigns isn't the setup — it's the abandonment. If you're going to set it up and never touch it again, hire someone. If you can commit to weekly 15-minute check-ins, do it yourself. I've seen both paths work. The difference isn't skill — it's discipline.

**Q: What's the one thing I should do today that will make the biggest difference?**

Call or text 5 past clients and ask how their holidays were. No sales pitch. Just a genuine check-in. That single action will generate more goodwill and more word-of-mouth referrals than any ad campaign. I had a coffee shop owner in Denver text 12 regulars on January 2 saying "Hope you survived the holidays. First coffee on me when you're back." Three of them came in that week with friends. He spent $15 on free coffee. Those three visits generated $87 in additional sales from the friends who bought pastries and other drinks. And those friends came back on their own later. Human connection in January is your cheapest and most effective marketing tool.
