Your coffee shop’s Google Ads might cost $1.80 per click, but if each visit only brings a $2.50 profit, you’re losing money. Use this formula:

**ROI % = ((Revenue - Cost) / Cost) × 100**

Example: A $300/month Meta ad campaign for a dog walker gets 15 new clients at $40 each = $600 revenue
ROI = ((600 - 300)/300) × 100 = **100%**

<Callout type="warning">Don’t forget to count time costs. That 100% ROI looks better until you factor in 10 hours/week managing the campaign.</Callout>

**The fix:** We shifted $600 of that budget to Google Local Services Ads and $400 to a simple postcard mailer targeting three zip codes within a 1-mile radius. **The outcome:** Monthly ad spend stayed the same, but booked jobs went from 20 to 31. Revenue increased by $7,500/month. The owner told me he felt stupid for not tracking calls sooner. I told him I’ve seen that exact blind spot at agencies billing $2M/year.

## How to Build a Simple ROI Tracker You’ll Actually Use

I know you don’t want another spreadsheet. But the reason most small businesses don’t track ROI isn’t that it’s hard — it’s that they overcomplicate it from the start. You don’t need a dashboard. You need a notebook and a calendar reminder.

Here’s the minimum viable tracking system I’ve used with eight different clients:

**Step 1: Pick three channels max.** If you’re running Google Ads, Yelp, Facebook, email, flyers, and billboards, you’re not tracking anything. Pick the two channels that bring in 80% of your customers and one experimental channel. For a coffee shop in Chicago, that might be: Google Business Profile, a weekly email to 500 subscribers, and one small Facebook ad test.

**Step 2: Assign a tracking mechanism to each channel.** For calls, use a unique phone number (Google Voice is free). For website visits, use UTM parameters — I’ve written these on sticky notes for clients who kept forgetting. For in-store redemptions, use a printed coupon code or a “mention this ad” offer. You need one concrete signal per channel. Not “brand awareness.” A signal.

**Step 3: Log results on the same day every week.** I tell clients to do it while their morning coffee is brewing. Open a Google Sheet with four columns: Channel, Cost This Week, New Customers Attributed, Revenue from Those Customers. That’s it. No formulas that break. No pivot tables. Four columns.

**Step 4: Calculate ROI every 90 days, not every week.** Weekly ROI calculations drive people insane because the numbers jump around. A slow week isn’t a failure. A good week isn’t a strategy. Look at the three-month trend and ask: did this channel pay for itself? If yes, keep going. If no, reallocate.

A real example: A barbershop in Brooklyn ran this system for three months. They tracked $1,200 spent on Google Ads, which generated 47 appointments (average ticket $45). That’s $2,115 in revenue — 1.76x ROI. Not great, but positive. They tracked $0 spent on their Google Business Profile, which generated 89 calls that booked. That’s $4,005 in revenue. The owner put more effort into profile optimization and less into ads. His overall marketing ROI went from 1.2x to 3.8x in six months, with no budget increase.

## Why Most Small Businesses Get Google Ads Wrong (And How to Fix It)

I’ve managed Google Ads for more than 40 accounts across the US and Europe. The mistakes are almost always the same, regardless of city or industry. And they’re all fixable in an afternoon.

**The biggest error: Broad match keywords.** Google’s default setting for new campaigns is to show your ads to anyone who might vaguely be interested. A pet groomer in Denver bidding on “dog grooming” had their ad shown to people searching for “dog food near me” and “grooming tools for poodles.” They spent $340 in one week with zero clicks that turned into bookings. I switched them to phrase match and exact match only. Their clicks dropped by half, but their conversion rate tripled. They stopped wasting money on people who weren’t actually looking for a service.

**The second error: No negative keywords.** I reviewed a hair salon’s account in Portland and found they were showing up for “hair salon jobs,” “hair salon for sale,” and “how to open a hair salon.” Those searches cost them $180/month. A list of 20 negative keywords — “career,” “jobs,” “salary,” “franchise,” “DIY” — eliminated that spend entirely. You can build this list in 15 minutes using the search terms report in Google Ads.

**The third error: Sending everyone to the homepage.** A fitness studio in Austin was running ads for “personal training Austin” and landing people on their general website homepage. The homepage had three offers, a class schedule, a studio tour video, and a blog. Everything looked professional, but the ad visitors clicked around for 11 seconds and left. We created a dedicated landing page with one headline (“One Free Session. No Commitment.”), a booking form, and a map. Conversion rate went from 2.1% to 9.6%. The landing page took two hours to build using Carrd.

**The fix that costs nothing: Dayparting.** Most small service businesses get 60% of their calls between 9am and 12pm. If you’re running ads at 3am, you’re paying for clicks that will never become customers. Set your ads to run only during business hours or 90 minutes before open. A coffee shop in Chicago reduced their ad spend by 22% with zero revenue impact just by pausing overnight ads. That’s $132/month back in their pocket.

## How to Track ROI from Offline Marketing (Flyers, Postcards, Referrals)

Small business owners often tell me “offline marketing is impossible to track.” It’s not. You just need to stop expecting the same precision as digital ads and accept that good-enough tracking beats no tracking.

**Flyers and postcards:** Use a unique phone number or URL. A dentist in Nashville printed 2,000 postcards with the URL “smile-nashville.com/flyer” and directed that to a landing page. From that, they tracked 34 appointments — 1.7% response rate. The campaign cost $800 (printing + postage). Those 34 appointments generated $13,600 in revenue. They ran the same campaign three more times that year. Total cost: $3,200. Total revenue: $54,400.

**Referral programs:** A dog groomer in Portland used Square to create a “Refer a Friend” card. The card gave the referrer $10 off their next grooming and the friend $10 off their first. The groomer tracked redemptions through Square’s register system. In six months, 112 referrals came through, generating $8,960 in new customer revenue and $1,120 in referral discounts. The net gain was $7,840 from a program that cost $15 to set up.

**Community events:** A bakery in Denver sponsored a neighborhood block party for $250. They brought a banner with their logo and handed out coupons with a QR code that tracked to a specific Mailchimp landing page. They collected 87 email signups and 22 people came in with the coupon the following week, spending an average of $18 each. That’s $416 in direct revenue from a $250 event — plus 87 email addresses they could market to for free.

**The system that works for everything: Offer codes.** Make every marketing channel use a different offer code. Flyer in the park? Use code PARK10. Email blast? Use code EMAIL20. Yellow Pages ad? Use code YP15. A pet store in Austin ran nine different offer codes across nine channels for one quarter. They learned that the $300 local magazine ad generated $42 in revenue, and the $50 Facebook boost generated $280. They cut the magazine ad and doubled the Facebook boost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I only have $500/month to spend on marketing. Which channel should I prioritize?**

Start with Google Business Profile optimization and local SEO. Both are free. For paid channels, put your $500 into Google Local Services Ads if you’re a service business (plumber, salon, pet groomer). If you’re retail or hospitality, put it into Google Ads with exact match keywords on your city + service. Skip Facebook ads until you have $1,000/month minimum to test properly.

**Q: My Google Ads are getting clicks but no sales. What am I doing wrong?**

Three most likely problems: your landing page doesn’t match the ad, your keywords are too broad, or your offer isn’t compelling. First, check that people who click are seeing a page that directly matches the ad they clicked. Second, pull your search terms report and add negative keywords for anything irrelevant. Third, make sure your offer is clear and urgent — “Book Today and Get 10% Off” outperforms “We Offer Quality Service” every time.

**Q: Do I need to track ROI for organic social media like Instagram and Facebook?**

Only if you’re spending time or money on it. If you’re posting three times a week and spending two hours doing it, that’s 104 hours a year. If those posts don’t generate bookings, calls, or in-store visits, you’re effectively paying yourself $0/hour for that time. Track whether people click the link in your bio, call from your profile, or mention seeing you on Instagram. If you can’t trace at least $500 in monthly revenue to your social media, it’s a hobby, not marketing.

**Q: How do I know if my email marketing is actually working?**

Look at one number: revenue per email sent. Divide total revenue attributed to email by total emails sent in a month. For a coffee shop sending weekly emails to 500 subscribers, if they generate $1,200 in monthly sales from those emails, that’s $2.40 per email sent — excellent. For a plumbing company sending to 200 subscribers, if they generate $0, something’s broken (bad subject lines, irrelevant offers, or people just aren’t opening). Most small businesses do well with $0.50–$1.50 per email sent.

**Q: Can I track ROI from Yelp ads, or is it a waste?**

You can track it, but most small businesses shouldn’t run them. Yelp ads work best for high-ticket, immediate-need services (plumbers, locksmiths, tow trucks). For coffee shops, salons, and pet groomers, the cost per lead is usually 3-5x higher than Google Ads. Track it with a unique phone number for 60 days. If your cost per booked appointment exceeds $50, stop. I’ve seen Yelp ads generate $80–$120 per lead for a hair salon in Chicago. That math rarely works.

**Q: I’m too busy running my business to track marketing. Can I just hire someone once a month?**

Yes. But hire someone who will set up tracking so you don’t need them every month. A good marketer should spend their first two weeks building systems — UTM parameters, call tracking, a simple spreadsheet — that you can glance at in five minutes. If the person you hire wants to meet weekly to “review performance” but hasn’t set up tracking, they’re not solving the problem. They’re selling you meetings.
