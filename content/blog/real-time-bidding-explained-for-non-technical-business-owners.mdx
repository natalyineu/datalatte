## What Is Real-Time Bidding (RTB)?

Real-Time Bidding (RTB) is the process of **automatically buying and selling digital ad space in real time**, usually on third-party websites or apps. Every time someone visits a site, space for an ad opens up, and advertisers like you bid on that space through an auction  -  all within **100 milliseconds**.

Think of it like this:
You're a local coffee shop owner. Someone visits a lifestyle blog. The blog needs to show an ad, and your ad could be shown to that person if you win the bid. That's RTB in action.

### How RTB Works in 3 Simple Steps:
1. **Ad Space Opens**  -  A user visits a website, and the site has a blank spot for an ad.
2. **Auction Happens**  -  Advertisers like you bid on that space in real time using automated systems.
3. **Ad is Shown**  -  The highest bidder's ad is displayed to the user.

The whole process is automated, and the technology handles it for you. You don't need to manually place your ad.

## Why Should You Care About RTB?

Because **RTB can help you reach the right people at the right time**  -  and more efficiently than traditional ad buying.

Here's what makes RTB powerful for small businesses:

### 1. **More Control Over Your Audience**
With RTB, you can target users based on factors like:
- **Demographics** (age, gender, location)
- **Behavior** (what they clicked, what they bought)
- **Context** (what website they're on, what device they're using)

### 2. **Better Budget Control**
RTB typically uses a **cost-per-thousand-impressions (CPM)** model, but you can also use **cost-per-click (CPC)** depending on the platform. You decide how much you're willing to pay for an ad view or a click.

### 3. **More Transparent and Efficient**
You're only bidding on ad space when it's relevant. This means **no wasted impressions** and higher chances of reaching people who are actually interested in your service, like your hair salon or fitness studio.

## How RTB Differs From Google Ads and Meta Ads

RTB isn't Google Ads or Meta Ads  -  but it's related. These platforms use programmatic ad buying in the background using RTB.

### RTB vs Google Ads:
- **Google Ads** are **search-based**, meaning you bid on keywords people use when looking for services like yours.
- **RTB** is **display-based**, meaning you bid to show your ad on websites your target audience visits.

### RTB vs Meta Ads:
- **Meta Ads** (Facebook, Instagram) are **social media ads** with a mix of search and display characteristics.
- **RTB** is **programmatic display**, meaning it's used more for banner ads on third-party websites.

You can use both  -  and they work best together. For example, you might use Google Ads to catch people actively searching, and RTB to follow them as they browse the web.

## Is RTB Right for Your Small Business?

Yes  -  if you:
- Want to **show ads to people who aren't actively searching** for your service (like pet groomers or fitness studios).
- Are targeting **local customers** and want to follow them online.
- Need a **more visual or branding-focused ad strategy**.

However, RTB is **not ideal** if:
- You're on a **very tight budget**, as programmatic ads often require a minimum investment.
- You're a **new business** with no data or brand awareness to build on.
- You prefer **total control** over your ad placements (RTB is automated, not manual).

## How to Get Started with RTB

Starting with RTB doesn't require technical knowledge. Here's a simple, step-by-step guide for small business owners:

### 1. Choose a DSP (Demand-Side Platform)
A DSP is your control center for RTB. Popular options include:
- **DV360** (Google's platform)
- **The Trade Desk**

> [Want to compare the pros and cons of DV360 vs The Trade Desk? Read our guide here.](/blog/dv360-vs-the-trade-desk-which-dsp-is-right-for-you)

### 2. Set Up Audience Targeting
Define your ideal customer in the DSP. For example, if you run a yoga studio:
- Target **women aged 25-45**
- Who live in your city
- Who have searched for "yoga near me" in the last 30 days

### 3. Set Your Budget and Bids
Start small. Most platforms let you set a daily budget and a maximum bid per impression.

### 4. Design Your Ad
Use high-quality visuals and a clear call-to-action. For local businesses, try to include:
- Your business name
- Location
- A link to your Google Business Profile or website

### 5. Monitor and Optimize
RTB is not a "set it and forget it" strategy. Check your performance weekly and adjust:
- Your **bids**
- Your **targeting**
- Your **creatives**

## Real-Time Bidding vs. Traditional Ad Buying

| Feature | Traditional Ad Buying | Real-Time Bidding |
|--------|------------------------|------------------|
| **How it works** | Manual negotiation with publishers | Automated auction in real time |
| **Targeting** | Limited to website or platform | Highly customizable |
| **Speed** | Weeks or months to place ads | Instant placement |
| **Cost** | Fixed rate per impression or space | Dynamic, based on competition |
| **Transparency** | Low  -  you get a report later | High  -  see where your ads are shown in real time |

RTB wins on speed, targeting, and transparency  -  but it requires more ongoing management.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched small business owners burn through thousands of dollars on programmatic ads. Not because the concept is hard, but because the execution is full of traps that nobody warns you about. Here are the four I see most often.

### Mistake 1: Targeting Too Broadly Because You Want “Everyone”

A women’s boutique in Austin called me after spending $1,400 in three weeks on RTB ads. They wanted to “get the word out.” Their targeting: all women aged 18–65 within 50 miles of the store.

What happened? They got 43,000 impressions and exactly two store visits. The boutique owner told me she paid $700 per foot in the door. That’s not marketing. That’s arson.

**What went wrong:** 50 miles in Austin covers Round Rock, Buda, Kyle, and parts of San Antonio. Most of those people are never driving to South Congress to buy a dress. The 18–25 demographic wasn’t buying $200 dresses. The 55+ audience was already loyal and didn’t need ads.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the campaign targeting women 28–48 within 8 miles of the store, layered with purchase intent signals (recently searched for “cocktail dress Austin” or visited competitors). We also excluded anyone who had visited the website in the last 7 days.

**The outcome:** CPM rose from $2.10 to $4.80 because we were bidding on quality inventory, not random page views. But cost per store visit dropped from $700 to $38. Same $1,400 monthly budget produced 37 visits instead of 2. Revenue from those visits was roughly $4,600. The previous campaign generated maybe $400.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Creative Completely

A pet grooming salon in Portland thought RTB was magic. They uploaded their logo and a generic “We groom dogs!” text. No photo. No special offer. No urgency.

The owner told me, “I figured the targeting would do the work.” It won’t. The algorithm bids on ad slots based on probability that a person will engage. If your creative looks like spam, no amount of smart bidding matters.

**What went wrong:** Their ad had a 0.02% click-through rate. For context, 0.10% is considered bad for display. They were paying for impressions that nobody acted on.

**The fix:** We swapped the generic text for a photo of a freshly groomed golden retriever with the headline “$15 Off Any Full Groom — This Week Only.” Red background, white text, one button. Took 20 minutes to make in Canva.

**The outcome:** CTR jumped to 0.31%. Cost per click dropped from $1.80 to $0.42. More importantly, they booked 22 new grooming appointments that month through the ad. Average ticket for a full groom is $65. That’s $1,430 in revenue from a $600 ad spend. The previous month they spent $500 and got zero attributable bookings.

### Mistake 3: Not Excluding People Who Already Know You

A coffee roaster in Denver (two locations) set up an RTB campaign to drive awareness. They didn’t upload their customer list as an exclusion. They didn’t exclude past website visitors. They didn’t exclude people who had already purchased online.

For three months, they served ads to their existing customers. Those customers saw the ad, didn’t click (why would they?), and the algorithm learned that their ads were being ignored. The platform started penalizing them with higher CPMs because it thought nobody was interested.

**What went wrong:** The business spent $1,200 over three months showing ads to people who already had a bag of their coffee in the kitchen. Worse, the algorithm got confused and started bidding on wrong audience segments.

**The fix:** We uploaded their email list (550 subscribers) as an exclusion. Then we set up two separate campaigns — one for new audiences, one for existing customers with a specific “come back” offer. We also installed a pixel to exclude anyone who had visited the website in the last 30 days.

**The outcome:** The next month, CPM dropped from $6.50 to $3.20 (because the algorithm was finally seeing fresh engagement). Cost per new site visitor went from $1.80 to $0.55. The roaster also ran a retargeting campaign for existing customers using different messaging — “Try our new seasonal blend” — which produced a 12% conversion rate. Total monthly spend stayed at $800. Estimated attributable revenue rose from $1,900 to $4,200.

### Mistake 4: Setting and Forgetting (The Silent Budget Killer)

A fitness studio in Nashville running small-group classes set up an RTB campaign in early January. It worked great — $1,200 in spend produced $4,800 in class passes. They left it running. In April, the same campaign produced $1,100 in revenue.

**What went wrong:** They never refreshed creative, never adjusted audiences, and never checked frequency caps. The same people saw the same ad 18 times. After the first 3 impressions, every additional showing was just noise. The studio was paying to annoy potential customers.

**The fix:** We set frequency caps at 3 impressions per user per week. We created 4 ad variations (different offers, different photos) and rotated them weekly. We also paused the campaign on days when the studio was already at 80% capacity — no point paying for ads when you have nothing to sell.

**The outcome:** Ad spend dropped to $800/month (because we weren’t wasting money on over-served audiences). Revenue stayed consistent at $4,500–$5,100/month. Monthly profit from ads went from roughly $2,800 to $3,700. All because we stopped showing the same ad to the same person on a Tuesday afternoon when they were already sick of seeing it.

## How to Actually Set Up an RTB Campaign (Without a Media Agency)

Most guides tell you to “choose a DSP” and leave you there. That’s like telling someone to “choose a car” when they need to move furniture across town. Not helpful.

Here’s the reality for a US small business owner who wants to test RTB without hiring a specialist:

**Step 1: Pick a platform you can actually manage.**

Google Ads Display Network is the most accessible option. You likely already have a Google Ads account. Display campaigns inside Google Ads are basically RTB — you’re bidding on individual impressions across millions of sites. No need to sign up for The Trade Desk or a specialized DSP.

If you want something more targeted, try **AdRoll**. It’s built for small businesses without media buyers. Starting budget is around $500/month. You can upload customer lists, connect your website, and they handle the auction mechanics. I’ve seen a Chicago bakery spend $600/month on AdRoll and get 38 new catering inquiries. That’s roughly 6 orders per month at $85 average — not life-changing, but profitable on day one.

**Step 2: Set your geography tight.**

If you’re a hair salon in Nashville, do not target all of Davidson County. Target a 5-mile radius around your studio. If you’re a coffee shop in Portland, target 2 miles unless you’re near a major commuter route. The biggest waste in small business RTB is showing ads to people who live 40 minutes away. They are never coming.

**Step 3: Create a real offer — not a brand ad.**

You are not Coca-Cola. Nobody is excited to see your logo. Give them a reason to click. A nail salon in Brooklyn ran “20% Off Your First Gel Manicure — Valid This Week Only” and got a 0.9% CTR on display. That’s triple the average. The offer cost them $8 in discount per redemption but brought in 47 new customers in 30 days. Average lifetime value of those customers was $180. Math works.

**Step 4: Install a conversion pixel and track actual events.**

Don’t track “page views.” Track “button clicked” or “form submitted” or “phone number copied.” If your website has a booking system like Booksy or Square Appointments, make sure the pixel fires when someone actually books, not just when they land on the booking page. I’ve seen businesses think they got 200 conversions when they actually got 12 bookings and 188 people who clicked the booking page and left.

**Step 5: Give it 14 days before you judge anything.**

RTB needs time to learn. Don’t look at day 2 data and panic. A Denver yoga studio paused their campaign after 3 days because they had zero bookings. I told them to wait. By day 9, they got their first booking. By day 21, they had 17 new sign-ups for their intro month. The first week was just the algorithm figuring out who actually converts.

## How Much Should a Small Business Spend on RTB? (Real Numbers, Not Theory)

I get this question every week. The answer isn’t “it depends.” It’s this:

**Start with $600–$800 per month for a single location.**

That’s roughly $20–$27 per day. In most US cities, that will get you 8,000–15,000 impressions per month on display inventory. If your targeting is tight and your creative is decent, you can expect 15–30 clicks per day. Depending on your offer and website, 2–5 of those should turn into leads or appointments.

Here’s a real example: A family-owned pizza place in Chicago spent $650/month on RTB through Google Display. They targeted a 3-mile radius, excluded anyone who had ordered online in the last 60 days, and ran a “Free Appetizer With Any Large Pizza” offer. In month one they got 22 redemptions. Average check for those customers was $38. That’s $836 in revenue off $650 in spend. Not a home run, but profitable. By month three, they tweaked the offer and got 44 redemptions. Revenue hit $1,672.

If you’re a service business (plumber, electrician, lawn care), you can spend less because your ticket price is higher. A plumber in Phoenix spent $400/month on RTB targeting homeowners who searched “water heater repair.” Their cost per lead was $12. Average job value was $340. ROI was roughly 28x.

If you’re a retailer, you need more budget because you’re competing with national brands for inventory. Expect $800–$1,200 per month to see meaningful results. A boutique fitness apparel store in Austin spent $900/month and got $3,600 in direct attributed sales. They also got 120 new email subscribers, which they then marketed to via Mailchimp. That list generated another $1,800 over the next 60 days.

**The uncomfortable truth:** If you can’t afford $500/month, RTB probably isn’t for you yet. At budgets lower than that, the platforms don’t have enough data to optimize. You’ll get sporadic, unreliable results. Better to put that $200/month into Google Local Services Ads or a Facebook community group.

## How RTB Fits With Your Other Marketing (And When It Doesn’t)

RTB is not a replacement for everything else. It’s a specific tool for a specific job.

**It works well alongside:** Google Search Ads (capture intent from people already looking), social media (build community and brand), and email (retain existing customers). RTB fills the gap — reaching people who haven’t heard of you but fit your customer profile.

**It works poorly when:** You have zero brand awareness and you’re expecting RTB to do all the work. If nobody knows your coffee shop exists, a display ad to someone in your neighborhood might get ignored because they’ve never seen your name. In that case, combine RTB with local social media ads or Yelp advertising so people see you multiple times in different places.

A pet supply store in Portland ran RTB alone for two months and got mediocre results. They added a small Facebook campaign ($300/month) targeting the same audience. The combination of seeing a Facebook post about a new shipment of dog beds and then seeing a display ad for the same store created a “oh yeah, I know them” effect. Combined spend was $900/month. Combined revenue was $4,200. The display campaign’s conversion rate doubled once Facebook had introduced the brand.

**When not to use RTB at all:** If your business relies entirely on impulse visits (a food truck, a pop-up, a seasonal stand), RTB is too slow. By the time someone sees your ad and decides to visit, you might be gone. Those businesses should focus on real-time social posts and location-based Google Ads.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I run RTB ads on a $200/month budget?**

Technically yes. Realistically, no. At that spend level, you’ll get maybe 3,000 impressions per month. The platform won’t have enough data to optimize who sees your ad. You’ll be paying for random traffic with no learning curve. I’ve seen it done, and the results are always the same — a few clicks, zero conversions, and a frustrated business owner. Save until you have $500 minimum.

**Q: How do I know my ads aren’t showing up on sketchy websites?**

Most DSPs let you block categories — news, politics, mature content, gambling. In Google Ads, you can exclude specific placements and entire categories. Set it and check every two weeks. If you see your ad on a site you don’t want to be associated with, add it to the exclusion list. I once found a Denver dentist’s ad running on a site about conspiracy theories. Took 30 seconds to block it.

**Q: Is RTB better than Facebook Ads for a local business?**

Different jobs. Facebook is better at building awareness and community — people are already scrolling and they might notice your post. RTB is better at reaching people outside your existing social graph — people who don’t follow you, haven’t visited your site, and aren’t in your lookalike audiences. Best approach: use both. Facebook for nurturing existing interest, RTB for finding new potential customers.

**Q: Do I need a website to run RTB ads?**

You need a landing page or a way to capture the person. A website is ideal, but you can send people to a Google Business Profile listing, a Square Online store, or a booking page on Booksy. The key is that the page matches the ad. If your ad says “20% off a haircut,” the page better show that exact offer. Sending people to a generic homepage is a waste.

**Q: How do I know if RTB is actually working?**

Track real business outcomes, not vanity metrics. Clicks don’t matter. Impressions don’t matter. What matters is: did someone book an appointment, make a purchase, call your business, or visit your store? Use unique phone numbers, promo codes, or booking source tracking. If you can’t tie a dollar value to a specific RTB campaign, you’re flying blind.

**Q: What’s the biggest mistake you see small businesses make with RTB?**

Expecting immediate results. RTB is not a switch you flip and money pours in. It takes two to three weeks for the algorithm to calibrate. Most people quit on day 5 because they haven’t seen a return yet. The businesses that stick with it for 30 days and actually analyze the data are the ones that see profit. The ones that panic after a slow first week end up writing a check to Google for $600 with nothing to show for it.
