**Optimizing Your Website for Local SEO**

To rank for engaged couple searches, you need to optimize your website for local SEO. Here's how:

1. ** Claim and optimize your Google Business Profile**: This is the first step in local SEO. Claim your business listing and optimize it with accurate and up-to-date information, including your business hours, address, and contact details.
2. **Use location-specific keywords**: Use keywords that include your city, state, or region in your website's content, meta tags, and titles. For example, "Wedding photographers in New York City" or "Wedding venues in Los Angeles."
3. **Create high-quality, location-specific content**: Create blog posts, videos, or infographics that showcase your expertise and highlight your location-specific services. For example, "Top 10 wedding venues in Chicago" or "Best wedding photographers in San Francisco."
4. **Build high-quality backlinks**: Build relationships with other local businesses and organizations to get high-quality backlinks to your website. This can include guest blogging, partnerships, or sponsorships.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Using "Wedding" as Your Primary Google Business Category

A photographer in Portland, Oregon came to me after six months of zero leads from Google. She had 47 reviews, a 4.8-star rating, and a website that looked like it cost $8,000. Her GBP was set to "Wedding Photographer" as the primary category.

Here's the problem: Google's algorithm treats primary categories as broad signals. When you pick "Wedding Photographer," you're competing against every vendor in Portland who also lists weddings — including florists, venues, and dress shops who set the same category. Google doesn't know what you actually do.

I changed her primary category to "Photographer" and added "Wedding Photographer" as a secondary category. Counterintuitive, I know. But "Photographer" is a narrower category in Google's taxonomy — it tells the algorithm you take pictures. The wedding signal comes from your service area, reviews, and content.

Result: Three weeks later, she appeared in the local map pack for "engagement photographer Portland." Leads went from zero to 8-12 inquiries per week. Her monthly ad spend on Instagram dropped from $1,200 to $400 because organic traffic replaced paid. She didn't change anything else.

The fix: Go into your GBP. If your primary category is a wedding-specific term, change it to the broader service category. Add wedding as secondary. Test for two weeks. Watch your impressions change.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Your "Service Area" Settings

A wedding florist in Austin, Texas told me she was getting calls from people in San Antonio. That's 80 miles away. She was paying $900/month for Google Ads targeting a 50-mile radius. Her conversion rate was 1.2%.

I looked at her GBP. She had listed her physical address but no service area. Google was showing her business to anyone searching for florists within 100 miles. That meant she was competing with San Antonio florists for searches in San Antonio — and losing, because local businesses get priority.

We removed the "deliver to" radius from her ad targeting, set her GBP service area to 15 miles, and added "Austin" to her business description. She also emailed her 83 past couples using Mailchimp and asked for Google reviews mentioning "Austin wedding florist" specifically.

Month one: Ad spend dropped to $450. Revenue from ads stayed flat at $3,200 because the calls were actually local now. Month three: Organic leads doubled. She stopped running ads entirely by month five.

The uncomfortable truth: Many wedding business owners set their service area to "50 miles" because they're afraid of missing potential clients. But Google punishes vagueness. You'd rather rank #1 for a 10-mile radius than #7 for 50 miles.

### Mistake #3: Treating Wedding Wire and The Knot Like Set-It-and-Forget-It Directories

A wedding DJ in Nashville spent $2,800 on a Wedding Wire premium listing. He got 14 leads in six months. That's $200 per lead. Most didn't book.

He assumed the platform did the work. He never updated his profile, never responded to reviews, and listed the same five photos he'd used for two years. Meanwhile, a competitor who paid for the same tier was updating photos monthly, posting sample playlists, and responding to every review within 24 hours. That DJ reported 40+ leads per quarter.

The fix wasn't to cancel Wedding Wire. It was to treat it like a living asset. We created a schedule: new photos every two weeks (from real weddings, not stock), a monthly blog post cross-posted from his site, and review responses within 12 hours. He also used Booksy to let couples book a 15-minute phone call directly from his profile.

Results in 90 days: Inquiry cost dropped to $45. Booking rate from Wedding Wire went from 12% to 38%. He added $8,400 in revenue that quarter from a platform he was about to abandon.

### Mistake #4: Writing Location Pages That Say Nothing

A wedding venue in Denver had five location pages: "Denver Wedding Venue," "Colorado Wedding Venue," "Denver Event Space," "Denver Wedding Reception," "Outdoor Wedding Venue Denver." Every page had the same 200 words with slightly different keyword stuffing.

Google saw them as duplicate content. None ranked.

We collapsed them into one page. Then we built real location content: a page about "Wedding Receptions in LoDo" with specific parking instructions for their venue, a page about "Winter Weddings in Denver" with weather backup plans, and a guide to "Denvers Best Wedding Photographers Near [Venue Name]" that linked out to other local businesses.

Those pages started ranking in two months. The "Winter Weddings" page alone brought in three bookings for January-March — their slowest season. Average booking value: $4,200 per wedding. Total revenue from that page: $12,600.

## How to Get Wedding Reviews That Actually Help You Rank

Not all reviews are equal. A review that says "Great service!" helps your rating. A review that says "Best wedding photographer in Austin — our engagement photos were stunning" helps your ranking.

Google's algorithm extracts keywords from reviews. So if 30 of your 50 reviews mention "wedding photographer" or "engagement photos," Google starts associating those terms with your business. That matters more than your star count past 4.3.

Here's what I tested with a wedding makeup artist in NYC. She had excellent reviews but none mentioned specific services. Her GBP was set to "Makeup Artist." She ranked #6 for "bridal makeup NYC." I had her email her past 48 clients using Mailchimp. The email said: "I'd love a quick review. If you felt comfortable, mentioning 'bridal makeup' or 'wedding look' would really help other brides find me."

The emails went out on a Tuesday morning. 22 clients left reviews within two weeks. The keyword density of her reviews shifted from 8% mentioning wedding terms to 44%. She hit #2 for "bridal makeup NYC" in 19 days.

You can do this without being pushy. Use Square or Booksy to send automated follow-up requests 48 hours after the appointment. Include a link to your GBP. Add a note: "If you mention what service you booked, it helps other couples find exactly what they need."

Do not offer incentives. Google will know and your reviews will disappear. Just ask directly.

A florist in Nashville tried the "10% off your next order" approach. She lost 14 reviews in one purge. Two months of work gone. Direct ask, no reward, works better long-term.

## When You Actually Need Google Ads (And When You Don't)

Every business owner asks me: "Should I run Google Ads?" The answer is usually no — until it's yes.

You should not run Google Ads if:

- Your GBP isn't fully optimized
- You have fewer than 30 reviews
- Your website takes more than 4 seconds to load on mobile
- You haven't set up conversion tracking
- You're averaging fewer than 10 inbound leads per month from any source

If those five things aren't in place, ads will cost you more per lead than they should. I've seen wedding venues burn $5,000 in 30 days with nothing to show for it.

You should run Google Ads if:

- You're turning away business from organic leads (meaning you have more demand than capacity)
- You're launching a new service or seasonal offering (winter weddings, elopements, etc.)
- You have specific inventory or dates you need to fill (venues, mostly)
- You can track exactly which keyword led to a booking

A wedding venue in Portland came to me with a problem: They had 20 open dates in October and November. Normally those months book up by July. It was August. They were looking at $84,000 in potential lost revenue (20 dates at $4,200 average).

We ran a Google Ads campaign targeting "Portland fall wedding venue" and "last minute wedding venue Portland." Budget: $1,500/month. Cost per click: $4.20. Cost per lead: $38. Booking rate: 22%. Total bookings from ads: 12. Revenue: $50,400. Ad spend: $4,500 over three months.

That's a 11x return. But notice: They didn't run ads year-round. They ran them to solve a specific problem. That's the difference between small business owners who understand ads and agencies who want your retainer.

The tools matter too. Use the Google Keyword Planner to find terms with "high purchase intent" — "book wedding venue" converts better than "wedding venue ideas." Use Yelp Ads only if you're getting consistent organic Yelp traffic first. Yelp's click costs are higher than Google's and their audience is browsing, not booking.

Set a maximum cost per lead in Google Ads. If you're paying more than $50 per lead for a wedding service, your targeting is wrong or your landing page is broken. Fix those before increasing the budget.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it take to rank for local wedding searches?**

Depends on your starting point. If your GBP is unclaimed and you have zero reviews, expect 6-12 weeks to see meaningful movement. If you already have a claimed profile with 20+ reviews, you might see changes in 2-4 weeks after optimization. Google wants to see activity and consistency. The fastest results I've seen were at 11 days — a florist in Chicago who updated her photos, added a service area, and got 8 new reviews in one week. Slowest was 5 months — a photographer who kept changing categories every two weeks.

**Q: Do I need a website, or can I just use my Google Business Profile?**

You need a website. Here's why: Google uses your website to verify your GBP. If your website doesn't match your business name, address, and phone number exactly, you'll struggle to rank. Also, couples click through to websites before booking. I've seen GBP listings with 5-star ratings lose leads because the website looked like it was built in 2007. Use Squarespace or Wix if you don't have one. Just make sure your contact info is identical across your site and your GBP.

**Q: Is Wedding Wire worth the money?**

Depends on your category. For photographers and venues, sometimes yes — the platform drives direct bookings. For caterers, bakers, and hair artists, the ROI is usually worse. The real question: Can you track which leads came from Wedding Wire versus Google? Set up unique phone numbers or landing pages. If your cost per booking exceeds $200, cancel it and put that money into Google Ads instead.

**Q: How many reviews do I need to show up in the local map pack?**

The floor is about 25. That's when Google starts treating you as an established business. But the map pack is a popularity contest combined with relevance. A florist with 30 reviews mentioning "wedding flowers" will outrank a florist with 60 reviews saying "great service." Focus on review quality and keyword inclusion, not just the count.

**Q: Should I optimize for "wedding vendor" or specific services like "wedding photography"?**

Specific services every time. "Wedding photography" will bring you couples ready to book. "Wedding vendor" brings you other vendors and tire-kickers. I had a DJ in Denver optimize for "Denver DJ" and "wedding DJ Denver" separately. The specific term brought 3x more qualified leads. The broad term brought people asking if he did bar mitzvahs.

**Q: Can I rank without paying for Google Ads?**

Yes. Most of my clients rank without paid ads. But you need to outwork the businesses that do pay. That means updating your GBP weekly, asking for reviews consistently, and building real content. Google's local algorithm rewards effort. The businesses that rank without ads are the ones treating SEO like a daily habit, not a one-time project.

**Q: My business is mobile-only (I go to clients). Do I need a physical address?**

No. Use the "Service Area Business" option in GBP. Hide your address. Set a reasonable service radius — 15-25 miles is typical for most wedding vendors. You'll still appear in local searches. Just make sure your website and listings use the same city names. A makeup artist in Phoenix serving Scottsdale should mention both cities everywhere.
