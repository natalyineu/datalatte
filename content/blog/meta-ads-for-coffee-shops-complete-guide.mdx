## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Ads For "Everyone Within 5 Miles"

**The story:** A coffee shop in Austin called *Brew & Bloom* (not their real name, but the numbers are real) came to me after spending $1,800 on Meta ads over three months. They'd set their radius to 5 miles, targeted "coffee lovers" and "breakfast," and waited for the magic to happen. Their CPA was $4.17 per click. Their in-store redemption rate was zero — not one person mentioned the ad.

**What went wrong:** "Everyone within 5 miles" includes the residential neighborhood behind the shop, the highway commuters who aren't stopping, and three other coffee shops' regulars. It also includes people who already know about the shop and pass it daily without ever walking in. The targeting was lazy. Worse, they had no way to track if anyone actually came in from the ad — no QR code, no offer code, no POS integration. They were spending money blind.

**The fix:** I had them shrink the radius to 1.5 miles and exclude their own address plus the addresses of known competitors. Then we set up a Square promotion code — "WELCOME10" for 10% off a first visit — and ran the ad with that code prominently displayed. We also uploaded their email list of existing customers into a custom audience and excluded them. The ads now only reached people who had *not* visited before.

**The outcome:** In week one, 47 people used the code. Week two, 83. Their cost per new customer dropped to $1.12. Over the next 60 days, the shop saw $3,800 in revenue directly tied to those codes. The owner told me, "I thought Facebook was a scam. Turns out I just didn't know what I was doing." She wasn't wrong.

### Mistake #2: Using Stock Photos of Coffee You Don't Serve

**The story:** A coffee roastery in Denver spent $2,400 on Meta ads over a holiday season. The photos were beautiful — latte art, steaming mugs, a perfect croissant. Problem was, the coffee in the photos wasn't theirs. The shop had a very specific industrial vibe — exposed brick, stainless steel, no frills. The stock photos showed a cozy farmhouse aesthetic with a copper mug. Every single customer who walked in from the ad said, "I thought it would be different in here."

**What went wrong:** The disconnect between the ad creative and the actual experience created distrust. People felt misled. The shop's return rate among ad-driven customers was under 15% — meaning 85% of those new customers never came back. The owner admitted she didn't have good photos of her own shop and defaulted to stock images because "they look professional."

**The fix:** I told her to spend $200 on a local photographer (she found one on Thumbtack for $150) and shoot real content: the barista pulling a shot, the actual pastry case, the owner wiping down the counter with a deadpan expression. We used those photos in a new campaign that leaned into the shop's actual personality: "Yes, we're ugly-pretty. Our espresso is not."

**The outcome:** The new ads cost about the same per click ($2.10), but the conversion rate tripled. More importantly, repeat visit rate from ad-driven customers jumped to 42%. The shop's revenue from Meta ads went from $1,200 in losses to $4,600 in net profit over the next quarter. Stock photos cost them $2,400. Real photos made them $4,600. Do the math.

### Mistake #3: Running One Ad for Your Entire Menu

**The story:** A bakery in Nashville — *Sweet Stack* — ran a single ad promoting "fresh pastries and coffee." Their menu had 40+ items. The ad was generic, the targeting was generic, and the results were generic: 0.3% CTR, $12 cost per conversion, zero measurable in-store pickup. After three months of this, the owner told me, "I think Meta just doesn't work for food businesses."

**What went wrong:** She was running one ad for every possible customer. A morning commuter wants a fast espresso and a scone. A remote worker wants a pour-over and a seat with an outlet. A weekend family wants a giant cinnamon roll and hot chocolate for the kids. These are three different people with three different needs. One ad cannot speak to all of them.

**The fix:** We set up three ad sets. Ad set 1 (morning commuters): ran 6–9 AM, showed a photo of a to-go cup and a scone, copy focused on speed ("In and out in under 90 seconds"). Ad set 2 (remote workers): ran 9 AM–2 PM, showed the seating area and an outlet sign, copy talked about WiFi speed and refill policy. Ad set 3 (weekend families): ran Friday–Sunday, showed the cinnamon roll, copy mentioned kids' hot chocolate and free coloring sheets. Budget: $500 total per month, split by time of day.

**The outcome:** Morning commuter ads: $0.80 per click, 12% in-store pickup rate. Remote worker ads: $1.10 per click, 19% pickup rate (these people tend to stay and buy more). Weekend family ads: $0.60 per click, 31% pickup rate. Total monthly revenue from Meta ads: $5,200, up from $0. Monthly spend: $500. That's a 10x return. She now runs separate ads for her seasonal specials and sends me a note each time she beats her own record.

## How to Track Meta Ads ROI Without a Massive Tech Stack

Most small business owners I meet think tracking ads requires some elaborate setup with Google Tag Manager, UTM parameters, and a data analyst who charges $150 an hour. For a coffee shop, that's overkill. Here's what actually works.

**Use your POS as the tracking engine.** If you're on Square, Toast, or Clover, you can create offer codes tied to specific campaigns. Square's dashboard lets you see exactly how many times a code was used, the total revenue generated by those transactions, and the average ticket size. I had a shop in Chicago use this method to track a $300/month ad spend and found that each code user spent an average of $9.40 per visit — not just the $4 coffee they came in for. That's the kind of data that tells you whether your ads are actually profitable.

**Set up a simple Google Sheet.** This sounds low-tech because it is. Low-tech works. Create four columns: Date, Ad Spend, Number of Code Redemptions, and Total Revenue from Code Users. Update it every Monday. After 90 days, you'll have enough data to make decisions. Most shop owners never do this, which is why they think ads don't work. They don't track what actually happened.

**Use Square's marketing integrations.** Square offers a native integration with Meta that lets you sync customer data and measure offline conversions. It's free. It takes 15 minutes to set up. If you're not using it, you're flying blind. I can't tell you how many times I've seen a coffee shop run $2,000 in ads and then guess whether it worked. Don't guess. Use the tool.

**If you're not on Square:** Toast has a similar integration. Clover has a workaround using QR codes in the ad that lead to a specific landing page (I use Linktree for this, $5/month). Even a handwritten code scribbled on a receipt counts, as long as you track it. The system doesn't have to be fancy. It just has to exist.

## How to Use Google Ads and Yelp Together With Meta Ads

Meta ads alone can work, but the smartest coffee shops I've worked with layer in two other channels: Google Ads and Yelp. Here's how they fit together.

**Google Ads captures intent.** Someone in Chicago searches "coffee shop near me" or "best latte Lincoln Park." That person has a problem (I need coffee) and is actively seeking a solution (a nearby shop). Google Ads lets you show up at that exact moment. The cost per click for "coffee shop near me" in midsize US cities runs $1.50 to $3.00 depending on competition. That's not cheap, but the conversion rate is high — people searching for coffee are ready to buy coffee.

I had a shop in Portland run Google Ads with a $400 monthly budget alongside their $600 Meta budget. The Google Ads brought in people actively looking for coffee. The Meta ads brought in people who didn't know they wanted a new coffee shop until they saw the ad. Together, they covered two different stages of the customer journey. The shop's total ad spend was $1,000/month. They saw $8,200 in incremental revenue over the first 90 days. The owner told me, "I thought I had to pick one platform. Turns out they don't fight each other — they complement each other."

**Yelp works for discovery, but you have to manage it.** Yelp's ad products are less sophisticated than Meta's, but Yelp has one advantage: people on Yelp are actively looking for places to spend money. A coffee shop in Denver added a Yelp ad for $200/month (you bid on keywords in your category and location). They saw an average of 15 new check-ins per week attributed to the ad. The CPL was about $3.30 per new visitor — not great, not terrible. But when combined with their Meta retargeting campaign (people who visited their Yelp page but didn't check in), the overall customer acquisition cost dropped to $1.80.

**The tool stack that works:** Meta ads for awareness and retargeting, Google Ads for intent-based search, Yelp for discovery — all tied together with a POS system that tracks real transactions. No fancy CRMs. No expensive attribution software. Just three ad channels and one point-of-sale. That's it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why would I run ads when I can just post on Instagram for free?**

Free posting works if you already have 10,000 followers and consistent engagement. Most coffee shops don't. I've worked with shops that posted daily for six months and gained 47 followers. Organic reach on Instagram is now below 10% for most business accounts. Ads let you reach people who don't follow you — which is, presumably, your entire target market of new customers. Post for free to keep your existing customers warm. Run ads to find new ones.

**Q: I tried Facebook ads once and got zero results. Why would this time be different?**

I hear this a lot. Usually the problem was one of three things: wrong targeting (you were showing ads to 50,000 people in your city instead of 5,000 nearby), wrong creative (stock photos, generic copy), or no tracking (you had no way to know if anyone came in). If you fix those three things, the ads usually work. I've never seen a coffee shop fail with Meta ads when the targeting was tight, the creative was real, and the tracking was in place.

**Q: What's the minimum budget that actually makes sense for a coffee shop?**

$500 per month is the floor. Below that, you don't have enough data to optimize. With $500/month, you can run two ad sets — one for morning commuters, one for afternoon remote workers — and get enough clicks in 30 days to know what's working. $300/month works if your margins are high and your location is dense with foot traffic. Anything under $300 and you're better off printing flyers and handing them out on the sidewalk.

**Q: How do I know the ads are bringing in new customers and not just my regulars?**

Exclude your existing customers. Upload your email list or phone number list (if you collect them at the counter) as a custom audience in Meta Ads Manager. Then exclude that audience from your ad sets. This guarantees — as much as anything can — that your ads only reach people who haven't visited before. Then use a unique offer code in the ad so you can track who comes in through the ad. If someone uses the code, they're new. If they don't use the code, they either didn't see the ad or they're a regular who found you anyway.

**Q: Should I run ads on both Instagram and Facebook, or just one?**

Both. Instagram and Facebook share the same ad platform, so there's no extra setup cost. The audiences overlap, but they're not identical. Instagram skews younger (18–34), more visual, more lifestyle-oriented. Facebook skews older (25–55), more community-oriented, more likely to share with friends. A coffee shop in Brooklyn ran the same ad on both platforms and found that Instagram drove more first-time visits, while Facebook drove more repeat visits from people who'd seen a friend's post. Both were profitable.

**Q: I only accept cash. Can I still run Meta ads?**

Yes, but tracking gets harder. You'll need a manual system — a QR code on the receipt that takes people to a feedback form, or a loyalty card with a unique stamp for "ad customers." I worked with a cash-only bakery in Philadelphia. We printed a small sign at the register: "Mention the Instagram ad for a free cookie on your next visit." We tracked redemptions in a notebook. It was low-tech, but it worked. They spent $300/month on ads and saw $1,800 in incremental revenue over 60 days. Cash-only doesn't mean ad-proof. It just means you need to be creative.
