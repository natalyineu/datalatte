## What Is Retargeting and Why Should You Care?

Retargeting (also known as remarketing) is the process of showing ads to people who have already interacted with your website or app. The goal? To bring them back into your sales funnel and encourage them to take action  -  like making a purchase, booking a service, or signing up for your newsletter.

The beauty of retargeting is that it works with *people who are already familiar* with your brand. They've already shown some level of interest  -  your job is to remind them why they should choose you.

Here are the main platforms you can use retargeting on:

- **Google Ads (Google Tag Manager + Google Ads Remarketing)**
- **Meta Ads (Facebook and Instagram Pixel)**
- **LinkedIn Ads (Business Match)**
- **Pinterest Ads (Tag Retargeting)**

Let's break down how to set it up on the most popular ones.

## How to Set Up Retargeting on Google Ads

Google Ads is one of the most effective places to retarget people who have visited your site but didn't convert.

### Step 1: Install Google Tag Manager

If you haven't already, you need to install Google Tag Manager (GTM) on your website. GTM is where you'll manage all of your retargeting tags.

1. Create a Google Tag Manager account.
2. Add the GTM container code to your website's `&lt;head&gt;` section.
3. Verify that GTM is working by using the GTM preview mode.

### Step 2: Install the Google Ads Remarketing Tag

Once GTM is installed, you can set up the Google Ads remarketing tag.

1. Navigate to **Google Ads → Tools → Audience Manager → Remarketing**.
2. Click **Create remarketing tag**.
3. Follow the prompts to generate the tag.
4. Add the tag to GTM and deploy it on your site.

> Pro tip: Use the "All Pages" tag for general retargeting and "Custom Pages" for retargeting specific audiences  -  like people who viewed a pricing page but didn't book.

### Step 3: Create and Launch Remarketing Campaigns

Now that the tag is set up, you can start creating campaigns.

1. In Google Ads, go to **New Campaign** and choose **Display Network** or **Search Network**.
2. Under **Audience**, select **Remarketing audiences**.
3. Choose the audience you want to target (e.g., "All visitors in the last 30 days").
4. Set your bid strategy (e.g., Maximize Conversions).
5. Launch the campaign and monitor performance using conversion tracking.

## How to Set Up Retargeting on Meta (Facebook & Instagram)

Facebook and Instagram (under Meta) are goldmines for retargeting because of their detailed audience segmentation and visual ad formats.

### Step 1: Install the Meta Pixel

The Meta Pixel is the tool that tracks user behavior on your site.

1. Go to the [Meta Business Suite](https://business.facebook.com/) and select your business manager.
2. Go to **Events Manager → Data Sources → Create Data Source → Pixel**.
3. Create a new pixel and copy the base code.
4. Install this base code via GTM or directly on your website.

> If you're using Shopify, Wix, or WordPress, you can install the Pixel through built-in apps or plugins.

### Step 2: Set Up Custom Audiences

Meta allows you to create audiences based on specific actions:

- **All website visitors**
- **People who added to cart**
- **People who viewed a specific page**
- **People who completed a form**

1. Go to **Ads Manager → Audiences → Custom Audiences → Website**.
2. Choose a data source (your Meta Pixel).
3. Set your time frame and event (e.g., "Page View" in the last 7 days).
4. Create your audience.

### Step 3: Launch Retargeting Campaigns

1. Go to **Campaigns → Create**.
2. Choose **Awareness**, **Consideration**, or **Conversion** as your campaign objective.
3. Select your custom audience.
4. Set your budget and target location.
5. Design your ad with a clear call-to-action.

> Pro tip: Use the **Dynamic Ads** feature to automatically show products or services a user viewed  -  perfect for local businesses with online booking or e-commerce.

## How to Set Up Retargeting on LinkedIn

LinkedIn is ideal for B2B services and professional audiences, but it can also work for local businesses offering high-ticket services (e.g., interior design, personal training, legal services).

### Step 1: Install the LinkedIn Insight Tag

1. Go to [LinkedIn Campaign Manager](https://www.linkedin.com/campaignmanager/).
2. Create a new tag under **Tags & Conversions**.
3. Install the tag on your website via GTM or directly.

### Step 2: Set Up a Business Match Audience

LinkedIn's Business Match allows you to retarget people who visited your website and are on LinkedIn.

1. Go to **Audiences → Custom Audiences → Business Match**.
2. Upload your LinkedIn email list or use the website tag to build an audience.
3. Wait 7-14 days for the audience to populate.

### Step 3: Run Retargeting Campaigns

1. Launch a new campaign in Campaign Manager.
2. Choose your Business Match audience.
3. Set your objective (e.g., Website Conversion, Lead Gen).
4. Craft a professional-looking ad with a clear value proposition.

## How to Set Up Retargeting on Pinterest

Pinterest is all about inspiration  -  and it's a great place to retarget users who are in the early stages of the buyer journey.

### Step 1: Install the Pinterest Tag

1. Go to [Pinterest Ads Manager](https://ads.pinterest.com/).
2. Create a new tag under **Tools → Tags → Create Tag**.
3. Add the tag to your website via GTM.

### Step 2: Set Up Retargeting Audiences

1. Go to **Audiences → Create Audience**.
2. Choose "Website" as the source.
3. Define your audience based on specific page views or actions.

> For example, if you're a pet groomer, you could retarget users who viewed your "Grooming Packages" page.

### Step 3: Create Retargeting Campaigns

1. Launch a new campaign in Pinterest Ads.
2. Select your custom audience.
3. Choose a visual ad format (e.g., Pin or Carousel).
4. Promote your services with eye-catching imagery and clear CTA.

## Retargeting Best Practices to Save Time and Money

Retargeting is powerful  -  but only if you do it right. Here are the top tips I've learned while helping over 100 local businesses:

1. **Use multiple platforms**  -  don't rely on one. People are everywhere online, and retargeting across Google, Meta, and LinkedIn increases your chances of re-engagement.
2. **Segment your audiences**  -  retargeting "all visitors" is too broad. Drill down to specific actions like "added to cart" or "viewed pricing".
3. **Set frequency caps**  -  show retargeting ads too many times, and users get annoyed. Most platforms allow you to cap how many times someone sees your ad.
4. **Test different ad creatives**  -  use A/B testing to see what visuals, copy, and offers work best.
5. **Don't forget email**  -  combine retargeting with email marketing for a 1-2 punch. We often see a 25% open rate boost in emails sent to retargeted audiences.

## Frequently Asked Questions

## Is $10 a day enough for Google Ads?

Absolutely  -  and more importantly, **it can be effective** if you're targeting the right audience. The key is to focus on relevance and quality of traffic, not just budget size. For example, a local hair salon we worked with used $10/day for retargeting ads and saw a 42% increase in walk-ins within a month. It's not about how much you spend  -  it's about how smartly you spend it.

## How do I set up Google Ads for my small business?

Start with these steps:
1. Create a Google Ads account.
2. Set up Google Tag Manager and install the remarketing tag.
3. Launch a small "test" campaign aimed at retargeting recent website visitors.
4. Use the "Maximize Conversions" bid strategy to let Google optimize your spend.

## Is $100 enough for Google Ads?

It depends on your industry and location. $100/month is a good starting point for retargeting, especially if you have a high-intent audience. If you're in a competitive area (like New York or San Francisco), you may need more. But for most local businesses, $100 is enough to test and refine your strategy.

## Why did Google Ads charge me $500?

There are a few reasons this could happen:
- You didn't set a daily or monthly budget cap.
- Your ads were running on the **Google Display Network** (which has higher CPMs).
- You had a **conversion goal** that triggered higher bids automatically.
Always set a **daily budget cap** and monitor your campaign performance closely.

## Can retargeting work if I don't have a website?

No  -  retargeting requires a website to track user behavior. If you don't have one, consider starting with **SMS marketing** or **local directory listings** (like Google Business Profile) to build engagement. If you want help setting up a website, [we can help](/contact).

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Retargeting Everyone the Same Way

**The story:** A hair salon owner in Austin, Texas came to me frustrated. She’d spent $1,200 on Google retargeting over two months. She was showing the same generic “Book Now” ad to every single person who visited her site — whether they landed on her pricing page, her blog about summer hairstyles, or her “Meet the Stylists” page. The result? A 0.3% click-through rate and exactly three bookings. That’s $400 per booking.

**What went wrong:** She treated all visitors as identical. Someone reading a blog might need nurturing. Someone on the pricing page is ready to book but got distracted. Someone on the “Meet the Stylists” page is in the consideration phase. Blasting the same message to all three ignores where they are in the decision process.

**The fix:** I helped her set up audience segments based on the page URL. Three lists:
- Blog visitors → retarget with a “New Client Special” ad (educational + incentive)
- Pricing page visitors → retarget with a limited-time 15% off for first appointments
- Booking page visitors who didn’t complete → retarget with a reminder and a one-click link to the exact time slot they viewed

**The outcome:** In the next month, her retargeting spend dropped to $450 (she cut budget on the low-intent blog segment). She got 14 bookings. Cost per booking: $32. That’s a 92% reduction in cost. Revenue from those bookings: $2,100. She stopped complaining about retargeting being a waste.

### Mistake 2: No Frequency Caps — You’re Just Annoying People

**The story:** A pet groomer in Nashville had a simple Facebook retargeting campaign. She set a $500 monthly budget and let it run. No frequency cap. She didn’t know that one of her regular customers saw her ad 22 times in a week. The customer called to say “I already book with you. Stop stalking me.” Awkward.

**What went wrong:** Without a frequency cap, Facebook will show your ad to the same person over and over because it’s cheap and gets you more impressions. But every additional impression after the first few has negative value. You’re paying for annoyance.

**The fix:** In Facebook Ads Manager, set a frequency cap of 3 impressions per 7 days per person. In Google Ads, set a frequency cap of 2–3 per day per cookie. Also exclude converted customers (people who completed a booking form or called from an ad) from the retargeting audience for at least 30 days.

**The outcome:** The pet groomer’s click-through rate went from 0.2% to 1.1% within two weeks. Cost per click dropped by 40%. She started getting compliments from customers saying “I saw your ad and finally remembered to book.” No more complaints. She saved about $200 a month in wasted impressions.

### Mistake 3: Mobile vs. Desktop Blindness

**The story:** A coffee shop in Portland, Oregon ran a retargeting campaign on Google Display. They used a beautiful square image with text about their new cold brew. Problem: 70% of their website traffic was mobile, and the image was sized for desktop. On a phone, the text was unreadable. The click-through rate was 0.08%.

**What went wrong:** They assumed one creative would work everywhere. They also didn’t check device breakdown in Google Ads. The shop’s website was mobile-friendly (people searching for coffee nearby on their phones), but the ads weren’t.

**The fix:** I had them create separate mobile-optimized ad variations: vertical images (1080x1920), shorter headlines, and a click-to-call button (for orders) in addition to the website link. Also increased bid adjustment for mobile by 30%.

**The outcome:** Their mobile retargeting click-through rate jumped to 1.4%. In the first month, they got 38 mobile ad clicks, of which 12 converted to orders via call or website. Total revenue from mobile retargeting: $3,800. That was three times their monthly ad spend on that campaign.

### Mistake 4: Letting Ad Fatigue Kill Your Campaign

**The story:** A fitness studio in Denver had one retargeting ad that ran for eight straight weeks. Same photo of their lobby. Same “Try a free class” headline. Week one: great. Week four: mediocre. Week eight: they were showing the same person the same ad for two months. The audience had seen it before — they ignored it. Cost per lead went from $5 to $22.

**What went wrong:** Ad fatigue is real. People don’t notice the same ad after 3–4 impressions. The studio didn’t rotate creatives or test new angles.

**The fix:** I told them to create three ad sets within the same campaign, each with a different creative:
- One with a photo of a class in action
- One with a testimonial quote from a member
- One with a limited-time offer (free week with purchase of a month)
Set each to rotate weekly using a simple schedule in Facebook Ads Manager. Also refresh creatives every two weeks.

**The outcome:** Within a month, their conversion rate recovered and actually improved 22% above the original peak. Cost per lead dropped back to $4.30. They added 20 new trial members from retargeting alone that month, worth about $3,000 in future membership revenue.

## What to Retarget: Beyond the Homepage

A common misunderstanding I see is business owners thinking retargeting means “show an ad to anyone who visited my site.” That’s like inviting everyone who walks past your store to come in — whether they were looking for a bathroom, a phone charger, or your actual service. You waste money and miss opportunities.

### Segment by Intent Level

Your website has pages that signal different intent. Here’s how I categorize them for retargeting:

- **High intent:** Pricing page, booking/checkout page, “Contact Us” page. These people were about to buy. Retarget them quickly (within 24 hours) with an incentive or a reminder. Example: A yoga studio in Chicago saw that people who visited their pricing page but didn’t book were 4x more likely to convert if shown a “First class free” ad within 2 hours. They set up a custom audience in Google Ads for visitors of /pricing with a 1-day membership duration. Cost: $180 in ad spend over a month. Result: 60 new sign-ups worth $900 in trial memberships and $4,500 in subsequent full-price memberships.

- **Medium intent:** Blog posts (e.g., “Benefits of Hot Yoga”), “About Us” page, service descriptions. These people are researching. Don’t push a hard sell. Retarget with educational content, testimonials, or a low-commitment offer (free guide, webinar). Example: A barbershop in Phoenix used a custom audience for blog readers and retargeted them on Facebook with a “Meet our barbers” video. The video cost $300 to produce. It generated 22 appointment bookings over two months, each averaging $45.

- **Low intent:** People who bounced in under 10 seconds or visited only the homepage. They might have landed accidentally or aren’t interested yet. Consider excluding them or using a very soft retargeting ad with brand awareness messaging and a long window (90 days). Don’t spend heavily here.

### Abandoned Cart / Booking Retargeting

For businesses using tools like Square, Booksy, or Shopify, you can create retargeting audiences based on cart or booking abandonment. Example: A nail salon in Portland used Square’s integration with Facebook to create a custom audience of people who added a service to cart but didn’t check out. They sent a Facebook retargeting ad with a “10% off your next visit” code. The ad cost $120 for two weeks. It recovered 14 abandoned carts, generating $980 in revenue. That’s a 700% return on ad spend.

### Exclude Recent Customers

One of the most important but ignored steps: exclude people who already converted. If someone booked an appointment last week, don’t show them a retargeting ad for a new customer discount. Save that budget for people who haven’t yet taken action. Set up a custom audience of converters (people who triggered your pixel’s “Purchase” or “Lead” event) with a 30-day exclusion window.

## How to Measure Retargeting ROI (Without Getting Lost in Vanity Metrics)

I get asked: “How do I know if retargeting actually works?” The answer is not “how many clicks you got.” Clicks are vanity metrics if they don’t convert into bookings or sales.

### The View-Through Conversion Problem

Retargeting works differently than search ads. Someone might see your ad, not click, but remember your name and type it into Google directly a week later. Standard Google Ads attribution (last click) would credit the Google search, not your retargeting. That’s why many small business owners think retargeting doesn’t work — they look at direct clicks only and ignore assisted conversions.

**Real example:** A barbershop in New York City spent $500 on Google display retargeting. Their direct click-to-conversion rate was low — only 4 bookings attributed via last click. That’s $125 per booking. They were about to kill the campaign. I showed them the “View-Through Conversions” column in Google Ads, which counts conversions for people who saw the ad (even if they didn’t click) and converted within 30 days. That number? 12 additional bookings. Combined, they got 16 bookings from $500 — $31 per booking. Average ticket: $80. Total revenue: $1,280. That’s a 256% ROAS.

**What to check:**
- In Google Ads: go to Campaigns → Columns → Modify columns → check “View-through conversions” and “Click-assisted conversions”
- In Facebook Ads: go to Ads Manager → Columns → Customize → add “View content + Add to cart + Purchase” and look at the “Attribution window” (Facebook defaults to 7-day click and 1-day view)
- Set your attribution window to at least 30 days for retargeting campaigns (Google allows up to 90)

### The Right KPIs for Retargeting

Ignore cost per impression (CPM) unless you’re doing pure awareness. Focus on:
- **Cost per incremental conversion** (how much you spend to get one booking from someone who didn’t convert on their first visit)
- **Return on ad spend** (revenue from retargeting / spend)
- **Assisted conversion value** (how often retargeting was part of the customer journey)

### Tools to Track Properly

- **Google Ads:** Set up conversion tracking with Google Tag Manager. Use different conversion actions for retargeting vs. search.
- **Facebook Pixel:** Use standard events (“ViewContent”, “AddToCart”, “Purchase”) and create custom conversions for form submissions or calls.
- **Square / Booksy / Mailchimp:** Many booking and POS systems have built-in integration with Facebook and Google. If you send a booking confirmation email, you can use Mailchimp to sync with Facebook Custom Audiences for exclusion (don’t retarget someone who booked) and for lookalike audiences.

**Example:** A hair salon in Boston used Google Ads conversion tracking linked to their Booksy account. They tracked each retargeting ad click that led to a completed booking. Their average retargeting cost per booking: $14. Average service value: $60. They scaled the campaign from $200 to $800 monthly, maintaining a 4x ROAS.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Won't retargeting creep people out?**
Yes — if you do it badly. If someone sees your ad 15 times in a day, they’ll feel tracked. Set a frequency cap (3 per 7 days is safe). Show relevant ads based on what they looked at. And most important: exclude people who already converted. A dentist in Philadelphia got complaints from two patients who saw his ad for “new patient special” even though they’d been coming for years. He fixed it by uploading his email list as an exclusion audience. Complaints stopped, and his ad spend dropped 20% because he wasn’t wasting money on existing patients.

**Q: How much should I spend on retargeting vs. new customer ads?**
A good starting rule: 20–30% of your total ad budget goes to retargeting, the rest to prospecting. A bakery in Austin spent $200 on retargeting and $800 on Facebook prospecting. The retargeting produced a 3x ROAS ($600 in sales from $200 spend). Prospecting gave 1.2x ROAS. So retargeting was more efficient, but prospecting brought in new names. Adjust based on your goals. If you have a small list (under 1,000 visitors per month), retargeting will run out of audience quickly. Keep it modest.

**Q: Do I need a separate landing page for retargeting ads?**
Not always, but it helps. If you’re offering a specific discount or promotion exclusively for retargeted visitors, use a unique URL. That way you can track exactly how many conversions came from retargeting. A nail salon in Portland used a landing page “nail-salon.com/first-visit-offer” for their retargeting ads. They could see in Google Analytics that 80% of that page’s traffic came from retargeting. It converted at 12%. Their home page converted at 3%. The dedicated page paid for itself in one week.

**Q: How long should I retarget someone?**
30 days is standard for low-ticket services ($20–$100). For high-ticket services (HVAC replacement, dental implants, legal services), extend to 90 days. An HVAC company in Chicago used a 90-day retargeting window for emergency repair services. They found that 40% of their retargeting conversions happened between 30 and 60 days after the first visit. If they had cut at 30 days, they would have missed those leads. Test your own window — start with 30, then extend if you see conversions happening late.

**Q: Can I retarget on platforms like Yelp or Nextdoor?**
Yelp allows audience targeting but it’s limited (you can target by category, location, and demographics, but not by your own website visitors unless you use Yelp’s pixel). Nextdoor offers a “Retargeting” option for local businesses. A pizza place in Austin used Nextdoor retargeting for delivery orders: they set up the Nextdoor pixel on their online ordering page and retargeted people who started an order but didn’t finish. They spent $150 over a month and recovered 8 abandoned orders worth $280. It’s not as robust as Google or Facebook, but worth adding if your audience skews older or hyper-local.

**Q: What if I have a low-traffic website? Can I still retarget?**
Yes, but you need to lower your expectations or use a longer window. If you only get 200 visitors per month, your retargeting audience will be tiny. Options: (1) Use a 90-day window instead of 30 to build a larger list. (2) Retarget across platforms (same pixel feeds both Google and Facebook). (3) Use email list retargeting — upload your existing customer or subscriber emails to Facebook Custom Audiences or Google Customer Match. A boutique in Nashville had only 300 website visitors a month but an email list of 2,000. They uploaded the emails to Facebook, created a retargeting audience, and ran ads to those people. The campaign brought in $2,500 in sales on a $400 ad spend.
