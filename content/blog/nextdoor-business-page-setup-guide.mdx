## What is a Nextdoor Business Page?
A Nextdoor business page is a free listing that allows you to showcase your business to local customers on the Nextdoor platform. It's similar to a Google My Business listing, but specifically designed for neighborhood-focused marketing.

## Claiming Your Nextdoor Business Page
Claiming your Nextdoor business page is a straightforward process. Here's how to do it:
* Go to Nextdoor.com and search for your business
* If your business is already listed, click on the "Claim" button
* If your business isn't listed, create a new listing by clicking on "Add a business"
* Verify your business ownership through a verification process (usually via phone or email)

<Callout type="tip">Make sure to use a clear and consistent business name, address, and phone number across all online platforms, including your Nextdoor business page.</Callout>

## Optimizing Your Nextdoor Business Page
Once you've claimed your Nextdoor business page, it's time to optimize it. Here are some key elements to focus on:
* **Business Description**: Write a clear and concise description of your business, including your products or services.
* **Business Hours**: List your business hours, including any special hours or holidays.
* **Categories**: Choose relevant categories for your business to help customers find you.
* **Photos**: Add high-quality photos of your business, products, or services.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched a lot of small business owners treat Nextdoor like a set-it-and-forget-it directory listing. That's not how it works. Here are the four mistakes I see most often — and what actually happened when my clients fixed them.

**Mistake #1: Claiming the page and then ignoring it**

A dog groomer in Austin, Texas, claimed her Nextdoor page in February 2023 and didn't touch it again for nine months. She assumed the platform would bring in leads automatically. It brought in exactly zero calls.

Here's the problem: Nextdoor shows businesses with recent activity higher in search results. A page with no updates, no new photos, and no responses to comments gets buried behind pages that look alive.

The fix took her two hours spread across one week. She uploaded six new photos of recent grooming work. She responded to every comment and question (there were only three). She posted one offer: "10% off nail trims for new clients, mention Nextdoor when you book."

Outcome: In the following 60 days, she booked 14 new clients through Nextdoor. Average spend per visit: $65. That's $910 in additional revenue from a single offer post. She now spends 15 minutes per week on Nextdoor and pulls in roughly $350–$450 per month in attributed bookings.

**Mistake #2: Using generic photos from your website**

A pizza place in Portland, Oregon, uploaded their logo, a photo of their storefront, and a stock photo of a pizza that wasn't theirs. Their Nextdoor page looked like an abandoned Yelp listing from 2017.

Nextdoor users trust recommendation posts that show real food, real people, and real spaces. When neighbors see a generic pizza photo, they assume you're a chain or a ghost kitchen. Neither drives local loyalty.

Fix: The owner spent a Saturday afternoon photographing actual pizzas coming out of their oven. Close-up shots of a pepperoni pizza with visible grease bubbles. A shot of the owner's son holding a box. A before-and-after of their lunch rush setup.

Nextdoor also supports short video clips. He uploaded a 15-second video of dough being tossed. No editing, no music, just a phone held steady.

Outcome: Photo views on his page went from practically zero to an average of 230 views per week. His offer posts (coupon code NDOOR for $5 off) generated 47 redemptions in the first month. Revenue directly attributed: $940. The video alone was shared by 12 neighbors in local recommendation threads.

**Mistake #3: Replying to reviews defensively**

A hair salon in Denver got a 2-star review from a customer who claimed the stylist "rushed through the cut." The owner replied within an hour: "I'm sorry you feel that way, but I was fully booked and gave you the same time I give everyone."

That reply stayed public for six months. Multiple neighbors commented under it calling the reply "defensive" and "unprofessional." The salon's Nextdoor rating dropped from 4.6 to 3.8 stars.

I understand the impulse to defend your work. I've seen it at agencies with Fortune 500 clients, where we had to physically stop brand managers from replying while angry. It never helps.

Fix: The salon owner deleted the original reply and wrote a new one: "Thanks for the feedback. I'd love the chance to make this right. Please call the salon directly and ask for me — I'll personally redo your cut at no charge." She then commented under her own reply (Nextdoor allows this) offering the same to anyone who had a similar experience.

Outcome: The original reviewer updated her rating to 4 stars. Two other neighbors who had neutral experiences saw the reply and scheduled appointments. The salon reversed the rating trend within 45 days. Their star rating returned to 4.5, and their "recent activity" section showed a positive tone that made neighbors more willing to engage.

**Mistake #4: Not tracking where customers come from**

A yoga studio in Chicago had 87 new client sign-ups over six months. The owner couldn't tell me which ones came from Nextdoor, Google, Instagram, or word of mouth. She just knew they were "getting clients."

When you can't attribute revenue to a channel, you can't make decisions about that channel. You also can't tell whether you're under-investing or wasting time.

Fix: I had her add a simple step to her intake process. When new clients filled out their waiver online, the form asked one question: "How did you hear about us?" with a dropdown including "Nextdoor" as an option. She also set up a unique phone number for her Nextdoor profile (Google Voice costs nothing) and a checkout code (NEXT10 for 10% off first class).

Outcome: After 90 days, she confirmed that 23 of her 44 new sign-ups during that period came from Nextdoor. Average lifetime value of those clients: approximately $380 each. That's $8,740 in attributable revenue from Nextdoor in three months. She stopped posting to Instagram (which had driven only 4 sign-ups) and redirected that time to Nextdoor.

I'm not saying every business will see numbers like that. But you cannot optimize what you don't measure.

## What to Post (And What to Skip) on Your Nextdoor Page

Most guides tell you to "post regularly" without telling you what to actually post. Here's what I've seen work across 30+ local business clients in the US and UK.

**Post types that perform well:**

***Local events.** A bookstore in Nashville posted about their monthly kids' story time every Tuesday morning. The posts included a photo of the featured book and the time (10:30 AM, which mattered because parents plan around nap schedules). Each event post generated 50–80 "interested" clicks and an average of 18 actual attendees.

***Limited-time offers with a neighbor discount.** "Show this post for 15% off your next oil change" works better than "We have great prices." Add a specific number: "First 20 neighbors who mention Nextdoor get a free car wash with purchase." That specificity triggers FOMO. A mechanic in Nashville who tested this saw 32 redemptions in 72 hours.

***Behind-the-scenes content.** A coffee shop in Philadelphia posted a video of their baker piping croissants at 5:45 AM. The post had no call to action. It just showed real work happening. That video was viewed 1,400 times and generated 11 comments from neighbors saying "Can't wait to try one" or "What time do you open?" Those comments equal free engagement that pushes your page higher in local search.

***Questions to the neighborhood.** "We're thinking of adding gluten-free pastry options — would that be useful for anyone?" This is not a poll. It's a conversation starter. Neighbors love being asked for input. A bakery in Chicago got 84 replies on a similar question, which meant 84 people now had a reason to check their page regularly. When they launched the gluten-free menu two weeks later, those 84 people got notified.

**Post types to avoid:**

***Daily menu updates.** Unless your menu changes every day (unlikely for most businesses), this is noise. A deli in NYC posted their specials board every morning for six weeks. Engagement dropped from 40 interactions per post to 7. Neighbors stopped paying attention because nothing felt urgent.

***Generic motivational quotes.** "Success is not final, failure is not fatal" with your logo slapped on it. This works on Instagram. On Nextdoor, neighbors scroll past it because it doesn't tell them anything useful about your business.

***Political content of any kind.** I cannot overstate this. Nextdoor neighborhoods have strong opinions. A single political post — even one you consider neutral — can destroy months of trust. A pet store in Denver posted about a local zoning issue, and within 24 hours, three separate recommendation threads about them were derailed by arguments. They lost an estimated $2,800 in potential business from neighbors who stopped engaging.

**Frequency that works:**

Two to three posts per week is the sweet spot I've seen across most verticals. One offer or event, one behind-the-scenes or business story, and one question or neighborhood engagement post. Spread them across the week — Sunday evening, Wednesday mid-morning, and Friday afternoon tend to get the highest engagement based on my client data.

Less than one post per week and your page looks inactive. More than one per day and neighbors start muting you, which reduces your visibility permanently.

## Connecting Nextdoor to Your Other Marketing (Without Losing Your Mind)

You don't have time to manage six separate platforms. I know this. Most of my clients run their business with two or three part-time employees and handle marketing themselves after closing.

Here's the pragmatic approach: treat Nextdoor as one component of a system, not a standalone project.

**Link your Nextdoor page to your Google Business Profile.**

This is a technical step most owners skip. In your Nextdoor settings, there's an option to connect your Google Business Profile. When you do this, Nextdoor pulls your hours, address, and phone number directly from Google. If you update your holiday hours in Google, Nextdoor updates automatically. One less thing to remember.

A coffee shop in Austin forgot to update their Thanksgiving hours on Nextdoor. Ten people showed up to a locked door. Four of them left 1-star reviews. That fix would have taken 90 seconds.

**Sync offers across platforms using a single tool.**

Use a tool like Mailchimp or Square to create offer codes that work across Nextdoor, email, and in-store. The code is the same. The tracking is the same. You control one offer, it shows up everywhere.

A salon in Nashville ran a "Spring Refresh" special: $20 off color services with code SPRING25. They posted it on Nextdoor, sent it to their email list, and printed a small sign for the front counter. The code was used 62 times. Nextdoor accounted for 23 of those redemptions. Email accounted for 31. In-store signage drove 8. Without a unified code, they'd have no way to compare channel performance.

**Use the same photos across both platforms.**

Take one set of good photos per month and upload them to both Google Business Profile and Nextdoor on the same day. You're already taking the photos for one. Double the use takes five minutes.

A restaurant in Portland started doing monthly photo uploads to both platforms simultaneously. Their Google profile saw a 15% increase in photo views. Their Nextdoor page saw a 22% increase. Same photos, same effort, double the result.

**Bookings: Square vs. Booksy vs. direct links.**

If you use Square Appointments or Booksy, put the booking link directly in your Nextdoor bio. Don't make neighbors message you to check availability. A yoga studio in Chicago switched from "DM to book" to a direct link and saw their Nextdoor booking conversion rate jump from 12% to 38% in two weeks. People want the path of least resistance. Give it to them.

If you use a direct booking widget on your own site (I use Tally Forms for clients who want simplicity), paste that link into Nextdoor. Test it yourself on a phone before publishing.

**One thing you should NOT do:**

Do not run Nextdoor ads without first optimizing your organic page. I've audited five small businesses that spent $300–$800 per month on Nextdoor Neighbor Ads and saw no return. Every single one of them had an incomplete profile, no recent posts, and zero reviews.

You are paying to send people to a bad first experience. Fix the page first. Then consider ads.

## Using Nextdoor Insights to Make Better Decisions

Nextdoor provides free analytics for business pages. Most owners never open them. That's a mistake, because the data tells you exactly what your neighbors actually want.

**What to look at first:**

***Page views over time.** If your views spike on a Thursday afternoon and you post on Monday mornings, you're missing your audience. A florist in Nashville adjusted her post schedule from 9 AM Mondays to 4 PM Thursdays based on her insights data. Her offer redemption rate increased from 8% to 17% in one month.

***Which posts get the most shares.** Shares on Nextdoor are more valuable than likes. A share puts your post in front of that neighbor's followers. If your most-shared post is a "Lost Dog" post you made voluntarily, that's a signal: neighbors pay attention to things that feel urgent and community-focused. A coffee shop in Portland started running "Buy a Coffee for a Neighbor" days (buy one, and they'd donate the second to a local teacher or nurse). Those posts got shared 4x more than any offer post.

***Which offers get the most redemptions.** If you run three offers and one gets 80% of the redemptions, kill the other two and run that offer more often. A salon in Chicago realized their "20% off any service" offer got 12 redemptions, while their "Free consultation with any color service" got 3. They stopped running the consultation offer and put all their effort into percentage discounts. Simple math.

***Which neighborhoods your viewers come from.** Nextdoor shows you the neighborhoods your page viewers live in. If 60% of your views come from two neighborhoods that are 10 minutes away, but your store is in a third neighborhood, you know where to focus your marketing. A bakery in Philadelphia discovered that 47% of their page views came from a neighborhood on the other side of a bridge. They ran a targeted offer just for that neighborhood: "Show your ID at checkout for a free cookie — we know you drove here." That single post drove 89 new customers from that neighborhood in six weeks.

**What to ignore:**

Total follower count. It matters, but not as much as engagement rate. I've seen pages with 400 followers and 25 weekly interactions outperform pages with 1,200 followers and 10 interactions. Followers who don't engage are just numbers. Interactions that lead to visits are revenue.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I already get customers from Google and Yelp. Why should I bother with Nextdoor?**

Because Nextdoor reaches a different audience. Google captures people actively searching for a service. Yelp captures reviewers. Nextdoor captures neighbors who are scrolling local conversations and might not have been looking for you at all — until they see your post recommending your own business. I've seen multiple clients pick up customers who had lived two blocks away for years but never searched for their type of business online. They weren't looking. They were scrolling.

**Q: Can't I just use my personal Nextdoor account to promote my business?**

You can, but I don't recommend it. Personal accounts don't have access to business features like insights, offer tracking, and review management. You also risk annoying your neighbors if you're posting business content on a personal profile. One furniture restorer in Denver used his personal account for two years, then got reported for "commercial activity" and temporarily suspended. He lost access to his established neighborhood connections. Claim the business page. It's free.

**Q: Do I need to respond to every comment on my Nextdoor page?**

You don't need to reply to every single comment, but you should respond to questions within 24 hours and thank people for positive comments. Nextdoor's algorithm favors pages with recent owner activity. A simple "Thanks, Sarah! Hope to see you soon" counts as activity. I tell clients to aim for responding to 80% of comments within 48 hours. The remaining 20% are usually one-word comments or spam.

**Q: Will Nextdoor send me spammy requests from people who aren't really neighbors?**

Yes, occasionally. Nextdoor has improved their verification process, but you'll still get the occasional message from someone asking for a "sponsorship" or a "featured placement." Ignore those. Do not pay for anything that isn't an official Nextdoor ad product. If a message feels off, it probably is.

**Q: Should I run Nextdoor ads, or is organic enough?**

Organic is enough for most local businesses if you post consistently and engage actively. I would only consider ads if you've optimized your page, you're posting 2-3 times per week, you've built up at least 10 reviews, and you still want more visibility. Even then, start small. I had a client in Portland run a $200 test over 14 days. It drove 14 new leads, of which 6 converted to paying customers at an average of $85 per visit. That's $510 in revenue on $200 spend — decent, but not life-changing. Scale up only after organic is working.

**Q: How do I delete a Nextdoor business page if I don't want it?**

You can't delete it directly. You have to submit a removal request through Nextdoor's support system, and they decide whether to remove it. This is frustrating. I've had clients wait 6-8 weeks for removal. If you're stuck with a page you don't want but can't delete, just update it with accurate info and a link to your website. Make it useful so it doesn't hurt your brand while you wait.
