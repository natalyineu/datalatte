## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Buying Fake Reviews or Review Swapping — Austin, TX

I had a call with a pet groomer in Austin last year. Let's call him Mike. He ran "Austin Paws & Claws" and was frustrated that a new competitor with 200 reviews was beating him in the local pack. So Mike joined a Facebook group where groomers agreed to leave five-star reviews for each other. Within three weeks, he had 80 new reviews.

Google flagged 60 of them within a month.

His Google Business Profile got a "suspicious activity" warning. His rankings dropped to page 4. New customer calls went from roughly 25/week to maybe 4. He lost an estimated $2,800 in grooming bookings over the next six weeks while he tried to get the suspension lifted.

The fix: Mike deleted the flagged reviews, stopped swapping, and ran a legitimate review campaign. I had him set up a simple workflow: after checkout, Square emailed each customer a link to leave a Google review. He added a small incentive — a $5 discount on their next visit if they showed the review confirmation. Within 90 days, he had 45 real, verified reviews. His local rankings came back and actually improved because Google trusted the new ones.

The outcome: His calls recovered to 30/week within four months. Revenue stabilized around $5,200/month from new clients alone. That's $3,800 more than during the suspension period.

Do not buy reviews. Do not swap reviews. Google's fraud detection is not stupid — it's gotten significantly better in the last two years. You will lose weeks of business and damage your reputation.

### Mistake #2: Using the Wrong Google Business Profile Category — Nashville, TN

A dog grooming salon in Nashville called "Music City Pet Spa" spent $800/month on Google Ads but was getting almost no clicks. I looked at their Google Business Profile and found the problem immediately: they had selected "Pet Store" as their primary category instead of "Pet Groomer."

This matters more than most people realize. Google's local algorithm heavily weights your primary category. A "Pet Store" category means Google tries to rank you for "pet supplies near me" and "dog food Nashville" — keywords that have nothing to do with grooming. Meanwhile, someone searching "dog grooming Nashville" saw a competitor whose primary category was exactly that.

The fix: I changed the primary category to "Pet Groomer" and added "Dog Walker" and "Pet Boarding" as secondary categories only if they actually offered those services (they didn't, so I left them off). Then I updated their services list to include specific grooming types: "nail trim," "full groom small dog," "de-shedding treatment," "breed-specific cuts."

The outcome: Within two weeks, their impressions for "dog grooming Nashville" went up 340%. Ad spend stayed the same at $800/month, but cost per click dropped from $4.50 to $1.80 because Google now understood what they actually did. They booked an extra $1,200 in appointments the next month.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Schema Markup — Portland, OR

A Portland-based mobile pet groomer — "Furry Road Grooming" — had good reviews and used Semrush for keyword tracking. But they couldn't break past position 7 for "mobile dog groomer Portland."

I ran a Site Audit in Semrush and found they had zero schema markup on their website. Not even basic LocalBusiness schema. Their competitors had review schema, price range schema, and service type schema. Google was essentially guessing what Furry Road offered.

The fix: I added LocalBusiness schema with their service area (Portland metro), price range ($$), and specific service types (mobile grooming, de-shedding, flea baths). I also added FAQ schema on their services page answering questions like "Do you come to my house?" and "What areas do you cover in Portland?" Took about 45 minutes using a plugin.

The outcome: Within 30 days, they moved to position 3 for their main keyword. Organic traffic from Google increased from roughly 120 visitors/month to 310/month. They added $1,600 in monthly revenue from new clients who found them through search. Schema markup is free to implement and takes less than an hour — there is no excuse to skip it.

## How to Run Google Ads That Actually Make Money for Your Pet Grooming Business (Without Wasting $1,200 Like One Client Did)

I worked with a dog grooming shop in Denver called "Mile High Mutts." They were spending $1,200/month on Google Ads with a generic campaign targeting "dog grooming Denver." Their cost per click was $5.80 and they were getting maybe three bookings per month from the campaign. That's $400 per booking. Their average groom was $65. They were losing money on every click.

Most local service businesses run ads the same way: broad keywords, no negatives, send everyone to the homepage. It doesn't work.

Here's what I changed for them — and what you should do instead:

**Step 1: Campaign structure matters.** Instead of one campaign, I built three:
- Campaign A: "dog grooming Denver" exact match — $500/month budget. This is your core.
- Campaign B: "dog grooming near me" phrase match — $300/month. Captures mobile and voice search.
- Campaign C: Specific services ("nail trim Denver," "de-shedding treatment Denver") — $200/month each. These have lower competition and cheaper clicks.

**Step 2: Add negative keywords immediately.** If you don't add negatives, Google will show your ad for "cat grooming Denver," "cheap dog grooming," "DIY dog grooming," and "dog grooming training." Mile High Mutts wasted roughly $400/month on irrelevant clicks before I added 47 negative keywords. Common ones for pet groomers: "free," "training," "adoption," "vet," "supplies," "how to," "cheap," "diy."

**Step 3: Create service-specific landing pages, not homepage.** I created four separate landing pages: one for full grooming, one for nail trim only, one for de-shedding, one for breed-specific cuts. Each page had the exact service name in the H1 tag, three bullet points explaining what the customer gets, a booking button using Booksy, and six photos of actual dogs they had groomed (not stock photos).

**Step 4: Set up conversion tracking in Google Ads + Semrush.** Most small business owners don't know if their ads are working because they have no tracking. I linked Google Ads to their Booksy booking system via Google Tag Manager. Semrush then tracked which keywords drove bookings, not just clicks.

The outcome after 30 days:
- Cost per booking dropped from $400 to $18
- Monthly bookings from ads: 22 (up from 3)
- Revenue from ads: $1,430 on $1,200 spend (positive ROI for the first time)
- Cost per click dropped to $1.90 because Google rewarded relevant campaigns with higher quality scores

One warning: Google Ads is not a set-it-and-forget-it channel. You need to check it twice a week for the first month and add negative keywords as they appear. After that, weekly is fine.

## Why Your Appointment Booking Tool Might Be Killing Your Local SEO Rankings (and How to Fix It)

I see this constantly. A pet groomer uses Booksy, Vagaro, or Square Appointments to manage bookings. These tools are great for scheduling. But they often create duplicate listings on third-party directories automatically — and these directories compete with your website in search results.

A client in Chicago — "Windy City Paws" — had this exact problem. They used Booksy for scheduling. Booksy automatically created a listing on their network. Then Vagaro (which they had used six months earlier) still had a stale listing with old hours and a phone number that no longer worked. Then Yelp had a third version. And Google was confused about which information to show.

When I ran a Semrush listing management scan, I found 14 directory listings for Windy City Paws — eight of them had incorrect hours, four had the wrong phone number, and two listed services they no longer offered.

The fix:

**Clean up existing directories first.** I used Semrush's Listing Management tool to identify every directory where they had a profile. Then I claimed each one and corrected the NAP information (name, address, phone number). I also deleted or marked as closed the two accounts from services they no longer used.

**Set up proper integration between your booking tool and your website.** Instead of sending customers to booksy.com/windycitypaws, I embedded the Booksy booking widget directly onto their website. This keeps traffic on their domain, which Google interprets as a positive ranking signal. It also means customers don't get redirected to a third-party site where they might book a competitor who appears in the "similar businesses" sidebar.

**Add booking schema to your website.** Once you have the widget embedded, add Service schema markup that includes price range ($35-$85 for grooming), appointment availability (same-day if you offer it), and service area. This helps Google show a direct booking button in search results. Windy City Paws started showing a "Book Now" button in their local search snippet within two weeks.

**Use a tool like Mailchimp or Square Marketing to follow up.** Here's a thing most guides skip: booking tools track the appointment, but they don't track whether the customer came back. I set up a simple Mailchimp automation for Windy City Paws: seven days after a grooming appointment, the customer got an email with a photo of their dog (they saved it from the booking system), a review request link, and a reminder that the next groom is due in three to five weeks. This alone added 12 reviews in the first month.

The outcome: Windy City Paws went from having 14 conflicting directory listings to 5 consistent listings. Their local SEO visibility improved — they moved from position 6 to position 2 for "dog grooming Chicago" within 45 days. Revenue from new organic clients increased by about $2,200/month. And they stopped losing customers to booking redirects that showed competitor profiles.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I don't have time to learn Semrush. Can I just pay someone to do the local SEO for me?**

If you pay someone, make sure they actually know local SEO — not just general SEO. I've seen agencies charge $1,500/month to "optimize" a pet groomer's site and all they do is write two blog posts and install Yoast. You want someone who runs Semrush audits, checks your Google Business Profile weekly, tracks rankings, and monitors citations. That's $500-$1,000/month if the person knows what they're doing. But you still need to understand what's being done so you can tell if they're full of it. I'd recommend spending two hours with Semrush yourself before you outsource anything.

**Q: How long until I see results from local SEO?**

Realistic timeline: 30 days for minor fixes (fixing wrong category, adding schema, cleaning up citations), 90 days for noticeable ranking improvements, four to six months for significant traffic increases. If someone promises you page one results in two weeks, they're selling you something that doesn't exist or they're using black hat tactics that will get your profile suspended. The Denver pet groomer I mentioned saw ad results in two weeks because ads are paid traffic. Organic rankings take longer.

**Q: Do I need a separate website or can I just use my Facebook page and Google Business Profile?**

You need a website. Google gives significantly more weight to businesses with a dedicated website. A Facebook page alone will not rank well for local searches. You also lose control — if Facebook changes its algorithm or your account gets locked (happens more often than you'd think), you have zero online presence. A basic website with five pages, WordPress, and a mobile-friendly theme runs about $500 one-time plus $30/month hosting. Skip the expensive custom builds at this stage — just get something functional up.

**Q: Should I be on Yelp? I've heard they're expensive.**

Yelp is a mixed bag for pet groomers in the US. If you're in a city where Yelp has strong adoption (San Francisco, NYC, Los Angeles, Chicago), you need a Yelp Business page with accurate information. But I would not recommend paying Yelp for advertising. Their click costs are high ($6-$12 in competitive markets) and their lead quality is inconsistent. Claim your free page, respond to reviews, post photos of your work. Do not sign up for their ad program until you have Google Ads running profitably first.

**Q: What if I have multiple locations? Do I need separate Google Business Profiles?**

Yes. One Google Business Profile per physical location. Each needs its own phone number, address, and set of reviews. If you try to use one profile for two locations, Google will either suspend the listing or show the wrong address for half your customers. You also need location-specific pages on your website. "Pet Grooming Denver – Downtown" and "Pet Grooming Denver – Capitol Hill" should be separate pages with unique content. Semrush's Position Tracking tool lets you track rankings separately for each location.

**Q: Can I manage all of this myself without hiring someone?**

Yes, but it depends on your tolerance for learning technical tools. Semrush has a steep learning curve for the first week. After that, you'll spend about two to three hours per week on: checking rankings (15 minutes), reviewing search queries and adding negatives if you run ads (20 minutes), responding to Google and Yelp reviews (20 minutes), checking your Site Audit report for errors (15 minutes), and looking at what competitors are doing in your area (30 minutes). If that sounds manageable, do it yourself. If it sounds like hell, find a freelancer who specializes in local SEO and does not manage accounts for 50 other businesses at once.
