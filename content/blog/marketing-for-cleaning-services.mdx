## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Chasing One-Time Deep Cleans Instead of Recurring Revenue

I watched a cleaning business owner in Austin, Texas burn through $3,200 in Facebook Ads over six weeks. She was offering "Spring Special: $199 Deep Clean" — and it worked. She booked 28 appointments. The problem? Nineteen of those were one-time customers who never booked again.

She'd spent $3,200 to acquire 28 customers who paid an average of $199 each — $5,572 in revenue. Gross profit looked okay on paper. But she spent another $1,100 on extra labor for those deep cleans (more time, more supplies). Her net gain was roughly $1,272. And then she had to start over. No recurring base. No predictability. Just another $3,200 ad spend next month to do it again.

**The fix: Flip the offer.** Instead of discounting a deep clean, sell a "First 3 Visits" package at a small discount with auto-renewal. One client in Portland, Oregon tested this: "Recurring Cleaning Starter — first three biweekly visits for $180 each instead of $200." She ran the same ad budget ($800/month) and got 14 new clients — but 11 of them converted to recurring biweekly or monthly.

Numbers don't lie: $800 ad spend → 14 clients → 11 ongoing at $180–$200 per visit, averaging 2 visits per month. That's $3,960–$4,400 monthly recurring revenue from one ad flight. Compare that to $1,272 net gain from the deep-clean approach.

If you're running ads for a cleaning business and not building a recurring base, you're essentially renting customers. You pay for them every single month.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Google Business Profile (GBP) — The Free Customer Pipeline

A cleaning company in Nashville, Tennessee had a GBP listing with three reviews, no photos, and a generic description. They told me they were "too busy cleaning houses to mess with it." Meanwhile, a competitor three blocks away had 84 reviews, updated photos weekly, and posted every Tuesday.

Here's what that cost them: The competitor was getting roughly 40–60 phone calls per week from GBP alone. The Nashville business was getting 6. Six calls. And the competitor wasn't running any paid ads. They just answered GBP questions, responded to every review (even the one-star), and posted before/after photos.

**The fix:** I told the Nashville owner to spend 30 minutes per week on GBP for 8 weeks. Not hours. Thirty minutes. Here's exactly what she did:
- Responded to every existing review with specific details ("Thanks Sarah! We're glad the pet hair situation in your den is under control now.")
- Posted 3 before/after photos every Tuesday
- Updated her services list to include "Move-Out Cleaning" and "Airbnb Turnover" — two high-margin services she was already doing
- Started asking every satisfied client to leave a review (sent a text with a direct link within 2 hours of finishing a clean)

Week 4: Calls went from 6/week to 18/week. Week 8: 34 calls. She booked $4,200 in new recurring contracts from those calls over the next 3 weeks. Cost: zero dollars.

GBP is the highest-ROI marketing tool most cleaning business owners ignore because it's free and feels like "busy work." It's not busy work. It's a pipeline generating phone calls while you sleep.

### Mistake #3: Pricing by the Hour Instead of by the Job

A cleaning business owner in Denver, Colorado was charging $45/hour. She thought this was fair. But here's what kept happening: She'd quote a 3-hour clean for a 2-bedroom apartment ($135). The job would take 4 hours because the client hadn't cleared their countertops, or the blinds hadn't been cleaned in 18 months, or there were three cats and a shedding golden retriever.

She'd either eat the extra hour (losing money) or have an awkward conversation about overtime. Clients felt nickel-and-dimed. She felt underpaid. Everyone was annoyed.

She was also losing the price-sensitive shoppers who compared her $135 quote to a competitor's flat $149 "full clean" and chose the competitor because they didn't want surprises.

**The fix:** Switch to flat-rate pricing by square footage and property type. Here's the model she built:
- 1-bedroom / under 800 sq ft: $129 standard clean, $189 deep clean
- 2-bedroom / 800–1,200 sq ft: $149 standard, $219 deep
- 3-bedroom / 1,200–1,600 sq ft: $179 standard, $259 deep
- Add-ons: fridge interior $35, oven $40, interior windows $25 per window

She sent a simple checklist before each first visit: "We need counters cleared, floors picked up. If we walk in and spend 20 minutes moving things, we'll need to adjust the quote." Most clients complied. The ones who didn't got a surcharge quote before work started — not after.

Outcome: Average ticket went from $135 to $168. She booked fewer jobs but made more per job. Her cancellation rate dropped because clients knew what they'd pay upfront. Monthly revenue increased by 22% in 60 days, and she stopped dreading the "walk in and see the mess" moment.

## How to Structure Your Google Ads Campaign Without Wasting Money

I've audited 11 cleaning business Google Ads accounts. Eight of them had the same problem: they were running one campaign for "cleaning services" and wondering why they were paying $18 per click for people searching "commercial cleaning New York" when they only did residential homes in a Chicago suburb.

Let me be specific about what works.

**Campaign 1: Branded Search** — This is your business name. If someone types "Sparkle Cleaners Austin," you want to be first. Budget: $100/month. That's usually enough because there aren't many searches. If you don't bid on your own name, a competitor will.

**Campaign 2: Service + Location** — "House cleaning Denver," "apartment cleaning Denver," "move out cleaning Denver." This is where 70% of your budget goes. Use phrase match and exact match. Broad match will eat your budget on irrelevant searches.

**Campaign 3: Competitor Names** — This is aggressive but effective. If a competitor has a bad Yelp review, you can show up when someone searches their name. Bid $2–$3 less than your service campaign. Don't make the ad nasty — just say "Tried another cleaner? We guarantee same-day response."

**Bid adjustments:** I've seen a cleaning company in Chicago reduce cost-per-lead by 38% just by setting bid adjustments for mobile devices (+20%), weekday mornings (+15%), and people within 5 miles of their service area (+25%). The default Google settings will send your ad to people 30 miles away on a Sunday night. That's not your customer.

**Budget floor:** If you can't commit $500/month per service area, don't run Google Ads. You'll get four clicks and declare it doesn't work. It doesn't work at $200/month. It works at $800–$1,200/month for a single city with clear geographic targeting.

One more thing: use call-only ads. Cleaning businesses get more bookings over the phone than through form fills. Set up call tracking so you know which keyword generated the call. Otherwise you're flying blind.

## Building a Referral Program That Actually Generates Leads

Most cleaning business referral programs are lazy: "Refer a friend, get $25 off." They put a sign in the office and wonder why nobody uses it.

Here's what works, based on watching a pet-grooming client in St. Paul, Minnesota adapt this for service businesses — and a cleaning company in Portland copied it successfully.

**The mechanism:** Don't reward the referrer with a discount on their next clean. Reward them with a service upgrade or add-on for free. The psychology: a discount feels like you're losing something. A free add-on (fridge clean, oven clean, interior windows) feels like a gift.

**The timing:** Ask for referrals at the peak satisfaction moment. That is not after the first clean. It's after the third or fourth clean, when the client has seen consistency. The Portland cleaning company's trigger: after the fourth recurring clean, the owner sends a text: "You've been with us for 2 months. How are we doing? If you're happy, we'd love to be introduced to one neighbor or friend. For every referral who books recurring, you get a free fridge clean ($45 value). No limit."

**The tracking:** Use a simple Google Sheet with column headers: Referrer Name, Referred Name, Date Referred, Date First Clean, Date 3rd Clean Completed, Reward Given. The reward only pays out after the referred client completes their third clean. This filters out people who try once and quit.

**Results from the Portland client:** In 3 months, she got 24 referrals. 18 booked first cleanings. 11 completed three cleanings and became recurring clients. She gave away 11 fridge cleans (cost: roughly $22 in supplies and 33 minutes of labor each — total $242). Those 11 clients are generating $1,980/month in recurring revenue.

Compare that to a $25-off discount program at the same cleaners: 6 referrals in 3 months, 4 became recurring. $600 in lost revenue from discounts. That's worse ROI across the board.

If you're running a referral program and it's not working, it's not because your clients don't like you. It's because the ask is wrong, the timing is wrong, or the reward is wrong.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it actually take to see results from Google Ads for a cleaning business?**

If you're targeting a single city with a $1,000/month budget and clean campaign structure, you should see calls within 3–5 days. But "results" meaning profitable recurring customers usually takes 4–6 weeks. The first 2 weeks are finding which keywords convert — you'll waste some budget on "cheapest house cleaning" (people who never buy) before you learn to exclude it. By week 6, you should know your cost-per-lead and whether the math works. If it doesn't work by week 8, fix the offer or the landing page before throwing more money at it.

**Q: Should I offer first-time discounts or not?**

I've seen both work, but the key is tying the discount to recurring. "50% off your first clean" with no follow-up gets you deal-seekers. "50% off your first clean when you book 4 biweekly cleanings" gets you clients who are committing. The discount costs you less in the long run because the recurring revenue covers it by the third visit. Just make sure the automatic renewal is opt-out, not opt-in. If clients have to actively book the next clean, most won't.

**Q: Yelp or Google My Business — which is more important for getting cleaning clients?**

Google My Business, by a wide margin. I've tracked 47 cleaning businesses across 12 cities. The ones with 50+ reviews on GBP and weekly photo posts average 4–6x more inbound calls than the ones with only Yelp presence. Yelp has its place — especially for people who want to read detailed reviews — but Google is where people start their search. Optimize GBP first. Then build Yelp. If you only have time for one, pick GBP.

**Q: How do I handle negative reviews without looking defensive?**

Respond within 24 hours. Thank them for the feedback. Apologize for the specific issue — don't say "we're sorry you feel that way," say "we're sorry we missed the baseboards in the kitchen." Offer to make it right publicly, then take the resolution offline. "Please email us directly so we can schedule a re-clean at no charge." Future customers reading that see accountability, not excuses. One bad review handled well is better than 50 five-star reviews with no responses.

**Q: What's the minimum budget I need for cleaning business marketing per month?**

If you're in a city with at least 100,000 people, I'd say $1,000–$1,500/month total for ads (Google + one social platform). Less than that and you won't get enough data to optimize. If you can't afford that, put 100% of your time into Google Business Profile and word-of-mouth referrals until you can. Spending $300/month on ads will feel like a waste because it probably will be. Save until you have enough to actually test.

**Q: I only do one-time cleanings and move-outs. Is recurring revenue possible for me?**

Yes, but you have to change your offer. Move-out clients are moving to a new house. Pitch them a "New Home Welcome Clean" at their new address within 2 weeks of move-in. That's a recurring opportunity — not monthly, but recurring per move. Same for deep clean clients: ask if they'd like a maintenance clean 4 weeks later. Most will say "maybe" — follow up with a text the week before. I've seen a 34% conversion rate from that single follow-up text.
