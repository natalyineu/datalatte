## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Google Ads Without Negative Keywords or Location Targeting

**The story:** A plumber in Austin, Texas — let's call him Mark — came to me after burning through $2,400 in three weeks on Google Ads. He was getting clicks, sure. But exactly one of those clicks turned into a booked job. A toilet repair for $180. His ROI was negative by about $2,200.

I looked at his search terms report. He was showing up for "plumbing classes near me," "DIY pipe repair," and "how to fix a leaky faucet." People in San Antonio, Houston, and even a few from Oklahoma were clicking his ad. He had his location set to "within 50 miles of Austin" but Google's default interpretation of "50 miles" includes a lot of cow pasture and distant suburbs where he didn't even offer service.

**The fix:** We tightened his location targeting to a 10-mile radius around his service area and set the location option to "Presence: People in or regularly in your targeted locations." Then we added 85 negative keywords — anything with "how to," "DIY," "class," "training," "parts," "repair cost," "emergency" (the last one sounds counterintuitive, but I'll explain in a second). We also excluded mobile users outside his target cities during overnight hours, because people searching for plumbers at 2 a.m. from a bar in Dallas are not his customers.

**The outcome:** His cost per lead dropped from $137 to $22. Over the next 60 days, he spent $1,800 and got 82 leads. He booked 23 jobs from those leads, totaling $8,740 in revenue. His ad spend became profitable in the second week. Mark later told me he wished he'd called someone before setting it up himself. I told him I wish more business owners did.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Google Local Services Ads (and Relying Only on Traditional Google Ads)

**The story:** A plumbing company in Denver — three trucks, five employees, decent reputation — was spending $3,200/month on standard Google Search Ads. They had a 4.5-star Google rating. But their phone was not ringing as much as it used to. The owner, Jen, was frustrated because she knew customers were searching for plumbers in her area.

I checked her Google Ads account and found she had zero Local Services Ads (LSAs) running. For plumbers, LSAs are the "Google Guaranteed" ads that show at the very top of search results, with a green checkmark. They are pay-per-lead, not pay-per-click. You only pay when someone contacts you through the ad. Most plumbers don't know about them, or they assume they're too expensive.

**The fix:** We set up an LSA campaign for her Denver service area. The verification process required a business license, insurance, and a background check, but once that was done, her ads appeared above the standard Google Ads. We set a weekly budget of $300 and used the "flexible" bidding option to maximize leads. We also cleaned up her Google Business Profile to match the LSA requirements — same address, same phone number, same service categories.

**The outcome:** Within the first week, she received 18 leads from LSAs at an average cost of $16 per lead. She booked 7 jobs from those leads, worth $2,450. After two months, she reduced her standard Google Ads spend by 40% and shifted that budget to LSAs. Her overall lead cost dropped by 52%, and her revenue from Google grew by 34% in the next quarter. The best part? She only paid for actual leads, not for clicks from window-shoppers.

### Mistake #3: Not Tracking Phone Calls (The Silent Revenue Killer)

**The story:** A small plumbing outfit in Portland, Oregon — two guys, one van, lots of word-of-mouth. They had a website, a Google Business Profile, and a Facebook page. The owner, Rick, thought he was getting most of his leads from referrals. When I asked how many calls he got per week, he said, "Maybe 15-20? I don't really count." When I asked how many of those turned into jobs, he said, "Most of them, I think."

That's a dangerous "I think." I installed call tracking (using CallRail) on his website, Google Business Profile, and Facebook ads. After two weeks, the data was sobering: he was getting 28 calls per week on average. Of those, 12 went to voicemail because he was on another call or didn't answer. Of the 12 voicemails, only 3 called back. That's 9 lost opportunities per week. At an average job value of $350, he was leaving roughly $3,150 per week on the table.

**The fix:** We set up a simple call routing system: missed calls forward to a team member's mobile, and after-hours calls go to a voicemail that immediately sends a text with a link to book online. We also added a "click-to-call" button on his website that tracked the source. Then we trained Rick to answer the phone on the first ring whenever possible during business hours. He also started returning voicemails within 10 minutes instead of hours later.

**The outcome:** In the first month, his answered call rate went from 57% to 92%. He booked an additional 14 jobs that month, worth $4,900 in new revenue. Tracking also showed him that his Facebook ads were generating cheaper leads than his Google ads, so he shifted spend accordingly. He told me later, "I never realized how many calls I was just missing." That's the thing — most plumbers don't.

### Mistake #4: Relying on Word-of-Mouth Alone (and Ignoring Review Generation)

**The story:** A plumbing company in Chicago — established for 12 years, great reputation, but flat growth. The owner, Tony, was proud that he never "paid for leads." He relied entirely on referrals and repeat customers. His annual revenue had been stuck around $450,000 for three years. Google's local search algorithm heavily favors businesses with more recent and frequent reviews. Tony was invisible in search results for "plumber Chicago" unless someone typed his exact business name.

**The fix:** We implemented a simple review generation workflow: after every completed job, the customer received a text message 24 hours later with a direct link to leave a Google review. We also sent a follow-up email with a template for a short review. Tony was skeptical — "People won't do that" — but I told him to just try it for 30 days. We also responded to every single review, positive or negative, within 48 hours.

**The outcome:** In three months, Tony's review count went from 23 to 118. His average rating stayed at 4.6 stars. His Google Business Profile impressions increased by 340%. Phone calls from Google search (not ads) went from about 5 per week to 22 per week. His revenue for that quarter hit $137,000 — a 22% increase over the same quarter the previous year. ## How to Actually Measure What Works (and Kill What Doesn't)

Most small business owners I meet are drowning in data they never look at, or they're flying blind. There's no in-between. The plumber who calls me says, "I'm spending $500 a month on Facebook and I'm not sure if it's working." The other guy says, "I don't do any marketing, I just rely on word-of-mouth."

Neither approach is sustainable.

Here's the truth: you need three numbers to know if a channel is working. Not fourteen. Just three.

1. **Cost per lead** – How much you spend to get someone to call, fill out a form, or book online. For plumbers, a good cost per lead on Google Ads is $20-$40, depending on your market. In NYC or LA, it can be $50-$75. In smaller markets like Wichita or Boise, it should be under $20. If you're paying more than that consistently, something is wrong — bad keywords, bad targeting, or bad landing page.

2. **Lead-to-job conversion rate** – What percentage of leads actually become paying customers. This should be 40-60% for emergency plumbing calls, maybe 25-35% for scheduled maintenance or larger projects. If you're converting less than 20%, you're either getting poor-quality leads or your sales process is broken. Record your phone calls for a week and listen back. You'll hear it.

3. **Average job value** – The revenue from a typical booked job. This varies wildly — a leaky faucet might be $150, a water heater replacement $1,200, a sewer line repair $3,000. But you need to know your baseline. Then you can calculate return on ad spend (ROAS) by multiplying leads × conversion rate × average job value, and dividing by total ad spend.

I use Google Analytics 4 (painful, I know, but necessary) combined with a call tracking tool like CallRail or WhatConverts. These tools tell you exactly which keyword, which ad, and which channel drove the phone call. Without call tracking, you're guessing. And guessing with money on the line is not a strategy.

One more thing: set a two-week testing budget for any new channel. If after 14 days you're not getting leads at a cost you can live with, kill it. Don't wait three months. The "let's see how it goes" mentality is how you end up spending $2,400 on clicks from Oklahoma.

## The One Channel Most Plumbers Ignore (That's Costing You Jobs)

Ask ten plumbers what their best lead source is, and nine will say "Google" or "referrals." Very few will say "Yelp." And that's because Yelp has a reputation — sometimes deserved — for aggressive sales calls and holding reviews hostage.

But here's the uncomfortable truth: Yelp is the second-largest search engine for local services in the United States. In cities like Nashville, Austin, Denver, and Seattle, Yelp drives more than 20% of all plumbing searches. If you're ignoring it, you're leaving money on the table.

The key is not to treat Yelp like Google. You don't need an expensive "Yelp Ads" campaign that charges you per click whether or not the customer converts. You can get leads for free by optimizing your Yelp Business Page — and yes, it actually works.

Here's what to do:

- Claim your Yelp page and fill out every single field. Service area, photos of your work (actual before-and-after shots, not stock photos), list of services, business hours, and a detailed description that includes your primary service area zip codes.
- Get at least 10 reviews on Yelp. I know, Yelp's algorithm suppresses pages with few reviews. But here's the loophole: ask your best customers directly. Do NOT offer incentives — Yelp bans that and will flag you. Just ask. "Hey, if you liked our work, a Yelp review would really help our small business."
- Respond to every review, positive or negative, within 48 hours. For negative reviews, apologize publicly and offer to make it right offline. Prospective customers read those responses. A professional reply to a complaint can actually win you a new customer.

The results? A plumbing company in Nashville — four vans, competing against 20+ other plumbers — started getting an average of 7 Yelp leads per month after following this process. No ad spend. Just optimization and review requests. Those 7 leads turned into 3-4 jobs per month, worth about $1,000-$1,500 in additional revenue. Over a year, that's $12,000-$18,000 from a channel they were previously ignoring.

One caveat: Yelp's mobile app has a "Request a Quote" feature that sends your info directly to the customer. Make sure you respond within an hour. Yelp tracks response time, and faster responses get prioritized in search results.

## Pricing Strategy and Lead Qualification: Don't Waste Time on Tire-Kickers

I've seen this pattern at three different clients: a plumber gets a flood of leads from a new campaign, celebrates for a week, and then realizes half the leads are price shoppers who never book. They waste hours on estimates for customers who then hire the cheaper guy down the street. The result? High lead volume, low revenue.

The fix is not to lower your prices. The fix is to qualify leads before you send a truck.

Here's how I do it with clients:

**Get the customer on the phone first.** A form fill or email inquiry is not a lead. It's a maybe. Call them within 5 minutes. During that call, ask these three questions:

1. What's the specific problem? (Not "my toilet is running" — that's too vague. Dig into "the toilet runs every 3 minutes and you can hear water trickling.")
2. How long has it been going on? (Urgency.)
3. Have you gotten other quotes? (If yes, they're shopping. Quote higher.)

If they can't answer #1 clearly, they're likely a tire-kicker or a DIYer who wants free advice. Politely offer a service call fee — "We charge $85 to diagnose the issue, and if you go ahead with the repair, that fee is waived." This filters out 70% of non-serious leads.

I had a client in Philadelphia who was spending $900/month on Google Ads and getting 30 leads per month — but only booking 5 jobs. After we implemented phone qualification (and a $79 diagnostic fee that was credited toward any repair), his lead volume dropped to 18 per month, but his bookings went up to 9. His revenue actually increased by $1,200 per month because he wasn't wasting time on dead-end estimates.

**Use a booking tool that requires payment info.** For non-emergency work (water heater installs, repiping, etc.), use a platform like ServiceTitan or Housecall Pro that lets customers schedule a time slot and confirm with a credit card. No card? No appointment. This eliminates no-shows and reduces time wasted on ghosting.

**Price transparently, but not cheaply.** I tell clients to list starting prices on their website for common jobs — "Water heater installation starting at $850" — because it sets expectations and scares away buyers who can only afford $400. The customers who click "Call Now" after seeing that price are already pre-qualified. You're not losing business; you're losing bad business.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I use Yelp if they keep calling me to advertise? Isn't it a scam?**

Yelp's sales team is aggressive, I'm not going to defend them. But the platform itself is not a scam. You do not need to pay for Yelp ads to get leads. Just claim your free page, optimize it, and collect reviews organically. Ignore the sales calls — or tell them you're not interested. The leads you get from a well-optimized free Yelp page are real. I've seen it work for plumbers in Austin, Portland, and Nashville without spending a dime on Yelp ads.

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads per month to see results?**

For a single plumber or small team in a mid-sized city (e.g., Austin, Denver, Nashville), start with $500-$800 per month. That's enough to test 10-15 keywords and get 20-40 leads, assuming your campaign is set up correctly. If you're in a competitive market like New York City or Los Angeles, budget $1,200-$2,000 per month because clicks cost more. But don't increase budget until you know your cost per lead is under $40 and your conversion rate is above 30%. Throwing money at a broken campaign just burns cash faster.

**Q: Do I really need a website if I have a Google Business Profile?**

Yes. Google Business Profile is a listing, not a website. It has limited customization. You cannot add detailed service pages, customer testimonials with photos, a blog, or a booking system. More importantly, Google favors businesses with a website when ranking local results — especially if the website has a clear "Call Now" or "Book Online" button. A simple one-page website with your services, pricing, contact info, and reviews is enough. Use Squarespace or Wix, cost $20/month. Skip the fancy animations. Just make it fast and mobile-friendly.

**Q: Why do I get leads that never answer their phone?**

Two reasons. First, you may be calling them too late. Studies show you have a 90% chance of booking if you call within 5 minutes of an inquiry. After 30 minutes, that drops to 20%. Second, some leads are just browsing — they filled out a form on three different plumber's sites at once. You need to be the first one to call. Use a tool like CallRail or a CRM that sends you an instant notification when a lead comes in. And when you call, leave a voicemail AND send a text with a link to your booking page.

**Q: Should I offer free estimates?**

Here's my honest answer: for small, common jobs (unclog a drain, fix a leaky faucet), no. Charge a service call fee of $49-$89 and waive it if they approve the repair. For larger jobs like a water heater install, you can offer a free estimate — but only if you show up on time and bring a tablet with a clear proposal you can send immediately. If you're giving free estimates over the phone, you're wasting time. The tire-kickers love free phone estimates because they can hang up and call the next guy.

**Q: How long until I see results from SEO (search engine optimization) for plumbers?**

Honest answer: 3-6 months before you see a meaningful increase in organic leads. SEO is not a quick fix. If you need leads next week, run Google Ads or LSAs. But long-term, SEO is a better investment because you're not paying per click. The fastest win is optimizing your Google Business Profile — add photos, update services, respond to reviews, post weekly updates. You'll see a bump in impressions within 30 days. Full organic rankings on your website take longer, but the ROI over two years is enormous.
