## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Running TikTok Ads to a 50+ Audience

A boutique coffee roaster in Austin, Texas, spent $2,100 over six weeks on TikTok ads. The owner, Mark, wanted to promote a new cold brew line to “everyone in Austin who drinks coffee.” He set the targeting to ages 25–65 with a 10-mile radius. The campaign generated 47,000 impressions, 312 link clicks, and exactly zero in-store redemptions of the QR code offer.

The problem wasn’t the ad creative — the video was solid, shot by a local food photographer. The problem was the audience. TikTok’s active user base in Austin skews under 35. The people who actually walk into a coffee shop between 7 and 9 a.m. on a weekday? Mostly 30–55, picking up beans on the way to the office, dropping kids at school, working remotely from the corner table. Mark’s ad was shown to people who scroll TikTok after midnight and order delivery, not people who buy $18 bags of single-origin on a Tuesday morning.

The fix: We moved the budget to Snapchat, focused on audiences 28–50, and used a “drive to store” ad format with a same-day offer. The creative was a simple photo of the cold brew shelf with the text “Show this at register → 20% off your first bag.” Budget was $700/month.

Outcome: 87 coupon redemptions in the first month, average ticket $24. That’s $2,088 in direct revenue from a $700 ad spend — and most of those customers bought again. The coffee was good. The targeting just needed to match the actual buyer.

### Mistake 2: Using TikTok for a Business That Can’t Film Daily

A hair salon in Nashville called Parlour & Co. hired a freelance videographer to make five polished TikTok videos for $1,500. They posted one per week. After six weeks, they had 23 followers, 4 booking inquiries, and 1 actual appointment that didn’t show.

The salon owner, Jenna, thought TikTok was about quality. It’s not — not for local businesses. TikTok’s algorithm rewards volume and trend-matching. A single high-production video posted weekly gets buried. The hairstylists who win on TikTok post three to four times per day, roughly filmed on a phone, showing before-and-after transformations in real time.

Parlour & Co. didn’t have the staff bandwidth for that. Jenna’s stylists were booked 75% of the time. Asking them to manage a TikTok calendar was a nonstarter.

The fix: We paused TikTok entirely and moved to Snapchat Sponsored Geofilters and Snap Ads targeting people within 2 miles of the salon, during 10 a.m.–2 p.m. windows (when people search for last-minute appointments). We also set up a booking link via Booksy and ran a “Book online, get $10 off” offer. Total monthly spend: $600.

Outcome: 23 new-client bookings in the first month. The average first visit was $95 (cut and blow-dry). That’s $2,185 in revenue off $600 in ad spend. Month two? Repeat bookings started coming in. The stylists didn’t have to touch a camera once.

### Mistake 3: Splitting a Tiny Budget Across Both Platforms

A pet grooming business in Denver decided to “test both” Snapchat and TikTok simultaneously. Their total monthly ad budget was $800. They put $400 on each platform, ran for four weeks, and ended up with 12 total interactions across both — zero bookings.

The cosmetics brand down the street got 3,000 likes in a week. What did they have that the groomer didn’t? First, the cosmetics brand had a product you can ship. Pet grooming is a location-based service. Second, $400 per platform is too small to reach statistical significance on TikTok’s auction system. You’re competing against national advertisers spending $50,000 a day. Your $400 gets shown three times to the same 200 people, then the algorithm decides your ad is underperforming and stops delivering.

The fix: Put the entire $800 into Snapchat with a 2-mile radius, targeting pet owners aged 25–55. The creative was a 6-second video of a fluffy dog walking out of the shop with a bow. The CTA: “Tap to book your groom — mention this ad for 15% off.”

Outcome: 18 bookings in the first week alone. Average groom price: $65. That’s $1,170 in revenue. Over the month, the salon booked 52 new clients from Snapchat. One of those clients now brings in three dogs every five weeks. That’s roughly $780 per year in recurring revenue from a single $800 ad spend.

## When a Single Platform Strategy Beats Both

Sometimes the answer isn’t Snapchat or TikTok. It’s neither.

I’ve worked with a locksmith in Portland, Oregon, who was convinced they needed social media ads because their neighbor’s bakery was killing it on Instagram. But here’s the thing: people don’t discover locksmiths while scrolling for entertainment. They Google “emergency locksmith near me” at 11 p.m. after locking keys in the car. The intent is immediate, and the search window is tiny.

The business owner had been spending $900/month on TikTok ads targeting homeowners aged 30–60. They got 14 calls in two months. Most were people asking for prices, then hanging up. No jobs booked.

We pulled the social budget entirely and put it into Google Local Services Ads and a small Google Search campaign targeting “emergency locksmith Portland” and “car unlock Portland.” The LSA program charges per lead, not per click, and gives you a Google Guaranteed badge. Monthly spend: $750. Cost per lead: $18–$22.

Month one results: 41 booked jobs. Average ticket: $185. That’s $7,585 in revenue. The TikTok ads had been generating roughly $1.30 per dollar spent. The Google campaign was running at 10x that.

If your business solves an urgent, location-specific problem — locksmith, plumber, dentist, towing, urgent care — social media advertising is often the wrong move. People aren’t browsing TikTok and thinking, “You know what, I should get a root canal.” They search when the pain hits. Make sure you show up there first.

The tools that matter here: Google Local Services Ads (not just regular Google Ads — the LSA product specifically), Google Business Profile optimization, and a local service aggregator like Yelp if your category is active there. Skip the LinkedIn agency consultants who tell you to “build brand awareness.” If someone needs you at 2 a.m., awareness is irrelevant. Being the first result is what matters.

## The Tools That Actually Help You Track Foot Traffic

I keep hearing small business owners say, “I ran ads but I don’t know if they drove foot traffic.” That’s because they’re not setting up tracking before the ads go live. Here are the tools that fix this, with real setup time required.

### Square or Clover POS integration

If you run a coffee shop, salon, or boutique and you’re still punching orders into a register from 1998, fix that before you spend a dollar on ads. Set up Square or Clover, create a unique “ad offer” as a product or discount code in the POS, and train your staff to ask, “How did you hear about us?” manually at checkout. Yes, it’s low-tech. It works.

A bakery in Brooklyn called Ovenbird did this. They ran a $500 Snapchat campaign offering “Free croissant with any coffee purchase — show this at register.” They created a “Snapchat Offer” button in their Square POS. Over four weeks, 143 people redeemed it. Average spend per redemption (coffee plus extras): $9.50. Total attributable revenue: $1,358. Without the POS code, they’d have no way to know which sales came from the ad versus a passerby.

### Mailchimp or Klaviyo for repeat attribution

Here’s something most guides skip: An ad that drives a first-time visit is only valuable if that customer comes back. Set up a simple email capture at checkout — even if it’s a physical sign with a QR code: “Text COFFEE to 555-1234 → get 10% off next visit.” Use Mailchimp or Klaviyo to tag customers by acquisition channel.

A fitness studio in Chicago used this method. They ran a $400 Snapchat campaign with a “first class free” offer. Anyone who signed up via the ad link was tagged in Mailchimp as “Snapchat Q1.” Then they sent a three-email nurture sequence: a thank-you, a class schedule, and a “book your second class at $10” offer. Of the 64 people who took the free class, 19 booked a paid class within three weeks. The studio’s average monthly membership is $120. Even if half those 19 convert to full members, that’s $1,140 in recurring monthly revenue from a $400 campaign.

### Booksy or Vagaro for service businesses

Salons, barbers, pet groomers, massage therapists — these businesses live or die on booking rates. Booksy lets you create a “promo code” field at checkout. Add a code like “SNAP20” to your Snapchat ad creative. When someone books using that code, you know exactly which platform drove the lead.

A barbershop in Dallas ran $300 in Snapchat ads with a “$5 off your next cut” code. Over the month, 41 appointments were booked with the code. Average cut price: $40. That’s $1,640 in revenue directly tied to the ad. Without the code, they’d just see “busy month” and guess.

## The Geography Factor: City-Specific Platform Performance

Platform performance varies more by city than most guides admit. National averages are useless when you’re trying to move inventory in a specific zip code.

In New York City, TikTok has a massive presence among 18–34 year olds in Brooklyn, Queens, and Manhattan. But Snapchat still dominates in Staten Island and parts of the Bronx where the demographic skews younger and more family-oriented. I’ve seen a pizza shop in Bushwick crush it on TikTok ($1,200 ad spend → $3,800 in online orders over two weeks). Meanwhile, a dentist in Staten Island got exactly zero calls from a TikTok ad campaign that cost $600.

The difference? In Bushwick, people are out late, order delivery frequently, and share TikTok videos of giant pizza slices. In Staten Island, people search Google for “dentist near me” and call the first result.

In Portland and Austin, Snapchat performs better than TikTok for businesses with a 25–55 target audience. In Denver and Nashville, it’s closer to a tie. In Chicago, TikTok wins for food and beverage, but Snapchat wins for services like auto repair and HVAC.

If you’re in a mid-sized city (300,000–1 million population), here’s a cheap way to test: Spend $200 on each platform for one week, with the exact same creative and offer. Track results by source code or unique phone number. Whichever platform drives more qualified leads in week one gets the next month’s budget. This isn’t sophisticated. It works.

The only time this test fails is when $200 is too small for the platform to optimize. On TikTok, I’d recommend a minimum of $500 per week for local service businesses. On Snapchat, $200 can work if your radius is tight and your offer is specific.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long until I see actual results from Snapchat or TikTok ads?**

If you’re running a direct-response campaign with a clear offer and proper tracking, you should see results within the first week. I’ve seen campaigns generate redemptions within 24 hours when the targeting and creative are aligned. If you’ve gone two weeks with zero conversions, the problem is either your audience, your offer, or your tracking — not the platform. Pause, fix one variable, restart.

**Q: I don’t have video content. Can I still run ads on these platforms?**

Yes, but the quality bar is lower than you think. On Snapchat, a still photo with text overlay and a clear CTA works fine. On TikTok, you need video, but it doesn’t need to be produced. A 15-second clip shot on a phone, in your store, showing your product or service in use, outperforms polished studio content 80% of the time. Don’t outsource your ad creative to an agency that’s three states away. Film it yourself.

**Q: What’s the minimum monthly budget for these platforms to actually work?**

For Snapchat, I’d say $500/month minimum for a local business in a metro area. Below that, the targeting gets too narrow and the algorithm doesn’t have enough data to optimize. For TikTok, $800–1,000/month minimum. Below $500, you’re paying for impressions, not results. If your budget is $200/month, put it into Google Local Services Ads instead.

**Q: My customers are 35–55. Should I even bother with these platforms?**

Yes, if you use the right one. Snapchat’s user base has aged up. The 30–55 demographic is active, especially for local discovery. TikTok skews younger — if your core customer is 40+, I’d test Snapchat first. I’ve seen a 50-year-old dentist in Cincinnati generate 12 new patients from a $400 Snapchat campaign targeting ages 30–55. It’s not a Gen Z platform anymore.

**Q: Can I run both platforms at the same time with a small budget?**

Not effectively. With a combined budget under $2,000/month, you’re splitting your data pool in half. Neither platform gets enough conversions to optimize. Pick one, run it well for 60 days, then test the other if you have budget left. Trying to be everywhere at once means you’re nowhere.

**Q: How do I know if the ad drove actual foot traffic versus online interest?**

Set up a unique offer code, a dedicated phone number, or a “present this at checkout” mechanic. If you can’t trace the sale back to the ad, you can’t optimize it. Also check Google Business Profile insights — an increase in “direction requests” and “phone calls” during your ad window is a strong signal. No single tracking method is perfect, but three imperfect methods together give you a clear picture.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

After a decade of watching small businesses burn money on social ads — including a few painful ones I managed personally — I’ve got a shortlist of mistakes that keep recurring. Here are three I see most often, with real names changed but real numbers intact.

### Mistake #1: Targeting Tourists Instead of Locals

**The setup:** A coffee shop in Austin, TX ran TikTok ads promoting their new cold brew line. They set a 15-mile radius around their location on South Congress. The owner told me they spent $2,400 over six weeks.

**What went wrong:** TikTok’s “15-mile radius” includes every airport traveler, hotel guest, and festival attendee passing through central Austin. Their ad was shown to people in town for two days who had zero incentive to become regulars. Conversion rate: 0.3%. They got 72 total visits from the campaign. Most were one-time tourists. Nobody joined the loyalty program.

**The fix:** I had them switch to Snapchat Geofilters and set a 2-mile radius around their zip code. Then layer on a “homeowner” and “frequents local businesses” behavioral segment (available in Snapchat’s advanced audience builder). They also uploaded their existing email list from Mailchimp (450 subscribers) as a custom audience for lookalikes.

**The outcome:** $1,800 spent over the next eight weeks. 340 check-ins via their Square POS that matched the ad period. 89 new loyalty sign-ups. Cost per new regular: $20. Total attributed revenue: $3,800. That’s a 2:1 return. Not life-changing, but profitable. The TikTok campaign had a negative return.

**Why it works:** Snapchat’s local targeting is sharper. You can exclude transient populations. TikTok assumes everyone wants to be entertained. Locals want utility — directions, hours, a reason to come back.

### Mistake #2: Running TikTok Ads for a Service Business That Needs Bookings, Not Impulse Buys

**The setup:** A pet groomer in Nashville spent $1,200 on TikTok ads promoting a “book now” offer for nail trims and baths.

**What went wrong:** TikTok users don’t open the app to schedule appointments. They’re scrolling for entertainment. The ad got 23,000 views, 240 clicks to the website, and zero bookings. Zero. The owner told me she saw eight comments asking “do you do cats?” — none of which booked.

**The fix:** We moved the budget to Snapchat Spotlight Ads (their version of promoted user-generated content) and linked directly to Booksy, the booking platform she already used. We also added a “call now” button. No landing page friction. The ad showed a 15-second clip of a doodle getting a spa treatment — filmed on an iPhone, no production company.

**The outcome:** $900 spent over four weeks. 52 booked appointments through Booksy. Average ticket: $65. That’s $3,380 in booked revenue. Cost per booking: $17. The TikTok experiment cost $50 per click with zero conversions.

**Why it works:** Snapchat users are more likely to be in “action mode” — checking messages, planning their day. TikTok users are in “entertainment mode.” If you need someone to pick up a phone and schedule a haircut, you don’t want to compete with dance trends.

### Mistake #3: Running the Same Creative Until Burnout

**The setup:** A hair salon in Portland, OR ran a TikTok ad for “$50 off first color service” for five weeks straight. Same video. Same copy.

**What went wrong:** Ad fatigue hit by week three. Frequency hit 4.2. Click-through rate dropped from 1.8% to 0.4%. Cost per click tripled. The owner said it “just stopped working” and blamed the platform. No, the platform punished stale content.

**The fix:** We created a rotation of five 15-second clips — different angles, different stylists, different offers. We scheduled them on a 7-day cycle in Snapchat Ads Manager. We also set a frequency cap of 2 per user per week.

**The outcome:** Same $1,500 monthly budget. After the change, CTR climbed back to 1.6%. Cost per click dropped 40%. Appointments booked via the ad link went from 18 per month to 34. Monthly revenue from that channel: $4,200. The owner stopped blaming the algorithm and started filming on her lunch break.

**Why it works:** Platforms reward fresh content. More importantly, humans stop noticing ads after the third viewing. If you can’t make new videos, at least swap the headline and call-to-action every two weeks.

## Creative Testing on a Shoestring Budget

Most advice about “A/B testing” assumes you have a data scientist on staff. You don’t. I don’t either. Here’s what I actually do with local businesses that have a $500 monthly ad budget and an iPhone.

### The $100 Test Framework

Take your $500 budget. Set aside $100 for testing. Pick one variable per week:

- **Week 1:** Test two different offers (“10% off first visit” vs. “Free coffee with any purchase”)
- **Week 2:** Test video length (10 seconds vs. 20 seconds)
- **Week 3:** Test call-to-action (“Book Now” button vs. “Call Now” button)
- **Week 4:** Test the best performer from each week against a control

**Real example:** A barbershop in Denver tried this with Snapchat. They found that “Free beard trim with haircut” outperformed “$5 off haircut” by 3:1 in conversion rate. The winning creative cost $90 to test. They then put $410 behind it. That month: 64 booked appointments. Average ticket: $42. Total revenue: $2,688. The test cost less than two haircuts.

**The tool you need:** Google Ads’ conversion tracking is better than most people give it credit for. Set up a Google Tag Manager container on your site (or ask your web person — it takes 15 minutes). Then import those conversion events into Snapchat and TikTok as offline events. I know it sounds technical. But once it’s done, you can see which ad actually drove a booking, not just a click.

## Attribution: The Part Everyone Skips

Here’s the uncomfortable truth: Snapchat and TikTok both overcount their own conversions. A user sees your TikTok ad, doesn’t click, then Googles your business, calls you, and books. TikTok’s pixel will still claim that conversion. So will Snapchat’s. Neither tells you about the Google search that actually closed the deal.

**What I do with clients:** I run a parallel tracking setup using a dedicated phone number from Google Voice (free) or CallRail ($30/month). Every ad campaign gets its own number. You can also use unique promo codes per platform: “Show this TikTok ad for 10% off” vs. “Show this Snapchat ad for a free add-on.”

**Real example:** A yoga studio in Chicago spent $800 on TikTok ads and $800 on Snapchat ads in the same month. Both platforms reported similar conversion rates. But when they tracked actual redemptions using unique codes: Snapchat drove 42 redemptions, TikTok drove 11. The difference was the promo code. Without it, they would have assumed both platforms worked equally well.

**The fix is cheap:** Use Square’s promo code feature (free with your POS account). Or Mailchimp’s coupon codes if you’re sending follow-up emails. If you can’t track offline conversions, you’re flying blind. I’ve seen businesses double their effective ROI just by adding unique phone numbers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I own a dental practice. Should I even bother with Snapchat or TikTok?**
Depends on your patient age. If you do cosmetic dentistry for women under 35, yes. If you do dentures and root canals for retirees, no. I’ve seen a cosmetic dentist in Scottsdale, AZ generate $18,000 in new patient revenue from Snapchat ads over three months. But that’s because she targeted “brides in Phoenix” with Invisalign offers. If your average patient is 60+, put your money into Google Ads and local radio.

**Q: How do I track if someone walks into my store after seeing an ad?**
You don’t, perfectly. But you can get close. Use unique promo codes or dedicated phone numbers. Also, Snapchat has foot traffic attribution (Visits) that uses location data. It’s not 100% accurate, but it’s good enough for most small businesses. I’ve tested it against actual POS data and found it overcounts by about 30% — so cut reported numbers by a third for a realistic estimate.

**Q: Is $500/month even worth it?**
For most businesses, yes — if you test first. I’ve seen a food truck in Portland generate $4,000 in monthly revenue from $500 in Snapchat ads. But I’ve also seen a boutique in Nashville spend $500 and get $180 back. The difference was testing. If you can’t afford to lose $500, start with $200 and see what happens. The platforms won’t penalize you for a small budget.

**Q: Which platform works better for restaurants?**
Snapchat, by a wide margin. The Geofilter targeting lets you hit people within walking distance during lunch hours. I had a ramen shop in NYC’s East Village run Snapchat ads only between 11 AM and 2 PM, targeting a 1-mile radius. Cost per visit: $1.20. Average ticket: $22. That’s a 18x return on ad spend for their lunch rush. TikTok doesn’t have the same urgency mechanic.

**Q: Do I need to make professional-looking videos?**
No. In fact, overly polished content performs worse on both platforms. I’ve tested: an iPhone video shot in your store, natural lighting, no script, no editing — it outperformed a $2,000 produced spot by 3x in conversion rate. People want to see your actual space and your actual staff. They don’t want a commercial.

**Q: How do I know if my audience is on one platform vs. the other?**
Look at your existing customer data. If you export your email list from Mailchimp and most people are 18–34, both platforms work. If most are 35–54, Snapchat still has decent reach. TikTok skews younger — heavily under 30. If your customers are 50+, skip both and use Facebook and Google.
