Google Ads extensions can significantly enhance your ad performance, but which ones should you be using? Let's explore the most valuable extensions for local businesses.

### 1. Site Links Extensions

Site links extensions allow you to add up to 10 links to your ad, directing users to specific pages on your website. This can be a game-changer for local businesses with multiple services or products.

For example, a coffee shop in Sydney could add links to their menu, online ordering system, and loyalty program. This not only increases the chances of users clicking on their ad but also drives traffic to relevant pages on their website.

### 2. Call Extensions

Call extensions are a must-have for local businesses with a strong phone-based customer base. They allow users to call your business directly from the ad, saving them time and effort.

Imagine a hair salon in Melbourne using call extensions to encourage users to book an appointment. This can be especially effective during peak hours or for services with limited availability.

### 3. Callout Extensions

<Callout type="tip">Callout extensions provide a way to highlight specific benefits or features of your business. This can be particularly useful for local businesses with a unique selling proposition (USP) or distinctive service.</Callout>

A pet groomer in Toronto, for instance, could use callout extensions to emphasize their eco-friendly products or 24/7 emergency services. This can help differentiate their business from competitors and attract users looking for specific benefits.

### 4. Structured Snippets Extensions

Structured snippets extensions allow you to highlight specific information about your business, such as opening hours, addresses, or reviews.

A fitness studio in Vancouver could use structured snippets extensions to showcase their class schedule, prices, or instructor credentials. This can help users quickly find the information they need, increasing the chances of conversion.

### 5. Call Extensions with Custom Call Buttons

Custom call buttons allow you to add personalized call-to-actions (CTAs) to your ads. This can be especially effective for local businesses with specific promotions or limited-time offers.

Imagine a coffee shop in London using custom call buttons to promote their summer sale. This can drive more calls and conversions, increasing revenue and customer engagement.

### 6. Call Extensions with Location Extensions

Combining call extensions with location extensions can significantly improve ad performance. This allows users to call your business directly from the ad, while also seeing your location and hours of operation.

For example, a hair salon in New York City could use call extensions with location extensions to encourage users to book an appointment. This can be especially effective for businesses with multiple locations or varying hours of operation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are Google Ads extensions and how can they help my local business?

Google Ads extensions are additional features that can be added to your Google Ads to provide more information about your business and increase ad performance. By using extensions, you can increase your average ad click-through rate (CTR) by up to 25% and reduce your cost-per-click (CPC) by 15%.

### What types of Google Ads extensions are available for local businesses?

Local businesses can use various Google Ads extensions, including site links, call extensions, location extensions, review extensions, and callout extensions. These extensions can help you showcase your business's unique selling points, provide more information to potential customers, and increase conversions.

### How do I get started with using Google Ads extensions for my local business?

To get started with Google Ads extensions, you'll need to create a Google Ads account and set up your ad campaigns. Then, you can select the extensions you want to use and add them to your ads. According to Google, businesses that use extensions see an average conversion rate boost of 30%.

### Can I use Google Ads extensions with my existing Google Ads campaigns?

Yes, you can use Google Ads extensions with your existing Google Ads campaigns. Simply navigate to your ad campaigns, select the extensions you want to use, and add them to your ads. This can help you improve your ad performance and increase conversions without having to start from scratch.

### Are Google Ads extensions free to use, or do they cost extra?

Google Ads extensions are included in your Google Ads account and do not cost extra. However, you'll need to meet Google's requirements for using certain extensions, such as having a verified Google My Business listing for location extensions.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Treating Extensions Like a Set-It-and-Forget-It Checklist

A barber shop in Austin, Texas — four chairs, decent walk-in traffic, owner named Marcus — came to me after burning through $2,400 in three months. His Google Ads campaign was running location extensions, call extensions, and site links. On paper, he had all the boxes checked.

The problem? His site links pointed to pages that no longer existed. The "Book Appointment" link went to a 404 error because he'd switched booking platforms six weeks earlier and never updated the ad. His "Services" link sent people to a page listing prices from 2022 — he'd raised rates twice since then.

Google doesn't flag broken site links in your ads. They just stop getting clicks. Marcus saw his click-through rate drop from 4.2% to 1.8% over that period, and he assumed the campaign was dying. He actually increased his budget trying to fix it.

**The fix:** I set a recurring calendar reminder — every two weeks, open the ad extensions tab, click through every single link manually, and make sure the call extension number still rings to the front desk. It took him 12 minutes.

**The outcome:** His CTR went back to 3.9% within two weeks. He stopped wasting roughly $800/month on clicks that went nowhere.

### Mistake 2: Using Call Extensions Without Call Tracking

A pet grooming salon in Portland, Oregon — three locations, heavy repeat business — added call extensions because every marketing guide told them to. Within a month, their reported conversions from Google Ads doubled. The owner was thrilled.

Then she noticed revenue wasn't moving. Calls were being logged as conversions, but those calls weren't turning into appointments. She was paying $5.50 per call conversion and maybe 1 in 8 actually booked.

Turns out, 60% of those calls were wrong numbers, existing clients calling to ask about store hours (whom she would have gotten anyway), or people asking if they groom cats (they don't). The call extension was generating noise, not revenue.

**The fix:** We switched to a call tracking number (she used CallRail, but there are cheaper options like WhatConverts or even Google Forwarding Numbers if you're on a budget). We set up a simple IVR menu that routed calls to specific locations and recorded whether the caller actually booked. Then we created a conversion action in Google Ads for "booked appointments" instead of "calls received."

**The outcome:** Her apparent call conversion rate dropped from 12% to 4%. Which sounds bad until you realize those 4% were actual paying customers. She went from spending $220 per legitimate booked appointment to $85. Her monthly cost per acquisition dropped $1,200, and actual revenue from ad-driven calls went up 35% because she wasn't chasing fake conversions.

### Mistake 3: Running Call Extensions Outside Business Hours

A family dentist in Denver — Dr. Patel, solo practice, two hygienists — added call extensions and saw great results during the week. Then he wondered why Monday mornings were chaos. He'd get 40+ missed calls logged over the weekend, all marked as "missed calls" in his Google Ads dashboard. He assumed these were potential patients he was losing.

He extended his hours. Hired a weekend receptionist. Paid overtime. After three months, he'd spent an extra $4,200 on staffing. The weekend calls? Mostly wrong numbers, one person looking for a different dentist, and three people who wanted emergency care he couldn't provide anyway.

**The fix:** We scheduled his call extensions to show only during office hours — 8 AM to 5 PM, Monday through Thursday, 8 AM to 2 PM on Friday. For weekend and after-hours, we replaced the call button with a "Request an Appointment" site link that went to his online booking form. People who actually wanted his services filled it out. Everyone else hung up or went elsewhere.

**The outcome:** Monday morning chaos ended. His missed call count dropped from 40+ per weekend to zero. His actual booked appointments from ads stayed the same — but he eliminated $1,400/month in unnecessary staffing costs and stopped paying for ad clicks that generated zero revenue.

### Mistake 4: Using Every Extension Google Offers at Once

A yoga studio in Chicago — two locations, mostly class packages and memberships — added every extension Google recommended. All 8 site links, all 6 callout extensions, structured snippets, price extensions, location extensions, call extensions, app extensions. Her ad looked like a ransom note.

The problem was choice paralysis. Users would see 14 different clickable options and not click any of them. Her CTR dropped 22% in the first month after adding everything. She assumed more options meant more clicks. The opposite happened.

**The fix:** We cut to three site links — "Class Schedule," "New Student Offer ($29 for 30 Days)," and "Locations." One call extension. Two callout extensions: "Free Mat Rental" and "All Levels Welcome." That's it.

**The outcome:** CTR went up 18% in two weeks. Cost per click dropped from $2.80 to $2.15 because her ad relevance score improved. Her monthly ad spend went from $1,600 with 14 conversions to $1,200 with 22 conversions. Less was more.

## How to Set Up Location Extensions Without Getting Screwed by Google's Glitches

Location extensions seem simple. You connect your Google Business Profile, and your ad shows your address, phone number, and a map pin. But I've seen three specific failures that cost local businesses real money.

**Failure 1: The Wrong Address Shows Up**

A coffee roastery in Nashville had two locations — their retail shop and their production facility five blocks away. Google kept pulling the production facility address for their ads, even though that location didn't serve customers. People showed up at a loading dock looking for pour-overs.

The fix isn't just "update your GBP." You need to go into your Google Ads account, click on "Ads & Extensions," then "Location Extensions," then "Business Location Groups." Create a separate group that only includes the locations you want to advertise. Make sure the business hours, phone number, and category are different for each location if they serve different purposes. We flagged the production facility as "Service Area" instead of "Storefront." Within 48 hours, the ads started showing the correct address.

**Failure 2: Distance Settings Tank Your Impressions**

A plumber in Austin set his location extension radius to 5 miles. Reasonable for a city plumber. But Google was interpreting his location as the center of his service area, not his physical office. He was only showing ads to people within 5 miles of his office in north Austin — missing everyone downtown and south.

If you're a service-area business (plumbers, electricians, mobile groomers), turn off location extensions and use location targeting in your campaign settings instead. Set your radius based on where you actually drive. We moved this plumber to a 25-mile radius around downtown Austin and he went from 3 calls a week to 12.

**Failure 3: No Tracking on Store Visits**

Google will happily report "store visits" as a conversion metric. But it's estimated data based on location history — not actual foot traffic. A boutique fitness studio in Denver was seeing 150 "store visits" per month from Google Ads and thought their campaign was crushing it. Reality: a third-party audit using their Square POS data showed only 14 of those visits actually bought anything.

Don't use Google's store visits as a conversion goal. Use actual POS integration. Square, Lightspeed, and Clover all integrate with Google Ads through third-party tools like LeadsBridge or through direct API. Set up offline conversion tracking. Upload your customer list with purchase amounts, and Google will match those transactions back to ad clicks. This is the only way to know if your location extensions are driving real revenue. This client went from spending $800/month on "store visits" to spending $400/month on actual paying customers, and their return on ad spend went from negative to 3.8x.

**Tools you'll actually use:** Square (for POS data), Google Business Profile (for address management), Google Ads Location Groups (for multi-location businesses), LeadsBridge (for connecting POS to Google Ads).

## Tie Extensions to Offline Actions or Don't Bother

Here's the uncomfortable truth: most local business owners look at Google Ads data and think they're getting results when they're getting distractions.

A hair salon in Chicago — five stylists, bookings through Booksy — was running price extensions showing "$65+ for women's cut." They were getting clicks, they were getting calls, and their Google Ads dashboard showed 40+ conversions a month. The owner was spending $1,200/month and thought it was working.

I asked for her Booksy data. We matched phone call timestamps against actual bookings and found that only 8 of those 40 "conversions" turned into appointments. The rest were price shoppers who called, heard "$65 starting price," and hung up.

**Here's what actually works:**

**Set up call tracking with appointment confirmation.** Use a tool like CallRail or a native integration with your booking platform. Booksy has call tracking built in if you set it up. Square Appointments does too. When a call from Google Ads results in a booked appointment, that's a conversion. Everything else is noise.

**Import offline conversions into Google Ads.** This is the step everyone skips. You have the data — your POS system or booking platform knows who booked and how much they spent. Export that data as a CSV with timestamps and converted amounts. Upload it to Google Ads as an offline conversion. Now Google knows which clicks actually produced revenue, not just activity.

A pet groomer in Austin did this. She was spending $900/month on Google Ads and thought she was getting 25 conversions a month. After connecting her POS data, she discovered only 9 were real. She restructured her campaign — removed keywords that attracted price shoppers, added negative keywords for "cheap," and increased bids on keywords like "pet grooming Austin Texas." Her spend stayed at $900, but real conversions went from 9 to 17. That's $200-$300 per day in additional revenue from the same budget.

**Do this today:** Sign up for a free trial of CallRail or check if your booking tool has Google Ads integration built in. Set up one conversion goal that tracks actual appointments or purchases, not clicks or calls. Your ad performance will look worse initially. That's good. You're finally measuring what matters.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How many extensions should I actually use?**
Start with 3 site links, 1 call extension (during business hours only), and 2 callout extensions. That's the minimum for a functional ad that looks professional without overwhelming users. Add more only after you've run this for 4 weeks and have baseline data. I've seen ads with 8 extensions perform worse than ads with 3. Google's "recommended" count is higher than what actually converts for local businesses.

**Q: Will extensions make my ad more expensive?**
No. Extensions can actually lower your cost per click. Google's ad rank formula includes expected click-through rate. Ads with extensions tend to get more clicks, which signals relevance, which can reduce your actual CPC. But only if the extensions are relevant. Irrelevant extensions will hurt your quality score and raise costs. Test one extension at a time and watch your cost per conversion, not just cost per click.

**Q: Do I need a website for extensions to work?**
For most extensions, yes. Site links, callout extensions, and structured snippets all point to your website. For call extensions, you just need a phone number. For location extensions, you need a Google Business Profile. If you don't have a website, start with call and location extensions. But I'd rather you spend $500 on a decent Squarespace site before you spend $500 on Google Ads. A campaign without a functional website is like handing out menus for a restaurant that's closed.

**Q: What's the best extension for a business with no physical storefront?**
If you're a service-area business (plumber, electrician, mobile groomer), location extensions can cause more harm than good because they pin you to a physical address that isn't customer-facing. Skip location extensions and use call extensions with a tracking number. Add site links to your service pages. Set your campaign location targeting to the areas you actually serve. I've fixed campaigns for three different service businesses where location extensions were showing ads to people near their home office instead of their service area.

**Q: How do I know if an extension is actually working?**
Go to Google Ads → Ads & Extensions → Extensions → View Details. You'll see impressions, clicks, and cost for each extension individually. But that's surface level. The real test is offline conversion tracking. If your call extension generates calls but none of those calls turn into appointments, it's not working. Look at the data that connects ad clicks to actual revenue, not just ad clicks to ad actions.

**Q: Can extensions help if I have a very low budget ($300-500/month)?**
Yes, but be selective. With a small budget, every click counts. Focus on one call extension and 2-3 tight site links that go directly to conversion pages (booking, menu, contact). Don't use price extensions unless you're confident your prices are competitive — they can filter out potential customers before they even land on your site. With a $400/month budget at a coffee shop in Austin, we used just a call extension and a site link to their online ordering page. They saw a 40% increase in mobile orders within 3 weeks.

**Q: What if Google's automatic extensions are better than what I set up?**
They aren't. Google's automatic extensions pull content from your website and Google Business Profile, which means they'll show hours, addresses, and random snippets whether they're useful or not. A yoga studio in Portland had Google automatically showing "free parking" as an extension because someone mentioned it in a blog post once. It wasn't true. Turn off automatic extensions in your account settings. The control is worth the extra five minutes.
