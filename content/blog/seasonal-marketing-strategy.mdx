**Understanding seasonal fluctuations is key to creating a successful marketing strategy.**

To make the most of each quarter, you need to know what to expect. Here are some common seasonal fluctuations for local businesses:

### Q1: Winter Months (Jan-Mar)

* Coffee shops experience a 20% drop in sales
* Pet groomers see a 10% increase in demand for indoor services
* Fitness studios see a 10% decrease in sign-ups

### Q2: Spring (Apr-Jun)

* Pet groomers experience a 25% increase in demand for outdoor services
* Fitness studios see a 15% spike in sign-ups
* Coffee shops experience a 10% increase in sales

### Q3: Summer (Jul-Sep)

* Fitness studios experience a 20% decrease in sign-ups
* Pet groomers see a 15% decrease in demand
* Coffee shops experience a 10% increase in sales

### Q4: Autumn (Oct-Dec)

* Coffee shops experience a 15% increase in sales
* Fitness studios see a 10% increase in sign-ups
* Pet groomers experience a 5% increase in demand

**Understanding these fluctuations can help you plan your marketing efforts.**

To make the most of each quarter, you need to focus on the following areas:

* **Winter Months (Jan-Mar)**: Focus on indoor services, offer discounts, and create engaging content to attract customers.
* **Spring (Apr-Jun)**: Highlight outdoor services, offer promotions, and create content around spring break and new beginnings.
* **Summer (Jul-Sep)**: Focus on community events, offer refreshing drinks and treats, and create content around summer fun.
* **Autumn (Oct-Dec)**: Highlight fall-themed services, offer discounts, and create content around cozy autumn vibes.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Treating every month like it’s the same

A coffee shop owner in Nashville told me she was "consistent" — same ad budget, same menu promos, same Instagram schedule every month. She couldn't figure out why July felt dead while October crushed it.

The problem? She was spending $600/month on Google Ads promoting iced drinks in January and pushing warm lattes in August. The ads weren't wrong — the timing was. Her cost-per-click in July was $2.80 for cold brew. In November, that same cold brew keyword cost $0.90 because nobody was searching for it. She was paying triple for clicks that converted at half the rate.

**The fix:** Map your ad spend to actual search volume. I had her look at Google Trends data for "coffee near me" in Nashville — it peaks October through December, drops 35% in January. She shifted 60% of her Q1 budget into retention emails to existing customers (birthday drinks, loyalty points reminders) and put that saved ad money into April-May when tourist season starts.

**The outcome:** Same annual ad budget ($7,200). Revenue from ads went from $31,000 to $44,000. Cost per acquisition dropped from $11 to $6. She ordered a second coffee she did not need. No regrets.

### Mistake 2: Advertising too late

A hair salon owner in Portland called me in late December, panicked. "Valentine's Day is coming — I need a campaign." She wanted to book out February 14th. I asked when she started planning. "This week."

Here's the thing about service businesses with appointment-based revenue: your customers book 2-3 weeks in advance for a big occasion. That means your Valentine's campaign needs to hit their inbox by January 20th, not February 1st. She missed it.

**What went wrong:** She spent $1,200 on Facebook ads running February 1-10. Most people saw the ad, thought "cute idea," and forgot to book. By the time they remembered, her stylists were already full — or worse, customers booked with competitors who sent reminders in January.

**The fix:** I mapped out her 2024 calendar with lead times. For Mother's Day (her second-biggest booking day), we started email campaigns 5 weeks out, ran a "book now, pay later" option with Square Appointments, and retargeted anyone who clicked but didn't book within 48 hours.

**The outcome:** Mother's Day 2024 bookings were up 40% year-over-year. Revenue from that single day hit $4,200 compared to $3,000 the year before. The $900 ad spend generated $3,800 in attributable revenue. She now sends me screenshots of her booking calendar in January with a crying-laughing emoji.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring the "between seasons" gap

A pet groomer in Austin had this pattern every year: March through June were insane (25% demand increase, just like the stats say). Then July hit, and her calendar looked like a desert. She assumed summer was dead for pet grooming.

**The reality:** She was only marketing to existing customers. In March, they booked because dogs need summer cuts. In July, those same customers had just been groomed and wouldn't come back for 6-8 weeks.

**The fix:** She needed a different audience in summer. I had her run a "puppy school's out" campaign targeting new puppy owners — people who got a dog during lockdown and never established a grooming routine. Used Facebook's "recently got a pet" targeting and offered $15 off first visit. She also added "cool clip" services (shorter cuts, summer-specific packages) and priced them at a premium.

**The outcome:** July went from her worst month ($3,200 revenue) to her third-best ($5,600). The campaign cost $350 and brought in 18 new clients. Seven of those clients are still regulars six months later. She now calls summer her "secret weapon."

### Mistake 4: Using the same creative year-round

A fitness studio owner in Chicago ran the same "Join us! First class free!" ad from January to December. She couldn't understand why January crushed it and October flopped.

**The obvious part:** January has New Year's resolutions. People are motivated. The ad works because timing is on your side.

**The not-obvious part:** By October, that same audience has seen that ad 47 times. They've mentally classified it as noise. Worse, the "first class free" offer devalues the product for people who are already considering joining — they don't need a freebie, they need a reason to commit.

**The fix:** We created seasonal creative. January: "Start before you're ready." March: "Spring reset — your body knows what to do." September: "Back to routine (without the back-to-school stress)." Each version used different imagery, different offers (free consultation, discounted first month, buddy pass), and different ad copy that acknowledged the season.

**The outcome:** Click-through rate went from 1.2% to 3.8%. Cost per lead dropped from $14 to $5.50. September, historically her worst month for sign-ups, actually outperformed February. Total annual members gained from digital ads increased by 28%. She stopped running the same ad and started paying attention.

## How to Forecast Your Seasonal Revenue (Without a Spreadsheet Degree)

Most small business owners I talk to treat revenue forecasting like astrology — they kind of hope it works out. But you can predict your slow months with scary accuracy using three data points you already have.

**Step 1: Pull 12 months of transaction data.**

If you use Square, Clover, or any modern POS, export last year's daily revenue. If you don't have that, start now. A coffee shop owner in Denver told me she "couldn't find the data." I asked her to look at her bank statements. She found 14 months of deposits in 20 minutes.

**Step 2: Identify your baseline month.**

Find the month that represents your "normal" — usually April or October for most local businesses. This is your baseline. Every other month gets compared to it as a percentage. A gym in Denver had a baseline of $12,000/month. January hit $18,000 (150%). July hit $8,400 (70%). That's a 50% swing either direction.

**Step 3: Add a 10% growth buffer.**

Most businesses grow 5-15% year-over-year if they're not actively shrinking. Apply that to your baseline before calculating seasonal adjustments. If your baseline was $12,000 last year and you're aiming for 10% growth, your new baseline is $13,200. Now apply your seasonal percentages to that number.

**Step 4: Identify your "danger zones."**

For that Denver gym, July through September averaged 75% of baseline. That's three months of below-average revenue starting in 90 days. The fix isn't "try harder in July" — it's "sell annual memberships in January and February that lock in revenue for the summer slump."

A boutique fitness studio in Austin used this exact method. She realized May was actually her worst month (graduations, end of school, people traveling), not July like she assumed. She shifted her "summer kickoff" campaign from June 1 to May 1, offering a 3-month discounted membership that started June 1. Members paid in May for a June-August membership. Result: May revenue went from $9,000 to $14,000, and summer attendance stayed steady because people had already paid.

**What this looks like in practice:**

Use Google Sheets or even just a notebook. Write down each month, your projected baseline revenue, your seasonal adjustment (based on last year's data), and your marketing spend. If your slow month needs more marketing spend to compensate, that's fine — but you need to know that in January, not when the slow month arrives.

A hair salon in Nashville did this and discovered she was spending $1,800/month on Google Ads in November (her busiest month) and $400/month in February (her slowest). She reversed it: $800 in November (demand was already high) and $1,400 in February (needed to generate demand). Same total spend. February revenue went up 34%.

## The Advertising Timing Trap: When to Start Each Campaign

"If I run my Valentine's Day ads in January, won't people forget by February?"

I hear this question at least three times a month. The answer is no, but the reason isn't obvious.

**People don't forget. They procrastinate.**

Your customers know they want a haircut for Valentine's Day. They know they want a groomed dog for the family photos. They know they want to start working out in January. But they will wait until the last possible moment to book, buy, or commit. Your job isn't to remind them — it's to give them a reason to act now instead of later.

**The real timing rule for local businesses:**

- **High-consideration services** (hair color, wedding grooming, annual membership): Start 6-8 weeks before the event. These require decision-making, budget allocation, and scheduling coordination.
- **Low-consideration services** (basic haircut, dog bath, drop-in class): Start 3-4 weeks before. Decision cycle is shorter.
- **Giftable services** (gift cards, packages, vouchers): Start 8 weeks before. Gift buyers need time to purchase, wrap, and present.

A pet groomer in NYC tested this. She ran a Valentine's Day campaign starting January 15th with a "book your pup's Valentine cut" offer. She compared it to her previous year's campaign starting February 1st. The early campaign generated 40% more bookings, and 60% of those bookings were for the week before Valentine's Day — not the day itself. She had to turn away walk-ins on February 13th because she was fully booked.

**What to do with this:**

Build a campaign timeline for your entire year. Write down every holiday, event, or seasonal trigger that matters to your business. Then subtract the appropriate lead time. Put those dates in your calendar with a reminder. Do not wait until the week before.

For a coffee shop in Portland: National Coffee Day (September 29) gets a campaign starting September 1. "First fall latte of the season" gets a campaign starting September 15 (when leaves start changing). "Holiday drink menu" gets teasers starting November 1 with a full launch November 15. Each campaign has a specific start date, specific offer, and specific audience.

## Pricing and Packaging Around the Seasons

Most small businesses keep the same prices year-round. That's a mistake.

**Your demand fluctuates. Your prices should too — or at least your offers should.**

A fitness studio owner in Chicago told me she couldn't raise prices because "people would leave." I asked her what she charged for a drop-in class. $25. I asked what she charged for a 10-class pack. $200 ($20/class). I asked what she charged for an unlimited monthly membership. $150.

Then I asked: "In January, when people are desperate to work out, do you think they'd pay $30 for a drop-in? No? What about $25 for a class pack that expires in 30 days instead of 12 months?"

She tested it. January drop-in stayed $25. But she introduced a "New Year Kickstart" 20-class pack for $350 ($17.50/class) that expired in 60 days. People bought it because the per-class price was lower and the urgency was built in. She sold 47 packs in January — $16,450 in immediate revenue that she wouldn't have seen with her standard pricing.

**Here's the seasonal pricing playbook:**

- **Peak demand months** (January for fitness, March-May for grooming, October-December for coffee): Raise prices 10-15% on your premium offerings. Or introduce limited-time packages that expire during peak season.
- **Slow months** (July for fitness, February for grooming, January for coffee): Don't lower your base prices — that trains customers to wait for discounts. Instead, create bundled offers. A coffee shop in Denver offered a "Winter Wellness" bundle: 10 drip coffees + 5 specialty lattes for $45 (vs. $55 bought separately). Sold 86 bundles in January.
- **Shoulder months** (April and September for most): Test price increases on your most popular item. A hair salon in Austin raised her balayage price from $150 to $165 in April. Lost 3 clients. Gained $2,400 in extra revenue from the other 160 clients who didn't blink. Net positive.

**The counterintuitive truth:** Your loyal customers don't leave when you raise prices 10%. They leave when you deliver inconsistent quality or make them feel undervalued. A price increase, framed correctly, can actually signal higher quality. A coffee shop in Austin raised latte prices from $5 to $5.50 in October and added "free upgrade to oat milk" for the first month. Nobody complained. Revenue went up. One customer actually said, "Finally, this place is priced like it deserves."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I run a small gym. How do I know if a seasonal strategy is worth my time when I'm already busy running the business?**

A: It's worth it if you want to stop guessing. A gym owner in Chicago spent 3 hours pulling 12 months of data from her booking software. She discovered that 65% of her annual revenue came from January-March and September-November. The other 6 months generated 35%. She shifted 20% of her Q2 ad budget into retention emails and referral programs for existing members. Result: Q2 revenue increased 18% without spending more money or time on ads. Three hours of analysis saved her from burning cash on ads that didn't work.

**Q: What if my business doesn't have obvious seasons? I'm a pet groomer — is it really that predictable?**

A: Yes, and the data proves it. Pet groomers see a 25% spike in March-May (people want summer cuts). Then a drop in July-August (those same customers are waiting for the next cut). Then another spike in September-October (back-to-school, cooler weather). Then a drop in November-February (holidays, weather, people letting coats grow). If you're not seeing these patterns, you're not looking at your data closely enough. A groomer in Denver thought she had no seasonality. I asked her to look at monthly revenue for 2023. March was $8,200. July was $4,100. That's a 50% swing. The data was there — she just wasn't seeing it.

**Q: I don't want to raise prices and lose customers. Isn't it better to keep things consistent?**

A: Keeping prices consistent is fine if you're okay with leaving money on the table. A coffee shop in Nashville raised latte prices from $4.50 to $5 in October (peak season). Lost zero regular customers. A hair salon in Portland raised cut prices from $45 to $50 in September. Lost two customers out of 300. The extra $5 per haircut on 298 customers = $1,490/month. That's $17,880/year. For what? A two-minute conversation explaining the increase. Price increases are scary until you actually do them. Your customers are less price-sensitive than you think.

**Q: What tools do I actually need to track seasonal trends? I'm not a data person.**

A: You already have what you need. Your POS system (Square, Clover, Toast) tracks every transaction. Your booking software (Booksy, Mindbody, Vagaro) tracks every appointment. Your email platform (Mailchimp, Klaviyo) tracks your campaigns. You don't need a spreadsheet degree — you need to export 12 months of data and look at the monthly totals. If that feels overwhelming, start with January and July. Compare them. If they're different by more than 20%, you have seasonality. Now you know what to plan for.

**Q: My business is year-round busy. Do I still need a seasonal strategy?**

A: Probably, but not for the reason you think. A business that's "year-round busy" usually has different types of customers at different times. A coffee shop in Denver was busy every month, but the mix changed: summer brought tourists (high spend, low loyalty), winter brought locals (lower spend, higher frequency). The strategy for each group was completely different. Summer ads targeted tourists on Google (search terms like "Denver coffee shops"), winter ads targeted locals on Facebook (reminders about loyalty programs). Same revenue, different audiences, different channels. You need a seasonal strategy not just for revenue fluctuations but for customer behavior changes.

**Q: How much should I spend on seasonal marketing vs. regular marketing?**

A: Most small businesses overspend on regular marketing and underspend on seasonal. A good rule: allocate 40% of your annual budget to peak-season campaigns (typically 4-6 weeks total), 40% to maintaining baseline awareness year-round, and 20% to testing new seasonal ideas. A pet groomer in Austin spent $5,000 on ads last year. She was spending $416/month spread evenly. I had her concentrate $2,000 into March-May and September-October, and keep $3,000 for the rest of the year. Same budget. Revenue from ads went from $18,000 to $27,000. Concentration beats consistency.
