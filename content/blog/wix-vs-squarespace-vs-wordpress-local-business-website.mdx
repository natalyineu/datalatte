### Choosing the Right Website Builder for Your Local Business

When selecting a website builder, consider the following factors:

* Ease of use: How user-friendly is the platform?
* Cost: What are the costs associated with building and maintaining a website?
* Customization: Can you personalize your website to fit your brand's unique needs?
* Integration: Can you easily integrate third-party tools and services?

### Wix: The All-in-One Solution

Wix is an all-in-one website builder that offers a range of templates and drag-and-drop tools to create a beautiful website. With Wix, you can:

* Choose from over 500 templates and customize them to fit your brand's style
* Use drag-and-drop tools to create a website without coding knowledge
* Integrate third-party tools and services, such as payment gateways and email marketing software
* Host your website on Wix's servers, eliminating the need for external hosting

However, Wix has its limitations. The platform can be slow, and the customization options are limited compared to other website builders.

### Squarespace: The Stylish and Modern Option

Squarespace is a stylish and modern website builder that offers a range of templates and design tools. With Squarespace, you can:

* Choose from over 100 templates and customize them to fit your brand's style
* Use design tools to create a website that's visually appealing and modern
* Integrate third-party tools and services, such as payment gateways and email marketing software
* Host your website on Squarespace's servers, eliminating the need for external hosting

However, Squarespace can be expensive, especially for large or complex websites.

### WordPress: The Flexible and Customizable Option

WordPress is a flexible and customizable website builder that offers a range of templates and plugins. With WordPress, you can:

* Choose from over 10,000 free templates and customize them to fit your brand's style
* Use plugins to add custom functionality and features to your website
* Integrate third-party tools and services, such as payment gateways and email marketing software
* Host your website on a third-party server, giving you more control over your website's performance

However, WordPress requires more technical knowledge and can be time-consuming to set up and maintain.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

After a decade watching small business websites launch (and fail), I can tell you the pattern is almost boring in its consistency. Business owners make the same three or four decisions, usually because some "top 10 website builders" article told them to. So let me save you the wasted budget and the 2 AM frustration.

### Mistake 1: Picking the Easiest Option Then Ignoring Local SEO

A bakery owner in Austin, Texas came to me after six months of paying $42/month for Wix. Her site looked beautiful—pastel colors, nice photos of croissants, a contact form. She was frustrated because she was getting zero website traffic. Zero. Her Google Analytics looked like a flatline.

The problem wasn't Wix. The problem was *how* she built it. She chose a template, threw up some text, and never touched it again. She didn't claim her Google Business Profile. She didn't put her address on the site. She didn't write a single page that answered "best chocolate croissant near downtown Austin." Wix can rank just fine—I've seen it happen—but only if you treat SEO like a requirement, not an afterthought.

**The fix:** Before you write a single line of content, claim your Google Business Profile. Use the exact same business name, address, and phone number on your site that you use everywhere else. Put your city and service area in your page titles. Write a page called "Best [your service] in [your neighborhood]." I had this bakery owner rewrite her homepage header from "Sweet Treats Bakery" to "Fresh Croissants & Custom Cakes in South Austin, TX." She added her address to the footer and wrote a page about wedding cakes in Austin.

**The outcome:** Within 8 weeks, her organic traffic went from 47 visitors total to roughly 340 visitors per month. She booked $1,800 in wedding cake orders directly through the site that quarter. The fix cost her zero dollars—just a Saturday afternoon rewriting text.

### Mistake 2: Building on WordPress Without a Maintenance Plan

A hair salon owner in Nashville decided WordPress was "the professional choice." She hired a freelance developer from a job board for $2,500 to build the site. Six months later, the developer had disappeared, her contact form was broken because a plugin had auto-updated and crashed, and her site was showing a PHP error on mobile. She had no idea how to fix any of it.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: WordPress is not a set-it-and-forget-it platform. It's a publishing system that requires updates, backups, and someone who understands what a MySQL database is. If you don't have $50–100/month to pay someone to maintain it, or the willingness to learn how to do it yourself, WordPress will bite you. I've seen it happen at three different clients across different industries. The salon owner lost approximately $1,200 in missed appointment bookings over three months because her contact form wasn't working and she didn't know.

**The fix:** If you want WordPress, budget for maintenance. That means automated backups (UpdraftPlus is fine), a security plugin, and either a managed WordPress host like WP Engine that handles updates, or a local freelancer you can call when things break. Alternatively—and this is what I told this salon owner—use Squarespace. It costs more per month but the maintenance cost is built in. She switched, took one afternoon to recreate her site, and hasn't had a single technical issue in two years.

**The outcome:** After switching to Squarespace, her site stayed online consistently. She integrated Acuity Scheduling for bookings. Her booking rate stabilized at roughly 22 appointments per week from the website. That's about $3,800/month in revenue from site-generated bookings. The WordPress downtime had been costing her without her even knowing it.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Mobile Performance Because "It Looks Fine"

A pet grooming business in Portland, Oregon had a Squarespace site that their owner thought looked professional on desktop. And it did. But on mobile—where roughly 68% of her traffic was coming from—the images were slow to load, the "Book Now" button was hidden behind a hamburger menu, and the phone number wasn't tappable.

She was running Google Ads to this site, spending $500/month. Her click-through rate was decent. Her conversion rate was 1.2%. That's roughly six leads per month from $500 in ad spend. That's $83 per lead. For dog grooming.

**The fix:** I had her simplify the mobile layout. Move the "Book Now" button to the top of the page—visible immediately, no scrolling. Make the phone number a clickable link. Compress her images (TinyPNG is free and does this in 30 seconds). Remove two slow-loading animations that looked nice but delayed the page load by three seconds.

**The outcome:** Her mobile page load time dropped from 4.7 seconds to 1.9 seconds. Her conversion rate went from 1.2% to 4.8% on mobile. With the same $500/month ad spend, she went from roughly 6 leads to 24 leads per month. That's $20 per lead instead of $83. She booked an additional $2,100 in grooming appointments that month directly from the improved site. The fix took me 45 minutes. Her cost was zero.

### Mistake 4: Using the Free Domain From Your Website Builder

A yoga studio owner in Denver signed up for Wix on a promotional deal. She got a free domain for the first year. She was thrilled. A year later, she got an email that her domain renewal was $45/year. She thought that was expensive. She didn't renew. She let the domain expire. Then someone else bought it and put up a generic landing page with unrelated ads.

For three weeks, if someone typed her studio name into a browser, they got a page that looked nothing like her business. She lost an estimated $1,600 in class pass sales during that time—people who searched for her, found the squatted domain, and assumed she was out of business.

**The fix:** Always buy your domain name separately from your website builder. Use a dedicated registrar like Namecheap or Google Domains. Set it to auto-renew. It costs roughly $12/year. Then point it at whatever platform you choose. If you ever want to switch from Squarespace to WordPress, you keep your domain. It's yours.

**The outcome:** She bought her domain back from the squatter for $200. Set up auto-renewal. Total annual cost going forward: $12/year. She hasn't lost a domain since. That $12 saves her from ever repeating a $1,600 loss.

## When to Pick Wix (And When to Run)

Wix gets a bad reputation among web developers. I get it. The code is messy. Templates can feel generic. But for a coffee shop or a pet groomer who needs a functioning website in an afternoon and doesn't care about custom PHP? Wix is fine. Here's where it works:

**You should use Wix if:** You want to build the site yourself in under 4 hours. You don't need a blog with complex categories. You want integrated booking or appointment tools. You're willing to follow basic SEO instructions (write good page titles, put your address on the site, add alt text to images). Your budget is under $25/month.

**You should NOT use Wix if:** You plan to eventually sell products online with a serious inventory. You need a multilingual site. You want to run A/B tests on landing pages. You have a developer who will need to access the backend code. Your site will have more than 50 pages.

I worked with a food truck owner in Chicago who used Wix for his catering site. He had four pages—home, menu, booking inquiry form, about us. He set it up in an afternoon. He integrated a Square payment link for deposits. His monthly cost was $23. His site generated roughly $600/month in catering inquiries. That's a completely sensible use of Wix. No one needs a headless CMS for a food truck.

## What About SEO for Each Platform?

Since I mentioned local SEO earlier, let me be specific about how these platforms actually perform in search results. Because most comparisons tell you "WordPress is best for SEO" without explaining why.

**WordPress** wins because of plugins like Yoast or RankMath, and because you have full control over page speed, schema markup, and URL structure. You can install a caching plugin, compress images, and write custom meta descriptions for every page. If SEO is your primary concern, WordPress gives you the most tools. But the tools only work if you use them. I've seen WordPress sites with terrible SEO because the owner didn't install any plugins and wrote page titles like "Home" and "Services."

**Squarespace** has good built-in SEO. Clean code, automatic sitemaps, editable meta descriptions, proper heading structure. You can't customize as deeply, but for a local business targeting "coffee shop in downtown Nashville," you don't need deep customization. You need consistent NAP (name, address, phone) across the web, good content, and a fast site. Squarespace handles the technical parts well enough.

**Wix** has improved significantly. They now have decent SEO tools, automatic sitemaps, and structured data options. They still have some issues with page speed on complex sites, and you can't control your URL structure as much. But for a simple service business with 5–10 pages, Wix can rank. I've seen it happen. The bakery in Austin from mistake #1 eventually ranked on page one for "custom cakes Austin TX" after fixing her content and local listings. She was on Wix the whole time.

The honest take: For a local business, the platform matters less than whether you actually do the SEO work. I'd rather have a Wix site with good local content than a WordPress site with a default template and no meta descriptions.

## Integrating With Your Real Business Tools

Here's something most guides skip: your website doesn't exist in a vacuum. It needs to talk to the tools you actually run your business with.

If you're a **coffee shop**, you probably use Square for payments. You want a website that can embed a Square menu, accept gift card purchases, or link to a Square Online store for merchandise. Squarespace has a native Square integration. Wix does too, through the Square app marketplace. WordPress needs a plugin. None of this is hard, but some require a paid plugin subscription.

If you're a **hair salon or barber**, you're likely using Booksy, Vagaro, or something similar. You want your website's "Book Now" button to open your booking system directly. All three platforms can do this with a simple link. The mistake I see is salon owners embedding a full calendar widget that slows down their page load. Just use a button. It works better.

If you're a **fitness studio**, you probably use Mindbody or Pike13. Same logic. Link directly to your booking page. Don't try to embed the full scheduling interface unless you're paying for premium hosting that can handle the load.

If you're a **pet groomer** or **service business**, you might use a simple Google Form for inquiries or Mailchimp for email capture. Make sure your website has a clear signup form or a contact form that integrates with your CRM. Squarespace has built-in form functionality. Wix does too. WordPress needs a plugin like Gravity Forms or Contact Form 7. Gravity Forms costs $59/year. Contact Form 7 is free but can be a pain to style.

The point: Before you choose a platform, write down the three tools you use daily. Then check whether the platform integrates natively. If you need to connect four different services and three of them require paid plugins, that adds up. A client in Denver realized her WordPress site needed $240/year in plugin subscriptions to match what Squarespace did out of the box. She switched.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I just use a Facebook page instead of a website?**

You can, but you shouldn't. Facebook controls what your customers see, when they see it, and whether they see it at all. Organic reach on business pages is roughly 2–5% of your followers. That means if you have 1,000 followers, about 20–50 people see your posts. A website is something you own. You control the content, the design, and the user experience. Also, Google cannot index your Facebook posts properly for local search. A website is the foundation. A Facebook page is an addition.

**Q: Which platform is cheapest in the long run?**

Wix starts around $16/month for the Combo plan. Squarespace starts at $16/month for the Personal plan, but you'll probably need the Business plan at $23/month if you want integrated scheduling or ecommerce. WordPress itself is free, but you pay for hosting ($10–30/month), a domain ($12/year), and potentially plugins ($0–200/year). If you factor in your own time for maintenance and troubleshooting, WordPress often ends up costing more. For most local business owners, Squarespace at $23/month is the simplest and most predictable cost.

**Q: I already built my site on Wix. Should I switch?**

Not necessarily. If your current site is generating leads, ranking in search, and your customers can find you, a redesign costs time and money without guaranteed improvement. I only recommend switching if: your site is slow, you can't make changes yourself, you're hitting technical limitations, or you're not getting traffic despite doing the right things. One of my clients was on Wix and perfectly happy. She only switched to WordPress because she wanted to start a blog with custom categories and complex membership content. Most coffee shops don't need that.

**Q: Can I take payments and bookings directly on the site?**

Yes, on all three. Wix has Wix Bookings and Wix Payments. Squarespace has Acuity Scheduling and Squarespace Payments. WordPress has WooCommerce for products and countless booking plugins. The question is not whether you can, but how much you'll pay in transaction fees. Squarespace charges 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction on the Business plan. Wix charges 2.9% + $0.30 on their Business plans. WooCommerce with Stripe is 2.9% + $0.30. They're all similar. Don't overthink this.

**Q: What happens if I want to switch platforms later?**

Your content needs to be manually moved or exported. Squarespace and Wix both allow you to export your content as an XML file, but design doesn't transfer. You'll rebuild the look from scratch on the new platform. Your domain stays with you if you bought it separately. Your URLs will change unless you set up redirects. It's doable—I've migrated clients from Wix to WordPress and Squarespace to WordPress—but it takes a full weekend. This is another reason to buy your domain separately and keep your content organized from day one.

**Q: Do I need a developer for any of these?**

For Wix and Squarespace, no. For WordPress, probably yes the first time, and occasionally after that. I've taught non-technical business owners to build simple WordPress sites using page builders like Elementor, but there's a learning curve. The first time you see a white screen of death because a plugin conflicted, you'll understand why some people pay for managed WordPress hosting or ongoing support.
