## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Everyone Within 50 Miles

**The story:** A coffee shop in Austin, TX called Brew & Battery came to me after burning through $1,200 in six weeks. The owner, Sarah, had set her ad radius to 50 miles. She thought bigger radius meant more customers. What she got was a lot of clicks from people in San Antonio who were not driving 90 minutes for a latte.

**The fix:** We shrunk the radius to 2 miles. Then we excluded anyone who lived outside a 10-minute drive time. We also layered in interest targeting for "coffee enthusiast" and "remote worker" — because her shop had good WiFi and was popular with laptop crowd. We switched the ad creative to show her patio seating and a photo of her pour-over setup.

**The outcome:** Cost per visit dropped from $8.50 to $1.20. She pulled in 47 tracked visits in the first two weeks after the change. Monthly ad spend stayed at $600. She made back $3,800 in revenue from those customers in month one.

If you're advertising a local business, your radius should match how far someone is willing to travel for what you sell. A hair salon? 5-8 miles max. A pet groomer? 10 miles if they're specialized. A coffee shop? 2 miles. Anything beyond that is burning money.

### Mistake #2: Using a Generic Photo of a Happy Customer

**The story:** A pet groomer in Nashville called Paws & Claws was running ads with a stock photo of a golden retriever. Cute dog. Zero conversions. She spent $900 over three weeks and got two messages that both ended with "how much do you charge?" — no bookings.

**The fix:** I told her to take 15 minutes and shoot three photos on her iPhone. One of her actual workspace (clean, organized, bright). One of a dog she'd just groomed (before and after collage in the caption). One of herself holding a brush and smiling at the camera. No filter, just natural light. We used those in a new ad set with a local offer: "$10 off your first groom when you mention this ad."

**The outcome:** The new ads generated 23 booked appointments in two weeks. Total ad spend: $450. Average ticket per groom: $55. That's $1,265 in revenue from a $450 spend. The before-and-after photo alone accounted for 14 of those bookings.

Local customers want to see the actual place and the actual person doing the work. Stock photos signal "this is a template business." Real photos signal "I exist here and I'm ready for you."

### Mistake #3: Setting It and Forgetting It

**The story:** A fitness studio owner in Denver called Core Collective set up a Facebook ad campaign for her new member offer. She checked it once after launch, saw it was spending $200/day, and figured she'd wait a week to review results. On day five, the campaign had spent $1,000. The ad had been shown to someone in Miami, someone in London, and a lot of people who clicked "interested" but never came in. The tracking pixel wasn't set up properly, so Facebook optimized for link clicks instead of actual leads.

**The fix:** I showed her how to check the ad manager daily for the first week. We installed the Facebook pixel correctly (she'd skipped a step during setup). We added a lead form directly on Facebook so people could book without leaving the app. We set a daily budget cap of $30 so she couldn't accidentally blow past her comfort zone. And we scheduled a 15-minute check-in every morning for the first two weeks.

**The outcome:** The $30/day budget generated 8-12 leads daily from local women aged 25-45 in Denver. She converted 22% of those into trial memberships ($49 each). After three months, she had 31 paying members from that campaign alone. Total ad spend: $2,700. Revenue from those members: $15,190.

You cannot set up a Facebook ad campaign and walk away. The algorithm needs hand-holding in the beginning. Small budgets require more attention, not less. Check it daily. Tweak the audience. Kill the ad that isn't working. Double down on the one that is.

## Tools That Make Local Ad Management Profitable

Managing Facebook ads for local businesses is more than just clicking buttons in Ads Manager. You need a stack of tools to make the math work at small budgets. Here's what I use and what I charge clients for.

**Square (for point-of-sale and customer tracking)**
If your client uses Square for payments, you can track exactly how many customers came from ads by using Square's marketing integrations. I helped a bakery in Brooklyn set this up. We tagged every "new customer" transaction with a note: "FB ad — special offer." Within 30 days, we had 84 tagged transactions totaling $2,380. The owner could see the ROI in her own system. That's better than any dashboard I could build.

**Mailchimp (for email follow-up)**
Local businesses forget that 70% of people who click an ad won't buy on that first visit. Email is how you stay in front of them. I set up a simple automation for a dog daycare in Chicago: someone clicks the ad → they land on a page with a free "first day trial" offer → they enter their email → they get a reminder email 24 hours later if they didn't book → a second email on day 3 with a testimonial → a final email on day 7 with a "last chance" for the trial. Cost to set up: zero (Mailchimp free tier). The daycare got 19 new sign-ups in month one.

**Booksy (for service-based businesses)**
Hair salons, barbers, nail techs — they all use Booksy for booking. Their Facebook pixel integration is decent. But the real trick is using Booksy's "book now" button as the call-to-action in the ad instead of sending people to a website. I tested this for a barbershop in Nashville. Ads with the Booksy booking link converted at 4.2%. Ads sending people to a general website converted at 1.1%. The difference was 14 booked appointments vs. 3 over a two-week period.

**Canva (for ad creative)**
I do not pay a designer for local business ad creative. I use Canva templates. I spent 20 minutes making a template for a nail salon in Austin. It was a square photo with a salmon-colored border, the salon logo in the top corner, and "New Client Special: $30 Gel Manicure" in large text. That single template ran for four months with different photos swapped in. Cost per conversion: $4.80. I charge the client $150/month for "creative management." It takes me 30 minutes total.

**Google Local Services Ads (not Facebook, but necessary)**
For plumbers, electricians, HVAC, and home services, Facebook ads work okay, but Google Local Services Ads work better. I've tested both. For a plumber in Denver, Facebook generated one lead every three days at $12/lead. Google Local Services generated three leads per day at $8/lead. The difference: Local Services ads show up when someone is actively searching for what you do. Facebook interrupts their scrolling. Both have a place, but don't ignore Google if the business is urgent-need (broken pipe, dead AC).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can't I just boost a post from my business page instead of running actual ads?**

Sure, you can. You'll also pay more per result and have less control over who sees the ad. Boosting a post is the equivalent of asking Facebook "show this to anyone who might vaguely like it." Actual ads let you target by zip code, exclude people who already visited your website, and set a cost-per-lead goal. I've run side-by-side tests. A boosted post cost $1.50 per click. A properly targeted ad cost $0.40 per click. The difference over a month at $500 spend: roughly 333 clicks from boosting vs. 1,250 clicks from ads. Do the math.

**Q: How do I know you're not just wasting my money on people who will never buy?**

You look at two metrics: cost per lead and cost per sale. If you're spending $50 and getting zero inquiries, something is wrong. If you're spending $50 and getting five inquiries but zero sales, the problem is your offer or your follow-up, not the ad. I track every lead back to its source using a call tracking number or a unique landing page. If an ad campaign isn't producing sales after one week, I kill it. I'd rather lose $100 on a bad test than $500 on a bad month.

**Q: Why should I use Facebook instead of Google Ads?**

You should use both, but start with Facebook if your business has a visual component or a specific local audience. A coffee shop with a nice interior, a pet groomer with cute before-and-after photos, a fitness studio with class videos — those perform well on Facebook. Google Ads is better for "I need a plumber right now" searches. For most local businesses, I recommend a 60/40 split: 60% Facebook for awareness and consideration, 40% Google for capture. But if you have a $500/month budget, put it all on Facebook until you see traction.

**Q: How long until I see results?**

If you set up correctly, you'll see data within 48 hours. But "results" meaning actual paying customers? Usually 5-10 days if the offer is good and your business is open and ready. If you're a hair salon running a "20% off your first cut" ad, someone will likely book within the first week. If you're a dentist running a "free consultation" ad, it might take two weeks because people think about dental work longer. I tell clients: give me two weeks to validate, then we decide whether to scale or change direction.

**Q: What happens if my budget is only $300/month?**

Then we spend $10/day and we get very disciplined. No broad targeting. One clear offer. One ad set. We check it every morning. At $10/day, you cannot afford to waste a single dollar. I've run successful campaigns for a laundromat in Chicago at $300/month. They offered free dry cleaning on your first visit. We spent $300, got 22 new customers, and the owner said they made $1,100 from those customers within 30 days. Small budgets work if you're surgical.

**Q: Do I need a website for this to work?**

It helps, but not always. You can run ads that send people directly to your Facebook page, your Google Maps listing, or a booking link like Booksy. I ran a campaign for a mobile dog groomer in Denver who had zero website. We used his Google Maps listing as the destination. People clicked "Get Directions" and called him. Cost per call: $3.20. He booked 8 appointments in week one. A one-page website with a booking form would have been better, but you can start without one.
