**Getting Started with Spotify Ads**

To get started with Spotify Ads, you'll need to create a Spotify Ad account. This is a straightforward process that requires basic information about your business, including your name, location, and social media handles. Once you've set up your account, you can begin creating your ad campaigns.

Here are a few key decisions to make when creating your Spotify Ads campaign:
* **Target Audience**: Who are you trying to reach with your ads? You can target listeners based on demographics, interests, and behaviors.
* **Budget**: How much are you willing to spend on your ad campaign? You can set a daily or total budget, and Spotify will help you optimize your ad spend to reach your target audience.
* **Ad Creative**: What will your ads look like? You can upload images, videos, or audio files to create eye-catching and engaging ads.

**Spotify Ads Formats**

Spotify offers a variety of ad formats to help you reach your target audience. Here are a few popular options:
* **Audio Ads**: These are the most basic type of Spotify Ad, and they work by playing a short audio clip before or after a listener's music.
* **Display Ads**: These ads are visual and appear on the Spotify desktop and mobile apps.
* **Video Ads**: These ads are full-screen videos that play before or after a listener's music.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Too Broad “Because More People = More Customers”

I watched a coffee shop in Austin, Texas burn through $1,200 in three weeks. The owner, smart guy, great cold brew, wanted to “get the name out there.” He set his Spotify ad to target all of Travis County. 18–65. Both genders. All genres.

You know who heard his ad? A 62-year-old retired accountant in Cedar Park who listens to smooth jazz and lives 22 miles from the shop. She never drives south of the river. She’s not his customer. But Spotify charged him for every impression anyway.

**What went wrong:** He treated Spotify like a billboard. It’s not. It’s a radio that follows specific people. You cannot afford to pay for ears that will never walk through your door.

**The fix:** I had him rebuild the targeting. We set a 3-mile radius around the shop (5 miles max — Austin traffic is real). We selected 25–45 age range. We picked three genres: indie rock, alternative hip-hop, and modern folk — based on what his regulars actually listen to when they’re in line. We added an interest target for “coffee enthusiasts.”

**The outcome:** His cost per impression dropped from $0.012 to $0.004. More importantly, his promo code (“BURNTENDEAVOR” for 15% off) got 43 redemptions in the next month. That’s roughly $860 in incremental revenue from about $400 in ad spend. His previous campaign? Zero redemptions.

**Rule of thumb:** You are not a national brand. Target like you’re handing out flyers on one street corner. Anything outside a 5-mile radius for a local service business is noise.

### Mistake #2: Running a 30-Second Ad When You Need 15

A hair salon in Nashville spent $1,800 over six weeks running a beautifully produced 30-second ad. It had music. A voiceover. Two stylists talking about balayage. It was lovely.

Nobody listened past the first eight seconds.

Spotify’s own data shows that completion rates for 30-second audio ads hover around 60–70% on desktop and drop below 50% on mobile. That means half your money buys silence, not attention. For a local business, where your budget is maybe $500–$2,000 a month, that math breaks you.

**What went wrong:** The owner thought longer meant more value. In audio advertising, the opposite is true. People listen to Spotify while driving, cooking, or working. They’re not leaning in. If you haven’t said something useful by second five, you’ve lost them.

**The fix:** We cut the ad to 15 seconds. Script structure: problem (first 3 seconds), solution (next 7 seconds), offer with business name (last 5 seconds). No music bed competing with the voice. One clear call to action: “Use code NASH20 for 10% off your first cut. Book on Booksy.”

**The outcome:** 15-second completion rate hit 81%. Cost per completed listen dropped by 40%. The salon booked 17 new clients from the campaign. Average ticket: $85. That’s $1,445 in new revenue against $600 in ad spend in the following month.

**Rule of thumb:** For local businesses, start with 15 seconds. If you find your completion rate is above 80% and you have more to say, *then* test a 30-second version. Do not start there.

### Mistake #3: No Promo Code or Trackable Offer

A pet grooming business in Portland, Oregon ran a three-month Spotify campaign. They spent roughly $2,400 total. The owner asked customers, “Did you hear our ad on Spotify?”

Nobody said yes.

But the owner couldn’t tell if that was because nobody heard it or because people forgot. They had no tracking. No way to know if the campaign did anything at all.

**What went wrong:** Brand awareness is a luxury small businesses cannot afford. You don’t have a national CPG budget to “build mindshare.” You need a phone to ring or a booking to happen. If you cannot tie a dollar of revenue to a dollar of ad spend, you are guessing. And guessing with real money is how businesses waste $2,400.

**The fix:** We gave them a specific offer: “Mention SPOTIFYGROOM when you book and get $10 off your first full groom.” Not a discount code — a spoken word. We also set up a unique phone number through CallRail that only appeared in the Spotify ad. And we added a UTM-tagged link in the Spotify ad’s display component (the visual that shows up on the screen) pointing to a landing page with the offer.

**The outcome:** Over three months, 22 people used the phrase “SPOTIFYGROOM” when booking. 14 calls came through the tracking number. Average groom value: $65. That’s $2,340 in attributable revenue from $2,400 in spend. Break-even, basically. But that’s before you calculate repeat business — and three of those new customers became monthly clients. The tracking also told us the ad performed best between 7–9 AM on weekdays. So we shifted the schedule to hit morning commuters. The next month’s cost per acquisition dropped by 22%.

**Rule of thumb:** If you cannot point to a specific number and say “this came from Spotify,” turn the campaign off until you set up tracking. It takes thirty minutes. The cost of not tracking is your entire budget.

### Mistake #4: Forgetting Spotify Catches You While You’re Multitasking

A fitness studio in Denver ran a video ad campaign on Spotify. Yes, Spotify does video ads. They spent $900 on a slick 30-second clip showing people lifting weights. Great production value.

Their click-through rate was 0.03%.

That’s not a typo. Three hundredths of a percent.

**What went wrong:** Video ads on Spotify run when the user is looking at their phone screen. But here’s the thing — most Spotify users are not staring at the app. They’re driving. Jogging. Cooking dinner. The phone is in their pocket or propped on a counter. Video ads get seen about as often as a billboard in a fog bank.

**The fix:** We switched the format to audio-only display ads (the standard audio ad with a companion image) and dropped the video budget entirely. We focused on writing a tight 15-second script that worked without visuals. We also added a text overlay on the companion image that said “NEW MEMBER SPECIAL — 14 DAYS FREE” because even if they glanced at the screen for half a second, they’d see that.

**The outcome:** Cost per website visit dropped from $2.30 to $0.42. The studio got 31 new trial sign-ups in six weeks. Nine converted to full memberships at $149/month. That’s $1,341 in monthly recurring revenue from a $600 campaign. Not bad for a format most advertisers ignore.

**Rule of thumb:** If you’re a local business, prioritize audio with a strong companion image. Video ads on Spotify are for brands that need to say “we ran a video campaign” in board meetings. You need to say “we got customers.”

## How to Integrate Spotify Ads with Your Other Marketing (Without Losing Your Mind)

Running a Spotify ad in isolation is like planting one seed in an empty field. It might grow. But if you water the rest of the field too, you’ll get a harvest.

Here’s how small businesses I’ve worked with actually connect Spotify to the rest of their stack.

**Pair Spotify with Google Local Service Ads.**

A locksmith in Chicago spent $500/month on Spotify targeting homeowners within 5 miles. Meanwhile, they were running Google Local Service Ads (the ones with the green checkmark) for the same area. The Spotify ad built awareness — “Having lock problems? Call us when you’re stuck.” The Google ad caught the demand — people actively searching “locksmith near me.”

The result: The Spotify campaign alone drove 12 calls over two months. But the combined campaigns drove 44 calls. The Google ads were already working. The Spotify ads just made the name familiar enough that people clicked the Google ad more confidently. That’s not a guess. Call tracking showed the combined cost per lead was $18, versus $32 for Google alone.

**Add a Square or Mailchimp integration.**

A bakery in Brooklyn ran a Spotify promotion for a weekend pastry box. The ad directed people to a Mailchimp landing page with a 10% discount in exchange for an email address. They spent $300 on the campaign. They collected 89 emails. They sent one follow-up email the next Monday — “Missed the box? Pre-order next week’s.”

Nine people ordered. Average order: $42. That’s $378 from the email list alone, before you count what the ad itself drove. The ad paid for itself *and* built an asset (the email list) that keeps paying.

**Sync with your booking system.**

If you use Booksy, Square Appointments, or Mindbody for bookings, put the ad’s call to action behind a booking link. Do not send people to your homepage. A hair salon in Denver sent Spotify traffic directly to their Booksy booking page with a “new client” category. Their conversion rate from ad to booked appointment was 14%. Most local businesses would kill for 2%.

**What not to do:** Do not run Spotify ads and then forget about your Google Business Profile. I’ve seen this three times. A business runs a Spotify campaign driving people to search for them. Those people search. They find a Google Business Profile with old hours, no photos, and a 3.8 rating with two reviews. The Spotify money evaporated into a bad impression. Fix your Google profile before you spend a dollar on audio.

## Creative Strategy: Writing a Spotify Ad That Doesn’t Sound Like an Ad

I’ve written about forty Spotify scripts for local businesses. Most of them were bad the first time. Here’s what I’ve learned.

**The worst ads announce the business name first.**

“At Smith’s Auto Repair, we’ve been serving Chicago drivers since 1998…” Nobody cares yet. You haven’t earned their attention. Start with the problem.

Example from a campaign I wrote for a laundromat in Philadelphia:
*First five seconds:* “Your dryer ate another sock? Yeah, we know.”
*Next seven seconds:* “Bring your laundry to Suds & Duds in Fishtown. We fold. We don’t judge. And we haven’t lost a single sock this month.”
*Last three seconds:* “Suds & Duds. Google us. Bring a book. We’ll do the rest.”

Total time: 15 seconds. No music bed. One voice. Clear offer. It sounded like a person talking, not a commercial.

**Use a second-person “you” problem, not a first-person “we” statement.**

Bad: “We offer same-day service with certified technicians.”
Good: “Your garbage disposal just growled at you. It’s 8 PM on a Sunday. You need help now.”

The second version makes the listener feel seen. They’ll keep listening because you described their situation. Then you hit them with “Call FixIt Plumbing in Austin. We answer after hours. Seriously.”

**Record it yourself or use a real human voice.**

Do not use an AI voice. I can tell in under two seconds. So can your customers. AI voices sound like they’re reading terms of service. Pay a local voice actor $100–$200 for a recording session. Or record it yourself on a decent microphone in a quiet room. The slight imperfections — a breath, a beat, a naturally awkward pause — make it sound like a human being, which is exactly what you want.

**Include an awkwardly specific detail.**

A dog groomer in Austin added this line: “We have a basket of treats by the front door. Your dog will smell it from the car.” That one sentence got more positive comments from customers than the entire rest of the script. Specificity builds trust. Generic praise (“we love your pets!”) builds nothing.

## Measuring Spotify Ads Without a Data Science Degree

Most small business owners I talk to ask two questions: “How do I know this is working?” and “Can I just look at the dashboard?” The answer to the second is no. The Spotify dashboard shows impressions, clicks, and spend. It does not show revenue.

Here’s the three-number system I use with clients.

**Number 1: Cost Per Offer Redeemed.**

Take your total spend. Divide by the number of people who used your promo code, mentioned the ad, or booked through your tracking link. If you spent $500 and 10 people redeemed, your cost per acquisition is $50. Is that good? It depends on your average customer lifetime value. For a $15 coffee purchase, $50 is terrible. For a $150 haircut with a 70% return rate, $50 is fine.

**Number 2: Assisted Conversions.**

Not everyone will convert on the first touch. A person hears your ad on Monday, forgets about it, then searches “best pizza in Denver” on Friday and clicks your Google ad because the name sounds familiar. That’s an assisted conversion. You can’t measure this perfectly without multi-touch attribution software (which you don’t need). But you can ask new customers: “How did you hear about us?” If 20% say some version of “I saw your name somewhere, then Googled you,” your Spotify ads are working as a top-of-funnel assist.

**Number 3: Foot Traffic Uplift.**

If you’re a retail or service business, run a Spotify campaign for one month and compare foot traffic (or bookings) to the previous month. Control for seasonality — don’t compare December to January. I had a bike shop in Portland do this. Their November traffic was flat versus the prior year. They ran $600 in Spotify ads in December. December traffic was up 23% year-over-year. Was it all Spotify? No. Christmas shopping helped. But that 23% against a flat November tells you something happened.

**Tools to use:**

- **CallRail or WhatConverts** for call tracking (about $30–45/month, worth every dollar).
- **UTM parameters on the display URL** (the clickable link in the ad). If you use Spotify’s “display URL” feature, tag it with utm_source=spotify&utm_medium=audio. Then check Google Analytics to see if anyone clicked.
- **Specific promo codes** per platform. Don’t use the same code on Spotify and Yelp. If you do, you’ll never know which one worked.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I really target people in specific neighborhoods, or is that just a marketing gimmick?**

It works. Spotify lets you target down to a ZIP code or a radius around a location. But the accuracy depends on how Spotify gets location data — usually from the user’s IP address or GPS on mobile. It’s not perfect. A person might be traveling through your city and get your ad. But the majority of impressions will go to people who actually live or work in your radius. I’ve run campaigns for a dentist in downtown Austin and the ZIP code targeting delivered 85% of impressions within the target area. That’s good enough for local advertising.

**Q: How much do I need to spend to see any results at all?**

I won’t launch a Spotify campaign for less than $500/month. At $300, you might get data but not enough to learn anything. At $500, you can run a 15-second audio ad for about 20–30 days with solid frequency (meaning listeners hear your ad 3–5 times). At $1,000, you can start testing different audiences or creative. If your budget is $200, put it on Google Local Services Ads instead. You’ll get better returns at that price point.

**Q: What if my business doesn’t have a “hip” vibe? I run an HVAC company. Will Spotify work for me?**

Yes, if you target correctly. An HVAC company in Denver ran Spotify ads targeting homeowners within a 10-mile radius. They ran the ads in the mornings and evenings — times when people are at home and might notice their furnace acting up. The script was straightforward: “Your thermostat says 62 and it’s not supposed to. Call us.” They spent $750 and got 14 service calls. Average job value: $220. That’s $3,080 in revenue. Spotify isn’t just for indie coffee shops. It’s for anyone whose customer listens to music — which is everyone.

**Q: Can I just use the same ad I run on the radio?**

Please don’t. Radio ads are built for a different environment. They have more time (30–60 seconds), they use jingles and sound effects, and they often repeat the business name three times. Spotify listeners are using headphones or car speakers, not a dusty AM dial. A radio ad translated verbatim to Spotify sounds dated and aggressive. Rewrite it for a single listener, not a crowd. Shorter sentences. One voice. Less “call now” energy.

**Q: How do I track someone who heard my ad but booked online a week later?**

That’s the hard part. Spotify ad platforms don’t have a direct path to your booking system. What you can do: use a unique promo code for Spotify, track UTM clicks to your website, and run a survey during checkout. A simple checkbox: “How did you hear about us?” with “Spotify ad” as an option. Most booking platforms (Square, Booksy, Vagaro) let you add custom fields. Add that one. Over time, you’ll see a pattern.

**Q: Is Spotify better than Instagram ads for a local business?**

Depends on what you sell. If your business is visual — hair color, tattoos, interior design — Instagram is probably better because people can see the work. If your business solves a problem — plumbing, locksmith, dog grooming, dry cleaning — Spotify can work as well or better because you’re talking directly to someone in a moment where they’re not scrolling. They’re listening. And if you describe their problem accurately, they will remember you. I’ve seen both work. The question isn’t “which platform is better.” It’s “which platform does your specific customer use during the moment they need you.” For problem-solving businesses, that moment is often on Spotify, during the commute home when they’re thinking about all the things they need to fix.

**Q: Do I need a professional recording studio?**

No. I recorded a campaign for a coffee shop in an Airbnb closet using a $40 microphone. The script was good. The offer was clear. The recording was slightly imperfect — you could hear a breath. Customers said it sounded “real.” That’s better than “produced.” Spend your money on a good script and a clear offer. The recording quality matters, but it matters less than you think. If the words are right, people listen.
