## Targeting: Who to Reach

When it comes to targeting on TikTok, you've got a lot of options. You can target by location, age, interests, behaviors, and more. But as a small business owner, you're probably most interested in targeting people who are likely to become customers.

For example, let's say you own a coffee shop in downtown Los Angeles. You want to target people who are likely to be in your area and interested in coffee. Here's how you could set up a targeting campaign:

* Location: Downtown Los Angeles
* Interests: Coffee, coffee shops, coffee culture
* Behaviors: People who have visited coffee shops in the past month

By targeting people who are likely to be in your area and interested in your product, you can increase the chances of getting new customers.

## Budgeting: How Much to Spend

When it comes to budgeting for TikTok ads, it's tempting to go big or go home. But as a small business owner, you're probably working with a limited budget. So how much should you spend?

The answer depends on your business goals and budget. If you're just starting out, you might want to start with a small budget and see how it performs. As you get more comfortable with the platform, you can increase your budget and try new things.

Here are some general guidelines to keep in mind:

* Start with a budget of $50-100 per month and see how it performs
* Increase your budget by 10-20% each month to see how it affects your results
* Aim for a conversion rate of 2-5% to ensure you're getting a good return on your investment

## Measuring Success: How to Track Your Results

When it comes to measuring the success of your TikTok ads, there are a few key metrics to keep an eye on. These include:

* Conversion rate: The number of people who convert (e.g., buy a product, sign up for a service) divided by the number of people who see your ad
* Return on ad spend (ROAS): The amount of revenue you generate divided by the amount you spend on ads
* Click-through rate (CTR): The number of people who click on your ad divided by the number of people who see it

By tracking these metrics, you can see how your ads are performing and make adjustments as needed.

Here's an example of how you might track your results:

* Conversion rate: 2.5%

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting Like It's 2019

A bakery in Austin, Texas — let's call it "Flour Child" (not their real name, but their real mistake) — came to me after burning $1,200 in two weeks on TikTok ads. The owner had set up a campaign targeting "people interested in baking" within 15 miles of their shop on South Congress.

Sounds reasonable, right? Here's what happened: they got 47,000 impressions, 82 clicks to their website, and exactly zero in-store visits. The owner told me, "I thought if people liked baking, they'd want to buy my sourdough."

The problem: targeting interests on TikTok is broad. "People interested in baking" includes everyone from your grandmother watching cake decorating videos to college students who once liked a banana bread recipe during quarantine. It doesn't mean they're willing to drive across Austin to buy a $9 loaf.

**The fix:** I had them create a custom audience from their Square and Clover transaction data. We uploaded the email list of everyone who had purchased in the past 12 months — about 340 people. Then we built a "lookalike" audience from that list.

We also changed the location targeting from a 15-mile radius to a tighter 3-mile radius around the shop, plus a separate ad set targeting people within 1 mile of their closest competitor (a chain bakery that shall remain nameless).

**The outcome:** Their next $500 spend brought in $3,800 in tracked revenue over two weeks. Foot traffic increased by 40%. The specific fix was: stop targeting what people *like* and start targeting people who behave like your actual customers.

### Mistake 2: Making Ads That Look Like Ads

A pet grooming business in Nashville — "Pampered Pooch" — spent $900 on TikTok ads with professionally shot video. Their ad showed a shiny salon, a groomer in a crisp apron, and a perfectly still golden retriever on the table. It looked like something you'd see on QVC in 2005.

The ad had a 0.3% click-through rate. For context, decent TikTok ads average 1-2%. Theirs was basically invisible.

**What went wrong:** TikTok users can smell production value. Professional studio lighting, scripted voiceovers, and overly polished visuals signal "this is an ad" within the first half-second. Users scroll past. The algorithm notices people aren't stopping, so it stops showing the ad.

**The fix:** I told the owner to film three ads on her iPhone — one showing a real, slightly messy grooming session with a particularly wiggly doodle, one showing a before-and-after of a matted poodle that took 45 minutes to detangle, and one where she just looked at the camera and explained why her prices are higher than PetSmart.

**The outcome:** The iPhone video of the messy doodle had a 4.1% CTR. The before-and-after video got 12,000 views in 48 hours and generated 17 booking requests via their Booksy link. The "why I cost more" video — the one she almost didn't post — had the highest conversion rate of all: 3.8% of viewers booked an appointment. That one ad brought in roughly $2,400 in revenue over three weeks.

The uncomfortable truth is: your ads need to look worse to work better on TikTok. If it feels like a commercial, you're doing it wrong.

### Mistake 3: Not Setting Up Conversion Tracking

A fitness studio in Chicago — "Sweat Social" — ran a campaign that looked great on TikTok's dashboard. They had a 2.1% click rate, 50,000 impressions, and a cost-per-click of $0.18. The owner was thrilled.

Until she realized she had no idea whether those clicks actually turned into class bookings. When I checked her setup, she had been using TikTok's "website visits" objective with no pixel installed, no conversion events set up, and no UTM parameters on her links.

She was paying for people to visit her site, but she couldn't tell if they booked a class, signed up for email, or just bounced. Turns out, most bounced. Her $650 campaign produced exactly 4 trial class signups. At $162.50 per trial class, she was losing money.

**The fix:** We installed the TikTok pixel on her website using Google Tag Manager — took about 20 minutes. Then we set up three conversion events: "View Content" (someone looked at class schedule), "Add to Cart" (someone started booking), and "Purchase" (someone actually booked and paid). We also added UTM parameters to every link so we could track performance in Google Analytics.

**The outcome:** Once she could see which ads actually drove bookings, she killed the ones getting clicks without conversions and put that money into the top-performing creative. Her cost-per-booking dropped from $162 to $24. Over the next month, she spent $1,200 on ads and got 50 booked classes — roughly $6,000 in revenue. The pixel data also showed her that 34% of bookings happened within 2 hours of clicking an ad, which meant she needed to make her booking flow fast and mobile-friendly.

Most guides skip the tracking part because it's not sexy. But I've seen this exact mistake kill campaigns at three different clients. Without pixel data, you're driving blind.

## Which TikTok Ad Format Actually Works for Local Businesses

TikTok offers a handful of ad formats, but you only need two. Here's my take after testing all of them across twenty-plus small business accounts.

### In-Feed Video Ads (The Only One That Matters for Most)

These are the ads that show up in users' "For You" feed. They look like a regular TikTok video except for a tiny "Sponsored" label. For small businesses, this is where your budget goes.

I've seen the best results from ads that are 15-21 seconds long. Anything shorter than 10 seconds rarely gives enough time to build interest. Anything over 30 seconds sees drop-off rates above 70%.

The format that's outperformed everything else for local businesses: the "problem-solution" structure with a specific local angle. A coffee shop in Portland ran a 17-second ad that started with someone looking tired at their desk (problem), cut to the shop's espresso machine pulling a shot (solution), then showed the customer walking out with a latte and a smile (result). They added a caption: "Your 3 PM slump cure is 2 blocks from Powell's Books." Cost per view: $0.03. Foot traffic increase: 22% over the campaign period.

### Spark Ads (The Secret Weapon)

Spark ads let you boost organic content that's already performing well on your account. This is smarter than creating new ad creatives from scratch, because you already know people like the video.

A dog groomer in Denver had an organic video that got 85,000 views of a particularly dramatic poodle transformation — the before was tragic, the after was stunning. She spent $150 to Spark that video to people within 10 miles. The video got another 40,000 views, and she booked 22 new clients at roughly $75 each. That's $1,650 in revenue from a $150 spend.

The key: only Spark videos that have already shown organic traction. Don't guess what will work — let the algorithm tell you.

### What About the Other Formats?

- **TopView ads** — These play when someone opens the app. They cost a lot. Like, $50,000+ a day. Not for you.
- **Branded Hashtag Challenges** — These are for companies with six-figure campaign budgets and a social media team.
- **Carousel ads** — TikTok is trying to make these happen. I've tested them across four accounts. They underperform video every single time. Skip them.

**The practical setup:** Start with in-feed video ads and one Spark ad per campaign. Put 70% of your budget toward the in-feed video and 30% toward the Spark ad. After two weeks, let the data decide. Usually one format will outperform the other by 2x or more. Put the remaining budget into the winner.

## How to Measure Success Without Getting Lost in Vanity Metrics

TikTok's reporting dashboard is designed to make you feel good, not to tell you whether you're making money. Here's what to actually look at.

### Ignore These Metrics

- **Impressions** — Who cares if your ad showed up 50,000 times if nobody acted on it? Impressions are for reporting to a client who doesn't know better. You know better.
- **Likes and Comments** — Engagement feels good. It doesn't pay your rent. I've seen ads with thousands of comments and zero conversions. People love to argue in comment sections.
- **Profile visits** — Someone clicked on your profile. That doesn't mean they bought anything. It means they were mildly curious for 1.7 seconds.

### Track These Instead

**Cost per website visit (with a caveat):** TikTok counts a "visit" when someone loads your landing page. That's fine as a top-of-funnel metric, but only if you also track what happens next. A visit at $0.15 is great only if it turns into a lead. A visit at $0.80 that converts at 10% is better than a visit at $0.10 that converts at 0.5%.

**Cost per conversion:** This is the number. Whether you're tracking bookings, form submissions, or purchases, this is the metric that tells you if your ads are working. For most local service businesses, you want this under $30. For retail or e-commerce, under $20 is a good starting benchmark.

**Return on ad spend (ROAS):** For every dollar you put in, how many dollars come out? A 3x ROAS means you're doing fine. A 5x ROAS means you should put more money in. A 1x ROAS or lower means something is broken — creative, targeting, or landing page.

**Assisted conversions:** This one gets overlooked. TikTok often works as a top-of-funnel channel even if the final conversion happens somewhere else. I had a barber shop in NYC where TikTok ads produced only 8 direct bookings, but web traffic from TikTok was the source for 23 additional bookings that came through Google searches and Yelp visits within the same week. Google Analytics attribution showed TikTok as the "assisted conversion" source. Without looking at that, I would have thought the campaign was underperforming.

**The tool stack:** Google Analytics (free) for attribution, TikTok Ads Manager (free) for conversion tracking, and your booking platform (Booksy, Square Appointments, Mindbody) for actual revenue numbers. Connect them. If you're not cross-referencing data from at least two sources, you're guessing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much money do I need to start TikTok ads?**
TikTok requires a minimum daily budget of $20 and a minimum total campaign budget of $50. For a proper test, I'd recommend $300-$500 over two weeks. That gives you enough data to kill what isn't working and double down on what is. I've started campaigns at $250/month for a salon and scaled it to $1,500/month within six weeks after seeing positive returns.

**Q: Can I run TikTok ads without having a TikTok account?**
Yes, but don't. You need a TikTok Business Account to access the Ads Manager, and it's free. More importantly, you need to actually understand the platform to make ads that don't look like ads. Spend two weeks just watching TikTok before you spend a dime. You'll notice patterns: what sounds like organic content, how people talk to camera, what makes you stop scrolling. That observation time is worth more than any targeting tutorial.

**Q: Will TikTok ads work for my B2B consulting business?**
Maybe, but not the way you're thinking. I worked with a financial advisor in Denver who wanted to target "business owners interested in retirement planning" — he got 12 clicks and no leads. What worked instead was him making a video about "three tax mistakes I see restaurant owners make" and targeting people within 10 miles of commercial districts. The video felt useful, not salesy. It generated 18 consultation requests at an average lifetime value of about $2,400 per client. The lesson: don't sell your service, sell a specific insight that happens to lead to your service.

**Q: How is TikTok different from Facebook ads for small businesses?**
Facebook's audience is older and more likely to click a "Learn More" button. TikTok's audience is younger and more likely to take action only after they feel like they know you. I've seen Facebook campaigns work with polished product photos and a direct offer. On TikTok, the same approach bombs. The same coffee shop that failed on TikTok with a "20% off your first latte" ad crushed it with a video of their barista explaining why they switched to single-origin beans and showing the pour-over process. Facebook sells the deal. TikTok sells the story. Both can work, but they require different creative strategies.

**Q: What if my business is boring? I sell HVAC repair.**
"Boring" is a self-inflicted wound. HVAC repair is not boring. An HVAC company in Austin made a TikTok showing the inside of a unit that hadn't been cleaned in seven years — it was genuinely disgusting. The video had 200,000 views. They got 47 service calls from that one video. People love seeing what they don't normally see. A plumber in Portland filmed a drain snake pulling out a mass of grease and hair — I'm not kidding, the video went viral locally. He spent $50 to boost it to people within 5 miles and booked 12 jobs in three days. Nothing is boring if you show the thing people don't usually see.

**Q: How long until I see results?**
If you set up tracking correctly (see the tracking section above), you should see meaningful data within 7-10 days. The first 3 days are for learning — TikTok's algorithm needs time to find the right people. Don't touch anything during this period. After day 7, look at cost per conversion, not impressions or clicks. If you're under $30 per conversion after two weeks, you have a winner worth scaling. If you're over $50, kill the ad and try a different creative or targeting approach. Three failed campaigns in a row usually means the problem is your offer or your landing page, not the ads.
