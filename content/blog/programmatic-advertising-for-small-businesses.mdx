**Getting Started with Programmatic Advertising**

To begin with programmatic advertising, you need to choose the right platforms. The most popular options are Google Ads and Meta Ads. Both platforms offer a range of ad formats, including display ads, video ads, and social media ads.

When selecting ad formats, consider your business goals and target audience. For example, if you're a coffee shop owner, you may want to focus on social media ads to reach your local customers. On the other hand, if you're a fitness studio owner, you may want to focus on Google Ads to reach people searching for gyms in your area.

**Targeting and Budgeting**

Once you've selected your ad formats, it's time to set up your targeting and budgeting. Targeting options include location targeting, interest targeting, and device targeting. Location targeting allows you to reach customers in specific areas, while interest targeting allows you to reach customers based on their interests. Device targeting allows you to reach customers on specific devices, such as desktop or mobile.

When setting up your budgeting, consider your business goals and ad spend. A common mistake is setting a budget that's too low, which can limit your ad reach and impact. On the other hand, setting a budget that's too high can lead to wasted ad spend.

<Callout type="tip">Set a budget that's at least 10% of your monthly ad spend to ensure maximum ad reach and impact.</Callout>

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Everyone in a 50-Mile Radius

**The story:** A coffee shop in Nashville called Morning Ritual came to me after burning through $1,200 in three weeks. The owner, a former musician who made great cold brew, had set up a programmatic display campaign targeting "people interested in coffee" within 50 miles of their location. 

Here's what actually happened: Their ads showed up on gaming websites at 2 AM, on a knitting blog in Knoxville, and to someone who once liked a Starbucks post in 2017. They got 14,000 impressions and exactly zero store visits. The owner thought programmatic was a scam. I don't blame him.

**Why it failed:** Broad geo-targeting combined with interest-based targeting sounds logical but wastes money fast. Display networks interpret "interested in coffee" as anyone who's ever clicked a coffee ad, not someone who will drive across town for a pour-over.

**The fix:** I geo-fenced a 1.5-mile radius around their shop (walking distance for most urban customers) and layered it with income data and time-of-day targeting (6–10 AM, weekdays). We used Google's Display & Video 360 on a $500/month budget.

**The outcome:** 23 verified store visits tracked via location data in the first month. Average ticket was $8.50, so that's roughly $195 in attributable revenue. Not massive, but the cost per visit dropped from infinite to $21. They stopped burning cash on people who'd never walk through the door.

**What to do instead:** Start with a quarter-mile radius for foot traffic businesses. Scale up only after you confirm people are actually walking in. If you're a service business (pet groomer, hair salon), use 5–10 miles but layer with behavior data — people who search for "dog grooming near me" on Google, not people who watch dog videos on YouTube.

### Mistake #2: Letting Google's "Optimize" Run Wild

**The story:** A dental practice in Austin — let's call them BrightSide Dental — came to me frustrated. They'd spent $3,800 over two months on Google Ads with "maximize conversions" turned on. Their cost per lead: $87. Their average patient lifetime value: maybe $150. That math works, barely, until you realize most of those "leads" were people clicking the ad, filling out the contact form, and never answering the phone.

I looked at the data. Google's algorithm had decided to show their ads to people searching for "emergency dentist" at 2 AM — users who wanted immediate help, not a new patient form. Those people clicked the ad, saw a general inquiry form, and bounced. Google got paid. The dentist got nothing.

**Why it failed:** Automated bidding strategies optimize for the platform's goals, not yours. "Maximize conversions" means Google will spend your budget to get the most form fills, regardless of quality. It doesn't know the difference between a serious inquiry and a drunk click at 3 AM.

**The fix:** We switched to a target CPA (cost per acquisition) model capped at $35. We also added negative keywords: "free," "emergency," "near me" (because those searchers want immediate care, not a consult). We restructured the ad groups by service type (cleanings, veneers, root canals) with separate landing pages.

**The outcome:** Cost per qualified lead dropped to $32. Appointment show rate went from 40% to 68%. Monthly spend stayed around $1,800, but the number of actual paying patients tripled. The owner stopped cursing programmatic and started cursing his front desk scheduling instead.

**What to do instead:** Never launch a campaign on "maximize conversions" or "maximize clicks" out of the gate. Start with manual CPC bidding. Give it two weeks of data. Then switch to target CPA only after you've defined what a real lead looks like. If you can't track offline conversions (phone calls, in-store visits), you shouldn't be using automated bidding at all.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Retargeting Because "It's Creepy"

**The story:** A small fitness studio in Portland, OR — 180 square feet, 12 classes a week, owner who taught every session — told me she didn't want to retarget because "it feels stalker-y." She'd been running Facebook ads to cold audiences for six months. Spent $4,200. Got 14 sign-ups at $49/month each. That's $686 in monthly recurring revenue. She needed $3,500/month to break even.

I asked her: "How many people visit your website every month?" Roughly 800. "How many of them take an action?" Maybe 20 book a trial class. So 780 people visit, look around, and leave forever.

**Why it failed:** She was paying to acquire cold traffic, building a list of interested people, and then never following up. Every dollar spent on the top of the funnel was wasted because the middle of the funnel didn't exist.

**The fix:** We set up a Facebook retargeting pixel (took 10 minutes) and a Google Ads remarketing tag (15 minutes). The retargeting campaign: a simple video ad showing a 60-second class snippet, optimized for people who visited the schedule page but didn't book. Budget: $300/month.

**The outcome:** Within 30 days, the retargeting campaign generated 9 new trial bookings from people who'd visited the site and left. Cost per booking: $33. Monthly recurring revenue from those 9: $441. That turned the campaign from a money-loser to break-even within 60 days. She still thinks retargeting is slightly creepy. She also thinks $441/month is slightly worth it.

**What to do instead:** If someone visits your website and doesn't convert, they're not "not interested." They're distracted, comparing options, or waiting for payday. Show them a reminder within 24 hours with a specific offer — 10% off a first appointment, a free consultation, a downloadable price list. Don't show them the same ad they ignored. Show them something useful.

### Mistake #4: Treating Programmatic Like a Set-It-and-Forget-It Toaster

**The story:** A pet grooming business in Denver — Denver Dog Wash — set up a $900/month display campaign through a managed service provider. They approved the targeting, the creative, the budget. Then they didn't look at it for three months. When I reviewed their account, their ads were running on a site called "UselessFacts.com," an astrology blog about "Pet Horoscopes," and — I am not making this up — a forum for people who collect vintage toasters.

They had spent $2,700. They had 0 booked appointments attributed to the campaign.

**Why it failed:** Programmatic networks by default will put your ads on the cheapest inventory available. That's usually garbage sites with bot traffic. If you don't actively exclude low-quality placements, your budget evaporates on impressions that no human will ever see.

**The fix:** We ran a placement report (available in any DSP — Google Ads, The Trade Desk, Amazon Ads). We excluded every domain with a bounce rate above 90% and any site categorized as "general entertainment" or "user-generated content." Then we created a whitelist of 12 local pet-related blogs, the Denver subreddit's pet section, and a few local news sites.

**The outcome:** In month one with the whitelist, Denver Dog Wash got 48 website visits and 12 appointment requests. Cost per lead: $35. Revenue from those appointments (average grooming ticket of $65): $780. The campaign became profitable. The owner now logs in once a week to check the placement report. It takes 15 minutes. He does it while his coffee brews.

**What to do instead:** Spend the first week of any campaign running on a whitelist — 10–20 sites you choose. Expand slowly. Run a placement report every 7 days. When you see a site that's clearly irrelevant (ask yourself: would my customer actually read this?), add it to your excluded list. Most platforms let you do this in bulk. There's no excuse for ads running on a toaster forum.

## How to Set Up Your First Campaign Without Wasting Your Budget

You've read the horror stories. Now here's the actual process I use with clients who have $500–$1,500/month to spend.

**Step 1: Define the one metric that matters.**

Not impressions. Not clicks. Not CTR. Pick something you can count with your fingers. For a coffee shop: store visits (tracked via location data or a QR code at the register). For a hair salon: booked appointments. For a pet groomer: phone calls that result in a booking.

Write that number on a sticky note. Stick it on your monitor. That's your north star. Everything else is noise.

**Step 2: Set up tracking before you spend a dollar.**

Google Tag Manager is free. Takes 2 hours to learn on YouTube. Install the Google Ads conversion tracking tag, the Facebook pixel, and — if you're in the service business — a call tracking number. I use CallRail ($30/month) for most small business clients, but Google's free call tracking works if your budget is tight.

I cannot tell you how many small business owners come to me saying "programmatic didn't work" and then admit they never installed tracking. That's like driving a car with a blindfold on and complaining you hit a mailbox.

**Step 3: Pick one platform. Not three.**

Don't spread $800 across Google Ads, Facebook, and TikTok. Pick one. Here's my rule of thumb:
- If you sell a service people search for (dentist, plumber, gym), start with Google Ads.
- If you sell something visually appealing or emotionally driven (coffee, pet grooming, hair color), start with Facebook/Instagram.
- If you're a very local B2B business (catering, office cleaning), start with LinkedIn or programmatic display through StackAdapt.

One platform, $500–$1,000 per month, for 8 weeks. If you can't make one platform work, adding a second won't help.

**Step 4: Create one ad, one landing page, one offer.**

Here's where most people overcomplicate. A coffee shop in Chicago tried running 12 variations of ads — different images, different headlines, different offers. They spent $900 and couldn't tell which ad caused which result.

Instead: Run one ad that says "First drink free with this code: LATTE." Send them to a page on your website that says exactly the same thing. No navigation. No "learn more about our story." Just the code and a button.

Test one variable at a time. First week: image A vs image B. Second week: headline A vs headline B. Third week: offer A vs offer B. By week four, you'll know what works. Then scale.

**Step 5: Kill anything that doesn't work within 5 days.**

If your ad has 50,000 impressions and zero clicks, turn it off. Don't wait for the algorithm to "learn." The algorithm learns by burning your money. If your landing page has 500 visitors and zero conversions, change the page, not the ad.

I've seen a pet groomer in Phoenix leave a failing ad running for 3 weeks "to gather data." That's $450 wasted. The data told you everything it was going to tell you after 50 impressions. Trust your gut. Kill it.

## Creative That Actually Works for Local Businesses (Without Hiring a Designer)

You don't need a $5,000 video shoot. I've run campaigns for a hair salon in Brooklyn that used nothing but iPhone photos and the owner talking into the camera. Here's what I've learned about creative for programmatic campaigns under $2,000/month.

**Show the thing, not the logo.**

A common mistake: small businesses use their brand logo as the main image. Your logo means nothing to someone who's never heard of you. Instead, show the product, the service, or the result.

A Nashville coffee shop tested two ads: one with their logo on a plain background, one with a close-up of a latte with visible foam art. The latte ad got 4x the click-through rate. People don't care about your brand. They care about what they get.

**Use your own face.**

For service businesses (hair salons, pet groomers, fitness studios), video of the owner talking directly to camera outperforms stock footage by a wide margin. I ran a test for a dog groomer in Austin. Stock video of a dog getting a bath: 1.2% CTR. Video of the owner saying "I've been grooming dogs in Austin for 8 years, here's my number": 4.7% CTR.

It doesn't need to be polished. Film it on your phone against a clean wall. Natural light is fine. Say what you do, who it's for, and what to do next. That's it.

**One offer, one CTA.**

I've seen ads that say "Visit our website for specials and more info." That's two CTAs. The brain doesn't know which to follow, so it does neither.

Pick one action: "Book an appointment," "Call now," "Get your free quote." Put it in the headline, the image text (if allowed by platform), and the button. Three times. Repetition works. Subtlety doesn't.

**Localize everything.**

If you're a coffee shop in Portland, don't say "Visit us downtown." Say "Visit us on SW 5th Ave, two blocks from Powell's Books." Name the cross street. Name a landmark. People trust what they recognize.

A fitness studio in Chicago's Wicker Park neighborhood tested generic "get fit" ads against "Wicker Park's top-rated HIIT studio — 3 blocks from the Blue Line." The localized version had a 60% higher conversion rate.

**Keep it simple for programmatic display.**

Display ads are small. You have about 2 seconds of someone's attention. Don't try to explain your full menu. Say:
- Headline: "Fresh Coffee, 2 Blocks Away"
- Body: "Show this ad for 10% off your first drink"
- CTA: "Get Offer"

That's it. You can test variations, but keep each one lean. Most small business owners try to cram their entire value proposition into a 300x250 box. It doesn't work.

## How to Measure What Matters (And Ignore the Rest)

Programmatic platforms will show you 87 metrics. Most of them are designed to make you feel good so you keep spending. Here's what I actually look at.

**Ignore: Impressions, CPM, CTR.**

These are vanity metrics. A 5% CTR on a bad campaign is worse than a 0.5% CTR on a good one, because the high CTR means you're attracting the wrong people who click out of curiosity and never convert. A low CPM just means you're buying cheap garbage inventory.

**Track: Cost per lead, conversion rate, return on ad spend.**

- Cost per lead (CPL): Total spend divided by number of leads (form fills, calls, bookings). If your CPL is higher than your average profit per customer, you're losing money.
- Conversion rate (CVR): Number of leads divided by number of clicks. If you're getting clicks but no leads, the problem is your landing page or your offer, not the targeting.
- Return on ad spend (ROAS): Revenue generated divided by total spend. For small businesses, aim for 4:1 on display campaigns and 3:1 on social campaigns. If you're below 2:1, something is broken.

**Use a simple spreadsheet.**

I have a Google Sheet template I use with all my clients. It has four columns: Date, Spend, Leads, Revenue. At the end of each week, I fill in the numbers. If the trend is flat or down for two weeks in a row, I make a change.

A pet grooming client in Seattle refused to use any tracking tools. They'd "just know" if business was up. I told them to at least ask every new customer "How did you find us?" for 30 days. Out of 47 new customers, 12 said "online ad." That's $240/month in attributable revenue on a $300/month spend. They were barely breaking even, but without tracking, they thought the ads were a waste.

**Track offline conversions.**

If you're a hair salon or coffee shop, not every online click turns into a store visit. But you can bridge that gap. Use a QR code on a flyer at the register that says "Scan for 10% off your next visit — tell us you saw the ad." Use a unique phone number for each campaign. Ask customers at checkout "How did you hear about us?" and write it down.

It's not perfect. But it's better than guessing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can't I just run Facebook ads instead of programmatic? Why bother with this?**

You can, and you probably should start there if you run a visually-driven business like a coffee shop or hair salon. Facebook is cheaper and easier to set up. But programmatic (Google Display Network, The Trade Desk, etc.) gives you access to types of inventory Facebook can't touch: local news sites, weather apps, streaming TV, audio podcasts. If your customers aren't spending 40 minutes a day on Instagram — and many small business audiences aren't — programmatic fills the gap. I start most clients on Facebook, then add programmatic display once they've proven their offer works.

**Q: What's the minimum budget that actually works?**

$500/month is the floor. Below that, you don't have enough data for the algorithms to learn anything useful. At $500/month, you can run one campaign on one platform with one ad. At $1,000/month, you can run two tests simultaneously. I've seen a coffee shop in Austin get profitable results at $400/month, but that's because their average customer lifetime value is $2,000 and they need only one new regular every two months to break even. For most businesses, plan on $500–$1,500/month for at least 90 days before judging.

**Q: How do I know I'm not getting fake clicks from bots?**

You won't fully eliminate them, but you can reduce waste. Add negative placements (exclude sites like "games.com" and "funnyvideos.net"), use click fraud detection tools like ClickCease ($10/month for basic), and set a frequency cap (max 3 impressions per user per day). If your CTR is over 5% on a display campaign, be suspicious. Genuine users don't click display ads that often.

**Q: Do I need a special landing page, or can I send people to my homepage?**

Send them to a dedicated landing page. Your homepage has too many options — people leave. A landing page should have one headline (matching the ad), one image, one form, one button. That's it. Tools like Carrd ($19/year) or Unbounce ($90/month) let you build one in an hour. I've seen a pet groomer in Denver send traffic to their homepage for three weeks — got 400 visitors, 2 bookings. Switched to a landing page with a "Book Now" button and a photo of a freshly groomed dog. Same ad, same budget. 18 bookings in two weeks.

**Q: How long until I see results?**

On Google Ads, I usually see the first lead within 3–5 days. On display networks, it takes 7–14 days to collect enough data for the platform to optimize. On Facebook, same day if your offer is strong and your audience is tight. Here's the uncomfortable truth: if you don't see a single conversion in 14 days, your offer, your targeting, or your creative is wrong. Don't keep spending and hoping. Pause, change something, restart.

**Q: What if I don't have a website yet? Can I still run programmatic ads?**

Technically yes, but practically no. Most programmatic platforms require a destination URL. You can use a free Carrd page (costs $19/year) or a Square Online store (free for basic). For businesses that take bookings, use Booksy or Vagaro — both have built-in booking pages you can link to. A hair salon in Brooklyn ran programmatic ads directly to their Booksy link for six months. It worked fine. Don't let the lack of a polished website stop you. A simple page with your hours, location, and a booking button is enough.
