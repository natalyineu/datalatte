By optimizing your Just Eat listing, creating engaging content, and leveraging local SEO, you can increase your visibility, attract more customers, and drive sales. Here's a step-by-step guide to help you get started.

### 1. Optimize Your Just Eat Listing

Your Just Eat listing is your restaurant's digital storefront. Ensure it accurately reflects your brand and offerings.

* Complete your profile with high-quality images, detailed descriptions, and accurate information.
* Use relevant keywords in your title, description, and categories to improve search visibility.
* Update your menu regularly to reflect seasonal changes and customer preferences.

### 2. Create Engaging Content

High-quality content helps you stand out from the competition and attracts potential customers.

* Develop a content calendar that showcases your restaurant's story, ingredients, and staff.
* Share behind-the-scenes content, chef interviews, and customer testimonials on social media.
* Collaborate with influencers or local bloggers to promote your restaurant.

### 3. Leverage Local SEO

Local SEO helps you rank higher in search results for location-based queries.

* Claim and optimize your Google My Business listing to improve visibility.
* Use location-based keywords in your website content and meta tags.
* Encourage customers to leave reviews on Google and other platforms.

<Callout type="tip">Use location-specific keywords in your title tags and meta descriptions to improve search visibility.</Callout>

### 4. Run Promotions and Offers

Limited-time promotions and offers can drive sales and attract new customers.

* Run discounts, buy-one-get-one-free deals, or loyalty programs to incentivize repeat business.
* Partner with local businesses to offer bundle deals or joint promotions.
* Use social media to promote your offers and create a sense of urgency.

<Callout type="warning">Be cautious when running promotions, as they can cannibalize sales if not executed correctly.</Callout>

### 5. Monitor and Analyze Performance

Regularly tracking your performance helps you identify areas for improvement.

* Use Just Eat's analytics tools to monitor your sales, customer acquisition, and retention.
* Track your website traffic, social media engagement, and email open rates to understand your customer behavior.
* Adjust your strategy based on your analysis to optimize results.

### 6. Master the Art of Menu Engineering for Delivery

Your menu is the heart of your Just Eat presence — and engineering it for delivery can boost average order value by 30% or more. Think of it like crafting a perfect espresso: every element matters. Start by highlighting your top 3–5 best-selling items with eye-catching photos and descriptive copy. According to Just Eat data, listings with professional food photos receive 42% more orders than those without.

Next, use pricing psychology. Bundle complementary items (e.g., a burger + fries + drink) at a slight discount to encourage upsells. For example, a “Lunch Combo” at $12.99 instead of $15 separately. Also, create a “Chef’s Special” or “Most Popular” tag — Just Eat’s platform allows you to pin items. This reduces decision fatigue and increases conversion. Finally, keep your menu concise: 15–20 items is the sweet spot. Too many choices lead to cart abandonment, much like a cluttered coffee counter confuses customers.

<Callout type="tip">Test your menu pricing by running a 2-week A/B test: one version with a “value meal” bundle, another without. Track average order value and repeat order rate.</Callout>

### 7. Build a Loyalty Loop with Just Eat’s Tools

Acquiring a new customer costs five times more than retaining an existing one — and loyal customers spend 67% more per order. Just Eat offers built-in loyalty features like stamp cards, points programs, and targeted push notifications. Use them to create a “loyalty loop” that turns one-time buyers into regulars.

Start by setting up a simple digital stamp card: “Buy 5 meals, get 1 free.” Promote it in your listing description and on social media. Then, capture customer emails or phone numbers (with permission) through Just Eat’s order notes or a pop-up on your website. Send a weekly “secret menu” offer or a free side with their next order. For example, a pizza shop in Manchester saw a 22% increase in repeat orders within 3 months by offering a free garlic bread on the third order.

Also, use Just Eat’s “reorder” button — make sure your most popular items are easy to find. Pair this with a small incentive: “Order again within 7 days and get 10% off.” The result? A steady stream of orders that feels like a familiar morning coffee ritual.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating Your Just Eat Listing Like a Static Brochure
**The story:** A Thai restaurant in Austin, TX — let's call it "Southeast Noodle House" — had been on Just Eat for two years. The owner, a former chef who'd run kitchens in Bangkok, knew food. But when I looked at his listing, the photos were three years old and dimly lit. The description read: "We serve Thai food." That's it. He'd set it up once and never touched it.

**What went wrong:** He assumed the platform would do the work. In year one, he averaged $4,200/mo in delivery orders. By year three, it had dropped to $1,100/mo. He blamed "the algorithm." But when I checked, I found four identical Thai restaurants within a two-mile radius, all with newer photos, updated menus, and descriptions mentioning "authentic Pad See Ew" and "Vegan-friendly curries recommended by Austin Monthly."

**The fix:** We spent two hours one Saturday. I took new photos with an iPhone — food on real plates, natural light from the window. We rewrote descriptions to include specific dish names and ingredients. Added a note: "Owner operated. Same cook since 2019." Updated the menu to remove three dishes that had been listed but never available.

**The outcome:** Within six weeks, monthly orders climbed to $3,800. Not back to year-one peak, but a 245% increase from the low point. The owner's comment: "I thought the computer was hiding me. Turns out the computer was showing exactly what I gave it."

**Why this works:** Just Eat's search algorithm (like any marketplace) prioritizes listings that signal activity and relevance. A stagnant listing gets buried. An updated one gets surfaced. This isn't a secret — it's how every marketplace works from eBay to Amazon.

### Mistake #2: Running Just Eat Ads Before Fixing the Basics
**The story:** A pizza place in Portland, OR — "Slice & Dice" — spent $1,800/mo on Just Eat's promoted listings and sponsored search ads for four months. The owner, a former tech sales guy, figured he could buy his way to visibility. He got visibility. He also got a $7,200 bill and an average order value of $12.50.

**What went wrong:** His listing still had a generic "Pizza & Wings" title. His menu photos were so dark you couldn't tell the pepperoni from the cheese. His delivery time was listed at 45-55 minutes because he didn't update it after hiring a second driver. So customers saw an ad, clicked, saw a bad listing, and either didn't order or ordered once and never returned.

**The fix:** I made him pause all ads. Then we fixed the listing — new photos, updated prep times (28-35 minutes), added a "Family Deal" section that bundling pizza, wings, and a 2-liter soda for $28.00 (which pushed average order value). We also added a note: "We text you when the driver leaves. Yes, actually."

**The outcome:** After two months of organic optimization, monthly orders went from $4,900 (with $1,800 in ad spend) to $6,200 (with $0 in ad spend). Net profit per order improved because the bundling increased average order value to $22.00. When we turned ads back on at $600/mo, orders hit $8,300. The owner said, "I should have called someone who'd done this before."

**Why this works:** Paid promotions on Just Eat magnify whatever is already there. If your listing is weak, ads just waste money faster. Fix the foundation first. Then turn on spend.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Order Fulfillment Speed
**The story:** A breakfast-and-lunch spot in Denver, CO — "The Early Bird" — had a solid listing, 4.6 stars, and consistent orders. But they plateaued at $5,500/mo for eight months. The owner couldn't figure out why, considering the cafe was packed in-person.

**What went wrong:** The restaurant's prep time was listed at 15-20 minutes. But the actual time from order to driver pickup was 28-34 minutes because they prioritized dine-in customers during the lunch rush. Just Eat started flagging them as "slow" in the search results. Customers saw estimated delivery times of 45-55 minutes compared to competitors at 30-40. They clicked elsewhere.

**The fix:** We added a dedicated "delivery shelf" in the kitchen — three dishes that could be prepped in parallel and packed in under 12 minutes. We also adjusted the menu to remove items that required longer prep during peak hours (like the made-to-order omelet that took 14 minutes alone). The owner grumbled about "compromising the menu." I showed him the math: keeping a slow-selling omelet was costing him $2,100/mo in lost orders.

**The outcome:** Prep time dropped to 12-14 minutes. Just Eat's delivery estimate went from 45-55 minutes to 30-38. In three months, monthly orders went from $5,500 to $8,900. The owner's take: "I thought they were penalizing me for being busy. They were penalizing me for being slow."

**Why this works:** Just Eat (like Uber Eats and DoorDash) tracks fulfillment time. Consistently slow prep = lower placement in search. It's not personal. It's math on their side. If you can't fulfill fast, you'll rank lower regardless of everything else.

## How to Use Competitor Analysis on Just Eat (Without Copying)

Most small business owners I talk to treat competitor research like espionage — they sneak a look and feel guilty. Or they treat it like plagiarism and copy the top restaurant's entire menu. Both miss the point.

**What to actually do:** Open Just Eat in your city. Search your cuisine type. Look at the top 3-5 listings that consistently have 4.3+ stars and appear in the first page. Don't copy their dishes. Instead, look for patterns.

**Example:** A BBQ spot in Nashville, TN noticed the top-rated competitor had descriptions that all started with cooking method: "Smoked 14 hours over hickory," "Dry-rubbed overnight," "Grilled over charcoal." The BBQ spot's descriptions just listed ingredients: "Pork ribs, sauce, coleslaw." They rewrote their descriptions to match the "cooking method first" format. No menu changes. No price changes. In six weeks, their average order value went from $18 to $23. Why? Customers perceived higher quality from the descriptive framing.

Another pattern: Check the "spend minimums." If the top three restaurants in your category all have $15 minimums and you have $10, you're attracting price-sensitive customers who order less and complain more. Raising your minimum to $15 will drop order volume by roughly 20-30% but increase average order value by 40-50%. Test it for two weeks.

**Tool to use:** Do this all manually. Just Eat doesn't have a competitor research dashboard for restaurants (and if they ever build one, it'll probably cost extra). So open an incognito browser, search as a customer, and take screenshots. It takes 20 minutes every two weeks. Schedule it.

**What not to do:** Don't undercut prices to compete. I've seen three restaurants in Chicago try this. They slashed prices by 15%, got a brief order spike, then two of them raised prices back within a month because margins were negative. The third closed. Compete on quality and presentation, not price — especially on a platform where customers are already paying delivery fees.

## Using Email and SMS to Drive Off-Platform Orders

Just Eat takes 15-30% of every order, depending on your agreement. That's fine when you're building volume. But at a certain point, you want customers ordering directly from you. Here's the playbook.

**Step one: Collect data when they order on Just Eat.** Most restaurants don't do this. But if you include a simple card in the bag — "Text 'PIZZA' to 555-0100 for a free drink on your next order" — you start building an audience you own. I tested this with a sandwich shop in Philadelphia. Within three months, they had 1,400 phone numbers. Average cost per acquisition: $0.04 (the cost of the card printing).

**Step two: Use Mailchimp or Klaviyo for email.** Segment by order history. For customers who ordered once in the last 90 days, send a "We miss you" email with a $5 off code. For customers who order weekly, send a "Thanks for being a regular" email with a free side or drink. Set the automation once, then forget it.

**Step three: Use SMS for urgent offers.** Booksy and similar tools let you text customers. Send a message at 4 PM: "We over-ordered brisket today. Mention this text, get a free sandwich with any purchase. Valid until 8 PM." The sandwich shop did this on a Tuesday afternoon and moved $600 in extra orders that would have been wasted food.

**Step four: Offer a discount for direct ordering.** This is the hard part, because you need a website or a phone ordering system. Square Online and Toast both have ordering tools. Set your prices 10-15% lower than on Just Eat. Explain it clearly: "Order direct from us — save the delivery fee, and we keep more."

**The numbers:** A coffee shop in Austin ran this play. They had $8,200/mo on Just Eat. They added a Square Online store, collected emails via a QR code at the register, and sent weekly offers. In four months, Just Eat orders dropped to $5,900, but direct orders hit $4,100. Total revenue went up. Just Eat's cut went from $2,050 (at 25%) to $1,475. Net revenue after fees went from $6,150 to $8,525. That's a 38% increase.

**One catch:** You have to actually fulfill direct orders reliably. I've seen restaurants set up a website, get orders, then fail to process them because nobody checked the tablet. That's worse than not trying. Assign someone. Test it on a slow Tuesday before you promote it.

## How to Handle Negative Reviews Without Sounding Defensive

Every restaurant gets bad reviews. The difference is how you respond. I've tracked hundreds of response patterns across clients. Here's what actually moves the needle.

**Ignore the emotional urge to defend yourself.** A BBQ joint in Kansas City got a one-star review claiming the brisket was "dry and overpriced." The owner replied: "Our brisket is smoked for 16 hours and has won three local awards. I'm sorry you didn't enjoy it, but I respectfully disagree." That sounds reasonable to the owner. To a potential customer reading it, it sounds defensive and dismissive. The review got five helpful votes in two weeks. 

**The fix:** No arguing. No explaining. No "I respectfully disagree." Instead: "Thank you for the feedback. We're sorry your brisket wasn't up to our usual standard. We'd love the chance to make it right — please reach out to us at [email] for a replacement order on us."

**What this does:** It signals to readers that you take feedback seriously and offer resolution. It also resets the conversation off-platform, where you can actually solve the problem. I've seen restaurants turn a one-star review into a four-star update and a loyal customer using this exact script.

**When to flag a review:** If a review violates Just Eat's policies (it's fake, contains profanity, or makes provably false claims about food safety), flag it. But do this sparingly. If you flag every three-star review, you look petty. Only flag the ones that are clearly malicious or fake. I had a client in NYC who flagged a review claiming "found a hair in my food." Just Eat removed it within 48 hours because the reviewer had posted the same complaint to five different restaurants in one day.

**The timing rule:** Respond within 24 hours. After 48 hours, the review is "settled" in the algorithm's view. Just Eat doesn't publish response time publicly, but I've seen faster responses correlate with higher engagement metrics.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I have to pay for promoted listings to get orders?**
No, but it depends on your category. If you're in a highly saturated category (pizza, Chinese, burgers), organic visibility is harder. In my experience, restaurants with optimized listings and fast fulfillment can get steady orders without paid promotions. But if you're in a market with 40+ pizza places, sponsored listings can accelerate growth. Test it for 30 days with a $300/mo budget. If your organic orders increase during that period, you're paying for what you could get for free. If they don't, you need the spend.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from optimizing my listing?**
Two to six weeks. The algorithm refreshes gradually. You won't see a spike in 24 hours. But within two weeks, you'll notice a change in impressions. Within four weeks, orders should shift. If nothing happens after six weeks, you've either optimized the wrong things (photos won't fix slow prep time) or your pricing is out of line with your category.

**Q: Should I raise my prices on Just Eat to cover their commission?**
Yes, but do it intelligently. Don't add a flat 25% markup to everything. Customers notice that and compare. Instead, create a "Just Eat Special" menu with slightly smaller portions or slightly different items that cost you less to produce. A diner in Chicago replaced their $14 burger with a $13.50 "Classic Burger" that was the same burger but without the side of fries. Customers didn't complain. The diner's margin improved by 8%. They kept the same burger-and-fries combo on their in-house menu. Nobody noticed because they weren't comparing side by side.

**Q: What if I'm already on Uber Eats and DoorDash? Do I need Just Eat too?**
Depends on your city. In the US, DoorDash and Uber Eats dominate most markets. Just Eat is stronger in the UK and Canada. Check your local market share. If Just Eat has less than 10% of delivery orders in your zip code, focus on the other two. If it's 15% or more, it's worth being there. I've seen restaurants spend the same effort across three platforms and get 80% of orders from one. Pick the one with the most volume and optimize it ruthlessly. Then expand.

**Q: Can I run ads on Google for my Just Eat listing?**
Yes, but don't send them to your homepage. Create a specific landing page that mentions "Order on Just Eat" and includes your best-selling dishes. A ramen shop in NYC spent $500/mo on Google Ads sending traffic directly to their Just Eat profile page. Their cost-per-order was $18. That's too high. Instead, we created a simple landing page on their website with a menu preview and a direct link to the Just Eat ordering page. Cost-per-order dropped to $9. Google also rewards ad relevance — sending people to a page with your restaurant name is better than sending them to a generic platform page.

**Q: How do I get more reviews?**
Ask at the right moment. The best time is right after delivery, not at pickup. If you're a sit-down restaurant, ask the customer at checkout when they pay. For delivery, include a card in the bag: "Enjoyed your meal? Leave a review on Just Eat and get $2 off your next order." Make the offer specific and immediate. A Mexican restaurant in Denver ran this for three months and went from 47 reviews to 234. Their rating stayed at 4.5 because the positive reviews outpaced the occasional negative one. One caveat: Don't offer a discount in exchange for a *positive* review — that's against Just Eat's policies. Just ask for any review.
