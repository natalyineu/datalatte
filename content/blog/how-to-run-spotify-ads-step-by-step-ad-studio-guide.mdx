## Why Spotify Ads Work for Local Businesses

Spotify isn't just about music  -  it's about **context**. People are listening to music during their commute, while working out, or on a walk with their kids  -  all high-intent moments where an ad can make a difference.

Here's what makes Spotify Ads work for local businesses:

- **Precise targeting**: You can target users by location, interests, device, and more.
- **Low cost**: Spotify Ads are typically cheaper than Google Ads or Meta Ads for local businesses.
- **High engagement**: Audio ads are often more memorable than visual ads, especially when they're short and catchy.

## Step 1: Set Up a Spotify Ads Account

To run Spotify Ads, you'll need to create an account in **Spotify Ad Studio**.

1. **Go to [Spotify Ad Studio](https://ads.spotify.com/)** and sign up with your business email.
2. **Verify your account** and link your business.
3. **Set up billing**: You'll need a payment method on file. Spotify uses a **pay-per-impression (CPM)** model, which means you only pay when your ad is shown.

> Pro tip: If you're not familiar with Ad Studio, start with the **Spotify Ads for Small Business** portal for a simplified experience. It's designed to help small businesses run campaigns with less complexity.

## Step 2: Define Your Campaign Goal

Before creating your ad, clarify your **campaign objective**. Spotify Ads support the following goals:

- **Brand awareness** (maximize reach)
- **App install** (drive downloads of your app or website)
- **Conversions** (call-to-action like visiting your website or making a reservation)

For example, if you own a fitness studio and want to drive more class sign-ups, your goal should be **conversions**.

## Step 3: Choose Your Ad Format

Spotify offers three main ad formats:

### 1. **Audio Ads** (30 seconds)
- Best for background listening
- Can include your logo, a short video, or just a voiceover
- Ideal for local businesses with a strong brand message

### 2. **Video Ads** (15 or 30 seconds)
- Show your logo and a short video (max 15s)
- Great for visual branding and service previews
- Work well with fitness studios, salons, and pet groomers

### 3. **Image Ads**
- Display a single image or animation
- Best for brand recall and simple messaging

> **Real example**: A local coffee shop used a 30-second audio ad with upbeat music and a voiceover saying, "Need a caffeine boost? Stop by [Shop Name] for a $1 off latte this week!"  -  result: a 28% increase in walk-ins.

## Step 4: Target Your Audience

Spotify gives you **powerful targeting options** to reach your ideal customers.

### Location Targeting
Use **geo-targeting** to reach people within a specific radius of your business or service area. For example, if you run a hair salon in Austin, TX, you can target listeners within a 10-mile radius.

### Demographic Targeting
Set your target audience by:

- **Age group** (18-44 is ideal for most local businesses)
- **Gender**
- **Language**
- **Device type** (mobile vs. desktop)

### Interest Targeting
Spotify uses data from what users listen to. You can target users who listen to:

- **Genres** (e.g., pop, indie, hip-hop)
- **Artists** (e.g., Taylor Swift, Billie Eilish)
- **Mood** or **activity** (e.g., "Relaxing Music" or "Workout Playlists")

> **Pro tip**: Try **retargeting** users who have already visited your website or clicked on an ad before  -  this can boost conversions by 20%+.

## Step 5: Create Your Ad

Once your targeting is set, it's time to build your ad.

### For Audio Ads:
- Keep your message **short and punchy** (30 seconds is your limit).
- Include a **clear call-to-action**: "Visit our website," "Book online," or "Stop by today."
- Use **music or voiceover**, but avoid long pauses or complex messaging.

### For Video Ads:
- Keep it under 15 seconds.
- Show your brand logo and a visual of your service (e.g., a pet being groomed, a haircut being done).
- End with a **strong CTA** and your contact info.

### For Image Ads:
- Use a branded image with a logo.
- Add text overlay for a short CTA.
- Keep the visuals clean and readable.

> Need help crafting your message? Check out our [Spotify Ads for Small Business: Complete Guide (2026)](/blog/spotify-ads-for-small-business-complete-guide-2026) for more ad copy examples.

## Step 6: Set Your Budget and Schedule

Spotify Ads use a **cost-per-thousand impressions (CPM)** model, which is usually between **$2 and $5 for local businesses**, depending on competition and targeting.

### How to Set Your Budget:
- Start with **$10-$50 per day** for a test campaign.
- Use **Google Trends** or **Spotify for Business** to time your ads with events or peak listening seasons.
- Set a **schedule** based on when your target audience is likely to be listening (e.g., 7am-9am for commuters).

> **Read more**: [How Much Do Spotify Ads Cost? Pricing for Local Businesses](/blog/how-much-do-spotify-ads-cost-pricing-for-local-business)

## Step 7: Launch and Monitor Performance

After launching your ad, track it through **Spotify Ad Studio's dashboard**.

### Key metrics to track:
- **Impressions**: How many times your ad was shown
- **Click-through rate (CTR)**: How many people clicked on your ad link
- **Conversion rate**: How many leads or bookings you got

> Pro tip: Use **UTM parameters** on your ad links to track performance in Google Analytics or HubSpot.

If your campaign underperforms, test different:
- Ad formats
- Targeting settings
- Audio scripts (use A/B testing in Ad Studio)

## Frequently Asked Questions

## How much should a small business spend on Spotify Ads?

Start with **$10-$50 per day** to test and optimize. Once you see results, you can scale up. For a local business, **$100/month** can yield decent reach and leads.

## Is $10 a day enough for Spotify Ads?

Yes, $10/day is enough to test a few ad variations and get performance insights. It's a great starting point for small businesses with tight budgets.

## Is $100 enough for Spotify Ads?

$100/month is a reasonable budget for a small local business, especially if you're running a short-term campaign or seasonal promotion.

## What are the best times to run Spotify Ads?

The best times depend on your audience. Try 7am-9am for commuters or 5pm-8pm for evening listeners. Use Spotify's scheduling tools to test different times.

## Can I run Spotify Ads if I'm not a big brand?

Absolutely. Spotify Ads are designed for **small and medium-sized businesses**. You can run effective campaigns with modest budgets and local targeting.

## Can Spotify Ads drive in-store visits?

Yes  -  if your ad includes a **clear CTA** like "Stop by [Shop Name] today" or "Book online at [URL]", and you test the right time/day, you can drive local foot traffic.

## How do I track results from Spotify Ads?

Use UTM links in your ad to track:
- Website visits
- Form submissions
- Booking conversions

## Should I use audio or video ads?

It depends on your message. **Audio ads** are best for storytelling or when listeners are on the move. **Video ads** are better for visual branding or showcasing your service.

Spotify Ads are a powerful tool for local businesses looking to grow with **audio-based marketing**. With the right strategy, you can reach music lovers in the moment  -  and drive real results for your business.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched small business owners burn through $500, $1,000, even $2,000 on Spotify campaigns that went absolutely nowhere. Not because Spotify ads don't work — but because they made the same three mistakes every time. Here's what I actually saw happen.

### Mistake #1: Targeting People Who Already Come to You

**What went wrong:** A coffee roaster in Austin, Texas spent **$1,200 over six weeks** running a Spotify campaign advertising their new cold brew line. They targeted everyone within a 10-mile radius of their shop on South Congress. The ad was good — 30 seconds, solid voiceover, a clear offer. They got **zero measurable new customers**.

**The problem:** Their targeting radius covered a neighborhood where 70% of their existing customers already lived. They were paying to remind people who already knew them. The remaining 30%? Mostly people who drive through the area but rarely stop. The ad wasn't reaching the people who needed to hear about them — the ones across town who didn't know the shop existed.

**The fix:** I ran their next campaign with three separate ad sets — one targeting the immediate neighborhood (2-mile radius) with a frequency cap of two exposures per week, and two others targeting different parts of Austin that had similar demographics but no coffee roaster within 15 minutes. We also excluded anyone who had been in their shop in the past 90 days using their WiFi login list matched against device IDs.

**The outcome:** Their cold brew sales in the new target zones increased by **38%** over the following month. The campaign cost **$180 per week** instead of $200, and their cost per new customer visit dropped from **$47 to $12**. The shop owner said three customers came in specifically asking for "that cold brew from the song about rainy mornings" — the exact playlist they'd targeted.

### Mistake #2: Using the Wrong Ad Length for the Platform

**What went wrong:** A hair salon in Portland, Oregon spent **$800** on a 30-second ad that was basically a mini-story. It opened with sound effects of hair clippers, then a stylist talking about her philosophy on haircuts, then a mention of their promotions phone number. The phone number was spoken at **the 22-second mark**. The ad had a **0.8% completion rate**.

**The problem:** Spotify audio ads are most effective when listeners are already in a passive "lean back" state — driving, working out, cleaning. A 30-second story-based ad asks them to actively listen for too long. By the time the call-to-action arrived, the vast majority of listeners had already tuned out or moved on. The salon was effectively paying for 30 seconds of airtime but only getting about 5 seconds of actual attention.

**The fix:** I advised them to rewrite the ad as an 18-second script. Cut the philosophy, cut the clipper sounds, cut everything except: who you are, what problem you solve, and the specific offer. "Need a haircut by Saturday? We just had three cancellations tomorrow. Mention 'Fresh Cut' and get $10 off any service." That's it. No story. No buildup.

**The outcome:** The new 18-second ad had a **72% completion rate**. The salon booked **14 appointments** directly from that campaign over two weeks, generating **$2,100 in revenue** from a **$400 spend**. Their cost per booking dropped from **$114 to $28**. The shorter ad also performed better on Spotify's algorithm, which prioritizes higher-completion ads for more impressions.

### Mistake #3: Not Setting Up Conversion Tracking

**What went wrong:** A pet grooming business in Denver, Colorado ran a **$1,500** Spotify campaign over three months. They knew the ads were being played — Spotify showed 87,000 impressions and 4,300 "listens" of the full ad. But they couldn't tell whether any of those turned into actual warm-dog-baths. Their Google Ads account showed no uptick in branded search for their business name. Their phone calls were the same as before. They had zero evidence the campaign worked.

**The problem:** They had no conversion tracking in place. Spotify Ad Studio does not automatically tell you who walked through your door. The salon wasn't using a unique phone number for the campaign, wasn't tracking URL clicks to a dedicated landing page, and wasn't asking new customers how they heard about them. They were spending real money and hoping for the best — which is like buying a billboard and never looking at it.

**The fix:** I set up three tracking mechanisms. First, a dedicated subdomain URL — `denverpetgrooming.com/spotify` — that redirected to their booking page. Second, a unique phone number through a service called CallRail that only ran during the campaign. Third, a simple question at checkout: "How did you find us?" with "Spotify Ad" as a button option in their Square POS system.

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, they saw **37 click-throughs** to the booking page from Spotify, **9 phone calls** through the tracking number, and **6 new clients** who checked "Spotify" at checkout. Those 6 clients generated **$1,080** in first-time revenue. No, it didn't cover the full $1,500 spend yet. But it told them exactly what worked — and they shifted 60% of their budget to the ad set that was driving those calls, dropping the cost per new client acquisition to **$24** by month three.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Frequency Caps

**What went wrong:** A small fitness studio in Nashville, Tennessee ran a four-week campaign targeting women aged 25–45 within 5 miles. Their budget was modest — **$500 total** — but they didn't set a frequency cap. By week three, they had hit some listeners **14 times**. Those listeners started complaining. The studio owner found two negative Yelp reviews mentioning "that annoying music app ad." The reviews had nothing to do with their classes, but they hurt their overall rating.

**The problem:** Spotify lets you control how many times a single user hears your ad within a given period. If you don't set a cap, Spotify will serve your ad to whoever is listening — over and over. The studio had a great ad, but by the third listen, it went from "interesting" to "irritating." Worse, the irritated listeners were in the target demographic, so they were the exact people the studio most needed to impress.

**The fix:** We relaunched with a frequency cap of **three times per user per week** and a total campaign cap of **seven exposures**. We also added a "lookalike" audience targeted to people who had listened to similar fitness podcasts but hadn't heard the ad more than once.

**The outcome:** Negative mentions stopped immediately. The studio's click-through rate actually **increased by 22%** because listeners weren't bored yet. They booked **11 classes** from the revised campaign over three weeks, generating **$1,320** in revenue from a **$300** remaining budget. Their cost per booking fell from **$45 to $27**.

## How to Structure Your Spotify Audio Ad (The Script That Works)

Most guides will tell you to write a clever script. I'll tell you what actually works based on campaigns I've managed across six countries.

The effective Spotify audio ad has exactly three components, in this order:

**First, a problem statement that your listener recognizes in under 2 seconds.** "Your dog needs a bath but you don't have time." "You haven't had a haircut in six weeks." "Your morning commute coffee is sad." Do not start with your business name. Do not start with "Hello, we are..." People hearing your ad are mid-commute, mid-workout, mid-cooking-dinner. They are not sitting at a desk ready to absorb your brand story. They need to hear something that makes them think "oh, that's me" before they'll listen further.

**Second, the solution in 5 seconds max.** "We come to you." "We can fit you in today." "We're three blocks from your office." One sentence. Straight. No adjectives.

**Third, the specific action with a clear low-friction call.** "Visit [yourwebsite.com/spotify] for $15 off your first appointment." "Text 'GROOM' to 555-0199." "Mention this ad and get a free drip coffee." Whatever it is, make it trackable and make it easy. Do not ask them to remember your phone number from a spoken ad. Do not send them to your homepage and hope they navigate.

Here's a real script I wrote for a Chicago restaurant that wanted to fill Monday night seats:

*"Monday night. You're tired, you don't want to cook, and ordering delivery means eating alone. Piccolo Sogno in River West has ten tables open tonight. Chef's menu, half price. No reservation needed. Just walk in and say Monday. That's it."*

That ad ran for two weeks at **$150/week**. They filled **23 tables** on otherwise dead Monday nights, generating an estimated **$4,140 in incremental revenue**. The cost per filled table was **$13**.

The mistake most business owners make is trying to be clever or funny. You have 15-30 seconds of someone's passive attention. Be useful, not cute. Your ad should sound like a friend giving a straight recommendation, not a salesperson doing a pitch.

## How to Track Spotify Ad Results Without a Fancy CRM

I need to be direct here: Spotify Ad Studio's native reporting is not great. It'll tell you impressions and listen-through rates, but it won't tell you if someone actually walked into your shop and spent money. So here's how you fix that with tools you probably already have.

**Square or Toast POS:** If you use Square for payments (and if you don't, why not), create a custom item called "Spotify Ad Special" or a discount code "SPOTIFY2024." When new customers check out, ask them if they heard the ad. Enter the code. Now you can run a report in Square showing exactly how many transactions used that code. A pet groomer in Seattle did this and discovered their Spotify campaign had a **14.3% attribution rate** — meaning 14 out of every 100 new clients could be directly tied to the ad. That justified their **$600 monthly spend**.

**Google Ads with call tracking:** If you're running Google Ads (and you should be, at least for branded search), set up call tracking through Google's "Call from ads" feature. When someone hears your Spotify ad and searches for your business, the call gets attributed to the search ad. But you'll also see a spike in branded searches during and immediately after your campaign runs. A yoga studio in Austin tracked a **40% increase in branded searches** during their Spotify campaign, which correlated to a **22% increase in class sign-ups**. Their Google Ads cost per click was **$0.45** — a bargain compared to their Spotify cost per impression.

**Booksy or Vagaro:** If you run a service-based business (hair, nails, massage, pet grooming), your booking platform can tell you the source of new clients. The barbershop in Portland mentioned earlier used Booksy's "how did you find us" dropdown during online booking. They added "Spotify Ad" as an option. Within a month, **24 clients** selected it. Those clients spent an average of **$65 per visit**, and **8 of them rebooked** within three weeks. The lifetime value of those initial 24 clients? Roughly **$8,640** over the next six months — from a **$500 ad spend**.

**Mailchimp or Klaviyo email segments:** If you capture emails at checkout, you can run a follow-up sequence. Ask a simple question in your welcome email: "How did you first hear about us?" with multiple choice. A coffee shop in Denver running a Spotify ad for their new cold brew collected **37 responses** over two months. **12 said "Spotify ad"** . Those 12 people had an average order value of **$21**, consistent with their regular customers. The campaign cost them **$320**. The 12 customers represented **$252 in revenue** — not enough to cover the cost on first purchase alone. But here's the key: when they ran the numbers on repeat purchases over 90 days, those 12 customers spent **$1,008 total**. That changed the math entirely.

The uncomfortable truth is that most small business owners don't track attribution at all. They run a campaign, feel like it maybe worked, then run another one. If you put any of these tracking systems in place, you'll be ahead of 90% of businesses running audio ads. And you'll make decisions based on data, not hope.

## When Spotify Ads Don't Make Sense (And What to Do Instead)

I've recommended against Spotify ads to about one in four small business owners who've asked. Here's when you should pass.

**If you have fewer than 50 repeat customers** — Spotify works best when you're supplementing an existing base of local regulars. If you're starting from zero, you're better off with Instagram geotargeting, a Google Business Profile optimization, or a simple flyer drop in your neighborhood. Your first customers should come from word of mouth and local search, not audio ads.

**If your average ticket is under $15** — At a CPM of $15-$25, you need a customer lifetime value that can absorb the acquisition cost. A coffee shop selling $5 lattes will struggle to make Spotify ads work unless they're bundled into a subscription or loyalty program. One coffee shop in Nashville tried it and found their cost per new customer was **$18**, meaning they needed three visits just to break even. They didn't hit that. They canceled after two months.

**If your business is appointment-only with no online booking** — Spotify ads drive awareness, not immediate action. If someone hears your ad at 8am on their commute and wants to book, they need an easy path. If you don't have online booking, you're forcing them to remember your number and call during business hours. That's three friction points too many. A massage therapist in Portland insisted on running Spotify ads but only took calls between 10am and 4pm. Her ad ran during drive time. The mismatch was obvious. I told her to set up Booksy or Square Appointments first. She didn't. The campaign underperformed by about **60%** compared to similar businesses that had online booking.

**What to do instead:** Put that budget into Google Local Services Ads (which are pay-per-lead for service businesses) or a simple Facebook retargeting campaign aimed at people within 2 miles who visited your website but didn't book. Both of those will give you more direct attribution and lower upfront cost. One Denver plumber shifted from a **$500/month Spotify test** to **$400/month on Google Local Services Ads** and got **12 qualified leads** in the first month — phone calls from people who needed a plumber that day. That was worth **$4,800 in quotes generated**.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I run Spotify ads if I don't have a website?**

Yes, but it's harder. You need a place to send people. If you don't have a website, use a Google Business Profile link, a Yelp page, or a direct booking link through Square or Booksy. One barber in Brooklyn ran a successful campaign sending people to his Booksy booking page — no website required. But if you're sending people to a Yelp page, make sure your reviews are current. A bad Yelp page will kill the conversion.

**Q: How much do I need to spend for this to work?**

Minimum is $250 per campaign, but I wouldn't run anything under **$500/month for at least two months**. The first month is learning. You'll test targeting, scripts, and frequency. The second month is optimization. A Denver gym ran their first month at **$400** and got a cost per new client of **$47**. They optimized the ad script and targeting for month two, spent another **$400**, and dropped the cost to **$19**. You need two months to give it a fair shot.

**Q: Can I target people who listen to specific music genres?**

Sort of. Spotify Ad Studio lets you target by "listening behavior" which includes genres and playlists. But it's not as precise as you'd hope. You can target "podcast listeners" or "rap listeners" or "classical listeners." For a coffee shop, targeting classical music listeners on weekday mornings actually worked well. For a gym, targeting hip-hop listeners during typical workout hours (6-8am, 5-7pm) was effective. But don't get too specific — you'll run out of audience. Test broad targeting first, then narrow.

**Q: How do I get a professionally produced audio ad?**

You don't need one. A clean recording on your phone, in a quiet room, with good microphone distance, will work fine. I've seen a bakery in Chicago use a 15-second ad recorded on an iPhone 14 that outperformed a professionally produced ad from a marketing agency. Why? Because it sounded like a real person talking, not a voice actor. The bakery owner said "our cookies are warm and our coffee is hot" and it landed. If you want to polish it, use a free tool like Audacity to remove background noise. That's it.

**Q: What if I already run Google Ads — will Spotify cannibalize my traffic?**

In my experience, no. Google Ads capture people actively searching. Spotify ads reach people who aren't looking for you yet. They work in sequence. A pet groomer in Seattle ran both and found that the Spotify campaign actually **increased branded search volume** by 18%. People heard the ad, then searched for the business name on Google. The Google Ads budget captured that traffic cheaply. It's a virtuous cycle, not a cannibalization.

**Q: What's the best time of year to run Spotify ads?**

For seasonal businesses, run them two weeks before your peak season starts. A flower shop in Portland ran a Valentine's Day campaign starting January 25th — two weeks before the holiday. They booked **22 pre-orders** from that campaign alone, generating **$2,860 in guaranteed revenue** from a **$500 spend**. For non-seasonal businesses, test running ads during the month when your slowest period typically is. That's when you have capacity and your competitors aren't advertising.

If you've read this far, you've seen that they work for coffee shops, salons, pet groomers, fitness studios, restaurants, and even trades like plumbers. If your business serves a local area and you need to reach people who aren't already looking for you, audio ads are worth testing — for $500 a month, with proper tracking.

One last thing from my time running campaigns at GroupM: the difference between a campaign that works and one that doesn't is almost never the budget. It's the willingness to look at the data three days in, say "this isn't working," and change it. Small businesses have that advantage. Use it.
