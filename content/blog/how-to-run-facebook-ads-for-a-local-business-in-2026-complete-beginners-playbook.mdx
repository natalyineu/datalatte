## Setting Up Your Facebook Ads Account
Before you start creating ads, you need to set up your Facebook Ads account. This involves creating a Facebook business page, installing the Facebook pixel on your website, and setting up your ad account. If you're new to Facebook ads, it's essential to understand the different types of ad accounts, including personal ad accounts, business ad accounts, and agency ad accounts. As a local business owner, you'll likely need a business ad account, which provides more features and flexibility.

To set up your Facebook Ads account, follow these steps:
* Create a Facebook business page for your local business
* Install the Facebook pixel on your website to track conversions and optimize your ads
* Set up your ad account and add a payment method
* Verify your business page to ensure your ads are compliant with Facebook's policies

## Targeting Your Local Audience
One of the most critical aspects of running effective Facebook ads for your local business is targeting the right audience. You need to identify your ideal customer and create ads that resonate with them. To target your local audience, you can use Facebook's location targeting feature, which allows you to target people based on their location, including country, region, city, or zip code.

Here are some tips for targeting your local audience:
* Use location targeting to reach people in your local area
* Target people who have shown interest in your business or similar businesses
* Use lookalike targeting to reach people who are similar to your existing customers
* Exclude people who are not in your target audience to avoid wasting ad spend

## Creating Effective Ad Content
Your ad content is what drives people to take action, so it's essential to create ads that are compelling and relevant to your target audience. Here are some tips for creating effective ad content:
* Use high-quality images or videos that showcase your products or services
* Write clear and concise ad copy that communicates your value proposition
* Use calls-to-action (CTAs) that drive people to take action, such as "Sign Up" or "Learn More"
* Test different ad formats, such as image ads, video ads, or carousel ads, to see what works best for your business

For example, let's say you're a coffee shop owner who wants to promote your new summer menu. You could create a Facebook ad with a high-quality image of one of your signature drinks, along with ad copy that highlights the unique flavors and ingredients. You could also include a CTA that drives people to visit your website or Facebook page to learn more.

## Optimizing Your Facebook Ads
Once you've launched your Facebook ads, it's essential to monitor and optimize them to ensure they're performing well. Here are some tips for optimizing your Facebook ads:
* Monitor your ad metrics, such as click-through rate (CTR), conversion rate, and cost per conversion (CPC)
* Use Facebook's automated optimization features, such as budget optimization and bid optimization, to improve your ad performance
* Test different ad creatives, targeting options, and bidding strategies to see what works best for your business
* Use Facebook's split testing feature to compare the performance of different ad variations

<Callout type="tip"> Install the Facebook Pixel on your website before spending any money on ads. Without it, you can't retarget visitors, track conversions, or create lookalike audiences — which are the three most powerful tools in Facebook Ads for local businesses.</Callout>

## Common Facebook Ads Mistakes to Avoid
When running Facebook ads for your local business, it's essential to avoid common mistakes that can waste your ad spend and reduce your return on investment (ROI). Here are some common mistakes to avoid:
* Targeting too broad of an audience, which can reduce your ad relevance and increase your cost per conversion
* Using low-quality ad creatives, which can reduce your ad engagement and click-through rate
* Not monitoring and optimizing your ad performance, which can lead to wasted ad spend and reduced ROI
* Not using Facebook's automated optimization features, which can improve your ad performance and reduce your workload

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Everyone in a 50-Mile Radius

A coffee shop owner in Portland, Oregon came to me after burning through $1,200 in three weeks. She had a great product, a beautiful space in the Pearl District, and zero customers from ads. She'd set her targeting to "people interested in coffee" within 50 miles of her shop.

Here's what actually happened: Facebook showed her ad to people in Vancouver, Washington, and Gresham — suburbs 20+ minutes away. Nobody drives 25 minutes for a pour-over when they have three cafes on their own block. Her CTR was 0.4%. She got 14 page likes and zero in-store visits she could trace.

The fix was uncomfortable because it felt small: a 2-mile radius, excluding neighborhoods with no foot traffic patterns to her street. We also added a "frequent travelers" layer — people whose location history showed they moved through her ZIP code during commute hours.

The outcome? $380 in ad spend over the next two weeks drove 47 in-store visits tracked through her Square POS system. Cost per visit: $8.09. She stopped running ads to people who would never walk through her door.

If your business depends on foot traffic, your radius should match how far someone will actually carry a hot drink or a bag of dog food. For a hair salon in Austin? Four miles max. For a pet groomer in a strip mall? Two miles. Test tighter before you test wider.

### Mistake #2: Using "Boost Post" as Your Only Strategy

A fitness studio owner in Nashville told me she'd been boosting posts for eight months. She spent $4,200 total. She showed me her "reach" numbers proudly — 12,000 people per month. Her actual new members from those boosted posts over eight months? Three. Two of them were her friends.

Boosting a post is Facebook's most profitable feature — for Facebook. It takes whatever you threw together and shows it to the warmest possible audience (people who already kind of know you) with zero optimization for a business outcome. You're paying to be seen by people who were going to see you anyway.

We switched her to a proper conversion campaign in Ads Manager targeting new parents in the 37212 ZIP code who had searched for "postnatal fitness" or visited gym-related pages. Ad creative was a 15-second video of an actual class — no polished production, just real people looking uncomfortable in a good way.

Spend: $900 over six weeks. New members from ads: 24. Average lifetime value of those members (she calculated using her retention data in Glofox): $780 each. That's $18,720 in projected revenue from $900 in ad spend.

Stop boosting. Open Ads Manager. It takes 45 minutes to learn the layout and you'll never go back.

### Mistake #3: Sending Everyone to Your Website's Homepage

A dentist in Chicago was running ads for teeth whitening specials. His ad copy was fine. His targeting was reasonable — 3-mile radius, people interested in cosmetic dentistry. His landing page was his homepage. The homepage had 17 menu items, a "meet the team" video that autoplayed, a paragraph about his philosophy, and the whitening special buried below the fold.

He spent $2,100. He got 8 form fills. Cost per lead: $262. He told me Facebook ads didn't work for dentists.

The fix wasn't cute. I asked him to create a single-page landing with exactly three things: the special price ($299), a before-and-after photo of a real patient (he had consent), and a booking button that went straight to his online scheduler through Booksy. No navigation. No "learn more." No team photo.

Spend: $450 over two weeks. Leads: 31. Booked appointments: 19. Revenue from those appointments: $5,681 (including upsells for additional work during the visit).

Your ad's job is to get a click. Your landing page's job is to convert that click into an action. If your landing page has more than one job, you lose most of them.

### Mistake #4: Measuring the Wrong Things

A dog grooming business in Denver was celebrating a 3% CTR. They told me their ads were working. I asked how many bookings came from those clicks. Silence.

They were tracking ad clicks in Meta's dashboard and nothing else. That 3% CTR — which is genuinely good for local — was mostly people accidentally clicking the "Shop Now" button they'd added to the ad because a YouTube tutorial told them to. Their actual bookings, tracked through a manual spreadsheet, were 5 per month from ads. Their ad spend was $800/month. Cost per booking: $160. Their average ticket: $55.

They were losing money on every booking and celebrating a vanity metric.

We switched the campaign to optimize for "calls" and "messages" instead of link clicks, and connected their Calendly booking system to the ad account using the Meta Conversions API. Within three weeks, they could see exactly which ads produced actual appointments. We killed the two ads that generated clicks but no bookings, put that spend into the one that did.

Monthly ad spend stayed at $800. Bookings went from 5 to 21. Cost per booking dropped to $38. Revenue from those bookings: $1,155 per month.

Stop looking at CTR. Start looking at booked appointments, scanned loyalty cards, and POS transactions. Everything else is noise.

## Choosing the Right Ad Format for Local Businesses

Not all ad formats work equally well for local businesses. I've tested most of them across clients in different verticals. Here's what actually works and what's a waste of money in 2026.

**Single image ads** still outperform everything else for immediate action. A clean photo of your storefront, your best-selling menu item, or a client's finished haircut drives more conversions than glossy video productions. I ran a test for a pizza shop in Brooklyn: static image of a margherita pizza vs. a 30-second video of the dough being tossed. The static image produced 40% more "order now" clicks at half the cost per click. People scrolling on their phones at 6pm don't want cinema. They want dinner.

**Carousel ads** work well if you have multiple services or products to showcase. A hair salon in Austin used a three-card carousel: haircut, color, and balayage. Each card linked to a separate booking page. Cost per booking dropped 22% compared to their previous single-image ad that tried to promote all three at once. Let people pick what they want.

**Offer ads** are undervalued by most business owners. These are the ads with a "Claim Offer" button that automatically sends people a notification or email. A pet groomer in Denver ran a "First visit: 20% off" offer ad with a $200 budget. Facebook handled the delivery and reminders automatically. 34 people claimed the offer. 27 actually booked. Cost per new customer: $7.40. The offer ad format also creates a sense of scarcity — people treat it like a coupon they'll lose if they don't use it.

**Lead generation ads** that keep people inside Facebook are better than lead ads that send people to a website — for most local businesses. When a coffee shop in Portland tried to send people to a website form, 83% of people dropped off. When they switched to Facebook's native lead form (name, email, phone — three fields max), completion rate hit 64%. The leads are less qualified? Maybe. But 64% of 500 people is more than 17% of 500 people.

Skip the "dynamic creative" and "catalog sales" formats unless you're an ecommerce business with 500+ SKUs. Small local businesses don't have the inventory or the data to make those work. Single image. Carousel for multiple services. Offer ads for new customer acquisition. Lead gen forms for email capture. That's your toolkit.

## Integrating Facebook Ads with Your Other Marketing Tools

Running Facebook ads in isolation is like opening a restaurant with no signage. You need the ads to talk to everything else you're doing.

**Google Business Profile**: I saw a dog groomer in Denver spend $600 on Facebook ads directing people to her website. Her Google Business Profile had 47 five-star reviews, 12 photos, and a "Book Now" button that went to her actual scheduler. Her website had a contact form that emailed her and she checked twice a day. We changed the ad destination from her website to her Google profile. Bookings increased immediately — people want to see reviews and photos before they trust a small business. The $600 started producing $1,800 in monthly bookings instead of $400.

**Square or Clover POS**: Connect your ad account to your point-of-sale system if possible. This requires some technical setup (the Meta Conversions API), but it's the only way to track in-store purchases from online ads. A coffee shop in Portland that did this discovered that customers who clicked on their ads spent 31% more per visit than organic customers. They started running ads specifically for higher-margin items to this audience segment.

**Mailchimp or Klaviyo**: Your email list is free money that most people ignore. A hair salon in Austin ran Facebook ads to people who had visited their website but not booked. Those same people were on her email list from a popup offer. She created a custom audience from her Mailchimp list and ran ads specifically to them. Cost per booking: $4.27. The people who already knew her just needed a reminder.

**Booksy, Vagaro, or Mindbody**: If you're a service-based business using these platforms, build your ads around booking specifically. The dentist in Chicago I mentioned earlier went from $262 per lead to $23 per lead simply by linking his ads directly to his booking page in Booksy. The platform handles the availability, confirmation, and reminder — your ad just needs to get them there.

Your email list, booking system, and review profiles are assets you already own. Use them before you spend more money finding new people in Facebook's auction.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Facebook ads as a local business?**

Start at $300–500 per month. I know that sounds low, but you need at least two weeks of data at a reasonable spend level before you can make any decisions. $100/month won't get you enough impressions to test anything meaningful. $1,000/month before you know what works is throwing money away. $500 gives you room to test two ad sets with different targeting, see which one produces results, and double down. A hair salon in Austin started at $400/month, found that ads for "balayage consultation" booked at $12 per lead while "haircut special" booked at $34 per lead, and shifted 80% of budget to balayage. Scale after you have proof, not before.

**Q: Should I target people who already follow my business page?**

Yes, but don't start there. Your warm audience (page followers, website visitors, email subscribers) will convert at a higher rate but the pool is small. If you only target them, you'll run out of people to show ads to within a week. A better approach: run 70% of your budget to cold audiences (new people in your radius) and 30% to warm audiences. The warm audience ads need different creative — no explanation needed, just a direct offer and a booking link. The cold audience ads need social proof, a clear value proposition, and a reason to trust you.

**Q: Can I run Facebook ads without a website?**

Yes. Use the "Get Directions" ad objective to drive foot traffic. Use the "Send Message" objective to start conversations in Messenger. Use the "Lead Generation" objective to collect contact information inside Facebook. A pet groomer in Denver ran ads exclusively to Messenger for six months. People messaged, asked about pricing and availability, and she scheduled them directly. Cost per booking was $5. No website needed. You will eventually want one for credibility, but you don't need one to start making money.

**Q: How long does it take for Facebook ads to work?**

Two to four weeks to know if your targeting and creative are fundamentally right. Seven to ten days for the algorithm to exit the "learning phase" and start optimizing delivery. If you see zero bookings or leads after two weeks at $500 spend, something is broken — creative, targeting, or landing page. Fix it before spending more. If you see some results but not enough to justify the spend, keep running for another two weeks while testing small adjustments. The worst thing you can do is change everything every three days.

**Q: Is it worth running ads during off-peak seasons?**

Depends on your business. A coffee shop doesn't need to run ads in December — foot traffic is already high. A dentist with cosmetic services should run ads in January when people are spending their insurance benefits and thinking about their appearance. A gym should run ads in January (New Year's resolutions) and September (back-to-school routine). Look at your own sales data from the last two years. Run ads when you know demand is about to dip. Stop running them when you're already busy.

**Q: What's the difference between a "reach" campaign and a "conversion" campaign?**

Reach campaigns get you seen by as many people as possible for the lowest cost. Conversion campaigns get you a specific action — a booking, a call, a form fill — at the lowest cost per action. If you're a hair salon, a reach campaign might cost you $0.02 per person reached but produce zero bookings. A conversion campaign at $15 per booking is far more expensive per reach but far cheaper per outcome. Always optimize for the outcome you actually want. Reach is for politicians and brand awareness agencies. Conversions are for businesses that need to pay rent.

**Q: Should I run ads on Instagram too?**

If you're a visual business (hair salon, bakery, tattoo studio, interior design), yes. If you're a service business (plumber, electrician, accountant), probably not. Meta lets you toggle Instagram placements on or off within your ad set. A coffee shop in Portland saw Instagram generate 60% of their ad-driven foot traffic because their pour-over photos performed well in the feed. A dentist in Chicago saw Instagram produce zero bookings in six weeks. Check your own performance by splitting out placement data in Ads Manager. Don't assume Instagram works for your business just because it works for someone else's.
