## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Running Ads to the Whole City Instead of a Realistic Service Area**

A dog trainer in Nashville, Tennessee, came to me after burning through $1,800 in three weeks. She had set her campaign to target “Nashville” — the entire metropolitan area including suburbs 30 minutes out. Her logic: more people means more leads.

What actually happened: She got 47 clicks, two phone calls, and zero booked evaluations. The calls were from people in Brentwood and Franklin, areas she refused to drive to because the commute ate into her training window. She was paying for traffic she couldn't convert.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the campaign with a 10-mile radius around her home address, then excluded specific zip codes where she had no intention of traveling. We also set her ad schedule to run only during her training hours — no sense paying for clicks at 10 PM when she's asleep.

**The outcome:** Same $600/month budget. She started getting 8–12 calls per week, all from her actual service area. Within 45 days, she booked 14 evaluation sessions at $75 each ($1,050 in booking revenue alone) before selling any packages. The wasted spend dropped to near zero.

**The rule:** Map your actual driving range before you write a single ad. If you won't drive there, exclude it.

**Mistake #2: Using Generic Keywords Like “Dog Training” Without Location Modifiers**

A trainer in Portland, Oregon, was bidding on “puppy training,” “obedience training,” and “dog behaviorist” with a $500 monthly budget. Her average position was 2.3, and she was paying $4.80–$7.20 per click. She got 18 clicks and exactly one lead in 30 days.

The problem: Google showed her ad to people searching for training content, videos, and national directories — not local services. One click came from someone in Seattle who was just researching methods. She paid $6.40 for that.

**The fix:** We paused all broad match keywords and switched to exact match with city names baked in: “puppy training Portland OR,” “dog obedience classes Southeast Portland,” “private dog trainer near me.” We also added negative keywords: “free,” “YouTube,” “DIY,” “book,” “eBook,” “how to.”

**The outcome:** Her click-through rate jumped from 1.2% to 4.7%. Cost per click dropped to $2.10. She was getting 30+ clicks per month, all from local searchers. She booked 6 new clients in the first month, paying for the ad spend 3x over. She stopped running the ad in month three because she had a waiting list. That is a good problem.

**Mistake #3: Sending Ad Clicks to a Generic Homepage Instead of a Booking Page**

A dog trainer in Austin, Texas, was sending all his Google Ads traffic to his website homepage. The homepage had a hero image of his dog, a 30-second video about his philosophy, and a contact form at the very bottom. He wondered why he spent $750 and got three form submissions.

I checked his Google Analytics. Average time on page: 11 seconds. Bounce rate: 81%. People saw the photo, didn't see a way to book immediately, and left.

**The fix:** I built a dedicated landing page — no menu, no navigation, no philosophy video. Just:
- A headline: “Need a Dog Trainer in Austin? Book Your Evaluation in 60 Seconds”
- Three bullet points about his specialties (puppy training, leash reactivity, separation anxiety)
- One button: “Book Your Evaluation — $75”
- A map showing his service area
- Three text reviews from past clients

We also set up call tracking through Google Ads so calls from ads went to a separate number. That caught the people who don't fill out forms.

**The outcome:** Conversion rate went from 0.9% to 6.4%. He booked 18 evaluations in the first 30 days, and 12 of those turned into $1,200 training packages. Total revenue from ads: $14,400. Total ad spend: $750. ROI: 19:1. He still uses the same landing page two years later.

## How to Track a Call From an Ad to a Paid Client

Most small business owners set up Google Ads, get a few calls, and have no idea which calls came from ads versus their Yelp page versus someone who walked by their studio. You can't optimize what you can't measure.

**What you actually need:**

Set up Google Tag Manager on your website. It's free. Then install Google Call Conversion Tracking. Google will assign a forwarding number that shows on your site only when someone clicks your ad. If that number gets a call lasting longer than 60 seconds, it counts as a conversion in your ad dashboard.

I watched a dog trainer in Denver lose $1,400 in three months because she was attributing every call from her cell phone to “word of mouth.” Turns out 70% of her new clients were finding her through Google Ads. She nearly killed her most profitable channel.

**The tool stack I use with clients:**

- **Google Ads call tracking** — tracks ad-driven calls, records duration, lets you listen to call recordings (with client consent)
- **Booksy or Vagaro** — many pet service businesses use these for scheduling. If your booking software has a referral URL field, you can tag bookings from Google Ads
- **Square Appointments** — same idea. Tag your booking forms with UTM parameters so you know which source drove the appointment
- **Mailchimp or ConvertKit** — if you capture email leads, tag them by source. Check later: did the Google Ads lead buy a package, or did they just download your free guide and disappear?

**One number that matters:**

Your cost per booked client. Not cost per click. Not cost per lead. Cost per client who actually pays you money.

If you spend $500 on ads and get 10 calls, but only 2 people buy your $1,200 package, your cost per client is $250. That's a 4.8x return. But if you don't track which calls came from ads, you'll think the ads are a waste.

I tell every client: set up tracking before you spend your first dollar. If that sounds like work, hire a freelancer for $200 to do it in a day. It will save you thousands.

## Why Your Google Ads Need Yelp and Reviews in the Search Results

Here's something most guides skip. When someone searches “dog trainer near me,” Google shows not just ads, but also your Google Business Profile, your Yelp rating, and sometimes your average review count. The ad itself might only be three lines of text, but the user sees your star rating right next to it.

A dog trainer in Chicago was running great ads — solid keywords, good landing page, strong call to action. But she was getting outclicked by a competitor with a 4.9-star rating on Google. Her rating was 3.8 because she had three old reviews from clients who were mad about a scheduling conflict she'd resolved two years ago.

**What I made her do:**

1. Respond to every negative review publicly, politely, and specifically. “Hi Karen, I apologize for the scheduling issue in June 2023. We've since implemented a text reminder system and haven't had a missed appointment since. Please reach out at [email] — I'd love to make this right.”
2. Set up a review generation system. Every time a client finished their training package, she sent a text through Mailchimp with a direct link to leave a Google review. She offered nothing in exchange — that violates Google's policy. But making it easy got her 22 new reviews in 90 days.
3. Claimed her Yelp business page and added photos of her training sessions. Yelp penalizes pages with no activity. She started posting a photo once a week.

**The outcome:** Her Google rating went from 3.8 to 4.7. Her click-through rate on ads went from 2.1% to 3.8% without changing a single headline. Why? Because people saw the star rating next to her ad and trusted it more than the competitor with 3.5 stars. Her cost per click actually dropped $0.80 because Google rewards higher-quality ads and better user experience signals.

**The lesson:** Your ad quality score isn't just about keywords and landing pages. It's about your entire online presence. A mediocre review profile will make even the best ad underperform.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads as a new dog training business?**

If you're in a mid-sized US city (think Austin, Denver, Nashville), start with $500–$800 per month. That's enough to test 8–12 keywords and get meaningful data in 30 days. Don't start with $100. You'll get 10 clicks, zero conversions, and conclude ads don't work. I've seen this exact scenario at least 15 times. Give the campaign enough budget to actually run.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads without a website?**

Technically yes — you can use a Google Business Profile or a call-only campaign that shows your phone number directly. In practice, a simple landing page will outperform call-only ads by about 2x. People want to see your pricing, your location, and a photo of your facility before they call. Build a one-page site on Squarespace or Carrd for $20/month. It will pay for itself in a week.

**Q: How long until I see results?**

Plan on 30 days of testing before you get reliable data. The first two weeks are Google's learning phase — it's figuring out which searches and locations convert. You might get 2 calls the first week and 10 the fourth week. That's normal. Do not kill the campaign after day four because you're impatient. If you see zero leads after 45 days, something is broken. Get help.

**Q: What if someone clicks my ad but doesn't book — am I just wasting money?**

Some waste is inevitable. A 5–10% conversion rate from click to booked evaluation is typical for dog trainers. That means 90–95% of clicks don't convert immediately. But some of those people will remember your name, search for you later, or recommend you to a friend. Google's own data shows that 50% of users who click a local ad visit the business within 24 hours. Not all of them call first.

**Q: Should I also run Facebook ads, or just Google Ads?**

Both serve different purposes. Google Ads catch people who are actively searching for a dog trainer right now — high intent, ready to buy. Facebook ads show your services to people who didn't know they needed a trainer — lower intent, better for brand building. If you're on a tight budget, start with Google. One dog trainer in Portland switched from Facebook to Google and saw her cost per lead drop from $35 to $8. Your mileage may vary, but that's a common pattern.

**Q: What happens when I stop running ads?**

Your leads will drop. That's the uncomfortable truth. Google Ads work like a faucet — turn it on, water comes out; turn it off, it stops. Some clients will find you through organic search or word of mouth, but if ads were driving 60% of your new business, expect a significant dip. That's why I suggest building an email list of past clients and running a referral program. Diversify. No single channel should be more than 50% of your lead generation.
