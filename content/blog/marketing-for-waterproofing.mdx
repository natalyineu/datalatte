## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Google Ads to "Waterproofing" and Wondering Why You Get Wrong Calls

**The story**

Mike runs a basement waterproofing company in Austin, Texas. He'd been in business for eight years, mostly on referrals. When referrals slowed down, he opened Google Ads himself. He targeted "waterproofing" and "basement waterproofing" within 50 miles of Austin. $2,400 in the first month. Great.

Except 80% of the calls were people asking him to seal their garage floor, fix a leaky roof, or apply driveway sealer. Homeowners don't know the difference between "waterproofing" and "water sealing." Mike spent his mornings explaining, "No, I don't do roofs" instead of quoting basements.

He kept the campaign running for four months. Total spend: $8,400. Revenue from ad-driven jobs: $3,200. He told me he was "testing" Google Ads and it didn't work.

**What went wrong**

Mike's keywords were too broad. Google's algorithm happily showed his ad to anyone typing "leaky roof" because the keyword "waterproofing" was vaguely related. Mike also used broad match and didn't add negative keywords. He didn't target "basement" or "foundation" specifically.

**The fix**

I had him scrap the broad campaign. New setup:

- Exact match keywords only: "basement waterproofing Austin," "foundation crack repair Austin," "sump pump installation Austin," "French drain Austin"
- Negative keywords: "roof," "driveway," "garage," "sealant," "deck," "paint," "DIY"
- Location: zip codes where basements are common in Austin — Tarrytown, Hyde Park, Barton Hills, Zilker. Not the entire metro area.
- Call-only ads. No website visits from people who'd bounce. Straight to his phone.
- Budget: $1,200/month, not $2,400.

**The outcome**

Month one: 22 calls. 16 were legitimate basement jobs. He quoted 12. Closed 7. Average job value: $4,200. Revenue from ads that month: $29,400. Ad spend: $1,200. That's a 24.5x return. He called me and said, "I can't believe I wasted $8,400."

I can. I've seen this exact mistake at three different clients across different industries. The fix is boring. Exact match keywords, tight location targeting, negative keyword list. Do that before you spend a dollar.

### Mistake #2: Having an "OK" Google Business Profile and Calling It SEO

**The story**

Chris runs a waterproofing company in Nashville. He had a Google Business Profile — photos, hours, services. He'd been on page one for "Nashville waterproofing" for years. Then he noticed leads dropping. Competitors were showing up above him. One competitor had 87 reviews. Chris had twelve.

He figured SEO was a mystery he'd never solve. He called me asking about "SEO packages."

**What went wrong**

Chris thought a complete profile was enough. Google's local ranking algorithm heavily weights three things: proximity, relevance, and prominence. Proximity is location. Relevance is keywords in the profile. Prominence is reviews — both quantity and recency.

Chris had twelve reviews total. Two were from 2019. The competitor with 87 reviews was getting the calls. Google assumed they were the more popular choice. That's the algorithm's logic: if 87 people took time to review them, they're probably better than the guy with twelve.

**The fix**

Chris couldn't control what competitors did, but he could control his own review volume. Here's the process I gave him:

- Every single job, after payment, send a text: "Hey [name], thanks for choosing us. If you're happy with the work, would you mind leaving a Google review? Here's the link: [short link]. It takes 30 seconds."
- Offer an incentive he was already doing: a $25 discount on future work for anyone who leaves a review. He'd been giving that discount for referrals anyway.
- Respond to every review — positive and negative — within 48 hours. Polished, not defensive. (More on negative reviews later.)
- Target two new reviews per week. That's 100 per year.

**The outcome**

Six months in, Chris had 54 new reviews. His average rating stayed at 4.7. He moved back to the top of the Map Pack for "Nashville basement waterproofing." Phone calls from Google Maps increased by 40%. He told me he spent exactly zero dollars on this. Just a text automation tool ($29/month) and fifteen minutes a week.

Most local business owners skip this because it feels like "not real marketing." They'd rather spend $2,000 on a new website. The uncomfortable truth is that 54 fresh reviews will outperform a redesigned website for local search, every single time.

### Mistake #3: Chasing "Brand Awareness" When You Need Phone Calls

**The story**

A foundation repair company in Portland, Oregon, hired a marketing agency. The agency ran a beautiful display ad campaign — photos of cracked foundations, emotional copy about protecting your family's home, the works. They ran it across local news sites, home improvement blogs, and Facebook. $5,000/month.

Campaign metrics: 340,000 impressions, 1.2% click-through rate. The agency reported "strong brand lift." The owner asked, "How many jobs came from this?" The agency said it's hard to track. The owner fired them six months and $30,000 later.

**What went wrong**

Display ads and brand awareness campaigns work for Coca-Cola. They don't work for waterproofing companies. Homeowners don't see a banner ad about foundation cracks and call you. They see a banner ad, maybe remember your name, then a month later when their basement floods, they Google "waterproofing near me."

By then, they're calling the first result, not recalling your brand. You paid $30,000 to maybe be the second or third result in someone's mind.

**The fix**

Stop spending on anything that doesn't generate a trackable phone call or form fill. The only channels that matter for waterproofing:

- Google Ads (call-only campaigns)
- Local service ads (Google's version of paid leads for home services)
- SEO (strictly local, not national)
- Yelp ads (test small, track calls)

No display. No social media "engagement" campaigns. No YouTube pre-roll unless you're running a specific retargeting campaign for people who visited your website.

**The outcome**

The Portland company redirected that $5,000/month into a simple setup: $2,500 on Google call-only ads, $1,500 on local service ads, $1,000 on a Yelp ad test. First month: 47 phone calls from Google Ads, 12 from local service ads, 3 from Yelp. Total new jobs that month: 14. Average job value: $3,800. Revenue: $53,200. Ad spend: $5,000.

The owner's exact words: "Why did I let an agency sell me on awareness?"

## Why Your "Good Enough" Website Is Costing You $2,000 a Job

I look at a lot of waterproofing company websites. Most of them share the same problems.

**Problem one: no phone number at the top**

Your phone number should be in the header, visible on every page, without any clicking. Text size at least 18px. Contrast that doesn't blend into a white background. I still see sites where the phone number is in the footer, or it's a generic contact form. You sell emergency services. People call when water is in their basement. Make the number impossible to miss.

**Problem two: no before-and-after photos**

Homeowners don't care about your company history. They care about whether you can fix their cracked wall. Show them. Before-and-after photos of actual jobs in their city. Not stock photos from a supplier catalog. I can tell when a "before" photo was taken in Georgia and the "after" in Ohio because the soil looks different. So can your customers.

**Problem three: no pricing transparency**

You don't have to list exact prices because every job is different. But you can say: "Most basement waterproofing jobs in Nashville range from $3,500 to $8,500. We provide a free estimate with no obligation." That sets expectations and filters out people who can't afford you. Without pricing context, you get calls from people with $500 budgets who waste your time.

**The fix**

Rebuild your site around one metric: calls per visitor. That's it. Every page should make someone pick up the phone or submit a form. Remove distractions — no blog sidebar, no "about our team" with paragraphs of text, no auto-playing video. A local SEO specialist I work with in Chicago tested this: removing the homepage slider and putting a single "Call Now" button above the fold increased calls by 32%. Cost: one hour of web work.

**Tool recommendation:** Use SquareSpace or WordPress with a simple theme. Don't custom-build. don't hire a $10,000 agency for a five-page site. Spend that money on ads. A functional $500 site with good photos and clear calls-to-action will outperform a $10,000 site with animations and no phone number.

## How to Run Google Ads Without Burning $500 a Day

I mentioned tight targeting earlier. Let me give you the full campaign structure I use with every waterproofing small business I work with.

**Campaign one: brand search**

Bid on your business name plus "waterproofing." Example: "Austin Dry Basement waterproofing." This costs almost nothing. You want to own the search result when someone already knows you. Budget: $100/month. Usually spends $40.

**Campaign two: service + city**

Exact match. "Basement waterproofing Austin." "Foundation crack repair Austin." "Sump pump installation Austin." "French drain installation Austin." Each gets its own ad group with two ads, each mentioning the specific service. Budget: $800–$1,200/month. This is your workhorse.

**Campaign three: emergency keywords**

"Emergency basement waterproofing." "Basement flooding now." "Water in basement help." These searches happen at 2 AM on a Saturday. Budget: $300/month. Bid higher. People in crisis call immediately and don't price shop. Average emergency job is 1.5x your normal rate.

**What to avoid:**

- Broad match. Never. You'll get calls for "water leaks" that are actually a plumber's job.
- "Foundation repair" without "basement" or "crawl space." Foundation repair generalists are different and cheaper. They compete on price. You don't want those calls.
- Running ads across all hours. Test your call times first. Most waterproofing calls come between 8 AM and 7 PM. Save your budget for when people actually answer.
- Not using call tracking. A tool like CallRail or even Google's built-in call tracking will tell you which keyword generated each call. Without it, you're flying blind.

**Real example from a client in Denver:**

He ran all three campaigns for three months. Spend: $2,400/month total. Calls: 89. Jobs closed: 23. Revenue: $96,600. Cost per acquisition: $313. Average job value: $4,200. He was spending $5,000/month before with a general contractor agency getting half the results. I showed him how to set this up in two hours on a video call.

## The Yelp Problem (And How to Solve It)

Yelp has a bad reputation among small business owners. I get it. Their sales calls are aggressive. Their review filter is mysterious. But here's what actually happens with waterproofing companies on Yelp.

**The reality:** People search Yelp for "basement waterproofing" before they call. They read the top 3–5 reviews. If you have three reviews and a 3.5 star rating, and your competitor has 40 reviews and a 4.5 star rating, you lost that customer before they ever visited your website.

You don't have to love Yelp. But ignoring it costs you money.

**What to do:**

1. Claim your Yelp page. Fill it out fully. Same photos as your website. Same services listed.
2. Get 15–20 reviews. Ask customers directly. "Would you mind leaving a review on Yelp?" Most will say yes. You'll get 2–3 that go through. Yelp filters some. That's normal.
3. Respond to every review. Thank the positive ones. For negative reviews, never argue. "Thank you for your feedback. We take all concerns seriously. Please contact us directly at [email] so we can make this right." That's it. You look professional and reasonable.
4. Test Yelp Ads at $300/month for two months. Yelp will push you to spend more. Don't. Run it small. Track calls. If you don't get at least 2–3 jobs from it, turn it off. I've had clients where Yelp Ads worked great and others where it was a total waste.

**Why this matters:** A waterproofing company in Chicago told me 60% of his new customers mentioned Yelp. He'd been ignoring it for two years because "Yelp is a scam." He spent $500 to get 20 reviews and started answering calls from people who said, "I saw you on Yelp."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I get most of my work from referrals. Why should I spend money on marketing?**

Because referrals are unpredictable and they cap your growth. I worked with a company in St. Louis that got 80% of jobs from referrals for ten years. Then the housing market slowed, referrals dropped, and they lost 30% of revenue in one quarter. Marketing creates a second pipeline. You don't need to replace referrals. You need a backup that's running when referrals are slow. Even $1,000/month on Google Ads is cheap insurance.

**Q: How long until I see results from Google Ads?**

First phone call can happen within hours of launching. First job within a week if you're responsive. But profitable results — where your cost per acquisition is under $400 — usually take two to three months of optimization. You'll waste money in month one on keywords that don't work. That's normal. The companies that succeed are the ones who don't panic and turn off ads after week two.

**Q: Do I need a website or can I just use Google Ads without one?**

Call-only ads let you skip the website entirely. The ad shows your phone number, people call directly. That works. But if someone searches your business name and finds nothing, that's a trust problem. Spend $500 on a simple one-page site. It doesn't have to be fancy. Name, services, phone number, service area, five photos, five reviews. That's it.

**Q: Should I advertise in a nearby city even if I don't usually work there?**

Only if you have capacity to drive there. Waterproofing companies in Portland, Oregon, often get leads from Vancouver, Washington. It's 20 minutes away. That's fine. But don't target cities two hours away. You'll spend more on travel time than you'll make. Set your radius to 30–40 miles maximum, and only if that's realistic for your crew.

**Q: What's the single biggest mistake you see waterproofing companies make with marketing?**

They try to look bigger than they are. They write about "our team of 50" when they're a crew of three. They pretend to be a national brand. Customers see through it. Your advantage is that you're local. You know the soil in that neighborhood. You know which basements flood after heavy rain. Lean into that. "Local company, 12 years experience, we've waterproofed 400 basements in Austin." That beats "premier waterproofing solutions provider" every time.

**Q: How do I handle a bad review without looking defensive?**

Thank them for the feedback. Apologize for their experience. Offer a direct line to resolve it. Never argue in public. "Hi [name], I'm sorry to hear about your experience. We take this seriously. Please contact me directly at mike@company.com so we can make things right." That's it. Other customers reading this will see that you handle problems professionally. The review stays up, but your response makes you look good.
