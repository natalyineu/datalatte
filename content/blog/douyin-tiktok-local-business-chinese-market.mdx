Douyin (抖音) and TikTok are built on the same technology by the same company (ByteDance), but they operate on completely separate networks — one for mainland China, one for the rest of the world. A video that goes viral on Douyin is invisible on TikTok, and vice versa. For a local business trying to reach Chinese customers, this distinction matters enormously: you need a strategy for each platform based on where your target audience actually spends their time.

Here's the practical breakdown: Chinese locals and tourists who are currently in China use Douyin. Chinese diaspora communities (people who moved from China to Australia, UK, US, etc.) often use **both** — Douyin to stay connected to Chinese culture and TikTok to engage with their adopted country. Chinese tourists actively travelling typically continue using Douyin via VPN.

## Why short video works differently for Chinese audiences

Video content performs exceptionally well with Chinese social media users — not because Chinese users are fundamentally different, but because Douyin's algorithm is particularly aggressive at surfacing local and niche content, and Chinese platform culture strongly encourages sharing personal experiences.

A Chinese customer who has an exceptional experience at your café is more likely to film a 30-second video than write a text review — video is the native format on both Douyin and TikTok. A food-focused video posted to Douyin by a tourist with even a modest following can reach 50,000–200,000 users in the first 24 hours due to ByteDance's aggressive initial distribution model.

This means: **the quality of your customer's experience determines your organic reach on these platforms**, more so than any content strategy you implement yourself.

## Douyin strategy for businesses targeting Chinese tourists

Since Douyin users are primarily in mainland China, organic content on Douyin requires a Chinese entity for a business account. However, there are effective ways to leverage Douyin for tourist marketing without having a Chinese business account:

**Strategy 1: Encourage tourist-generated Douyin content**

When Chinese tourists visit your business, many will naturally film short videos for their Douyin followers. Help them make better content:

- Create a dedicated "filming spot" in your business — great lighting, an interesting background, your signature product prominently displayed
- Post a small sign (in Chinese) saying "适合拍视频的角度" (best angle for filming) with an arrow — tourists will appreciate the tip and use the spot
- Ensure your business name in Chinese is visible somewhere in the frame — either on your packaging, on a sign, or on a small card you place near the filming area

**Strategy 2: Partner with Chinese travel Douyin accounts**

Chinese travel creators (旅游博主, lǚyóu bókè) post extensively about overseas travel experiences. A creator with 100,000 Douyin followers who posts about visiting your café in Sydney can generate 200,000+ views, leading to a surge of tourist visits for weeks.

**How to connect with travel creators:**
- Search Douyin for "[your city name] 旅游" (city + travel) — identify creators who post regularly about your city
- Look for creators with 10,000–200,000 followers (micro-influencers) — they're more accessible and have higher engagement rates than mega-influencers
- Message them offering a complimentary experience in exchange for an honest post (not a guaranteed positive review — authenticity is valued on Douyin)

<Callout type="tip">Reach out to travel creators during China's major holiday periods (Golden Week, Chinese New Year) when they're actively looking for content about international destinations. These are their highest-traffic content windows and they're highly motivated to find great filming locations.</Callout>

## TikTok strategy for reaching Chinese diaspora communities

For Chinese diaspora communities in Australia, UK, and North America, TikTok is often more accessible than Douyin (which requires a mainland Chinese SIM for some features). Creating content on TikTok that resonates with Chinese diaspora audiences requires understanding what this specific community values.

**What the Chinese diaspora TikTok community responds to:**

**Bilingual content:** Videos where you or a staff member speak both English and Mandarin — even just a brief "欢迎来到我们的咖啡厅!" (Welcome to our café!) at the start — generate strong positive reactions from diaspora viewers who appreciate businesses that make this effort.

**Familiar flavours with a twist:** Content showing how you incorporate Chinese ingredients or flavours (matcha, taro, pandan, osmanthus, red bean) into your product gets high shares within diaspora food communities. A coffee shop doing a "pandan latte" video with Chinese subtitles taps directly into this.

**"Best [city] spots for Chinese students/families":** These are high-search-volume micro-niches on TikTok. If your business is near a university with a large Chinese international student population, content specifically welcoming international students performs well.

## Creating content that performs on both platforms

If you have a team member who is comfortable on camera and speaks Mandarin (even conversationally), short bilingual videos can be cross-posted to both TikTok and Douyin with minor adaptations.

**The format that works on both:**

**Opening (0–3 seconds):** Visual hook — the most striking image of your product, pulling directly out of a package or being plated. No talking. No logo. Just the most compelling visual you can create.

**Middle (3–25 seconds):** Context — where you are (your city and neighbourhood), what this is (the product name in English and Chinese), and what makes it special. Keep it conversational, not scripted.

**Ending (25–30 seconds):** Soft CTA — "地址在 [address]" (address is at) with text overlay, or "关注我们" (follow us) with your account name visible.

**Technical specs that differ between platforms:**
- Douyin: vertical video only, Chinese captions required for algorithm boost, post between 7–9am and 9–11pm Beijing time
- TikTok: vertical video standard, English captions with Chinese optional, post in your local timezone during peak hours (6–9pm)

<Callout type="example">A Brighton (UK) bakery owner who learned basic Mandarin phrases started posting 30-second videos introducing each pastry in English and Mandarin. Their TikTok account reached 12,000 followers within 4 months — 40% of whom identified as Chinese or Taiwanese in the comments. They now have a regular morning queue of Chinese international students from the nearby university.</Callout>

## Running paid TikTok ads targeting Chinese diaspora

TikTok's ad platform (ads.tiktok.com) allows audience targeting by language preference — meaning you can specifically reach users who have set their TikTok language to Simplified Chinese or Traditional Chinese, even if they're in Australia or the UK.

**How to set up a Chinese-language targeted campaign:**
1. In TikTok Ads Manager, create a campaign with "Traffic" or "Conversions" objective
2. Under Audience → Languages, select "Chinese (Simplified)" and/or "Chinese (Traditional)"
3. Set location to your country (Australia, UK, etc.)
4. Set your video creative in Mandarin or bilingual
5. Budget: $10–$20/day is sufficient for a local test

CPM for Chinese-language targeted TikTok ads in English-speaking markets typically runs **$3–$7** — cheaper than English-language targeting because the audience is more specific.

**What creative works in paid TikTok ads for this audience:**
- A "day in the life of our café" style video with Mandarin subtitles
- A limited-time offer with Chinese text overlay ("本周特惠" = "This week's special")
- A cultural tie-in post ("庆祝新年" = "Celebrating New Year") for seasonal campaigns

<Callout type="warning">Do not run the same creative on both TikTok and Douyin paid ads. The platforms have different algorithms, different community standards, and different user expectations. Repurpose the concept but adjust the format, pacing, and language formality for each platform.</Callout>

## Measuring success on Chinese video platforms

Standard Western analytics tools won't capture traffic from Douyin. Use these attribution methods:

**For Douyin-driven tourist traffic:**
- Train staff to ask "How did you hear about us?" and specifically prompt "小红书? 抖音? 微信?" (Xiaohongshu? Douyin? WeChat?)
- Track spikes in bookings or walk-ins following Douyin posts by creators who visited
- Use a unique promo code in any creator partnerships ("抖音8折" = "Douyin 20% off") to measure direct attribution

**For TikTok paid campaigns:**
- Use TikTok Pixel on your booking page or website for conversion tracking
- UTM parameters for any link in bio campaigns
- Monitor follower growth and comment origin — Chinese-language comments indicate diaspora reach

## FAQ

**Do I need a Douyin account separate from TikTok?**
Yes — they're entirely separate apps and networks. Creating a Douyin account requires a Chinese phone number or a WeChat account linked to a Chinese number. Many Chinese diaspora community members can help set this up. Alternatively, focus your owned-channel efforts on TikTok and rely on tourist-generated content for your Douyin presence.

**Can my TikTok videos be seen in China?**
No. TikTok is blocked in mainland China. Chinese users in China access Douyin, not TikTok. Chinese tourists briefly visiting other countries may access TikTok via VPN, but Douyin remains their primary platform.

**What's the fastest way to get Chinese diaspora customers from TikTok?**
Paid TikTok ads targeting Simplified Chinese language speakers in your location, with a bilingual video creative, will show results within 7–10 days. Organic growth takes longer but builds a more sustainable audience.

**Is it worth hiring a Chinese-speaking content creator for our local business?**
If your business is in a high-density Chinese community area, yes — the ROI is strong. A part-time social media creator who is bilingual and understands both TikTok and Douyin culture can produce 3–4 weekly videos for $400–$600/month. For most local businesses, this pays back within 6–8 weeks.

**How do I handle negative Douyin videos about my business?**
Respond calmly and publicly in the comments (in Chinese). Acknowledge the issue, apologise if warranted, and offer a direct resolution ("请私信我们解决此问题" — "Please DM us to resolve this"). Chinese social media users are highly attuned to how businesses handle criticism publicly — a graceful response often generates positive follow-up comments that outweigh the original negative one.

## How to Build a Douyin Content Strategy That Drives Foot Traffic

Douyin's algorithm is a beast—and it's hungry for local content. Unlike TikTok, which tends to push viral content globally, Douyin aggressively surfaces videos that are geographically relevant. For a local business, this is your golden ticket. Here's how to bake location-based content into your strategy.

### Step 1: Optimise your Douyin business profile for location

Set your business location in the app. Douyin allows you to tag a physical address, and when users search for "coffee near me" or "hair salon [your city]," your profile appears in the local results. Ensure your profile description is in Mandarin and includes your address, phone number, and business hours. Add a QR code that links to your WeChat account or a Chinese-friendly booking tool. One coffee shop in Manchester saw a 25% increase in Chinese customer visits within two weeks of updating their Douyin location tag and adding a "欢迎光临" (welcome) banner.

### Step 2: Create hyper-local content that Chinese users will search for

Think about what a Chinese tourist or diaspora customer would type into Douyin's search bar. Examples: "悉尼最好吃的蛋挞" (best egg tarts in Sydney), "墨尔本华人理发店" (Chinese barber in Melbourne), "多伦多宠物美容推荐" (pet grooming recommendation in Toronto). For each keyword, create a short video that answers the query. A pet groomer in Brisbane made a 30-second video titled "布里斯班宠物美容避坑指南" (Brisbane pet grooming guide to avoid pitfalls). It showed their clean studio, friendly staff, and a before-and-after of a poodle. It ranked on the first page of search results and got 3,500 views in a month—all from local Chinese pet owners.

### Step 3: Live-stream to build trust and urgency

Douyin live streaming isn't just for e-commerce—it's a powerful way to show your business in action. A fitness studio in Vancouver went live every Tuesday at 7 PM (China time? No, local time for the diaspora). They offered a 10-minute free workout segment, then a special discount for viewers who booked within the next hour. The live stream had 200–300 viewers, and each session generated 8–15 bookings. The key: engage with comments in real time. Answer questions in Mandarin. One hair salon in New York live-streamed a haircut and asked viewers to vote on the style—engagement soared.

### Step 4: Use Douyin's local challenges and trends

Every week, Douyin has trending hashtags and challenges. Scan the "local" tab for your city. If a challenge like "#悉尼周末好去处" (Sydney weekend fun) is trending, film a 15-second clip of your business showing why it's a perfect weekend spot. For a coffee shop: show a customer sipping a latte with a view. For a pet groomer: show a dog wagging its tail after a grooming session. Use the exact hashtag, add your location, and post during peak hours (11 AM–1 PM or 8–10 PM local time for Chinese users). One fitness studio in London saw a 300% spike in Chinese customer inquiries after participating in a #伦敦健身日常 (London fitness daily) challenge—their video got 20,000 views.

### Step 5: Create a series that builds anticipation

Chinese audiences love episodic content. Start a weekly series: "咖啡知识小课堂" (Coffee Knowledge Class) or "宠物美容小技巧" (Pet Grooming Tips). A hair salon in San Francisco ran a series called "发型改变命运" (Hairstyle Changes Destiny)—each video featured a Chinese customer before and after a haircut, with a fun story. The series had 12 episodes and gained 5,000 followers. When the salon launched a referral program for Chinese customers, 30 new clients came in through word-of-mouth driven by the series.

### Real results: a case study from a coffee shop in Los Angeles

A small artisanal coffee shop in LA's Chinatown wanted to attract more Chinese customers. They followed the above strategy: optimised their Douyin profile, created 10 local-content videos (e.g., "LA唐人街最好喝的拿铁" - best latte in LA's Chinatown), ran a live stream every Saturday offering a 20% discount, and collaborated with a local Chinese food blogger (8,000 followers). In three months, their Douyin account grew from 200 to 4,500 followers—90% of them Chinese users within a 5-mile radius. Foot traffic from Chinese customers increased by 40%. Their average order value from this cohort was $12.50, compared to $8.50 from general foot traffic. The total investment: about $800 (blogger collaboration, live stream setup, a few free drinks). Return: an estimated $6,000 in incremental revenue over the quarter. That's a 7.5x return.

## Measuring Success: KPIs That Actually Tell You If You're Winning

It's easy to get distracted by vanity metrics—likes, shares, views. But when your goal is to get Chinese customers walking through your door, you need different numbers. Here are the KPIs I recommend my clients track.

### KPI 1: Douyin storefront visits and coupon redemptions

If you set up a Douyin storefront (free for business accounts), track how many users visit your storefront page and how many claim a coupon or offer. A pet groomer in Toronto added a "free nail trim with first full groom" coupon—70% of those who claimed it redeemed in-store. That's a direct lead. Measure cost per coupon claim: if you spent $100 on promoting the coupon and got 20 claims, your cost per lead is $5. Compare that to Google Ads or Yelp.

### KPI 2: Offline footfall attributed to Douyin

Set up a unique discount code for Douyin users only. For example, "Show this video for 10% off." Track how many times it's used. A hair salon in Sydney used the code "DOUYIN10" and saw 45 redemptions in a month. They also asked each customer how they heard about the salon—30% said Douyin. Simple but effective.

### KPI 3: WeChat Official Account followers and engagement

Many local businesses use Douyin to drive users to their WeChat Official Account (which allows direct messaging, appointment booking, and push notifications). Track the growth rate. If your Douyin video gets 10,000 views and you gain 100 WeChat followers, that's a 1% conversion rate. Aim for 2–3%. Also track how many WeChat followers book a service—if you have 500 followers and 10 book per month, that's a 2% booking rate. Fine-tune your content to improve.

### KPI 4: Average order value (AOV) from Chinese customers

This is crucial. Chinese customers, whether tourists or diaspora, often spend more per visit than the average customer? Yes, according to a 2025 survey by Nielsen China, Chinese tourists abroad spend 2.3x more than local customers in cafes and restaurants. For a coffee shop, that might mean they buy a pastry and a specialty drink instead of just a drip coffee. Track AOV from customers who mention Douyin or show a coupon. If it's lower than your average, you're attracting bargain hunters—adjust your offer to encourage upsells.

### KPI 5: Engagement rate on Douyin vs TikTok

Calculate engagement (likes, comments, shares) divided by views. On Douyin, a good engagement rate is 5–10% for local content; on TikTok, it's often lower (2–5% due to global competition). If your Douyin engagement is below 3%, your content isn't resonating. Look at comments—are they in Chinese? Are people asking about your location or services? If not, tweak your hook or your cultural references.
