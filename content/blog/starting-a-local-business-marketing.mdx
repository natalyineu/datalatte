Starting a local business is a thrilling venture, but it comes with its fair share of challenges. In today's competitive market, it's not just about providing great products or services – it's also about creating a strong online presence, engaging with your community, and adapting to changing consumer behavior. The good news is that with the right marketing strategies, you can attract and retain a loyal customer base. Here's a comprehensive checklist to get you started.

### 1. Define Your Target Audience

Before creating a marketing strategy, you need to understand who your ideal customer is. Take the time to research your target audience, including their demographics, interests, and pain points. This will help you tailor your marketing efforts to resonate with them.

### Callout: Tip
Don't try to be everything to everyone. Focus on a specific niche or target audience to increase your chances of success.

### 2. Develop a Unique Value Proposition (UVP)

Your UVP is what sets you apart from competitors. It's the unique benefits, features, or services that make your business attractive to customers. Clearly define your UVP and communicate it consistently across all marketing channels.

### Callout: Warning
Don't underestimate the importance of a strong UVP. A weak one can lead to confusion and a lack of differentiation in the market.

### 3. Build a Strong Online Presence

In today's digital age, having a professional website and social media presence is crucial. Ensure your website is mobile-friendly, easy to navigate, and provides a seamless user experience. Use social media platforms to engage with your audience, share updates, and build brand awareness.

### 4. Leverage Local SEO

Search Engine Optimization (SEO) is essential for local businesses. Ensure your website is optimized for local keywords, and claim your Google My Business listing to improve visibility in search results.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Setting Up Google Business Profile Once and Forgetting It

I had a coffee shop owner in Austin, TX email me last year. Let's call her Maria. She opened her shop in 2022, claimed her Google Business Profile herself, and hadn't touched it since. When I pulled up her listing, it had the wrong hours, no photos, and a single review from her mom that read "Great place! Proud of you, sweetie!"

She was spending $1,800 a month on Facebook ads targeting people within 3 miles of her shop. Her cost per lead was $14. She was getting maybe 10 new customers per month from it. She thought that was just "how marketing works."

Here's what was actually happening: When people searched "coffee shop Austin" near her location, Google showed a competitor's listing with 340 reviews, a menu with prices, and photos of their pastries. Maria's listing showed up three spots lower with no useful information. Nobody clicked on it.

**The fix:** We cleaned up her GBP. Updated hours, added 12 photos of the space and products, added a menu with prices, and started responding to every review within 24 hours. I also set up a simple Google Ads campaign for "coffee near me" searches — $600/month, hyperlocal, only during her open hours.

**The outcome:** Within 45 days, her GBP impressions went from 200/month to 4,800/month. Calls from Google Maps doubled. She cancelled the Facebook ads (they were bringing in maybe 3 of the 10 customers she thought). Her total marketing spend dropped from $1,800 to $600. Foot traffic from search increased 40%. That's roughly $2,400/month in additional revenue from a coffee shop with a $6 average ticket. She fired me after three months because she didn't need me anymore. I took it as a compliment.

**The lesson:** Your Google Business Profile is your front door. If it looks like nobody cares, customers assume the same about your shop. Spend 30 minutes a week on it before you spend another dollar on ads.

### Mistake #2: Spraying Social Media Ads at Everyone

A pet grooming business in Nashville contacted me because they were burning $1,200/month on Facebook ads and getting nothing. The owner, James, had given his nephew $500 to "set up the ads" and then just let them run for six months. The nephew targeted "people interested in pets" — which in Facebook's world means about 40 million people.

James was showing his ads to a 55-year-old woman in Miami who follows a cat rescue page. His shop was in East Nashville. He was paying for her click.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the campaign from zero. Set a 5-mile radius around the shop. Excluded anyone outside that area. Targeted interest groups specifically tied to dog ownership — people who follow local dog parks, use the Rover app, or follow specific breed pages. Changed the ad creative from a generic "we groom dogs" image to a photo of a specific poodle mix with a before-and-after haircut. The headline: "Your dog's haircut is overdue. We're 6 minutes from downtown Nashville."

Budget stayed at $1,200/month. But I also added a $50 coupon for first-time customers that required a phone number to redeem.

**The outcome:** First month: 32 new customers booked appointments. Average ticket: $65. That's $2,080 in revenue from new customers alone — plus a 60% return rate within 8 weeks. The second month, word-of-mouth from those 32 customers brought in another 18. Total revenue from the ad spend over 90 days: roughly $7,800. Cost per new customer: $37.50. Compared to the previous $1,200 for zero.

By month four, James had a waitlist and paused the ads entirely.

**The lesson:** Facebook lets you target a zip code and a specific behavior. If you're not using both, you're burning cash for clicks from people who will never walk through your door. Narrow your radius. Nail your offer. Track who actually shows up.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Phone Calls As a Lead Source

I worked with a plumbing company in Portland, OR. The owner, Derek, was 58, had run his business for 20 years, and was convinced the internet was "for the young guys." He spent $800/month on Google Ads, but he was tracking success based on form fills on his website. He was getting about 20 form fills a month. Maybe 5 turned into jobs.

What Derek didn't know: 70% of his leads were coming in through phone calls. Google was giving him a call button on his ads. His website had a click-to-call number. But he had no idea how many calls came from ads versus organic search versus his old van with a phone number painted on the side.

We set up call tracking through Google Ads. It cost $0 for the basic setup — just had to swap his phone number for a Google forwarding number that tracked the source.

**What we found:** He was getting 45 calls per month from his Google Ads alone. Only 12 got answered. The other 33 went to voicemail during business hours — because he was in the field or on another call. Of those 12 answered calls, 10 turned into jobs with an average ticket of $380.

**The fix:** We added a simple call-answering service ($150/month) that took messages when he was unavailable and sent them as text transcripts. We also added a call-back button on his website that queued callers for a return call within 15 minutes.

**The outcome:** Answered calls went from 12 to 38 per month. Jobs per month from ad calls jumped from 10 to 31. That's 21 additional jobs at $380 average — $7,980/month in revenue from leads he was previously losing to voicemail. The $800 ad spend became profitable overnight.

Derek now answers his own calls during work hours. But when he can't, the service catches it. He told me he "should have done this 10 years ago." He's not wrong.

**The lesson:** If your customers are the type to pick up the phone instead of filling out a form (and in trades, they usually are), your marketing is broken if you're not tracking calls. Answer rates of 25% or less are common in service businesses. Every missed call is a job you're handing to a competitor.

## Local SEO: The Free Traffic You're Sitting On

Most small business owners think SEO means writing blog posts about "10 Best Coffee Roasters in Denver" and hoping for the best. That's content marketing. Local SEO is different, and it works faster.

Here's the playbook I've used for clients in Chicago, Denver, and NYC. It's not complicated. But most people skip at least two of these steps.

**Step one: NAP consistency.** Name, Address, Phone number. It needs to be identical on your website, Google Business Profile, Yelp, Facebook, and every other directory. I audited a hair salon in Denver once that had three different phone numbers across Google, Yelp, and their website. One was the owner's personal cell, one was the landline at the front desk, and one was a number nobody answered. Google sees inconsistencies and decides you're unreliable. Fix this in an afternoon. It costs zero dollars.

**Step two: Local citations.** These are mentions of your business on directories like Yelp, Yellow Pages, Nextdoor, and industry-specific sites (Booksy for salons, Thumbtack for services). Each citation is a signal to Google that you're a real business in that location. A pet groomer in Nashville I worked with had zero citations outside of Google. We spent two hours adding her to 15 local directories. Within a month, her "pet grooming near me" ranking went from page 3 to the top of page 1. Foot traffic increased by roughly 30%.

**Step three: Collect reviews at the right moment.** Most business owners ask for a review at checkout or put a link on a receipt. That's too late. The best time is 2–3 days after the service, when the customer is still happy but the initial rush has worn off. For a coffee shop in Austin, we started sending a text message 48 hours after a purchase (via a simple Square integration) that said: "Hey, thanks for stopping by. If you loved your latte, would you leave us a review? Link here." Review volume tripled in 60 days. Average rating went from 4.1 to 4.6.

**Step four: Post to Google Business Profile weekly.** This is the most underused feature. Post a photo of a new menu item. Share a short video of the team. Highlight a customer story. Google prioritizes businesses that post regularly. A fitness studio in Chicago started posting three times a week — class schedules, member transformations, parking tips. Their GBP impressions went from 1,000/month to 8,000/month in 90 days. No ad spend. Just 15 minutes of posting per week.

Local SEO isn't a mystery. It's a series of boring, repetitive tasks that compound over time. Do the NAP fix. Get on directories. Ask for reviews on day 3. Post to GBP every Tuesday and Thursday. Do those four things for three months, and you will outrank 80% of your local competitors who never bothered.

## Email Lists: The Channel Your Competitors Forget About

I get it. Email feels old. You're on Instagram, TikTok, whatever. You watch your competitors post reels and think you need to do the same. Here's the uncomfortable truth: social media algorithms control who sees your content. You could have 10,000 followers and reach 200 of them. Email is the only channel where you own the audience.

A coffee shop in Nashville I worked with had an email list of 800 people. They were collecting emails at the register — a physical sign-up sheet with a pen. Nobody ever sent an email to that list. It sat in a spreadsheet for two years.

We moved the list into Mailchimp. Sent one email per week: a photo of the pastry of the week, the hours for the upcoming weekend, and a "reply to this email to pre-order" option.

**First week:** 42% open rate. 15 people replied to pre-order. Average order: $18. That's $270 from a single email to an existing list of people who already knew the shop. Cost: $0 in ad spend.

We added a prompt at the register: "Want a free latte on your birthday? Give us your email." Sign-ups tripled. Within six months, the list grew to 2,400 people. Revenue from the email list: roughly $1,200/month in repeat sales. The owner said it was "the easiest money" she had ever made.

**The math:** A $29/month Mailchimp account. One hour per week writing the email. That's roughly $100/month in time and software for $1,200 in return. 12x ROI. Show me a Facebook ad that does that consistently.

**What you should do right now:** If you have any email addresses, put them in Mailchimp or a similar tool today. Send one email this week. It doesn't have to be perfect. A photo and a sentence about what you're excited about this week. That's it. The people on that list already chose to hear from you. Give them a reason to show up.

For service businesses like hair salons or pet groomers, use Booksy or Square to automatically send appointment reminders with an upsell offer — "Add a nail trim for $10." A pet groomer in Austin started doing this and added $450/month in add-on services with zero additional effort.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a website? Can't I just use my Google Business Profile and Facebook page?**

No, and here's why: You don't own your Facebook page or your GBP. Google can change the algorithm tomorrow and bury your listing. Facebook can throttle your reach. Your website is the only piece of digital real estate you control completely. A simple one-page site with your address, hours, phone number, menu or price list, and reviews costs about $200 to set up on Squarespace or Wix and $15/month to host. That's cheaper than one month of bad Facebook ads. I've seen a plumbing company in Portland get 30% of their leads from their website after Google changed their local search layout and pushed organic results below ads. They had the site ready. Their competitors with only a Facebook page got crushed.

**Q: How much should I actually spend on marketing each month as a new local business?**

Start at $500/month and treat it like rent — non-negotiable for the first six months. Allocate $200 to Google Ads for hyperlocal search terms, $100 to a simple email tool and a review management platform, and $200 to one specific channel based on your business type. For a hair salon, that might be Instagram ads targeting your zip code. For a plumber, it might be Google Local Services Ads. After 90 days, look at cost per customer acquired and shift budget to the channel that performed best. Most small businesses spend too little to see real results or too much without tracking. $500/month is enough to test with real data.

**Q: Should I be on every social platform? Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn?**

No. Pick one and do it well. If you run a coffee shop or hair salon, Instagram. If you're a plumber or electrician, Facebook. If you're a fitness studio, Instagram or TikTok, depending on your target age group. Post three times per week for 90 days. If you don't get any traction — meaning likes, comments, shares, or actual customer inquiries — switch platforms. The business owners I see fail are the ones trying to maintain six accounts with no real effort on any of them. A yoga studio in Chicago that only used Instagram with 1,200 followers got more new clients than a competitor with 4,000 followers across four platforms. Consistency beats breadth.

**Q: Yelp keeps calling me to advertise. Is it worth it?**

Almost certainly not for a new local business. Yelp's ad model is designed to benefit Yelp, not you. I've audited dozens of local businesses who spent $300–$600/month on Yelp ads. The average return was negative. Yelp's algorithm is notorious for filtering positive reviews from non-active users, which means you're paying to get more traffic to a listing where your best reviews might be hidden. Invest in Google Ads and your Google Business Profile first. If you have extra budget and can prove Yelp drives customers, test it with $100/month for 60 days and track every call. If you don't see at least a 3x return, cancel it.

**Q: How do I get my first 50 reviews without begging customers?**

Ask at the right moment with the right tool. The wrong way: putting a sign at the register that says "Leave us a review!" The right way: send a text or email 48–72 hours after the transaction via Square, Booksy, or a simple tool like Podium. The message should say: "Thanks for visiting [Business Name]. If you had a great experience, would you mind leaving a review? Here's a direct link. If something wasn't right, reply to this message and let me make it up to you." This does two things: happy customers leave reviews, and unhappy customers tell you privately instead of posting publicly. For a coffee shop in Austin, this approach got 30 reviews in 30 days. Average rating: 4.7 stars.

**Q: Can't I just run my business from my personal Facebook page instead of creating a business page?**

You can, but it's a bad idea. Personal pages aren't searchable in the same way. You can't run ads from a personal profile. You can't collect reviews. And if Facebook decides your personal account looks like a business account, they can shut it down without warning. I've seen this happen to a dog groomer in Portland who had 5,000 friends and was booking clients through Messenger. Facebook disabled her account for "commercial activity on a personal profile." She lost every connection. A business page rebuild took her six weeks to recover the same level of bookings. It takes ten minutes to set up a business page. Do it now.
