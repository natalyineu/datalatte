- 97% of consumers search online for local businesses.

A well-structured local SEO audit can help you identify areas for improvement and create a roadmap for success. Let's dive into the process.

### Step 1: Keyword Research

The first step in conducting a local SEO audit is to identify relevant keywords for your business. This involves researching your target audience, competitors, and location-specific keywords. You can use tools like Google Keyword Planner or Ahrefs to find relevant keywords.

For example, if you're a coffee shop in New York City, some relevant keywords might include:

- "coffee shops in NYC"
- "best coffee in Manhattan"
- "coffee near me in NYC"

Use these keywords to create a list of target keywords for your business.

### Step 2: Technical SEO Audit

The next step is to conduct a technical SEO audit of your website. This involves checking for issues such as:

- Mobile responsiveness
- Page speed
- XML sitemap
- Robots.txt file
- Meta tags and descriptions

You can use tools like Google PageSpeed Insights or SEMrush to identify technical issues on your website.

<Callout type="tip">Make sure to check your website's mobile responsiveness, as this is now a key ranking factor.</Callout>

### Step 3: Local SEO Audit

The next step is to conduct a local SEO audit of your business. This involves checking for issues such as:

- Google My Business listing
- Consistency of business name, address, and phone number (NAP) across the web
- Online reviews
- Local citations

You can use tools like Moz Local or Ahrefs to identify local SEO issues on your website.

For example, if you're a pet groomer in Los Angeles, you might want to check the following:

- Is your Google My Business listing complete and up-to-date?
- Are your business hours consistent across the web?
- Do you have a good number of online reviews?

### Step 4: Analyze and Improve

Once you've identified issues on your website, it's time to analyze and improve. This involves creating a plan to address technical SEO issues, improve local SEO, and increase online visibility.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Inconsistent NAP Data Across Directories

**The story:** A pet grooming business in Austin, Texas — let's call them "Aussie Groom" — had three locations across the city. Their Google Business Profile said "(512) 555-0142". Their Yelp page listed a different number that routed to a disconnected line. Facebook showed an old address on Manor Road they'd moved out of two years prior.

I was hired because their calls had dropped 35% over six months. The owner blamed the economy. I blamed the fact that when someone searched "dog groomer Austin" and clicked their listing, the phone number rang a dead line.

**What went wrong:** They'd changed phone providers when they moved, but no one updated the citations. Google detected the inconsistency and stopped showing their profile in local pack results for keywords they'd previously ranked #1 for. The business was paying $800/month for Google Ads to compensate — essentially buying traffic they used to get free.

**The fix:** We audited 47 directories using Moz Local. Found mismatches in 22 of them. Corrected every single one. Then set up a tracking system: any time they updated a phone number, address, or hours, it flowed to all platforms within 48 hours.

**The outcome:** Within four weeks, their local pack rankings returned. Organic calls increased 40% month-over-month. They dropped their Google Ads spend to $300/month. Net savings: $500/month on ads, plus an estimated $1,200/month in new client revenue from organic calls.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Google Business Profile Q&A

**The story:** A hair salon in Nashville, Tennessee — "East Side Cuts" — had a fully filled-out GBP. Photos looked good. Reviews were 4.7 stars. But they weren't answering Q&A questions.

A prospective client asked: "Do you do balayage on short hair?" The question sat unanswered for three months. Another asked: "What's the price for a wash and trim?" No response. Meanwhile, a competitor two blocks away had answered their Q&A within 24 hours.

**What went wrong:** Google treats unanswered Q&As as a signal that the business isn't actively managed. It also means future customers see stale, unanswered questions and assume the salon doesn't care. The owner told me, "We didn't even know that section existed."

**The fix:** I answered every outstanding question — even the old ones. Set up a weekly reminder to check GBP and respond to new questions within 24 hours. Also pre-loaded 12 common questions with answers (parking, cancellation policy, whether they do extensions, etc.). That way, when Google prompts users with "Frequently asked questions," the salon already has the answer ready.

**The outcome:** Two weeks after clearing the backlog, the salon saw a 25% increase in "bookings from Google" (they use Booksy, so we could track the source). The owner estimated $3,800 in additional revenue the following month from bookings that started with a GBP Q&A interaction.

### Mistake #3: Using the Same Content for Multiple Locations

**The story:** A coffee roaster in Portland, Oregon — "Southeast Roast Co." — opened a second location in the Pearl District. Their marketing person copied the exact same GBP description, same photos, and same category from the original location. Google couldn't tell the difference between the two businesses.

**What went wrong:** Google flagged both profiles as duplicates. Neither ranked well for "coffee shop Portland" because the algorithm couldn't determine which location was which. The new location got zero organic traffic for the first three months. They spent $1,500 on local flyers and social ads trying to compensate.

**The fix:** We rewrote both profiles from scratch. Pearl District location got neighborhood-specific keywords ("near Powell's Books," "walk from the streetcar"). Southeast location got different content focused on their roastery tours. We uploaded unique photos for each — no cross-over. Added separate phone numbers and website landing pages.

**The outcome:** Within six weeks, both locations appeared in local pack results for their respective neighborhoods. The Pearl District location started getting 15–20 organic calls per week. The owner canceled the flyer campaign. Saved $1,500/month in wasted print.

### Mistake #4: Only Tracking Keyword Rankings, Not Actual Revenue

**The story:** A fitness studio in Denver, Colorado — "Highline Strength Lab" — was obsessed with keyword rankings. They celebrated hitting #1 for "Denver personal trainer." But class bookings were flat. The owner couldn't figure out why.

**What went wrong:** They were ranking for a broad keyword that attracted people looking for personal trainers at $30/session. Highline Strength Lab charged $120/session. The traffic was high, but the conversion rate was 2%. They were spending $400/month on SEO tools and $600/month on content, attracting the wrong audience.

**The fix:** We shifted their strategy to long-tail, high-intent keywords: "powerlifting coach Denver Cherry Creek" and "small group strength training Denver." We also started tracking calls with a Google Voice number specifically for the GBP listing. Then we monitored which calls resulted in booked sessions.

**The outcome:** Keyword volume dropped 60%. Revenue from organic search went up 35%. The studio booked $2,500 more in monthly revenue from better-matched traffic. They stopped paying for the expensive SEO tool and reallocated that $400/month to targeted local ads for their actual services.

## How to Audit Your Google Business Profile Like Someone Who's Done This 50 Times

Most local SEO advice treats a GBP audit like a checklist: "Fill out your hours. Add photos. Respond to reviews." That's fine if you want to be average.

Here's what I actually check when I audit a client's profile. And yes, I've seen each of these issues cost real money.

**Category selection is wrong.** Not just "close enough." Wrong. I audited a dental practice in Chicago that had "General Dentist" as their primary category. That's correct. But they'd chosen "Cosmetic Dentist" as secondary when they should have picked "Orthodontist" because 70% of their revenue came from Invisalign. We changed it. Their impressions for "Invisalign Chicago" doubled in three weeks. The practice added $4,200 in new patient revenue that month. One dropdown menu change.

**Services list is incomplete or incorrect.** Google now shows services directly in the search results. If you offer "curly hair cutting" but it's not in your services list, people searching for "curly hair salon Austin" won't see you. A salon in Austin added six missing services to their GBP list. They started appearing in local pack for "curly hair specialist Austin" within two weeks. First month: $1,800 in new bookings attributed to those terms.

**You're not using appointment links.** If you take bookings through a platform like Square Appointments, Booksy, or Vagaro, link it in your GBP. Google prioritizes businesses that let users book directly from the search results. A barber shop in Portland connected their Booksy link. Their booking rate from GBP increased 40%. That's not a vanity metric — that's paying clients.

**You're not posting.** GBP posts are basically free local ads. You get to show up in the search results with a call to action. A pet groomer in Denver posted weekly — "Wednesday walk-in special," "Senior dog discount," "We now do cat nails." They got 15,000 extra impressions over three months. The owner said it brought in "at least $600/month" in new business. Cost: 10 minutes per week.

**The review response strategy is backward.** Most business owners respond only to bad reviews. Google actually weights engagement on all reviews. Respond to the good ones too. Keep it short: "Thanks, Sarah! See you next month." That signals to Google that you're actively managing the profile. A coffee shop in Nashville responded to every review for eight weeks. Their local pack ranking improved from position 7 to position 3. No other changes.

## What to Track After Your Audit (Because Rankings Don't Pay Rent)

A local SEO audit is useless if you don't track whether the changes actually made money. I've seen too many small business owners celebrate ranking #1 while wondering why their revenue is flat.

Here's what I track for every client. No dashboards. No vanity metrics.

**Call tracking.** Use a Google Voice number or a service like CallRail. Put that number on your GBP listing. Track every call that comes in. Ask: "How did you find us?" The answer "Google" tells you nothing. The answer "I searched for bakery in Austin" tells you exactly what's working.

A bakery in Chicago did this. They discovered that 60% of their GBP calls were asking about custom cakes — a service they barely promoted. They added "Custom Cakes" to their GBP services list and created a dedicated page on their site. Three months later: $2,300/month in custom cake orders from that single change.

**Booking source tracking.** If you use Square Appointments or Booksy, tag the booking source. Most platforms let you add UTM parameters or manual tracking fields. A hair salon in NYC spent $500/month on Instagram ads. Their booking software showed exactly two bookings came from Instagram that month. They reallocated that $500 to GBP optimization. Next month: seventeen bookings from Google.

**Direction requests.** Google Business Profile shows you how many people asked for directions to your business. This is a leading indicator. If direction requests are up but sales are flat, something is wrong — maybe your hours are wrong, or your phone number doesn't work. If direction requests are down, your visibility is likely slipping.

A fitness studio in Portland watched their direction requests drop 30% over three months. They thought it was seasonal. We checked their GBP: someone had accidentally removed their "open" hours for Saturdays. Fixed it. Direction requests returned to normal within two weeks. The owner estimated they lost $1,500 in missed trial class signups during that period.

**The only number that matters.** At the end of the month, did your revenue from local search increase or decrease? If you can't answer that question, your audit isn't complete. Everything else is noise.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need to claim my Google Business Profile if I'm already getting customers through word of mouth?**

Yes, and here's why: 76% of people who search for a local business visit within 24 hours. If someone recommends your coffee shop but your GBP listing has the wrong hours, they show up at 8 AM on a Sunday when you open at 10 AM. They leave. They don't come back. One wrong detail can undo years of word-of-mouth marketing. Claiming your profile isn't optional — it's the single cheapest insurance policy you can buy for your reputation.

**Q: How long until I see results from a local SEO audit?**

Usually 4–8 weeks for noticeable changes, but it depends on what you fix. Inconsistent NAP data gets fixed fast — Google recalculates your trust score within a couple of weeks. Adding missing services or correcting your category can show results in 2–3 weeks. Building review volume takes longer. The fastest I've seen was a coffee shop in Austin: fixed their GBP description on a Tuesday, saw a 40% jump in calls by Friday. That's not typical, but it happens when you've been sitting on a broken listing for months.

**Q: Can I do this myself without hiring someone?**

You can, but you probably won't. Not because you're not capable — because running a business takes all your time. I've had clients who said they'd "definitely get to it" and then didn't. Six months later, their GBP still had the wrong hours. If you have 5–10 hours per week to dedicate to local SEO, you can absolutely do it yourself. Most business owners don't. That's not a criticism — it's reality. If your time is worth more than your money, hire someone.

**Q: What's the single most important thing I should fix first?**

Your Google Business Profile. Not your website, not your Yelp page, not your Facebook. Google owns local search. If your GBP is incomplete, inconsistent, or unmanaged, nothing else matters. I've seen a dentist in Denver get 90% of their new patients from GBP alone. Their website was a Wix template from 2018 with broken links. Didn't matter. The GBP was perfect. Fill out every field. Add real photos (not stock). Respond to every review. If you do one thing, do that.

**Q: Is Yelp actually worth dealing with?**

It depends on your industry. For restaurants, hair salons, dentists, and plumbers, Yelp drives significant traffic. For other businesses, it's marginal. But you can't ignore it because your customers might leave reviews there regardless. At minimum, claim your Yelp page, make sure the NAP data matches your GBP, and respond to reviews. Do not pay for Yelp ads unless you have a clear way to track ROI. I've seen businesses waste $2,000/month on Yelp ads that generated exactly three calls. I've also seen a moving company in Chicago spend $500/month and get $8,000 in revenue. Test small before committing.

**Q: Will changing my phone number or address hurt my rankings temporarily?**

Yes, but the damage from keeping incorrect information is worse. When you change your NAP data, Google needs to re-verify your listing. You may see a dip in rankings for 2–4 weeks. But an incorrect listing will slowly erode your rankings over months. The fix: update your GBP first, then update every other directory. Use Moz Local or BrightLocal to push changes out. If possible, keep your old number active and forward it for 3–6 months. I've helped clients through this transition dozens of times. Every single one recovered within a month and was in a better position than before.
