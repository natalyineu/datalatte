## What is a Social Media Audit?
A social media audit is a thorough examination of your social media presence, including your profiles, content, engagement, and analytics. It helps you understand what's working and what's not, and identifies areas for improvement. By conducting a social media audit with Google Sheets, you can get a clear picture of your social media performance and make data-driven decisions to improve it. For example, a coffee shop in New York City used a social media audit to identify that their Instagram account was driving the most sales, and adjusted their marketing strategy accordingly.

## How to Conduct a Social Media Audit with Google Sheets
Conducting a social media audit with Google Sheets is easier than you think. Here are the steps to follow:
* Gather all your social media data, including your profiles, content, engagement, and analytics
* Set up a Google Sheet to track your data, using templates or formulas to make it easier
* Analyze your data, looking for trends and patterns that can help you improve your social media presence
* Use your findings to adjust your social media strategy, including your content, engagement, and advertising

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Posting Everything Everywhere

**The story:** A coffee shop in Austin, Texas called Brew & Bloom had six social media accounts — Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, Pinterest, X (formerly Twitter), and a barely-used YouTube channel. The owner, Rachel, was spending 12 hours a week creating and scheduling content across all of them. She was exhausted, her espresso machine was neglected, and none of the accounts had grown in six months.

**What went wrong:** She was chasing platform presence instead of platform performance. The Pinterest account had 47 followers after two years. The X account was just auto-posting Instagram links with zero engagement. Meanwhile, her Instagram (where her actual customers were) got maybe one post per week, poorly timed, with no Stories.

**The fix:** We cut everything except Instagram and Facebook. I had her run a simple audit in Google Sheets — list every platform, note follower count, weekly engagement, and estimated time spent. Then I asked one question: "Where have you actually had a customer say they found you?" Answer: Instagram (14 customers that month) and Facebook (5 customers via events). The rest? Zero.

**The outcome:** Rachel dropped five platforms. She now posts three times per week on Instagram with actual photos of her pastries, runs one Facebook event per week for live music nights, and spends the saved 8 hours per week on her actual business. Her Instagram engagement went up 40% in two months because the content quality improved. Revenue from social media referrals went from roughly $800/month to $2,100/month. She also stopped resenting her phone.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Negative Comments and Reviews

**The story:** A hair salon in Nashville called The Velvet Chair had a steady flow of clients but noticed appointments dropping over three months. The owner, Marcus, was focused on posting beautiful haircut photos but had completely stopped checking Facebook reviews and Instagram comments. A disgruntled ex-client had left a detailed review claiming Marcus "rushed through her appointment and charged extra for toner." The review sat there for six weeks with no response. Then three more negative reviews appeared from people who saw the first one and decided to share similar experiences.

**What went wrong:** Marcus thought a social media audit meant counting likes and followers. He never included review monitoring in the process. The negative reviews were visible to anyone searching his salon, and potential clients were clicking away before booking.

**The fix:** During the audit, we added a "Review Health" tab to the Google Sheet. We listed every platform with reviews (Google Business Profile, Facebook, Yelp) and tracked: total reviews, average rating, number of unresolved negative reviews, and average response time. Marcus committed to responding to every review within 48 hours — good or bad. For the toner review, he publicly apologized, explained the miscommunication, and offered a free adjustment appointment.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, the negative review was updated by the client to a 4-star review with a note about great customer service. Two new 5-star reviews came in from clients who saw the exchange and appreciated the transparency. Appointments recovered over six weeks, and the salon added roughly $1,500/month in new client revenue. Marcus also discovered that Yelp was driving more bookings than Instagram — something he'd completely missed because he never looked at the data side by side.

### Mistake #3: Treating All Social Media Like a Billboard

**The story:** A pet grooming business in Portland, Oregon called Furry & Fresh was posting daily — but every post was a promotion. "20% off nail trims!" "Book your holiday grooming now!" "$10 off first visit!" The owner, Jenna, had read somewhere that you need to post every day, and she figured sales pitches would drive appointments. Instead, her engagement rate dropped to 0.4%, and her reach on Facebook plummeted. She was essentially shouting into an empty room.

**What went wrong:** Jenna's content strategy was 100% promotional. She never posted behind-the-scenes photos, never showed her team, never shared tips for at-home grooming between visits. The algorithm read her content as low-value and stopped showing it. Her existing clients didn't engage because they already knew about the services. New people never saw the posts at all.

**The fix:** During the audit, we reviewed her last 30 posts in Google Sheets. We categorized each as: promotional, educational, entertaining, or community-building. The split was 30 promotional, zero anything else. We rebuilt the content mix to 40% educational (how to brush your dog's teeth, signs of matting), 30% community (photos of dogs mid-groom, staff introductions), 20% entertaining (blooper reels, transformation videos), and 10% promotional. The promotional posts were reserved for actual limited-time offers, not just generic discounts.

**The outcome:** Within six weeks, Facebook reach tripled. Engagement rate went from 0.4% to 3.1%. But the real metric: appointment bookings from social media went from inconsistent to reliable — roughly $2,400/month in groomings booked through Instagram and Facebook links. Jenna also noticed clients showing up saying "I saw that video of the poodle who hated bath time" — she was now part of their daily feed, not an interruption.

## How to Turn Audit Data Into an Actual Content Calendar

Most audits stop at "here's what's happening." Then nothing changes. The uncomfortable truth is that a spreadsheet full of numbers doesn't make your coffee shop more popular. You need to turn those findings into a calendar you'll actually use.

Here's the process I've used with clients from Chicago to Denver. After you've completed your audit in Google Sheets, create a new tab called "Content Plan." Set up columns for: Date, Platform, Content Type, Topic, Goal (awareness/engagement/conversion), and a checkbox for "Posted."

Now — and this is where most guides skip the useful part — actually look at your audit data to decide what to post. If your audit showed that Instagram Reels get three times the engagement of static photos, your calendar should have more Reels. Not "balance." Not "diversifying." More Reels. If Facebook drives the majority of your bookings, post your promotional content there first, not on TikTok where nobody is buying.

For a pet groomer in Denver I worked with, the audit revealed that posts featuring before-and-after photos got 5x the saves and 8x the comments compared to any other content type. So for her calendar, I scheduled three before-and-after posts per week, one educational post about seasonal grooming needs, and one "day in the life" post. That's it. Five posts per week, all backed by data from her own accounts.

The calendar also needs to account for time. If you're a salon owner and you have 30 minutes per day for social media, do not plan a TikTok dance trend that takes two hours to shoot. Plan a photo of a fresh haircut (3 minutes), a quick caption (5 minutes), and responding to comments (10 minutes). The rest of your 30 minutes goes to reviewing your audit sheet to see what's working.

One trick I've stolen from agency life: every Monday, look at your Content Plan tab and cross out anything that doesn't serve a clear goal from your audit. If you don't know why you're posting it, don't post it. You'll cut 30% of your planned content immediately and your engagement will go up.

## Using Google Sheets to Track Social Media ROI Back to Actual Revenue

This is where the audit stops being theoretical and starts paying for itself. The core question every small business owner should ask: "Did this Instagram post actually put money in my register?"

Most social media tools will tell you impressions and engagement. They will not tell you if someone walked into your Austin coffee shop and ordered a latte because they saw your Reel about holiday flavors. That requires a different setup — and yes, you can do it in Google Sheets.

Step one: Set up a "Revenue Sources" tab in your audit sheet. Create columns for: Date, Customer Name, Service/Product Purchased, Revenue Amount, How They Found You (Instagram, Facebook, Google, Yelp, Walk-in, Referral), and Notes.

Step two: This is the part that requires a little discipline. Every time a customer mentions social media — or you can reasonably attribute the visit — write it down. At my friend's salon in Chicago, the receptionist asks every new client: "How did you hear about us?" They track this in a simple Google Form that feeds directly into the sheet. Takes five seconds per client.

Step three: Once you have two to three months of this data, start cross-referencing with your audit. Let's say your audit shows Instagram has 1,200 followers and 3% engagement, but your Revenue Sources tab shows that only two clients in the last month came from Instagram, worth $180 total. Meanwhile, Yelp has 200 followers and zero engagement on posts, but drove 14 clients worth $1,300. Your audit just told you the truth: Yelp is your highest-ROI channel, not Instagram.

I saw this exact pattern with a fitness studio in Denver. The owner was obsessed with growing her Instagram following — posting daily Stories, investing in Reels, the whole game. Her audit showed strong Instagram metrics. But when we added the revenue tracking, we found that 80% of her new clients came from Google searches and Yelp. She was spending 10 hours per week on Instagram for roughly $400/month in new business, and ignoring Google Business Profile and Yelp altogether.

We shifted her time: one hour per week on Instagram, two hours on responding to Google and Yelp reviews, and two hours on a simple Google Ads campaign targeting "Denver fitness studio [neighborhood name]." The Google Ads cost $300/month. Within six weeks, she was getting roughly $2,400/month in new client revenue from that campaign. The review management brought in another $900/month from people who chose her because she responded professionally to reviews.

The Google Sheets audit gave her the numbers. The Revenue Sources tab told her where to actually spend her time and money. That is the entire point.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often do I actually need to do this audit? I'm busy running a business.**

Full audit — the kind where you dig into every platform, check reviews, and update your Revenue Sources tab — once per quarter. That's four times a year. But you should spend 15 minutes every Monday morning updating your Content Plan tab and checking your engagement numbers. If something drops significantly, you catch it in a week, not three months.

**Q: Can't I just use the analytics inside Instagram or Facebook? Why do I need a separate sheet?**

You can absolutely use platform analytics. The problem is that Instagram tells you about Instagram. Facebook tells you about Facebook. Neither tells you how they compare to each other, and neither tells you which one actually drove a customer to your shop. A single Google Sheet lets you see everything side by side and connect it to actual revenue. I've never met a business owner who regretted having clearer data. I've met plenty who regretted flying blind.

**Q: What if my social media doesn't drive direct bookings? I'm a coffee shop — people just walk in.**

Then track what they order. If someone walks in and says "I saw your cold brew post on Instagram," that's a data point. Ask your barista to note it on the receipt or keep a small notebook by the register. Even if it's only 10% of your customers, that 10% tells you which platform is actually working. A coffee shop in NYC did this for two months and discovered that their TikTok posts about seasonal drinks drove three times more new customers than Instagram, even though Instagram had more followers. They shifted their content strategy and saw a measurable lift in seasonal drink sales.

**Q: How do I handle platforms I don't use anymore? Should I delete them?**

If you have an abandoned Pinterest account with 12 followers and no posts since 2019, delete it or at least make it private. An abandoned profile looks worse than no profile. When a potential customer searches for your business, an empty X account with a generic header photo signals "this business doesn't care about details." The one exception: if the platform has your business name and you can't delete it (some legacy accounts), update the profile to say "We're most active on Instagram and Facebook — find us there" with links.

**Q: I don't have time to respond to every review. Is it really that important?**

Yes, and it's not close. A study from Harvard Business Review found that a one-star increase in Yelp rating leads to a 5-9% increase in revenue. But the real number that matters: businesses that respond to reviews see 33% more engagement on their profiles. For a salon or pet groomer, that's the difference between someone clicking "Book Now" or scrolling to the next option. You don't need to write novels. A simple "Thank you, Sarah — so glad you loved the cut! Hope to see you again" takes 30 seconds and directly impacts whether the next person books.

**Q: What's the one thing I should track that most people ignore?**

The cost of your time. Every hour you spend on social media is an hour you're not doing something else for your business — making coffee, cutting hair, grooming dogs, training clients. I've seen owners spend 8 hours per week on content that generates $200 in revenue. That's $12.50 per hour. If you tracked that, would you still do it? Put a dollar value on your time in your audit sheet. It changes every decision.
