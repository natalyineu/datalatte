Here are 7 clear signs that your local business website needs a redesign:

### 1. Your website is outdated

Is your website still using Flash or has a outdated design? If so, it's time to update. Not only will this improve the user experience, but it will also make your website more accessible on mobile devices.

### 2. You're not getting enough online visibility

Are you not showing up in search engine results or getting few online reviews? A well-designed website can help improve your online visibility and attract more customers.

### 3. Your website is slow or unresponsive

If your website takes too long to load or is unresponsive on mobile devices, users will quickly lose patience and leave. This can lead to a decline in sales and revenue.

### 4. Your website is not user-friendly

Is your website easy to navigate or is it cluttered with too much information? A user-friendly website is essential for converting visitors into customers.

### 5. You're not using local SEO best practices

Are you not optimizing your website for local search? This can make it harder for potential customers to find you online.

### 6. You're not tracking website analytics

Are you not tracking your website's performance or understanding your audience? This can make it difficult to make informed decisions about your business.

### 7. Your website is not mobile-friendly

Is your website not optimized for mobile devices? This can lead to a poor user experience and a decline in sales.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched local business owners burn thousands of dollars on website redesigns that actually hurt their revenue. Here are the mistakes I see most often — and what to do instead.

### Mistake 1: Copying a Competitor's Design Without Understanding Why It Works

**The story:** A coffee shop in Austin, Texas — let's call it "Third Wave Brew" — spent $4,200 on a website that looked exactly like their main competitor two blocks away. Same layout. Same color palette. Same "about us" structure.

**What went wrong:** The competitor had been in business for 12 years with 800+ Google reviews and a built-in reputation. Third Wave Brew had been open for 8 months. Their copycat site had no reviews, no social proof, and no mention of what made them different. The competitor's design worked because their brand was already established. Third Wave Brew's version looked like a knockoff — because it was.

**The fix:** I walked them through pulling their own Google Business Profile data. Their customers were actually coming in for two things the competitor couldn't offer: cold brew on tap and a dedicated workspace with outlets. We rebuilt the site around those differentiators. Homepage headline changed from "Austin's Best Coffee" (generic) to "Cold Brew on Tap + Workspace Wi-Fi That Actually Works" (specific).

**The outcome:** Their bounce rate dropped from 72% to 41% in six weeks. Online orders increased by $1,800 per month. That $4,200 redesign cost them two months of potential revenue they'll never get back.

**What to do instead:** Before you even open a design tool, pull your own data. What are customers actually searching for to find you? What do they mention in your reviews? Design around your actual differentiators, not someone else's.

### Mistake 2: Prioritizing How the Site Looks Over Whether It Works

**The story:** A pet grooming business in Denver — "Paws & Claws" — hired a freelance designer who made them a beautiful, image-heavy site. Full-screen hero video of dogs getting baths. Custom illustration of a golden retriever. Smooth scroll animations. Total cost: $3,800.

**What went wrong:** The booking button was hidden behind a hamburger menu in the top right corner. The phone number was only in the footer, in 11pt gray text on a dark background. Their contact page had a form that took six field inputs before submitting — on mobile, that meant scrolling past three decorative images to get there. Their most frequent customer action (calling to book) required two extra clicks they didn't need.

**The fix:** We moved the phone number to the top of every page in 18pt bold text. Replaced the hamburger menu with a persistent "Book Now" button that stayed visible while scrolling. Cut the contact form from six fields to three. Added a click-to-call button that worked on mobile.

**The outcome:** Phone calls increased by 300% in the first month. Online bookings went from 8 per week to 31 per week. Total monthly revenue from website-driven appointments: $5,400. The original design cost them at least three months of those numbers.

**What to do instead:** Before you approve any design, test three things on your phone: (1) Can you find the phone number in under three seconds? (2) Can you complete the primary action (book, order, call) in under 30 seconds? (3) Does anything require two taps when one tap would do? If the answer to any is no, the design is broken regardless of how good it looks.

### Mistake 3: Building a "Set It and Forget It" Website

**The story:** A family dentistry practice in Portland, Oregon — "Pearl District Dental" — launched a new site in 2021. Cost: $6,500 including a year of hosting. The owner assumed that was done for the next five years.

**What went wrong:** Their 2021 site had a "COVID-19 Safety Protocols" banner at the top that still mentioned mask requirements — in 2024. Their service list included "Teeth Whitening" with a price of $350 that they'd actually raised to $500 in 2022. Their "Special Offers" page advertised a new patient discount that expired 18 months ago. Google noticed the site hadn't been updated and dropped their local pack ranking from position 3 to position 9.

**The fix:** We set up a quarterly review calendar. Every three months, update pricing, remove expired promotions, and add any new services. We also added a blog section (two posts per month, written by a dental hygienist on staff) about basic oral care tips. Total ongoing effort: about 2 hours per month.

**The outcome:** Local pack ranking returned to position 3 within two months. New patient bookings increased 40% year-over-year. The owner said the biggest surprise was how many patients mentioned the blog posts during appointments. Annual cost of maintenance: about $1,200. Annual revenue from new patients acquired through the site: roughly $38,000.

**What to do instead:** Treat your website like a piece of equipment you maintain, not a billboard you hang once. Schedule two hours per month to update pricing, remove old content, and add something new. If you won't do that, budget for someone else to.

## What a Redesign Should Actually Cost — And Why You Shouldn't Go Cheap

I get asked about pricing constantly. Here's the honest breakdown based on what I've seen work for local US businesses.

A functional redesign for a single-location business with 5-10 pages should cost somewhere between $3,000 and $8,000. Less than that and you're probably getting a template with your logo slapped on it. More than that and someone's probably selling you features you don't need.

Here's what that $3,000-$8,000 should include:

- A homepage that clearly states what you do, who you serve, and what makes you different (about 40% of your visitors will leave if they can't figure this out in 5 seconds)
- Service/product pages that answer the questions people actually search for
- A contact page with your phone number, address, hours, and a short form
- Google Business Profile integration (reviews showing on the site drives trust)
- Mobile-first design that actually works on a phone
- Basic SEO setup (title tags, meta descriptions, proper heading structure)
- Google Analytics and Google Search Console setup

What you don't need at this stage:

- Custom animation or parallax scrolling (adds cost, hurts load time)
- A blog if you won't update it (a blog with three posts from 2019 is worse than no blog)
- An e-commerce system if you don't sell products online
- Custom illustration or photography (stock photos that match your vibe work fine)

I worked with a hair salon in Chicago that paid $22,000 for a website from an agency that specialized in e-commerce brands. The site had a custom booking system that didn't integrate with Booksy (which they already used), three landing pages they'd never fill, and a photo gallery with 200 images that took 12 seconds to load. Their old $800 Squarespace site had been getting more bookings.

**The real cost of a bad redesign isn't the money you spend. It's the customers you lose while the new site performs worse than the old one.** I've seen businesses lose $1,200-$3,000 per month in revenue for 4-6 months after a poorly executed redesign. That $22,000 site probably cost that salon about $10,000 in lost bookings before they switched back.

## What to Do With Your Current Site (Don't Delete It Yet)

Here's something most redesign guides skip: what happens to your old site while the new one is being built.

If you delete your old website on Friday and launch your new one on Monday, you'll lose any search ranking your old site had. Even a temporary 404 error can drop a local business from Google's local pack for weeks.

**The process that actually works:**

Step 1: Keep your old site live while the new one is being built. Don't touch it. Don't redirect anything yet.

Step 2: Build the new site on a subdomain like `new.yourbusiness.com` or a staging URL that Google can't index. Most hosting providers offer this. If yours doesn't, get one that does.

Step 3: Before launch, map every URL from your old site to the corresponding URL on the new site. If your old site had `yourbusiness.com/services` and the new one has `yourbusiness.com/what-we-do`, you need a 301 redirect telling Google and visitors where to find the page.

Step 4: Submit the new sitemap to Google Search Console. Request indexing of your most important pages.

Step 5: Monitor Google Search Console for crawl errors for at least two weeks after launch. Every time Google tries to access an old URL and can't find it, you lose a little ranking.

A yoga studio in Nashville I worked with lost their entire local pack ranking for three months because they launched a new site without redirects. Their traffic dropped by 65%. They went from 45 new student inquiries per month to 12. That's roughly $2,400 per month in lost revenue from intro class packages. Three months of that equals $7,200 — more than the redesign itself cost.

**The fix took one afternoon:** We set up the redirects, submitted the new sitemap, and three weeks later they were back to their old rankings. But those three months of lost traffic were gone forever.

## Don't Forget Your Google Business Profile

Your website and your Google Business Profile work together. One of the fastest ways to get more traffic from a redesign is to make sure they're consistent.

I checked 12 local business websites last week. Seven of them had different hours listed on their site than on their Google Business Profile. Two had different phone numbers. One had a completely different address.

Google notices this inconsistency and penalizes both the site and the profile in local search results. It's not a huge penalty, but in a competitive market, "slightly worse" is enough to drop from position 3 to position 7.

**What to check during your redesign:**

- Hours of operation (exact match)
- Phone number (same formatting)
- Address (exact match, including suite numbers)
- Services or categories (match the language)
- Photos (yes, the platform you used for Google photos should match your site's aesthetic)

A pet groomer in Austin told me she gained 15% more bookings just from matching her hours across all platforms. That's free traffic she was leaving on the table.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can't I just use a template from Squarespace or Wix and save the money?**

Yes, if you have the time and willingness to learn the platform. The problem isn't the template — it's that most business owners don't have 15 hours to set up a proper site, another 5 hours to learn basic SEO, and another 3 hours per month to maintain it. I've seen Wix sites that outperform $8,000 custom builds because the owner took the time to optimize them. I've also seen $4,000 "DIY" sites that cost more in lost revenue than a professional would have charged. If you have the time, use a template. If you don't, hire someone.

**Q: How long should the redesign process actually take?**

For a 5-10 page local business site, 3-4 weeks is reasonable. Week 1: planning and content review. Week 2: design and initial build. Week 3: populate content and integrate any tools (Google Analytics, Booking, etc.). Week 4: testing, redirects, launch. If someone tells you they need 8 weeks for a basic local site, they're either overwhelmed or they're padding the timeline. If someone promises 5 days, they're cutting corners and you'll discover the problems after launch.

**Q: Will I lose my Google rankings during the redesign?**

You will lose rankings if you don't set up proper 301 redirects. You will also lose rankings if you change your URL structure without redirecting old URLs. You will not lose rankings if you handle the migration correctly. I've launched 60+ redesigned sites. The ones that lost rankings made one of two mistakes: no redirects or changed page content so dramatically that Google didn't recognize the pages anymore.

**Q: Do I really need a blog on my local business website?**

No. A blog that you update once every six months is worse than no blog at all. It signals to Google that your site isn't actively maintained. If you can commit to at least one post per month that answers a question your customers actually ask (not "5 Tips for Brushing Your Teeth" but "How Much Does a Dental Crown Cost Without Insurance"), then yes, it helps. If you won't update it, skip it and focus on keeping your service pages and hours current.

**Q: What about a FAQ page? Do those actually help?**

Yes, but only if the questions are real. I worked with an HVAC company in Denver whose FAQ page had questions like "What is the history of air conditioning?" No one was searching for that. We replaced it with questions from their actual service calls: "How much does a new furnace cost?" "Do you charge for estimates?" "What brands do you service?" That page started ranking for local search queries within six weeks and brought in about $800 per month in new service calls. Use your actual customer interactions to build your FAQ, not generic questions from competitor sites.

**Q: Can I redesign my site and still keep my old content for SEO?**

Yes, and this is often the smartest move. If your old site had pages that were ranking well, keep the content and improve it. Update the design around it. Don't rewrite content that's already working. I had a dentist in Portland who had a page about "how much Invisalign costs" that was ranking on page 1 for that exact search term. The redesign team wanted to rewrite it because it "didn't match the new design tone." I told them to leave it alone. We updated one sentence about pricing and moved on. That page still brings in 40-60 visitors per month and generates about $1,500 in consultations. If it isn't broken, don't fix it.
