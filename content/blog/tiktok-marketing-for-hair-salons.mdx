As a small business owner, you're likely wondering how to get started with TikTok marketing. Don't worry, we've got you covered. Here are the top TikTok marketing strategies for hair salons to attract new clients:

### Creating Engaging Content

When it comes to TikTok, content is king. To stand out from the competition, you need to create content that's both visually appealing and engaging. Here are some tips to get you started:

* Use high-quality visuals: Invest in a good camera and editing software to create high-quality videos that showcase your salon's services and style.
* Keep it short and sweet: TikTok users have a short attention span, so keep your videos concise and to the point.
* Use catchy music: Music plays a huge role in making TikTok videos memorable. Choose a catchy tune that complements your content.
* Be authentic: Don't try to be someone you're not. Be authentic and genuine in your content, and your audience will respond positively.

### Using Hashtags

Hashtags are a great way to increase the visibility of your TikTok videos. Here are some tips to use hashtags effectively:

* Research relevant hashtags: Use tools like Hashtagify or RiteTag to research relevant hashtags in your industry.
* Use a mix of niche and broad hashtags: Using a mix of niche and broad hashtags can help you reach a wider audience.
* Don't overdo it: Using too many hashtags can look spammy. Stick to 3-5 relevant hashtags per video.
* Create a unique hashtag: Create a unique hashtag for your salon and encourage your clients to use it when sharing their experiences.

### Collaborating with Influencers

Influencer marketing is a great way to reach a wider audience and build credibility for your salon. Here are some tips to collaborate with influencers effectively:

* Research relevant influencers: Use tools like Influencer Marketing Hub or AspireIQ to research relevant influencers in your industry.
* Reach out to influencers: Don't be afraid to reach out to influencers and propose a collaboration.
* Be clear about your goals: Make sure you're clear about your goals and what you're looking for from the collaboration.
* Track your results: Use analytics tools to track your results and see what's working and what's not.

### Running a Contest

Running a contest is a great way to increase engagement and attract new clients to your salon. Here are some tips to run a contest effectively:

* Choose a relevant prize: Choose a prize that's relevant to your salon and will appeal to your target audience.
* Make it easy to enter: Make it easy for people to enter the contest by providing clear instructions and a simple entry process.
* Promote the contest: Promote the contest across all your social media channels and encourage your followers to share it with their friends and family.
* Follow up: Follow up with the winner and provide them with the prize as promised.

<Callout type="tip">Make sure to clearly outline the rules and regulations of the contest to avoid any confusion or disputes.</Callout>

### Tracking Your Results

Tracking your results is crucial to understanding what's working and what's not with your TikTok marketing strategy. Here are some tips to track your results effectively:

* Use analytics tools: Use analytics tools like TikTok Insights or Google Analytics to track your results.
* Set clear goals: Set clear goals and track your progress towards them.
* Monitor your engagement: Monitor your engagement rates and adjust your strategy accordingly.
* Adjust your strategy: Be willing to adjust your strategy based on your results.

<Callout type="warning">Don't get too caught up in tracking your results. Remember, the goal of TikTok marketing is to increase brand awareness and drive foot traffic to your salon.</Callout>

TikTok marketing is a powerful tool for hair salons looking to attract new clients and increase brand awareness. ## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### 1. Posting Content That Has No Local Hook

A salon owner in Austin, Texas — let’s call hers “Luxe Locks” — started posting beautifully shot braid tutorials. Great lighting, good music, clean edits. After three weeks she had 12,000 views on one video and fewer than five new inquiries. She was frustrated. What went wrong? The videos were generic. No mention of Austin, no tag of the neighborhood, no “come see us on South Congress.” TikTok’s algorithm showed her content to people who liked braids, but not necessarily people who lived within a 30-minute drive.

**The fix:** She added her location in the video caption, used #AustinSalon and #ATXHairstyles, and started filming with recognizable local landmarks in the background — even if it was just her storefront. She also pinned a comment saying “Austin locals get 10% off first visit — mention this video.”

**The outcome:** In the next 60 days, her TikTok-driven bookings went from $0 to roughly $2,800 per month. Total cost for the pivot: zero dollars. Her time investment: an extra 15 minutes per video for location tagging and replying to local comments.

### 2. Having a Viral Video but No Way to Book

A stylist in Portland, Oregon posted a 15-second “before and after” of a vivid color transformation. It got 400,000 views. The comments were full of “Where are you?” and “How do I book?” Her profile bio said “DM for appointments” — but she had no scheduling link, no website, no way to actually collect those leads. She estimated she lost at least 40 potential clients in the first week.

**The fix:** She set up a free Square Appointments account (zero monthly cost for basic), created a link in bio, and added a button on her TikTok profile that goes directly to her booking page. She also started using the “Link in Bio” sticker on her most popular videos.

**The outcome:** In the month after adding the booking link, she booked 18 new clients directly from TikTok. At an average service price of $70, that’s $1,260 in revenue from a fix that took 20 minutes. She told me later, “I can’t believe I didn’t do that sooner.”

### 3. Polishing the Videos Until They’re Dead

A salon in Chicago spent $500 on a ring light, a tripod, and a better microphone. Then the owner spent two hours editing each 30-second clip — perfect cuts, color grading, sound syncing. The videos looked professional. They also got almost no engagement. The comments read “This feels like an ad” and “Too scripted.”

**The fix:** I told her to film the next appointment on her phone with natural lighting, no cuts, just a raw 60-second before/after with a laugh at the end when the client said they loved it. She posted it without any edits except trimming the start.

**The outcome:** The raw video got 15,000 views — more than any of her polished ones. Engagement rate shot up from 1.2% to 6%. Over the next 90 days, client bookings from TikTok increased by 25%, adding roughly $1,500 per month in revenue. The $500 in equipment sits in a closet. She uses her iPhone.

### 4. Ignoring Comments and Duets

A barber shop in Nashville (also a hair salon for our purposes) had a video that went semi-viral — 50,000 views. The owner replied to zero comments. He didn’t duet any of the “how do I get that look?” responses. He didn’t even say thank you. Two months later, he complained that TikTok “doesn’t work for local businesses.”

**The fix:** I had him start replying to every comment within 12 hours, including asking a question back (“What color are you thinking?”). He also began dueting videos from clients who tagged him, adding his own reaction and a brief tip.

**The outcome:** Reply rate increased engagement, and the algorithm started pushing his content to people who had interacted before. Within six weeks, his consultation calls from TikTok increased from 2 per month to 8 per month. At an average conversion of three new clients per month, each spending $120, that’s $360 additional monthly revenue. The cost: maybe 10 minutes per day.

## How to Measure What Matters (Not Just Views)

A lot of salon owners get excited about view counts. I get it — it feels good to see 10,000 people watched your video. But views don’t pay rent. What matters is how many of those people actually book an appointment. Here’s the uncomfortable truth: you can have 100,000 views and zero new clients if you don’t connect the dots.

**Start with a tracking link.** Use a tool like Bitly or UTM parameters in Google Analytics. For example, if you’re using Square Appointments or Booksy, create a unique link for TikTok. Something like `yoursalon.square.site?utm_source=tiktok&utm_medium=social`. Then put that link in your bio. You can track exactly how many clicks come from TikTok and, more importantly, how many of those clicks turn into a completed booking.

**Set up a TikTok Pixel.** If you run any paid ads on TikTok (which I’ll get to in a minute), the pixel lets you see which users viewed your ad and then booked. That’s the only way to calculate your true cost per acquisition. Without it, you’re flying blind.

**A real example:** A salon in Denver — let’s call her “Mountain Color” — was posting 3–4 times a week and getting around 2,000 views per video. She assumed that was “good enough.” I asked her to install the TikTok pixel and put a UTM link in her bio. Turns out, out of those 2,000 views, only 30 people clicked the link, and only 2 booked. That’s a conversion rate of 0.1%. Not great.

**The fix:** She ran a simple $100 local awareness ad promoting her best-performing before-and-after video, targeted to women within 10 miles of her salon. The ad got 5,000 views. The pixel tracked 120 link clicks and 12 bookings. At an average ticket of $85, that’s $1,020 in revenue from a $100 spend. That’s a 10x return.

**What you should track monthly:**
- Total TikTok views
- Clicks on your bio link (via Bitly or UTM)
- Number of new clients who mention TikTok
- Revenue from those clients
- Cost per acquisition (CPA) if you run ads

Most guides skip this part because it’s less flashy than “go viral!” But the salon owners who actually grow their business are the ones who measure the boring stuff.

## Tools That Actually Work for Scheduling and Tracking TikTok Leads

You don’t need a marketing stack. You need three tools that work together without making you want to throw your laptop out the window.

### Square Appointments (Free for basics)
If you don’t already have an online booking system, start here. Square Appointments has a free tier that lets you schedule, accept payments, and send reminders. Their link-in-bio page is clean and mobile-friendly. I’ve seen a salon in Atlanta switch from a “call to book” model to Square and immediately see a 30% increase in bookings because people can schedule at 2 a.m. when they’re doom-scrolling TikTok.

### Booksy ($30–$80/month)
Booksy is purpose-built for salons. It integrates with your TikTok link, allows clients to see your real-time availability, and can automatically remind them of appointments. A stylist in Los Angeles told me she got 15 new clients in one month simply because her Booksy link was easy to find in her TikTok bio. The downside: the monthly fee eats into margins, so calculate your break-even. If one extra appointment per month covers the cost, it’s worth it.

### Mailchimp ($13–$20/month for Essentials)
Once someone books from TikTok, you need to stay top-of-mind. Mailchimp’s free tier supports 500 contacts. Set up an automated welcome email that says “Thanks for finding us on TikTok — here’s a $10 discount on your next visit.” That small nudge can increase repeat bookings by 20–30%. I tested this with a salon in Charlotte, North Carolina. They added the email capture to their booking form and saw $460 in additional revenue from repeat clients in the first month.

### Google Ads ($300–$600/month for local campaigns)
TikTok is great for discovery. But when someone searches “best hair salon in Phoenix” at 10 a.m. because they saw your TikTok last night, you want to be on Google. Run a small local search campaign for keywords like “[your city] hair salon,” “color specialist [city],” or “balayage near me.” Budget $300–$500/month. Track conversions with a phone call or a form. One salon in Phoenix spent $400/month on Google Ads and attributed $3,800 in monthly revenue directly to those ads — many of those clients had first seen the salon on TikTok.

## Collaborations and Cross-Promotions That Work

You don’t have to do this alone. Partnering with another local business doubles your audience without doubling your work.

**Example from Nashville:** A hair salon teamed up with a nearby coffee shop. The deal: the coffee shop promoted the salon on their TikTok with a video of the stylist getting a latte and talking about her services. The salon posted a similar video from inside the coffee shop with the owner. Both included links to book or order. The campaign ran for two weeks. Cost: a few free drinks and a free haircut for the coffee shop owner. Result: the salon got 50 new client inquiries, and the coffee shop saw a 15% bump in daily foot traffic. The salon attributed roughly $1,500 in new revenue from that two-week push.

**Another from Austin:** A barber shop partnered with a local men’s clothing boutique. They filmed a “style swap” — the barber did a haircut for the boutique owner, and the boutique owner dressed the barber in new clothes. Both posted it. The video got 35,000 views. The barber saw 12 new bookings worth about $960. The boutique sold three suits from the post.

**How to set this up:**
1. Find a business that shares your target client but isn’t a competitor — coffee shops, gyms, nail salons, skincare studios.
2. Pitch a simple swap: you post about them, they post about you.
3. Use a shared UTM link or promo code so you can track which clients came from the partner.
4. Keep the collaboration short (one to two weeks) so you can measure it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much time per week do I actually need to spend on TikTok?**
Honestly? About 3–4 hours if you’re doing it right. That includes filming, editing (keep it under 15 minutes per video), posting, replying to comments, and checking your analytics. You can cut that to 2 hours by batching your content — film 4 videos in one morning, then post them throughout the week. If you can’t spare 2 hours, delegate to a staff member who’s comfortable on camera.

**Q: Do I have to show my face? Can I just show the hair?**
You can start by just showing the hair, but you’ll get better results if you show your face and voice. People book people, not mannequins. I’ve seen a stylist in Denver who hated being on camera get 20% more leads when she finally did a 5-second intro. You don’t need a full vlog — just say “Hey, I’m Jen, here’s a copper balayage I did today.” That’s enough.

**Q: Will TikTok actually bring people into my physical salon? I’m skeptical.**
Yes, if you do the math. A salon in Portland went from 0 to 180 new clients in six months using TikTok. Before that, they relied on Google and Yelp. But the key is that TikTok works as a top-of-funnel discovery tool — people see your work, get curious, then search for you on Google or click your booking link. It’s not a replacement for your website or Google Maps listing. It’s an addition.

**Q: What if I’m not trendy? I don’t follow TikTok dances or trends.**
Good. You don’t need to. Most trends last 72 hours anyway. What works consistently is educational content — “How to ask for a curtain fringe” or “3 things you should never do to your hair at home.” That content gets saved and shared, which signals the algorithm to push it to more people. You don’t have to be cool. You have to be useful.

**Q: Can I just repost my Instagram photos and videos?**
You can, but don’t expect great results. TikTok’s algorithm prefers native content — videos shot and edited within the app, using its sounds and text overlays. Reposting a square Instagram photo as a still image will get very low reach. If you have a good video from IG, at least re-edit it with a TikTok-style caption and a trending sound.

**Q: Should I run paid TikTok ads or stick to organic?**
Start with organic for 30 days. Post 3–4 times a week, engage with comments, see what works. If you get decent organic traction, then test $100 in local awareness ads to boost your best-performing video. That’s low risk. A salon in Chicago spent $100 and got $1,200 in bookings. But don’t start with ads if you don’t have the organic foundation — you’ll just waste money showing a bad video to people who won’t book.
