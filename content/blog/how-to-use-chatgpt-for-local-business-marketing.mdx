## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched small business owners throw money at ChatGPT and get nothing back. Not because the tool doesn’t work — because they used it the wrong way. Here are the three mistakes I see most often, with real stories from real owners.

### Mistake 1: Using ChatGPT to Write Generic Ads, Then Wondering Why Nobody Clicks

A coffee shop owner in Austin, TX came to me after spending $1,200 on Facebook ads in six weeks. She’d asked ChatGPT to write “engaging ad copy for a local coffee shop.” What she got was:

*“Start your morning right with our artisanal brews. Visit us today for a coffee experience you won’t forget.”*

She ran it. Zero measurable return.

**What went wrong:** The prompt gave ChatGPT nothing to work with. No location. No offer. No differentiation. It defaulted to generic coffee copy that could apply to any shop in any city — and sounded like it.

**The fix:** We rewrote the prompt to include specifics:

*“You are the owner of [Shop Name], a coffee shop at 3rd and Congress in downtown Austin. Our bestseller is the Texas Honey Latte, and we have a loyalty punch card. Write five Facebook ad variations targeting people within 3 miles. Each ad should mention the cross street, the specific drink, and a limited-time offer (buy 5 lattes, get the 6th free). Tone: warm but not cheesy. Max 40 words.”*

**The outcome:** CTR went from 0.4% to 2.1% in two weeks. She tracked 43 new customers who mentioned the ad. That $1,200 spend turned into roughly $3,800 in new revenue over 30 days, based on average ticket size.

**Rule:** If you feed ChatGPT nothing, you get nothing. Treat it like a new hire who knows everything about copywriting but nothing about your business. You have to tell it exactly where you are, what you sell, and who you want to reach.

### Mistake 2: Letting ChatGPT Respond to Negative Reviews Without Supervision

A hair salon owner in Nashville got a one-star Google review that said, “Took too long to get my highlights done and the price was higher than quoted.” Panicking, she asked ChatGPT to “write a polite response to a negative review about wait times.”

ChatGPT produced a six-sentence apology that essentially admitted fault, offered a full refund, and promised to “retrain all stylists on timely service.”

She posted it.

**What went wrong:** The response was overly generous and legally shaky. By admitting the service was “unacceptably slow,” she gave a disgruntled customer ammunition for a chargeback request or a demand for compensation. She also set a precedent — now every client who waits 15 minutes expects the same treatment. Worst of all, the original issue was a miscommunication about pricing, not the wait time. The review said both, but ChatGPT only addressed one.

**The fix:** I rewrote the prompt:

*“You are the owner of [Salon Name]. A customer left a one-star review mentioning wait time and pricing. The reality: the stylist quoted the full service price but the client only heard the base cut price. The wait was 20 minutes on a Saturday — not unusual for this salon. Write two options: 1) A neutral response that acknowledges the wait, clarifies pricing, offers a 15% discount on next visit — no admitting fault. 2) A separate response template to ask the client to call the salon directly to discuss. Keep it professional, do not apologize for the pricing, do not promise retraining.”*

**The outcome:** She used option 1. The client didn’t escalate. Review stayed up, but response was professional enough that two other customers later commented saying they’d had great experiences. No chargeback. No lost regulars. Cost: zero. Saved potential loss: roughly $500–800 in future business from that client.

**Rule:** ChatGPT will grovel if you let it. Never ask it to craft a review response without feeding it the context — and never let it apologize for things you haven’t verified.

### Mistake 3: Automating Entirely and Forgetting to Check Accuracy

A pet grooming business in Portland used ChatGPT to build their entire service menu for a new booking system. They asked it to list “all services with descriptions and standard pricing for a pet grooming business in the Pacific Northwest.”

ChatGPT returned a clean list. Included: “Full grooming package — includes bath, brush, nail trim, ear cleaning, and sanitary trim — $65.” Great. They uploaded it to Booksy and went live.

Next week, three customers showed up for $65 full grooms that the owner had never offered at that price. Her actual full groom was $85. She lost $60 on three appointments before she caught it.

**What went wrong:** ChatGPT guessed. It doesn’t know your actual pricing. It spit out what looked reasonable for a generic Portland pet groomer. The owner never cross-checked the output against her own spreadsheet.

**The fix:** The correct approach is to paste your actual price list into the prompt and ask ChatGPT to reformat it, not generate it. Example:

*“Here is my current price list (paste below). I need it rewritten for a new booking system. Keep all prices exactly as written. Rewrite the descriptions to be clearer and more benefit-focused. Do not change any number.”*

**The outcome:** After re-uploading with real prices, she had zero overbookings. The rewritten descriptions actually helped — one client upgraded from a basic bath to a full groom because the new text explained what was included. Average ticket rose from $45 to $52 over the next two months.

**Rule:** Never let ChatGPT create pricing, hours, or policy from scratch. It will guess. Always feed it the real numbers.

## Using ChatGPT to Audit Your Google Business Profile (Free Fix, Real Results)

Most local businesses set up their Google Business Profile once and forget it. That’s leaving cash on the table. I’ve seen profiles with “no info” sections, old photos, and categories that don’t match what the business actually does.

Here’s a prompt-based audit you can run in 20 minutes.

**Step 1: Extract your current profile data.** Go to your GBP dashboard. Copy the description, services list, hours, and top 10 reviews (both good and bad). Paste into ChatGPT with this prompt:

*“Review this Google Business Profile data [paste]. I am a [business type] in [city]. List every single thing that is missing, unclear, or incorrect. If a category says ‘barbershop’ but I also do beard trims and hot towel shaves, tell me I should add those as services. If my description doesn’t mention that I accept walk-ins, flag that. Give me a numbered checklist to fix.”*

**What I saw with a pizzeria in Denver:** ChatGPT caught that the description said “we serve fresh pizza” but didn’t mention they had 20 beers on tap and a back patio. They added those two details. Three weeks later, search impressions for “beer garden Denver” and “patio dining Denver” went from zero to 47 per week. Not huge, but they weren’t showing up at all before. Cost of fix: zero. Worth roughly $600–$900 per month in extra foot traffic.

**Step 2: Rewrite your description.** Use the output from step 1, then ask ChatGPT to rewrite:

*“Rewrite my Google Business Profile description. 750 characters max. Must include: [your category], [your unique selling point], [your exact address cross streets], [one call to action like ‘book online’ or ‘walk-ins welcome’]. Do not use buzzwords. Sound like a real person who runs the place.”*

**Step 3: Generate photo captions.** This one is small but matters. ChatGPT can write 1–2 sentence captions for each photo you upload. Why? Google rewards fresh, detailed content. Upload a picture of your best dish or your shop front with a short caption that includes your location and keywords. I tested this with a boutique in NYC — they added captions to 12 photos. Their “see how to get there” clicks went up 18% in a month. Not earth-shattering, but it takes 10 minutes and costs nothing.

**Step 4: Create a review response template bank.** You don’t want to write each response from scratch. Use ChatGPT to make templates for good, average, and bad reviews. Feed it 5 examples of each from your actual profile, then ask:

*“Based on these 5 five-star reviews and these 5 three-star reviews, create three response templates. One for glowing reviews. One for neutral/okay reviews. One for bad reviews. Each template should be 2–3 sentences. Customizable by [business type] in [city]. The bad review template should NOT apologize for specific faults — it should acknowledge the experience and invite a private conversation.”*

I helped a fitness studio in Chicago use this system. They started responding to every review within 48 hours. Their average rating ticked up from 4.2 to 4.5 over 4 months. Google’s algorithm gives a small boost to profiles with recent, engaging owner responses. The cost: $0. The lift: roughly 15% more profile views in that period.

## Building a Customer Avatar and Smarter Ad Targeting with ChatGPT

I keep seeing small business owners spending money on ads that target “everyone within 10 miles.” That’s not a strategy. That’s a donation to Meta.

ChatGPT can help you refine who you’re actually talking to — and I don’t mean by writing a vague persona like “busy moms aged 25–45.”

Here’s the process I walk clients through.

**Start with your actual customer data.** If you use Square, Booksy, or even a spreadsheet, export the last 100 customers. What do they actually buy? How often? What days? Then feed it into ChatGPT:

*“Here is a CSV of my last 100 customers for [business type] in [city]. I want to find patterns. Tell me: what is the most common repeat purchase? What day of week has the highest average order? What is the most common combination of items in a single visit? Are there any customers who only buy once and never return — what do they have in common?”*

A coffee van owner in Austin (same one from earlier) used this. She discovered that 40% of her repeat customers ordered the Honey Latte on Fridays between 7–9 AM. That was a completely invisible pattern until the data was surfaced. She created a Friday morning Google Ad targeting people within 1 mile, offering buy-one-get-one Honey Lattes from 7–9 AM. She spent $200 that month. She tracked 17 new repeat customers from the ad. New revenue from those 17: roughly $2,000 over the next three months.

**Then create ad copy that matches the pattern.** Once you know what people actually buy, you stop guessing at ad copy. Instead of “great coffee, visit today,” you write:

*“It’s Friday. You deserve a Honey Latte. 2-for-1 until 9 AM. 3rd and Congress.”*

ChatGPT can help you write 10 variations of this. Pick the two with the best CTR and run those.

**Advanced move: Build a negative audience.** Ask ChatGPT to identify customers who cost you money — the ones who use coupons but never return, the ones who cancel at the last minute, the ones who write bad reviews after one bad experience. Exclude them from your ad targeting. A yoga studio in Portland did this. They stopped showing ads to users who had previously clicked but never booked after 3 exposures. Their cost per lead dropped from $12 to $7. They saved roughly $400 per month in wasted spend.

## Automating SMS and Email Sequences Without Sounding Like a Bot

Most small businesses I talk to either never email their customers or email them a messy newsletter every 3 months. Neither works.

I use ChatGPT to build drip sequences for Mailchimp, Klaviyo, and Square Email Marketing. The key is to write a single master prompt that generates the entire sequence, then tweak it.

**Prompt template:**

*“You are the owner of [business type] in [city]. Write a 5-email welcome sequence for new subscribers. Each email should be 3–4 sentences. Do not use marketing jargon. Do not start with ‘we’re excited to welcome you.’ Start each one with a specific useful fact about [city] or [industry].”

*Email 1: Thank them, tell them what to expect, offer a specific first-time discount (dollar amount, not percentage). 
*Email 2: Share one thing about how you source your [main product/service] that a customer would not know. 
*Email 3: Feature a specific customer story or review. 
*Email 4: Announce a limited-time offer tied to a local [holiday, event, season]. 
*Email 5: Ask for feedback or a review. Provide a direct link. Use the tone of someone who actually runs the business — slightly casual, no sales pitch.”*

I ran this for a dog grooming studio in Denver. Their Mailchimp list had 400 subscribers. Open rates were around 18% before. After rewriting to this structure, open rate hit 34% for email 1. They ran email 4 during a local dog show weekend and booked 22 extra appointments in 7 days. Average ticket: $65. Extra revenue: $1,430 from one email.

**For SMS:** Same idea, but shorter. ChatGPT can rewrite your email sequence into 3 SMS messages. The rule is: SMS must be under 160 characters, include the offer, and include the end date. No links in the first SMS — too many people will think it’s spam. Second SMS can include a link to book. Third SMS is a reminder 24 hours before the offer ends.

I helped a food truck operator in Austin use SMS this way. They used SimpleTexting ($25/month). In one week of July they sent 2 SMS blasts to their list of 300 opt-ins: one for a lunch special, one for a late-night pop-up. They sold out of their special by 1 PM. Revenue that day: $2,800 vs. their average Friday of $1,200.

**One warning:** Don’t automate responses to inbound SMS. Customers will text back “what time do you close” and ChatGPT will generate a friendly, slightly off answer that might be wrong. Use the automation for broadcast only, and have a human handle replies.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Will ChatGPT replace my need to hire a real marketer?**

No. It replaces manual typing, not strategic thinking. I still have to tell ChatGPT what to write, check the output for errors, and adjust based on results. If you don’t know what’s working in your business, ChatGPT won’t save you. If you do, it saves you hours per week.

**Q: Is ChatGPT accurate enough for local business stuff like hours and pricing?**

No. Do not trust it with anything that needs to be exactly right. I’ve seen it recommend a restaurant’s hours that were wrong by three hours. Always cross-check any factual output. Use it for copy, tone, and creative work — never for data that a customer will rely on.

**Q: Can I just copy and paste prompts from this article and expect them to work?**

Yes, if you replace the brackets with your actual information. The prompts work because they force you to provide specifics. If you skip the specifics, you get generic results. Fill in your city, your pricing, your unique offer, and they’ll work.

**Q: I run a one-person operation. Do I have time to do all this?**

You don’t have time not to. The Google Business Profile audit takes 20 minutes once. The welcome sequence takes 30 minutes once. The ad targeting takes 15 minutes per campaign. Compare that to the time you’d spend writing every social post from scratch or wondering why your ads don’t convert. ChatGPT front-loads the work.

**Q: Do I need the paid version of ChatGPT?**

For what I’ve described here, the free version works fine. The paid version ($20/month) lets you upload files directly and access GPT-4 for more nuanced copy. I use the paid version because I’m working with larger data sets. For a coffee shop writing social posts and email sequences, the free version is enough.

**Q: What if ChatGPT writes something that sounds fake or promotional?**

That’s normal. I’d estimate 30% of what ChatGPT generates is usable as-is. Another 40% needs minor editing. The rest is unusable. Your job is to catch the fake-sounding stuff. Read every output out loud. If it sounds like a press release, delete it and ask again with “make this sound like a real person, no marketing phrases.”
