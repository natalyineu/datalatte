## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Using the Wrong Primary Category (And Not Using Secondary Ones at All)

**The story:** A bakery in Austin, Texas — call it “Flour Power” — came to me after six months of being stuck on page 3 of the Maps pack. They had “Bakery” as their sole category. Sounds fine, right? Except their actual revenue split was 60% custom wedding cakes and 30% coffee sales with a walk-up window. Google was showing them to people searching “bakery near me” and ignoring “wedding cakes Austin” and “coffee shop Austin” entirely.

**The fix:** I changed their primary category to “Wedding Cake Baker” and added “Coffee Shop,” “Dessert Shop,” and “Pastry Shop” as secondary categories. Then we updated their service descriptions and photo captions to match.

**The outcome:** Within 8 weeks, their Local Pack impressions for “wedding cake Austin” went from zero to 430/month. Phone calls about custom orders increased by 60%. Their average order value? $320 per custom cake. That was roughly $12,800 in new monthly revenue from a 15-minute settings change.

**What to do instead:** Open your Google Business Profile right now. Click “Edit profile” → “Business information” → “Category.” If your primary category is something generic like “Restaurant” or “Salon,” change it to the most specific thing you actually sell. Then add 3–5 secondary categories that describe your other revenue streams. Google uses these to match your business to long-tail searches — and those are usually the searches that convert.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Q&A (And Letting Customers Answer for You)

**The story:** A hair salon in Portland, Oregon — “Rose City Cuts” — had great reviews, decent photos, proper hours. They couldn’t figure out why a newer salon two blocks away kept ranking higher for “hair salon Portland” and “balayage Portland.”

I looked at their Google Business Profile Q&A section. It was a mess. A customer had asked “Do you do curly cuts?” The answer, posted by another customer, was “No, they told me they don’t.” That was wrong — Rose City Cuts actually had two stylists trained in DevaCurl techniques. But the business owner never answered, so Google treated that incorrect answer as the official one. The competitor’s Q&A was clean, answered by the owner, with 5–7 questions each.

**The fix:** We answered every unanswered question on the profile, corrected the ones with wrong answers, and added 8 new “frequently asked” questions ourselves with detailed answers that included keywords like “curly hair specialist Portland” and “DevaCut Portland.”

**The outcome:** 5 weeks later, Rose City Cuts appeared in the Local Pack for “curly hair salon Portland” for the first time. Appointment requests via Maps increased by 35%. Monthly revenue from curly cuts specifically went from $1,800 to $3,200.

**What to do instead:** Go to your GBP Q&A section. Answer every question that’s sitting there unanswered. Then add 10 questions yourself using the “Ask a question” feature from a separate Google account (or ask a friend). Use the phrasing your actual customers use in conversation. Don’t write SEO-optimized nonsense. Write: “Do you take walk-ins for men’s haircuts?” Answer: “Yes, we do — Tuesday through Thursday, we usually have same-day availability. Saturdays are booked 3–4 days out.” That’s useful. That’s what Google wants.

### Mistake 3: Letting Your NAP (Name, Address, Phone) Drift Across the Web

**The story:** A pet groomer in Nashville — “Paws & Claws Grooming” — had been in business for 8 years. Great reviews, solid repeat clientele. But their Local Pack ranking had been slowly dropping over 10 months. The owner blamed new competitors.

What actually happened: They had moved locations 18 months prior. The owner updated their GBP address, but they forgot about Yelp, their Facebook page, their Better Business Bureau listing, their Nextdoor profile, and 4 local directories they had signed up for over the years. All of those still showed the old address. Google’s algorithm sees conflicting NAP data as a signal that your business information is unreliable. It starts showing you lower.

**The fix:** We spent 4 hours tracking down every single citation — 14 total — and updated the address on all of them. We also used Moz Local to scan for any we missed (found 3 more).

**The outcome:** 11 days after the last update, Paws & Claws jumped from position 7 to position 4 in the Local Pack for “pet groomer Nashville.” Within a month, they were in position 3. New customer calls increased by 42%. Estimated monthly revenue gain: $2,400.

**What to do instead:** Search your business name and city in quotes. Make a list of every site that shows your address. Check each one. If any are wrong, fix them. Then use a free tool like BrightLocal’s citation checker to see if you missed anything. Do this quarterly. It’s boring. It works.

### Mistake 4: Posting in GBP Like It’s Instagram

**The story:** A fitness studio in Chicago — “River North Fit” — was posting to their Google Business Profile 4–5 times a week. Inspirational quotes. Stock photos of people running. Memes about leg day. Their Google Posts were getting almost zero clicks. The owner was proud of their “consistency,” but their Local Pack ranking was flat for 6 months.

**The problem:** Google Posts that don’t get engagement hurt your profile’s freshness score. Posting low-value content is worse than not posting at all. River North Fit’s posts had a 0.3% click-through rate. Google interpreted that as: “Users don’t find this business interesting when we show their content.”

**The fix:** We switched to 2 posts per week maximum. Each post had a specific offer with a hard end date (“$49 first month for new members — expires February 28”), a real photo of their actual trainers, and a direct call to action to book a trial class.

**The outcome:** Click-through rate went from 0.3% to 8.7%. Their profile views increased by 300%. Within 6 weeks, they moved from position 5 to position 2 in the Local Pack for “fitness studio River North.” New member sign-ups attributed to Maps increased from 3/month to 14/month. At $149/month average membership, that’s an additional $1,641/month in recurring revenue.

**What to do instead:** Post less. Make each post count. If you’re a coffee shop, post “Today’s special: Lavender latte with honey — available until 3 PM” with a photo of someone holding it. If you’re a salon, post “Two openings this Thursday for a balayage touch-up — book here.” Give a reason to act. That’s what Google rewards.

## How Your Reviews Strategy Actually Moves the Needle

Most local SEO guides tell you to “get more reviews.” That’s like telling a restaurant to “make better food.” Technically correct. Useless without specifics.

Here’s what I’ve seen work across 40+ small businesses in the US, from a barbershop in Denver to a pediatric dentist in Dallas to a yoga studio in Brooklyn.

**The review volume threshold is lower than you think.**

For most medium-to-low competition local markets, you don’t need 500 reviews. You need 25–40 reviews with an average rating above 4.4. That’s enough to cross the trust threshold Google looks for. Beyond 40 reviews, additional volume matters less than freshness and response rate.

One exception: if you’re in a high-competition market like a major city downtown core, the threshold is higher. For a coffee shop in Manhattan, you likely need 100+ reviews just to be considered relevant. But for a coffee shop in a suburb of Columbus, Ohio? 30 solid reviews will put you in the conversation.

**The real lever is response rate and response speed.**

Google tracks whether you respond to reviews (both positive and negative) and how quickly you do it. Responding within 24 hours is ideal. 48 hours is acceptable. Longer than that, and Google starts treating your profile as less actively managed.

I tested this with a pet groomer in Minneapolis. They had been ignoring review responses for 18 months. We set a 24-hour response rule. Within 3 weeks, their Local Pack position improved from 6 to 4. No new reviews were added. Just responses to the existing ones.

**Here’s the actual response script I use for positive reviews:**

“Thanks, [name]! [Mention something specific they said — e.g., ‘Glad you liked the blowout’]. See you next time — book online at [link].”

Short. Personal. Includes a call to action. Done in 30 seconds.

**For negative reviews:**

First: don’t get defensive. I’ve seen a business owner in Salt Lake City write a 400-word rebuttal to a 2-star review. That killed their rating momentum for 6 months because Google saw the interaction as hostile.

Instead, respond with: “Thanks for the feedback, [name]. I’m sorry we didn’t meet expectations. Would you be willing to email [email] so we can make this right? We take feedback seriously.”

That’s it. Professional. Solution-oriented. And it signals to Google that you’re an engaged business owner who handles conflict constructively.

**How to get reviews without being desperate:**

Do not put a review kiosk at the register. Do not ask every single customer. Do not email a review link to your entire mailing list.

Instead: identify the moments when your customer is happiest. For a hair salon, that’s immediately after the blowout when they look in the mirror. For a coffee shop, it’s when they take the first sip and say “this is good.” For a fitness studio, it’s the endorphin rush 10 minutes after class ends.

Train your staff to say, during those moments: “If you have a minute, a quick Google review would help us out a ton. I can send you the link.”

Then text them the direct review link (create it using the Google Business Profile URL shortener) within 60 seconds. Speed matters. 24 hours later, that happy moment is forgotten.

One bakery in Portland got 38 new reviews in 3 weeks using this method. Their Local Pack ranking went from 8 to 3. Monthly revenue from Maps-driven traffic increased by $1,900. Cost of the system: zero dollars. Just training staff to spot the happy moment.

## What Your Website’s Local Pages Need (That Most Agencies Skip)

Your Google Business Profile is the front door. Your website is the backstage pass. They need to tell the same story, or Google gets confused.

Most small business websites have a “Contact” page with an address and a phone number. That’s not a local SEO asset. That’s a digital business card.

**What actually works:**

Create a dedicated location page for your business. Not just an address block. A full page. Here’s what goes on it:

- Your full NAP (Name, Address, Phone) — exactly as it appears on your Google Business Profile. Word for word. If your GBP says “Suite 100” and your website says “Ste. 100,” that’s a mismatch Google can detect.
- An embedded Google Map showing your location
- A description of your neighborhood and nearby landmarks — not for SEO keywords, but for actual humans trying to find you. “We’re on the corner of 5th and Main, across from the blue apartment building with the mural.”
- Your hours of operation (including holiday hours — this signals active management)
- A link to book an appointment or place an order
- A “How to Find Us” section that includes parking information, public transit options, and walking directions from the nearest bus stop

I did this for a coffee shop in Denver — “Spill the Beans.” Their website previously had a contact page with just an address and phone number. We built a full location page with photos of the exterior, a map, parking instructions, and a note about their bike rack. Google indexed the page within 4 days. Their Local Pack ranking for “coffee shop Denver” went from 5 to 3. Direct bookings via the website increased by 27%.

**The technical part most guides skip:**

Your location page needs to be included in your XML sitemap. It needs internal links from your homepage and your “About” page. And it needs to load fast. I’ve seen location pages with 4MB hero images take 8 seconds to load. That’s a ranking killer.

Use a tool like Google’s PageSpeed Insights. If your location page scores below 80 on mobile, fix it. Compress images. Use a CDN if you’re on a shared host. Remove any autoplay videos (I’ve seen coffee shops do this. Please stop.)

One more thing: if you have multiple locations, do not put them all on one page. I’ve seen a dentist in Nashville with 3 locations listed in a single “Locations” page. Google could not figure out which location was relevant for which search. Split them into separate pages: /locations/midtown, /locations/green-hills, /locations/franklin. Each gets its own content, its own map, its own NAP.

Revenue impact for that dentist: within 2 months, their Midtown location went from 0 Local Pack appearances to 23/month. That location alone generated $4,200 in new patient bookings.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need to claim my Google Business Profile if I already show up in Maps?**

Yes. If you haven’t verified your profile, you cannot control your hours, respond to reviews, post updates, or fix incorrect information. I’ve seen unverified businesses lose their Maps ranking overnight because a customer reported them as “closed” and Google automatically applied it. Verification takes 5 days max via postcard or phone. Do it today.

**Q: How long does it actually take to rank in the Local Pack?**

For a new business in a low-to-medium competition market: 4–8 weeks if you do everything correctly. For a business in a high-competition market (downtown NYC, downtown LA): 3–6 months minimum, and you’ll need consistent effort. For an existing business that’s not ranking well: expect 6–12 weeks of cleanup work before you see movement. Anyone promising faster than that is selling something.

**Q: Can I rank in the Local Pack without a physical storefront?**

Yes. Google allows service-area businesses (SABs) like plumbers, electricians, pet sitters, and mobile dog groomers to hide their address and show a service area instead. You’ll rank based on proximity to the searcher, so the key is specifying a large enough service area (typically 20–30 miles from your base location). But note: if you hide your address, you won’t show up in the Local Pack for searches where you’re not physically nearby. That’s a tradeoff.

**Q: Does Yelp matter for Google Maps ranking?**

Indirectly. Yelp reviews don’t feed into Google’s algorithm. But Yelp is still a major source of traffic and can generate brand searches, which Google does track. If you ignore Yelp completely and let negative reviews pile up, a potential customer searches your business name on Google, sees conflicting info, and clicks on a competitor instead. So yes, manage Yelp. But prioritize your Google Business Profile first.

**Q: Should I pay for Google Ads to boost my Local Pack ranking?**

No. Ads and organic Local Pack rankings are separate systems. Paying for ads does not improve your organic position. I’ve tested this. I’ve seen clients waste $500–$1,000/month on Local Service Ads thinking it would help their organic rank. It doesn’t. Ads are useful for immediate visibility, but they’re a rental — the moment you stop paying, you disappear. Build the organic foundation first, then consider ads as a supplement.

**Q: What happens if my competitor is buying fake reviews and ranking higher?**

Two things. First, Google is getting better at detecting fake reviews. I’ve seen competitors lose 100+ reviews in a single night when Google ran a cleanup algorithm. Second, fake reviews create a false positive that usually crumbles under real customer experience. The business with 50 real reviews and a 4.7 rating will outrank the business with 200 fake reviews and a 4.3 rating over a 6-month period. Focus on earning real ones. The shortcut is a trap.
