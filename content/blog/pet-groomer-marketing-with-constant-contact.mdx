As a pet groomer, you can use Constant Contact to create effective email campaigns that bring in new customers and keep your existing ones coming back. Here's how:

### 1. Build Your Email List

Start by building a list of subscribers who are interested in your pet grooming services. You can do this by creating a sign-up form on your website, social media, or in-store. Make sure to offer incentives, such as discounts or free consultations, to encourage people to sign up.

### 2. Create Engaging Content

Once you have a list of subscribers, it's time to create engaging content that will keep them interested in your services. Use Constant Contact's built-in templates and design tools to create beautiful and easy-to-read emails. Make sure to include:

* Personalized greetings and salutations
* Relevant and timely content (e.g. seasonal promotions, new services)
* Calls-to-action (CTAs) to encourage subscribers to book appointments or make purchases

### 3. Send Regular Campaigns

Send regular campaigns to your subscribers to keep them engaged and coming back. Use Constant Contact's autoresponder feature to send automated emails based on specific triggers, such as:

* Welcome emails for new subscribers
* Reminders for upcoming appointments
* Abandoned cart emails to encourage subscribers to complete their booking

### 4. Track and Measure Performance

Finally, use Constant Contact's analytics and reporting tools to track and measure the performance of your email campaigns. This will help you identify what's working and what's not, so you can make data-driven decisions to improve your marketing strategy.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched dozens of small service businesses — including a heartbreaking number of pet groomers — torpedo their own email marketing. Not because the software was bad. Because of how they used it. Here are the three I see most often, with real stories that will cost you money if you ignore them.

### Mistake #1: Sending Every Single Week (And Wondering Why People Unsubscribe)

**The story:** A dog grooming shop in Austin called "Paws & Relax" had a solid list of 1,200 subscribers. The owner, Maria, decided she'd send a Constant Contact email every Tuesday morning. Every single Tuesday. After six weeks of this, she had lost almost 200 subscribers. Her open rate dropped from 42% to 18%. She thought her customers had stopped caring.

**What actually happened:** She sent the same kind of email every week — a generic "Come see us!" with a coupon for $5 off grooming. No segmentation. No variety. No reason to open. Her regulars were annoyed. The people who came once a month were overwhelmed. And the 200 people who unsubscribed? About 60 of them had been clients for over two years.

**What she did instead:** I advised her to cut frequency to twice a month, with a specific purpose for each email. First of the month: a helpful grooming tip (e.g., "How to prevent matting during Texas summer heat") with a soft booking reminder. Third week: a specific offer, but only to people who hadn't booked in 60+ days.

**The outcome:** After 90 days, her unsubscribes dropped to zero. Open rates climbed back to 38%. She tracked $1,460 in direct bookings from two of those targeted emails — revenue she would have lost if she'd kept sending generic weekly blasts.

**The fix:** Audit your list. If you're sending more than twice a month to everyone, you're over-messaging. Segment by behavior: new leads get a welcome series, loyal clients get loyalty perks, lapsed clients get re-engagement. Not everyone needs to see "Book now!" every week.

### Mistake #2: Buying a List (Then Wondering Why Nobody Opens)

**The story:** A groomer in Portland named "Happy Tails Grooming" decided to grow fast. He spent $300 on a purchased list of 5,000 pet owners in the Portland metro area. He uploaded it to Constant Contact, sent his first campaign, and got exactly 17 opens. Three people reported him as spam. Constant Contact flagged his account.

**What actually happened:** He paid $300 for a list that was likely compiled from public records, scraped websites, and expired opt-ins. Most of those people had never consented to receive email from him. Even the ones who had pets had never heard of his business. The email went straight to spam folders. Constant Contact's terms of service explicitly prohibit sending to purchased lists. He got a warning that his account could be suspended.

**What he did instead:** He deleted the purchased list. He set up a small sign-up form on an iPad at his front counter — just a name and email prompt with a checkbox for "Send me grooming tips and special offers." He also added a lead magnet: "Get our Seasonal Grooming Guide (PDF) when you join our email list." He started collecting names from walk-ins and booking inquiries.

**The 90-day result:** He built a list of 340 people — all actual clients or local leads who wanted to hear from him. His first campaign to this list got 112 opens and 21 booking inquiries. He generated $780 in revenue from a single email to 340 people. That's more than he got from the 5,000 purchased names.

**The fix:** Never, ever pay for an email list. The people you want on your list are people who know who you are and have agreed to hear from you. Every purchased email you send is a violation of trust and a risk to your sender reputation. Constant Contact will shut you down if you make a habit of it.

### Mistake #3: No Welcome Series (Then Asking Why People Forget You)

**The story:** A groomer in Denver called "Mountain Mutts" had a new email sign-up form on their website. They collected about 40 new subscribers per month. But they had no automated welcome series. New subscribers got a one-time "Thanks for signing up!" email and then... nothing. Three months later, those same people would get a promotional email and most of them had already forgotten why they'd subscribed.

**What actually happened:** The owner didn't realize Constant Contact had an automated welcome series feature built in. She assumed welcome emails were something you had to write manually for each new subscriber. When I asked if she had a series, she said, "I just send them the same email I send everyone else."

**What she did instead:** We set up a three-email welcome series using Constant Contact's automation tools:
- **Email 1 (Day 0):** "Welcome to Mountain Mutts! Here's your $10 first-time grooming credit." This went out within minutes of sign-up.
- **Email 2 (Day 3):** "What to expect at your first appointment" — a short, helpful guide explaining how she handles anxious dogs, what to bring, and a photo of her team.
- **Email 3 (Day 14):** "Meet our team" — a fun, personal email with photos of the groomers and their own pets. Soft ask: "Ready to book? Reply to this email and we'll get you scheduled."

**The 60-day outcome:** Of the 80 people who subscribed during those two months, 34 booked their first appointment directly from the welcome series. That's a 42.5% conversion rate from subscriber to paying client. The average ticket was $72 per grooming visit. That's $2,448 in new revenue from a sequence that took about 90 minutes to set up.

**The fix:** If you don't have a welcome series, you are leaving money on the counter. Setting one up in Constant Contact takes about an hour. It should be three emails max, sent over two weeks. Give value first, then make an offer. Automate it so you never forget.

## Integrating Email with Your Booking System (So You Don't Have to Do Everything Twice)

Most pet groomers I work with run their business through a booking platform — Booksy, Square Appointments, Mindbody, or Vagaro. And most of them treat their email marketing as a completely separate task. That's a mistake. Here's how to fix it.

**The real-world example:** A groomer in Nashville called "Bark 'n' Bath" was using Square Appointments to manage bookings and Constant Contact for email. She was manually exporting client emails from Square and uploading them to Constant Contact once a month. It took her about two hours. She'd always miss people. Half her list was outdated.

**What she actually did:** She connected Square Appointments to Constant Contact using a free integration through Zapier. Now when someone books an appointment in Square, their email is automatically added to her Constant Contact list with a tag that says "new lead." When someone comes in for their first visit, she tags them as "first-time client." When someone hasn't booked in 90 days, they get marked as "lapsed."

**The result:** She stopped wasting two hours a month on list maintenance. More importantly, she started sending triggered emails she'd never had before:
- "You're booked for tomorrow!" confirmation email (she turned off Square's default and used Constant Contact's branded version instead)
- "We miss you" email sent automatically 30 days after last visit — this alone brought back 18 lapsed clients in the first month
- "Happy birthday, pup!" email with a $15 grooming discount — generated $1,100 in revenue in 12 months

**What you should do:** Check if your booking software integrates with Constant Contact directly. If yes, set it up. If not, use Zapier or Make (formerly Integromat). The connection is simple and the ROI is immediate. You'll stop losing clients who slip through the cracks, and you'll stop doing manual data entry that wastes your time.

**The dollar numbers:** Bark 'n' Bath's automated lapsed-client email added $4,200 in revenue over six months from clients who would have otherwise just quietly stopped coming. That's $700/month in free money from a five-minute automation setup.

## Using Google Ads and Yelp to Feed Your Email List (Instead of Just Wasting Money on Clicks)

Here's something most marketing guides won't tell you: running Google Ads or Yelp Ads is often a waste of money if you're not capturing the lead's email address. You pay for a click. They browse your site. Maybe they call. Maybe they don't. You never see them again.

**The fix:** Turn your ad spend into a list-building machine.

**The story:** A pet grooming shop in Chicago called "Windy City Paws" was spending $600/month on Google Ads. They were getting about 80 clicks per month and converting maybe 4-5 into bookings. Rough math: $600 for five bookings = $120 per new client acquisition cost. Their average ticket was $65. They were losing money on every new client.

**What they did instead:** They changed the ad landing page. Instead of sending people to their general website, they created a dedicated landing page with a lead magnet: "Download our free Chicago Dog Grooming Checklist (10 things every owner should know before their first visit)." To get the PDF, visitors had to enter their email address. The page also had a prominent button: "Book your grooming appointment now" for people who were ready to buy.

**The 60-day outcome:** Their ad spend stayed at $600/month. Now, instead of 80 clicks that mostly bounced, they captured 45 email addresses from the people who were actually interested. Of those 45, 12 booked within 72 hours of receiving the welcome email (which included a $10 discount). The other 33 went into an automated 60-day nurture sequence — tips, reminders, a second offer at day 30.

**The hard numbers:**
- 12 immediate bookings at $65 average = $780 revenue
- 8 more bookings from the nurture sequence within 60 days = $520 revenue
- Total revenue from $600 spend: $1,300
- That's a 116% return on ad spend — better than break-even for the first time in months

**What about Yelp?** Same principle. Don't just run Yelp Ads and hope for calls. Add a lead capture element. Offer something useful — a guide, a discount checklist, a "What to expect" PDF — and collect emails before they leave.

**The point:** You are paying for every click. Stop sending that traffic into a black hole. Capture the email, and you can market to them again for free.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn't email marketing dead for small businesses? Why would pet owners open emails from a groomer?**

No, it's not dead. It's underutilized. The 75% stat in the article above is accurate — pet owners consistently prefer email over social media for business communications. The reason: email lands in a dedicated inbox. Facebook posts get buried in an algorithm. Email is direct. If someone has given you their email address, they've actively opted in. That's a stronger signal than a like or a follow. Open rates for pet grooming emails are typically 25-35% if you're sending useful content. That's higher than most industries.

**Q: Should I use Constant Contact or something cheaper like Mailchimp?**

It depends on your list size and whether you need phone support. Constant Contact is more expensive at the bottom tiers — around $20/month for up to 500 contacts, versus Mailchimp's free tier up to 500 contacts. But Constant Contact gives you access to actual phone support, which you will need at least once. Mailchimp's free tier has limitations on automation and templates. I've used both. For a pet grooming business with under 1,000 contacts, Mailchimp is fine if you're comfortable setting things up yourself. For anyone who wants help and doesn't want to Google "how to fix this error" at 9 PM on a Saturday, pay for Constant Contact. The $15-20 difference per month is worth avoiding one hour of frustration.

**Q: Do I really need to send emails every month? I'm too busy grooming dogs.**

No, you don't need to send every month. You need to send consistently. Twice a month is the sweet spot. That's two hours of work total — one hour to write the email, one hour to set up the automation or schedule the send. If you're too busy to do that, hire a virtual assistant or a freelance copywriter. Spend $100-150/month to have someone write your emails. If that generates $400-600 in bookings (which it should, based on the numbers above), you're ahead. The cost of not sending is worse than the cost of hiring help.

**Q: I have 200 email addresses. Is that even worth my time?**

Yes. 200 engaged subscribers are worth more than 2,000 people who never open. If you have 200 actual clients or leads who want to hear from you, a well-written email should convert at 2-5%. That's 4-10 new bookings per email. At an average ticket of $65, that's $260-650 per email. Multiply by two emails per month. That's $520-1,300 per month from a list of 200 people. Stop focusing on list size. Focus on list quality.

**Q: What should I actually put in the emails? I don't want to sound salesy.**

Don't be salesy. Be useful. Here's a simple template that works for groomers:
- **Subject line:** Something specific, not generic. "How to brush your golden retriever without losing your mind" beats "February grooming special" every time.
- **Body:** One helpful tip or story. One photo of a dog you groomed (with owner permission). One soft call to action — "Book your next appointment here" or "Reply to this email to schedule."
- **Offer:** Every third email, include a specific discount or offer. The other two should just be useful and engaging.

You are not a marketing agency. You are a groomer who sends emails. That authenticity works better than polished sales copy.

**Q: How fast should I expect results?**

If you set up a welcome series and send your first targeted email to your existing list, you should see results in 7-14 days. The first email brings back people who forgot about you. The welcome series converts new leads. If you have zero email list and start collecting today, give yourself 60-90 days to build a meaningful list and see consistent bookings. Email marketing is not a fast fix. It's a steady engine. But once it's running, it keeps running.
