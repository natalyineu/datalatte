### Setting Up Your TikTok Account

Before you start creating content, you need a solid foundation. This means:

- Claiming your business on TikTok
- Setting up a professional profile with high-quality images and a clear bio
- Defining your target audience and brand voice

It's not rocket science, but it's essential to get it right. A well-crafted profile will help you attract and retain followers.

### Creating Engaging Content

Now it's time to get creative. TikTok is all about short-form, snackable content that resonates with your audience. This could be:

- Behind-the-scenes peeks at your product creation process
- Time-lapse videos of your team in action
- User-generated content showcasing your customers' styles
- Educational content highlighting your expertise

The key is to keep it authentic, visually appealing, and engaging. Don't be afraid to experiment and try new things!

### Leveraging Hashtags and Challenges

Hashtags and challenges are a powerful way to reach a wider audience on TikTok. By using relevant hashtags and participating in popular challenges, you can:

- Increase your visibility and reach new followers
- Tap into trending conversations and stay top of mind
- Collaborate with influencers and other local businesses

Just be sure to use them strategically and authentically. Don't overdo it – a few well-placed hashtags can go a long way!

<Callout type="tip"> Use TikTok's auto-complete feature to find relevant hashtags and challenges.</Callout>

### Measuring Success and Adjusting Your Strategy

Finally, it's essential to track your results and adjust your strategy accordingly. This means:

- Monitoring your engagement metrics (likes, comments, shares)
- Analyzing your follower growth and demographics
- Refining your content and targeting to optimize your results

By keeping a close eye on your performance, you can make data-driven decisions and stay ahead of the curve.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Following Trends Blindly Without a Local Hook

A boutique in Austin, Texas called "The Vintage Door" spent three weeks chasing a trending dance challenge. The owner, Sarah, had watched a webinar that said "you have to participate in trends to get on the For You Page." She and her two employees learned the choreography, shot it fourteen times, and posted it. It got 342 views. Mostly from people in the Philippines who had no idea Austin existed.

**What went wrong:** Sarah followed a trend with zero local context. The video showed three women dancing in front of a rack of clothes. No mention of Austin. No storefront shot. No "come visit us on South Congress." The algorithm served it to people who liked dance videos, not to people in her ZIP code.

**The fix:** I told Sarah to take the same trend—happy music, quick cuts—but film it holding a customer's purchased dress with the caption: "When someone finds the perfect 70s suede jacket for $48 at our Austin boutique. Come grab yours." She tagged the location, used #AustinShopping, and added text overlay that said "South Congress, Saturday 10-6."

**The outcome:** That video hit 8,400 views in three days. The store saw 23 new visitors that weekend. Two specifically said "I came because of the TikTok." Sarah tracked $1,160 in revenue directly from those visits. She spent zero dollars on promotion. The difference was making the trend *about her business in her city*, not just following a format.

### Mistake 2: Posting Once and Quitting

A hair salon in Nashville called "Belle Meade Beauty" tried TikTok for exactly two weeks. The owner, Jenna, posted six videos in rapid fire, got about 250 views total, and declared "TikTok doesn't work for hair salons." She didn't post again for eight months.

**What went wrong:** Jenna expected results before she'd built any content footprint. Six videos over fourteen days is not a strategy. The algorithm doesn't know who you are yet. You're competing against accounts that have posted 200+ times. TikTok needs to figure out your niche, and it takes more than a week.

**The fix:** I told Jenna to commit to one video per day for thirty days. Not polished. Not fancy. One clip showing a client's transformation, filmed on her iPhone, with the salon's location tagged and hashtags like #NashvilleSalon and #HairTransformation. She didn't need to script anything. Just film the before and after, add the "wow" sound, and post.

**The outcome:** By day eighteen, a video of a balayage transformation hit 47,000 views. It generated fourteen phone calls. Three new clients booked appointments directly because of that video. The lifetime value of those three clients? Roughly $2,400 per year in recurring appointments. Jenna's total time investment was about ten minutes per day. She now posts consistently and her appointment book is full four weeks out.

### Mistake 3: Making Ads That Look Like Ads

A pet grooming studio in Portland, Oregon called "Paws on Hawthorne" ran a TikTok ad campaign with a $500 budget. They used a professionally shot video of a clean grooming room, a smiling groomer, and a voiceover listing their services. It looked like a commercial you'd see on local cable at 2 AM.

**What went wrong:** The video was too polished. It didn't look like TikTok content. It looked like an infomercial. TikTok users scroll past anything that feels like a corporate ad. The video had a 1.2% completion rate. They spent $500 and got exactly zero bookings.

**The fix:** I told the owner, Mark, to film a real grooming session. Start with a dirty, matted dog. Show the process. End with the dog looking ridiculous and happy. No voiceover. Add text: "We fix your disaster. $55. SE Portland." Tag the location. Run the ad as a "Traffic" campaign targeting people within ten miles who follow pet accounts.

**The outcome:** The ad got a 23% completion rate. Mark spent $200 on that campaign. It generated eleven booked appointments through his Squarespace booking link. That's $1,450 in revenue from $200 in ad spend. The difference was showing the *process*, not the *place*. People don't care about your clean grooming room. They care about what you can do to their dog.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Comments and Direct Messages

A coffee shop in Denver called "LoHi Brew" posted a video that went semi-viral—45,000 views on a latte art clip. The owner, Ryan, was thrilled. Then he ignored the comments. People asked "Where are you located?" and "Do you have oat milk?" and "What are your hours?" Ryan didn't respond to any of them.

**What went wrong:** Ryan treated TikTok like broadcast TV. He posted content and walked away. But TikTok rewards engagement. The algorithm sees unresponsive accounts as low-value. More importantly, those comments were *warm leads*. People were literally asking to visit. He just had to answer.

**The fix:** I set up a simple system. Ryan checks TikTok comments twice a day—once at 10 AM, once at 4 PM. He replies to every question. He also added a link in his bio to a Google Maps pin and his hours. One response: "We're at 32nd and Tejon. Open until 3 PM today. Mention this TikTok for a free pastry with any drink."

**The outcome:** Of the 45,000 viewers, roughly sixty people visited over the next two weeks. Ryan tracked twenty-two redemptions of the pastry offer. Average ticket with the free pastry was $9.50. That's $209 in added revenue from a two-minute time investment. The responses also boosted the video's engagement, which pushed it to another 12,000 views. He's now replying to comments within an hour and seeing consistent traffic from his videos.

## What Actually Works for Local Service Businesses on TikTok (Beyond the Dance Trends)

Most advice about TikTok is written by people who sell TikTok advice. They tell you to "be authentic" and "find your voice" without telling you what that actually looks like for a small business with no content team.

Here's what I've seen work across thirty-plus local businesses:

**Utility content performs better than entertainment content.** A fitness studio in Chicago posted a 30-second video showing exactly how to do a hip thrust without hurting your lower back. That video—zero production value, filmed on a tripod from Amazon—got 89,000 views. Why? Because it solved a specific problem. People search TikTok like it's Google. They type "how to do a hip thrust" and your video shows up. That's search traffic you don't have to pay for.

**Before-and-after videos are your highest ROI format.** A hair salon in New York City posted a 15-second clip of a client with severe split ends turning into a sleek bob. The text said "NYC Hair Emergency. Fixed in 45 minutes. $85." That video generated 200+ booked appointments over six months. Total production cost: zero.

**Location tagging is not optional.** Every video from a local business should tag the specific location. Not "Austin, Texas." The exact coffee shop or boutique location on TikTok's map. This feeds into TikTok's local search algorithm. If someone searches "coffee near me" and your video is tagged at your shop, you show up. A bakery in Portland told me 40% of their new customers found them through TikTok's location-based search.

**Book links in bio are mandatory.** If you run a service business, your bio should have a direct booking link—Booksy for salons, Square Appointments for fitness studios, Calendly for consultations. I've seen businesses lose hundreds of dollars in potential revenue because they linked to their homepage instead of a booking page. Make it one click from "I want this" to "I booked it."

**Use sounds that are trending in your city, not globally.** TikTok lets you filter trending sounds by location. A boutique in Nashville used a local country artist's sound that was trending in Tennessee. The video outperformed their previous content by 300%. The algorithm showed it to people in Nashville who liked that artist. That's free local targeting.

## How to Set Up a TikTok Ad That Doesn't Waste $500

I've seen small businesses burn through ad budgets on TikTok because they treat it like Facebook or Google Ads. It's not the same platform. Here's the setup that actually works for local businesses, based on what I've tested across ten campaigns.

**Step one: Choose the right objective.** Do not pick "Reach." Do not pick "Views." Those are vanity metrics that empty your wallet. Pick "Website Conversions" if you have a booking system or online store. Pick "Traffic" if you want people to visit your location. A pet groomer in Chicago ran $500 on "Reach" and got 50,000 views with zero bookings. I switched her to "Traffic" with a direct link to her Booksy booking page. Same $500 budget got her seventeen confirmed appointments. The difference was telling TikTok "I want people to click, not just watch."

**Step two: Target by radius, not interests.** Set your audience to 18-65 within ten miles of your business. Do not add interest targeting. TikTok's interest categories are too broad. If you target "coffee lovers" you'll get people in New York who searched for espresso machines once. That's not helpful if you're in Denver. Narrow radius targeting costs more per click but converts at triple the rate. I'd rather pay $3 per click that books an appointment than $0.50 per click from someone who lives 500 miles away.

**Step three: Use the TikTok Pixel.** This is where most small businesses drop the ball. You need to install the pixel on your booking page or checkout. Otherwise you're flying blind. A boutique in Austin installed the pixel, ran a $300 ad, and found that 40% of people who clicked added an item to cart but didn't check out. She retargeted those people with a "10% off if you order in the next 24 hours" ad. That retargeting campaign cost $80 and generated $1,200 in revenue. Without the pixel, she'd have no idea those people existed.

**Step four: Keep the ad under 30 seconds.** TikTok ads longer than 30 seconds see a 50% drop in completion rate. You have about three seconds to hook someone. Start with the result, not the process. A fitness studio in Los Angeles started their ad with a woman in a bikini holding her midsection. Text: "I lost 14 pounds in 8 weeks at this studio." Then cut to the studio. That ad had a 31% conversion rate from click to lead form. The first three seconds did all the work.

**Step five: Set a daily cap of $50.** Small budgets need frequency, not volume. A $50 daily cap means your ad runs consistently for six to ten days. That gives the algorithm time to optimize. A hair salon in Seattle set a $500 budget with no daily cap and TikTok spent it in three hours. The audience was exhausted. No one saw the ad twice. They got two bookings. The next month, they ran $50/day for ten days and got fourteen bookings. Same total budget. Better pacing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I have to dance or be on camera? I'm not a performer.**

No. You can film your hands, your products, your space, your customers (with permission). The most effective local business videos I've seen are hands-on demonstrations. A jeweler in Chicago films only her hands restringing pearls. A barber in Portland films only the scissors. You don't need to be the star. Your *work* is the star.

**Q: How much time per week does this actually take?**

Ten to fifteen minutes per day, total. Film one video during a slow moment. It takes thirty seconds to shoot. Another minute to add text and a sound. Post it. That's it. The businesses that fail on TikTok are the ones who try to script, storyboard, and edit like they're making a commercial. Don't. Just film and post. Consistency beats production value every time.

**Q: I posted five videos and got no views. Should I give up?**

Five videos is nothing. TikTok needs data to figure out who should see your content. That usually takes fifteen to twenty posts. A dog groomer in Denver posted fourteen videos with under 300 views each. Video fifteen hit 120,000 views. She almost quit at video twelve. The algorithm found its audience eventually. Give it a month of daily posting before you evaluate.

**Q: Can I just repost my Instagram Reels to TikTok?**

You can, but don't be surprised when they perform poorly. TikTok prefers vertical video with text overlays that read naturally on the platform. Instagram Reels often have different pacing and sound usage. If you repost, at least remove any Instagram branding. The TikTok algorithm deprioritizes content that clearly came from another platform. I'd rather you film a new 15-second video than repurpose a 60-second Reel.

**Q: Do I need to pay for ads, or can I do this for free?**

You can absolutely grow organically for free. Every example I gave above started with zero ad spend. Ads are useful once you have a video that already performs well organically. Then you put $50 behind it to amplify. Going straight to ads without organic testing is like throwing darts in the dark.

**Q: How do I track if a TikTok actually brought someone into my store?**

Three ways. One: create a custom QR code for your store counter that says "show us this TikTok for 10% off." Two: mention a specific item in your video and ask customers to mention it at checkout. Three: use a unique phone number or booking link in your bio. A boutique in Nashville used a TikTok-only discount code and tracked $3,800 in direct sales from five videos. You can't optimize what you don't measure.
