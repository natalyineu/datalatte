Optimising your menu on delivery platforms can seem daunting, but it's not rocket science. By following these simple steps, you can increase revenue, reduce waste, and boost customer satisfaction.

### Step 1: Analyse Your Menu

Take a close look at your menu and identify dishes that are most popular, profitable, and easy to produce. Consider factors like ingredient costs, preparation time, and portion sizes. You want to focus on items that will appeal to a wide range of customers and maximise your profit margins.

### Step 2: Use Data to Inform Your Decisions

Look at your sales data to see which dishes are selling well and which ones are not. Use this information to adjust your menu and pricing strategy. For example, if you notice that customers are consistently ordering a certain dish, consider offering it as a special or promotion to drive sales.

### Step 3: Optmise Your Menu for Delivery

When optimising your menu for delivery, consider the following factors:

* **Portion sizes**: Make sure your portion sizes are suitable for delivery. You don't want to send out large portions that are difficult to eat on the go.
* **Packaging**: Choose packaging that is eco-friendly, durable, and easy to use. Consider using compostable or biodegradable containers.
* **Menu simplification**: Simplify your menu to make it easier for customers to order and for you to produce. Consider removing items that are difficult to produce or have low sales.

### Step 4: Use Visuals to Stand Out

Use high-quality visuals to make your menu stand out on delivery platforms. Consider adding:

* **Photos**: Add high-quality photos of your dishes to make them look appealing to customers.
* **Videos**: Create short videos that showcase your menu items and cooking process.
* **Description**: Write detailed descriptions of your dishes to highlight ingredients, cooking methods, and unique selling points.

### Tip: Offer Customisation Options

Offer customisation options to make your menu more appealing to customers. This could include:

* **Special requests**: Allow customers to request special modifications to their orders, such as gluten-free or vegan options.
* **Toppings**: Offer a range of toppings or sauces that customers can add to their orders.
* **Combo meals**: Offer combo meals that include a main dish, side, and drink.

### Warning: Don't Overcomplicate Your Menu

Avoid overcomplicating your menu by offering too many options or complex dishes. This can lead to:

* **Menu fatigue**: Customers may become overwhelmed by too many options and choose to order from a competitor.
* **Production issues**: Complex dishes can be difficult to produce in large quantities, leading to production issues and delays.

### Example: Popular Menu Items

Here are some popular menu items that you may consider adding to your delivery platform:

* **Coffee**: A classic coffee with a range of milk and sugar options.
* **Sandwiches**: A variety of sandwiches, including vegetarian and vegan options.
* **Salads**: A range of salads, including classic green salads and more substantial grain-based salads.
* **Desserts**: A selection of sweet treats, including cakes, cookies, and pastries.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Listing Every Menu Item (Even the Losers)

A bakery and lunch spot in Portland, Oregon called “Tabor Bread & Butter” had forty-seven items on their DoorDash menu. Forty-seven. The owner, Sarah, told me she wanted to give customers options. What she actually gave them was decision paralysis and a kitchen that couldn't keep up.

Here's what was happening: Sarah's team spent 22 minutes on average prepping orders for delivery. Her top five items (avocado toast, breakfast burrito, turkey club, chocolate croissant, lemonade) accounted for 73% of all sales. The remaining forty-two items? They dragged down prep speed, caused ingredient waste (she was throwing away $340/month in unused specialty ingredients), and confused customers who'd scroll past the good stuff looking for something that wasn't there.

I told Sarah to cut her delivery menu to eighteen items. Keep the top sellers, two seasonal specials, and three high-margin items (coffee, pastries, cookies) that cost almost nothing to make but round out the order.

**The fix in three steps:**
- Removed items that sold fewer than five times per month
- Moved the top five sellers to the top of each category
- Added high-margin add-ons ($1.50 cookie, $0.75 coffee add-on) as “suggested” items

**The outcome:** Prep time dropped from 22 minutes to 14 minutes per order. Average ticket value went from $14.20 to $17.80 because customers weren't overwhelmed and actually read the menu. Revenue increased $2,100/month within six weeks. Sarah told me she wished she'd done it two years ago. She also stopped ordering those weird specialty syrups nobody bought.

### Mistake #2: Pricing Without Accounting for Platform Fees

A BBQ joint in Austin, Texas called “Smoke & Soda” was losing money on every delivery order and didn't know it.

Here's the math the owner, Mike, hadn't run: His pulled pork sandwich was $12 in-store. He listed it at $12 on Uber Eats. Uber Eats took a 30% commission ($3.60). His food cost was 35% ($4.20). After packaging ($0.50), labor ($1.00), and the commission, he kept $2.70. That's before rent, utilities, insurance, and his own salary. He was effectively paying customers to order delivery.

Mike was running a $4,800/month delivery operation that generated $1,100 in actual profit after all costs. That's a 23% margin—except he thought he was making more and had no cash reserve when one of his smokers broke.

**What I told him to do:**
- Raise delivery menu prices 22% (not 30%—you don't want to look greedy, but you need to cover the fee)
- Add a $0.50 “service fee” line item that customers expect on platforms
- Bundle high-margin items (coleslaw, extra sauce, mac and cheese) as $3 add-ons

**The outcome:** His average delivery order went from $18 to $24. After the 30% commission and costs, he kept $5.80 per order instead of $2.70. Monthly delivery profit jumped from $1,100 to $3,600. He lost exactly three customers who complained about prices—but gained $2,500/month in profit. Mike bought a new smoker with cash three months later.

## Pricing Strategy for Delivery: The Math Most Owners Skip

Most small business owners price their delivery menu one of two ways: same as in-store, or the same plus a dollar. Both are wrong.

Let me show you the math I run for every client.

**Step one: Know your actual platform cost.**

Uber Eats and DoorDash take 25–30% per order. But that's not the real cost. The real cost includes chargebacks (0.5–2% of orders), promo costs (if you're paying for featured placement), and the time your staff spends packing delivery orders differently than dine-in orders.

For most of my clients, the real platform cost is 32–38%. Not 15%, not 20%. Thirty-two to thirty-eight percent.

**Step two: Factor in your food cost separately.**

If your food cost is 35% and your platform cost is 35%, you're at 70% before labor, rent, and everything else. That leaves 30% for everything else. You're losing money.

**Step three: Set your delivery price so you keep the same margin as dine-in.**

Say you make 60% margin on a $12 dine-in sandwich. That's $7.20 gross profit. To make the same $7.20 on delivery, you need to charge $18.46 after a 30% commission ($18.46 × 0.7 = $12.92, minus $5.72 food cost = $7.20). So you raise the delivery price to $18.46.

Customers expect delivery to cost more. They're paying for convenience. If you're in a competitive market where $18.46 looks high, you drop the price to $16.50 and accept a lower margin—but you make it up by adding high-margin add-ons (drinks, desserts, extra sauce) that bring the total ticket up.

**What I actually do for clients:**

I use Square's reporting tools to pull item-level cost data. Then I build a simple spreadsheet that calculates the ideal delivery price for every item based on:
- Food cost percentage
- Platform commission rate (actual, not advertised)
- Labor cost for packing (yes, this matters)
- Desired margin

A taco shop in Denver did this and found fifteen items they were losing money on. They either raised the price or removed the item. Their delivery margin went from 12% to 28% in one month.

## The Psychology of Menu Item Placement on Delivery Platforms

People don't read menus. They scan them. And on a phone screen, they're scanning faster than you think.

I've watched dozens of user testing sessions where people order food on delivery apps. Here's what actually happens:

- They spend 8–12 seconds on the menu screen
- They look at the top three items in each category
- If nothing catches their eye, they scroll halfway down
- Most never scroll past the halfway point
- They look at photos first, then price, then title
- They rarely read descriptions

This means your item placement is everything.

**The anchor item strategy:**

Most coffee shops and restaurants put their cheapest items at the top because they think it looks friendly. Bad move. Your top item acts as an anchor—it sets the price expectation for everything below it.

If your top item is $4.50 coffee, everything else looks expensive. If your top item is a $14.50 signature sandwich with a great photo, everything under $12 looks like a deal.

Place your highest-margin, most visually appealing item at the top of each category. This is called anchoring. It works because the human brain compares everything to the first thing it sees.

**The decoy item strategy:**

Add one item that's intentionally overpriced to make everything else look reasonable. A $7.50 cookie next to a $5.00 cookie? Nobody buys the $7.50 cookie. But the $5.00 cookie suddenly looks like a smart choice.

A smoothie shop in Los Angeles tested this. They added a $12 “premium smoothie” with expensive ingredients next to their $8 bestseller. The $12 item barely sold. The $8 smoothie's sales increased 40% because it now looked like a better value.

**The forced choice:**

Never give customers a single option. If you offer one size of coffee, sales will be lower than if you offer small ($3.50), medium ($4.50), and large ($5.50). Most people pick medium. The large exists to make medium look reasonable.

A bagel shop in New York tested this with coffee: three sizes instead of one. Average coffee spend went from $3.75 to $5.20. No new customers. Just people who were already buying coffee spending more because the medium looked like the smart middle choice.

**Where to apply this on delivery platforms:**

- Put your most profitable item first in each category
- Add a decoy item (slightly too expensive) next to your bestseller
- Offer three size/price options whenever possible
- Use the “popular” tag on your top items (platforms let you do this—use it)
- Place high-margin add-ons (desserts, drinks, sides) at the bottom of the checkout flow, not the top

A pet grooming studio in Austin tried this with their booking platform. They offered a “basic wash” at $35, “full groom” at $55, and “premium spa package” at $85. The premium package barely sold. The full groom, previously $50, jumped to $55 and sales increased 30% because customers compared it to $85, not to $35.

## Using Promotions Without Killing Your Margins

Delivery platforms love promotions. They'll email you every week suggesting “20% off” or “buy one get one.” Most business owners do it because they feel pressured. Then they wonder why their margins are shot.

Here's what I've learned from running promotions for dozens of clients: Discounts work, but only if you structure them right.

**The mistake most owners make:**

They discount their most popular item. “30% off our best-selling burger!” This makes sense on the surface—people love the burger, they'll come for the deal. But you're cannibalizing full-price sales. Customers who were going to buy the burger anyway now get it cheaper. You've just given away money.

**What actually works:**

Discount your slow movers or add-on items. Not your hero product.

A pizza place in Seattle had a “mushroom truffle pizza” that sold about six times a week. The ingredients cost $8.50. They made $16.50 selling it at $25. They ran a “Buy any pizza, add a mushroom truffle pizza for $10” promo. The mushroom truffle pizza became a $10 add-on with $8.50 in food cost—$1.50 profit. But the average ticket went from $22 to $34 because people upgraded to a large and added the promo pizza. Gross profit per order went from $12 to $18.

**Three promo structures that actually work:**

1. **Free add-on with minimum spend:** “Free cookie with orders over $20.” The cookie costs $0.40 to make. Average order goes from $18 to $24. You gain $6 in revenue for $0.40 cost. This works because customers add items they wouldn't have bought to hit the threshold.

2. **Bundles that hide individual item prices:** “Lunch combo: sandwich, chips, drink — $14.” Instead of pricing each item separately ($9 sandwich, $2 chips, $3 drink = $14), you present it as a deal. The chips cost $0.50 and the drink costs $0.80. Combined with a sandwich that costs $3.50 to make, your total cost is $4.80 for a $14 combo. That's a 66% margin compared to 55% if they bought the sandwich alone.

3. **Tiered discounts that reward high spend:** “$5 off orders over $30, $10 off orders over $50.” This pushes customers to add more items. A coffee shop in Portland tried this and saw average order value jump from $14 to $26 over two months. The $5 discount cost them money on paper, but the higher ticket more than made up for it.

**What to avoid:**

- Percentage discounts on your top five items (you're training customers to wait for sales)
- BOGO on low-margin items (you're losing money on every order)
- Running promotions more than two weeks at a time (customers learn to wait)
- Platform-sponsored promos that you don't control (Uber Eats will offer 40% off your entire menu without asking—opt out of this)

A hair salon in Nashville that uses Booksy tried a “20% off all services” promotion. It brought in new clients but attracted price-sensitive customers who never returned at full price. They switched to “$20 off first visit for clients who book a color service” ($150+ ticket). They kept the same number of new clients but the average ticket for promo clients was $145 versus $85 before. They also had a 40% return rate within 60 days versus 12% from the blanket discount.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need separate photos for every item on my delivery menu?**

Yes. Items with photos get 2–3x more orders than items without. Items with bad photos (grainy, dark, unappetizing) get fewer orders than items with no photo at all. You don't need a professional photographer for every item, but you do need good, consistent lighting and a clean background. One hour with a local photographer ($200–400) can cover 10–15 items. If you can't afford that, take photos by a window in natural light with a clean white plate. No filters. No shadows. No other food in the frame.

**Q: What if my items don't travel well? Should I still list them on delivery platforms?**

No. If your item turns into a sad, soggy mess after twenty minutes in a delivery bag, remove it. A deli in Philadelphia kept their “crispy chicken wrap” on the menu even though it arrived as a wet, wilted disaster. It had a 28% return rate and a 1.2-star rating on Uber Eats. They removed it and replaced it with a grain bowl that held up better. Their overall delivery rating went from 3.8 to 4.6 within three weeks. Customers don't blame the platform—they blame you.

**Q: Should I lower my prices on delivery to compete with the big chains?**

Never. You cannot win on price against McDonald's. They have economies of scale you don't have. What you can win on is quality, uniqueness, and customer experience. Raise your prices 15–25% on delivery to cover platform fees. Customers expect it. If you're worried about looking expensive, add a $0.50 “free delivery” threshold that encourages higher orders. A bagel shop in NYC raised their delivery prices 20% and lost exactly two customers. Their revenue went up 18% and their profit went up 40%.

**Q: How often should I update my delivery menu?**

At minimum, once per quarter. At best, once per month. Remove items that sell fewer than five times per month. Add seasonal items. Update photos if you changed the recipe or presentation. A coffee shop in Denver updates their menu the first Monday of every month. They remove the bottom three sellers, add one seasonal item, and rotate their add-ons. Their repeat customer rate on delivery is 47% versus the industry average of 22%.

**Q: Is it worth paying for promoted placement on DoorDash or Uber Eats?**

Only if you track the math. Promoted placement costs 10–15% more in commission. You need to see a 30–40% increase in orders to break even. Test it for two weeks with a $200 budget. If your cost per acquisition is lower than your average profit per order, keep it. If not, stop. Most small business owners waste $500–1,000/month on promoted placement they never measure. A pizza place in Austin ran a $300 promotion that brought in $900 in revenue but only $180 in profit after the extra commission. They turned it off and redirected the money to better photos.

**Q: Should I use the same menu on all delivery platforms or customize per platform?**

Customize. Uber Eats customers tend to order dinner and are willing to spend more. DoorDash customers order lunch and are more price-sensitive. GrubHub customers skew older and order family-size meals. List different items and price points on each platform. A sandwich shop in Chicago runs their premium items on Uber Eats ($14–18 range) and their value combos on DoorDash ($9–12). Same kitchen, different menus, higher profit on both.
