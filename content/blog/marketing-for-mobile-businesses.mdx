**2. Paid Ads: Reaching a Wider Audience**

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Relying solely on a Facebook page as your "website"**

A pet groomer in Austin, Texas, came to me last year. She had 4,000 Facebook followers, ran a mobile grooming van, and couldn’t figure out why bookings had flatlined for six months. Her logic: "Everyone is on Facebook, so that's where my clients will find me."

What she didn't see: Facebook suppresses business page posts unless you pay. Her organic reach had dropped to about 2% of her followers. She was also invisible to anyone searching "mobile dog groomer Austin" on Google. A potential client who doesn't use Facebook? She never existed to them.

**The fix:** I helped her set up a three-page website on Squarespace ($26/mo) and claimed her Google Business Profile. We added her service area (ZIP codes she actually covers), photos of her van and finished grooms, and a direct booking link through Booksy.

**The outcome:** Within 60 days, she was getting 12–15 booking requests per week directly from Google searches. Her monthly revenue went from $3,200 to $5,800. The Facebook page? She still posts, but she stopped paying for boosted posts. That alone saved her $200/mo she'd been throwing at engagement that never booked.

**Mistake #2: Running Google Ads with no location targeting**

A food truck owner in Portland, Oregon, told me he'd spent $1,800 on Google Ads over three months and got exactly 2 catering leads. He was ready to declare all paid ads a scam.

I looked at his account. He'd set the campaign to target "Portland metro area" — which sounds right, except Google interpreted that as a 50-mile radius. His ads were showing to people in Salem, Vancouver (WA), and Hood River. A food truck parked in Southeast Portland does not benefit from a click from someone 40 miles away who will never drive in for lunch.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the campaign targeting only the specific neighborhoods within a 5-mile radius of his usual parking spots. Added location exclusions to prevent showing outside city limits. Changed keywords from broad match ("food truck Portland") to phrase match ("Portland food truck catering" and "lunch truck near Pearl District"). Set bid adjustments for mobile devices to +25% since most food truck searches happen on phones.

**The outcome:** The next month: 14 leads, 6 confirmed catering bookings, $3,200 in revenue from a $500 ad spend. He's now running ads consistently and tracking every conversion through a simple call tracking number.

**Mistake #3: Ignoring your online booking system after you set it up**

A barbershop in Nashville (technically mobile — they had a chair rental in a barbershop but also did house calls) spent $300 on a Square Online booking setup, then let it rot. Appointment times didn't match actual availability. Clients showed up to find the barber wasn't there because they'd taken a house call during that slot. No-shows hit 20%.

The barber told me, "I set it and forgot it. I thought it would run itself."

**The fix:** We synced Square with Calendar, set buffer times between appointments for travel, added automated text reminders (Square does this for free), and blocked off house call days. The biggest change: requiring a credit card on file to book. Yes, a few people complained. The no-show rate dropped from 20% to 3%.

**The outcome:** Revenue predictability went up. Before, he'd have $800 days and $200 days with no warning. After the fix, average daily revenue stabilized at $550–$650. He also stopped double-booking himself, which had been costing about $400/mo in lost appointments and angry clients leaving bad reviews.

## Why Your Google Business Profile Is Probably Broken (And How to Fix It)

I've audited roughly 50 mobile business profiles in the past two years. Almost all of them have at least one critical error. Here are the three most common and how to fix them yourself in under an hour.

**1. You're marking yourself as "permanently closed" or "temporarily closed" by accident**

This happens constantly. You change your phone number, update your hours, or someone at Google flags you for a review check. Suddenly your profile shows "permanently closed" and you don't even realize it. A yoga instructor in Denver lost two weeks of bookings because she updated her phone number on Google and the system glitched her status to closed. She only noticed when a regular client called asking if she'd retired.

**Fix:** Open your Google Business Profile right now. Check the status under "Business information." If it says closed, change it. Then add a note in Google's support forum. It happens more often than you'd think.

**2. Your service area is either too broad or too specific**

Mobile businesses tend to either list their entire metro area (which makes Google show you to people you can't actually serve) or one single address (which is your home and you don't want clients showing up).

**Fix:** Set a "service area" of specific ZIP codes, not a vague radius. If you serve Austin but can't drive 45 minutes to Cedar Park, exclude that ZIP code. Use Google's "add a service area" option under Location. If you run a mobile coffee cart and only operate in downtown and the north side, list those. I've seen a pet sitter in Chicago go from 3 leads/week to 11 just by narrowing from "Cook County" to "Lincoln Park, Logan Square, Wicker Park, and Bucktown."

**3. You're ignoring Q&A and reviews**

Google's Q&A section is a customer-generated disaster zone if you ignore it. Random users (not necessarily clients) can ask questions, and anyone can answer. I've seen profiles where a competitor or a troll answered "Do they do house calls?" with "No, they're always late and overpriced." The business owner had no idea.

**Fix:** Monitor Q&A weekly. Answer every question yourself within 48 hours. For reviews: respond to every single one — positive and negative. A 4.2-star rating with no responses looks less trustworthy than a 4.0 rating where the owner thoughtfully replied to every review. Responding to negative reviews doesn't fix the problem, but it shows potential clients you care enough to address it. I've seen a massage therapist in Portland go from 4.1 to 4.6 stars in 90 days just by responding to reviews consistently.

## The Real Costs of Running Paid Ads for a Mobile Business

Many mobile business owners I talk to have tried Google Ads or Facebook Ads exactly once, burned $500–$1,000, got nothing, and swore them off forever. The problem wasn't ads. The problem was they didn't know what they were doing.

Let me give you realistic numbers for what works.

**For a mobile pet groomer in Denver:**
- Budget: $400–$600/mo (Google Ads, local service ads)
- Targeting: ZIP codes within 12 minutes driving time
- Keywords: "mobile dog grooming [neighborhood]" and "pet groomer near me"
- Expected outcome: 15–25 clicks/week, 5–8 booking form fills, $1,200–$2,000 in direct revenue

**For a food truck or mobile coffee cart in Chicago:**
- Budget: $300–$500/mo (Facebook/Instagram ads with location targeting)
- Creative: Video of your line, your best sandwich, a regular customer smiling while holding coffee
- Targeting: People within 3 miles of your current location (update daily), interest in "food" and "local eats"
- Expected outcome: 8–12 event bookings/hour of actual engagement per month, $1,500–$3,500 in event revenue

**For a mobile hair or makeup artist in Nashville:**
- Budget: $200–$350/mo (Instagram only)
- Targeting: Brides and wedding planners within 30 miles, ages 25–45
- Creative: Before/after photos, short testimonial clips from real clients
- Expected outcome: 10–15 inquiries/mo, 4–7 bookings, $1,800–$2,800 in revenue

**The trap most mobile businesses fall into:** They run ads that say "book now!" but don't have a clear, easy booking process. If I click your ad and land on a general website where I have to scroll, find a phone number, and call during business hours? You just wasted that click money. 

Instead: Use Google's "Call only" ads if you answer your phone reliably. Or use "Lead form" ads on Facebook where clients fill out their info and you follow up. Or use Booksy or Square's direct booking link in your ad so someone can book and pay in 90 seconds.

**A real example:** A mobile nail technician in Austin spent $350/mo on generic Instagram ads for 4 months. Got 2 leads total. I switched her to Facebook "Lead Ads" with a form that asked: "What neighborhood are you in?" and "What service do you need?" She started getting 8-10 leads/month immediately. The form weeds out people who aren't serious because it asks for specific info. She now converts about 60% of those leads into $80–$120 bookings.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a one-person operation. I barely have time to do the work, let alone market. How much time do I actually need to spend on this weekly?**

Honestly? About 3 hours. On Sunday evening, spend 45 minutes scheduling 2–3 social posts for the week (I use Later; there's a free plan). 30 minutes responding to reviews and Q&A. 15 minutes checking your Google Business Profile for any alerts. 60 minutes following up on leads from the previous week. The remaining 30 minutes is buffer. Batch it. Don't open your marketing apps every day or you'll waste 15 minutes each time scrolling.

**Q: I tried Google Ads once and got a $600 bill with zero bookings. What did I do wrong?**

You probably set the campaign to "maximize clicks" and didn't add negative keywords. Google will happily spend your budget showing ads to people searching "free dog grooming" or "cat grooming supplies near me." You also likely didn't add location targeting tight enough. Fix: Restart with $10/day, manual CPC bidding at $2–$3, add 20 negative keywords upfront, and target only 3–5 ZIP codes you know convert well. See if you get a lead in the first week. If not, pause and adjust before spending more.

**Q: Should I use Yelp?**

For mobile businesses? Usually no, unless you're willing to deal with their aggressive sales calls. Yelp's free listing is fine. Fill it out, respond to reviews, but do not pay for their advertising. I've never seen a mobile business get positive ROI from Yelp ads. Their model rewards places with high ratings and lots of reviews organically, but their organic traffic is smaller than Google's. Spend your time on Google and direct booking instead.

**Q: I have a Google Business Profile but I'm not showing up in local search. Why?**

Three likely reasons: (1) You haven't verified it fully — check for a yellow "not verified" banner. (2) Your service area is set too far and Google doesn't know where to rank you. (3) You have no recent reviews or posts. Google favors profiles that look active. Post once a week to Google Business (a photo, a special offer, a "here's where I'll be this week" update). Ask 3–4 clients per month to leave a review. Do that for 60 days and watch your ranking improve.

**Q: I only want to pay for results. Is there a way to do pay-per-lead instead of pay-per-click?**

Yes. Google Local Services Ads (LSAs) run on a pay-per-lead model. You set a budget, Google charges when someone contacts you through the ad, and you only pay for actual leads. It's available for certain service categories: pet grooming, hair salons, fitness trainers, home services. You have to pass a background check (which costs about $50) and have decent reviews. But if you qualify, it's the best model for mobile businesses. The leads are higher quality because Google pre-qualifies them.

**Q: How do I get better reviews without sounding like I'm begging?**

Don't ask for reviews right after the transaction. Wait 24 hours. Send a text or email through your booking system (Booksy and Square both do this automatically) that says: "Thanks for booking with me yesterday. If you have 30 seconds, a review on Google helps other pet owners find me. No pressure, either way." That's it. Don't offer discounts for reviews — that violates Google's terms and can get your profile suspended. Just ask consistently and make it easy with a direct link.
