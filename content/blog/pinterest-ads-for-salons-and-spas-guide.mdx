### Setting Up Your Pinterest Account

Before you start running ads, you need to make sure your Pinterest account is set up correctly. This includes:

* Creating a business account
* Uploading a profile picture and cover photo
* Writing a compelling bio that includes your business's name, location, and contact information
* Adding relevant keywords to your profile and pins

### Creating Engaging Pins

Your pins are the face of your business on Pinterest, so it's essential to create engaging and visually appealing content. This includes:

* High-quality images that showcase your services and products
* Using relevant keywords in your pin descriptions
* Including a clear call-to-action (CTA) in your pin description
* Pinning regularly to keep your audience engaged

### Targeting Your Audience

Pinterest's targeting options are robust, and you can target your ads based on a variety of factors, including:

* Location
* Age
* Interests
* Behaviors
* Job title

You can also use Pinterest's Lookalike audiences to target people who are similar to your existing customers.

### Optimizing Your Ads

Once you've set up your ads, it's essential to optimize them for the best possible performance. This includes:

* A/B testing different ad creative and targeting options
* Monitoring your ad spend and adjusting your budget as needed
* Using Pinterest's built-in analytics to track your ad performance

### How to Create a Seasonal Campaign Calendar for Your Salon or Spa

One of the biggest advantages of Pinterest is its forward-looking nature. Users plan their next haircut, facial, or manicure weeks in advance. For salons and spas, this means you can capitalize on seasonal trends to fill your appointment book before the rush hits.

Start by mapping out your year around key moments: Valentine’s Day (couples massages and gift cards), wedding season (bridal hair trials and makeup packages), summer (bright nail colors and deep conditioning treatments), and the holiday season (spa gift sets and blowout packages). For each season, create 10–15 pins that highlight these services. Use Pinterest Trends (available in your analytics dashboard) to see exactly when searches for "bridal updo ideas" or "winter skincare routine" peak in your region.

For example, a salon in Toronto might see a spike in "holiday party hairstyles" searches starting in mid-November. By launching a campaign for "Festive Blowout Packages" on November 1st, you can capture those users while they're still planning. Set your budget to ramp up two weeks before the peak search date, then taper off after. This approach helped one of our clients—a spa in Sydney—increase December bookings by 40% with just $800 in ad spend.

### Using Video Pins to Showcase Your Services

Static images are great, but video pins drive 2.5x more engagement on Pinterest. For salons and spas, video is your secret weapon. A 15-second clip of a stylist creating a balayage transformation or a therapist performing a hot stone massage tells a story that a photo simply can't.

Here's how to create video pins that convert: Keep them under 30 seconds (Pinterest's algorithm favors shorter videos for discovery). Shoot in vertical format (9:16 ratio) to fill the screen on mobile devices. Start with a "hook" in the first three seconds—like a before-and-after split or a close-up of a product in action. End with a clear CTA overlay, such as "Book Now" or "Get the Look."

Don't overthink production. A smartphone with good lighting and a tripod is enough. Film during your quiet hours—early morning or late afternoon—so you're not interrupting client experiences. For a hair salon, show a quick styling tutorial. For a spa, film a serene treatment room setup with soft music. Upload these as Idea Pins (Pinterest's native video format), which get bonus distribution in the home feed. One of our pet grooming clients in Austin saw a 55% increase in click-throughs after switching from static pins to short video clips of their fluffiest clients.

### Retargeting Users Who Almost Booked

Not every user who clicks your ad will book immediately. That's normal. But you can bring them back with retargeting campaigns. Pinterest allows you to create custom audiences based on actions users took on your website, like visiting your booking page or viewing a specific service.

Set up the Pinterest Tag on your website (it's a small piece of code that tracks visitor behavior). Then, create a retargeting campaign for users who visited your "Services" page but didn't complete a booking. Offer them a gentle incentive, like "10% off your first appointment" or "Free consultation with any haircut." Because these users already showed interest, your cost per conversion will likely drop by 20–30%.

For spas, consider retargeting users who clicked on a specific service pin—say, "Luxury Facial"—but didn't convert. Show them a new pin featuring a limited-time bundle (e.g., "Facial + Neck Massage for $89") to sweeten the deal. We've seen retargeting campaigns for salons in the UK achieve a 45% higher conversion rate than cold traffic campaigns, with an average cost per booking under $12.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched enough small business owners hand Pinterest Ad money to the algorithm gods. Here are the three mistakes I see most often — along with the fixes that actually turned things around.

### Mistake #1: Targeting "Women" Instead of "Women Who Are Looking for What You Do"

A salon in Austin, TX came to me after burning $1,200 on Pinterest Ads over six weeks. The owner, who runs a mid-priced blowout and color studio near South Congress, had set her targeting to "women, ages 25–55, within 25 miles." That's it.

The result: 48,000 impressions, 212 link clicks, and exactly 0 booked appointments.

The problem wasn't Pinterest. It was that she was showing her ads to anyone with a pulse. Pinterest's algorithm will happily spend your budget showing your balayage pin to someone who last colored her hair in 2019 and has no intention of changing that. It will also show it to people who only use Pinterest for wedding planning and haven't opened the app in three months.

**What we did instead:** We narrowed her targeting to "women in Austin" with interests like "Hairstyles," "Balayage," "Blowout," "Hair Color," and "Salon." Then we layered on keyword targeting — phrases people actually search for on Pinterest like "blonde balayage Austin," "hair salon near me," and "summer hair colors." We also excluded anyone who hadn't been active on Pinterest in the last 30 days.

**Outcome:** The next $800 she spent generated 37 booked appointments over four weeks. That works out to roughly $4,600 in completed services at her average ticket. The conversion tracking finally showed actual revenue attributed to Pinterest, not just vanity metrics.

The fix is simple: don't let Pinterest guess who might be interested. Tell it exactly who you want, using the words they're already typing into the search bar.

### Mistake #2: Using Stock Photos Instead of Your Actual Work

A nail spa in Nashville spent $600 on a Pinterest campaign featuring professionally shot stock photos of hands with flawless nails. The images were beautiful. The problem? They looked exactly like every other nail ad on the platform.

CTR was 2.3%, which isn't terrible. But zero appointments came from it. Zero. The owner told me, "I thought if the photos looked professional, people would trust us more."

**What we did instead:** We replaced every stock image with photos taken on an iPhone 14 in good natural light — close-ups of actual nails done by their techs, showing the salon's branded environment in the background. We added the specific service name and price as an overlay on each pin. "Gel Manicure + Design — $55." "Full Set Ombre — $75."

**Outcome:** CTR jumped to 7.1%. More importantly, the next $500 in ad spend generated $2,100 in booked services over three weeks. The owner said people walked in mentioning the specific pins they'd seen.

Stock photos signal "I'm not really sure what I'm selling." Your actual work signals "This is exactly what you'll get." On Pinterest, which is a visual search engine, authenticity outperforms polish almost every time.

### Mistake #3: No Conversion Tracking — Flying Blind

A med spa in Portland, OR had been running Pinterest Ads for four months. When I asked how much revenue the ads drove, the owner said, "We get about 15 calls a month from Pinterest, I think."

"I think" is not a data point.

They were running ads to their website's booking page but had never installed the Pinterest conversion tag. They had no idea which pins led to bookings, which targeting was working, or whether they were even breaking even.

**What we did instead:** We installed the Pinterest base tag, set up a "Checkout" conversion event, and added a "Lead" event for their contact form. We also set up a "PageVisit" event to measure traffic. Within two weeks, we could see exactly which pins drove bookings and which ones just generated clicks that bounced immediately.

**Outcome:** The $900 they had been spending monthly was actually generating about $3,800 in revenue. But 40% of that came from two specific pin designs — the rest was essentially throwing money away. We paused the underperformers, doubled down on what worked, and the same $900 started generating $5,600.

If you can't measure what's happening after the click, you're gambling. The Pinterest tag is free. Install it before you spend a dollar.

## Budgeting: What a Working Pinterest Ad Spend Actually Looks Like

I'll give you a real budget breakdown based on what I've seen work for salon and spa clients in similar markets.

**For a single-location salon or spa in a mid-sized US city (Denver, Nashville, Austin):**

Start at $500–$800 per month. Yes, that's real money. But here's why it makes sense: the lifetime value of a salon client who rebooks every six weeks is roughly $1,200–$2,400 per year in services alone, not counting retail product sales. You only need to acquire 5–8 new clients from Pinterest each month for that $500 spend to be profitable.

Break it down like this:

- $350–$500 for prospecting — reaching new people who search for services you offer
- $150–$200 for retargeting — showing ads to people who visited your website but didn't book
- $50–$100 for testing — trying new pin designs and audiences

The worst thing you can do is spend $100 and decide Pinterest doesn't work. At that spend level, you're statistically unlikely to get enough data for the algorithm to optimize. You'll get impressions from people who are already in your targeting, but not enough conversions for Pinterest to figure out who's actually likely to book.

If $500 feels too high, start with $300 and focus entirely on retargeting. People who've already visited your site are your warmest leads. A retargeting campaign at $300/month with a specific offer ("Book by Friday, get 15% off your first service") has a much higher chance of showing ROI than spreading that same $300 across cold audiences.

## Tracking and Attribution: How to Actually Know If Pinterest Ads Are Working

Most small business owners I meet have a blind spot here. They check Pinterest's dashboard, see "50 clicks," and assume that means 50 people are interested. It doesn't. Here's what matters instead.

**Set up UTM parameters on every pin.** Use Google's Campaign URL Builder — it's free. Build links like this:

- Source: pinterest
- Medium: cpc
- Campaign: springblowout2025
- Content: pin_design_1

This lets you see Pinterest traffic in Google Analytics separately from organic reach, referrals, or direct visits. You'll know exactly which campaign drove the traffic and which pin design got the click.

**Connect your booking system to tracking.** If you use Booksy, Vagaro, or Square Appointments, most have some form of conversion tracking or can generate unique booking links. Use those links in your Pinterest ads so you can attribute bookings directly. I worked with a salon in Chicago that used Booksy's built-in analytics alongside Pinterest's tag and discovered that 60% of their Pinterest bookings happened within 24 hours of clicking the ad — not immediately. Without tracking, they would have assumed the ads weren't working because they didn't get same-day bookings.

**Track phone calls.** Most of your bookings probably come through phone, not online forms. Use a call tracking service like CallRail or the built-in call tracking from Google Voice. Assign a unique phone number to your Pinterest ads. This is the only way to know if someone found you on Pinterest and called you. At roughly $30/month for basic call tracking, it's cheaper than one wasted ad campaign.

**Set a realistic attribution window.** Pinterest is not Google Ads. People don't always click and book in the same session. They'll save a pin, come back three days later, search your name, and then call. Set your Pinterest attribution window to at least 7 days click-through and 1 day view-through. If you use a 1-day click window, you're underreporting by a lot.

## Retargeting: The Most Effective Pinterest Strategy for Salons

If I had to pick exactly one Pinterest strategy for a hair salon or spa, it would be retargeting. Here's why.

Someone visits your website, browses your services page, maybe checks pricing. Then they leave. Without retargeting, that person is gone. With retargeting, you can show them a pin on Pinterest — the same platform they use for inspiration — that reminds them you exist.

I set up a retargeting campaign for a nail studio in Brooklyn. They had decent traffic to their site from organic search and Instagram, but an abysmal booking rate. About 80% of visitors left without booking.

We installed the Pinterest tag, built an audience of "anyone who visited our booking or services pages in the last 30 days," and ran a simple campaign with one pin: "First-time client? 20% off your first gel manicure. Book here."

**Results from a $200/month retargeting spend:** 14 bookings in the first month. Average service value: $65. That's $910 in revenue from $200 in spend. And because those clients had a good experience, many became regulars — which makes the true ROI much higher than the first-visit numbers suggest.

The pin used a before/after of an actual client's nails, with the discount offer prominently overlaid. No clever copy. No lifestyle shot of a woman laughing while holding a coffee. Just: here's what we do, here's the deal, click here.

Retargeting works because the people you're reaching already know you exist. They just needed a nudge. Pinterest is especially good for this because it's a platform built around saving and returning to ideas — the user behavior patterns already align with "I'll come back to this later."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it take to see actual appointments from Pinterest Ads?**

If you set up conversion tracking correctly and use strong images of your actual work, you should see the first bookings within 7–10 days. The algorithm needs about 50 conversions to start optimizing well. For most salons, that takes 2–4 weeks at $500/month. If you're not seeing appointments by week three, the issue is usually targeting or creative — not the platform.

**Q: Is Pinterest better than Instagram or Facebook for a salon?**

For most salons and spas, yes — at least for acquiring new clients. Pinterest users are actively searching for ideas and services. They're in planning mode. Instagram users are scrolling for entertainment. A Pinterest user searching "blonde balayage Austin" is closer to booking than an Instagram user who happens to see your Reel. Facebook is better for local community building and events. Use them together if you can, but if I had to pick one for client acquisition, it's Pinterest.

**Q: What if my salon is in a small town?**

Targeting works the same way. Set your location radius to 15–25 miles. Your cost per click will likely be lower because competition is lower. The key is making sure your pins include location-specific keywords: "hair salon Bozeman," "spa Missoula." I've seen salons in towns of 30,000 people generate consistent bookings from Pinterest because they were the only local salon running well-targeted ads.

**Q: Do I need to pin every day for ads to work?**

No. Organic pinning and paid ads are separate systems. You can run ads without ever posting organically. That said, having an active organic presence helps build credibility when people click through to your profile. If you can post 3–5 organic pins per week, do it. If you can't, the ads will still work. The algorithm doesn't penalize you for low organic activity.

**Q: What's the minimum budget that actually works?**

$300 per month for retargeting only. $500 per month if you want to include prospecting. Below $300, you're unlikely to get enough data for the algorithm to optimize, and you'll probably decide Pinterest doesn't work when really you just underfunded it.

**Q: How do I know if someone booked because of my Pinterest ad?**

Set up the Pinterest conversion tag and connect it to a "Checkout" or "Lead" event on your booking page. If you take phone bookings, use a call tracking number unique to Pinterest. Ask every new client "How did you hear about us?" during check-in and track it manually in a spreadsheet or your booking software. Multiple data sources are better than one.
