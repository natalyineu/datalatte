## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Overly Broad Location Targeting

**The story:** A garage door company in Austin, TX — let's call them Austin Garage Kings — came to me after three months of Google Ads. They'd spent $4,200. They'd gotten 237 clicks. They'd booked exactly one job, and that job paid $150 for a spring repair. That's a $4,050 loss before you count their time.

I pulled their account. Their location targeting included "within 50 miles of Austin." If you've ever driven around Texas, you know that 50 miles puts you in places like San Marcos, Georgetown, and Bastrop. People in Bastrop aren't calling an Austin company at 9 PM when their garage door won't close. They're calling the guy who lives three streets over.

The uncomfortable truth is that service area businesses need tight radius targeting — tighter than most Google Ads guides recommend.

**The fix:** We narrowed the radius to 12 miles. We also used location exclusions. If most of their calls came from specific zip codes (78701, 78702, 78704, 78745 — the central and south Austin areas where older homes with aging garage doors sit), we bid higher there. For outlying zip codes, we either lowered bids or excluded them entirely. We also added a negative location layer: if a search happened outside the 12-mile radius, the ad didn't show.

**The outcome:** The next month, they spent $1,400, got 58 clicks, and booked 11 jobs worth $4,800. Their cost per booking dropped from $4,050 to $127. That's not a "leverage your synergy" result. That's a "you were showing ads to people who would never call you" problem.

### Mistake #2: Bidding on "Garage Door" Without Modifiers

**The story:** A Denver-based company — Rocky Mountain Door — had been running Google Ads for eight months. They were bidding on "garage door repair Denver" and "garage door installation Denver." On paper, those look like the right keywords. But their average cost per click was $9.87, and their conversion rate was 1.2%. That's brutal.

I looked at their search terms report. People were finding their ads when they searched "how to fix a garage door yourself," "garage door parts Denver," and "garage door opener remote replacement." None of those searchers want a service call. They want a YouTube tutorial or a trip to Home Depot.

The real kicker? Their ads were also triggering for "garage door sales Denver" — people shopping for a new door six months out, not someone whose spring snapped at 7 AM.

**The fix:** We restructured their keyword strategy into three groups:

- **Urgent repair keywords:** "emergency garage door repair Denver," "garage door spring broke," "garage door won't open," "24 hour garage door service Denver" — high intent, higher bids
- **Install keywords:** "replace garage door Denver," "new garage door installation cost," "garage door replacement quote" — moderate intent, moderate bids
- **Noise keywords:** added as negatives — "parts," "DIY," "how to," "manual," "remote," "opener only," "sectional door" — these went into a negative keyword list

We also added phrase match and exact match only. Broad match was bleeding them dry.

**The outcome:** His average CPC dropped to $5.20. Conversion rate climbed to 4.8%. In month two, he spent $2,100 and booked $9,600 in jobs. He told me later he'd wasted about $7,200 over the previous eight months on clicks that would never convert. I ordered a second coffee I did not need. No regrets.

### Mistake #3: Not Using Call Extensions or Call-Only Ads

**The story:** A garage door company in Nashville — Music City Doors — had a nice website with a contact form. They were getting form submissions. But their phone wasn't ringing. For a garage door service, that's a problem. When someone's car is stuck in the garage because the motor died at 8 AM, they're not filling out a form. They're calling the first number they see.

They had call extensions turned off. They were paying for clicks that went to a landing page with a form. The average time from click to form submission? I didn't measure it, but it's irrelevant. They were losing the urgent repair calls to competitors who had their phone numbers in the ad text and a click-to-call button.

**The fix:** We turned on call extensions. More importantly, we created a separate call-only campaign for the "urgent repair" keyword group. Call-only ads don't send people to a website — they trigger a phone call directly. We also set up call tracking through Google Ads so they could see which keywords actually generated calls versus form submissions.

We wrote ad copy that started with the phone number: "(615) 555-0122 | Garage Door Won't Open? We're There in 60 Min." That's not subtle. It's not clever. It works.

**The outcome:** In the first two weeks, call volume tripled. Their cost per call was $12. Their average repair job was $320. That's a 26x return on the ad spend if half of those calls book. Most did. The form submissions dropped by 40%, but that didn't matter — the calls were higher intent. People who call are ready to book. People who fill out a form are still shopping.

## What to Do About Competitors Bidding on Your Business Name

Here's something most Google Ads guides skip: another garage door company can bid on your business name. Your name. The one you spent years building. And if their ad shows up when someone searches "Austin Garage Kings," you're paying for that click — or losing it.

**The real story:** A garage door company in Phoenix called Sun City Door had a competitor — let's call them Desert Garage — running ads on Sun City Door's brand name. Every time someone searched "Sun City Door repair," Desert Garage's ad appeared above Sun City's organic listing. Sun City was paying for brand clicks that sometimes went to Desert Garage's landing page.

**The fix:** You have two options.

First: bid on your own brand name. It's cheap. Branded keywords usually cost $0.50–$1.50 per click versus $5–$10 for generic keywords. Your ad will show above theirs because Google prioritizes relevance and quality score when the search matches your domain.

Second: file a trademark complaint with Google. If a competitor is using your trademarked business name in their ad copy (not just bidding on it), you can report them. Google usually removes those ads within a few days.

**The tools:** This is where Google Ads' built-in auction insights report is useful. It shows you who's bidding against you in the same auctions. If you see a competitor showing up for your brand terms, you know exactly who's doing it. I check this for clients every month. It takes two minutes.

**The outcome for Sun City:** They started bidding $0.80 on their own brand name. They spent $120 in the first month and got 38 clicks. Those 38 clicks converted at 22% — nine jobs worth $3,100. Their competitor eventually stopped bidding on their name when they realized they couldn't win the top position without overpaying.

## Why Your Landing Page Matters More Than Your Ads

I've seen garage door companies with beautiful ads that send people to a slow, cluttered website. Google might click, but people don't stay. This isn't a "user experience" theory — it affects your actual costs.

**How Google calculates quality score:** When someone clicks your ad and immediately hits the back button, Google notes that. Enough of those, and your quality score drops. A lower quality score means you pay more per click. I've seen accounts where a poor landing page added $3–$4 to every click.

**What a good garage door landing page looks like:** I tested this with a client in Chicago — Windy City Garage Doors. Their old page had a hero image, four paragraphs about their company history, a photo of their fleet, and a contact form buried at the bottom. Average time on page: 18 seconds. Conversion rate: 0.9%.

We built a new page. Here's what it had:

- **Headline:** "Garage Door Broken? We'll Be There in Under 90 Minutes"
- **Subheadline:** "Same-day service • No overtime charges • Licensed & insured"
- **Call-to-action button:** "Call Now: (312) 555-0199" — visible without scrolling
- **Below the fold:** Three icons — "Free Estimate," "Emergency Service Available," "Senior & Military Discount"
- **One short testimonial:** "Called at 7 AM. Door fixed by 8:15. — Sarah K., Lincoln Park"
- **Phone number in the sticky header on mobile**
- **Load time under two seconds** (we used Google PageSpeed Insights to check this, then compresse all images)

**The outcome:** Average time on page went from 18 seconds to 2 minutes and 14 seconds. Conversion rate hit 5.6%. Quality score went from 4 to 7. Their cost per click dropped by $1.80. The page took one afternoon to build on their existing Squarespace site.

## How to Set Up Call Tracking So You Know What's Working

This is the part most small business owners skip, and it costs them thousands. Without call tracking, you're flying blind. You don't know which keywords are generating calls, which ads are working, or whether that $500 campaign did anything.

**The setup:** Use Google Ads' built-in call reporting. It's free. You get a Google forwarding number that shows in your ads. When someone calls it, Google records the call duration, the area code, and which ad they clicked. You can also listen to recordings and set up conversions for calls that last longer than a certain time — say, 60 seconds.

**The real example:** A garage door company in Portland, OR — Rose City Garage — thought their "garage door installation" keywords were driving all their business. They were spending $1,800/month on those keywords. When I set up call tracking for them, we found that 67% of their phone calls actually came from "broken garage door spring" and "garage door emergency repair." The "installation" keywords generated three calls in two months, and two of those were people asking if they could buy a single panel. Not install — buy the panel, like it's a retail store.

**The fix:** We shifted $1,200/month from install keywords to emergency repair keywords. Their cost per booking dropped by half, and they started answering more calls that actually booked jobs.

**Additional tools:** If your budget allows ($30/month), use CallRail or WhatConverts. They integrate with Google Ads, show you the exact keyword that triggered the call, and can route calls to different phones depending on the time of day. For a garage door company that gets emergency calls at 2 AM, that's useful.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads for my garage door business?**

Start with $1,000–$1,500 per month for one city. That's enough to get meaningful data without bleeding cash. If you're in a more expensive market like NYC or San Francisco, budget $2,000–$2,500 because CPCs are higher. Track your cost per booking. If you're paying $50 per booking and your average job is $300, you're in good shape. If you're spending $200 per booking, something's wrong — usually the keywords or the landing page.

**Q: Won't I just waste money on people clicking my ads by accident?**

You'll waste some — that's normal. The goal is to minimize it. Use negative keywords to filter out searches like "garage door parts," "garage door opener battery," and "DIY." Also check your search terms report weekly for the first month. That's where you'll see the nonsense queries. Add them as negatives immediately. Most guides skip this step because it's tedious. Do it anyway.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads myself, or do I need a professional?**

You can absolutely run it yourself if you're willing to learn. Google's own certification courses are free. But be honest with yourself: do you have two hours a week to audit keywords, check search terms, review call recordings, and test ad copy? If not, hire someone. A bad DIY account will burn $500/month with nothing to show for it. A good freelancer or agency will charge $500–$1,000/month to manage it and typically more than pay for themselves.

**Q: How long until I see results?**

If you set it up right, you'll see calls within the first week. But a steady flow of profitable bookings usually takes 4–6 weeks. The first two weeks are for testing keywords and ads. The next two are for cutting what doesn't work and doubling down on what does. By week six, you should have a clear picture of your cost per booking and conversion rate. Anyone promising instant results is selling something.

**Q: Should I be on Yelp too, or just Google Ads?**

Yes, but not the way most people think. Yelp's ads are expensive and their click fraud is notoriously bad. But Yelp's organic presence matters. Claim your Yelp Business Page. Make sure your hours, phone number, and services are correct. Respond to reviews. That free listing will show up when people search "garage door repair [your city]" and will give you credibility. Paid Yelp ads? I've seen very few service businesses make those profitable. Start with Google Ads, get that working, then test Yelp if you have budget left over.

**Q: What about Google Guarantee / Local Service Ads?**

This is worth testing. Local Service Ads (LSAs) are the ads at the very top of Google with the green checkmark. You pay per lead instead of per click. The catch: you need to pass a background check and business verification, and they only work for certain service categories in certain cities. For garage door services, LSAs are available in most major US markets. Cost per lead varies but is usually $15–$35. The leads tend to be high intent because they trust the "Google Guaranteed" badge. I'd allocate 20% of your budget to LSAs and 80% to traditional Google Ads to start.
