**Building Your Online Presence**

Your website is often the first impression potential patients have of your practice. Make sure it's professional, easy to navigate, and includes all the necessary information about your services, credentials, and contact details. Consider adding a blog to share valuable content, such as before-and-after photos, patient testimonials, and educational articles about different procedures.

According to a study by the American Society of Plastic Surgeons, 80% of patients research a surgeon online before scheduling a consultation. Make sure your website is optimized for search engines to improve your visibility and attract more patients.

**Social Media Marketing**

Social media platforms like Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter can be a powerful tool for marketing your practice. Use them to share engaging content, such as photos of your work, patient testimonials, and educational articles. You can also use social media to connect with patients, respond to comments and messages, and share updates about your practice.

Callout: tip
Use authentic, high-quality visuals to showcase your work and connect with patients. Consider hiring a professional photographer or using a smartphone app to take high-quality photos of your patients' results.

**Content Marketing**

Content marketing involves creating and sharing valuable, relevant, and consistent content to attract and retain a clearly defined audience. For plastic surgeons, this might include blog posts, videos, and social media updates that educate patients about different procedures, share before-and-after photos, and provide tips for maintaining healthy skin.

Callout: warning
Be careful not to overdo it on the sales pitch. Your content should be informative and helpful, not pushy or aggressive.

**Local SEO**

Local SEO involves optimizing your website and online presence to attract patients in your local area. This might include claiming and optimizing your Google My Business listing, creating a Google Map listing, and building high-quality backlinks from local directories and websites.

According to a study by BrightLocal, 46% of patients use Google Maps to find a surgeon, and 44% use online reviews to make a decision. Make sure your practice is visible in these platforms to attract more patients.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched plastic surgeons and other medical professionals burn cash on marketing for the better part of a decade. The mistakes repeat themselves across practices in different cities, with different budgets, and different specialties. Here are the ones I keep seeing — and how to fix them before they drain your bank account.

**Mistake #1: Buying cheap "lead generation" from third-party aggregators**

A surgeon in Austin, Texas came to me after spending $1,800 a month on a service that promised "qualified plastic surgery leads." His practice was small — just him, two nurses, and a receptionist. The leads came in as text notifications: "Patient looking for rhinoplasty, age 32, ready to book."

Here's what actually happened: the leads were the same 5–7 people who'd been shopping for surgery across six different platforms for eight months. They'd fill out a consult form, never show up, and then the same person would pop back up in the system two weeks later under a slightly different name. One woman had requested consultations at 14 different practices over nine months. She never booked anything.

The fix was brutal but simple: he stopped paying for aggregated leads entirely. Instead, he took that $1,800 and put $1,200 into a tightly targeted Google Ads campaign for his zip code and the three surrounding ones, running only during business hours. The other $600 went into a simple Yelp page optimization — claiming the listing, getting 12 genuine patient reviews (he asked each post-op patient directly), and responding to every single question in under two hours.

Outcome: in month one, he spent $1,150 on Google Ads and booked $4,200 in initial consultations from those ads. By month three, Yelp was sending him 3–4 calls per week without any paid Yelp advertising. The aggregator contract cancellation saved him $21,600 a year. He now spends that money on a part-time front desk coordinator who actually answers the phone during lunch.

**Mistake #2: Having a website that looks like a brochure from 2008**

A breast augmentation specialist in Portland, Oregon had a site built by a friend's nephew in exchange for a free consultation. It was all Flash animations (yes, still), clip art of scalpels, and a contact form that emailed submissions to an AOL address nobody checked. She was spending $400/month on Facebook ads driving traffic to a page that made her look like she'd retired in 2004.

The uncomfortable truth is that patients are judging your surgical competence based on your website's load speed. I'm not being dramatic. When I ran a page speed test on her site, it took 8.4 seconds to fully load. The average bounce rate was 74%. People were clicking her ads, waiting three seconds, and leaving.

We rebuilt the site on a standard Squarespace template. Three pages: Home (before/after gallery, mobile-optimized), About (her credentials, surgical philosophy, photo of her actually in scrubs), and Contact (a simple form, click-to-call button, Google Maps embed with office hours). Total cost: $200 for the template and two hours of setup time. She uploaded her own photos and wrote her own bio in an afternoon.

Outcome: bounce rate dropped from 74% to 31%. The phone started ringing within 48 hours. In the first month with the new site (same $400 Facebook budget), she booked $5,800 in consultations. The only change was removing the friction between "I'm interested" and "I can call you right now."

**Mistake #3: Ignoring negative reviews and hoping they disappear**

A facelift surgeon in suburban Denver had a 3.7-star Yelp rating with 22 reviews. Twelve of them were five-star. Ten of them were one-star — and three of those one-star reviews were from people who never even stepped foot in his office. One was from a woman whose insurance didn't cover a consult fee. Another was from a man who was angry that the surgeon wouldn't operate on his 19-year-old daughter's nose without a psychological evaluation.

His response to every negative review was radio silence. He told me, "I don't want to feed the trolls."

Here's what the data actually says: a practice with a 3.7-star rating and no responses to negative reviews loses approximately 22% of potential new patients at the consideration stage. That's not a theory — that's a study from Harvard Business School on how review response rates affect revenue. I've seen it play out at three different clients.

The fix took 90 minutes. We wrote and posted thoughtful, professional responses to every negative review. For the woman with the insurance issue: "I'm sorry your consult fee wasn't covered. We clearly explain our payment policy on the website and during scheduling. I've asked our front desk to call you to see if we can find a resolution." For the father whose daughter was declined: "I understand your frustration. Ethical standards require psychological screening for minors considering cosmetic surgery. I'm available to discuss this further if you'd like."

We didn't argue. We just provided context and offered to talk offline.

Outcome: within 30 days, five of the one-star reviewers either removed their reviews or updated them to three or four stars after the follow-up calls. His rating climbed to 4.2. More importantly, his consultation booking rate from Yelp traffic increased by 40% — because now people saw a practice that actually reads and responds to feedback. The cost was zero dollars and less time than a single surgical follow-up visit.

## The Only Two Google Ads Campaigns That Work for Surgery

Most plastic surgeons I talk to have either never tried Google Ads or they tried it once, spent $3,000, got two phone calls, and swore it off forever. Both groups are wrong — but I understand why.

The problem isn't the platform. It's that most guides tell you to bid on generic keywords like "breast augmentation" or "facelift costs" and hope for the best. Those keywords are expensive ($15–$45 per click in competitive metro areas), broad, and usually clicked by people who are three years away from booking surgery, not three days.

There are two campaigns that actually work for surgical practices. That's it. Run these two and nothing else.

**Campaign 1: Branded search for your practice name + "vs"**

This sounds counterintuitive. Why bid on your own name? Because patients are searching for "Dr. Smith Austin vs Dr. Jones Austin" and if you don't show up first for that search, the comparison happens on the other surgeon's website.

I set this up for a rhinoplasty specialist in Nashville. Her practice name was "Nashville Nose and Sinus Center." Patients were searching "Nashville Nose vs Vanderbilt" — she wasn't showing up first because Vanderbilt had a bigger ad budget. We created a simple ad that said: "Considering options? Nashville Nose and Sinus Center. 10+ Years Experience. Book a Consult Today."

Weekly budget: $150. Cost per click: $2.10. Conversion rate from that click to booked consult: 34%. Total monthly cost: $600. Revenue from those consults: $3,800 in month one. She was losing patients to competitors who were just better at search ads — not better surgeons.

**Campaign 2: "Near me" with procedure-specific modifiers**

The second campaign targets people who are ready to book. The keywords are: "[procedure] near me", "best [procedure] surgeon [city]", and "[procedure] cost [city]."

A practice in Chicago ran a campaign for "mommy makeover near Chicago" with a $600 monthly budget. They used ad copy that included estimated price ranges — "$9,500–$14,000 depending on scope" — which sounds risky but actually filters out people who aren't serious about spending that amount. The average cost per conversion (booked consult) was $43. Each consult converted to surgery at about 30%. So every $43 spent in ads was generating roughly $4,200 in surgery revenue.

Skip the generic "plastic surgeon + city" keywords. They attract students writing papers and people who just watched a reality show. Go narrow. Go specific. Track everything with a Google Ads conversion tag tied to your consult booking form.

## What To Do With the Consultation No-Show

Every surgeon I know complains about no-shows for initial consultations. The numbers vary, but across my clients, the average no-show rate for free consultations is 28%. That means almost a third of the people who made an appointment don't show up. You didn't just lose that time. You lost the opportunity to book $5,000–$15,000 in revenue.

I worked with a facial plastic surgeon in Miami who had a 35% no-show rate. He was spending $1,200 a month on ads driving traffic to his consult booking page, and more than a third of those consults never happened. He was effectively lighting $400 a month on fire.

The fix was mechanical, not motivational. Here's what we changed:

**Step one: Switch to a scheduling platform that sends automated reminders.**
He was using a manual system where his office manager would call 48 hours before. Half the time she forgot. We switched to Booksy for medical practices ($30/month). It sends an automated SMS reminder 24 hours before and a second one 2 hours before. The patient can confirm, reschedule, or cancel with one click.

**Step two: Ask for a credit card to hold the appointment.**
This is the part most surgeons hate. "I don't want to seem like I don't trust patients." The data doesn't care about your feelings. He added a $50 hold fee that was refunded at the appointment. If they cancelled 24 hours in advance, no charge. If they no-showed, they forfeited the $50.

**Step three: Send a follow-up email to every no-show within 2 hours.**
We set up an automated email through Mailchimp (free for his list size) that said: "We missed you today. If something came up, here's a direct link to reschedule. No judgment. No fee for the first reschedule." That email alone recovered 18% of no-shows within 48 hours.

Outcome: no-show rate dropped from 35% to 11% within three weeks. The $50 hold fee alone covered the cost of the scheduling software for two years. The recovered consults generated an additional $14,000 in booked surgeries over the next 90 days.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a solo practitioner. Can't I just ask happy patients to leave Google reviews and call it a day?**

You can, but you'll wait a long time. Patients forget. They leave happy and then get busy. If you're relying on "asking nicely," you'll have 11 reviews after five years. Instead, send a follow-up email exactly 7 days after the post-op appointment with a direct link to your Google Business profile. I use a simple Mailchimp automation for this — one email, one link, no other content. One surgeon in Kansas City went from 4 reviews to 38 in 90 days doing this. His phone started ringing from new patients who mentioned "I saw your great reviews" within a week.

**Q: How much should I actually spend on Google Ads per month for a single-location practice?**

Start at $500 per month for branded search only. Run that for 30 days. If you're getting at least 3 booked consults from that spend (and that's a low bar), then add a second campaign for "near me" procedural keywords at another $500. Going above $1,500 per month for a solo practice is usually wasteful until you have the operational capacity to handle the consults. More leads than you can see is not a growth problem — it's a money-wasting problem.

**Q: What do I say to a patient who leaves a one-star review because they didn't like their results?**

Do not argue. Do not defend your work in public. Write: "Thank you for your feedback. Patient satisfaction is important to us. Please contact our office directly at [phone number] so we can discuss your concerns in detail." That's it. You look professional, you protect patient privacy, and you don't get into a public debate about surgical outcomes. If the patient genuinely had a complication, that's a private conversation. If they're just unhappy with a result within normal variation, your response shows future patients you take concerns seriously.

**Q: Is Instagram worth it for a plastic surgeon, or is that just for influencers?**

Instagram is worth it if and only if you post before-and-after photos consistently. Not selfies. Not inspirational quotes. Not photos of you drinking coffee in your office (unless you're Nataliia). Patients want to see results. Post one before-and-after carousel per week. Use the location tag for your city. Write a short caption about the procedure — no medical claims, just "Rhinoplasty, 6 months post-op." One surgeon in Scottsdale gets about 40% of her new patients from Instagram, and she posts exactly three times a week. That's it. No stories. No reels. Just results.

**Q: I tried Yelp ads and they didn't work. Should I give up on Yelp?**

Yelp ads rarely work for plastic surgeons. But Yelp's free organic listing works extremely well. Claim your page. Fill out every section. Upload 15 photos. Ask patients to leave reviews (Yelp will flag you if you ask too aggressively, so pace it — one request per patient, after the appointment). Respond to every review. That's it. The paid ads are expensive and the clicks rarely convert. The free listing, if optimized, will send you consistent calls without spending a dime.

**Q: How long until I see actual patient bookings from digital marketing?**

If you do nothing wrong, you should see your first booked consult from Google Ads within 7 days. SEO takes 3–6 months. Review generation takes 30–60 days to build critical mass. Instagram results depend on how often you post — expect 60–90 days if you're consistent. Anyone promising you "instant results" from SEO is lying. But if you're not getting a phone call within two weeks of launching a proper Google Ads campaign, something is broken — either the ads, the landing page, or your call-handling process.
