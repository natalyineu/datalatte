## Understanding Local SEO
Local SEO is the process of optimizing your online presence to attract more business from local search results. It's essential to understand how local SEO works and why it matters for your small business. Local SEO is different from traditional SEO, as it focuses on geo-specific search terms and online directories. By optimizing your website and online presence for local search, you can increase your visibility, drive more foot traffic, and ultimately boost sales.

## Setting Up Your Google My Business Listing
Setting up and optimizing your Google My Business (GMB) listing is the first step in improving your local SEO. Your GMB listing is a crucial component of local search results, and it's essential to claim and verify your listing to ensure accuracy and consistency. Here are a few tips to optimize your GMB listing:
* Ensure your business name, address, and phone number are consistent across all online directories
* Choose the correct categories for your business
* Add high-quality photos and videos to your listing
* Respond promptly to customer reviews and feedback

## Building High-Quality Local Citations
Local citations are mentions of your business name, address, and phone number on other websites and online directories. Building high-quality local citations is crucial for improving your local SEO. Here are a few tips to build high-quality local citations:
* Ensure your business is listed in prominent local directories such as Yelp, Bing Places, and Apple Maps
* Use citation building tools such as Moz Local or BrightLocal to streamline the process
* Focus on quality over quantity, and prioritize relevant and authoritative directories

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched a lot of local SEO budgets go down the drain. Not because the business owners weren’t smart, but because someone on the internet convinced them that all the steps matter equally. They don’t. Here are the three mistakes I see most often, and what to do instead.

### Mistake 1: Setting Up Google Business Profile and Forgetting It

**The Story**

A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas — let’s call her shop “Brew & Bloom” — spent three hours setting up her GBP in January 2025. She filled in everything: hours, photos, description, category. Then she closed the tab and never logged in again.

By June, her ranking had dropped from the top three local pack spots to page two. Customers started posting photos of dirty tables (not her photo shoot shots). Someone commented that the latte machine was broken — that was true, but she’d fixed it two days later. She never replied to that review.

She called me because her foot traffic was down 18% compared to the same period the year before. She assumed Google had punished her. The reality: she’d handed Google a dead profile and said “this business doesn’t care about customers asking questions.”

**The Fix**

Post a photo or offer every seven to ten days. Reply to every review within 48 hours — good, bad, and weird. Check your Q&A section once a week (customers were asking if she had oat milk and she never answered, so someone else said “no” and she lost vegan traffic). Update your holiday hours before the holiday, not when you reopen.

**The Outcome**

We spent 20 minutes a week on GBP. After eight weeks, Brew & Bloom was back in the top three. Foot traffic recovered to baseline within three months. The latte machine complaint got a reply, she earned a new five-star review from the same customer who apologized for “being grumpy.” Revenue from Google-arriving customers went from roughly $4,200/month to $6,100/month.

### Mistake 2: Trying to Rank for “Best Coffee Shop in Austin” When You’re a Dog Groomer

**The Story**

A pet groomer in Nashville — “Paw & Order” — paid an SEO freelancer $1,200/month for six months. The freelancer was from a different state. He optimized the site for “best pet grooming in Nashville” (fine) but also “dog wash near me” (fine) and “pet store Nashville” and “animal clinic” and “pet hotel.” He was casting a wide net. The site ranked for nothing.

The owner spent $7,200. Her phone rang maybe three extra times. She almost gave up on local SEO entirely.

**The Fix**

Pick the five searches that your actual best customers type into Google. For Paw & Order, that was “dog grooming Nashville,” “mobile dog grooming Nashville,” “nail trim for dogs Nashville,” “self service dog wash Nashville,” and “poodle grooming Nashville.” We wrote a page for each. Not stuffed with keywords — just useful, specific pages about what she does. The “mobile dog grooming” page included a map of the neighborhoods she drives to, pricing, and a photo of her van.

**The Outcome**

Within three months, she ranked on page one for four of those five terms. She dropped the freelancer. Her monthly spend went to zero for SEO — she writes her own pages now. Revenue from online bookings went from roughly $1,800/month to $3,500/month. The $1,200 she’d been paying was covering two hours of work. Now she does it herself in one hour.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Phone Call Tracking Because You “Just Know”

**The Story**

A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon — “Scissor & Stone” — told me she got 90% of her customers from word of mouth. She didn’t need local SEO, she said. She had a full book. Then she wanted to grow, so she paid $3,000 for a website redesign. Beautiful site. She waited for new clients. None came.

I asked: “Do you know how many calls you get from Google Maps vs. Instagram vs. your website?”

She didn’t. She was running blind.

**The Fix**

Install a free call tracking number on your Google Business Profile (Twilio or CallRail costs pennies a month) and a different number on your website. Most of the time, a different tracking number on each channel. That way when your phone rings, you know where the call came from. Do this for 90 days.

**The Outcome**

Scissor & Stone learned that 42% of her new client calls came from Google Maps. Another 28% came from a third-party booking platform (Booksy). Instagram? 15%. Word of mouth was real — but it was 12%. She was spending zero time on the channel driving nearly half her new business. She started replying to every Google review within 24 hours. She added a “call now” button to her GBP that pointed to the tracking number. In six months, her Google-originating revenue grew from about $2,100/month to $4,500/month. She now tracks everything. She says she feels “stupid” for not doing it sooner. I say she’s smart for fixing it.

## How to Run Local Google Ads Without Throwing Money Away

If you’re a small business owner, the idea of Google Ads probably feels either exciting (more customers!) or terrifying (I will waste $500 in a week). Both feelings are correct.

Here’s the truth: most local ad accounts I’ve looked at are bleeding money because the owner let Google’s “Smart” campaigns run on auto-pilot. Smart campaigns are great for one thing: making Google money. They’re not terrible for a brand new business that has never run ads, but they will eventually spend your budget on people who are just curious, not ready to buy.

**What to do instead**

Start with a manual search campaign. I know it sounds intimidating, but it’s simpler than you think. Your targeting radius should be small — 5 to 10 miles max for a hair salon. 15 miles for a mobile pet groomer. 25 miles for a specialized service like wedding photography or pool cleaning. The wider you go, the more people will click who were never going to drive to you.

Use exact match and phrase match keywords. Not broad match. Broad match means your ad for “dog grooming Nashville” shows up when someone types “cat sitting Clarksville.” That’s real — I saw a $380 month of spend with zero conversions because the keywords were too loose.

Set a daily budget you’re comfortable losing. Not your “in theory this will pay for itself” budget. I mean an amount that if you spent it tomorrow and got zero calls, you’d shrug. For a small business, that’s usually $20–$40/day in most markets. You can go lower — I’ve seen $10/day work in smaller towns.

Track everything we talked about in the phone tracking section. If you can’t tell which ad brought you the phone call, you’re flying blind. And stop using the same phone number on every channel.

**A real example**

I worked with a fitness studio in Denver — “Mile High Movement” — that had been running Google Ads for eight months. They were paying $2,800/month for “Smart” campaigns. They had no idea which keywords were converting. Their cost per lead was $47. For a yoga class trial. That’s insane.

We switched to a manual campaign with 12 keywords, a 10-mile radius, and a $30/day budget. Cost per lead dropped to $12. After three months, monthly qualified leads went from about 60 (most of whom didn’t show up) to about 45 (most of whom showed up). Total spend dropped from $2,800 to $900. Same number of actual trial attendees. They saved $22,800 over the year.

Google Ads is not a game you have to win with a big budget. It’s a game you win by being specific.

## Why Online Reviews Matter More Than Your Website (And What to Do About It)

I don’t love saying this because I run a company that builds websites. But the truth is, for most local service businesses, your Google Business Profile and Yelp page matter more than your website for the first decision a customer makes.

Here’s why: when someone searches “plumber near me” on mobile, the result is usually the local pack. They see three businesses, their ratings, and maybe a photo. They click the one with 4.7 stars and 200 reviews, not the one with 4.2 stars and 12 reviews. They never visit your website. They never read your beautiful about page. They call. Or they book.

**The data**

A 2024 study from BrightLocal — I’ve got the PDF somewhere — showed that the average consumer reads 10 reviews before feeling they can trust a business. That number has been climbing for years. But here’s the detail most people miss: they also look for recency. A business with 200 reviews from 2020 looks less trustworthy than a business with 50 reviews from the last three months.

So if you’re not getting reviews consistently, you’re slowly dying in search.

**How to get reviews without begging**

Don’t send a generic text that says “leave us a review!” That gives people too much friction. Instead, target the moment right after the transaction when the customer is happiest.

For a hair salon: send a brief follow-up via Booksy or a service that sends a nice thank-you message 20 minutes after the appointment ends. “Hey, thanks for coming in today. If you have two minutes, a Google review helps us a ton. Here’s the link.” This works because the customer just left feeling good about their haircut. They’re not annoyed.

For a pet groomer: include a small business card with the QR code for your Google review link, handed with the leash when you return the dog. The customer just got their clean, happy dog back. That’s the moment.

For a coffee shop: put a small sign near the register that says “Love our latte? We’d love a Google review. Ask us for a free cookie when you show us you’ve left one.” That’s a $0.20 cost for a review that could bring you $200 in lifetime value.

**A real example (Chicago)**

A bakery in Chicago — “West Town Bread” — had 32 reviews. They were on page two of the local pack. Their croissants are genuinely the best I’ve had outside France. They were losing customers to a chain that had 400 reviews and worse bread.

We set up a simple system: a text message goes out 30 minutes after every online order (“Thanks for ordering! Enjoy your pastries. If you loved them, here’s our Google review link”). They added a similar note to in-person receipts. In 90 days, they collected 118 new reviews. Average rating stayed 4.8. Three months later, they were on page one for “bakery Chicago” and “best croissant Chicago.” Revenue from new customers who found them through Google increased by about $3,800/month.

Reviews are not a vanity metric. They are search ranking fuel, trust fuel, and conversion fuel all in one.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a website if I have a Google Business Profile?**

Yes, because Google prefers showing businesses that have a website. A site gives you control over your content, your booking flow, and your customer data. GBP is rented land — Google can change the rules tomorrow. Your website is yours. You don’t need a fancy site. A simple one-pager with your hours, services, pricing, contact info, and a booking button is enough to start. Budget $300–$800 for a decent template or someone on Fiverr.

**Q: How long does local SEO actually take to work?**

I’ll be honest: three to six months for meaningful improvements if you’re consistent. Faster if your competitors are doing nothing and you do everything right. Slower if you’re in a competitive market like a big city for a common service. I had a client in a small town outside Atlanta who went from page three to page one in six weeks — because no one else was maintaining their GBP. On the flip side, a dentist in Manhattan took nine months.

**Q: Can I do local SEO myself or do I need to hire someone?**

You can do most of it yourself if you have the time and patience. The basics — GBP setup, keyword research, citation building, review management — are not rocket science. The challenge is consistency. Most owners start strong, then get busy, then stop posting and replying to reviews. They lose the ranking they built. If you think you’ll fall off after two months, hire someone. If you can commit to 30 minutes a week for the next year, do it yourself.

**Q: Is Yelp still worth it in 2026?**

Yelp is complicated. It drives fewer conversions than Google for most businesses, but it still matters for reviews. A customer searching Yelp for a plumber or restaurant will base their decision on what they see there. I recommend claiming your Yelp page, keeping it accurate, and responding to reviews, but don’t obsess over it the way you obsess over Google. And never pay Yelp for advertising until you’ve exhausted the free things that work.

**Q: What’s the most underrated local SEO tactic?**

Phone call tracking. Hands down. Every business owner I talk to can tell me how many website visitors they get. Almost none can tell me how many phone calls came from Google, from their GBP, or from Yelp. That information is free if you use the right tools. It tells you exactly where to focus your effort and budget. Without it, you’re guessing.

**Q: Should I use the same NAP (name, address, phone number) across every directory?**

Yes, exactly the same. Every character matters. If your GBP says “123 Main St Unit 4” and Yelp says “123 Main St., Suite 4,” those are different addresses to Google. Consistency is a ranking signal. Use an automated tool like BrightLocal or Moz Local to check and fix your citations across 40+ directories. Most small businesses have at least five inconsistencies. Fixing them can move you up the rankings in a few weeks.
