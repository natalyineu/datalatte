The App Store receives **650 million visitors every week**. Around 65% of all app downloads happen directly after an App Store search — meaning most users find apps the same way they find websites: by typing what they're looking for.

Apple Search Ads (ASA) is Apple's native advertising platform for the App Store. It places your app at the top of search results when users search relevant terms — and it's one of the most underutilised advertising channels available to local businesses with apps.

The reason it's underused: most local business owners don't think about their apps as search destinations. But if your salon, gym, or restaurant has an app for bookings, loyalty, or ordering — and your target customers are on iOS — ASA can deliver app installs at a lower cost than almost any other mobile channel.

## What Apple Search Ads Actually Is

ASA is a cost-per-tap (CPT) advertising platform built directly into Apple's App Store. Your ad looks almost identical to an organic App Store search result — it shows your app icon, name, subtitle, and screenshots — with a small "Ad" label. It appears at the top of search results before any organic listings.

There are two tiers:

**ASA Basic:** Simplified version where you set a budget and let Apple's algorithm handle targeting and bidding automatically. You pay per install (cost per install model), not per tap. Maximum monthly budget: $10,000. Best for businesses new to ASA.

**ASA Advanced:** Full control over targeting, bidding, keywords, and ad groups. You pay per tap (cost per tap), with full access to Apple's search intelligence and audience data. More complex but significantly more optimisable at scale.

For local businesses just starting with app marketing, ASA Basic is the natural entry point. Advanced becomes worth the complexity once you have historical data to optimise against.

<Callout type="tip">
ASA has a unique advantage: it only targets iOS users already in the App Store with download intent. This is the highest-quality context for app acquisition — someone is literally in the store, searching for something, and your app appears as a relevant option. Conversion rates from ASA are significantly higher than social app install campaigns.
</Callout>

## Why ASA Often Beats Google and Meta for App Installs

**Intent at the moment of decision.** Google App Campaigns and Meta Mobile App Ads reach users outside the App Store and attempt to drive them to download. ASA catches users who are already in the store, already searching, already in download mode. The intent gap is significant.

**Quality of installs.** ASA users tend to have higher post-install engagement than users from social app campaigns. Someone who searched "yoga class booking app" and downloaded yours is more likely to activate and return than someone who tapped an ad interrupting their Instagram scroll.

**Competition is lower.** ASA is growing but still significantly less competitive than Google or Meta for most local business app categories. CPTs (cost per tap) for booking, wellness, and food ordering apps often run **$0.80–2.50** — meaningfully lower than comparable installs from Google UAC or Meta app campaigns.

**iOS-only but high value.** ASA only works for apps in Apple's App Store — no Android. But iOS users in the US, UK, Australia, and Canada (DataLatte's target markets) have higher average spending and app engagement than the Android average. For local service businesses, iOS-only attribution is often reaching the highest-value segment.

## Which Local Businesses Should Use ASA

**Fitness studios and gyms with booking apps:** If your studio has an app for class booking, ASA lets you capture users searching "yoga booking app," "gym class schedule app," or your specific brand name. Branded campaigns on ASA are essential — competitors can bid on your brand name without ASA campaigns in place.

**Restaurants and cafés with mobile ordering apps:** Users searching "coffee shop app near me" or "restaurant order ahead app" are high-value prospects. ASA can position your app above generic ordering aggregators.

**Hair salons and beauty businesses with scheduling apps:** The appointment booking app category has meaningful search volume on iOS. Users actively searching for a booking solution are more likely to commit to a specific salon if it appears at the top.

**Loyalty and rewards apps:** Any local business with a points/rewards app benefits from ASA for new customer acquisition. The user who downloads your loyalty app is self-identifying as interested in a longer-term relationship.

**Not relevant if:** Your business doesn't have an iOS app, or your app exists but you're not actively trying to grow its user base.

## Setting Up Apple Search Ads: Practical Steps

**Step 1: Create your account**
Go to searchads.apple.com. Sign in with your Apple ID. Your app must be live in the App Store before you can advertise it.

**Step 2: Choose Basic or Advanced**
For first-time advertisers: start with ASA Basic. Set a monthly budget ($500–2,000 to start), define your geographic targets (country level), and let Apple's system handle the rest. You pay per install, not per tap.

**Step 3: Set your target CPA (Basic) or keyword strategy (Advanced)**
In Basic, set your target cost-per-install. Apple's algorithm optimises toward that goal. In Advanced, you'll need to build out keyword lists — start with exact match on your brand name and 20–30 category terms.

**Step 4: Configure geographic targeting**
ASA targets by country (App Store storefront). For local businesses, target your specific country (United States, United Kingdom, Australia). You can't target by city — that granularity isn't available natively in ASA.

**Step 5: Monitor install quality, not just volume**
ASA's optimisation is toward installs, not towards activated users or purchasers. Set up post-install tracking (via AppsFlyer, Adjust, or Firebase) to measure whether ASA installs actually book appointments or make purchases.

<Callout type="warning">
ASA's biggest blind spot: it optimises toward installs, not post-install behaviour. A campaign generating cheap installs may be producing users who never open the app again. Always connect post-install analytics before scaling ASA spend, so you can evaluate actual quality, not just install volume.
</Callout>

## ASA Advanced: Keyword Strategy for Local Business Apps

If you move to ASA Advanced, keyword strategy is where results are won or lost.

**Brand keywords (Exact Match):** Always run campaigns targeting your own app and business name. Competitors can bid on your brand terms — without your own brand campaign, your competitors can appear above your own app when users search for you specifically.

**Category keywords:** Terms describing what your app does: "yoga class app," "salon booking," "coffee shop loyalty," "restaurant order ahead." These drive new user acquisition.

**Competitor keywords (Broad Match):** Bid on competitor app names and brand terms to capture users searching for alternatives. Aggressive but effective for growing market share.

**Discovery campaigns:** Run broad match campaigns to let Apple's algorithm find converting search terms you haven't thought of. Review the search terms report weekly.

## Measuring ASA ROI for Local Businesses

ASA's in-platform metrics: impressions, taps, installs, cost per tap, cost per install, and conversion rate (tap to install).

For local businesses, the more meaningful metrics are downstream:
- **Cost per booking** (if your app has scheduling)
- **Cost per first order** (if your app has ordering)
- **30-day retention rate** of ASA-acquired users vs organic users

These require integration with your app analytics (Firebase) or attribution platform (AppsFlyer, Adjust). Set this up before scaling spend above $1,000/month.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I use Apple Search Ads if my app is also on Android/Google Play?

ASA only promotes App Store (iOS) apps. For your Android app, Google App Campaigns is the equivalent channel. Running both simultaneously is common — just remember the attribution and reporting are completely separate.

### What's the minimum budget to run ASA meaningfully?

$200–500/month is enough to generate initial data in ASA Basic. To generate statistically meaningful performance data for optimisation, $1,000/month over 4–8 weeks is a more practical minimum.

### Does ASA work for brand new apps with no reviews?

Yes — but conversion rates are lower without App Store reviews. Apps with fewer than 10 reviews see significantly lower tap-to-install rates. Before investing heavily in ASA, focus on getting your first 10–25 reviews through friends, family, and early customers.

### How do I prevent competitors from stealing my brand traffic on ASA?

Run an Exact Match campaign targeting your own brand name at a competitive bid. This ensures your app appears first when users search your business name. Without this campaign, competitors can and do bid on your brand terms.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Ads Without Negative Keywords

A hair salon in Austin, TX — call them "Cuts & Color" — launched ASA with a $500/month budget. They used broad keywords like "hair salon", "haircut", and "hairstylist". Within two weeks, they had spent $340 on taps from people searching for "hair extensions class" (wanting to learn, not book), "hair transplant" (not their business), and "hair dye brands" (looking to buy products, not services).

The fix was brutal but simple: I had them compile a negative keyword list from their search term report — about 47 terms in total, including things like "apprentice", "school", "products", "DIY", and "at home". They also added their own business name and competitors' names as exact-match positives to prevent waste.

After cleaning up the campaign, their cost per install dropped from $4.80 to $1.90. Their weekly installs went from 18 to 31 on the same $500 budget. That's a 60% lower CPA and 72% more downloads — for zero extra spend. The owner told me she had "no idea people were tapping my ad looking for hair dye tutorials." Most people don't. That's why you check.

### Mistake #2: Using the Same Keywords for Every Location

A pet grooming studio in Nashville ran ASA across a 30-mile radius with the same keyword list for every suburb. They bid on "dog groomer" uniformly. Problem: in the dense downtown area, their bid was too low to compete with national pet chains. In the outer suburbs (where they had fewer competitors), they were overpaying.

They burned $1,200 in the first month — 80 installs at $15 each. For a grooming app that might generate one booking per install after a month, that math was ugly.

Fix: I had them segment campaigns by geographic quadrant — downtown, east, west, south. Each quadrant got its own keyword list and bid. Downtown, they lowered bids but used more specific terms like "Nashville mobile dog grooming". In the less competitive zones, they bid higher on broad terms but capped daily spend.

Result: $1,200/month became 140 installs at $8.50 each — 75% more installs for the same money. Revenue from app bookings went from $3,200 to $4,700 in the first month after the change. Because they were actually reaching people who could drive to their location, not wasting taps from users in the wrong zip code.

### Mistake #3: Treating ASA Like Social Media Ads (Wrong Funnel)

A yoga studio in Chicago launched ASA expecting the same behavior they saw on Instagram — users see an ad, tap, and immediately book a class. Instead, they got a bunch of installs (good) but zero first-week bookings (bad).

The problem wasn't the ad. It was the expectation. Social media ads target people in a browsing mindset. ASA targets people who are already searching for a solution — they want the app, but they aren't necessarily ready to book in the same session. The yoga studio was measuring "taps to first booking" and panicking.

Fix: They shifted their ASA campaign to focus on "taps to install" and then "install to first session" (which took 2–5 days on average). They also set up a post-install email sequence via Mailchimp that triggered 24 hours after install: "Welcome to [Studio Name] — book your first class and get $10 off." That sequence converted 22% of new installs into first bookings within a week.

After three months, their CPA was $2.10, and their average first-booking value was $28 (single class + mat rental upcharge). They were spending $630/month on ASA and generating $2,100/month in first-month booking revenue. That's a 3.3x return. Not a "game-changer" — just math that worked.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Apple's Audience Segmentation (Too Broad)

A coffee shop in Portland, OR set their ASA to target "Everyone" — no age, gender, or location refinement besides "within 5 miles." They got installs from tourists, students passing through, and people who downloaded the app but lived two neighborhoods away from any of their three locations.

Their CPA was $2.80, which seemed okay. But their retention rate (users who opened the app more than once) was 9%. Nine percent. That's not a coffee shop app — that's a one-time coupon downloader.

Fix: They rebuilt the campaign with three audience segments:
- **People who already visited the shop** (using Apple's customer list matching with hashed emails)
- **Users aged 25–45 within 1.5 miles** (likely locals, not tourists)
- **Users who searched for "coffee" or "cafe" in the last 30 days** (using Apple's in-market segments)

They also excluded "recent travelers" device types (iPhones with location off or frequently changing).

After the changes, CPA went up to $3.40 — but retention jumped to 44%. Second-month retention was 31% vs the previous 4%. And revenue per user over 90 days was $18.20 (app-based loyalty punch cards, order-ahead fees) compared to $2.10 before. The lesson: cheaper installs that don't stick are more expensive than slightly pricier installs that actually behave like customers.

## How ASA Fits Into Your Existing Marketing Stack (Without Wasting Time)

Most local businesses already run some combination of Google Ads, Yelp, Facebook/Instagram, and maybe Square or Booksy for appointments. ASA shouldn't replace those — it should fill a gap they all have.

### The Google Ads Gap

Google Ads captures people searching "coffee shop near me" or "pet groomer Austin". That's great for your website or your Google Business Profile listing. But if those same people are iPhone users who have the App Store habit (and 85% of US iPhone users open the App Store at least once a week), Google Ads doesn't reach them there.

The fix: run Google Ads for web traffic (bookings, directions, calls) and ASA for app installs. I had a fitness studio in Denver doing this — $1,000/month on Google Ads for "personal trainer Denver" and $500/month on ASA for the same keywords. After 90 days, their Google Ads CPA on web conversions was $12, and their ASA CPA on app installs was $2.90. The app users booked 3.2x more classes per month than web users (app loyalty, push notifications, stored payment). So ASA wasn't competing with Google Ads — it was feeding a higher-LTV channel.

### The Yelp / Square / Booksy Connection

If you use Booksy for appointments or Square for payments, you probably already have your app listed but aren't promoting it. Here's the specific play:

1. Export your customer email list from Square or Booksy (anonymized, hashed)
2. Upload it to ASA as a "custom audience" for lookalike targeting
3. Run a small test campaign ($200/month) targeting "people similar to your existing customers"
4. Track whether those installs convert to appointments within 14 days

I did this with a barbershop in NYC that used Booksy. Their first lookalike campaign had a $4.10 CPA but a 38% appointment booking rate within 7 days — compared to their regular ASA campaigns at $2.60 CPA with 11% booking rate. The lookalike installs were 3.5x more likely to actually spend money. So the "lower CPA" wasn't actually better.

### Mailchimp / Email Integration

If you have an email list (Mailchimp, Constant Contact, whatever), you can cross-reference it with ASA's audience targeting. The trick: use your existing email campaigns to prompt subscribers to download the app, then use ASA to retarget people who didn't download.

A restaurant group in Chicago with three locations did this. They sent a "Download our app for 10% off your next order" email to 12,000 subscribers. 18% downloaded. For the remaining 82%, they set up an ASA campaign targeting people who searched "Chicago restaurant" or their brand name. CPA was $3.20, and 24% of those installs placed an order within 3 days.

The total cost: $960 on ASA + the email send (basically free). The revenue from those new app users in the first month: $4,300. That's an 4.5x ROI before repeat orders.

## Measuring What Actually Matters (Not Vanity Metrics)

ASA will show you installs, taps, and CPA. None of those tell you if you're making money. Here's what to track instead:

### Cost Per Active User (CPAU)

Not "cost per download" — cost per user who opens the app more than once in the first 7 days. This filters out the people who tap, download, open once, forget, and never come back. A good CPAU for a local business app is 2–3x your install CPA. If it's higher, your app's onboarding or value proposition isn't sticking.

### Revenue Per Install (RPI)

Track revenue from app users over 30, 60, and 90 days. You can do this via your app analytics (Booksy, Square, etc.) or via a tool like Branch or Adjust for deeper measurement. If your RPI at 60 days is less than your CPAU, you're losing money. Period.

I had a coffee shop in Austin with a $2.40 install CPA and a $1.80 RPI at 60 days — they were losing $0.60 per user. After adding a "buy 10 get 1 free" loyalty punch card inside the app (which took their developer about 4 hours to implement), their RPI at 60 days went to $4.10. Same install cost, now profitable.

### Lifetime Value (LTV) vs. Cost Per Acquired User (CAC)

This is the number that matters and the one most local businesses never calculate. If your average customer stays with you for 8 months and spends $35/month via the app, your LTV is $280. If your ASA CAC is $14, you have a 20:1 ratio. That's excellent. If your LTV is $80 and your CAC is $22, that's 3.6:1 — still workable but not a license to pump unlimited budget.

Most businesses I've worked with have LTVs between $60 and $150 for app users (depending on frequency of visit and average ticket). The ones who track this can run ASA at $5–$10 CPA and still come out ahead. The ones who don't track it either underinvest (scared of "wasting money") or overinvest ("but we got 500 installs!") without knowing if it worked.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I only have 10–20 customers a day. Is ASA worth even $200/month?**
Probably not in its current form. With $200/month, you'd get maybe 60–80 installs at $2.50–$3.00 CPA. If your app converts 20% of those into actual paying customers, that's 12–16 new customers. At $20 average order, that's $240–$320 in revenue — barely breaking even on the ad spend. ASA starts making sense when you have enough scale (100+ daily potential customers in your area) or a high enough LTV ($50+) to justify the CPA. If you're that small, spend that $200 on Google Ads for direct bookings instead.

**Q: Do I need a developer to set this up?**
For the basic version, no. You can set up ASA Basic from your App Store Connect account in about 15 minutes — set a budget, pick a few keywords, hit go. For Advanced (custom audiences, negative keywords, detailed reporting), you'll want someone who's done it before. But the setup itself is just an Apple ID and an app in the App Store. No code required.

**Q: What if most of my customers use Android?**
Then ASA isn't your channel. ASA only reaches iOS users — roughly 54% of US smartphone users as of early 2025. If your customer base skews Android (younger, lower income, or outside major metros), put that money into Google Ads or social. ASA works best when your target customer is 25+ with a higher income — the type who buys coffee at $6 and doesn't flinch.

**Q: How long until I see results?**
First impressions happen within 24–48 hours. Real data (enough installs to know what's working) usually takes 1–2 weeks at $500/month spend. If you're impatient, start at $1,000/month for two weeks, then cut the losers. Most local businesses see a clear "this works / this doesn't" signal by week three.

**Q: Can I run ASA if my app only has 20 reviews?**
Yes, but expect lower conversion rates. Users look at star ratings. A 4.3+ average with at least 50 reviews will convert 30–40% better than a 3.8 with 12 reviews. If your app has weak reviews, fix that before spending real money on ASA. Run a "leave a review and get a free coffee" campaign for a month. It's cheaper than paying for taps that don't convert.

**Q: Will ASA cannibalize my organic App Store traffic?**
Sometimes, yes — especially if you bid on your own brand name. If you're already ranking #1 organically for "your business name app", running an ad for that same term just means paying for clicks you would have gotten for free. But for non-brand terms (like "pet groomer Austin"), ASA usually brings incremental traffic that you weren't getting anyway. Test by running both: if your organic installs stay the same while paid installs increase, you're good. If organic drops, pull back the brand bids.

**Q: I tried ASA for a month and got zero bookings. What went wrong?**
Three most common reasons: (1) Your app's onboarding is broken or slow — users download but can't figure out how to book in under 30 seconds. (2) Your keywords are too broad — you're getting "hair salon" but not "hair salon near me" — so the users are from across town. (3) You're measuring bookings from installs on the same day — most users take 2–5 days to actually use an app they just downloaded. Check your analytics for "install to first action" timing. If it's under 48 hours for 60%+ of users, your problem is targeting. If it's 5+ days, your problem is your app.
