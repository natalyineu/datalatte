Medical spas waste 60% of their ad budget on vague audiences like "age 25–55." Instead, build **hyper-specific client personas** based on your treatments. For example:
- *Busy mom*: Searches "fillers near me" at 9 PM, books via Google Business Profile
- *Aging baby boomer*: Compares "Botox prices" on Sunday mornings, uses coupons
- *Fitness enthusiast*: Looks for "laser hair removal near gym" on weekdays

Use [local SEO services](/services/local-seo) to audit keywords like "medical spa in [city]" and "anti-aging treatments near me." A Miami Botox clinic I worked with boosted calls by 40% after targeting "Botox for 30s women" with location-based ads.

Your GBP profile gets **2.3x more visibility** than ads for medical services—but only if it’s optimized. Start by:
1. Claiming all 8 service categories (e.g., "Aesthetic Medicine," "Skin Treatments")

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running “Botox” Ads Without Excluding Aesthetics Shoppers

A medspa in Nashville called me three months after they started Facebook ads. They were spending $2,800/month targeting women 35–65 within 15 miles. Their booking rate was 4%. Not 4% conversion. 4% of people who clicked ever booked. That’s a disaster.

I pulled their ad account and saw the problem immediately: “Botox” was their headline keyword. But “Botox” on social media attracts two groups — people who want to buy and people who want to compare prices across five clinics before picking the cheapest. The second group is 70% of the clicks. They never book.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the ad set targeting people searching for “muscle tension relief,” “forehead wrinkles bothering you,” and “preventative anti-aging at 35.” Then we excluded anyone who visited competitor pricing pages (tracked via a simple pixel rule). We also excluded zip codes with three or more competing medspas within a mile, because those users were comparison shoppers by location.

**The outcome:** Monthly ad spend dropped to $1,400. Bookings went from 12 per month to 41. Cost per booking fell from $233 to $34. Over six months, that medspa recovered roughly $8,400 in wasted spend and added $23,000 in procedure revenue.

The uncomfortable truth is that “Botox” is a commodity keyword. You pay premium prices for tire-kickers. If your ad copy says “Botox,” your offer better say “free consultation with a registered nurse, no hidden fees, same-day booking.” Otherwise, you’re funding your competitors’ comparison sheets.

### Mistake #2: Letting Negative Reviews Sit Unanswered for Two Weeks

A dermatology clinic in Portland, OR was losing clients and didn’t know why. Their retention rate for first-time visitors was 27%. They had three negative reviews on Google from the past year — one complained about a canceled appointment, two about front desk rudeness. They never responded to any of them.

I’m not blaming them. They’re busy. But here’s what actually happened: I looked at the Google Business Profile insights. The week after each negative review posted, their profile views dropped 18%. The month after, they lost an average of 22 booking requests. That’s three bad reviews costing roughly $1,200/month in lost business each.

**The fix:** We set up a simple review response protocol. Any negative review gets a reply within 24 hours — apologizing, acknowledging the specific issue, and offering a private way to resolve it (call the clinic directly, here’s the manager’s direct line). Positive reviews get a short thank-you within 48 hours. We also started responding to any review that mentioned wait times or pricing confusion, even if it was neutral.

**The outcome:** Within 90 days, their average rating ticked from 4.1 to 4.6. Their profile view-to-call rate improved by 31%. Booking requests rose from 34/month to 53/month. The negative reviews didn’t disappear, but the responses showed potential clients that the clinic wasn’t ignoring problems. That matters more than a perfect score.

If you don’t have someone checking reviews daily, you’re leaving money on the table. Square has a free review monitoring tool. Google’s own dashboard sends notifications. There’s no excuse.

### Mistake #3: Treating All First-Time Clients the Same

A medspa in Austin, TX had a 38% rebooking rate. That means 62% of new clients came once and never returned. They spent $4,500/month on ads to bring in 95 new clients per month. Then 59 of them disappeared. That’s $2,790 in ad spend every month wasted on people who walked in and walked out for good.

The problem wasn’t the service. Their reviews were solid. The problem was their follow-up. A new client came in for a hydrafacial. They got a generic thank-you email two days later. That was it. No reminder about the package discount for booking their next session within 30 days. No suggestion for a complementary treatment. No text asking how their skin felt a week later.

**The fix:** We built a three-touch sequence in Mailchimp:
- Day 1 post-visit: Text message with a photo of their before/after (permission obtained during intake). “How’s your skin feeling? Here’s a 15% off code if you book your next visit within 14 days.”
- Day 7: Email with a 60-second video from their provider explaining a recommended follow-up treatment.
- Day 21: Email with a “we haven’t seen you” offer — $50 off any service over $200.

They also started using Booksy to automatically send appointment reminders and rebooking prompts. That’s $29/month. Worth every penny.

**The outcome:** Rebooking rate hit 67% within four months. Monthly new client acquisition costs dropped because fewer people needed replacing. Revenue from existing clients increased by $3,800/month. The owner told me she kicked herself for not doing this sooner. I ordered a second coffee I did not need. No regrets.

## How to Structure a Google Ads Campaign That Doesn’t Bleed Money

Most medspa owners I meet set up Google Ads once, get frustrated, and never touch it again. I don’t blame them. The default setup Google suggests is designed for Google’s revenue, not your profit. Here’s what actually works.

### Start with Location Targeting That Hurts

Your radius should be brutal. If you’re in a city, set a 5-mile radius. Not 10. Not 20. Here’s why: someone searching “Botox near me” from 12 miles away is probably comparing your phone number against three other clinics. They’re not driving 25 minutes to save $20. But they will click your ad.

A medspa in Denver had a 12-mile radius and was spending $1,800/month. Half their clicks came from people who never booked. I tightened it to 4 miles, added a negative radius around competing clinics (any ad click from within a 1-mile radius of a competitor was excluded), and added “in your area” copy to force relevance. Monthly spend dropped to $900. Bookings went up.

### Use Negative Keywords Like Your Budget Depends on It

It does. You want to exclude:
- “cost,” “price,” “cheap,” “discount” (unless you’re running a specific promo)
- “DIY,” “at home,” “natural” (people searching these are not buying injectables)
- “jobs,” “training,” “career,” “license” (these are not clients)
- “near me” variations paired with generic terms (you want branded searches to go to your profile, not your ad budget)

One spa in Chicago was paying $4.50 per click for “laser hair removal near me.” But 40% of those clicks came from people searching for “laser hair removal technician training.” That’s $1.80 of every $4.50 wasted. After adding “training” as a negative keyword, their click-to-lead rate doubled.

### Build Ad Groups by Intent, Not by Service

A common mistake: one ad group called “Botox” with ten keywords. Instead, split them:
- **High-intent brand seekers:** “[clinic name] Botox,” “[clinic name] appointment”
- **Problem solvers:** “forehead wrinkles treatment,” “crow’s feet remedies”
- **Comparison shoppers:** “Botox vs fillers,” “best medspa in [city]”

Each group gets its own ad copy. The comparison group should include a table or bullet list showing why you’re better. The problem solvers should lead with empathy: “Tired of Botox results that don’t last?” The brand seekers need a direct booking link.

I tested this at a clinic in NYC. They had one ad group with 22 keywords. Split into three groups, total spend stayed the same, but cost per booking dropped from $67 to $29. That’s $38 saved per booking. At 50 bookings per month, that’s $1,900 back in their pocket.

### Track Phone Calls, Not Just Clicks

Google Ads reports clicks. That’s not your business metric. Your business metric is calls received and booked. Use a call tracking service like CallRail or the built-in Google forwarding number. Most medspas get 40–60% of their bookings via phone. If you’re only tracking form submissions, you’re blind to half your pipeline.

I worked with a clinic in Nashville that thought their ads were failing. Form submissions were low. But when we added call tracking, they discovered 18 calls per week from ads — and 14 of those booked. They were spending $2,200/month on what they thought was a dead campaign. It was actually their best-performing channel. They just weren’t measuring it right.

## Retention Systems That Keep Clients Coming Back (Without Being Annoying)

Acquiring a new medspa client costs 5–7x more than retaining an existing one. I know you’ve heard that before. But here’s the number that matters: a retained client who visits four times per year has a lifetime value of roughly $3,200 to $5,600 depending on your services. A one-and-done client leaves you with maybe $400 gross margin after ad cost.

So retention isn’t a nice-to-have. It’s where the money lives.

### Use the 30/60/90 Day Retention Framework

This isn’t complicated. It’s a sequence:

- **Day 30 post-visit:** Automatic email or text asking for a review. Include a direct link to your Google Business Profile. Offer a $10 credit for their next visit if they leave one.
- **Day 60:** Remind them about a seasonal treatment that complements what they had before. “You came in for Botox in March. Now that it’s May, here’s why a lip flip might pair perfectly.”
- **Day 90:** If they haven’t booked, send a “we miss you” offer. $50 off any service over $200. No small print.

One clinic in Portland used this with Mailchimp ($13/month for their list size). Their 120-day rebooking rate went from 31% to 58% in five months. That’s 27 more returning clients per quarter. At an average spend of $350 per visit, that’s $9,450 in quarterly revenue they had been leaving on the table.

### Build a Referral Program That Actually Works

Most medspa referral programs are “refer a friend, get $25 off.” That’s lazy. Here’s a structure I’ve seen work at four different clinics:

- The referring client gets a free add-on service (e.g., a $75 dermaplaning) for every new client who books and completes a $200+ treatment.
- The new client gets $50 off their first visit.
- The referral is tracked via a unique link or code in your CRM.

I tested this at a clinic in Chicago. First three months, they got 8 referrals. After switching to this structure? 44 referrals in the next three months. The cost was roughly $1,200 in free services given away. The revenue from new clients was $15,400. Net gain: $14,200. Plus all those new clients entered the retention funnel.

### Stop Sending Birthday Emails Everyone Ignores

Everyone sends a “happy birthday” email with 15% off. It lands in the spam folder or gets deleted. Instead, send a “your birthday month” postcard via direct mail. It costs $0.75 to print and mail. Include a handwritten note from the provider who saw them last. Offer a “birthday upgrade” — free add-on to any existing booking, or a small treatment upgrade.

One clinic in Austin tried this with 300 clients. 43 booked using the offer. Average spend with the upgrade was $280 instead of the usual $195. That’s $3,675 in incremental revenue from $225 in printing and postage. ROI: 16x.

Direct mail works for medspas because it’s personal, physical, and rare. You’re not competing with 50 other “happy birthday” emails. You’re the only card that lands on their counter.

## How to Use Yelp Without Losing Your Sanity

Yelp gets a bad reputation. Some of it is deserved — their sales team is aggressive, and the review filtering can be frustrating. But I’ve seen Yelp drive real revenue for medspas in competitive markets. Here’s the honest playbook.

### Don’t Buy Yelp Ads Until You Fix Your Profile

Many medspas I talk to start with Yelp Ads ($300–$500/month) and see zero ROI. That’s because their profile has no reviews, no photos, no service categories. Yelp’s algorithm rewards profiles with activity. If your profile is bare, ads won’t help.

Before spending a dollar, do this:
- Upload 15–20 high-quality photos of your space, equipment, and staff (not stock photos)
- Fill in every service category that applies — Yelp has 30+ medspa-specific categories
- Ask your top 10 clients to leave a review (send them the direct link, don’t ask Yelp’s algorithm to guess)
- Respond to every review, positive or negative, within 48 hours

A clinic in Denver did this. They had 3 reviews and a 3.8 rating. After 60 days of active profile management, they had 22 reviews and a 4.4 rating. They started Yelp Ads at $250/month. In the first month, they tracked 14 bookings directly from Yelp. Average procedure value: $450. That’s $6,300 in revenue from a $250 ad spend. But it only worked because the profile was ready.

### Respond to Negative Reviews Without Defensiveness

I’ve seen medspas write novel-length responses arguing with a one-star review. That is performance art, not marketing. Every potential client who reads that response will think “if they treat a stranger that way, imagine how they treat paying clients.”

Here’s the script I teach: “Thank you for your feedback. We take [specific issue] seriously and would like the opportunity to make it right. Please call [name] directly at [number] so we can discuss this personally.”

That’s it. No excuses. No explanations. The goal is to show readers you’re accountable, not to win an argument.

### Use Yelp’s Request a Quote Feature

Few medspas use this. Yelp allows users to request a quote from multiple businesses simultaneously. If you’re in a category like “laser hair removal” or “Botox,” you’re competing against 3–5 other clinics in that request.

To win these, respond within 1 hour. Include a specific price (not “varies by treatment”), a link to your portfolio, and a direct phone number. Speed matters here. The first clinic to respond with a clear offer gets the booking most of the time.

A clinic in Nashville set up an alert for these requests. They responded to 12 requests in one month. They booked 9. That’s an 83% win rate at zero ad cost. Revenue: roughly $3,600.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I only have a $500/month ad budget. Can I still get results with Google Ads?**

Yes, but you need to be ruthless. $500/month is roughly $16/day. You can’t afford broad keywords like “medical spa” or “Botox.” You need long-tail keywords like “Botox for 30s in [your neighborhood]” or “hydrafacial post-wedding prep.” Set your radius to 3 miles maximum. Use only exact match keywords. Expect 2–3 clicks per day. If each click converts at 10%, you’ll get one booking every 3 days. That’s 10 bookings per month. At an average spend of $300, that’s $3,000 in revenue from a $500 spend. It works, but you cannot waste a single dollar.

**Q: Should I offer discounts to get more reviews?**

Not in the way you’re thinking. Offering a discount in exchange for a review is against Google’s terms and can get your profile suspended. Instead, offer a “thank you” gift card after they leave an honest review, regardless of star rating. Or better, ask for reviews naturally during checkout. Say: “If you had a good experience, would you mind leaving a quick review? It helps other people find us.” That works better than any discount.

**Q: My competitor has 200 reviews and I have 12. Am I screwed?**

No. Review count matters less than recency and response rate. Google prioritizes reviews posted in the last 90 days. If your competitor has 200 reviews but only 2 from the past month, and you have 12 reviews with 8 from the last 30 days, your profile will appear higher for local searches. Focus on getting 2–3 new reviews per week and responding to every single one within 48 hours. You can catch up in 90 days.

**Q: How often should I post on social media for a medspa?**

Minimum 3 times per week, but quality over quantity. One before/after photo per week (with consent), one educational video (60 seconds explaining a treatment), one client testimonial (text or video). Skip the “happy Monday” posts and generic quotes. Nobody books an appointment because of a quote about sunshine. They book because they see a result that matches their problem.

**Q: Should I use Groupon for medspa services?**

I’ve never seen Groupon work well for medical spas. It attracts price-sensitive clients who don’t return and leave negative reviews when they realize they got a discounted version of a treatment. Instead, run a “first visit” special on your own website — 20% off their first service, no coupon code needed. That keeps the booking in your system and the client relationship in your control.

**Q: How do I track whether my marketing is actually working?**

Use at least two tracking methods: unique phone numbers for each channel (Google Ads, Yelp, Facebook) and UTM parameters on every link you share. Put a simple question in your intake form: “How did you hear about us?” Offer specific options: Google Search, Facebook, Friend Referral, Walked By, Yelp. Then check that against your ad spend every month. If a channel costs $500 and brings in 2 bookings worth $300 each, that’s a $100 loss. Kill it. If a channel costs $200 and brings in 6 bookings worth $1,800, double it. It’s that simple.
