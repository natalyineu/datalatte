## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve seen pharmacies blow through ad budgets like a teenager with a credit card. Here’s what actually happens when you skip the strategy and just “turn on Google Ads.”

### Mistake #1: Running Generic “Pharmacy Near Me” Campaigns

**The story:** A pharmacy owner in Austin, Texas spent $1,200 in January on a campaign targeting “pharmacy near me” across a 20-mile radius. No negatives. No location adjustments. Just broad matches and hope.

Here’s what happened: $1,050 went to people searching for 24-hour CVS at 2 a.m. and people looking for pet pharmacies in Round Rock. The pharmacy generated exactly 12 clicks to their homepage. Zero conversions. $1,200 for basically a digital billboard that nobody relevant saw.

**The fix:** I told them to tighten location targeting to a 3-mile radius and add negative keywords: “24 hour,” “pet,” “vet,” “Walgreens,” “CVS,” “online pharmacy,” “delivery,” “poison control.” Then we created specific ad groups for services they actually offered — flu shots, blood pressure checks, medication synchronization.

**The outcome:** Their next month cost $850 total. They saw 47 calls requesting flu shot appointments and 23 walk-ins tracked to the ad. Estimated revenue from those two services alone: $3,800.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Prescription Refill Search Intent

**The story:** A neighborhood pharmacy in Nashville ran ads for “prescription refill” and “refill my prescription” but sent everyone to their generic homepage. The homepage pushed OTC deals and COVID boosters front and center. 

Their bounce rate on paid traffic hit 81%. The owner told me Google Ads “doesn’t work for pharmacies.” I asked what page the ads went to. He showed me. I ordered a second coffee I did not need.

**The fix:** You need a dedicated refill landing page. Not a homepage. Not a services page. A page that says in big letters: “Refill Your Prescription in Under 2 Minutes.” Include a form, a phone number, and a note about same-day readiness. 

**The outcome:** After building that page and updating ad copy to say “Refill Online — Ready in 1 Hour,” their conversion rate went from 1.2% to 6.8%. That pharmacy got 31 online refill requests in the first week alone. Each refill visit averages $22 in OTC add-on sales. Do the math.

### Mistake #3: Running Ads Without Call Tracking

**The story:** A family-owned pharmacy in Portland, Oregon ran ads for six weeks. They had no idea which calls came from Google Ads versus their yellow page listing. The owner told me “we got a lot of calls this month.” I asked how many. Silence.

**The fix:** Set up call tracking through Google Ads’ call extensions plus a third-party service like CallRail or WhatConverts. Tag calls as “new patient” or “refill request.” Track duration. If calls last less than 30 seconds, that’s a wrong number or a hang-up. You want calls that hit 60 seconds or more.

**The outcome:** They discovered that 68% of their calls were existing patients looking for the wrong pharmacy. Their ad was triggering calls from confused customers. After we changed ad copy to include the pharmacy’s name and cross-street, the wrong-number calls dropped 52%. Their cost per qualified call fell from $18.50 to $5.75. Worth the $39/month for tracking software.

## Local Service Ads vs. Search Campaigns for Pharmacies

If you haven’t looked at Google’s Local Service Ads yet, you’re leaving money on the counter. I’ll keep this simple.

Search campaigns: You pay per click. People type “flu shot Austin TX,” they see your ad, they click, they land on your page. You pay whether they call or not. 

Local Service Ads: You pay per qualified lead. Someone searches “pharmacy near me” or “urgent care pharmacy.” Google shows your ad at the very top with a green checkmark. You pay only when someone actually contacts you through Google’s platform — call or message.

**Which one for a pharmacy? Both. But start with local service ads if your budget is under $1,000/month.**

Here’s why: I worked with a pharmacy in Chicago that was burning $2,000/month on search ads competing against Walgreens and CVS for “same day prescription.” They were losing. Big chains outbid them every time.

We moved 60% of that budget to Local Service Ads. The pharmacy had to go through Google’s background check and licensing verification (annoying, but it gets you the green checkmark). First month: 41 qualified leads at $14 per lead. Their previous search campaign cost-per-lead was $32.

For OTC products — vitamins, allergy meds, first aid — keep a small search campaign running. For anything that requires a consultation or a visit: Local Service Ads win.

**One catch:** Local Service Ads only work in certain markets. Check if your metro area is eligible. If it’s not, double down on search ads with location extensions and call buttons in the ad itself.

## How to Retarget Pharmacy Visitors Without Creeping Them Out

Retargeting a pharmacy visitor is different than retargeting someone who looked at shoes. There’s HIPAA. There’s the fact that people don’t want Google to know they searched for “hemorrhoid cream” at 11 p.m.

So how do you do it without being creepy?

**Rule one:** Never retarget based on specific product pages. If someone looked at your diabetes testing supplies page, do not show them an ad for diabetes testing supplies. That’s a HIPAA violation waiting to happen.

**Rule two:** Do retarget based on general visits. Someone came to your website — any page. Show them a generic ad: “Your neighborhood pharmacy. Fast refills. Friendly staff.”

**Rule three:** Use a 7-day cookie window, not 30 or 90. Pharmacy decisions are fast. Someone needs a prescription filled today, not next month. A week is plenty.

**Real example:** A pharmacy in Denver set up a retargeting campaign through Google Ads with a 7-day window. They served ads only to people who visited their refill page or contact page but didn’t complete a form. The ad said “Forget something? Refill your prescription online in 60 seconds.”

Cost: $380. New refill requests from retargeted visitors: 27. Average revenue per refill visit with add-ons: $34. Total revenue: $918. ROAS: 2.4x in two weeks.

**Tools you actually need:** Google Ads remarketing tag (free). A clean website with separate URLs for services (not products). Mailchimp or Constant Contact for email follow-ups after the visit (double opt-in only, HIPAA-compliant).

**One more thing:** Do not retarget on YouTube or display networks for pharmacies. You’ll show your ad to someone’s spouse who has no idea they visited your site. Trust me, you don’t want that call.

## How to Structure Your Pharmacy’s Google Ads Account

Most pharmacy owners set up one campaign with ten keywords and wonder why nothing works. Here’s the structure I use for every pharmacy client.

**Campaign 1: Prescription Services**
- Keywords: “prescription refill,” “transfer prescription,” “new pharmacy,” “pharmacy near me”
- Ads: Focus on speed. “Transfer your prescription in 5 minutes. Ready same day.”
- Landing page: Refill request form + phone number + hours

**Campaign 2: Clinical Services**
- Keywords: “flu shot,” “COVID vaccine,” “blood pressure check,” “travel vaccines,” “strep test”
- Ads: Mention insurance accepted. “Flu shots available. Walk-ins welcome. Most insurances accepted.”
- Landing page: Services page with prices (or “call for pricing”) and appointment booking

**Campaign 3: OTC Products**
- Keywords: “allergy medicine,” “vitamin D supplements,” “first aid kit,” “pain relief”
- Ads: Mention brands you carry. “Claritin, Zyrtec, and generic options in stock. Curbside pickup available.”
- Landing page: Category page or product list page, not individual products

**Budget split example for a $1,500/month pharmacy:**
- Prescription services: $700 (highest intent, highest lifetime value)
- Clinical services: $500 (seasonal, but high conversion)
- OTC products: $300 (lower margin, use for brand awareness)

**Why this works:** Each campaign targets a different intent. A person searching for “flu shot” doesn’t want to see your vitamin aisle. A person searching for “prescription refill” doesn’t care about your flu shot prices. Separate campaigns mean separate budgets, separate ads, separate landing pages.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is Google Ads worth it for a small pharmacy with a $500/month budget?**

Depends. If you’re in a city with heavy chain pharmacy competition, $500/month gets you maybe 50-80 clicks. That’s tight. But if you focus on clinical services (flu shots, vaccines, health screenings) with local service ads, $500 can generate 20-30 qualified calls. That’s $500 well spent. Just don’t try to compete on “pharmacy near me” against CVS with a $500 budget. You’ll lose.

**Q: Can I target people who already have prescriptions at other pharmacies?**

No. You can target by location and search intent, but you cannot target specific customers or use customer lists without explicit consent. HIPAA applies. Ads are not medical records. Focus on keywords like “transfer prescription to [your pharmacy].” That targets people actively looking to switch.

**Q: How do I track if someone actually comes to my store from an ad?**

Two ways. First, use Google Ads’ store visits metric (works if you have location tracking enabled and enough traffic). Second, use a tracked phone number. Google Ads offers call extensions with forwarding numbers. Or use a tool like CallRail. When someone calls that number, you know it came from the ad. Then track whether they showed up. Old school, works every time.

**Q: Should I advertise prescription prices?**

Careful. Some states prohibit advertising prescription prices. Even where it’s legal, you’ll trigger comparison shopping that hurts your margin. Better to advertise service speed and convenience. “Same day refill” beats “$4 generic” every time for long-term customer value.

**Q: How long until I see results from Google Ads?**

If you set it up correctly, you’ll see calls within the first week. Actual revenue attribution takes 2-4 weeks because prescriptions take time to fill and refills happen on cycles. Don’t judge a campaign by the first 48 hours. Judge it after 30 days and $500 spent minimum.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads for my pharmacy if I’m not in the US?**

Yes, but the rules differ. Canada has stricter health advertising regulations. The UK and Australia have their own advertising codes for pharmacies. You need to check local laws before running health-related ads. I can’t speak to every country’s rules, but I can say this: do not run prescription-specific ads outside the US without legal review. Stick to OTC and services.
