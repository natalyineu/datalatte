**Step 1: Awareness**

The first step in the marketing funnel is to create awareness about your business. This is where most local businesses go wrong – they try to push their products or services on people without building any rapport or trust. But it doesn't have to be that way. Here are a few strategies you can use to create awareness:

*   Invest in local SEO to rank higher in search engine results pages (SERPs)
*   Create high-quality content that showcases your expertise and provides value to your audience
*   Use social media to share behind-the-scenes glimpses of your business and engage with your followers

**Step 2: Interest**

Now that you've created awareness, it's time to generate interest in your business. This is where you start to build a relationship with your potential customers and show them why your business is the best choice for them. Here are a few strategies you can use to generate interest:

*   Create a lead magnet that provides value to your audience and encourages them to opt-in to your email list
*   Use retargeting ads to reach people who have visited your website but haven't converted yet
*   Host events or webinars that showcase your expertise and provide value to your audience

**Step 3: Desire**

Now that you've generated interest, it's time to create desire for your business. This is where you start to build a sense of urgency and encourage people to take action. Here are a few strategies you can use to create desire:

*   Create a sense of scarcity by offering limited-time promotions or discounts
*   Use social proof to show people that others have had success with your business
*   Offer a free consultation or trial to give people a taste of what you have to offer

**Step 4: Action**

Now that you've created desire, it's time to take action. This is where you encourage people to take the final step and become a customer. Here are a few strategies you can use to take action:

*   Make it easy for people to book an appointment or make a purchase by providing clear calls-to-action (CTAs)
*   Use retargeting ads to reach people who have abandoned their carts or haven't converted yet
*   Offer a loyalty program or rewards scheme to encourage repeat business

**Step 5: Retention**

Finally, it's time to retain your customers and encourage them to become loyal advocates for your business. Here are a few strategies you can use to retain customers:

*   Offer regular communication and updates to keep your customers informed and engaged
*   Use surveys and feedback to understand what your customers want and need
*   Provide exceptional customer service to ensure that your customers are happy and satisfied

<Callout type="tip">Don't neglect your existing customers! Retention is just as important as acquisition when it comes to growing your business.</Callout>

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've walked into more small businesses where the owner says "we have a funnel" and what they mean is "we post on Instagram sometimes and hope for the best." Here are the mistakes I see most often — and what I tell my clients instead.

### Mistake #1: Obsessing Over Awareness While Ignoring Everything Else

**The story:** A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas spent $2,500/month on Instagram ads for six months. Beautiful photos of lattes, shots of the barista pouring heart designs, that sort of thing. She tracked views and likes religiously. Her revenue? Up maybe 3%. She blamed the algorithm.

**The problem:** She was throwing money at the top of the funnel while the middle and bottom were full of holes. People saw her ads, thought "cute coffee," walked in once, and never came back. She had no email list, no loyalty program, no way to reach anyone who didn't immediately buy a latte that same day.

**The fix:** I told her to cut the ad budget to $800/month and spend the remaining $1,700 on three things: a simple Mailchimp signup at the register ("get a free cookie on your next visit"), a Google Ads campaign targeting "coffee shop near me" with a specific offer, and a loyalty card system tracked through Square's built-in tools.

**The outcome:** Three months later, her revenue was up 22%. She had 400 email addresses and could send a "rainy day 20% off" email that actually brought people in. The Instagram ads were pretty. The funnel actually worked.

### Mistake #2: Treating Every Customer Like They're the Same

**The story:** A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon had been in business for fourteen years. She had a stack of client cards — paper cards — with handwritten notes about who liked what haircut. She thought that was her CRM. She ran one monthly promotion: "20% off any service." It worked sometimes, but mostly it attracted the bargain hunters who never came back at full price.

**The problem:** She was marketing to her regulars the same way she marketed to strangers. Her loyal clients — the ones who came every five weeks without fail — didn't need a 20% off email. They needed a "first look at our new stylist's portfolio" email or an invite to a private after-hours event. The discount was actually devaluing the relationship.

**The fix:** We segmented her list using Mailchimp's tagging feature. Regulars (4+ visits in the last year) got exclusive previews and referral bonuses. Occasional visitors (1–3 visits) got a "we miss you" email with a specific $25 discount. Strangers got the standard offer — plus a Google Business Profile optimization so she showed up in search results for "women's haircut Portland."

**The outcome:** Her repeat visit rate went from 41% to 63% in five months. She stopped running the blanket 20% discount. Her average ticket increased because her regulars started booking more expensive services (color, extensions) instead of just a basic cut.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the Middle of the Funnel Entirely

**The story:** A pet groomer in Nashville spent $1,200 on a Yelp ad package. She got calls. People booked. Then they showed up once and disappeared. She'd ask "when should I schedule your next appointment?" and they'd say "I'll call you." They never called.

**The problem:** She had no bridge between "first appointment" and "regular customer." No follow-up email. No reminder system. No incentive to come back. She was relying on people remembering her, which they don't. People forget things. That's not a moral failing, it's a marketing opportunity.

**The fix:** We set up Booksy — $30/month — so clients could book online and get automatic reminders. Then we built a simple automated email sequence: Day 1 after appointment ("thanks for visiting, here's 15% off your next groom if you book within 4 weeks"), Day 10 ("your pup's groom is probably looking rough about now"), Day 21 ("your next appointment window is opening up"). The whole thing ran on autopilot.

**The outcome:** She went from 12% repeat booking rate to 38% in eight weeks. That $1,200 Yelp spend? She canceled it after the contract ended. Booksy and email automation did more for her retention than Yelp ever did.

## Retargeting Locally: Why Window Shoppers Leave, And How To Bring Them Back

Most small business owners think retargeting is something big ecommerce brands do. They're wrong. Retargeting for a local business is simple and costs less than you think.

**The concept:** Someone visits your website, looks at your menu or services page, and leaves. They didn't buy. They didn't book. They probably got distracted by a kid or a work email. That person is not a lost cause — they're a lead who hasn't committed yet. You need to remind them you exist.

**The tool:** Google Ads has a feature called Customer Match. Upload a list of email addresses from people who've inquired but didn't book, or website visitors who didn't convert. Google will show them your ad when they search for your category again. I've seen this work for a yoga studio in Denver that had a 40% "bounce from the pricing page" rate. They uploaded those email addresses. Retargeting cost them $300/month. They recovered $4,200 in new memberships within 30 days.

**The local angle:** Facebook ads allow radius targeting. You can show an ad to anyone who visited your website in the last 30 days _and_ lives within 3 miles of your shop. That's gold. A pizza place in Chicago ran this exact campaign — $5/day for two weeks. They tracked 17 new customers who mentioned the ad, with an average order of $34. That's a 4x return.

**The hard part:** You need to actually track who visits your website. Install the Google Pixel or Facebook Pixel. It takes ten minutes. If you're not doing this, you're sending people into a black hole.

**The mistake I see most:** Business owners run retargeting for one week, get zero conversions, and quit. Retargeting works on a delay. Someone needs to see your ad 3–5 times before they act. Give it four weeks minimum. If it's not working after that, your offer is wrong, not the strategy.

## The Loyalty Loop: Turning Regulars Into Recurring Revenue

The top of the funnel gets all the attention. The bottom of the funnel — where loyal customers live — is where the money actually is.

**The data I use with clients:** A returning customer is worth 5–7x more than a first-time customer over their lifetime. That's not a vague statistic. That's me looking at Square data for 30+ clients across three countries. The pattern holds.

**The strategy that actually works:** Don't just give them points. Build a system that makes it harder to leave than to stay.

**Example from NYC:** A boutique fitness studio had 2,000 active members and a 35% monthly churn rate. That's brutal — over a third of their members quitting every single month. They had a punch card system. Ten classes, get one free. It wasn't working.

We replaced the punch card with a tiered membership model. Three tiers: bronze (4 classes/month, $89), silver (8 classes, $149), gold (unlimited, $199). The key was the auto-renewal. Members had to actively cancel — not actively renew. Human nature is lazy. They kept the gold tier people by offering a free guest pass every month so they could bring a friend, which also served as free acquisition at the bottom of the funnel.

**The tool:** We used Mindbody, which integrates with Square and Mailchimp. The studio's revenue went from $42,000/month to $67,000/month in four months. Churn dropped to 18%. They didn't run a single new customer acquisition campaign during that period. All growth came from keeping the people they already had.

**The uncomfortable truth:** Most local businesses spend 80% of their marketing budget trying to get new customers and 20% trying to keep them. It should be the opposite. If you have 500 customers who love you, and you keep each one coming back one extra time per year at $50 per visit, that's $25,000 in revenue — no ad spend required.

**What to do this week:** Look at your Square, Booksy, or Mindbody data. Identify your top 20% of customers by visit frequency. Send them a handwritten thank-you note. Not an email. Paper. Cost you $2 in postage. Return on that is absurd.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a small business, not a corporation. Do I really need a funnel? I just want people to walk in the door.**

Yes, you do. The funnel isn't a corporate thing. It's "how do people find me, what happens when they do, and how do I get them to come back." That's it. If you're ignoring any of those three questions, you're bleeding money. I worked with a bakery in Kansas City that had no funnel — just a sign on the sidewalk. They were losing 60% of people who walked past because the sign only listed the business name, not what they should do. A simple "fresh croissants inside — try one for free" sign cost nothing and increased walk-ins by 30%.

**Q: I tried email marketing. Nobody opened the emails. Why would I keep doing it?**

Because you're sending the wrong emails. I see this constantly: "10% off coupon" every single time. People get numb to that. Send something useful. A coffee shop sent a "here's how we source our beans" story with a short video. Open rate: 48%. Compare that to their standard "Friday deal" email at 11% open rate. The fix isn't "email doesn't work." The fix is "what you're sending is boring."

**Q: How much money do I actually need to spend on ads to see results?**

For a local business? Start at $300–$500/month on one platform. Not three. Pick one. Google Ads if you have people searching for what you do ("dentist Austin"). Facebook if you need to create demand ("you didn't know you needed this service but here's why"). Put that budget in for 60 days. If you don't see at least 3x return, kill it and try the other platform. Do not double down on something that isn't working just because you feel invested.

**Q: What's the biggest waste of money you see local businesses making?**

Yelp advertising. I'm not saying it never works, but I've audited 40+ local business accounts and in 38 of them, Yelp ad spend had the worst ROI of any channel. The platform makes its money by making you pay to hide negative reviews. That's not a partnership, that's a protection racket. Spend that money on local SEO and Google Business Profile optimization instead.

**Q: I don't have time to manage all this. Can't I just hire someone?**

You can, but hire for the right thing. Don't hire a "social media manager" who posts pretty pictures. Hire someone who understands funnel mechanics — how to move someone from Instagram to email to booking. That's a different skill set. If you can't afford that yet, focus on one channel and do it well. A pet store in Denver hired a local college student for $15/hour to manage their Google Business Profile and respond to reviews. Their foot traffic went up 18% in three months. You don't need a full marketing department. You need someone who does the specific thing that moves the needle.

**Q: What if my town is too small for any of this to matter?**

I hear this from business owners in towns of 5,000 people. Here's the truth: your town is not too small for a funnel. It's too small for you to ignore customer retention. If there are only 10,000 people in your area, you can't afford to lose a single customer. Every person who walks in needs to become a regular, or you eventually run out of new people. A hardware store in rural Wisconsin had 3,200 repeat customers. They started a "frequent buyer" program tied to their Square account. Same town. No new residents. Revenue increased 14% in six months because they stopped leaking customers.
