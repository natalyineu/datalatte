### Setting Clear Goals for Your Webinar

Before you start planning your webinar, it's essential to set clear goals. What do you want to achieve with your webinar? Do you want to:

* Promote your coaching program?
* Build your email list?
* Generate leads for your business?
* Showcase your expertise?

Whatever your goal, it's crucial to define it clearly and make sure it aligns with your business objectives.

### Creating Engaging Content

Your webinar content should be engaging, informative, and relevant to your audience. Here are some tips to help you create compelling content:

* **Use a conversational tone**: Speak in a way that feels natural and conversational.
* **Use storytelling techniques**: Share personal anecdotes, case studies, or examples that illustrate your points.
* **Use visuals**: Incorporate images, videos, or slides to break up the presentation and make it more engaging.
* **Keep it concise**: Keep your presentation to the point and avoid unnecessary tangents.

### Choosing the Right Platform

With so many webinar platforms available, choosing the right one can be overwhelming. Here are some popular options:

* **Zoom**: A popular choice for webinars, Zoom offers high-quality video and audio, and is easy to use.
* **GoToWebinar**: A professional-grade webinar platform that offers advanced features and integration with other tools.
* **Demio**: A user-friendly platform that offers a range of features, including automated follow-up emails and analytics.

### Promoting Your Webinar

Promoting your webinar is just as important as creating engaging content. Here are some tips to help you get the word out:

* **Use social media**: Promote your webinar on Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn, and other social media platforms.
* **Email marketing**: Send targeted emails to your list, highlighting the benefits of your webinar.
* **Influencer marketing**: Partner with influencers in your niche to promote your webinar to their followers.
* **Paid advertising**: Consider running paid ads on Facebook, Google, or other platforms to reach a wider audience.

### Measuring Your Success

Measuring the success of your webinar is crucial to understanding its impact and improving future webinars. Here are some key metrics to track:

* **Attendance**: The number of people who attend your webinar.
* **Engagement**: The level of engagement, such as questions, comments, and interactions.
* **Conversion rate**: The percentage of attendees who take action after the webinar, such as signing up for your coaching program or purchasing a product.
* **Revenue**: The revenue generated from your webinar, such as sales or leads.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve run, consulted on, or audited roughly 200 webinars over the past decade. Most of them were fine. Some were incredible. A few were actively painful to sit through. Here are the three patterns that killed the most results — all with real stories from small business owners I worked with.

### Mistake 1: Building a Webinar That Shows Off Instead of Selling

**The story:** A life coach in Austin, Texas — let’s call her Sarah — spent three weeks designing a webinar titled “Five Secrets to Inner Peace.” She had a slick slide deck, a meditation video, and a PDF handout. She promoted it on Facebook for two weeks. Forty-one people registered. Eleven showed up. Zero signed up for her $1,200 coaching program.

**What went wrong:** Sarah treated the webinar like a free sample of her expertise. She taught five complete techniques. The audience got the full meal. There was no reason to buy the program. She also never mentioned her program until slide 47, which was titled “By the way, I offer coaching.” By then, people had already mentally checked out.

**The fix:** I told Sarah to rip out three of the five secrets and turn the remaining two into problems she could solve in the session but couldn’t *fully* solve without her program. She added a specific offer at minute 40: “If you want the step-by-step system for this, including the accountability structure, I have three spots open. Here’s what it costs, here’s what you get, here’s the deadline to join.”

**The outcome:** Her next webinar produced five enrollments at $1,200 each — $6,000 in revenue. She ran it twice a month for three months. Total cost per webinar was about $150 for ads and her Zoom subscription. That’s a 40x return. She dropped the free PDF and started selling.

**The rule:** If your webinar teaches everything for free, nobody needs to pay you. Teach enough to prove your method works. Then stop.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring the Follow-Up Sequence

**The story:** A pet grooming salon owner in Portland, Oregon — I’ll call him Mark — ran a webinar called “How to Groom Your Dog at Home (Without Crying).” He had 34 attendees. He gave a great demo. He promoted his premium grooming kit ($89) and online course ($250). Nobody bought during the webinar. He assumed it flopped and moved on.

**What went wrong:** Mark didn’t send a single follow-up email. No replay link. No “hey, here’s the offer one more time.” Nothing. He logged off Zoom and went back to clipping claws. I asked him if he had a list of registrants. He did. I asked if he ever emailed them again. He said no.

**The fix:** I wrote a three-email follow-up sequence for him. Email one (same day): replay link + offer summary. Email two (day two): a testimonial from a past student who used the course to stop their dog from biting during grooming. Email three (day five): “Last chance — doors close Friday at midnight.” Total time to write: 45 minutes. Cost: zero dollars.

**The outcome:** Of the 34 attendees, 7 bought the kit, and 4 bought the course. That’s $623 in kit sales and $1,000 in course sales — $1,623 total from a webinar he thought was a failure. He’d spent about $400 on Facebook ads to get those registrants. That’s a 4x return, entirely from emails he didn’t send the first time.

**The rule:** The webinar is the middle of the funnel, not the end. Most conversions happen in the 72 hours after the live event. If you don’t follow up, you’re leaving money on the table.

### Mistake 3: Making the Registration Process Too Hard

**The story:** A fitness studio owner in Denver — let’s call her Jen — ran a free webinar called “Mobility for Desk Workers.” She had a great topic. She had a good offer. She had 12 registrations. Two people showed up. She blamed the time slot.

**The reality:** Jen’s registration page had ten fields: name, email, phone, business name, how did you hear about us, what is your biggest challenge with mobility (long text), have you taken a class with us before, what times are you available (checkboxes), do you agree to receive marketing emails, and a captcha. It took me 90 seconds to fill it out in a test run. That’s an eternity.

**The fix:** I cut the form to two fields: name and email. No phone. No challenge question. No captcha (Zoom’s registration link already handles spam). I also added a one-click calendar link so registrants grabbed the webinar time immediately instead of leaving to think about it.

**The outcome:** Her next webinar had 57 registrants and 19 attendees. She sold three memberships at $149/month each. That’s $447 in monthly recurring revenue from a single webinar. The only change was removing friction from the signup form.

**The rule:** Every extra field in your registration form costs you roughly 10–15% of potential registrants. You don’t need their life story. You need their email. Get it fast, then ask for more later.

## How to Use Simple Retargeting to Reclaim No-Shows

Here’s the uncomfortable truth: the average webinar attendance rate is somewhere between 30% and 45% of registrants. That means over half the people who said “yes” never showed up. Most business owners shrug and move on. That’s a mistake, because those no-shows are still warm leads. They raised their hand. They just forgot to show up.

**The fix:** Build a tiny retargeting campaign on Google Ads and Facebook Ads specifically for no-shows. Here’s how it looks in practice.

A bakery owner in Nashville ran a webinar called “How to Price Your Baked Goods for Profit.” She had 120 registrants. Forty-eight attended. Seventy-two did not. She exported her attendee list and her no-show list from Zoom as two separate CSV files. Then she uploaded the no-show list as a custom audience in Facebook Ads Manager.

She created one ad: a short video replay clip (60 seconds) showing a specific pricing mistake she corrected during the webinar. The caption said: “You registered for my pricing webinar but couldn’t make it. Here’s a quick fix you can use today. The full replay is available for the next 48 hours.” She set a budget of $10 per day for three days.

**Total cost:** $30 in ad spend.

**Outcome:** Thirteen of the 72 no-shows watched the replay. Three of those 13 booked a 30-minute consultation call. Two bought her pricing template ($97 each). That’s $194 in direct revenue from $30 in ads — a 6.5x return on ad spend. And one of those two later signed up for her full pricing coaching program ($1,500). She didn’t even run a second ad.

You can do the same thing with Google Ads remarketing lists for search ads (RLSA). Set a small bid adjustment for no-shows, then serve them a search ad when they Google “pricing mistakes for bakers” or “how to set bakery prices.” The cost per click will be higher because the audience is smaller, but the conversion rate will be dramatically higher because they already know you.

**The tool stack:** Zoom (for registration and attendee data), Facebook Ads Manager (for retargeting), Google Ads (for RLSA). Optionally, use Mailchimp or ActiveCampaign to tag your no-shows and send them a separate email sequence with the replay link plus a limited-time offer. The email costs nothing. The ads cost pocket change. The returns are real.

## Booking Software Integration: Automating the Handoff

Most coaches and service business owners end their webinar with something vague like “book a call with me” or “sign up for my program.” That’s a handoff that relies on the attendee to figure out the next step. Human nature: they won’t. You need to automate the handoff.

Here’s what I mean. A hair salon owner in Chicago ran a webinar called “Low-Maintenance Color for Busy Moms.” She had 35 attendees. She ended the webinar by saying, “If you want to book a color appointment, go to my website and use the booking form.” Results: two bookings.

**The problem:** The attendee had to leave the Zoom window, open a browser, find the website, navigate to the booking page, type in their information, and choose a time. That’s six steps. People lose interest at step two.

**The fix:** She integrated her booking software (she used Booksy, which is common for salons) directly into the webinar workflow. She added a unique booking link as a pinned message in the Zoom chat. Clicking it opened a pre-filled booking form with her name, the service (color consultation), and a list of available times that week. The attendee only had to tap a time slot and confirm.

**Outcome:** After the integration, the same webinar with a similar audience produced 11 bookings. Nine of those converted into color appointments averaging $220 per service. That’s $1,980 in booked revenue from a single 45-minute session. The integration took one afternoon and cost nothing beyond her existing Booksy subscription.

**For coaches:** Use Calendly or Acuity Scheduling. Create a specific “Webinar Follow-Up Call” event type with a 30-minute slot. Add a custom field: “What was your biggest takeaway from the webinar?” This gives you context for the call. Put the link in the Zoom chat at minute 40, send it in the post-webinar email, and include it in the thank-you page for the replay recording.

**For product-based businesses:** Use Square online checkout or Stripe payment links. Share a discounted “webinar-only” product link during the session. Frame it as: “This link works for the next 60 minutes. After that, the price goes back to normal.” Urgency drives action.

The point is: remove every single click between “I want this” and “I booked this.” Your job during the webinar is not to convince people to buy later. Your job is to make it so easy to buy that they almost buy by accident.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I don’t have a big email list. Can I still run a successful webinar?**
Yes, but you’ll have to work harder on the promotion side. With a small list (say 200–300 people), your attendance will likely be 30–60 people. That’s enough to validate your offer. Use Facebook ads to bring in cold traffic — start with $15–20 per day for a week. Target people who follow similar accounts or have engaged with competitor content. I saw a coach in Minneapolis generate 14 registrations from a $120 ad spend on a list of zero. She had no email list at all. She just ran ads. It works.

**Q: Do I need to use a paid webinar platform? Or can I just use Zoom?**
Zoom works fine for most cases. The free version limits you to 40 minutes, which is actually useful — it forces you to be concise. Upgrade to the paid plan ($15.99/month) for longer sessions and better analytics. Don’t overthink this. I’ve seen excellent results from Zoom webinars where the host just shared their screen and talked. The platform is not the bottleneck.

**Q: Should my webinar be free or paid?**
Free if your goal is lead generation. Paid if you want to filter out tire-kickers. For a small business owner running their first webinar, I’d recommend free. The volume of registrants will be higher, and you can use the follow-up offer to convert. If you’re worried about no-shows, charge $7 or $10. That filters out most people who aren’t serious while still being low enough that the barrier to entry is low. A pet trainer in Austin charged $15 for her webinar and got 25 attendees — all qualified, all ready to buy. She closed 8 of them.

**Q: How long should my webinar be?**
Thirty to 45 minutes, plus 10–15 minutes for Q&A. Any longer than an hour, and you’ll see drop-off. I’ve tested 30-minute webinars and 75-minute webinars. The 30-minute versions consistently had higher attendee retention and lower bounce rates. People’s attention is limited. Respect it.

**Q: What’s the best time of day to run a webinar?**
Tuesday through Thursday at 12:00 PM ET or 1:00 PM ET. Avoid Mondays (people are catching up) and Fridays (people are checked out). Evening webinars can work for local audiences — a yoga studio in San Diego had great success with a 6:00 PM PT slot because people were done with work. But for national audiences, lunchtime is the safest bet. Test and adjust based on your own data.

**Q: I already have a YouTube channel. Can I just repurpose that content into a webinar?**
You can, but don’t expect the same conversion rate. Webinars work because they’re live, interactive, and have a time-limited offer. A recorded video on YouTube doesn’t have that urgency. If you repurpose content, make it clear that the webinar is a live event with a specific call to action. You can always turn the recording into a YouTube video later — that’s fine as a lead magnet or evergreen content. But live is where the money is.

**Q: What if nobody asks questions during the Q&A?**
Pre-pave. Ask people to submit questions during registration. Pull one or two from the list and say, “Someone asked a great question during registration…” This breaks the ice. If that still fails, answer questions you *wish* they’d ask. “One thing I often get asked is…” then answer it. Keep talking. A silence of more than 10 seconds feels painful, but it’s not fatal. Just keep moving.
