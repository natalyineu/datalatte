## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Relying on Unlicensed "Handyman" SEO Agencies

I've seen this exact scenario play out at least four times with different clients. A home remodeling business owner in Nashville, TN — let's call him Mike — had a kitchen and bath company pulling in about $420,000 annually. He hired an SEO agency that promised "first-page rankings in 30 days" for $800/month.

Here's what actually happened: The agency built fifty doorway pages targeting "Nashville kitchen remodel," "Nashville bathroom remodel," "kitchen remodel Nashville TN," and every possible variation. They stuffed keywords into invisible text. Google caught on in about six weeks. Mike's site got a manual action penalty. His organic traffic dropped from about 2,300 monthly visits to zero. The agency disappeared.

**The fix:** Mike cut his losses at $2,400 wasted over three months, plus the cost of a new site rebuild ($3,500). He hired a local SEO consultant who actually understood the construction industry. They built ten genuinely useful pages — real project galleries with square footage, material costs, timeline breakdowns. Six months later, Mike's traffic was back to 2,800 monthly visits, but more importantly, his lead quality improved. He was getting calls for $45,000 whole-home remodels instead of $8,000 bathroom refreshes. His revenue hit $680,000 the following year.

**What to do instead:** If an SEO person can't explain in plain English what they're building for each page, walk away. Ask for three client references in your industry. Call them. If the reference says "I don't really know what they do" — that's your red flag.

### Mistake #2: Running Awareness Ads When You Need Direct Response

A remodeling company in Austin, TX — let's call it Hill Country Renovations — spent $3,200/month on Facebook and Instagram ads in 2022. The ads showed beautiful lifestyle shots of kitchens with soft lighting and no text overlay. They got 47,000 impressions a month. They also got exactly zero booked consultations from those ads over five months.

The owner, Sarah, came to me frustrated. "Everyone says they saw my ad, but nobody calls."

**The problem:** She was running brand awareness campaigns for a service that people don't buy on impulse. Nobody sees a pretty kitchen photo while scrolling Instagram and thinks "Yes, I will now commit to an eight-week construction project." That's not how renovation purchasing works. Renovation clients research for three to six weeks before reaching out.

**The fix:** We stopped all awareness campaigns. We redirected that $3,200 into Google Local Services Ads and targeted search campaigns with specific intent keywords: "kitchen remodel cost Austin TX," "bathroom renovation contractor," "home addition builder Austin." We also created a landing page with actual numbers — average project cost ($52,000), typical timeline (6-8 weeks for a kitchen), and three specific project breakdowns with before/after photos and price tags.

**The outcome:** In the first full month, the new setup generated 14 qualified leads. Seven turned into booked site visits. Two signed contracts worth $47,000 and $63,000. The ad spend was $3,100 for the month. That's a 35X return on ad spend from the first month. Sarah told me she'd wasted about $16,000 before we made the switch.

**What to do instead:** If you have less than $2,500/month for ads, skip all social media advertising entirely. Put it all into Google Local Services Ads. You can set a weekly budget, you only pay for leads, and Google vets your business against licensing and insurance requirements. It's not glamorous, but it works.

### Mistake #3: Making Your Website a Brochure Instead of a Sales Tool

I worked with a custom home builder in Portland, OR who had a gorgeous website. Stunning photography. Elegant typography. A slow-loading video background. The site cost $14,000 to build.

It also had a contact form buried on a page called "Get in Touch" — third item in the navigation. No phone number above the fold. No clear call to action. The builder was getting about three form submissions per month from a site that cost $1,200/month in hosting and maintenance.

**The fix:** This was painful. The owner had to accept that his beautiful website was costing him money. We redesigned the home page with four changes: phone number in the top right corner with a tracking number, a "Book a Free In-Home Estimate" button that went straight to a Calendly link, a one-paragraph explanation of his pricing model ("Custom homes starting at $350/sq ft. Fixed price contracts. No change orders without your written approval."), and three case studies with actual project budgets.

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, phone calls increased from roughly 8 per week to 22. The Calendly link doubled booked site visits. His close rate actually dropped slightly — from about 30% to 25% — because he was getting more unqualified leads. But the total number of signed contracts went up. His revenue increased by about $380,000 in the next 12 months from that one change. The website cost $4,800 to rebuild.

**What to do instead:** Before you redesign anything, test one thing: put a phone number and a booking link on every single page of your current site. Track calls for two weeks. If that doesn't increase leads, the problem isn't your website — it's something else.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Your Existing Client List

This one is painfully common. A remodeling company in Chicago — family-owned, been around since 1998 — had a list of about 1,400 past clients in a spreadsheet. Nobody had emailed them in three years. The owner genuinely believed that "people will call us if they need work again."

**The problem:** People forget. They also assume you're too busy or too expensive if they don't hear from you. This company was losing about 40% of its repeat business to competitors who were actively staying in touch.

**The fix:** I had the office manager send a simple email to the entire list using Mailchimp (free tier). No fancy template. Just text: "Hey, it's been a while. We're booking spring kitchen and bath projects. If you've been thinking about that remodel, reply to this email and I'll come take a look at your current space. No obligation, no sales pitch."

**The outcome:** That single email generated 47 replies. Fourteen turned into consultations. Seven signed contracts totaling $189,000 in revenue over the next four months. The cost was exactly zero dollars and about 30 minutes of the office manager's time.

**What to do instead:** If you don't have an email list, start one today. Every client who finishes a project gets added. Send a check-in email every 90 days. Ask about their remodel. Offer a free maintenance visit (check caulking, tighten cabinet hinges, reseal grout). That small touch generates more business than any paid ad.

## Local SEO That Actually Works for Remodelers

Most local SEO advice is written by people who have never managed a renovation project. They tell you to "optimize your Google Business Profile" and "get more reviews" without explaining what that actually means for a contractor who's trying to install a shower pan before noon.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: Google Business Profile (GBP) is the single most important marketing asset for a home remodeling business. Not your website. Not your Facebook page. Your GBP listing. When someone in your area searches "bathroom remodel near me," the top three local service results control about 92% of the clicks. The organic website results below them get scraps.

**What actually works:**

1. **Categories matter more than you think.** The default category for most remodelers is "General Contractor." That's wrong for most of you. If you primarily do kitchens and baths, set your primary category to "Kitchen Remodeler" or "Bathroom Remodeler." If you do additions, use "Home Builder." If you do both, create separate GBP listings for each service (if you have separate addresses or service areas).

2. **Post weekly — with actual useful content.** Not "We're the best remodelers in town!" Posts like "Here's what a kitchen remodel costs in Denver right now: average $55,000 for a 200 sq ft space, 6-8 weeks, includes cabinets, countertops, and appliances. Here's how to budget." That post will get more engagement than any ad you pay for.

3. **Respond to every single review — good and bad.** This is a time sink, I know. But I've tracked it. A remodeling company in Denver that responded to 100% of reviews within 48 hours saw a 22% increase in their GBP visibility score over three months. The owner replied to each review with a specific detail from the project — "Thanks, Susan! Glad that blue glass backsplash worked out better than the sample showed." Google's algorithm notices specificity.

4. **Add photos every month.** Not professional photos. Your phone photos. A crew member installing crown molding. A progress shot of a demo day. A finished master bath. Name each photo with something useful: "Denver master bath remodel with heated floors and custom tile shower — April 2024." Location data from the phone photo helps Google confirm you're working in that area.

**Real numbers:** A client in Nashville with 34 reviews and consistent weekly posts went from appearing in the local map pack for about 6 of his 15 target keywords to appearing for 13 of them. His monthly website traffic from GBP alone went from 180 visits to 420. He spent zero dollars on this. Just 45 minutes a week.

## Getting Reviews That Build Trust (And Rank)

Every remodeler knows they need reviews. Most do it wrong.

**The wrong way:** "Hey, if you liked our work, leave us a Google review!" People mean well, but that gets you one review for every twenty requests. And the reviews you get tend to be generic. "Great work. Would recommend." Those don't help your SEO or your sales.

**The better way I've seen work consistently:**

Send a follow-up email three days after project completion. Include a few specific questions:
- "What was your biggest worry before we started?"
- "What surprised you most about working with us?"
- "Would you share one specific detail about the finished project you love?"

People will reply to those questions. Then you reply back: "Would you mind posting that on Google? Here's a direct link." The specific answers become the review content. Specific reviews rank better in Google's local algorithm because they contain keywords naturally.

I watched a remodeler in Portland implement this system. He used Square's automated follow-up tool (free with their payment processing). In the first 60 days, his Google review count went from 12 to 31. More importantly, the average review length went from 18 words to 74 words. His GBP started showing for "Portland custom kitchen" — a keyword he'd been chasing for two years.

**One more thing:** Don't respond to negative reviews with generic corporate apologies. If someone complains about a delay because of supply chain issues (which happens constantly in remodeling), say exactly what happened: "You're right, the Italian marble tile was backordered 11 weeks. We should have communicated the timeline better. Here's what we've changed to prevent this — we now order materials 90 days before the project start date." That specific response has actually converted people who were reading reviews. They see honesty and a clear fix.

## Which Ad Platforms Waste Money for Remodelers

I've tested every major ad platform for remodeling clients. Here's my honest ranking based on actual campaign data from 2022-2024:

**1. Google Local Services Ads (LSAs) — Best for almost everyone**

This is the only platform where you can set a budget and know exactly what you're paying per lead. In my experience across 12 remodeling clients, the average cost per lead from LSAs is $18-$45. Compare that to Google search ads at $45-$120 per click (not per lead — per click). The tradeoff: LSAs only work if you have clean licensing, insurance, and background checks. Google vets you. That's actually good — it filters out unlicensed competitors.

**2. Google Search Ads — Good if you do it right**

The key is negative keywords. Most remodelers waste money showing up for "kitchen remodel cost" from people who will never hire them. Add these negative keywords: "how to," "DIY," "cost," "price," "ideas," "plans," "free estimate." You want people searching for "kitchen remodel contractor Nashville" — not people who need a budget spreadsheet.

**3. Facebook and Instagram ads — Only for very specific use cases**

I ran a test for a high-end remodeler in San Francisco. We targeted homeowners in $2M+ ZIP codes, age 45-65, interests in architecture and interior design. We spent $2,800 over two months. Got 14 leads. Zero closed projects. The owner told me later that people who clicked his ads were "browsing inspiration, not ready to write a check." He now spends that money on Google LSAs and gets 5-8 booked consultations per month.

**4. Nextdoor — Surprisingly effective for smaller projects**

A remodeling company in Austin ran a Nextdoor campaign targeting neighborhoods within 5 miles. Budget: $400/month. They got 12 leads in the first month. Three turned into projects — a deck rebuild ($12,000), a bathroom update ($8,500), and a kitchen refresh ($14,000). The audience on Nextdoor skews older and more local-minded. These people hire neighbors. If you can get three positive mentions in local Nextdoor posts, you'll get more business than any ad buy.

**5. Angi (formerly Angie's List) — I'd skip it**

I've worked with six remodelers who tried Angi. Average cost per lead: $65-$120. Average close rate: 8%. That's $810-$1,500 per closed client in acquisition cost. Most of my clients dropped it within three months. The leads tend to be price-shoppers comparing four bids. You're competing on price immediately. Unless you're the cheapest in your market, this isn't worth it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on marketing per month?**

For a remodeling business under $1M annual revenue, you should be spending 8-12% of projected revenue on marketing. If you're at $500K and want to grow to $800K, that's $40,000-$96,000 per year, or roughly $3,300-$8,000 per month. Start at the low end and increase as you can prove ROI. I've seen businesses grow fine on $2,000/month if they're smart about where it goes. I've also seen businesses burn $10,000/month on the wrong ads and go backwards.

**Q: Do I really need a website, or can I just use Facebook and Google Business Profile?**

You need a website. Not an elaborate one. A simple one-page site with your services, service area, phone number, booking link, and 5-7 project photos. Why? Google Business Profile links to your website. Facebook pages don't convert renovation clients — people want to see that you're a real business with a real address and real project documentation. A Squarespace site costs $16/month. Skip the fancy features. Get the basics right.

**Q: Should I pay for Yelp advertising?**

I have never seen Yelp ads work for a remodeling business. Not once in 10+ years. The Yelp ad platform is designed to keep you spending money. You'll get a lot of leads — almost all of them price-shoppers who request five bids. If you have the patience to track every lead source meticulously, you can test Yelp with a $200 monthly budget for 90 days. But I'd put that $600 into Google LSAs instead.

**Q: How do I compete with bigger companies that have bigger budgets?**

You stop trying to outspend them. You out-position them. Bigger companies are slow, bureaucratic, and send project managers you'll never meet. Your advantage: you're the owner. You show up to every estimate. You know your suppliers by name. You can make decisions in five minutes instead of five days. Put that in your marketing: "You'll work directly with the owner from estimate to final walkthrough." That message converts.

**Q: What's the best way to handle slow seasons?**

Renovation businesses in cold climates typically slow down November through February. Don't try to out-advertise the slump. Instead, use that time to email your past client list. Offer a discount on design consultations for spring projects. "Book your design consultation by January 31 and we'll credit $150 toward your renovation." That brings in cash during slow months and locks in spring work. I've seen this generate $30,000-$50,000 in pre-booked work for remodelers who do it consistently.

**Q: How long until Google ads start working?**

For a new ad account with no history, plan on 4-8 weeks of testing. The first two weeks are data collection. Weeks 3-4 are optimization. By week 6, you should have a clear picture of which keywords and audiences work. If you're not seeing qualified leads by week 8, either your targeting is wrong or your offer isn't competitive. Don't let an agency tell you "it needs more time" past 120 days. By then, you should have clear ROI numbers or you should fire them.
