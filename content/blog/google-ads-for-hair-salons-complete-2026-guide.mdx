### 1. Create a Google Ads Account

If you don’t already have one, go to [Google Ads](https://ads.google.com/) and sign up with your Google account. You’ll need a credit card for billing, even if you start with a small budget.

### 2. Set Up a Google Business Profile (GBP)

Before you launch any ads, make sure you have a [Google Business Profile](https://www.google.com/business/) set up. This is the foundation for your local visibility.

- Add your salon’s name, address, phone number, hours, and logo.
- Upload high-quality photos of your team and space.
- Encourage clients to leave reviews.

> **Pro tip:** We cover GBP setup for salons in detail [here](/blog/best-marketing-for-salons-in-2026-what-actually-works-with-real-examples).

### 3. Define Your Target Audience

Google Ads lets you target people based on:

- **Location**  -  Show ads only to people within a set radius of your salon.
- **Search terms**  -  Target people who’ve searched for phrases like "hair salons near me" or "best haircuts for women."
- **Device**  -  Most salons see 70%+ of clicks on mobile devices.
- **Budget**  -  Start small, say $10 a day, and adjust based on results.

> Need help picking the right keywords? Check out our guide on [best Google Ad keywords for salons in 2026](/blog/best-google-ad-keywords-for-coffee-shops-in-2026).

### 4. Choose the Right Campaign Type

For salons, the best campaign type is **Search Network**. This shows your ads on Google search results for specific keywords. You can also run **Google Maps Ads** through your GBP, but Search Network gives you more control over targeting.

Here’s how to choose:

- **Search Network**  -  Best for targeting people actively searching for your services.
- **Display Network**  -  Good for retargeting people who’ve visited your site before.
- **Maps Ads**  -  Shows your GBP when someone searches for your service locally.

For now, stick with Search Network and build from there.

### 5. Write Compelling Ad Copy

Your ad copy needs to make people act. Use clear, urgent language and include a strong call-to-action (CTA). Here’s an example:

> **Hair Salon in [City] | Book Your Appointment Now!**
> 💆♀️ Get a fresh cut, color or style. Same-day appointments available. No wait. No hassle.

Use power words like "book now," "book online," "discount," or "free consultation."

> **Bonus tip:** Use **extensions** like "Call Now" or "Book Online" buttons. They increase click-through rates by 15-20%.

### 6. Set a Realistic Daily Budget

We recommend starting with **$10 to $20 per day**. That’s enough to get a feel for what works and what doesn’t. If your ads convert well (i.e., bring in clients), you can increase your budget.

> **Rule of thumb:** Don’t spend more than 10% of your monthly profit on Google Ads until you know what works.

This helps you avoid wasting money on clicks that won’t turn into bookings.

## How to Measure Success and Optimize Your Google Ads

Google Ads is all about testing and learning. Even the best campaigns need constant tweaking to stay effective in 2026.

### Key Metrics to Track

| Metric | Why It Matters |
|--------|----------------|
| Click-through rate (CTR) | Shows how attractive your ad is |
| Conversion rate | How many clicks turn into appointments |
| Cost per click (CPC) | How much you pay for each click |
| Cost per appointment | How much you pay for each client |
| Return on ad spend (ROAS) | How much revenue your ads generate |

### Optimization Strategies

Here are a few ways to improve your ads:

- **A/B test your ad copy**  -  Try 3-4 versions to see which gets the most clicks.
- **Retarget website visitors**  -  Show ads to people who’ve visited your site but didn’t book.
- **Time your ads for peak hours**  -  Most salons see the most clicks between 9 AM and 3 PM on weekdays.
- **Use Google Business Profile to boost visibility**  -  Get more exposure in local search and maps.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Google Ads worth it for small businesses?

Yes - if you target the right audience and track your results. Salons with smart Google Ads strategies see faster client acquisition and higher ROI than those relying on word-of-mouth alone.

### Is $10 a day enough for Google Ads?

$10/day is a great starting budget. It gives you enough data to test different keywords, ad copy, and targeting strategies without spending too much. Just make sure you track your results and adjust as needed.

### Is $100 enough for Google Ads?

$100 a month is usually too low to get meaningful results. Try at least $10 a day for 30 days to get enough data to optimize your campaign.

### Is $20 a day good for Google Ads?

Yes, $20 a day is a solid budget for most small local salons. This allows you to test multiple ad variations and get a clearer picture of what’s working.

### How much should a small business spend on Google Ads?

There’s no one-size-fits-all answer. Start with $10-$20 a day and increase your budget based on performance. If your cost per appointment is under $20, you’re doing well. If it’s higher, keep optimizing.

### How do I set up Google Ads for my small business?

1. Create a Google Ads account.
2. Set up your Google Business Profile.
3. Define your target audience and keywords.
4. Choose the right campaign type (usually Search Network).
5. Write compelling ad copy with a strong CTA.
6. Set a daily budget and start testing.

### Why did Google Ads charge me $500?

If you were charged unexpectedly, it’s likely due to one of these reasons:

- You accidentally increased your daily budget.
- Your ad was shown for expensive keywords.
- You targeted a broad location or keyword set.
- You didn’t set up negative keywords.

Always review your campaign settings and keyword list to avoid surprises.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Targeting 30 miles when your clients drive 3**

A salon owner in Austin, Texas came to me after spending $1,200 in six weeks with exactly two bookings to show for it. She'd set her radius to 30 miles because "more people seeing the ad means more clients, right?" Wrong. Google happily served her ads to people in Round Rock and Cedar Park who had zero interest in driving across town for a haircut. Her cost per click was $4.50, and those clicks turned into nothing.

**The fix:** I cut her radius to 5 miles and added location exclusions for the parts of Austin that are separated by the river and take 40 minutes to cross. Within two weeks, her cost per click dropped to $1.80 and her booking rate went from 0.3% to 4%.

**The outcome:** Monthly ad spend went from $800 to $450. Revenue from ads hit $2,100 in month two. She was spending less and getting four times the return.

**What to actually do:** Set your radius based on drive time, not distance. A 5-mile radius in downtown Austin is very different from 5 miles in suburban Dallas. Use Google's location extensions and check where your existing clients come from before you launch.

---

**Mistake #2: Bidding on "hair salon" and wondering why you're broke**

A barbershop in Portland, Oregon was burning $600/month on the keyword "hair salon" and getting zero bookings. They were competing with 47 other salons in their area, paying $7 per click for generic traffic that bounced within 8 seconds. The owner told me, "I thought I needed the big keywords to get noticed."

**The fix:** We switched to long-tail keywords that actually matched what clients type when they're ready to book: "men's fade haircut Portland downtown," "balayage specialist near me," "curly hair cut SE Portland." We also added negative keywords like "at home," "DIY," "products," and "jobs."

**The outcome:** Cost per click dropped to $1.60. Conversion rate went from 0.1% to 6.5%. Monthly spend stayed at $600, but revenue jumped to $3,600 because the ads were showing to people who actually wanted what they offered.

**What to actually do:** Pull your Google Search Terms report within two weeks of launching. Add negatives daily. Your goal isn't impressions — it's booking appointments with people who want your specific service at your specific price point.

---

**Mistake #3: No conversion tracking, then blaming the platform**

A salon owner in Nashville told me Google Ads "didn't work." She'd spent $1,800 over three months. I asked how many calls came in. She didn't know. How many bookings from the website? "I think a few."

**The fix:** We set up Google Tag Manager with call tracking using a unique number, installed the Google Ads conversion tracking snippet on their Booksy booking confirmation page, and set up a Google Form for walk-ins to track which ad they came from.

**The outcome:** Within a week, we saw that 60% of their conversions were phone calls — not web bookings. Their cost per lead was actually $8.50, not the $45 they assumed. Once they optimized for calls, cost per lead dropped to $5.20. Monthly ad spend of $600 generated $4,100 in booked appointments.

**What to actually do:** Spend the first week of any campaign setting up proper tracking before you launch a single ad. If you can't measure it, you can't fix it. Use a tool like CallRail or just a separate Google Voice number for your ads. Track everything.

---

**Mistake #4: Running one ad set for every service you offer**

A full-service salon in Denver had one campaign running for "hair services" that included haircuts, color, extensions, bridal, and keratin treatments in the same ad group. Their ad said "Hair Salon in Denver" and pointed everyone to their homepage. Their quality score was 3 out of 10. They were paying double what they should have.

**The fix:** We split into separate campaigns: "Color Services," "Haircuts & Styling," "Extensions," and "Bridal & Events." Each ad spoke directly to that client's problem. The color ad said "Gray coverage or balayage? We do both, starting at $85." The bridal ad said "Trial run included with your wedding booking."

**The outcome:** Quality score jumped to 8. Cost per click dropped 55%. Overall ad spend stayed at $500/month, but booked revenue hit $3,800 — up from $900 in their previous campaign.

**What to actually do:** One ad per service category. Specific landing pages for each. If you don't have separate pages, at least send them to the right section of your Booksy or Vagaro booking page.

## How to Use Google's Local Services Ads Alongside Standard Google Ads

If you're only running standard Google Ads, you're leaving money on the table. Local Services Ads (LSAs) work differently — you pay per lead instead of per click, and your ad sits right at the top of search results with a Google Guaranteed badge.

I tested this with a pet grooming client in Chicago first, then rolled it out to a salon in NYC. Here's what happened.

The salon in NYC was spending $700/month on standard search ads getting 18 leads. They added LSAs with a $400/month budget and got 22 additional leads — at $18 per lead, roughly 40% cheaper than their search ads. The Google Guaranteed badge also increased their booking rate because skeptical clients trust the green checkmark.

**Where LSAs fall short:** You can't control which keywords trigger your ad. Google decides based on your business categories. You also can't run A/B tests on ad copy. So LSAs work best as a supplement, not a replacement.

**How to set it up:** Go to Google Local Services Ads, verify your business, pass the background check, and set your budget. Keep your standard search ads running for the specific services and keywords LSAs can't target well.

**The numbers that matter:** A client in Austin saw their overall cost per lead go from $14 to $9 by adding LSAs. Their total ad spend went up 30%, but revenue increased 85%. The LSAs cannibalized some search ad leads, but the net gain was still positive.

I recommend starting with $300/month on LSAs and running it four weeks. Compare the lead quality against your search ads. If LSAs bring more bridal bookings or higher-ticket services, shift more budget there. If they bring mostly $30 haircut requests, keep them on a short leash.

## Retargeting Campaigns: Getting Back the Clients Who Clicked and Left

Here's a number that stopped a salon owner in Portland cold: 97% of people who visit your website won't book on their first visit. They're busy, they're comparing prices, or they got distracted by a text from their ex.

Standard Google Ads won't catch these people. Retargeting will.

I helped a salon in Austin set up a retargeting campaign using Google Ads' Display Network. We showed ads to anyone who visited their booking page but didn't complete an appointment. The ad said something direct: "Still thinking about it? We hold same-week availability for new clients. Book now."

**The cost:** Their retargeting campaign cost $0.35 per click. They spent $150 total and got 17 additional bookings. That's $8.80 per booking — which is absurdly cheap compared to their standard search ads at $12 per booking.

**How to do it right:** You need the Google Ads retargeting tag on your website. If you use Squarespace or Wix, you can usually paste it into the header settings. If you use WordPress, install a plugin like Insert Headers and Footers.

**What to avoid:** Don't show the same ad to everyone. Segment by page visited. Someone who looked at your bridal services gets a different ad than someone who looked at haircuts. Also, cap your frequency at 3 impressions per day per person. Otherwise, you become the creepy salon that follows people around the internet.

**The setup I use for salon clients:**
- Segment 1: Booking page visitors who didn't convert — "We saved you a spot this Thursday"
- Segment 2: Service page visitors (balayage, extensions, etc.) — "Still comparing? Here's what our $180 service includes"
- Segment 3: Home page visitors with no further clicks — "New client special: $15 off your first appointment"

Total budget for this: $200/month. Clients typically see 3-5x ROI within the first 30 days.

## Seasonal Campaigns: Capture Prom, Wedding, and Holiday Traffic Without Wasting Budget

Hair salons have natural spikes — prom season, wedding season, and the holiday rush. Most salons either ignore these completely or blast the same ad to everyone and hope for the best. Neither works well.

**Prom season (March-May in most states):**
A salon in Nashville spent $1,200 on generic prom ads in 2024 and got 12 bookings — $100 each. The problem? Their ad said "Prom Hair" and pointed to the main booking page. We restructured: a dedicated landing page with prom package pricing ($85 for up-do, $120 for up-do + trial run), photos from last year's prom clients, and a button that said "Book Your Prom Appointment."

The result the following year: $900 in ad spend, 43 bookings, and $5,700 in revenue. Cost per booking dropped from $100 to $21.

**Wedding season (peak: June-October):**
A salon in Portland started wedding ads 60 days before peak season. They targeted engaged women using in-market audiences in Google Ads — people actively searching for wedding vendors. They also used location targeting to reach brides in surrounding areas who might travel for a stylist.

Spend: $400/month for three months. Revenue: $11,000 from bridal services including trials, day-of styling, and bridal party packages. The key detail: they required a non-refundable deposit for wedding bookings, so there were zero no-shows.

**Holiday season (November-December):**
People get haircuts and color before family photos, holiday parties, and New Year's Eve. A salon in Chicago ran "Holiday Glow" ads from November 15 to December 20. They offered a small discount ($10 off color services) and required online booking.

Spend: $600 total. Revenue: $4,200 in incremental appointments. Most of those clients returned in January for regular services, so the lifetime value made the campaign even more profitable.

**How to set seasonality campaigns in Google Ads:**
Create campaigns 3-4 weeks before the season starts. Use ad scheduling to run ads during peak booking hours (lunch breaks and evenings). Include "book by [date]" urgency in the ad copy. Track everything by campaign so you know exactly which season performs best.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I tried Google Ads once and lost $2,000. Why would this be different?**
Because last time you probably set it up yourself or handed it to a junior at an agency who didn't understand local service businesses. Most failed campaigns I've fixed had three problems: no conversion tracking, too-broad targeting, and generic ad copy. Fix those three things, and you're already ahead of 90% of salon campaigns.

**Q: How much should I spend per month as a single-chair salon?**
Start at $400/month. That's enough to get meaningful data without bleeding cash. If you're in a competitive city like NYC or LA, start at $600. After 30 days, you'll know your cost per booking. Increase budget only if your cost per booking is under $25 and you have appointment availability.

**Q: Do I need a website, or can I just use my Booksy/Square link?**
You need a website. Google Ads rewards dedicated landing pages with better quality scores and lower costs. Your Booksy link works in a pinch, but you'll pay 20-30% more per click. Minimal viable setup: a one-page site with your address, services, pricing, and a booking button. Squarespace can build it in a weekend.

**Q: Will Google Ads work if I'm in a small town with 5,000 people?**
Depends on your pricing. If your average ticket is $40, you'll struggle to make the math work. If you're charging $120 for color and have a 30% repeat rate, it can work even in small towns because the lifetime value carries the campaign. Set a tight radius (5 miles), use call-only ads, and test for two months.

**Q: Should I run ads for every service or just my most profitable ones?**
Run ads for your three most profitable services only. For most salons, that's color services, extensions, and bridal. Haircuts have thin margins and low lifetime value. Nail the high-ticket services first, then add haircut ads only if you have open slots that would otherwise go empty.

**Q: How long until I see results?**
Within 7 days you should see clicks. Within 14 days you should see bookings if your setup is right. If you haven't booked a single appointment in two weeks, something is broken — targeting, ad copy, or your landing page. Don't wait. Fix it or ask someone to audit your account.
