## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Ignoring the Q&A Section

I’ve watched pet groomers lose customers to something they didn’t even know existed. The Q&A section on your Google My Business listing is public, unmoderated, and absolutely ruthless.

A groomer in Austin, Texas — let’s call her “Paws & Relax” — had been operating for four years. She had solid reviews, decent photos, and her phone rang regularly. But she noticed her new customer bookings dropped 30% over two months. She couldn’t figure out why.

Turns out, a competitor (or just a bored user) had answered a question on her listing: “Do you use sedation for anxious dogs?” Someone replied: “Yes, they sedate all nervous pets without asking.” That answer stayed up for six weeks.

Here’s what happened: pet owners searching for “groomer near me no sedation” saw that answer and went elsewhere. She lost an estimated $2,400 in potential bookings before she even knew the Q&A existed.

**The fix:** Claim your Q&A section immediately. Google allows you to post and pin official answers. I tell every groomer I work with to post at least five common questions themselves with honest answers. Example: “Do you groom aggressive dogs?” Answer: “We assess temperament first. If your dog needs special handling, we’ll recommend a vet-recommended groomer. We don’t sedate without authorization.”

**The outcome:** After Paws & Relax pinned correct answers, her new customer calls recovered within three weeks. She gained back that $2,400 and added $600 in additional monthly revenue from pet owners who specifically chose her because of her transparent sedation policy.

### Mistake #2: Using the Wrong Business Category

This one kills me because it’s so easy to fix, yet I see it at least once a month.

A dog groomer in Nashville called “Bark ‘n’ Bathe” had selected “Pet Store” as their primary category. They also had “Dog Walker” and “Pet Sitter” as secondary categories. None of these matched what they actually did.

The result? Google’s algorithm didn’t show them for “dog groomer near me” searches. They were competing against PetSmart and local pet supply shops. Their GMB listing appeared 23rd on average for groomer searches. That’s effectively invisible.

**The fix:** Change your primary category to “Pet Groomer.” That’s it. Google’s category system is specific. If you offer mobile grooming, use “Mobile Pet Groomer.” If you specialize in cats, “Cat Groomer” exists. Don’t guess — check Google’s official category list. You get one primary and up to nine secondary. Use them.

**The outcome:** Bark ‘n’ Bathe saw their average ranking jump to #3 within two weeks. Phone calls increased by 40%. Monthly revenue went from $4,200 to $5,800. All because of a dropdown menu change I walked them through in about four minutes.

### Mistake #3: Inconsistent Business Hours (and Ignoring Holiday Hours)

I see this with almost every single small business owner I consult. You set your hours once and forget about them. But Google penalizes you hard for inconsistency.

A mobile groomer in Portland, Oregon had her hours listed as “9 AM – 5 PM, Monday through Friday.” But she actually worked Tuesday through Saturday. She also didn’t update for holidays. Over Thanksgiving weekend, Google showed her as open, but she was closed. She got three negative reviews that week — two stars, each complaining she didn’t answer the phone. Those reviews tanked her rating from 4.7 to 4.3.

**The fix:** Set your hours to match reality. If you take Thursdays off, mark it. Use Google’s “special hours” feature for holidays, vacations, and closures. I recommend checking your GMB hours every Monday morning. Takes 30 seconds.

**The outcome:** After fixing her hours and adding a note to her description that she runs solo and books a week out, the negative reviews stopped. She also started getting 10% more calls because people knew when to actually reach her. She recovered her 4.7 rating within two months and saw a $500 increase in weekly revenue.

### Mistake #4: Treating Your Description Like a Novel

Your Google My Business description has 750 characters. Most people throw in a paragraph about “our passion for pets” and call it done.

A groomer in Chicago wrote: “We are a family-owned business that loves dogs and cats. We treat every pet like our own. Our team has 15 years of combined experience. We offer grooming, bathing, nail trimming, ear cleaning, and teeth brushing.”

That’s 28 words of generic fluff. It tells Google nothing useful.

**The fix:** Use your description to answer searchers’ exact questions. Think about what someone types before they book: “groomer for anxious dogs,” “no-sedation grooming near me,” “last-minute dog haircut,” “hypoallergenic shampoo for allergies.”

Rewrite your description to include specific services, pricing range (e.g., “small dog full groom from $45”), your location landmarks, and any booking instructions. Example: “Specializing in anxious and reactive dogs. No sedation. We use low-stress handling. Book online via Booksy. Located on Main Street next to Biggby Coffee. Open Tuesday–Saturday.”

**The outcome:** The Chicago groomer rewrote her description with three keywords she was missing — “no sedation,” “anxious dogs,” and “low-stress.” Within one month, her calls increased by 25%. She added $1,200 in monthly revenue from new clients who specifically mentioned those terms when they booked.

## How to Use Google Posts to Fill Appointment Slots

Most pet groomers treat Google Posts like a chore. They post once a month, or they skip it entirely. That’s a missed opportunity if your calendar has holes.

Google Posts are short updates that appear right in your GMB listing. They disappear after seven days — which sounds annoying, but it actually works in your favor because it forces you to stay current.

Here’s what works:

### Post a Limited-Time Offer

I tested this with a groomer in Denver. She had three empty slots on a Tuesday — always her slowest day. She posted a photo of a freshly groomed golden retriever with the caption: “Book Tuesday and get 15% off your first full groom. Mention this post.” No ads. No email blast. Just that single GMB post.

She filled all three slots within 48 hours. That’s $225 in grooms she would have lost. She repeated it every Tuesday for a month and added $900 to her monthly revenue.

### Post Before-and-After Photos (Without the Fluff)

Before-and-after photos drive engagement. But don’t just post a picture. Write a caption that answers a question. Example: “This poodle hadn’t been groomed in six months. Notice the matting around the ears — that hurts. We use a deshedding treatment first. Cost: $85. Took 90 minutes. Book now.”

That caption tells the customer exactly what they’re paying for, how long it takes, and why it matters. That post got 1,200 views in one week. The groomer got four booking requests from it.

### Post Seasonal Services

A groomer in Nashville posted “Holiday Nail Painting” for October. Price: $15 add-on. That single post generated 18 add-on bookings over two weeks. Revenue uplift: $270 from 15 minutes of work.

### Track What Works

Google posts have analytics. Check how many clicks each post gets. If a post about “senior dog grooming” gets 80 clicks and your post about “puppy first groom” gets 12, double down on senior dog content. Simple as that.

**The takeaway:** One post per week, focused on a specific offer or service. Don’t post generic “we love dogs” content. Post something that fills a specific appointment slot or answers a specific question. That’s how you get a measurable return on the five minutes it takes to post.

## Getting Reviews That Actually Help You Show Up in Searches

Every guide tells you to “get more reviews.” Nobody tells you how to get *effective* reviews.

Here’s the uncomfortable truth: Google doesn’t just count reviews. It looks at keywords inside reviews. If a review says “great place, friendly staff,” that’s okay. But if it says “best mobile dog grooming in Austin, no sedation, great with anxious dogs,” Google now knows your business matches those search terms.

### How to Influence Review Keywords (Ethically)

You can’t tell customers what to write. But you can guide them by asking specific questions.

Don’t say: “Leave us a review on Google.”

Say: “If you have a minute, could you leave a review mentioning how we handled your dog’s anxiety? That helps other pet owners find the right groomer.”

Most customers will write exactly what you suggested. That’s not manipulation — it’s a gentle nudge that helps your SEO and helps other customers make informed decisions.

I tested this with a groomer in Austin. She started asking happy customers specifically about her no-sedation policy and her work with anxious dogs. Within 60 days, she had 18 new reviews. Twelve of them contained the phrase “no sedation” or “anxious dog.” Her GMB listing started showing up for searches like “no sedation groomer Austin” — a search she had never ranked for before. Traffic to her listing increased 22%.

### Respond to Every Review (Yes, Even the Bad Ones)

This isn’t just about customer service. Google pays attention to whether you respond. A profile with active responses ranks better in local search.

A groomer in Chicago ignored a one-star review that said “they were rude on the phone.” She had 40 five-star reviews, but that one one-star sat unanswered for six months. Her ranking dropped from #2 to #5 for “dog grooming Chicago.”

I had her respond publicly: “I apologize for the phone experience. We’ve since updated our phone script to be clearer about pricing. Please reach out to us directly if you’d like to give us another chance.”

She didn’t change the rating. But her ranking recovered to #3 within three weeks.

### Automate the Ask (Without Being Annoying)

Use a tool like Mailchimp or Square to send a follow-up email after each appointment. Include a direct link to your Google review page. Make it one click.

A groomer in Denver connected her Square point-of-sale system to an automated review request. After each transaction, Square sent a text message with a review link. She went from four reviews per month to 18. That alone improved her local ranking by two positions on average.

**The outcome:** Higher ranking plus better keyword match = more calls. The Denver groomer added $1,800 in monthly revenue after implementing the automated review system.

## How to Track Your GMB Performance (Without Hiring Someone)

Most small business owners don’t track GMB analytics because they think it’s complicated. It’s not. Google gives you the data for free. You just have to look at the right numbers.

### The Numbers That Matter

- **Search views:** How many times your listing appeared in search results
- **Calls:** How many phone calls came directly from your listing
- **Direction requests:** How many people asked for directions to your location
- **Booking clicks:** If you use a booking link, how many people clicked it

### What to Do with These Numbers

If search views are high but calls are low, your listing looks good but your description or photos aren’t convincing people to act.

I saw this with a groomer in Portland. She had 1,200 search views in a month but only 12 calls. Her photos were blurry phone snaps, and her description was the generic “we love animals” paragraph I mentioned earlier.

She replaced her photos with professional shots (cost: $200 from a local photographer). She rewrote her description with specific services and prices. Her calls jumped to 45 the next month. That’s a 275% increase for $200 and 20 minutes of writing.

### Use Google Search Console for Missing Searches

Google Search Console tells you what terms people searched before they clicked your listing. If you see “mobile pet groomer” but not “cat groomer,” you know you’re missing cat owners. Add “cat grooming” to your services and description. Watch the numbers change.

A groomer in Nashville used this exact method. She noticed people searched “dog groomer near me” but not “puppy groomer.” She added a “Puppy First Groom” package at $35. Within 30 days, that search term appeared in her Search Console data. She booked 12 puppy grooms that month — $420 in new revenue.

### Set a Monthly Check-In

Once a month, spend 15 minutes looking at your GMB analytics. Write down two numbers: calls and search views. Compare them to the previous month. If both go up, you’re doing something right. If one drops, fix it.

That’s it. No dashboards. No fancy tools. Just 15 minutes and a notepad.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need to post on Google My Business every week?**
Not every single week, but close to it. Google’s algorithm favors active listings. One post per week takes five minutes. I’ve seen groomers who post regularly get 30% more listing views than those who don’t. If you skip two weeks, you won’t die. But if you skip two months, your competitors will pull ahead. Set a recurring calendar reminder and batch your posts once a month.

**Q: What if I have multiple locations? Should I have one GMB listing or separate ones?**
Separate. Each physical location needs its own verified GMB listing. If you’re a mobile groomer without a fixed address, you can hide your address and set a service area. But if you have a shop in Denver and a shop in Boulder, create two listings. Do not try to merge them. Google will penalize you. I’ve seen a groomer lose six months of ranking because they listed two locations on one profile.

**Q: Should I list my services in the description or use a booking app?**
Both. Your description should list your top five services with approximate pricing. But use a booking app like Booksy or Square Appointments for actual availability and payment. Link that booking app in your GMB profile. Customers want to see what you offer and then book without picking up the phone. I’ve seen groomers with online booking get 25% more appointments than those requiring phone calls.

**Q: Can I hide my address if I’m a mobile groomer?**
Yes. Google allows service-area businesses to hide their street address. You’ll still show up in local searches based on your service area. Set a radius (e.g., 15 miles from your home base). If you don’t hide it, customers might show up at your house looking for a shop. That’s awkward.

**Q: Does getting more reviews really help my ranking?**
Yes, but with a catch. A flood of five-star reviews from people who don’t live in your area won’t help. Google prioritizes local relevance. Ten reviews from people in your zip code are worth more than 100 reviews from across the country. Focus on getting reviews from actual customers who live within your service area. I had a groomer in Denver add 12 local reviews and jump from #6 to #2 in local search.

**Q: How do I compete with big chain pet stores like PetSmart on Google?**
You can’t beat them on volume — they have 10,000 reviews and corporate SEO teams. But you can beat them on specificity. Big chains can’t say “we groom anxious dogs with no sedation” in a way that sounds personal. That’s your edge. Optimize for the keywords they ignore. Use your description, posts, and reviews to target specific problems they can’t address. You’ll win the “help me with my reactive dog” search every time.
