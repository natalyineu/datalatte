**What are Google Local Service Ads?**

Google Local Service Ads (LSAs) are a type of paid advertising on Google that helps customers find and connect with local service businesses like yours. With LSAs, you can increase your online visibility, drive more leads, and grow your business. In this guide, we'll walk you through the setup process for Google Local Service Ads.

**Step 1: Check Eligibility and Create a Google Account**

To run Local Service Ads, your business must be eligible and have a Google account. Check the Google Local Service Ads eligibility requirements and create a Google account if you don't already have one.

**Step 2: Set Up Your Google Local Service Ads Account**

Once you've checked your eligibility and created a Google account, follow these steps to set up your Local Service Ads account:

1. Sign in to your Google account and go to the Google Local Service Ads website.
2. Click on "Get started" and follow the prompts to set up your account.
3. Enter your business information, including your name, address, and phone number.
4. Set up your payment method and budget for your Local Service Ads campaign.

**Step 3: Choose Your Service Areas and Service Categories**

Next, you'll need to choose the areas where you want to offer your services and the categories of services you provide. This will help you target the right customers and ensure that your ads are shown in the right locations.

**Step 4: Set Up Your Ad Creative**

Now it's time to create your Local Service Ads ad creative. This includes writing a compelling headline, description, and call-to-action that will entice customers to click on your ad.

**Step 5: Set Up Your Budget and Bidding**

Determine your budget and set up your bidding strategy to control how much you spend on your Local Service Ads campaign.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Setting Up Your Service Area Wrong — And Wasting $1,200 in Two Weeks**

Here’s what actually happened. A locksmith in Austin, Texas — let’s call him Mark — came to me after running LSAs for three weeks. He’d spent $1,200. He’d gotten zero booked jobs. Zero. His phone rang maybe four times, and two of those calls were people asking if he serviced Round Rock (he did). The other two were spam.

The problem? He’d set his service area as a 50-mile radius around his shop. Google throttled his ads because they couldn’t confirm he could actually service that whole area efficiently. His average response time to leads was 45 minutes. By then, the customer had called someone else.

The fix was brutal but simple: I shrunk his service area to 15 miles. That’s all of Austin proper plus the immediate suburbs. Then I made sure he answered every lead within 5 minutes — not 45. We also cleaned up his business hours in his Google Business Profile to match when he actually answers the phone (not when he’s on a roof fixing a lock).

Outcome: He spent $800 the next month. He got 14 leads. He booked nine jobs. Revenue from those jobs: $3,800. His cost per lead dropped from something embarrassing to $57.

Set your service area to where you can *actually* show up within 45 minutes. Google tracks this. If you claim you service 50 miles and take two hours to get there, the algorithm penalizes you.

**Mistake #2: Ignoring the Review Requirement — Then Wondering Why You’re Invisible**

A pet groomer in Nashville — Chelsea, runs a shop called Paws & Polish — called me frustrated. She’d set up LSAs perfectly, or so she thought. Her profile was complete. Her pricing was listed. She had photos. After two weeks, she’d gotten exactly one lead. One.

She had 12 Google reviews. Her competitors had 40, 60, 100. LSAs rank businesses partly on review quantity and recency. Google wants to show customers businesses that have recent, frequent feedback. Chelsea’s last review was from eight months ago.

We spent a month getting her from 12 reviews to 38. The tactic wasn’t complicated: She put a small card in every pickup bag that said, “If you loved our service, a 30-second review helps us keep our prices low.” She also sent a follow-up email (via Mailchimp) to every customer after their appointment. The email included a direct link to her Google review page — not a QR code, just a clickable button.

Outcome: Within 60 days, her LSAs started showing in the top 3 positions for “dog grooming Nashville.” Her leads went from 4 per month to 18 per month. Her cost per lead dropped from $45 to $22 because her ad rank improved.

You need at least 10 reviews to even be considered for LSAs. Aim for 25+ within 90 days of starting. And keep getting new ones. Google favors businesses with reviews less than 30 days old.

**Mistake #3: Treating LSAs Like Set-It-and-Forget-It — Losing $900 a Month**

A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon — Jasmine, runs a mid-sized shop with five stylists — set up her LSAs in May 2024. She set a weekly budget of $250. For the first month, it worked. She got 20 leads, booked 12 appointments. Solid.

Then June came. Portland had two weeks of perfect weather. Nobody wanted to sit inside getting highlights when the sun was out. Her response rate dropped because her staff was outside on break. Her lead volume fell by half. Google started showing her ads less frequently because the conversion rate dropped.

Jasmine didn’t touch the account for six weeks. She spent $900 in that time with almost nothing to show for it — maybe three actual bookings.

The fix: We turned off LSAs entirely during slow periods. I know that sounds counterintuitive, but here’s the math: If you run ads when nobody’s booking, you’re paying for leads that don’t convert. Those leads hurt your quality score. Then when business picks up, your ad rank is lower, and you pay more per lead.

Instead, we turned LSAs back on the first week of September. Portland weather gets gray again. People want haircuts. She spent $400 that month and got 15 leads, 11 bookings, $2,700 in revenue.

Monitor your LSAs weekly. If you’re not booking jobs, pause the ads. Don’t wait. Two weeks of bad performance can drag down your account for months.

## How to Budget for LSAs Without Burning Cash

Most guides skip this part: how much should you actually spend? I’ve seen the right answer vary by industry and city. Here’s how to figure it out.

Start with your average job value. If you’re a plumber in Chicago and your average service call is $250, you can afford to pay more per lead than a dog walker in Denver whose average visit is $35.

The formula is simple: Maximum cost per lead = (average job value × 0.3). You pay up to 30% of your job value to acquire that customer. If your average job is $250, you can spend $75 per lead. If it’s $35, you can spend $10.50.

Now set your weekly budget. Multiply your ideal cost per lead by 10. That’s a safe weekly budget to start. $75 per lead × 10 = $750/week. That’s $3,000/month. For the dog walker: $10.50 × 10 = $105/week. $420/month.

I tested this with a fitness studio owner in Denver. Her average client paid $120/month for unlimited classes. She wanted LSAs to bring in warm leads. We set her cost per lead target at $36 (30% of $120). Weekly budget: $360. First month spend: $1,440. Leads generated: 42. Booked free trials: 28. Converted to paying: 12. Revenue from those 12: $1,440/month in recurring revenue.

She hit ROI in month one. That’s unusual. Most businesses take 2-3 months.

If your numbers don’t work at 30%, you have two options. Raise your prices or lower your expectations. Do not lower your ad budget below the point where you get consistent leads. Five leads at $50 each is better than two leads at $20 each. Volume matters for learning data.

## Integrating LSAs With Your Other Tools

LSAs don’t live in a vacuum. They feed into whatever booking system you use. And if your booking system is a mess, your LSAs will fail.

I worked with an HVAC company in Phoenix. They spent $2,500/month on LSAs. Leads came in. But their booking process was: call the office, leave a voicemail, wait for a call back. Average callback time: 4 hours. Their lead-to-booking rate was 8%. That’s terrible.

We connected their LSAs to Booksy. When a lead came through, the system automatically texted the customer with available appointment slots. They could book online within 30 seconds. Lead-to-booking rate jumped to 31%. Same ad spend. More jobs.

Here are the specific tools I see work best:

- **Square Appointments**: If you’re a salon, spa, or fitness studio. Integrates directly with Google. Customer books the appointment from the ad. Done.
- **Booksy**: Better for barbers, pet groomers, estheticians. Has built-in waitlist and text reminders, which reduces no-shows by about 20%.
- **Housecall Pro**: For plumbers, electricians, HVAC. Handles dispatch and routing. If you’re not using this and you’re in trades, you’re losing money on gas and time.
- **Mailchimp or Constant Contact**: After you book a job, send a follow-up email asking for a review. Automate this. I’ve seen review volume increase 3x in 60 days with a simple “How was your visit?” email sequence.

The key integration most people miss: your phone number. If your LSAs send calls to a personal cell phone and you screen calls, you’re losing leads. Get a business line through Google Voice or Dialpad. Answer every call within 2 minutes during business hours. Google tracks your answer rate. Fall below 80% and your ads drop in rank.

I had a client in NYC — a house cleaning service — who was answering calls 65% of the time. I made them install a dedicated app on the owner’s phone and set up auto-forwarding to a backup number if the first call wasn’t answered in 30 seconds. Answer rate went to 94%. Lead volume increased 40% within two weeks.

## How to Handle LSAs When You’re Fully Booked

The uncomfortable truth is: If you’re too successful with LSAs, you might not have room for the new leads. Business owners tell me this all the time. “Nataliia, my schedule is full for the next two weeks. Why would I keep running ads?”

You shouldn’t. Turn them off.

But here’s the nuance: Don’t turn them off forever. Pause for a week. Or two. Or three. When your schedule opens up, turn them back on. The algorithm doesn’t punish you for pausing. It only punishes you for not answering leads or for low conversion rates.

A massage therapist in San Francisco ran LSAs for three months. She got so busy she had to pause. She paused for six weeks. When she turned LSAs back on, her ad rank was exactly where it was before. No penalty. No ramp-up period.

The reverse is also true: If you keep running ads when you’re full, you get leads you can’t serve. You either ignore them (bad for your account quality), refer them out (bad for your business), or try to squeeze them in (bad for your existing clients). None of those are good.

The only time I’d say keep running ads when you’re full is if you’re planning to hire. Maybe you have a new employee starting next month. In that case, run ads at a reduced budget (50-60% of normal) to build a waitlist. Then when the new person starts, you have customers ready.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I run LSAs for a business that operates from my home?**

Yes. Google doesn’t require a physical storefront. You just need to verify your business address. I’ve worked with handymen, cleaners, mobile pet groomers, and painters who all operate from home addresses. Google will verify your business location with a postcard or phone call. The customer sees your service area, not your home address.

**Q: What happens if I get a lead but the customer is outside my service area?**

You can decline it. Google will ask why. Select “Outside service area.” This won’t hurt your account if you don’t do it constantly. If it happens more than 10% of the time, shrink your service area. That lead you declined still cost you money — you pay for the phone call or message regardless.

**Q: Can I run LSAs and regular Google Ads at the same time?**

Yes. But LSAs show above regular Google Ads in the search results. If LSAs are working, you might not need regular ads for your local terms. I usually recommend starting with LSAs only for 60 days. Then add standard Search Ads for broader keywords or competitors’ names. The two don’t conflict.

**Q: How long does it take to get approved?**

Plan for 7 to 14 days. Google verifies your business license, insurance, and background check (for certain industries). If you have all your paperwork ready, it can be as fast as 3 days. If you’re missing something, expect delays. I’ve had one client wait six weeks because their insurance certificate had the wrong policy number. Double-check everything before you submit.

**Q: Is there a minimum budget?**

Yes. Google requires a minimum weekly budget of $100 in most markets. In competitive cities like Los Angeles or New York, the minimum can be $150 per week. If you can’t afford at least $400 per month, LSAs might not be right for you. Your budget determines how often your ads show. At $100 per week, you might get 5-10 leads. That’s enough to test.

**Q: Can I target only certain neighborhoods within a city?**

Yes. You can draw your service area as a shape on the map. I do this all the time. A client in Denver might want to target Highlands and RiNo but skip Cherry Creek and downtown. You can exclude specific zip codes. This is useful if your business is in one part of town and you only service certain areas.

**Q: What if I get a bad review from an LSA lead?**

You can respond publicly. Don’t argue. Apologize briefly, explain what went wrong, and take the conversation offline. If the review violates Google’s policies (irrelevant, fake, contains profanity), you can flag it for removal. I’ve successfully gotten fake reviews removed by reporting them through Google’s support portal. It took three days.
