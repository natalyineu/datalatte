### Audit What You’re Already Doing  
Start by spending 15 minutes each week reviewing your current efforts. Log into Google Business Profile to check for incomplete info—missing hours or photos? Send your website to a friend: does it load in 3 seconds? Compare your competitor in the same city (say, the coffee shop next door). Are they getting 3x reviews? This isn’t busy work—it’s your baseline.

<Callout type="tip">Use a free tool like Ubersuggest to see what keywords your site is already ranking for. Even if the list looks messy, it’s your starting point.</Callout>

Take a solo barbershop in Toronto as an example. Their website was missing key info like “haircut $35” in the URL. Fixing that increased walk-ins by 20% in three weeks.

### Focus on High-Impact Local SEO  
Spend 20 minutes weekly on three things:  
- **Claim or update your Google Business Profile** (add new photos, respond to reviews)  
- **Fix NAP (name, address, phone) inconsistencies** across directories  
- **Post 1–2 weekly updates** (e.g., “New pet groomer in our team! Book online”)

### Create a Weekly Paid Ads Routine  
Yes, even on a $50 budget. Spend 15 minutes every Friday setting up a Google Ads campaign. Start with search ads using keywords like “pet groomer near me” or “yoga for beginners [city name].” BID ONLY $1–$2 per click. Use the “Schedule” feature to run ads during your peak hours (9 AM–3 PM on weekdays).  

<Callout type="coffee">I’ve seen a coffee shop in Austin double its lunchtime orders with this setup. They tested “Buy 1 get 1” vs “$2 off” and picked the winner.</Callout>

Track conversions (even if it’s just phone calls) and pause underperforming ads by Monday. This isn’t about spending more—it’s about testing smart.

### Leverage Marketing Automation  
Dedicate 10 minutes to automate customer retention. Use tools like Mailchimp or Canva to create:  
- A welcome email for new subscribers (offer a 10% off coupon for their first visit)  
- A 3-month birthday campaign (anniversary discount + referral bonus)  
- A “You missed us!” email for inactive clients  

A fitness studio in Sydney automated this and saw a 35% drop in monthly churn. The key: keep messages simple and tied to real actions, like “Your next class is full—book now.”

<Callout type="example">A dog walker in Boston automated “Weather Alert” texts: “Snowy tomorrow? I’ve got indoor options.” Result? 40% fewer cancellations.</Callout>

### Track and Adjust Weekly  
Use 10 minutes every Sunday to measure:  
- Did last week’s Google update increase your call button clicks?  
- Are your automated emails getting 15% opens? (If not, A/B test the subject line.)  
- Is your ad spend generating 1+ new customer per week?  

If not, cut it. Small business marketing isn’t about “doing more.” It’s about finding the 1–2 things that work and doubling down.

### Frequently Asked Questions  
**How do I start if I have no budget?**  
Focus on free local SEO: update your Google Profile, add high-quality photos, and ask for 3–5 reviews weekly.

**Should I use Facebook Ads or Google Ads?**  
Google is better for local service businesses. Facebook works for low-cost, high-frequency services like nail salons. Test both with small budgets.

**Can I do this without a website?**  
No. Even a 3-page site (Home, Services, Contact) is worth the time. Use Wix or Squarespace—build it in 2 hours and revisit weekly.

**How do I ask for reviews without sounding desperate?**  
Say: “If you loved your haircut, a 5-star review would help others find us!” Add a QR code to your receipt.

**What if my time is even tighter than an hour?**  
Focus on 1 priority: either local SEO updates or paid ads. Do it all in 30 minutes and skip the rest.

**How long before I see results?**  
Local SEO takes 3–6 months. Paid ads work in days but require constant tweaking. Automation shows results in 2–4 weeks.

**Are tools like Google Business Profile enough?**  
No. They’re a starting point. You need to optimize them weekly and fix issues like broken links, outdated info, or low ratings.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much time should I spend on marketing each week?

You should spend at least 60 minutes a week on marketing to see growth in customers. This can be broken down into smaller tasks such as 15 minutes reviewing current efforts and 45 minutes on other marketing activities. Consistency is key, so try to dedicate the same 60 minutes each week.

### What is the return on investment for local SEO?

For every dollar spent on local SEO, you can expect a return of around $12. This is because local SEO helps your business appear in search results for people in your area, driving more foot traffic and sales. By optimizing your Google Business Profile and website, you can increase your chances of appearing in local search results.

### How can I automate my marketing tasks?

Around 45% of salons use automation to streamline their marketing tasks, and you can do the same. Start by identifying tasks that take up the most time, such as social media posting or email marketing, and look for tools that can automate these tasks. By automating just a few tasks, you can free up more time to focus on other areas of your business.

### What are the most important metrics to track in my marketing plan?

You should track metrics such as website load time, review count, and ad spend. Aim for a website load time of under 3 seconds, and try to increase your review count by at least 10% each quarter. By tracking these metrics, you can identify areas for improvement and make data-driven decisions about your marketing strategy.

### How often should I review and update my marketing plan?

You should review and update your marketing plan at least every 6-12 months, or whenever you notice a significant change in your business or target market. Take 15 minutes each week to review your current efforts and make adjustments as needed. By regularly reviewing and updating your plan, you can stay on track and achieve your marketing goals.
