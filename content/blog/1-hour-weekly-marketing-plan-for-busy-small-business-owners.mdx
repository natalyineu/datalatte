### Step 1: Review Your Current Marketing Efforts (10 minutes)

Before we dive into the 1-hour weekly marketing plan, it's essential to take a step back and review your current marketing efforts. Ask yourself:

* What are my business goals for the next 3-6 months?
* What marketing channels am I currently using (social media, email, Google Ads, etc.)?
* Which channels are performing well, and which ones need improvement?

Take 10 minutes to review your current marketing efforts, and make a list of areas that need attention.

### Step 2: Set Your Marketing Goals (10 minutes)

With your current marketing efforts reviewed, it's time to set your marketing goals. Ask yourself:

* What specific actions do I want my customers to take (make a purchase, sign up for a newsletter, etc.)?
* What metrics will I use to measure the success of my marketing efforts (website traffic, social media engagement, sales, etc.)?

Set specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals for your marketing efforts.

### Step 3: Choose Your Marketing Channels (20 minutes)

Now that you have your marketing goals in mind, it's time to choose the channels that will help you achieve them. Consider the following options:

* Social media (Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, etc.)
* Email marketing
* Google Ads (Google Search, Google Display, etc.)
* Local SEO (search engine optimization for your business's location)
* Referral marketing (encouraging customers to refer their friends and family)

Take 20 minutes to research and choose the marketing channels that best fit your business goals and budget.

### Step 4: Create a Content Calendar (20 minutes)

A content calendar is a schedule of content that will be published on your chosen marketing channels. Take 20 minutes to create a content calendar that includes:

* The type of content (blog post, social media post, email newsletter, etc.)
* The date and time it will be published
* The target audience for the content
* The metrics you'll use to measure its success

### Step 5: Implement Your Marketing Plan (30 minutes)

With your content calendar in place, it's time to start implementing your marketing plan. Take 30 minutes to:

* Create and schedule your content
* Share your content on your chosen marketing channels
* Monitor and adjust your marketing efforts as needed

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Trying to Be Everywhere at Once

**The story:** A bakery in Portland, Oregon called Tabor Bread. The owner, a former pastry chef, was convinced she needed to be on Instagram, TikTok, Facebook, Pinterest, and Yelp simultaneously. She spent about four hours a week creating content for every platform — photographing croissants for Pinterest, filming sourdough timelapses for TikTok, posting daily specials to Instagram Stories. After six months, she had 12,000 Instagram followers, 47 TikTok videos with an average of 89 views, and exactly zero measurable new customers from Pinterest. Her total weekly marketing time: 4–5 hours. Her total new customer inquiries from all that effort: roughly 2 per week. She was exhausted, resentful of marketing, and ready to give up.

**What went wrong:** She spread her time across too many platforms, diluting quality and impact. She had no tracking system to know which channel actually drove in-store visits. She was doing the work because she felt she *should*, not because the data pointed her there.

**The fix:** I had her audit her customer data from Square — the bakery's POS system. Over 90% of new customers in the previous three months found Tabor Bread through either Google Search or Yelp. Not Instagram. Not Pinterest. Not TikTok. We cut everything except Google Business Profile optimization and Yelp review management. She spent that 4–5 hours instead on one thing: responding to every Yelp review (good and bad) within 24 hours, and posting one photo per week of whatever was selling best that week on Google Business Profile.

**The outcome:** In eight weeks, her Yelp rating went from 3.9 to 4.5 stars. Google Business Profile impressions increased 140%. Foot traffic attributed to those two channels rose by 22%. She went from spending 4+ hours to 45 minutes per week. Revenue increased by $1,200 per month in that period alone. She told me she felt like an idiot for all that "hustle content" she'd been making. I told her most small business owners make the same mistake. It's not about working harder on marketing. It's about working on the channels where customers actually find you.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Google Business Profile (Or Treating It Like a Listing)

**The story:** A dental practice in Austin, Texas — Westlake Family Dentistry. The owner, Dr. Patel, had a Google Business Profile because someone told him it was "important." He'd checked the box and forgotten about it. His profile had a generic photo of a dental chair from 2019. His hours hadn't been updated since COVID. He had 17 unanswered Google reviews — three of them complaints. Meanwhile, a competing practice two blocks away had a fully maintained profile with 85 reviews, answered questions, and a photo of the staff holding a charity check. Dr. Patel was spending $2,500 per month on Google Ads to "compete." He thought his ad spend was the problem, so he'd doubled down on it.

**What went wrong:** He was paying for visibility on Google while his free listing was actively repelling customers. Google prioritizes profiles with recent activity, photos, and answered reviews. His profile was a ghost town. Worse — unanswered negative reviews signal to Google that the business doesn't engage with customers. His ad campaign was fighting a massive headwind from his own neglected listing.

**The fix:** I told him to pause the Google Ads entirely for two weeks. Take the $1,250 he would have spent and put it toward something boring but effective: a proper Google Business Profile cleanup. We updated all photos. We set a schedule to post one photo per week — real photos of his team, before-and-after shots (with permission), a shot of their coffee station in the waiting room. We responded to every review — even the angry ones. "We're sorry you had a long wait. Here's what we've changed to prevent that." He was horrified at first. "You want me to respond to the 1-star reviews publicly?" Yes. Especially those.

**The outcome:** In 30 days his profile impressions tripled. His average rating climbed from 3.6 to 4.2. When he turned the ads back on — at $1,500 per month instead of $2,500 — his cost per click dropped 35% because his profile quality score improved. He booked an additional $3,800 in new patient revenue in that first month. Total time investment: about 20 minutes per week. The fix had nothing to do with ads and everything to do with a free piece of real estate most businesses neglect.

### Mistake 3: Treating Email Like a Newsletters (It's Not 2015)

**The story:** A boutique fitness studio in Denver called Elevate Cycle. The owner, Jenna, had been collecting emails at sign-up for three years — over 1,200 addresses. She sent a weekly newsletter: class schedule, a motivational quote, a link to book. Open rate: 11%. Click rate: 0.8%. She was proud she was "doing email marketing" but she couldn't tell you a single dime it had ever earned. She'd been doing it for two years and had zero idea if it worked.

**What went wrong:** She was treating email like a broadcast channel, not a revenue channel. No segmentation. No offers. No urgency. She was sending the same thing to everyone — people who'd been coming for three years and people who'd signed up once at a pop-up class in 2021. Worse, she wasn't tracking which emails converted into bookings. The cost wasn't money; it was the opportunity cost of 1,200 warm leads she was doing nothing with.

**The fix:** We split the list into three segments: active (booked in last 60 days), lapsed (booked 60+ days ago), and cold (never booked or single class only). She'd never done this before because Mailchimp makes it look complicated. It's not. Then we set up two automated funnels. For lapsed members: "We miss you. Here's a free class on us — no strings attached." For cold leads: a three-email sequence over two weeks — a short intro from Jenna, a testimonial video from a current member, then an offer for a $49 unlimited first month (normally $199). The weekly newsletter became a single email on Sunday: one specific offer, one link, no fluff.

**The outcome:** The lapsed member email brought back 47 people in 30 days. The cold sequence converted 12 new members. Total revenue from those two automations: $14,700. Her open rate on the Sunday offer emails hit 38%. Total weekly time: still under 30 minutes, most of which was picking the offer. She told me she felt stupid for two years of "we love our community" emails. I told her the fix was a Saturday morning of setup work. Most people won't do it because it feels like work. She did. It paid off.

### Mistake 4: Running Ads Without Conversion Tracking

**The story:** A hair salon in Chicago — The Parlour on Halsted. The owner, Marcus, was spending $800 per month on Instagram ads. He was "getting leads" — he had a spreadsheet of 40+ inquiries per month. But when I asked how many of those turned into appointments, he had no idea. "I think most of them?" he said. We pulled his Booksy booking data and cross-referenced it with his ad leads. Of those 40+ "leads" per month, exactly 6 turned into bookings. His cost per booked client: $133. He was spending $9,600 per year to get 72 new clients, most of whom would come once and never return. He was losing money on every single ad spend.

**What went wrong:** He was measuring vanity metrics (DM replies, link clicks) instead of actual bookings. His ads were going to a landing page that required three clicks to book. He had no retargeting for people who visited the booking page and left. He was running one single ad set for all service types — a $300 balayage ad reaching people who clicked because the photo was pretty but would never pay $300 for a haircut.

**The fix:** We installed the Meta pixel with a dedicated Booksy conversion event. We split his ad budget into two campaigns: one for high-ticket services (color, extensions) at $600/month, one for new-client offer (first haircut $25 off) at $200/month. We created a one-click booking link and made it the only button in the ad. Then we added a 3-day retargeting campaign for anyone who clicked but didn't book.

**The outcome:** In 60 days, his cost per booked client dropped from $133 to $37. Monthly bookings from ads went from 6 to 22. Revenue from those bookings: $5,800 per month (up from $1,200). He spent the same $800 per month. The difference was tracking and targeting. He later told me he'd been considering a bigger ad budget because he thought more leads = more business. He was wrong. More tracked, specific, retargeted leads = more business. Everything else is a tax on optimism.

## The Tool Stack That Actually Works for a 1-Hour Week

I've seen small business owners spend hours per week fighting with tools that should save time but don't. Here's the stack I recommend for someone with one hour per week. I've tested these across clients in 12 US cities. They work.

**Google Business Profile (free, non-negotiable):** This is your single highest-ROI marketing channel if you're a local business. A 2023 study from BrightLocal showed 87% of consumers read Google reviews before visiting a business, and profiles with regular photo updates get 42% more requests for directions. I want you to spend 10 minutes per week on this. Post one photo — a new menu item, a team shot, a space improvement shot. Respond to any new reviews. That's it. I've seen a single photo post drive a 15% increase in calls for a plumbing company in Nashville. Your photos are free ads.

**One social platform (pick one, abandon the rest):** If your customers are 25–45, choose Instagram. If they're 35–55, choose Facebook. If you're a service business where customers book appointments (salons, dentists, vets), add Yelp but treat it as a review management platform, not a content platform. Spend no more than 15 minutes per week. Schedule using Meta Business Suite (free, built into Facebook/Instagram) or Later (free tier works for one account). Post one short video or photo. No carousels. No Reels that took you 40 minutes to edit. One post. Done.

**Email via Mailchimp's free tier:** If you have fewer than 500 contacts, Mailchimp is free. If you have more, switch to Brevo (formerly Sendinblue) which is cheaper. Set up one automated sequence — a welcome email for new subscribers that includes your best offer. Then send one weekly email: one offer, one link, no more than 100 words. Use Square or Booksy to see who actually booked. If your email didn't drive bookings, change the offer next week. That's your feedback loop.

**Square or Clover for POS data:** You already have data on what customers buy, when, and how much. Most business owners never look at it. Spend 10 minutes per week looking at your top-selling product or service. Whatever is selling best this week — that's what you should feature in your Google photo and your email offer. Don't guess. The data is sitting in your POS system.

**The total time breakdown:**
- Google Business Profile: 10 minutes
- Social media post: 15 minutes
- Email (review draft + send): 15 minutes
- Review data from POS: 10 minutes
- Quick check of what worked / didn't: 10 minutes
- **Total: 60 minutes**

I've set this up for a dog groomer in Nashville who was spending 6 hours per week on marketing across three platforms with no tracking. In six weeks, her weekly time dropped to 55 minutes and her revenue from marketing-related bookings went up 32%. The tool stack matters less than the fact that you pick one set of tools and actually use them consistently.

## How to Use What You Already Have (Your Customers Are Free Data)

Here's a truth that most marketing guides skip: you don't need more customers. You need to sell more to the customers you already have. The data to do this is already sitting in your point-of-sale system, your booking software, or your customer list. You just haven't looked at it.

**The repeat customer gap:** Most small businesses get 20–30% of their customers to return. The top 10% of businesses get 60%+ repeat rates. The difference isn't better service — it's following up. A customer who comes to your coffee shop three times in a month is worth 10x a one-time customer. But most business owners spend 80% of their marketing budget trying to acquire new people and 0% trying to get that third visit.

**The fix that takes 15 minutes:** Export your customer list from Square, Booksy, or your email provider. Find every customer who has bought from you exactly once in the last 90 days. Send them a one-time offer that expires in 7 days: "Come back and get 15% off your next visit." No segmenting, no fancy automation. Just a single email or text. I watched a salon in Portland do this on a Wednesday afternoon. They sent 83 emails. Twelve people booked appointments within 48 hours. That's $1,400 in incremental revenue from one 15-minute task.

**The review loop:** You need reviews. Everyone knows this. But most small business owners ask once, get ignored, and give up. Here's what works: ask for a review immediately after a transaction — in person, with a QR code printed on the receipt. The window is about 2 minutes after payment. I've seen a bakery in Austin go from 12 reviews to 83 in a month by printing a small sign at the register that said "Take 30 seconds to leave us a Google review. Show this to the counter person for a free cookie." The cost: $0.50 per review in cookies. The outcome: their Google rating went from 3.9 to 4.5, and their search visibility went up enough that organic traffic doubled.

**The data you're ignoring:** Your most profitable customers. If you're a fitness studio, it's not the person who buys a month of unlimited classes. It's the person who buys 4 private training sessions per month plus supplements. Find your top 10 customers by total spend. Figure out what they have in common. Then go find more of those people. That's not marketing. That's pattern recognition.

## What to Do When the 1-Hour Plan Isn't Enough (Signs It's Time to Spend Money)

I'm not anti-paid ads. I'm anti-wasting money on paid ads before you've done the free stuff. But there comes a point where one hour per week hits a ceiling. Here's how to know when you've hit it.

**Sign 1: Your organic channels are maxed out.** You're posting to Google Business Profile weekly. You're responding to every review. Your email list is active and converting. You've captured every customer who walks through the door. And you're still not hitting your revenue goal. That's when ads make sense — as a complement, not a replacement. A dog grooming business in Denver hit this point after three months of the 1-hour plan. Their revenue from organic channels had grown 40%, but they had capacity for 10 more clients per week and no way to reach them. We started a $500/month Google Ads campaign targeting "dog grooming near me" with a $10-off first-visit offer. In the first month, it brought 17 new clients. Average lifetime value from those clients: $240. That's $4,080 in potential revenue from $500 in ad spend.

**Sign 2: You're spending more than 1 hour per week on admin.** If you're spending 30 minutes just scheduling social posts or fighting with Mailchimp's segmentation, your tools are wrong. A yoga studio owner in NYC was spending 90 minutes per week on email alone because she was manually copying and pasting her schedule. We switched her to a simple email template in Gmail (yes, Gmail) and cut that time to 15 minutes. Sometimes the fix isn't more budget. It's simpler tools.

**Sign 3: You keep abandoning the plan.** If you've tried and failed three times to stick to 1 hour per week, the problem isn't discipline. It's that the plan requires you to do something you hate. If you hate writing emails, don't write them. Hire a VA for 2 hours per week to do it. One of my clients — a barber shop in Chicago — paid a local college student $15/hour to respond to Yelp reviews and post one photo per week. The student spent 3 hours per week. The barber shop's revenue from Yelp increased by $2,800 in the first two months. Cost: $180. That's a 15x return.

The 1-hour plan is designed to work until it doesn't. When it stops working, the answer isn't to add more hours. It's to add either automation, better targeting, or the right kind of paid spend. Start with free. Then spend. Never reverse that order.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I've tried posting on Instagram for 6 months and got 50 followers. What am I doing wrong?**

You're probably posting what you think is interesting, not what your customers want to see. A bagel shop in Chicago posted photos of their bagel-making process for a year — 200 followers. They started posting photos of customers holding their bagels, plus the finished product on a plate. Followers doubled in 6 weeks. The difference? People want to see themselves or their future meal, not your flour-dusted hands. Look at what your competitors' top posts look like, then copy the format. Not the content. The format.

**Q: I don't have $500/month for ads. Can I still market my business effectively?**

Yes. The 1-hour plan doesn't require any paid spend. A coffee shop in Portland went from 50 to 200 customers per week using only Google Business Profile posts and a simple email signup at the register. They asked every customer who paid with a card if they wanted to join the "free coffee on your 10th visit" email list. 300 people signed up in 90 days. They emailed once per week with a new seasonal drink special. No ads. No budget. $0.

**Q: How do I know if my marketing is actually working?**

You need to track two numbers: new customers per week and revenue from those customers. Use Square or Booksy's reporting. Look at the source of each new customer — how they found you. If 60% say "Google search" and you're spending zero time on Google, you have a problem. If 0% say "Facebook" and you're spending 4 hours there, you also have a problem. Track one metric: cost per acquired customer, including your time. If your time is worth $50/hour and you spend 4 hours on marketing per week, that's a $200 cost. If you get 2 new customers from that effort, your cost per acquisition is $100. If those customers spend an average of $40 per visit and come twice, you're losing $20 per customer. Your marketing is a net loss. That's the math most business owners never do.

**Q: I tried Google Ads and spent $300 with zero sales. Why did it fail?**

Two reasons most likely. First, you didn't use negative keywords. You probably showed up for searches like "free haircut" or "cheap haircut" when you're a premium salon. Second, your landing page was your general website, not a specific offer. A dentist in Nashville was spending $500/month on ads for "teeth whitening" but the ad went to his homepage. We created a simple landing page (one page, one offer, one booking button). Cost per lead dropped from $45 to $8. The ad wasn't the problem. The path from ad to booking was broken.

**Q: Do I really need a website? Can't I just use Instagram or Yelp?**

You can, but you're renting your audience. Instagram changes its algorithm and your reach drops. Yelp can remove your listing. Google can shut your Business Profile. Your website is the only digital asset you fully control. That said, a one-page website with your address, hours, services, and a booking link is sufficient. A dog groomer in Austin built a one-page site on Squarespace in 4 hours. Cost: $192/year. It generates about 25% of her new customer inquiries. For $16 per month, she gets direct traffic that she owns. That's a low-cost insurance policy against any platform changing their rules.

**Q: I have no time to write emails or post on social media. What's the minimum I can do?**

The absolute minimum: update your Google Business Profile hours once per month, respond to reviews, and post one photo per week. That's 15–20 minutes. If you can't do that, pay someone $50/week to do it. A pet groomer in Denver pays a high school student $20/week to take one photo of each groomed dog and post it to Google Business Profile. She got 15 new clients from Google in the first two months. Cost: $160. Revenue from those clients: $2,250. You can outsource the minimum. You can't skip it.
