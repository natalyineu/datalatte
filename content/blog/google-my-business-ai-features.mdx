## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Setting It and Forgetting It

A pet groomer in Austin, Texas, set up her Google Business Profile in 2021. She filled in the basics — name, address, phone — and then never touched it again. By March 2023, she was getting 12 calls a week asking if she still existed. Her hours on the profile still said "Closed on Sundays," which was true, but they also said "Open until 6 PM Tuesday," which hadn't been true since she changed to 4 PM closings in early 2022.

The real damage? She had a Yelp profile and a website that both showed updated hours. Google was picking up conflicting signals and decided to display "Hours may differ" on her listing. Customers interpreted that as "probably closed" and called one of the three other groomers within a two-mile radius.

The fix took 11 minutes. She logged in, updated her hours, added a note about holiday closures for the next three months, and turned on the "Popular times" data so Google could cross-reference her actual traffic. Within two weeks, her profile views increased by 34%. Within a month, her booking rate went from 18 appointments per week to 26. At an average of $65 per grooming session, that's an extra $520 a week — or roughly $2,080 a month in revenue she'd been leaving on the table because no one could figure out when she was open.

**What to do instead:** Set a recurring calendar reminder for the first of every month. Spend 10 minutes reviewing your profile. Check hours, add a new photo, respond to any recent reviews. That's it. Ten minutes. If you can't commit to that, outsource it to an intern or a VA for $50 a month. The ROI on a 34% increase in profile views is hard to argue with.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring the Q&A Section

A coffee shop in Portland, Oregon, had a robust Google Business Profile. Great photos, 4.6 stars, 200+ reviews. But they never looked at the Q&A section because, in their words, "nobody asks questions there."

Except they did. Three different customers had asked variations of "Do you have oat milk?" over six months. Since the business never answered, Google's algorithm grabbed text from their website. Their site mentioned "oat milk latte" in a blog post from 2019. The problem? They had stopped carrying oat milk in early 2022 after a supply chain nightmare and switched to an alternative.

Customers showed up, ordered oat milk, were told it wasn't available, and left annoyed. Some left one-star reviews specifically mentioning the discrepancy. The shop's rating dropped from 4.6 to 4.3 over four months. Local search volume declined 22% in that period, according to their analytics.

The fix? The owner spent 30 minutes answering every unanswered question on the profile. She also added a "Frequently Asked Questions" section on her website that auto-updates via a plugin. She now checks the Q&A tab every Tuesday while drinking her morning espresso. Three months later, her rating climbed back to 4.5, and the negative reviews mentioning oat milk stopped completely.

**What to do instead:** Check the Q&A tab weekly. If you get a question, answer it within 24 hours. Better yet, add the five most common questions people ask at your business (Do you take reservations? Is there parking? Do you cater? What's your cancellation policy?) and answer them preemptively. You can do this by posting questions yourself from a separate Google account and answering them from your business account. It feels weird. It works.

### Mistake #3: Hoarding Negative Reviews

A hair salon in Nashville, Tennessee, got a two-star review from a customer who was upset about a last-minute cancellation fee. The salon owner's instinct was to ignore it — "Don't feed the trolls" was his mantra. But the review wasn't the problem. How he handled it was.

The customer had booked a $200 color treatment, canceled 45 minutes before the appointment, and was charged the $50 cancellation fee outlined in the booking confirmation. The customer left a review saying the salon was "unfair and money-hungry." The owner's response? Silence. For three months.

Here's what happened next: Google's algorithm tracks response rates and sentiment. A profile with unanswered negative reviews (especially recent ones) gets dinged in local search rankings. The salon dropped from position three to position nine in "hair salon Nashville" searches over that three-month period. Their call volume dropped roughly 30%.

The owner eventually responded — not by apologizing for the fee (he was right to charge it), but by explaining the policy clearly and offering to chat further offline. He also started responding to every review, positive or negative, within 48 hours. Within six weeks, his ranking climbed back to position four. More importantly, customers mentioned liking the "transparent and professional" responses in newer reviews.

**What to do instead:** Respond to every review within 48 hours. For negative reviews, acknowledge the person's frustration, explain your side without being defensive, and offer to take it offline. For positive reviews, say something specific — "Glad you enjoyed the lavender latte, Sarah" beats "Thanks for the 5 stars!" every time. You don't have to grovel. You just have to show you're a real human who cares.

### Mistake #4: Using the Wrong Category — or No Category at All

A fitness studio in Denver, Colorado, categorized itself as "Gym" on Google Business Profile. Technically true — they had weights and treadmills. But their actual business model was 80% yoga classes and 20% barre. They were competing against 24 Hour Fitness, Planet Fitness, and Gold's Gym for search terms like "gym near me." They were losing badly.

A competitor who also offered yoga but listed themselves as "Yoga Studio" and "Pilates Studio" in secondary categories was showing up in searches for both. The Denver studio's profile views were half of what they should have been.

The fix took five minutes. The owner changed the primary category to "Yoga Studio" and added "Physical Fitness Program" and "Personal Trainer" as secondary categories. Within 30 days, profile views increased 41%. Phone calls asking about class schedules increased 28%. At $18 per drop-in class and an average of 15 new students per week, that's roughly $1,080 in additional weekly revenue — $4,320 a month — from a category change alone.

**What to do instead:** Pick the most specific primary category that describes what you actually sell, not what your industry is. If you're a bakery that also sells sandwiches, your primary category should be "Bakery" and your secondary should be "Sandwich Shop." Not "Restaurant." Categories affect which searches you appear in. Get this wrong, and you're invisible to the people looking for exactly what you offer.

## Using Google Posts to Drive Immediate Action

Most business owners treat their Google Business Profile as a static directory listing. Fill it out once, forget about it. Meanwhile, Google Posts — the little announcements that show up in your profile — are sitting there, completely underutilized.

Here's what a Google Post looks like: a short blurb (up to 1,500 characters), a photo or video, a button (Learn More, Book, Buy, Sign Up, Call), and a publish date. They stay visible for seven days. After that, they're archived but still searchable.

I tested this with a Chicago coffee shop that wanted to promote a new cold brew flavor. Normally they'd post it on Instagram and hope people saw it. Instead, we put up a Google Post with a photo of the drink, the text "New: Honey Lavender Cold Brew. Available now. Show this post for $1 off today only," and a "Call to Order" button. The post ran for seven days.

Results: 1,280 impressions. 312 clicks on the button. 47 people redeemed the offer in-store. At $5.50 per drink (minus the $1 discount), that's $211.50 in direct revenue from a post that took 15 minutes to create. Plus, the 47 people who came in bought an average of $4.50 in pastries each — another $211.50. Total incremental revenue: $423 from a single post.

Most small businesses don't do this. They post once when they open and never again. Here's what you should actually use Google Posts for:

- **Events:** Hosting a live music night? Running a workshop? Post it three days before, the day of, and a recap after.
- **Offers:** Limited-time discounts, BOGO deals, seasonal specials. Keep them short and specific.
- **New products/services:** "We now offer express blowouts. Same price, half the time. Book online."
- **Behind-the-scenes:** "Here's our baker starting the sourdough at 4 AM." People love this stuff.
- **COVID-19 or policy updates:** Still relevant for mask policies, appointment requirements, or capacity limits.

One more thing: Google Posts impact your ranking. Profiles with fresh, relevant posts get a slight boost in local search results. It's not a massive swing, but in a competitive market, it can mean the difference between position three and position four.

If you're using a scheduling tool like Later or Buffer for social media, add Google Posts to your weekly rotation. Most scheduling tools don't support Google Posts directly, but you can manually create them in 10-15 minutes. Do it every Monday morning. I promise the ROI beats scrolling through Instagram.

## Tying Google Business Profile Insights to Your Booking or POS System

The most underused feature in Google Business Profile is the Insights tab. It tells you how many people searched for your business, how they found you, what actions they took (calls, direction requests, website clicks), and even where your customers are coming from geographically.

But here's the problem: raw data without action is just trivia. Knowing that 45 people asked for directions to your shop last week is interesting. Knowing that those 45 requests came from a specific zip code, and cross-referencing that with your Square or Booksy data to see which appointments actually booked? That's useful.

Let me give you a real example from a hair salon in Austin. The owner noticed through Insights that 22% of her direction requests came from a zip code 12 minutes away — an area she'd never advertised to. She checked her Booksy booking data and found that only 3 clients in the last six months had come from that area. She had a gap between interest and conversion.

Here's what she did: she created a Google Post specifically targeting that zip code — "First-time clients from [neighborhood] get 20% off their first cut and color. Show your ID at checkout." She also ran a small Google Ads campaign targeting keywords like "hair salon [neighborhood name]" with a $300/month budget.

Within two months, she had 14 new clients from that area. Average first visit spend: $180. Total revenue from those new clients: $2,520. Her ad spend was $600. Net gain: $1,920. And those 14 clients each had a projected lifetime value of $1,200 based on her average retention rate. Total LTV from this single insight-driven action: $16,800.

If you're not connecting your GBP Insights to your actual booking system, you're flying blind. Here's how to start:

1. **Export your GBP Insights data monthly.** Google doesn't make this easy — you'll have to manually copy the numbers into a spreadsheet. Do it anyway.
2. **Cross-reference with your POS or booking tool.** Look at Mailchimp or Square for customer acquisition sources. Which zip codes are over-indexing on searches but under-indexing on actual visits? Those are your opportunities.
3. **Run a targeted test.** Pick one zip code, create one offer, run it for 30 days. Measure the results. If it works, scale it.
4. **Track phone calls.** Google provides a unique forwarding number that tracks calls from your profile. Use it. "I got 10 calls last week" is a guess. "I got 14 calls, of which 8 booked appointments" is a data point.

This isn't complicated. It's tedious. But it's the difference between hoping your marketing works and knowing it does.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn't Google Business Profile just another thing I have to manage? I'm already stretched thin.**

Yes, it's another thing. But it's the one thing that directly determines whether your business shows up when someone searches for exactly what you offer. A cleaned-up profile takes 30 minutes to set up and 10 minutes a month to maintain. Compare that to the time you spend on Instagram, Facebook, or Yelp. GBP drives more direct action — calls, directions, website visits — than any other free platform for local businesses. If you only have time for one marketing channel, this is it.

**Q: Can I just hire someone to set it up and then ignore it?**

You can, but you'll get mediocre results. The businesses that see the most benefit update their profiles regularly — new photos, posts, responses to reviews. Think of it like cleaning your storefront. You don't do it once and call it done. You sweep every day. Same with Google. If you really don't want to touch it, hire a VA to spend 30 minutes a week on updates. It'll cost you maybe $100 a month and likely return 5-10x that in increased visibility.

**Q: What if I have multiple locations? Do I need separate profiles?**

Yes, each physical location needs its own Google Business Profile. You can manage them all from one dashboard if you have fewer than 10 locations. For 10+, you'll need Google's bulk location management tools. Pro tip: don't try to run 20 locations yourself. Hire someone part-time or use a tool like Yext or BrightLocal to manage the data. It's worth the cost.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from optimizing my profile?**

The Google algorithm updates relatively quickly — you can see ranking changes within a few weeks after making significant changes. But "results" is subjective. Some changes (like updating hours or adding categories) produce immediate improvements in visibility. Others (like building a review response habit) take months to compound. Typical timeline: noticeable ranking improvement in 2-4 weeks, measurable increase in calls or bookings in 4-6 weeks, significant revenue impact in 3-6 months.

**Q: Can I use Google Business Profile instead of a website?**

You can, but you shouldn't. Your GBP profile is not a replacement for a website. It's a front door. A website is where people go once they decide they're interested. You need both. That said, a good profile with photos, posts, and Q&As can serve as a temporary landing page if you're just starting out. Just don't plan on that being your long-term strategy.

**Q: Does responding to reviews actually matter? I've heard it does, but I'm skeptical.**

It does, and here's proof: BrightLocal's 2023 Local Consumer Review Survey found that 52% of consumers say they're more likely to use a business that responds to reviews. Google's own guidelines say responding to reviews is a best practice for improving your profile's performance. I've seen profiles with consistent review response rates rank 2-3 positions higher than identical profiles that ignore reviews. Plus, it makes you look like you care. That's worth something.
