## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating Your Google Business Profile Like a Yellow Pages Listing

I worked with a salon owner in Austin, Texas — let's call her Maria. She'd owned her shop for six years, had a solid client base, but new customer acquisition had flatlined. Her Google Business Profile was technically "set up": name, address, phone number, hours. That was it. No photos updated since 2019. No posts. No responses to reviews.

She was getting maybe 50 profile views a month. For context, a well-optimized salon profile in a city like Austin should see 300–500 views monthly minimum.

**What went wrong:** Maria assumed Google would figure out the rest. She wasn't posting service updates, wasn't adding photos of current work, and wasn't replying to reviews — including a 1-star review from someone who'd never actually been to her salon. Google's algorithm treats engagement signals seriously. A profile that looks abandoned gets buried.

**The fix:** We spent two hours one Thursday afternoon. Took 40 new photos (cut and color work, interior shots, team headshots). Wrote descriptions for each service category. Set up Google Posts twice a week — promotions, before/after shots, seasonal offers. Responded to every review within 48 hours, including that fake 1-star (flagged and removed within a week).

**The outcome:** Within 45 days, profile views went from 50/month to 410/month. Direction requests increased 280%. She booked 12 new clients directly from Google search in the first month after changes — roughly $1,400 in new revenue from people who'd never walked past her door.

Don't set it and forget it. Treat your GBP like a live storefront, not a phone book entry.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Category Selection

A barbershop in Denver — not a salon, but the lesson applies directly — had been stuck at the bottom of local search results for two years. The owner, Dan, was frustrated. He'd done everything "right." Website. Reviews. Consistent NAP. Nothing moved.

I looked at his Google Business Profile. He'd selected "Barber shop" as his primary category. Fine, that's what he is. But he hadn't selected any secondary categories. No "Men's hair salon." No " Hair salon." Google uses secondary categories to understand what else you offer and match you to broader searches.

**What went wrong:** Someone searching "hair salon Denver" would never see Dan's shop, even though he did everything a salon does — just for men. Google didn't know to include him.

**The fix:** Added secondary categories: "Hair salon," "Men's haircut," "Beard grooming." It took three minutes.

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, Dan's shop appeared in 40% more local search results. He tracked 8 new clients who specifically said they found him searching "hair salon near me" — not "barber shop." That's roughly $480 in new monthly revenue from a three-minute settings change.

Check your categories. If you're a salon that also does waxing or brow shaping, add those as secondary categories. If you specialize in curly hair or color corrections, see if there's a relevant category (there isn't always — then use the description field strategically).

### Mistake #3: Wasting Money on the Wrong Local Ads

A nail salon in Portland, Oregon was spending $1,200/month on Google Ads. The owner, Jenna, was getting clicks — about 200 a month — but almost zero bookings. She was about to declare all paid search a scam.

I looked at her campaign. She was bidding on generic terms like "nail salon Portland" and "manicure near me." Her ad copy said "Best nail salon in Portland." Her landing page was her homepage — a generic grid of services with no specific offer, no booking button above the fold, and no phone number until you scrolled halfway down.

**What went wrong:** Three problems. One: She was bidding on high-intent but high-competition terms against every other salon in Portland. Two: Her ad copy was generic — no urgency, no differentiation. Three: Her landing page had zero conversion optimization. People clicked, saw nothing compelling, and left.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the campaign from scratch. Targeted long-tail terms: "gel x extensions Portland," "Russian manicure Portland," "nail art Portland." Changed ad copy to include specific offers: "Book a gel manicure — $10 off first visit." Created a dedicated landing page with one question ("What service do you need?"), a booking calendar (using Booksy), and social proof (five recent reviews with photos).

**The outcome:** Ad spend dropped to $800/month. Click-through rate actually went up slightly (3.2% vs 2.1%). But the conversion rate went from 0.5% to 8%. She went from 1 booking per month from ads to 16 bookings per month. At an average ticket of $55, that's $880 in revenue from $800 in ad spend — finally profitable. The previous campaign was losing $1,200/month with near-zero return.

If your local ads aren't working, it's almost never "Google is a scam." It's your targeting or your landing page. Fix those first.

### Mistake #4: Letting Negative Reviews Sit Unanswered

A hair salon in Nashville had a 4.1-star average on Google with 200+ reviews. Respectable. But three unaddressed 1-star reviews from the past six months were sitting at the top of their reviews section — Google shows most recent first. One mentioned "the stylist was rude." Another said "my color came out orange." The third was a vague "would not recommend."

The owner, Tanya, told me she didn't respond because she "didn't want to engage with negative people."

**What went wrong:** Google's algorithm weights recency and engagement. Three negative reviews with zero owner responses signals to Google that this business doesn't care about customer experience. Worse, potential clients read those reviews first and assume the worst.

**The fix:** We responded to each one within 24 hours. The "rude stylist" review: "I'm sorry you felt that way — we've since added customer service training for all team members. Would you be open to a conversation about your experience? Please call me directly at [number]." The orange color: "That shouldn't have happened. If you'd like to come back for a corrective treatment at no charge, please reach out." The vague one: "We'd love to learn more about what went wrong so we can improve."

**The outcome:** Google started showing more recent positive reviews higher. Average rating didn't change much (4.1 to 4.2), but the tone of new reviews shifted. Three of the negative review authors actually came back (two were regulars who'd had one bad visit). Within 60 days, the 4.1 looked more like a 4.4 because the negative reviews got pushed down by newer, positive ones with owner replies. Tanya estimated $2,100 in retained business from people who'd told her they almost booked elsewhere but changed their mind after seeing she handled complaints professionally.

Respond to every review. Even the bad ones. Especially the bad ones.

## How Online Booking Platforms Can Feed Your Local SEO

Most salons treat booking platforms like Booksy, Vagaro, and Square Appointments as scheduling tools. They're that. But they're also SEO assets you're probably underusing.

Here's the mechanic: When you create a profile on Booksy, that page ranks in Google search. I've seen salon Booksy pages rank higher than the salon's own website for branded searches. That's not ideal, but it's real. More importantly, those platforms have authority — high domain ratings that your new website doesn't have yet.

**What this means for you:** Claim and fully complete your profiles on Booksy, Vagaro, Square Appointments, and Yelp (yes, Yelp — I'll get to that). Each profile is another entry point for potential clients. Make sure your NAP (name, address, phone) is identical across every platform. One wrong digit and Google gets confused.

**Real example:** A salon in Chicago was getting 60% of its new clients through Instagram. That's fine until Instagram changes its algorithm (which it does constantly). The owner, Derek, set up Booksy, Square, and Yelp profiles in one afternoon. He didn't optimize them beyond filling in the fields.

Six months later, I checked his analytics. Booksy was the #3 source of new client bookings — 18 per month. Square Appointments was #5 with 9 per month. Combined, those two platforms were driving 27 new clients monthly, roughly $2,160 in revenue at an $80 average visit. Most of those people found him through Google, not through the platform's internal search.

**What to do specifically:**

- **Booksy:** Complete your entire profile. Add every service. Upload 15+ photos. Enable online booking. The platform rewards completeness with better internal search placement and higher Google rankings for your profile page.
- **Square Appointments:** If you use Square for payments, you have this already. Make sure your appointment page is public and linked from your website. Square profiles often rank for "salon near me" searches because of Square's domain authority.
- **Yelp:** I know Yelp gets a bad reputation. The sales calls are aggressive. The review filtering is frustrating. But Yelp pages rank. Period. Claim your page. Fill it out completely. Respond to reviews. Don't pay for advertising unless you've tested it and it works for your specific location.

One caveat: Don't let these platforms replace your own website. Your website should be the hub. But treat each platform as a spoke — another way for Google to find you and send traffic.

## Building Local Links That Actually Move the Needle

Link building for local SEO is different from link building for a national blog. You're not trying to get featured on Forbes. You're trying to get listed on the Chamber of Commerce website, the local wedding planning directory, and the city's "best of" list.

Most hair salons ignore this entirely. They focus on GBP optimization and reviews — both essential — but they skip local citations and backlinks. Then they wonder why a newer salon with worse reviews outranks them.

**Real example:** A salon in Denver (different from the barbershop earlier) was ranking #7 for "hair salon Denver" while a competitor with fewer reviews and lower ratings was ranking #3. I checked the competitor's backlinks. They were listed on:
- Denver Wedding Guide (brides look for salons)
- Colorado Small Business Directory
- North Denver Chamber of Commerce
- A local lifestyle blog's "Best Salons in Denver" post (from 2019, still active)

The #3 salon had 5 backlinks from relevant local sites. The #7 salon had 0.

**The fix:** We spent four hours over two weeks claiming and submitting the #7 salon to local directories. Not spam directories — actual local resources that Google trusts. We found:
- The Chamber of Commerce ($300/year membership, but the member directory page is a dofollow link and generates referral calls)
- Two bridal directories (free)
- A neighborhood blog that featured local businesses (free, just had to submit a short blurb)
- A partnership with a nearby boutique (mutual website links)

**The outcome:** Within 90 days, the salon moved from #7 to #4 for "hair salon Denver." Direction requests increased 35%. The owner tracked at least $1,800 in additional revenue from clients who said they found him "on Google" — he couldn't attribute all of it to links, but the timing was clear.

**What to do:**

- Check your local Chamber of Commerce. Most have a member directory with backlinks. Worth the membership fee if you're in a competitive market.
- Find local wedding and event planners. Brides book salon appointments for wedding parties. Get listed as a recommended vendor.
- Partner with complementary local businesses. A boutique, a makeup artist, a photographer. Cross-link on each other's websites. "Recommended by" sections work well.
- Look for local blogs or publications that write "best of" lists. Submit yourself. Most are happy to include new businesses, especially if you're doing something interesting.
- Sponsor a local event or team. A $200 sponsorship for a 5K run gets you listed on their website and often in email newsletters. Both have SEO value plus real-world visibility.

Link building is slow. It takes 30–90 days to see movement. But once those links exist, they compound. Each new link makes the next one slightly more valuable.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I have 50 reviews and a fully optimized Google profile. Why am I still not ranking in top 3?**

Could be a few things. Your NAP might not be consistent across all platforms — check Yelp, Facebook, Booksy, and your website for any discrepancies. You might not have enough local backlinks (see the section above). Or your competition might simply have more proximity advantage — Google favors businesses physically closer to the searcher. If you're on the edge of a city, you'll rank for that neighborhood but not for the whole city. That's normal. Consider running a small Google Ads campaign targeting the city center to compensate.

**Q: Is Yelp worth the hassle?**

For SEO, yes. Yelp pages rank well in Google. Claim your page, fill it out, respond to reviews. Don't pay for advertising unless you've tested it with a small budget and seen a positive return. Yelp's sales team will push hard — be prepared to say "not right now" repeatedly. But don't ignore the organic value.

**Q: Should I pay for Google Ads or focus only on organic SEO?**

Both, but start with organic. Fix your Google Business Profile, your website, and your local citations first. That's free (or very low cost) and has compounding returns. Once you have a baseline of organic traffic that's converting, add Google Ads to capture searches you're not ranking for yet. Start with $300–500/month. Track every dollar to booking. If a keyword doesn't convert in 30 days, kill it and try another.

**Q: I'm a solo stylist renting a chair. Can I still use these strategies?**

Yes, and you should. Your Google Business Profile doesn't need a physical storefront — you can list as a "service area business." Your reviews matter even more because you don't have salon-level volume. Get 20+ reviews from real clients. Respond to every one. Optimize your single page website (or even just your Booksy profile) with clear booking instructions. The same principles apply at smaller scale.

**Q: How often should I post to Google Business Profile?**

Twice a week minimum. More if you have something to share — new services, before/after photos, team updates, seasonal promotions. Google rewards active profiles. A post takes five minutes. You can schedule them using the GBP dashboard or tools like SEMrush. Don't overthink it. Just show Google you're alive.

**Q: Can I do all of this myself, or do I need to hire someone?**

You can do 80% of it yourself if you're willing to spend 2–3 hours per week. The time-consuming parts are taking photos, responding to reviews, and submitting to local directories. If you'd rather spend that time cutting hair, hire someone — a VA can handle directory submissions and review responses for $15–20/hour. But at minimum, you should understand the strategy well enough to know if they're doing it right.
