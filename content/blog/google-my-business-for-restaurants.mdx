## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Putting Your Street Address in the Wrong Place

A barbecue spot in Austin, Texas — let's call it "Smoke Ring" — had been in business for six years. They had loyal customers, decent reviews, and a solid menu. But their phone was quiet. Walk-ins were flat. The owner, Mike, figured Google just hated small barbecue joints.

His Google Business Profile had the street address listed correctly, but he'd checked the box that said "Hide your address (you serve customers at your location)." He'd done this because he read somewhere that hiding your address was good for privacy.

Here's the problem: Google's local algorithm treats hidden-address businesses differently. If your address is hidden, Google assumes you're a service-area business that doesn't take walk-ins. Smoke Ring was losing the "near me" search game because Google didn't know customers could actually show up and eat.

**The fix:** Mike unchecked that box. He kept his real address visible. He also made sure the "Service Area" settings were blank — because he doesn't do catering, he serves brisket to people who walk through the door.

**The outcome:** Within four weeks, Smoke Ring's profile appeared in the local 3-pack for "barbecue Austin" for the first time in two years. Phone calls increased from about eight per week to twenty-three. Mike estimated the change brought in an additional $1,200 per week in orders. Not bad for unchecking a single box.

**The rule:** If customers can walk in and sit down, show your address. If you're a plumber, hide it. Mixing those up costs you real money.

### Mistake 2: Treating Your Profile Like a Digital Business Card

A bakery in Portland, Oregon — "Pear and Thyme" — had a Google Business Profile that listed their hours, phone number, and a photo of their storefront from 2018. That was it. No menu. No posts. No answers to questions.

The owner, Jenna, told me: "I claimed it five years ago and never touched it again. People know where we are."

Except they didn't. A competitor two blocks away had the same number of reviews but was ranking above Pear and Thyme for "Portland bakery" searches. Google's algorithm looks for engagement signals — profile updates, photo uploads, customer Q&A — to decide which businesses are actively operating. Jenna's profile looked abandoned.

**The fix:** Jenna spent two hours one Sunday uploading fresh photos of current menu items (not stock photos, not old shots). She added a menu PDF that she updates monthly. She turned on the Q&A feature and answered the six questions customers had already asked (including "Do you have gluten-free options?" which was sitting there unanswered for eight months).

**The outcome:** Within six weeks, Pear and Thyme jumped from position 7 to position 3 for their primary keyword. Jenna tracked an additional $3,800 in monthly revenue directly to new customers who said they found her through Google search. She now spends thirty minutes per week on profile updates — that's a $1,900 per hour return on her time.

**The rule:** A profile you set up once and ignore is a profile that's actively losing you money. Google wants to see you're alive. Prove it.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring the Categories Section

A pizza shop in Chicago — "Slice Proper" — had been open for eighteen months. The owner, Tony, had optimized everything: photos, hours, description, reviews. He was doing everything right except one thing. His primary category was "Pizza restaurant."

That's... fine. But not great.

When I looked at his profile, I noticed Google had also listed "Italian restaurant" and "Delivery restaurant" as secondary categories. Tony hadn't touched those. He didn't know he could add more. He also didn't realize that Google sometimes auto-fills categories based on your website content — and if you don't override them, Google guesses.

The auto-filled categories were wrong for his business. He doesn't do delivery. He's a sit-down joint with a bar. Google was listing him for delivery searches — searches where he had no intention of competing.

**The fix:** Tony changed his primary category to "Pizzeria" (more specific than "Pizza restaurant"), then manually added "Bar & grill" and "Family restaurant" as secondary categories. He removed the irrelevant "Delivery restaurant" and "Italian restaurant" tags.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, his profile started appearing for "bar food Chicago" — a term he'd never targeted. His average position in search results improved by about 40%. He also noticed a drop in calls from people asking about delivery (which he doesn't offer), meaning he was wasting less time on wrong-fit customers.

**The rule:** Google gives you up to ten categories. Use them deliberately. Let Google guess for you, and Google will guess wrong.

### Mistake 4: Letting Negative Reviews Rot

A nail salon in Nashville — "Gloss & Co." — had a 4.2-star average with forty-seven reviews. That's decent. But three of those reviews were one-star complaints from the past eighteen months. The owner, Danielle, hadn't responded to any of them.

One review said: "They didn't honor my appointment time. I waited 40 minutes." Another said: "The gel manicure chipped in two days."

Danielle told me: "Those people are just hard to please. I didn't want to engage."

Here's the uncomfortable truth: Google notices when you ignore reviews. Not responding to negative reviews tells the algorithm one of two things — either you don't care about customer experience, or you're not actively managing your profile. Both hurt your ranking. More importantly: potential customers read those reviews and see silence, which confirms the complaint.

**The fix:** Danielle responded to every unanswered review — positive and negative — in one sitting. For the negative ones, she apologized directly, explained what she'd done to fix the issue (retrained staff on appointment timing, switched to a different gel brand), and invited the reviewer back for a complimentary service.

**The outcome:** Two of the three one-star reviewers updated their ratings to four stars after the response. Danielle's average climbed from 4.2 to 4.4. More importantly: new reviews started rolling in at a higher rate. Google appears to reward profiles that show active engagement with reviewers. Her phone calls increased by about 17% over the next two months, and she estimated the extra foot traffic at roughly $500 per week.

**The rule:** A negative review you respond to is a problem you've partially solved. A negative review you ignore is a sales rep you're paying to lose customers.

## Why Your Google Ads Spend Depends on Your Profile Quality (and Vice Versa)

Most small business owners think Google Business Profile and Google Ads are separate universes. They're not. They're sharing a bank account.

Here's how it works in practice: when someone searches for "coffee shop Denver" and clicks your Google Ad, they land on... your Google Business Profile. If they click "Call," they never see your website at all. The first impression of your business is your profile — not your homepage.

I worked with a coffee roaster in Denver who was spending $1,800 per month on Google Ads. Their click-through rate was decent — about 4%. But their conversion rate (people who called or visited after clicking) was terrible — under 2%. The owner couldn't figure out why.

The problem: his Google Business Profile was a mess. The hours were wrong (said he closed at 5pm, actually closed at 3pm on weekends). The photos were blurry. The primary category was "Coffee shop" when he's actually a roastery that sells wholesale. People clicked the ad, saw the profile, and didn't trust it.

**What we did:**

- Updated the profile completely (hours, photos, categories, description)
- Changed the Google Ads landing page setting from "Website" to "Business Profile" for mobile traffic (because 73% of local searches convert on mobile with a call)
- Added call tracking to see exactly which calls came from ads vs. organic

**The outcome:** In thirty days, the conversion rate from ad clicks to calls jumped from 2% to 11%. The cost per conversion dropped from $48 to $8. The owner stopped burning $1,200 per month on ineffective ads without spending a dime more on ad budget.

The tool I used for call tracking was CallRail — $30/month for the basic plan, no contract. There are cheaper options too. But the point is: you don't need to spend more on ads. You need to fix the landing page Google shows them, which is your profile.

If you're running Google Ads for your restaurant, salon, or service business, open your profile right now and answer one question: "If a stranger saw this and nothing else, would they call me?" If the answer is no, fix that before you add another dollar to your ad budget.

## The Truth About Yelp, Booksy, and Third-Party Listings (They're Not Your Enemy, But They're Not Your Friend Either)

A fitness studio owner in Austin — "Sweat & Soul" — came to me frustrated. She was paying $150/month to Yelp for their advertising program. She'd had three leads in six months. She was also on Booksy, Mindbody, and ClassPass. Her Google Business Profile was an afterthought.

She asked: "Which one do I actually need?"

Here's what I told her: You need Google more than Google needs you. You need Yelp only if your customers are on Yelp. You need Booksy or Mindbody if you do online booking.

**The real issue:** Her business name, address, and phone number were inconsistent across all these platforms. Booksy had her listed as "Sweat & Soul Fitness." Mindbody had "Sweat and Soul." Yelp had "Sweat n Soul." Her Google Business Profile had the correct name — "Sweat & Soul" — but Google's algorithm cross-references these third-party sites to verify your information. When the data doesn't match, Google lowers your trust score.

**What we did:**

- Standardized her business name across every platform to exactly "Sweat & Soul" (no variations)
- Used Moz Local ($129/year) to check for inconsistencies across fifty-plus directories. Found seventeen errors.
- Synced her Google Business Profile with Booksy so class availability showed directly on her Google profile

**The outcome:** Within eight weeks, her Google Business Profile started ranking for "Austin fitness classes" — a term she'd never shown up for. She dropped the Yelp advertising (saving $1,800/year). Her Booking button on Google started getting about thirty clicks per week. New members who signed up via Google mentioned seeing the real-time class availability before they called.

**The numbers:**

- $1,800 saved on Yelp ads
- +14 new members per month from Google (at $150/month average, that's $2,100/month revenue)
- $129/year cost for Moz Local (paid for itself in the first week)

**The rule:** Third-party platforms are not the enemy. But they're also not the priority. Get your Google presence right first. Then make sure everything else matches perfectly. A misspelled street address on Yelp can cost you ranking on Google even if your Google profile is flawless.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it take to see results after optimizing my Google Business Profile?**

Usually two to six weeks. Google re-crawls profiles on its own schedule — there's no "submit for review" button. I've seen profiles move up in ranking within ten days if the changes were significant (wrong category fixed, missing hours added). But expect the full impact to show in about a month. If nothing changes after eight weeks, you've probably missed something else — like inconsistent citations on other directories.

**Q: Do I have to pay for Google Business Profile? Is there a catch?**

It's free. I know that sounds suspicious. Google makes money when people search and click ads. They want accurate local profiles because that keeps people searching on Google instead of switching to Yelp or Apple Maps. The "catch" is that Google will try to sell you Google Ads. Just ignore those prompts. Your free profile will still rank fine without spending a cent.

**Q: What if someone leaves a fake negative review? Can I get it removed?**

Yes, but only if it violates Google's policies — which includes fake reviews, spam, off-topic comments, and reviews that contain profanity or personal attacks. "I didn't like the food" is not removable. "This place is a scam and the owner is a criminal" might be. Report it through your profile. If Google rejects your removal request (which happens often), you can respond publicly with a calm, factual statement. That's usually more effective than fighting the removal system.

**Q: Can I have the same Google Business Profile for multiple locations?**

No. Each physical location needs its own profile. If you open a second shop, create a separate profile with that location's specific address, phone number, and hours. Google will connect them if they share a brand name. Do not try to list both locations on one profile — that gets your profile suspended, and getting it reinstated takes weeks.

**Q: Is it worth using Google Posts on my profile? Do they actually help ranking?**

They help indirectly. Google Posts don't boost your ranking the way keywords or reviews do. But they make your profile look active — which tells Google you're an operating business, not a zombie profile. More importantly, they give searchers a reason to click. A post about "Trivia Night Wednesdays" or "New spring menu" can be the difference between someone scrolling past and someone calling. I've seen restaurants get twenty to thirty extra clicks per week just by posting once weekly. Takes five minutes.

**Q: What's the one thing most business owners get wrong about Google Business Profile?**

They think it's set-and-forget. It's not. Google expects to see signs of life — new photos, updated hours for holidays, responses to recent reviews, fresh posts. I've consulted with businesses that set up a perfect profile, then ignored it for two years, and wondered why their rankings dropped. You don't need to obsess over it daily, but spending fifteen minutes per week is the difference between staying visible and getting buried by a competitor who's actually updating theirs.
