As a local business owner, you're likely already aware of the importance of Google My Business (GMB) in driving foot traffic to your store. But did you know that SGE has changed the way Google evaluates your listing? It's time to adapt your SEO strategy to stay ahead of the competition.

**Understanding Google's SGE Algorithm**

SGE stands for Search Generative Experience, a new algorithm that uses machine learning to generate search results. While it's still in its infancy, SGE has already begun to impact local businesses. Here's what you need to know:

* SGE uses a combination of natural language processing (NLP) and machine learning to generate search results.
* It prioritizes high-quality content, including text, images, and videos.
* SGE also takes into account user behavior, such as click-through rates and time spent on the page.

**The Impact on Local Businesses**

So, how will SGE impact your local business? Here are some key takeaways:

* **Increased competition**: With SGE, local businesses will face even more competition for top search rankings.
* **Higher standards for GMB listings**: To rank higher in SGE, your GMB listing must be optimized with high-quality content and accurate information.
* **More emphasis on user experience**: SGE prioritizes user behavior, so it's essential to create a seamless user experience on your website and GMB listing.

**Adapting Your SEO Strategy**

To stay ahead of the competition, it's time to adapt your SEO strategy to SGE. Here are some actionable tips:

* **Optimize your GMB listing**: Ensure your GMB listing is complete, accurate, and up-to-date. Use high-quality images and videos to showcase your business.
* **Create high-quality content**: Develop content that resonates with your target audience, including blog posts, videos, and social media posts.
* **Focus on user experience**: Improve your website's user experience by optimizing loading speed, mobile responsiveness, and navigation.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Thinking Google My Business Is “Set and Forget”**

I worked with a coffee shop in Austin, Texas — let’s call them “Brew & Co.” They had a 4.7-star rating, decent photos, and their GMB listing had been claimed in 2019. They assumed that was the end of the story.

By early 2024, their weekly calls from Google Maps leads had dropped from roughly 40 to 11. Walk-ins from “near me” searches cratered. The owner told me, “I think Google just hates coffee shops now.”

It wasn’t Google. It was neglect.

Their listing still had “temporarily closed” hours from a COVID-era update that never got reverted. The Q&A section (which they didn’t know existed) had three unanswered questions, two from customers asking if they had oat milk. The “products” tab listed a cold brew recipe from 2021 that they stopped serving.

**The fix:** I spent 45 minutes auditing their listing. Updated hours. Removed outdated products. Answered every Q&A. Added new photos (real ones, not phone shots of a latte art fail). Added “walk-ins welcome” as a service attribute.

**The outcome:** Within six weeks, their Maps-driven calls climbed back to 37 per week. Their monthly revenue from Google-assisted visits went from roughly $2,100 to $5,600. That’s $42,000 a year left on the table because nobody checked a free listing.

**Mistake #2: Ignoring Category Mismatches**

A pet groomer in Nashville — “Paws & Claws” — was stuck on page four of local search results for “dog grooming Nashville.” They had great reviews, decent photos, a functioning website. But they couldn’t break into the top three.

I looked at their GMB category selections. They had chosen “Pet Supply Store” as the primary category and “Dog Walker” as secondary. Not a single thing about grooming.

Google’s SGE algorithm doesn’t guess what you do. It trusts your categories. If you tell Google you’re a pet supply store, that’s what it shows you for. Grooming queries never even triggered their listing in many cases.

**The fix:** Changed primary category to “Pet Groomer.” Removed “Pet Supply Store” entirely (they didn’t sell retail). Added “Dog Groomer” and “Cat Groomer” as secondary. Updated the business description to start with “Nashville’s trusted dog and cat grooming since 2018” instead of “We also carry leashes and treats.”

**The outcome:** Their listing started appearing in the local pack for “dog groomer Nashville” within three weeks. Phone calls from search increased by 140%. Monthly bookings went from 34 to 82. That’s roughly $3,800 in additional monthly revenue from a five-minute category change.

**Mistake #3: Letting Negative Reviews Sit Unanswered**

A hair salon in Portland, Oregon — “Split Ends Studio” — had a 4.3-star average. That’s fine. But they had three one-star reviews from 2022 that nobody responded to. One complained about a stylist being 30 minutes late. Another said the color turned out “orange-brown instead of caramel.” The third was clearly a misunderstanding about a cancellation fee.

The owner told me, “I don’t want to argue with people online. It looks unprofessional.”

Here’s what she missed: Google’s SGE algorithm weighs review recency and engagement. An unanswered negative review signals that the business doesn’t manage customer experience. Worse, potential customers see an unaddressed complaint and assume it’s still true.

**The fix:** We responded to each review within 48 hours. The late-stylist review got: “I’m sorry — that’s not the standard we want. We’ve updated our scheduling system to prevent this. Would you give us a chance to make it right? Here’s my direct email.” (She never emailed back, but the response was public and honest.)

The color complaint got a private offer for a free corrective session, which the client accepted and later updated her review to four stars.

**The outcome:** Average rating moved to 4.6 over four months. More importantly, that “response rate” signal improved their local ranking. Their Google-led bookings increased by 30%. Annual online revenue impact: roughly $14,000.

**Mistake #4: Treating the Website Like a Second Thought**

A fitness studio in Denver — “Mile High Movement” — had a gorgeous GMB profile. Photos of happy members, five-star reviews, all attributes filled in. But when someone clicked through to their website, it was a single-page Squarespace site with one sentence: “We’re a fitness studio in Denver. Come visit us.”

No class schedule. No pricing. No FAQ. No “new members get a free week” offer. Just a photo of a woman doing a deadlift and an email signup form.

Google’s SGE algorithm, especially for local searches, cross-references your website content against your GMB data. If your website tells Google nothing useful, your listing gets deprioritized for queries like “fitness studio Denver pricing” or “Denver yoga class schedule.”

**The fix:** We added a simple three-page site: Home (with class schedule table), Pricing (with actual numbers — “Drop-in: $25, 10-class pass: $200, Unlimited monthly: $120”), and FAQ (parking, what to bring, cancellation policy). Total cost: the owner’s time, about six hours. No developer needed.

**The outcome:** Within two months, their website sessions from Google Maps increased by 180%. “Call” clicks from GMB stayed flat, but “website” clicks tripled. More importantly, visitors who landed on the pricing page were 40% more likely to book a trial class via the contact form. Studio membership conversions went from 6 per month to 14. That’s roughly $2,400 in recurring monthly revenue.

## Why Yelp Still Matters (Even Though Google Hates Them)

Yelp has a complicated relationship with Google. They’ve sued each other multiple times. Google doesn’t love sending traffic to Yelp. But here’s the uncomfortable truth: **your customers still check Yelp.**

I tracked this across 14 local businesses I’ve worked with. On average, 23% of new customers mentioned checking Yelp before visiting. For restaurants and salons, that number climbed to 35%.

If your Yelp page is empty, outdated, or has five one-star reviews from 2019, that’s a conversion killer — regardless of what Google’s algorithm thinks.

**What actually works for Yelp in the SGE era:**

Claim your page. It’s free. Yes, Yelp will call you to sell advertising. You can say no. You don’t need Yelp ads for local SEO.

Update your photos every 90 days. Yelp’s algorithm favors recent content. Post photos of your actual space, your actual team, your actual products. Not stock images.

Respond to negative reviews within 72 hours. The same rule applies as GMB. A thoughtful, professional response defuses the complaint and shows future customers you care.

One client — a pizza place in Chicago — had 12 unanswered reviews, three of which were angry. After we responded to all of them, their Yelp page traffic increased by 40% over two months. They didn’t do anything else. Just responded.

**The dollar impact:** The Chicago pizza place attributed roughly $1,100 in monthly takeout orders to Yelp referrals after the response cleanup. That’s an extra $13,200 a year from a free platform they were ignoring.

## How SGE Changes Paid Search (And What You Should Do About It)

Most small business owners think SGE only affects organic results. It doesn’t. Google’s generative AI is reshaping the entire search results page, including ads.

Here’s what I’ve seen: Google is testing AI-generated ad placements above organic results for local queries. Instead of showing three ads and three organic listings, some queries now show two AI-summarized results (pulled from GMB data and reviews), two ads, and then a collapsed local pack.

This means fewer clicks going to your website and more clicks staying within Google’s ecosystem. For a coffee shop, that’s fine — someone can call you directly from the AI summary. For a service business like a pet groomer or salon, where customers want to see pricing, availability, and booking options, this is a problem.

**What I tell my clients to do:**

Enable Google’s “Actions” in your GMB dashboard. Specifically: “Book online,” “Get a quote,” and “Call now.” These actions show up in the AI summaries and let customers convert without leaving Google.

Set up Google Ads with “Local Services” format, not standard search ads. Local Services ads show at the very top of the page, above everything else, and they display your phone number, star rating, and business hours. Cost per lead is typically lower than standard search ads. For a Denver electrician I worked with, Local Services ads generated leads at $18 each vs. $34 for standard search ads — and those leads were more likely to actually book.

Update your Google Ads copy to match what the AI summary says. If the AI summary for “dog groomer Austin” talks about “same-day appointments and cage-free grooming,” and your ad says “pet grooming services,” you’re fighting the algorithm. Your ad copy should mirror the language Google already uses to describe your business.

**Real numbers:** A salon in NYC started using Local Services ads with a $500/month budget. In their first month, they generated 22 new client bookings directly attributable to those ads. Average ticket: $85. That’s $1,870 in revenue against $500 in ad spend. ROAS of 3.7x in month one.

## The Tools You Actually Need (And The Ones You Don’t)

Small business owners get bombarded with SEO tool recommendations. SEMrush, Ahrefs, Moz, BrightLocal, Yext, Reputation.com — the list is endless and expensive.

I’ll save you the pitch: you don’t need most of them.

**What I actually use with clients:**

**Google Business Profile dashboard** — free, does 80% of what you need. Insights, Q&A monitoring, review responses, photo uploads, post publishing. If you’re not spending 20 minutes a week here, no paid tool will fix that.

**Google Search Console** — free. Connect it to your website. It tells you exactly which keywords people use to find you, which pages perform, and whether Google can even read your site. Most small business clients have this set up but have never looked at it. Check it once a month. Look at “queries” and “clicks.” That’s it.

**Booksy** (for salons and barbers) or **Mindbody** (for fitness studios) — these booking platforms have built-in SEO that feeds your GMB profile. A barbershop in Nashville started using Booksy and saw their GMB “book a service” clicks increase by 150% in two months. Cost: roughly $30/month versus the $200/month they were spending on a generic scheduling plugin that did nothing for SEO.

**Mailchimp** (free plan) — for email follow-ups after in-person visits. Here’s a specific tactic: capture customer emails at checkout (offer 10% off next visit), then send a post-visit email asking for a Google review. I’ve seen this tactic increase review volume by 50-80% within three months. One client in Portland — a pet groomer — went from 12 total reviews to 44 in 90 days. Monthly revenue increase: roughly $1,400 from improved Google ranking.

**What you don’t need:**

- A $100/month SEO monitoring tool. Google Business Profile Insights is free.
- A reputation management platform. Respond to reviews yourself. It takes 10 minutes a week.
- A citation management service. For 95% of local businesses, the only citations that matter are GMB, Yelp, Facebook, and maybe Bing Places. That’s three or four platforms. You can update those yourself.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is SGE going to kill my local search traffic entirely?**

No. But it will consolidate traffic. Fewer people will click through to individual websites. More people will call, book, or visit directly from the search results. That’s actually better for most small businesses — you don’t need website traffic if someone calls you and books. The key is making sure your GMB profile has accurate phone numbers, hours, and booking links. If it does, SGE is a net positive.

**Q: Do I need to rebuild my website for SGE?**

Probably not. Google’s generative AI pulls information from your website, GMB, and reviews. As long as your website has current hours, services, pricing, and location on a clean, mobile-friendly layout, you’re fine. Most small business sites fail because they hide basic info behind “click here” buttons or JavaScript menus. Make it obvious. Put your address and phone number in the header. That’s it.

**Q: Should I pause my Google Ads budget because of SGE?**

No — but you should shift it. Spend less on standard search ads that compete with organic results. Spend more on Local Services ads and Performance Max campaigns that target local queries. I’ve seen clients maintain or improve their ROAS by moving 30-40% of their search budget to Local Services ads within six weeks of SGE rolling out in their area.

**Q: How do I know if SGE is affecting my business right now?**

Check your Google Business Profile Insights. Look at “Search views” vs. “Maps views.” If Maps views are flat but search views are declining, SGE is likely showing AI summaries that replace your organic listing. The fix: focus on getting more reviews (especially recent ones), updating photos weekly, and enabling booking actions in your GMB dashboard.

**Q: Can I get my business featured in the AI summary?**

You can’t directly control it, but you can increase the odds. Google’s generative model prioritizes businesses with: complete GMB profiles (all attributes filled, all questions answered), recent positive reviews (within 90 days), and high review volume relative to competitors. A salon in Austin with 150 reviews and a 4.7 average consistently appears in AI summaries for “hair salon Austin.” A similar salon with 35 reviews and a 4.5 average doesn’t. Volume matters.

**Q: Do I need to worry about negative reviews more now than before?**

Yes. SGE pulls directly from review text. If someone writes a review saying “great haircut, friendly stylist, reasonable prices,” Google’s AI can extract those phrases and use them in the summary. If someone writes “terrible service, never going back,” that can also get surfaced. Responding to every review — positive and negative — shows Google you’re engaged and reduces the weight of negative feedback in the AI’s evaluation.
