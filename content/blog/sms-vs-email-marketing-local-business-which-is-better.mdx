To get the most out of email marketing, focus on:
* Building a strong email list with opt-in subscribers
* Creating engaging content that resonates with your audience

## The Power of SMS Marketing
SMS marketing, on the other hand, offers a more personal and immediate way to connect with customers. ## Comparing Email and SMS: Which is More Effective?
So, which channel is more effective for getting customers back? The answer depends on your business goals and audience. However, here's a general breakdown:

* Email marketing is ideal for:
	+ Nurturing leads and building relationships
	+ Sharing detailed content, such as newsletters or promotions
	+ Targeting specific segments of your audience
* SMS marketing is ideal for:
	+ Sending time-sensitive offers or reminders
	+ Providing exclusive deals or promotions
	+ Encouraging immediate action

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched local business owners burn cash on SMS and email for over a decade. Usually the same three or four mistakes. Here's what actually happens when you get them wrong.

### Mistake #1: Treating SMS Like Cheap Email

**The story:** A coffee shop in Austin, Texas — let's call it Brew & Bloom on South Congress — decided to blast their entire SMS list every Tuesday morning with the same message: "Come grab a latte! 10% off today only!" They had 1,400 subscribers. Within six weeks, 380 people had unsubscribed. Their open rates dropped from 94% to 61%. The owner told me she was "just keeping people informed."

She wasn't. She was annoying them.

**What went wrong:** SMS is a high-intimacy channel. Every text lands in someone's personal message inbox next to their kid's school pickup reminder and their spouse's grocery list. When you send a generic "10% off" blast, you're not building loyalty — you're training people to ignore you. Or worse, to block you.

**The fix:** We cut her send frequency from weekly to twice per month. Every message had to answer one question: *Would I want this text if I hadn't opened my email this week?* We replaced the generic discount texts with specific, time-sensitive offers tied to actual inventory: "We made 40 cranberry scones this morning. They'll be gone by noon."

**The outcome:** Unsubscribes dropped to near zero within a month. Open rates went back to 91%. But the real win was revenue per send: her average SMS campaign brought in $1,470 in attributed sales, up from $380. That's a 287% improvement just by sending less often, with better messages.

**The rule:** If you wouldn't text a friend the same message, don't text your customers.

### Mistake #2: Buying Lists Instead of Building Them

**The story:** A hair salon in Nashville — let's call it Eastside Cuts — spent $500 on a "targeted local email list" from a vendor that promised 8,000 subscribers within a 10-mile radius. The owner was thrilled. Three campaigns later, she had exactly zero new bookings. Not one. Her open rates hovered around 8%. She got three spam complaints.

**What went wrong:** Those 8,000 emails were scraped, expired, or belonged to people who never opted in. Even if a few were real, they had zero relationship with the salon. Email providers flag high bounce rates and low engagement. After the third send, Mailchimp put her account under review. She almost lost the *legitimate* list of 400 clients she'd been building for two years.

**The fix:** We deleted the purchased list entirely. Hard reset. Then we set up a simple opt-in process at the checkout counter: "Text GO to [number] and get $10 off your next visit." We also added an email sign-up box on her booking confirmation page in Square. No incentives beyond that — no contests, no free stuff for friends.

**The outcome:** In the first month, she added 63 text subscribers and 41 email subscribers. Small numbers. But those 104 people booked a combined 47 appointments over the next 60 days, generating roughly $4,230 in revenue. Compare that to zero from the $500 list. The cost per acquisition for the opt-in list was effectively zero dollars. The purchased list cost her $500 plus the time she wasted.

**The rule:** A list of 200 people who actually want to hear from you is worth more than 8,000 people who don't know you exist. Buying lists is not marketing. It's littering.

### Mistake #3: No Segmentation, Same Message to Everyone

**The story:** A pet grooming business in Portland, Oregon — Furry Friends Grooming — had 1,100 email subscribers and 450 SMS subscribers. For two years, they sent the exact same message to every single person. "20% off grooming this month" went to everyone, including the customer who had just booked an appointment the day before.

The owner didn't think segmentation mattered. "They're all pet owners who live nearby," she said. "Same audience."

**What went wrong:** Her repeat customers got annoyed by irrelevant offers. New customers got the same discount as loyal ones who had spent $800 that year. Nobody felt special. Her email click-through rate was stuck at 1.2%. Her SMS click rate was 4%. For context, good email click rates for local services run around 3-4%. SMS should be 15-20%.

**The fix:** We segmented her audience into three groups based on Square booking data:
- **New clients** (0-2 visits): Welcome sequence with store info, grooming tips, 15% off next visit
- **Regulars** (3-10 visits): Loyalty reminder, prepaid package offers, referral bonus
- **VIPs** (10+ visits): Early access to appointment slots, birthday groom special, "thank you" offer

Each group got a different email and a different SMS. Same business, different messages.

**The outcome:** Within three months, email click-through rates went from 1.2% to 3.8%. SMS click rates jumped from 4% to 14%. But the real number: average revenue per email send increased from $220 to $780. The VIP segment alone generated $1,100 per campaign, mostly from prepaid package purchases.

**The rule:** If your message doesn't change based on who's receiving it, you're not marketing. You're spamming.

### Mistake #4: No Clear Call to Action

**The story:** A yoga studio in Denver, Colorado — Mountain Flow Yoga — sent beautiful weekly emails. Professionally designed. Good photos. Nice copy. One problem: they couldn't tell you what they wanted you to do. The emails talked about studio philosophy, upcoming workshops, teacher spotlights, and new class times — all in the same email. The call to action at the bottom was a generic "Visit our website."

The owner told me she wanted people to "feel connected to the community."

**What went wrong:** Connection is not a conversion metric. Her open rates were fine (22%), but nobody clicked. Her SMS messages were worse — a single link with no context: "Check out our new classes! [link]" That's it. No reason to click. No urgency. No specific offer.

**The fix:** Every email and every SMS got exactly one job. One. We rewrote the SMS to say: "New 6am HIIT flow starts Monday. First class is free for SMS subscribers. Reply YES to claim yours." That's specific. That's urgent. That's one action. For email, we picked one primary offer per send and made the button text descriptive: "Claim Your Free First Class" instead of "Learn More."

**The outcome:** SMS reply rate went from basically zero to 8%. Those replies turned into 22 new student registrations in the first month, worth about $2,640 in intro packages. Email click-through rate went from 1.8% to 5.2%. The owner was nervous about being "too salesy." I told her: your customers can't buy what they can't find. Give them a clear path.

**The rule:** If your customer has to guess what to do next, you've already lost them.

## The Surprising Truth About Channel Overlap

Here's something most guides skip: email and SMS don't compete with each other. They compete with your customer's attention span.

I worked with a coffee shop in Chicago's Wicker Park neighborhood that was running both channels completely independently. The email person didn't know what the SMS person was sending (they were actually the same person, but she wasn't tracking it). Customers would get an email about a new cold brew flavor on Tuesday, then a text about the exact same cold brew on Thursday. The first message would have worked. The second one just felt like noise.

We fixed it by giving each channel a specific lane:

- **Email** became the "thinking channel." Long-form content. Recipes from the barista. Behind-the-scenes stories about sourcing beans. Monthly menu previews. Things people want to read when they have a moment.
- **SMS** became the "doing channel." "We just pulled a single-origin Ethiopian espresso. First 20 people get a free sample. Come now or miss it." Time-sensitive, location-specific, immediate action.

The results surprised me. Not because the numbers were good — I expected that. What surprised me was the **cross-channel behavior**. Customers who received both emails and texts visited 2.4 times more frequently than customers who only got emails. The two channels reinforced each other. The email built the relationship. The SMS triggered the visit.

**The hard number:** In the first three months of lane separation, the coffee shop's average monthly revenue from email+SMS subscribers increased by $3,800 compared to the previous quarter. That's not a small bump for a single location.

Most local businesses run SMS and email because someone told them they should. Few actually design a system where the two channels have different jobs. That overlap kills performance.

If you're sending the same thing in both channels, you're paying double for half the impact.

## What to Send When: Timing and Frequency for Local Businesses

I get asked about timing more than anything else. "Should I send emails on Tuesday or Thursday?" "Is it okay to text on Sunday?" "How often is too often?"

The real answer depends on your business type, not some universal playbook. But after watching dozens of local campaigns, here's what actually works:

### Coffee Shops and Cafes

Your customer makes decisions in minutes, not days. They're deciding where to grab their morning caffeine in the 15 minutes before they leave the house.

**SMS:** Monday through Friday, 6:30-7:30 AM local time. One text per week max. The message should be about something that exists right now. "We roasted a new batch of Guatemalan beans. First 30 people get a free pour-over taste." Don't send coffee texts on Saturday or Sunday unless you're offering a specific weekend deal.

**Email:** Tuesday or Wednesday, mid-morning. One email per week. This is where you share the story behind the beans, introduce the new pastry supplier, or announce the monthly "community cup" charity blend. Open rates for coffee shop emails tend to be higher on Wednesdays — people have settled into their week and are looking for a lunch break read.

A cafe in Brooklyn tested Monday vs Wednesday sends. Wednesday outperformed by 34% on click-through. Small sample, but the pattern held across three other coffee clients.

### Hair Salons and Barber Shops

Your customer books ahead. They're planning their schedule 1-3 weeks out.

**SMS:** Thursday or Friday afternoon, 2-4 PM. This is when people start thinking about weekend plans and realizing they should look presentable. One text per week, max two. Appointment reminders work well here too — "Your cut with Jess is tomorrow at 2pm. Reply C to confirm."

**Email:** Sunday evening or Monday morning. People check their email on Sunday night while planning the week ahead. Send your monthly menu of services, introduce new stylists, or offer a "book your appointment early" incentive.

A salon in Charlotte, North Carolina shifted their email sends from Thursday to Sunday. Their booking rate from email went up 27%. The owner said it was "the easiest change we ever made."

### Fitness Studios and Gyms

Your customer has a habit they're trying to build. You're competing with their alarm clock.

**SMS:** Evening before, 6-8 PM. "Tomorrow's 6am HIIT has 5 spots left. Text YES to claim one." Or morning of, if you're running a same-day class. But don't text before 7 AM unless you want people to hate you.

**Email:** Weekly newsletter on Monday or Tuesday. Class schedule updates, member spotlights, nutrition tips. Keep it brief — gym-goers are scrolling on their phone between sets.

A CrossFit box in Austin tested SMS reminders with and without a specific class link. The ones with the link had a 40% higher attendance rate. The link made it zero-effort to commit.

### Pet Groomers and Dog Walkers

Your customer is booking for their pet, not themselves. The decision maker is the human, but the emotional weight is the dog.

**SMS:** Three days before the pet's scheduled groom, send a reminder. Day-of, send a "Your pup is next up!" text with a photo if possible. Post-appointment, text a photo and a thank you. This is one of the few businesses where multiple texts per appointment cycle actually increase loyalty.

**Email:** Monthly newsletter with seasonal tips. "Spring shedding season is here — here's how to manage it." "Winter paw care for snowy walks." Pet owners love breed-specific content. If you have their pet's info in your system (and you should), use it.

A pet groomer in San Diego started sending post-appointment SMS photos. Repeat booking rate went from 52% to 71% within six months. Cost of the photos: zero. Value: thousands.

### What About Frequency Limits?

Here's my rule of thumb from actual campaigns:

- **SMS:** No more than 4 messages per month for most local businesses. 6 is the absolute ceiling. Every message after that increases unsubscribe rates by roughly 3-5%.
- **Email:** 1-2 per week for most businesses. 3 per week is fine if you have highly engaged subscribers and fresh content. Anything above that and you're testing people's patience.

I had a client in Nashville who wanted to send daily SMS deals. I told her no. She tried it anyway for two weeks. Unsubscribes jumped 18%. She went back to two texts per week. Lesson learned.

The uncomfortable truth is that most local businesses over-communicate. They think more messages equal more sales. It doesn't work that way. Your customers have lives. They don't want to hear from you every day.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need both email and SMS, or can I pick one?**

You can pick one. Many businesses do fine with just email, especially if they're in a service category where customers book weeks in advance. But I'll tell you what I tell every client: if you're only doing email, you're leaving money on the table. SMS has a 90%+ open rate within three minutes. Email has maybe 20% within three hours. They serve different purposes. Email builds the relationship, SMS drives the immediate action. Together, they work better than either one alone.

**Q: Won't SMS annoy my customers? I hate getting marketing texts myself.**

You hate getting *bad* marketing texts. Everyone hates those. But you know what people don't hate? Getting a text that says "Your dog's haircut is done, here's a photo" or "Free coffee if you come in the next hour." That's utility, not spam. The difference is relevance and timing. If you're sending generic blasts to everyone, yes, your customers will hate it. If you're sending specific, timely, valuable messages, they'll appreciate it. I've never had a client lose customers from well-executed SMS. I've had multiple clients lose customers from bad email.

**Q: What's the actual cost difference between email and SMS?**

Email is basically free if you're using a platform like Mailchimp or Constant Contact on a small list. You're paying $20-50/month for the software. SMS costs money per message — typically $0.01 to $0.03 per text depending on volume and provider. For a list of 500 people, sending one SMS per week runs about $20-40 per month. That's not nothing, but compare it to the return: if one out of every 100 texts brings in a $50 customer, you're making money. The real cost isn't the SMS fees — it's the time you waste sending bad messages.

**Q: How do I get people to actually sign up for SMS without being pushy?**

The best method I've seen: make the offer specific and immediate. "Text COFFEE to 555-1234 and get a free latte on your next visit." That's it. No contest. No newsletter. One clear value exchange. Put that sign-up call on your receipt, your store window, your checkout page. For email, offer something similar but longer-lasting: "Sign up for our email list and get a free guide to [something relevant]." A coffee shop could offer a "Brewing Guide for French Press." A salon could offer a "Summer Hair Care Checklist." The key is that the incentive is directly useful, not a generic coupon.

**Q: What email or SMS platform should I use for a small local business?**

For email, Mailchimp is fine for most small businesses. The free tier handles up to 500 contacts. Constant Contact is also solid and has slightly better customer support. For SMS, I've had good results with SimpleTexting and TextMagic. Both are straightforward, no hidden fees. Square has built-in SMS if you're already using them for payments. Booksy has it for salons. The tool matters less than how you use it. I've seen businesses do amazing things with ugly platforms and terrible things with beautiful ones. Pick something simple and start sending.

**Q: Is it true that email is dying? Should I just focus on social media?**

Email is not dying. That myth has been around since 2010. Email marketing still generates $36 for every $1 spent, according to the Data & Marketing Association. Social media is important for awareness, but you don't own your social audience. Instagram changes its algorithm, your reach drops. TikTok gets banned in certain markets, your followers disappear. Email and SMS are the only channels where you actually own the relationship. If someone unsubscribes from your email, that's fine — they weren't going to buy anyway. If Instagram decides to show your posts to fewer people, you have no recourse. Build your owned channels first. Social media is the amplifier, not the foundation.
