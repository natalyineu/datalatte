### Setting Up Google Ads for Retargeting

Google Ads is an excellent platform for retargeting, especially if you're targeting users who have visited your website. To set up retargeting ads on Google Ads, follow these steps:

1.  Create a new campaign in Google Ads and select "Target and remarket" as your campaign type.
2.  Choose "Website" as your target type and select the website you want to target.
3.  Set your budget and bidding strategy.
4.  Create ad groups and ads that match your content.

When setting up retargeting ads on Google Ads, make sure to use relevant keywords, ad copy, and targeting options to ensure that your ads are shown to the right people.

### Setting Up Facebook Ads for Retargeting

Facebook Ads is another excellent platform for retargeting, especially if you're targeting users who have engaged with your content on Facebook. To set up retargeting ads on Facebook Ads, follow these steps:

1.  Create a new campaign in Facebook Ads Manager and select "Conversions" as your campaign objective.
2.  Choose "Website" as your target type and select the website you want to target.
3.  Set your budget and bidding strategy.
4.  Create ad sets and ads that match your content.

When setting up retargeting ads on Facebook Ads, make sure to use relevant targeting options, ad copy, and image to ensure that your ads are shown to the right people.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: No Frequency Cap (The Austin Bakery That Annoyed Everyone)

A bakery in Austin spent $800/month on retargeting. Their cookies are excellent. Their retargeting was not.

They set up a Facebook retargeting campaign without a frequency cap. One customer visited their site to check hours, then saw their ad 47 times in three days. She didn't buy. She left a one-star review: "Your ads are following me everywhere. I'll go somewhere else."

**What went wrong:** Frequency capping is not optional. The platform will show your ad until people hate you. By default, Facebook and Google will not limit frequency unless you tell them to.

**The fix:** Set frequency caps immediately. I recommend 2-3 impressions per person per day for retargeting. For a 7-day window, cap at 7-10 total. You want to remind, not harass.

**The outcome:** After implementing a 2-per-day cap, the bakery's click-through rate went from 1.2% to 3.8%. Ad spend dropped to $500/month. Revenue from retargeting in month two was $2,400. Their review score recovered. The customer who left the one-star never came back, but nine others did who had previously been muted or hidden the page.

**Nataliia's rule:** If someone is annoyed enough to leave a review about your ads, you have lost that customer and potentially dozens of others who saw that review. Frequency caps are not a suggestion.

### Mistake #2: Retargeting Everyone Who Landed on Your Site (The Portland Salon Problem)

A hair salon in Portland was spending $600/month retargeting every single person who hit their site. Including the five people who clicked the "Careers" page. Including the vendor who accidentally landed on their booking page while checking a backlink.

Including the person who called, made an appointment, showed up, paid, and left happy. They were still seeing ads for the booking page they had already used.

**What went wrong:** No audience segmentation. Retargeting a converted customer is literally paying to annoy people who already gave you money. Retargeting someone who looked at careers is useless — they aren't buying hair services. Retargeting the one person who clicked a blog post about balayage and then bounced? That person might be worth $1,800/year in services.

**The fix:** Segment your audiences. Create separate lists:
- People who viewed services pages but didn't book (high intent)
- People who visited the booking page but didn't complete (extremely high intent)
- People who only viewed the blog (low intent, send them content)
- Exclude anyone who has booked in the last 90 days
- Exclude your own IP address and your employees' IPs

**The outcome:** The salon paused retargeting entirely for three weeks while they rebuilt their audiences. They spent the same $600/month split across two segments: booking page abandoners (70% of budget) and service page viewers (30%). Month one: 14 new bookings directly attributed to ads. Average ticket: $165. Total revenue: $2,310. ROI: 3.85x.

Before segmentation, they were getting maybe 2-3 bookings per month they could trace to retargeting. That's $400-$500 in revenue on $600 spend. They were losing money and didn't know it.

### Mistake #3: Using the Same Creative as Your Original Ad (The Denver Gym Mistake)

A fitness studio in Denver ran a retargeting campaign using the exact same photo and headline from their initial Instagram ad. The initial ad had already run for six weeks. Everyone on their retargeting list had seen that image 15-20 times before.

The retargeting campaign had a 0.4% CTR. They spent $900 for three new memberships at $99 each. Negative ROI.

**What went wrong:** Retargeting creative must be different from your prospecting creative. If someone didn't click the first 15 times, they are bored of that image. Showing it again is not going to change their mind.

**The fix:** Create specifically designed retargeting creative. Test:
- Social proof: "Join 400+ members who started this year"
- Scarcity: "March special ends Friday — first class free"
- Urgency: "5 spots left in the 6 AM class"
- Different format: If your prospecting ad was a static image, make your retargeting a 15-second video
- Solve an objection: "Too far from home? Our 7 AM class is in Capitol Hill, right on the bus line"

**The outcome:** The studio created three new ad variations for retargeting only. One showed a video of a real member before and after. One offered "First class free + free towel" (addressing the common objection about forgetting a towel). One used a testimonial from a member who lost 22 pounds. Budget stayed at $900. Month two: 11 new memberships. Revenue: $1,089 in first-month dues plus $792 in initiation fees. Total: $1,881. ROI: 2.09x. Still not amazing, but trending up. They refined further in month three and hit 4.2x ROI by adding a "bring a friend free" retargeting offer.

### Mistake #4: Not Creating a Post-Click Experience (The Chicago Plumber That Wasted $2,000)

A plumbing company in Chicago ran Google Ads retargeting with a generic ad: "Need a plumber? Call now." When someone clicked, they landed on the homepage. The homepage had 14 menu options, a blog feed, a photo gallery of previous jobs, and a form buried at the bottom.

The retargeting campaign drove 312 clicks in a month. Zero form submissions. Two phone calls — both for drain cleaning, not the emergency service they were specifically adverting.

**What went wrong:** The landing page had nothing to do with why the visitor clicked. The ad promised emergency plumbing. The homepage offered... everything. The visitor had to hunt for what they wanted. They didn't.

**The fix:** For every retargeting ad, build a dedicated landing page. One offer. One call to action. No navigation bar (you want them to stay on that page, not wander to your "About Us"). The page for the emergency plumbing retargeting should have:
- A headline that matches the ad copy exactly
- A phone number at the top, middle, and bottom
- A one-click call button on mobile
- No menu, no blog, no gallery
- A guarantee: "We'll be there in 60 minutes or the service call is free"

**The outcome:** The plumber replaced their homepage link with a dedicated landing page. Same budget ($500/month). Month one after the change: 47 calls, 31 booked appointments. Average job value: $380. Revenue: $11,780. Ad cost: $500. Landing page build: $350 one-time. ROI for that month: 13.8x.

The page is still live a year later. The plumber told me it's the best money he ever spent.

## Retargeting Beyond the Search and Social Duopoly

Google and Facebook get all the attention for retargeting. They work. But local businesses have a few other tools that can produce better results for less money — because nobody else in your niche is using them yet.

### Yelp Retargeting Doesn't Get Enough Credit

Yelp allows retargeting of people who viewed your business page but didn't call or request a quote. I know business owners hate Yelp. I get it. But if you're a plumber, a hair salon, a restaurant, or a mechanic, people are searching for you on Yelp whether you like it or not.

You can set up Yelp's "Message from Business" ads to target people who visited your Yelp page in the last 30 days. The ad shows as a highlighted message in their Yelp app or on desktop. It feels more personal than a display ad.

A BBQ joint in Nashville used this. They spent $200/month. Their Yelp page had 43 views from the retargeting in month one. Seven of those turned into takeout orders. Average order: $52. Revenue: $364. ROI: 1.82x. Not amazing, but the ad cost was low enough that it paid for itself. They increased it to $350/month in month two and hit 2.4x ROI.

The key: Yelp retargeting works best when your Yelp page has recent photos, updated hours, and positive reviews. If your Yelp page looks abandoned, don't send people there.

### Nextdoor Is Underrated for Local Retargeting

Nextdoor allows you to target by neighborhood and by interests. You can create a retargeting campaign for people who viewed your Nextdoor business page. In my experience working with home service businesses — landscapers, cleaners, contractors — Nextdoor retargeting often outperforms Facebook for local.

A house cleaning company in Denver tested this. They ran Facebook retargeting at $500/month and Nextdoor retargeting at $300/month. Facebook generated 4 new clients in 30 days. Nextdoor generated 7. The Nextdoor clients were closer to their service area, so travel time dropped. Average job value on Nextdoor: $185. Facebook: $210. But the volume from Nextdoor was higher at a lower cost per acquisition.

The catch: Nextdoor's ad manager is clunky. You have to set up conversion tracking manually. Most business owners give up after the first attempt. Push through it. The competition is lower because other businesses gave up.

### Email Retargeting: The $0.10 Channel Everyone Ignores

Most local businesses collect emails but never use them for retargeting. If you're using Square, Mailchimp, Booksy, or any booking platform, you likely have hundreds of emails from people who visited but never booked.

A pet groomer in Austin had 1,400 email addresses from their Square booking system. Emails were collected from people who filled out a "request appointment" form but never completed the booking. They had never sent a follow-up email.

We set up a three-email sequence in Mailchimp:
1. Day 1: "Hey, we noticed you started booking a grooming appointment. Here's a direct link to finish." (Same day, 2 hours after they abandoned)
2. Day 3: "Your pup deserves a spa day. Book now and get 10% off your first visit."
3. Day 7: "Still need a groomer? Here's what our customers say about us." (With 5 reviews)

Cost: Mailchimp free plan (up to 500 contacts, they paid $13/month for the next tier). Time to set up: 2 hours.

Month one results: 21 completed bookings from the abandoned appointment emails. Average booking: $65. Revenue: $1,365. Cost: $13. ROI: 105x.

No one does this. It's absurd that no one does this.

The email retargeting also fed their Facebook pixel. People who opened the email but didn't book got added to a Facebook retargeting list. Combined channels drove another 9 bookings in month two.

## Budget Strategy for Local Retargeting: How Much to Spend and When to Walk Away

Business owners ask me this constantly. "How much should I spend on retargeting?" The answer depends on your margins and your average customer value. Here's a framework I've used across probably 40 local businesses.

### The Simple Math

Calculate your break-even point:

1. What is your average customer value? (One visit or one sale. Not lifetime value — start with the immediate transaction.)
2. What is your cost of goods or service delivery? (If you're a coffee shop selling a $5 latte, your margin might be $3. If you're a mechanic doing a $500 repair, margin might be $250.)
3. How many website visitors does it take to get one sale without retargeting? (If 100 visitors = 2 sales, that's a 2% conversion rate.)
4. How many retargeted visitors does it take to get one sale? (Industry average: 6-10%. Start with 8% as your estimate.)

If your average ticket is $50 with $30 margin, and you need 12 retargeted clicks for one sale, you can afford to pay $2.50 per click and still break even.

Most local businesses overspend because they don't run this math.

### A Real Example: Coffee Shop in Portland

A coffee shop had a $6.50 average transaction. Their margin after beans, milk, labor, and rent was roughly $2.50 per transaction. They were spending $700/month on Google retargeting. Their cost per click was $1.20.

To break even on one transaction: they needed a click to cost less than $2.50. At $1.20 per click, they were technically under the break-even point. But their conversion rate from retargeting was 3.2%, meaning they needed 31 clicks for one sale. That's $37.20 in ad spend for $2.50 in margin.

They were losing $34.70 every time someone bought a latte from their retargeting ad.

The fix: We paused retargeting for one-time purchasers entirely. We created a segment for people who had visited their "catering" page. Average catering order: $380. Margin: roughly $200. At $1.20 per click and an 8% conversion rate (catering tends to convert higher because it's a considered purchase), they needed 12.5 clicks per sale. Cost per sale: $15. Margin: $200. ROI: 13.3x.

The coffee shop was trying to retarget people for a $6.50 purchase. They should have focused on the $380 purchase. The latte buyers will come back on their own if the coffee is good.

### When to Walk Away

If your retargeting campaign has not shown at least a 2x return on ad spend after 60 days, pause it. Not forever. But pause, audit, and fix before spending another dollar.

The most common reason for failure: you're retargeting the wrong audience. The fix is almost always segmentation, not more budget.

I've seen a dog daycare in Brooklyn spend $2,300 over four months on retargeting. They got 12 new clients. Average client spent $85 per visit. At 2 visits per month for three months, that's $510 per client. Total revenue from retargeting: $6,120. ROI: 2.66x. That looks fine until you realize they had no frequency cap and 34% of their budget went to people who clicked an ad but never converted — because the ad went to their homepage (mistake #4). After fixing the landing page, they dropped to $400/month and got 8 new clients in month five. ROI jumped to 8.5x.

Sometimes walking away means restarting after you fix the fundamentals.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can't people just block my retargeting ads? Won't this waste my money?**

Yes, some people block ads. In my experience, roughly 15-25% of desktop users have ad blockers installed. Mobile is much lower, maybe 5-8%. You are not wasting money on blocked ads because you don't pay for impressions that don't load. The platforms only charge for delivered impressions. If someone blocks the ad, you don't pay. That said, don't rely on retargeting as your only channel. It works best as a follow-up to other marketing.

**Q: How long should I retarget someone? What if they bought from me already?**

Retarget for 14-30 days max for low-ticket items. For high-ticket services (roofing, law firms, medical procedures), you can retarget for 90 days because the buying cycle is longer. For anyone who has already converted, exclude them for at least 90 days. You can create a separate "upsell" retargeting campaign for past customers if you have a specific offer, but do not show them the same "come back and book" ad they already acted on.

**Q: I only get 200-300 website visitors a month. Is retargeting even worth it?**

Yes, but keep your expectations realistic. With 300 visitors, you might have 200 unique people after deduplication. At industry average conversion rates, that's maybe 16-20 conversions per month if your ads and landing pages are good. A florist in Nashville started with 150 visitors per month. They spent $150/month on retargeting and generated 4 additional orders per month. Average order: $45. Revenue: $180. That's barely break-even. But after three months, their traffic grew to 400 visitors because the retargeting ads drove word-of-mouth. At 400 visitors, retargeting generated 11 orders at $495 in revenue. It took three months to become profitable. Small audiences work, but you need patience.

**Q: Should I use Google Ads or Facebook for local retargeting?**

For most local businesses: start with Facebook if you sell to consumers, start with Google if you sell to people actively searching for your service. A vet clinic in Austin got better results from Google retargeting because their clients were searching "vet near me" and then abandoning the booking form. A pizza place in Chicago got better results from Facebook because their customers were scrolling during lunch and responding to visual ads of pizza. Test both with $100 each for two weeks. Let the data decide.

**Q: How do I track if someone bought from me in-store after seeing a retargeting ad?**

This is the hardest problem in local advertising. Online-to-offline attribution requires either phone call tracking, a promo code unique to the ad, or a QR code that leads to a dedicated landing page. The cleanest solution is a unique phone number on your retargeting landing page. Use a service like CallRail or WhatConverts. For a coffee shop, use a QR code that leads to a "show this for a free cookie with purchase" page. When someone shows the code, you know the ad worked. Without attribution, you are guessing.

**Q: I run a service business with different offerings. Do I need separate retargeting for each service?**

Yes, and this is where most service businesses mess up. A plumber should not retarget someone who looked at "drain cleaning" with an ad for "water heater installation." They will ignore it because it's irrelevant. Set up separate retargeting lists for each high-value service page. A plumbing company in Denver ran 5 separate retargeting campaigns: drain cleaning, water heater repair, burst pipes, toilet repair, and sewer line. Each had its own ad copy and landing page. Total spend: $800/month across all campaigns. Revenue: $8,400 in month one. ROI: 10.5x. The "burst pipes" campaign had the highest conversion rate because when someone searches for that, they need help immediately.

If your website is confusing, retargeting won't fix it. If your pricing is wrong, retargeting will just waste money faster.

But if you have a good business with decent traffic and you're losing people because they get distracted, forget to book, or need one more nudge — retargeting is the cheapest fix you aren't using correctly.

Start small. Segment ruthlessly. Cap your frequency. Build a dedicated landing page. Track everything. If it works, scale slowly. If it doesn't, pause and fix before spending another dollar.
