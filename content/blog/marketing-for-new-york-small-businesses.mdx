## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Burying Your Google Business Profile and Hoping for the Best

I had a client in Austin, Texas — a coffee shop in the Zilker neighborhood called something I won't name here. Great product. Great location. They were getting maybe 15 walk-ins a day. They had a website that looked like it was built in 2008 and a Google Business Profile that listed their hours as "Varies."

When I pulled their GBP analytics, they had 43 searches total in the previous three months. For a coffee shop near a major park.

**The fix:** We claimed the profile properly, added actual hours (including holiday closures), uploaded 15 photos of their best pastries and their pour-over setup, and started posting once a week — a new drink special, a photo of the barista, whatever.

**The outcome:** In 60 days, their profile views went from 43 to 1,240. Calls from the listing went from zero to 17 per month. They estimated an additional $2,300 in monthly revenue from people who found them through Google Maps.

Cost to fix: Two hours of my time and a willingness to take decent photos on an iPhone.

---

### Mistake 2: Throwing Money at Facebook Ads Without a Funnel

A pet grooming business in Portland, Oregon, came to me after spending $1,800 on Facebook ads in 30 days. They got 14,000 impressions and 212 link clicks. Zero new customers.

They were running a single ad that said "We groom dogs. Call us." No offer. No landing page. No tracking beyond Facebook's pixel. They assumed clicks would convert. They didn't.

**The fix:** We created a $35 first-time grooming special, built a simple landing page in Squarespace, set up a tracking spreadsheet, and retargeted people who visited the page but didn't book. Budget dropped to $600/month.

**The outcome:** First week: 9 bookings from the new ad. Second week: 14. Monthly revenue from the Facebook channel hit $3,800 within 60 days. The owner told me she had spent more money on coffee for her staff than on that ad account setup.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Reviews Entirely

A hair salon in Nashville had a Booksy booking page, a website, and a solid social presence. They were also sitting on 12 Yelp reviews — four of which were from 2019, one was a one-star about a scheduling error that the owner never replied to.

The one-star review was the third result when you searched "[Salon Name] Yelp." It had been there for 18 months.

**The fix:** We responded to every existing review — thanked the positive ones, acknowledged the negative one with an offer to make it right (the client did reach out, the reviewer updated to three stars). Then we implemented a simple system: after every appointment, Booksy automatically sends a review request. The owner checks once a week and responds within 48 hours.

**The outcome:** Review count went from 12 to 47 in four months. Average rating went from 3.8 to 4.6. The owner reported that new clients began mentioning "saw your reviews" in about 40% of intake conversations. Estimated revenue lift: $1,500–$2,000 per month.

### Mistake 4: Using a Generic "It Depends" Agency

A small fitness studio in Denver — four instructors, rented space, monthly nut of about $8,000 — hired a marketing agency that promised "custom strategy." What they got: a slide deck full of buzzwords, a content calendar that looked like it was written for a Fortune 500 brand, and a monthly bill for $2,500. After six months, they'd gained exactly 11 new members.

**The fix:** They fired the agency, set up a Mailchimp email list for former clients, ran a "Come back for free for one week" campaign, and spent $200/month on Google Local Services Ads targeting "yoga studio Denver" within a 3-mile radius.

**The outcome:** The email campaign reactivated 23 former members, 8 of whom re-signed at $150/month. The Google ads drove 40+ calls in the first month, converting to 12 new members. Total monthly marketing spend dropped from $2,500 to $650. Revenue increase from these two channels: about $3,000/month.

The owner told me, "I trusted the agency because they sounded like they knew what they were doing. I should have trusted the numbers."

## Why Your Email List Is Worth More Than Your Instagram Following

Social media feels urgent. Someone likes your post, you get a dopamine hit. But here's the uncomfortable truth: you do not own your Instagram followers. If the algorithm changes, your reach drops to zero. If Meta decides to charge for organic reach tomorrow (don't laugh — they've talked about it), you've got nothing.

Email is different. You own that list.

A small bakery in Brooklyn — one location, four employees, $14,000/month in revenue — had 1,400 Instagram followers and was posting daily. Their engagement was fine. Their sales from Instagram were impossible to track, and the owner guessed maybe $200/month.

They had a Square POS system that offered automated email receipts. They had been collecting emails for three years without ever using them. When I pulled the list, they had 2,800 email addresses of people who had actually paid them money.

**What we did:** We set up a Mailchimp account, segmented the list into "bought in the last 60 days" and "bought more than 60 days ago," and sent a simple weekly email: "Here's what came out of the oven this morning." No discounts, no gimmicks. Just photos and a short note.

**The outcome:** Within 30 days, open rates averaged 52%. Click-through rate was 11%. The bakery started seeing a bump in weekday morning sales — an average of $320 extra per week, which is about $1,300/month. This from a channel they had been ignoring for three years.

The cost: $0 for the platform (Mailchimp is free for under 500 contacts, and they were under 3,000 — they paid $12/month after going paid). The time cost: 20 minutes per week.

Contrast that with the Instagram content calendar that took four hours a week and generated maybe $200/month in traceable revenue.

**The takeaway for small business owners:** Start collecting emails today. Put a sign on your counter. Add a checkbox to your Square or Booksy checkout. Offer a small discount for signing up. The return on that list compounds over time in a way that social media cannot match.

## The Local Ad Strategy That Works When You Have Less Than $1,000/Month

Most small business owners I meet either don't run ads at all (scared of wasting money) or run them wrong (blowing through budget without tracking). There's a middle path that works.

I tested this with a pet store in Chicago's Logan Square neighborhood. Monthly marketing budget: $700. Their goal: more foot traffic and more phone calls from people looking for specialty dog food they already stocked.

**Here's exactly what we did:**

1. **Google Local Services Ads:** Not Google Ads. Google Local Services. Different product. You pay per lead, not per click. We set a budget of $300/month, targeting "pet store Logan Square" and "dog food near me" within a 3-mile radius. Google verifies your business, and you only pay when someone calls or messages you through the ad.

2. **Facebook Ads with a specific radius:** We set a 2-mile radius (most pet owners won't drive farther than that for food), targeted people interested in "dog" and "pet supply" and "veterinary," and ran a simple ad: "We stock [brand name] specialty food — in stock now, no backorder." Budget: $200/month.

3. **A very basic landing page:** Not the website homepage. A single page that said "we have this food in stock, come get it." Mobile-optimized, because 80% of traffic came from phones.

**The results:**

- Google Local Services: $300 in spend generated 23 calls in the first month. 17 of those converted to a sale. Average sale value: $65. That's $1,105 in revenue from $300 in ad spend.
- Facebook: $200 in spend generated 140 link clicks to the landing page. The owner couldn't track exact in-store attribution, but she started asking every customer how they heard about the store. In the first two weeks, 8 people said "Facebook ad." Estimated revenue: $520.
- Total monthly ad spend: $500. Estimated additional revenue: $1,625.

The owner scaled this to $800/month over three months and hit $2,400 in attributed revenue. She still runs the same setup today. It's not sexy. It doesn't win awards. But it pays the rent.

**What doesn't work for small budgets:** Brand awareness campaigns, video ads without a direct offer, display ads, or any strategy that requires someone to sit through a 30-second commercial before they know what you're selling.

If you have under $1,000/month to spend on ads, your only job is to get someone to call or walk through your door. That's it. Don't get distracted by "building brand equity" at this stage.

## How to Set Up Simple Tracking That Doesn't Make You Want to Quit

The biggest objection I hear from small business owners about marketing is "I can't tell what's working." And half the time, they're right — because they haven't set up any tracking.

But you don't need a data analyst or a $500/month dashboard tool. You need two things:

1. **A call tracking number.** You can get one from CallRail or even Google Voice for free. Put a different phone number on your website, your Google Business Profile, and your Facebook ad. When a call comes in on that number, you know exactly where it came from.

2. **A spreadsheet.** I'm serious. A Google Sheet with columns for: date, channel, cost, leads (calls or form fills), and revenue attributed. Update it every Monday morning while you drink your coffee. Takes 10 minutes.

I had a client — a hair salon in San Diego — who was spending $400/month on Yelp ads and couldn't tell me if they were working. They'd been doing it for two years. That's $9,600 spent with zero measurement.

We set up a Google Voice number, put it on the Yelp ad, and told the receptionist to ask every caller how they found the salon for 30 days. Turns out, exactly 2 calls came from Yelp ads in that month. Both were price shoppers who didn't book. The other 140 calls? Google search, referrals, and walk-ins.

They turned off the Yelp ads, redirected that $400 to Google Local Services Ads, and saw 15 booked appointments in the first three weeks. At an average service value of $120, that's $1,800 in revenue.

Two years of spending without tracking. Thirty days of simple measurement fixed it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I have a $500/month marketing budget. Where should I put it first?**

Start with Google Business Profile optimization and Google Local Services Ads. Both are free or pay-per-lead. Spend $300 on Local Services Ads targeting your specific service and location. Use the remaining $200 on email list building — offer a discount for signups and send a weekly email. Do not spend a dollar on Facebook or Instagram ads until you have tested the lower-cost channels and know they work. I've seen this playbook generate $2,000+ in monthly revenue from a $500 budget when done right.

**Q: Is Yelp worth it for a small business?**

Depends on your category, but generally: no, not for paid advertising. Yelp's ad model is expensive and you're competing with bigger brands. However, having a claimed Yelp profile with good reviews and accurate information is important because people search there. Reply to every review, positive or negative, within 48 hours. Spend your money on Google and email before you spend it on Yelp.

**Q: How many social media platforms should I be on?**

One. Pick the platform where your actual customers are, not where you think they should be. If you run a hair salon, Instagram. If you run a plumbing business, Facebook. If you're a B2B consultant, LinkedIn. Do that one platform well — posting consistently, engaging with comments, telling actual stories — before you think about adding a second. I've seen business owners burn out trying to maintain five platforms and end up doing none of them well.

**Q: How long before I see results from marketing changes?**

If you're running ads correctly, you should see data within the first week. Calls and bookings should start within 10–14 days. Organic channels like SEO and email lists take longer — 60–90 days to build momentum. If you've been running something for 90 days with zero results, stop. Change the approach. Don't keep throwing money at something that isn't working just because a blog post told you to "trust the process."

**Q: Should I use a marketing agency or do it myself?**

If you have the time and the willingness to learn basic ad platforms, do it yourself with a budget under $1,000/month. The tools are not that complex. If you're spending $2,000+/month or you genuinely don't have 3–4 hours per week to manage marketing, hire an agency — but vet them hard. Ask for case studies with real numbers from businesses your size. If they give you "it depends on your goals," walk away. Hire someone who tells you what they've done and what it cost.

**Q: What's the one thing most small business owners get wrong about marketing?**

They try to do too many things at once. They start a podcast, run TikTok ads, post on Instagram, send emails, attend networking events, and print flyers — all in the same month. Nothing gets done well, nothing gets measured, and they quit within 12 weeks because "marketing doesn't work." The businesses that actually grow pick one or two channels, do them consistently for six months, measure everything, and then add more. The boring approach wins.
