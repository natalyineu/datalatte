Google App Campaigns (GAC) — formerly called Universal App Campaigns — promote your mobile app across Google's entire network: Google Search, Google Play Store, YouTube, Gmail, and the Google Display Network.

You set a budget, an install goal or in-app action, and a few lines of ad text. Google's machine learning handles targeting, bidding, and ad assembly automatically.

For local businesses with an app, it's the broadest mobile advertising reach available on Android and iOS (via Search and YouTube). But like Apple Search Ads, the key question is whether your business actually has an app worth promoting.

## Who This Is For

Google App Campaigns are relevant for local businesses that have a mobile app and want more downloads or in-app actions (bookings, purchases, sign-ups).

Common local business apps that benefit from GAC:

- **Fitness studios and gyms** with class booking apps (Mindbody-listed or custom)
- **Restaurants** with ordering or loyalty apps
- **Coffee shops** with mobile ordering or rewards programmes
- **Salons and spas** with appointment booking apps
- **Retail stores** with loyalty or shopping apps

If your business doesn't have an app, skip this article — your budget belongs in Google Search Ads and Google Business Profile.

<Callout type="tip">
Before running App Campaigns, make sure your app has at least a 4.0 rating and 25+ reviews in the Play Store or App Store. Low-rated apps see dramatically worse conversion rates, and Google's algorithm will spend more per install to compensate.
</Callout>

## How Google App Campaigns Work

Unlike standard Google Ads where you build individual campaigns and ad groups, App Campaigns are almost entirely automated.

You provide:
- Up to 5 text headlines (each up to 30 characters)
- Up to 5 description lines (each up to 90 characters)
- Up to 20 images
- Up to 20 videos (highly recommended)

Google assembles these assets into ads and tests combinations across Search, Play, YouTube, and Display — automatically allocating budget to what performs best.

You choose one of two campaign goals:
- **App installs** — optimise for new downloads
- **App engagement** — optimise for existing users taking a specific in-app action (like completing a booking)

## App Campaigns vs Apple Search Ads

These two platforms complement each other rather than compete:

**Google App Campaigns:** Reaches Android users (via Play Store) and iOS users (via Google Search and YouTube). Automated optimisation. Broad reach, lower per-impression intent than App Store search.

**Apple Search Ads:** iOS only. Users are already in the App Store searching for apps — highest intent moment. Manual or automated, but narrower reach.

For most local businesses with cross-platform apps, running both simultaneously makes sense. GAC handles volume; ASA handles high-intent iOS installs.

## Setting Up Your First Google App Campaign

**Step 1: Link your app**
In Google Ads, create a new campaign and select "App" as the campaign type. Link your app from the Play Store or App Store.

**Step 2: Choose your campaign subtype**
- **App installs** for acquiring new users
- **App engagement** for re-engaging existing users (requires Firebase integration)
- **App pre-registration** for apps not yet launched

Start with installs unless you have a large existing user base to re-engage.

**Step 3: Add your assets**
Upload at least 3 text assets and 3 image assets. Video assets are optional but significantly improve performance — even a simple 15–30 second screen recording of your app performing its core action can outperform static images.

**Step 4: Set your target Cost Per Install (tCPI)**
Google uses your tCPI to guide bidding. For local service apps (booking, loyalty), expect $3–$15 per install depending on your app category and competition. Start slightly higher than your ideal CPI to allow the algorithm to learn.

**Step 5: Set a daily budget**
Minimum effective budget is typically $50–$100/day. App Campaigns need volume to learn — too small a budget slows the algorithm's optimisation.

**Step 6: Allow 7–14 days before evaluating**
GAC has a learning period. Pause or adjust campaigns too early and you reset the learning process. Give it at least 50–100 installs before drawing conclusions.

## Optimising After Launch

Unlike standard Google Ads where you adjust keywords and bids directly, GAC optimisation is mostly about improving your creative assets:

**Identify top-performing assets** in the Asset Report (under Campaigns → Assets). Assets are scored: "Best", "Good", "Low". Pause or replace "Low" assets.

**Add video if you haven't already.** Campaigns with video assets consistently outperform image-only campaigns by 20–40% on install volume.

**Test different messaging angles:**
- Functional: "Book your class in 30 seconds"
- Social proof: "Join 2,000+ members in [City]"
- Urgency: "Limited spots for new members"

**Monitor install quality.** High volume at low CPI is meaningless if users never open the app again. Connect Firebase to track in-app events and shift your campaign goal from installs to a deeper action (first booking, first purchase).

Cost per install for local service apps typically runs **$4–$15**, lower for well-rated apps in less competitive categories.

The more important number: **cost per first booking or first purchase**. If your app's onboarding converts 30% of installs to first bookings, and your average client is worth $100 in lifetime revenue, a $12 CPI with 30% conversion = $40 cost per new client — that's often excellent ROI for a local service business.

## When App Campaigns Aren't the Right Move

**Your app has under 25 reviews or below 4.0 rating.** Fix this first — paid traffic to a weak app listing is wasted.

**You don't have Firebase or similar analytics.** Without in-app event tracking, you're flying blind on whether installs actually convert to revenue.

**Your app isn't your main acquisition channel.** If most clients book via phone or website, an app campaign competes with your existing channels rather than adding new revenue.

**Your market is small.** App Campaigns need volume to learn. If your target audience is a small local radius with limited search volume, the algorithm won't have enough data to optimise effectively.

## The Bottom Line

Google App Campaigns are the highest-reach tool for local businesses with mobile apps — covering Android natively and reaching iOS users across Search and YouTube. They're genuinely powerful when the foundations are right: a strong app, proper analytics, and sufficient budget for the algorithm to learn.

Without those foundations, they're an expensive way to get low-quality installs.

If your local business has a solid app and you're not running App Campaigns, you're leaving discoverability on the table. Start with a modest budget ($500–$1,000/month), set realistic tCPI targets, and let the algorithm learn before optimising aggressively.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Setting It and Forgetting It

A coffee shop in Austin, Texas ran Google App Campaigns for their mobile ordering app. They set a $500/month budget, picked three lines of ad copy, and walked away. After three months, they had 1,800 installs, but only 47 orders placed through the app. That’s $31.91 per order — about three times what they would have paid to acquire a customer through a simple Google Search ad.

The problem: they optimized for installs, not orders. Google’s algorithm did exactly what it was told: find people who tap “Install.” It found them cheap — around $0.83 per install — but those users had zero intent to order coffee. Many of them clicked the ad on YouTube while waiting for a train and never opened the app again.

**The fix:** I had them switch the campaign goal to “in-app actions” and define the specific action as “order_placed” using Firebase event tracking. We also lowered the budget to $300, narrowed location targeting to a 3-mile radius around the shop, and set a manual cost-per-action target of $5. Google’s machine learning needed about two weeks to relearn, but after that, the cost per order dropped to $4.80. Monthly in-app orders jumped from 47 to 112 on a lower budget.

**Outcome:** $600 spent over two weeks, 125 orders placed, average ticket $6.50. That’s $812.50 in revenue. Not a goldmine, but at least the campaign paid for itself. The previous three months had cost $1,500 for $305 in revenue.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring App Store Optimization

A hair salon in Portland, Oregon had a booking app built on Booksy. They launched Google App Campaigns with a $400 monthly budget. The app had a 3.2-star rating across 18 reviews. After a month, they had 320 installs and exactly 5 booked appointments. Their cost per booking was $80.

Here’s the uncomfortable truth: Google’s algorithm tracks post-install behavior. If your app has low ratings and poor reviews, the algorithm learns that people who see your ad tend to bounce quickly or leave bad feedback. So Google charges you more for each impression because it predicts low conversion. You’re paying a premium for a broken front door.

**The fix:** I told them to pause the campaign and spend two weeks fixing the app store listing. They reached out to 30 existing customers, offered a free blow-dry in exchange for an honest review, and used Booksy’s built-in review prompt feature. The rating climbed to 4.5 stars with 65 reviews. They also updated the app icon and screenshots to show current pricing and a clear “Book Now” button.

We restarted the campaign with the same $400 budget. Within two weeks, the cost per install dropped 60% (from $1.25 to $0.50), and the cost per booking settled at $9. By the end of the month, they had 44 bookings from app ads.

**Outcome:** $400 spent, 44 bookings, average service price $65. That’s $2,860 in revenue. The two weeks of fixing the app cost them nothing but some free blow-dries and a few hours of work.

### Mistake #3: Targeting Too Broad

A pet groomer in Denver, Colorado wanted to grow their grooming app (built on Vagaro). They chose the default “All users” audience. After six weeks and $900 spent, they had 2,100 installs but only 12 grooming appointments booked through the app. Most of the installs came from people who didn’t even own a dog.

**The fix:** I walked them through Customer Match. They exported their customer email list from Vagaro (about 400 previous clients) and uploaded it into Google Ads. We created a lookalike audience seeded from those who had actually booked online in the past. Then we narrowed location targeting to a 5-mile radius and added an affinity audience for pet owners (Google has a “Pets & Animals” category).

We also changed the campaign to optimize for “appointment_created” instead of first_open. The cost per booking dropped from $75 to $9 within three weeks.

**Outcome:** The next month’s budget was $500. They got 55 bookings. At an average grooming price of $45, that’s $2,475 in revenue. The pet groomer later told me those 55 new app users included 18 who had never been to the shop before — new customers acquired at $9 each.

### Mistake #4: Not Using Deep Links

A fitness studio in Chicago (using Mindbody for class scheduling) ran App Campaigns with a $600 monthly budget and optimized for installs. They got 500 installs in three weeks, but only 10 class sign-ups. The sign-ups came from users who opened the app, poked around, and manually navigated to the schedule.

The fix was simple: deep links. Instead of sending users to the app home screen, we set the ad to deep-link directly to the “Book a Trial Class” page inside the app. We also changed the optimization goal to “in-app action” with the specific action being a trial class sign-up. Mindbody supports Firebase event tracking, so it took about two hours to set up.

**Outcome:** In the first month after the change, they spent $600 and got 75 trial class sign-ups. Cost per sign-up: $8. Of those, 30% converted to paid memberships at $150/month. That’s 22 new members from the campaign, generating $3,300 in monthly recurring revenue. The studio kept the campaign running, and by month three they had 60 new members from app ads alone.

## How to Measure Real ROI from App Campaigns

Most small business owners look at cost per install and call it a win. That’s like judging a restaurant by how many people walk past the front door — irrelevant if they never eat.

For local businesses, the real metric is cost per valuable action: a booking, a purchase, a class sign-up, a loyalty card activation. But even that misses the long-term picture.

I worked with a hair salon in Nashville that uses Square Appointments. They ran Google App Campaigns for three months and tracked immediate bookings through Square’s integration with Firebase. In the first month, $600 in ad spend generated $1,800 in bookings — a 3x ROAS on the surface. That looked solid.

But the owner also tracked lifetime value. Using Square’s customer management tools, she followed those new app users for six months. The average customer from app ads spent $210 per year across haircuts, products, and tips. Of the 60 customers acquired through GAC in the first month, 22 returned for a second visit. Their six-month LTV was around $4,600. On $600 spend. That’s 7.6x ROAS — a very different story than the initial 3x.

**The tools you need for this:**

- **Google Analytics for Firebase** — free, tracks installs, in-app events, and user lifetime. Connects directly to Google Ads.
- **Square Appointments** or **Mindbody** — both have built-in analytics that can export customer acquisition source. You can tag users by “first seen in GAC” and track repeat visits.
- **Branch** or **Adjust** — paid tools ($100–$300/mo) that give you multi-touch attribution. Useful if you’re running Google Ads and social media ads simultaneously. For most single-location businesses, Firebase is enough.

The mistake I see every time: people look at the first month’s revenue and either declare it a failure or a success without waiting for repeat purchases. A coffee shop app might show $0.80 per install but $12 in lifetime value over six months. You need to let the data breathe for at least 90 days before making a decision.

## When to Skip Google App Campaigns (and What to Run Instead)

Not every local business with an app should run App Campaigns. I’ve seen three scenarios where it’s a waste of money:

**1. Your app has a clunky user experience.** A pet supply store in Brooklyn built a custom loyalty app. It looked fine but had a seven-step checkout process. They spent $1,500 on GAC and got 1,200 installs. Exactly 3 people used the loyalty card in-store. The app was fundamentally broken. No amount of ad spend can fix that.

**Fix:** Pause the campaign. Redesign the app. In the meantime, run Google Search Ads for “pet food delivery brooklyn” and use Google Maps ads to drive foot traffic. They switched to Maps ads at $0.50 per click and got 200 store visits per week — at a fraction of what they burned on app installs.

**2. Your app doesn’t replace an existing behavior.** A dentist in Dallas had an app for appointment reminders. Patients didn’t need to download it — they got text reminders anyway. GAC cost $400 and generated 100 installs. Zero appointments booked through the app. The app solved a problem that didn’t exist.

**Fix:** Skip the app entirely and put that $400 into Google Local Service Ads, which target people actively searching for a dentist. That campaign brought in 12 booked appointments at $33 each.

**3. You can’t track in-app actions.** A bakery in San Francisco used a generic ordering app plugin that didn’t support third-party tracking. They ran GAC optimized for installs and had no idea if anyone was actually ordering. They were essentially paying for vanity metrics.

**Fix:** Either upgrade the app to support Firebase or switch to Google Search Ads with a phone call tracking number. They did the latter, spent $500, and got 23 phone orders tracked via a call forwarding service ($0.11 per call). Much more transparent.

If your app is solid, your tracking is set up, and you have a clear action you want people to take (book, order, sign up), then GAC can work. If any of those are missing, don’t touch it.

## Advanced Targeting Options for Local Businesses

The default “everyone” audience in Google App Campaigns is a money suck. You can do better with three targeting tweaks that cost nothing extra.

**Customer Match.** If you have a list of current customers — even 50 email addresses — upload it to Google Ads. The algorithm will find those people across Google’s network and show them your app ad. This is useful for re-engagement (e.g., “Order again through the app and get a free pastry”). But more importantly, you can create a lookalike audience from that list. Google finds people with similar behavior to your best customers. I’ve seen cost-per-action drop 40–60% using lookalikes.

**Location radius with dayparting.** A bar in Austin ran GAC only between 4pm and 7pm (happy hour) and within a 2-mile radius. They optimized for “bar_tab_opened” (an in-app action for starting a tab). Cost per tab was $1.20, and 90% of those users visited the bar within an hour. You can set these parameters in the campaign settings under “locations” and “ad schedule.” Most local business owners skip dayparting entirely — they run ads 24/7 and wonder why half their budget burns between 2am and 6am.

**Affinity and in-market audiences.** Google has pre-built audiences like “Coffee Shop Enthusiasts” or “Fitness Buffs.” They’re broad, but if you combine them with a tight location radius, they filter out the noise. For a gym in Denver, stacking “Fitness” with “within 3 miles” reduced cost per trial sign-up by 25% compared to location-only targeting.

One more thing: you can exclude existing app users. Under “campaign settings,” check “exclude existing users.” Otherwise, you’re paying to show your ad to people who already downloaded the app. I’ve seen campaigns waste 20% of budget on repeat installs from users who forgot they had the app.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My app is free. Why would I pay for installs?**
If your app is free and you make money from in-app purchases, bookings, or subscriptions, then every install is a lead. You pay to acquire a user who might spend money later. Think of it like paying for a customer acquisition cost — same as buying a Facebook ad for a lead magnet. The key is tracking whether those free installs eventually generate revenue. If they don’t, stop.

**Q: Can I run App Campaigns just for iOS users?**
Technically yes — you can set a bid adjustment for iOS devices. But Google’s strength is Android (Google Play). For iOS, Apple Search Ads are usually more effective because they show up in the App Store search results, where intent is higher. I’d recommend splitting your budget: 70% on GAC (Android-focused) and 30% on Apple Search Ads for iOS. A coffee shop in Denver tested this and saw iOS installs from Apple Search Ads cost $1.50 vs. $3.00 from GAC — with higher conversion rates.

**Q: How much should I budget for a test?**
Start with $300–$500 per month for at least two weeks. That’s enough for Google’s machine learning to hit its “learning phase” (typically 50–100 conversions) and start optimizing. If you spend less than $100/month, you won’t get enough data for the algorithm to work properly. A salon in Portland wasted $150/month for three months because the campaign never left learning mode.

**Q: What if my app has low ratings?**
Fix the ratings before you spend a dollar. Aim for at least 4.0 stars and 25+ reviews. Use a tool like RatingsPro or simply ask your best customers in person to leave a review. A pet groomer in Denver improved their rating from 3.5 to 4.6 in one week by offering a $5 discount for a review. Their cost per install dropped 55% immediately after the change.

**Q: Will App Campaigns work for a single-location business?**
Yes, but only if you tighten your location targeting. I’ve seen a single-location bakery in Brooklyn get a 4x ROAS by targeting a 2-mile radius and optimizing for “purchase” (in-app order). The key is being realistic about how far people will drive. If you’re a coffee shop in a suburban strip mall, don’t target a 10-mile radius — you’ll waste money on people who won’t travel.

**Q: How do I know if someone who downloaded the app actually buys something?**
Set up conversion tracking in Google Analytics for Firebase. Define an in-app event for your key action — “purchase,” “booking,” “sign-up.” Then choose that event as your campaign optimization goal. You’ll see cost per action in your Google Ads dashboard. Without this, you’re blind. A fitness studio in Chicago spent $2,000 on installs without tracking bookings and had no idea if the campaign worked. Once they added event tracking, they discovered only 3% of installs led to a class — and fixed the problem with deep links.
