## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Inconsistent NAP Data Across the Web

A wedding photographer in Portland, Oregon — let’s call her studio "Willamette Valley Weddings" — came to me after six months of zero organic leads. She had a gorgeous website, solid portfolio, and decent reviews. Her Google Business Profile listed her address as "1234 SE Hawthorne Blvd, Suite 200." Her website footer said "1234 SE Hawthorne Blvd. Ste 2." Her Yelp page said "1234 Hawthorne Blvd."

Google’s local algorithm treats these as three different locations. You get zero local ranking credit when your NAP (name, address, phone) doesn’t match across platforms. She was invisible for "Portland wedding photographer" despite having better work than half the people ranking above her.

We spent four hours cleaning up every mention of her business across 30+ directories, citation sites, and social profiles. Used Moz Local and manually checked the rest. We made everything match her GBP exactly — down to "Suite 200" vs "Ste 2."

Within 60 days, her local pack ranking moved from page 4 to position 3. Her monthly inquiry calls went from zero to seven. At her average booking value of $3,800 per wedding, that’s $26,600 in monthly pipeline from a one-time cleanup that cost her $300.

**Fix:** Pull your business name, phone, and address from your GBP. Copy and paste that exact text into every single place your business appears online. Don’t abbreviate anything. Don’t change the format. Do this before you spend a dollar on anything else.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring the "Services" Section on Google Business Profile

A newborn photographer in Austin, Texas, was spending $500/month on Google Ads with mediocre results. She was getting clicks but hardly any bookings. I looked at her GBP. Her services section was blank. Not empty of content — the entire section didn’t exist.

Google uses your GBP services to match your business to specific searches. When someone searches "newborn photographer Austin," Google checks if your profile lists newborn photography as a service. If it doesn’t, you’re less relevant. Period.

We added every photography service she offered as separate line items: newborn photography, maternity photography, family portraits, cake smash sessions, milestone photography. For each one, we wrote a 2-3 sentence description with pricing ranges and what clients could expect.

Three weeks later, her organic calls doubled. She reduced her Google Ads spend to $200/month because organic leads were coming in faster than she could book them. Her monthly revenue from organic local search went from roughly $1,200 to $3,400.

**Fix:** Go to your GBP dashboard. Click "Services." Add every service you offer as a separate entry. Don’t lump everything under "photography." Be specific. If you shoot weddings, elopements, and engagement sessions — list all three. Google reads specificity as authority.

### Mistake #3: Treating Reviews Like a Passive Asset

A commercial photographer in Denver, Colorado, had 47 reviews on his GBP with a 4.8 average. Decent numbers, right? He was still getting beat by competitors with fewer reviews but higher engagement. The difference? He never responded to a single one.

Google measures review responsiveness as a ranking signal. Not just whether you reply, but how fast and how often. He had 12 unanswered reviews, some sitting for months. One negative review from a client who booked the wrong date sat unanswered for eight weeks. That was a red flag to both Google and potential clients.

I spent one hour with him writing reply templates. Thank the positive reviewers by name, mention something specific from their session, and invite them back. For the negative review, we apologized publicly, explained the miscommunication, and offered a resolution. It didn’t delete the review — nothing can — but it showed future clients that he handles problems professionally.

Within 30 days, his GBP click-through rate increased by 40%. He also started asking every client for a review at the right moment — right after delivering edited photos, not two weeks later when excitement faded. His review count hit 85 in three months. His ranking for "Denver commercial photographer" went from position 6 to position 2.

**Fix:** Reply to every review within 48 hours. For positive reviews, write something personal — "Glad you loved the Golden Hour session at City Park, that light was magic." For negative reviews, apologize and offer to make it right. Then ask every client for a review at the peak of their happiness with your work.

### Mistake #4: Using the Same Location for Every Service Page

A real estate photographer in Nashville had a smart idea — create separate pages for "Nashville real estate photography" and "Franklin real estate photography" to target different towns. Smart, except he used his Nashville studio address on both pages.

Google sees a Franklin page with a Nashville address and gets confused. If you don’t have a physical presence in Franklin, you can’t rank there for local searches. Period. He was wasting time creating pages that would never perform.

We changed the Franklin page to target "Franklin, TN real estate photography services" as a service area page. No address. Just clear service area information and a phone number. We built local backlinks from Franklin business directories, added testimonials from Franklin clients, and wrote content specific to Franklin’s housing market.

Three months later, he ranked in the top 3 for "Franklin real estate photographer" even without a physical address there. His Nashville page kept ranking for Nashville. He added $1,800/month in Franklin bookings to his existing $4,200/month in Nashville work.

**Fix:** If you serve multiple cities, create separate pages for each one. Use a local address only if you have a physical location in that city. Otherwise, use a service-area setup with local content, testimonials, and backlinks. Don’t fake an address — Google will catch it and penalize you.

## How to Build Local Backlinks That Actually Move the Needle

Backlinks still matter for local SEO. But most photographers chase the wrong ones. You don’t need a link from Forbes or The New York Times. You need links from sites that Google considers locally authoritative for your area.

I worked with a pet photographer in Chicago who was stuck in position 7 for "Chicago pet photographer." She had solid GBP optimization and good reviews. What she lacked was local link authority. Her backlink profile was all generic directory spam — YellowPages, Hotfrog, that kind of junk.

We identified 20 high-value local link opportunities in Chicago:
- The Chicago Business Journal (event features)
- Local wedding blogs that accept vendor guest posts
- Community event pages (she donated photo sessions for shelter pet adoption events)
- Local chamber of commerce directory
- Vendor partnerships with Chicago pet stores and groomers

The donation strategy was the winner. She offered free mini sessions at three pet adoption events hosted by a local rescue organization. The rescue’s website had high domain authority and linked back to her site in the event recap. She also got a mention on the event’s Facebook page — not a direct SEO benefit, but it generated referral traffic.

In total, she secured 12 quality local backlinks over four months. Her ranking moved from position 7 to position 3 for "Chicago pet photographer." Organic traffic to her website increased by 180%. Her booking rate from website visitors — people who clicked through from search results — went up because the backlinks from trusted local sites built credibility. Visitors saw she was featured by the Chicago rescue organization and trusted her immediately.

**The real numbers:** She invested about 15 hours total across four months on link building. Zero dollars spent on outreach tools. Her monthly revenue from organic search went from roughly $2,100 to $5,800. That’s a return of about $150 per hour of work. Not bad for something most photographers ignore entirely.

**Tools I actually use for local link building:**
- Ahrefs (free version is fine) — check competitor backlinks to find sites that might link to you too
- Google Search: "best [your city] photographers" — find roundup posts and pitch yourself for inclusion
- Local event calendars — look for photography or business events where you can volunteer
- Chamber of commerce — if you’re not a member, consider joining. The directory link alone is worth the fee in some cities.

One warning: don’t pay for links. I’ve seen photographers spend $200-$500 on "premium directory listings" that are thinly veiled link schemes. Google penalized a client of mine for exactly this. Paid links from non-relevant sites will hurt you more than they help.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a physical studio address to rank for local searches?**
Not necessarily. You can rank as a service-area business without a public-facing address. Just set your GBP to "service area" mode and hide your address. You’ll still show up in local search results for the areas you serve. I’ve seen photographers in Nashville, Austin, and Portland rank in the top 3 without a physical studio. The trade-off is that you may rank slightly lower than businesses with verified physical locations. But if you serve clients on location, hiding your address is often better — it prevents people from showing up unannounced.

**Q: How long does it take to rank for "photographer near me" in a competitive city?**
If you’re starting from nothing — no GBP, no reviews, no citations — expect 3-6 months before you see consistent leads. If you already have a GBP with some reviews, 6-12 weeks is realistic for noticeable movement. For a city like New York or Los Angeles where everyone is a photographer, you’re looking at 6-12 months minimum. The photographers who succeed in competitive markets are the ones who build local relevance faster — reviews, backlinks, and service-area pages. In less competitive markets like Boise or Raleigh, you can hit page 1 in 4-8 weeks with consistent effort.

**Q: Should I pay for Yelp advertising as a photographer?**
Yelp ads work for some businesses but generally not for photographers. Yelp’s algorithm prioritizes advertisers in search results, but the click-through rates are low — typically under 2% for photography services. I’ve seen photographers spend $300/month on Yelp ads and get exactly two inquiries. Neither booked. Meanwhile, their GBP optimization was generating 10-15 inquiries per week for free. Test Yelp ads with $100 for one month. If you get a booking that covers the cost, scale it. If not, pull the money and put it into Google Ads or review generation instead.

**Q: Can I rank for multiple cities without having a studio in each one?**
Yes, but you need separate service-area pages for each city. Don’t use the same page for "Denver photographer" and "Boulder photographer." Create a page titled "Boulder, CO Photography Services" with local content, testimonials from Boulder clients, and local backlinks. You won’t rank as high as a business with a physical Boulder address, but you can absolutely get page 2 or page 1 for less competitive terms. I helped a photographer in Portland rank for "Vancouver, WA photographer" — a city 15 minutes away — using this exact strategy. Took 3 months and generated $8,000 in bookings.

**Q: Is it worth buying Google Ads for local photography searches?**
Depends on your city and competition. In expensive markets like San Francisco, "wedding photographer" can cost $15-25 per click. You need a high conversion rate to make that work. Start with $200/month and track everything. Use Google’s location targeting to show ads only within a 20-mile radius. Exclude cities you can’t realistically serve. I’ve seen photographers in mid-sized cities like Columbus, Ohio, get bookings for $8-12 per click — that’s profitable if your average booking is $2,500+. Set a bid cap, use exact-match keywords ("Austin wedding photographer" not "photographer"), and watch your cost per conversion like a hawk.

**Q: How many reviews do I need to rank on the first page?**
There’s no magic number. I’ve seen a photographer in Chicago with 22 reviews rank above a competitor with 87 reviews. The difference was recency and relevance. The 22-review photographer had 12 reviews from the past 30 days — all mentioning "engagement photography" — and his reply rate was 100%. The 87-review photographer hadn’t replied to a review in six months and his last review was 45 days old. Google cares about velocity, not just volume. If you get 3-5 new reviews per month and reply to every single one within 48 hours, you’ll outrank someone with 100 reviews that went stale.
