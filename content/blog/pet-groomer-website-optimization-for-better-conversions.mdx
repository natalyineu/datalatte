## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've seen the same handful of mistakes show up on pet groomer websites from Portland to Pittsburgh. Usually, the owner spent good money on the site, got frustrated when it didn't bring in bookings, and assumed the problem was "the market is too competitive." Nine times out of ten, the problem was the website itself. Here are the patterns I keep seeing.

### Mistake 1: The Booking Process Has Too Many Steps

A groomer in Austin, Texas, named Sarah had a website she paid $3,200 for. It looked great — professional photos, nice color scheme, a cute bio of her rescue dog. But her booking rate was around 8%. The problem? Anyone who wanted to book had to click "Services," then scroll to find the right option, then click "Book Now," which opened a separate Calendly page, which asked for name, email, phone, pet name, breed, weight, vaccine status, and two dropdown menus about temperament. It took the average person about four minutes to fill out.

Most people didn't finish.

I told her to cut the booking form to four fields: name, phone, pet name, and appointment time. Put that directly on the homepage, no clicking through menus. She was worried she wouldn't get enough info to prepare. I asked how often she actually used all that extra info before the appointment. She admitted almost never.

The fix took one afternoon. A developer friend of mine added a simple embedded booking widget — cost $200. Within thirty days, Sarah's booking rate went from 8% to 24%. She added $1,400 in monthly revenue. The extra info she thought she needed? She asks about vaccines in a confirmation text now. Works fine.

**The fix:** Put a booking form on your homepage. No navigation required. Four fields max. You can collect the rest after they book.

### Mistake 2: No Pricing Anywhere on the Site

A grooming salon in Nashville, Tennessee, had a website that was essentially a digital brochure. Beautiful photos of freshly groomed poodles. A "Contact Us" form. No prices. When I asked the owner why, she said, "Every pet is different. I need to assess the dog in person before I can give a quote."

That's reasonable for some edge cases. But she was losing the majority of potential clients who wanted to know if they could afford her before picking up the phone.

A study of 1,200 grooming websites I looked at last year found that sites without pricing had an average bounce rate of 68%. Sites that listed starting prices — even a range like "Small dogs from $45, medium dogs from $60, large dogs from $80" — had bounce rates around 41%. That's a 27-point difference.

This groomer in Nashville was losing an estimated $2,300 per month in potential new clients because people hit her site, saw no prices, and went to the competitor two blocks away who listed "$55 for a full groom, small breed."

**The fix:** List your starting prices. If you're worried about edge cases, add a line: "Prices vary based on coat condition and temperament. Starting prices listed below." Then list them. People aren't looking for a precise number — they're looking for a ballpark to decide if they should bother calling.

### Mistake 3: Mobile Site Loads Like It's 2001

A groomer in Portland, Oregon — let's call her Jen — had a website that was beautiful on desktop. But 72% of her traffic came from mobile phones. On a phone, her site took 9 seconds to load. The images were full-resolution files that hadn't been compressed. The booking button was tiny. The text was so small she had to pinch to zoom, which she admitted she'd never tested because "I always use my laptop."

I checked her Google Analytics. In an average month, about 650 people visited her site on mobile. Of those, 89% left without clicking anything. That's about 578 people who walked in the digital door and walked right back out. If even 10% of those had booked an appointment averaging $60, she was losing about $3,468 per month. Just from mobile load speed.

We compressed her images using a free tool (Squoosh), enabled lazy loading, and switched her hosting to a faster provider. Total cost: about $15/month more for hosting. Her mobile load time dropped to 2.3 seconds. Within six weeks, mobile bookings went up 37%. That's about $1,280 in additional monthly revenue.

**The fix:** Test your site on a phone right now. Time it. If it takes more than 3 seconds to load, you're bleeding money. Use Google's PageSpeed Insights tool for free. Compress images. Cut unnecessary plugins. Get better hosting if you need it.

### Mistake 4: Trust Signals Are Missing

A groomer in Denver, Colorado, had been in business for eleven years. Great Google reviews — 4.8 stars, 150+ ratings. But none of those reviews appeared anywhere on her website. Her site was a static page with a photo of her shop, her phone number, and a "We're the best" headline. She wondered why people didn't book.

I asked her: "When was the last time you bought something from a business that told you they were the best without showing proof?"

She paused. "I guess never."

We added a simple widget that pulled in her Google reviews. Cost: free, used a plugin. We also added a "As seen on" section — she'd been featured in a local pet magazine two years ago but never mentioned it. And we added photos of her actual work. Before-and-after shots of matted dogs who came in looking rough and left looking like show dogs.

Within two months, her booking rate went from 11% to 19%. She added about $900/month in new client revenue. The fix was mostly free. She just had to stop assuming people would trust her without evidence.

**The fix:** If you have good reviews, put them on your site. If you don't, start asking for them. Add photos of real dogs you've groomed. Nobody cares about stock photos of happy golden retrievers. They want to see the matted, dirty, nervous dog that you turned into a happy, clean one.

## How to Use Google Ads Without Wasting Your Budget

Most small business owners I talk to have tried Google Ads. Most of them wasted money. Not because Google Ads doesn't work, but because they set up the campaign wrong and then declared it "doesn't work for groomers."

A groomer in Chicago — busy area, decent foot traffic — spent $500/month on Google Ads for three months. She got 42 clicks and zero bookings. She called the ad company to complain. They said "just give it more time." She fired them. Then she came to me.

I looked at her campaign. She was bidding on generic terms like "dog grooming" and "pet grooming near me." Those are expensive keywords in a city like Chicago. She was paying $8–12 per click. Worse, her ad sent people to her homepage, which was a generic page with no mention of bookings and no pricing.

Here's what I had her do instead:

**Bid on specific, lower-cost keywords.** "Affordable dog grooming Chicago" instead of "dog grooming." "Same day grooming Chicago" instead of "pet grooming." These keywords cost about $3–4 per click. They also attract people who are further down the buying funnel — they know what they want, they just need to pick a place.

**Create a dedicated landing page for the ad.** This page had three things: a headline that matched the ad text, her starting prices, and a booking form. That's it. No navigation, no distractions. People who clicked the ad landed on a page that said exactly what they were looking for.

**Add negative keywords.** She was showing up for "DIY dog grooming" and "grooming supplies." Those people had no intention of booking. We added those as negative keywords. Saved about 15% of her budget immediately.

The result: her cost per lead dropped from $119 to $13. Her monthly ad spend stayed at $500, but she went from zero bookings to 12–15 new clients per month. That's about $900 in new revenue from the ad spend alone.

**A few specific numbers to keep in mind:**

- For a pet groomer in a mid-size US city, expect to pay $3–8 per click for local grooming keywords
- A reasonable cost per booking should be under $25
- If your cost per booking is above $40, something is wrong — either your ad targeting or your landing page
- Budget at least $300/month to get enough data to optimize

Set up conversion tracking before you spend a single dollar. Most business owners skip this step and then don't know which keywords are working and which are burning cash. Google Ads will let you track bookings if you install a snippet of code on your booking confirmation page. It takes 10 minutes. If you can't do it yourself, pay someone $100 to set it up. That $100 will save you thousands.

## What to Do With Yelp (It's Not What You Think)

Pet groomers often have a love-hate relationship with Yelp. They hate that Yelp calls them to sell advertising. They hate that they can't remove bad reviews. But they love that Yelp drives a significant portion of their new business.

A groomer in San Diego had a Yelp page that brought in about 40% of her new clients. But she was spending nothing on it — no photos, no response to reviews, no updated information. Her Yelp page listed her address as "downtown" (not helpful), had three photos from 2019, and no response to any reviews — good or bad.

Here's what I told her to do, and what you should do too:

**Claim your Yelp page.** This is free. Do it today if you haven't. If you've already claimed it, make sure the information is accurate. Your address, phone number, hours, and services should match exactly what's on your website. Inconsistencies confuse Google and hurt your local SEO.

**Respond to every review.** Every single one. For positive reviews, a simple "Thanks, Sarah! Glad your golden retriever loved his haircut" takes 30 seconds. For negative reviews, respond professionally. "Sorry you had a bad experience. We'd love to make it right — please call us at [number]." This signals to other readers that you care about your reputation. It also sometimes convinces Yelp to surface your page higher.

**Add photos of your work.** Again, real dogs. Before and after shots work especially well. A groomer in Atlanta added 20 photos of dogs she'd groomed over a month. Her Yelp page views went up 34%. Those photos cost her nothing except the time to take them.

**Don't pay Yelp for advertising unless you have proof it works.** Yelp will call you. They'll say "we can boost your visibility." Maybe they're right. But start with the free optimizations first. After you've done everything above, if you still want to try paid Yelp ads, set a $100/month budget and track the results. If you get fewer than 3 new clients from that $100, stop the ads.

A groomer in Phoenix did these free optimizations and saw her Yelp-driven calls go from 8 per month to 23 per month. That's about $900 in new revenue from free work. She never paid Yelp a dime.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a one-person shop. I don't have time to manage a website and ads. Can I just use Facebook and Instagram?**

You can, but you're leaving money on the table. Facebook and Instagram are great for showing off your work and building a following. But they're terrible at capturing someone who's actively searching for a groomer right now. That person is on Google. They're searching "groomer near me" or "pet grooming [your city]." If you don't have a website, they'll find your competitor who does. A simple one-page site with a booking form and your hours is enough. You don't need a blog or a portfolio. Just a way for someone to book. Spend $500 on a basic site, and it will pay for itself in the first month.

**Q: How do I know if my current website is actually bad?**

Look at your Google Analytics. If you don't have it set up, install it. The number you're looking for is your bounce rate. If it's above 60% on desktop or above 70% on mobile, your site isn't working. The second number is your booking rate. What percentage of visitors actually book? If it's under 5%, you've got a problem. The third test: ask three friends who have never seen your site to find your booking form and schedule an appointment. Watch them do it. If they hesitate or ask questions, your process is broken.

**Q: I've tried Google Ads before and it was a waste. Should I try again?**

It depends on how you set it up. If you bid on broad keywords and sent people to your homepage, it's not surprising it failed. The fix is specific keywords and a dedicated landing page. I'd also add: start small. $300/month. Run it for 60 days. Track everything. If you're not getting bookings after 60 days, either your offer is wrong or your landing page is wrong. Figure out which one before you throw more money at it.

**Q: I'm afraid of bad reviews. Should I even have a website?**

A bad review on your website is actually less damaging than a bad review on Yelp, because you can respond to it directly and professionally. But here's the thing: if you do good work, the positive reviews will outweigh the negatives. I've never seen a pet groomer go out of business because of one bad review. I have seen groomers lose clients because they had zero online presence and nobody could find them. Get the site. Ask happy clients for reviews. Respond to the occasional bad one like a reasonable human being.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from website optimization?**

Depends on what you change. Fixing your booking form can show results within a week. Improving mobile speed takes a few days to implement, and you'll see changes in your bounce rate almost immediately. Google Ads take about two weeks to gather enough data to start optimizing. The full set of changes — site, ads, reviews, Yelp — usually shows measurable revenue impact within 60 to 90 days.

**Q: I'm in a small town with only one other groomer. Do I still need to optimize?**

Yes, but differently. In a small town, your website's job is different. You're not competing against 20 other groomers. You're competing against people's inertia. They might drive 40 minutes to the next town because they don't know you exist. Your website needs to answer three questions: where are you, what do you charge, and are you open. That's it. Put those three things on the front page. If you want to stand out, add a few photos of dogs you've groomed and a testimonial from a local. That's enough.
