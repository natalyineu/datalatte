## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating Glovo Like a Menu PDF

**The story:** A BBQ joint in Austin, Texas — let's call them Smoky's — spent three months frustrated that their Glovo orders averaged $18 while their in-store tickets ran $42. Owner Marcus blamed Glovo's fee structure. "The platform's eating my margin," he told me. He was half right.

**What actually went wrong:** Marcus had uploaded his dine-in menu verbatim. Same items, same descriptions, same portion sizes. But people ordering delivery want different things than people sitting at your picnic tables. His dine-in menu had too many "Build Your Own Plate" options that required five taps each on the app. Customers got annoyed and defaulted to the cheapest combo. His best-seller on Glovo was a $12 pulled pork sandwich that barely covered costs after Glovo's 27% commission.

**The fix:** We rebuilt his Glovo menu around delivery psychology. Three curated combos instead of thirty options. A "$25 Feed Two" box that included sides and drinks — higher perceived value, easier checkout. Items priced to account for the commission structure (targeted 30% food cost instead of his dine-in 28%). Added a $2 "Premium Cut" brisket upgrade that cost him $0.40.

**The outcome:** Average order value went from $18 to $31 in six weeks. His cost of goods stayed flat because the combos used cheaper cuts and bulk sides. Monthly Glovo revenue went from $4,200 to $8,900. Marcus's exact words: "I was giving them the wrong menu."

### Mistake #2: Running the Same Promo for Six Months

**The story:** A small bakery in Nashville — Sweet Stack — ran a "20% off your first order" promotion from March through August. Owner Rachel thought consistency would build loyalty. Instead, she was paying full commission on discounted orders for six straight months, and 74% of her Glovo customers were one-timers who never reordered.

**What actually went wrong:** Glovo's algorithm rewards new promos and limited-time offers. A static discount trains the platform's ranking system that your restaurant is only worth ordering from at 20% off. After month three, Rachel's organic (non-promo) orders dropped 40% because new customers saw her full prices as the "inflated" option. She was essentially paying to train customers to wait for a deal.

**The fix:** We killed the blanket discount cold. Replaced it with a rotating schedule: Monday-Thursday lunch boost (15% off orders over $25), weekend bundle deals (free cookie with any pastry order), and a "secret menu" item available only to repeat customers through Glovo's loyalty features. Two weeks of lower order volume, then things stabilized.

**The outcome:** Rachel stopped hemorrhaging $1,200/month in commission on discounted orders. Her repeat customer rate went from 26% to 41% over eight weeks. Average weekly revenue stayed the same ($5,400), but her net margin on Glovo improved from 3% to 11%. She told me she'd been "bleeding money on loyalty that didn't exist."

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the 10:00 AM Gap

**The story:** A coffee shop in Portland, Oregon — let's call it Third Wave — was open 7:00 AM to 6:00 PM but only received Glovo orders between 11:30 AM and 1:30 PM. Owner Jen assumed her customers just didn't order delivery from coffee shops. She was wrong.

**What actually went wrong:** Jen had her store hours set to "open" on Glovo at 7:00 AM, but her available items didn't change until 11:00 AM. So at 9:30 AM, a customer searching for "breakfast coffee + pastry" would see a generic lunch menu. Worse, Jen's prep time was set to 25 minutes because she was averaging lunch prep for sandwiches. A breakfast order of two coffees and a croissant took her 4 minutes to make, but customers saw a 25-minute wait and bounced.

**The fix:** Split the day into two menus on Glovo's backend. Breakfast menu (7:00–11:00 AM): coffee, pastries, breakfast sandwiches, with prep time set to 8 minutes. Lunch menu (11:00 AM–6:00 PM): sandwiches, salads, wraps, with prep time set to 18 minutes. Added a $6 "Morning Rush" combo (coffee + pastry) that cost her $1.80 to make.

**The outcome:** Breakfast orders went from zero to averaging 14 per day within three weeks. Those orders had a 68% margin because coffee and pastries are cheap. Monthly revenue increased by $2,800, and her overall Glovo ranking improved because she was now active across two meal periods instead of one. Jen's comment: "I literally lost a year of breakfast orders because I didn't think about time."

## Using Google Ads to Drive Glovo Orders (Not Wasting Budget)

Most restaurant owners I talk to think they can't run ads for delivery because Glovo doesn't offer a built-in ad platform like Grubhub or Uber Eats. That's true. But you don't need to advertise inside Glovo. You need to advertise for Glovo.

A hot chicken spot in Denver ran this play. They were doing fine on Glovo — about $6,000/month — but owner Sarah wanted to grow without cutting margins further. Here's what we did:

**Step one:** Created a Google Ads campaign targeting "hot chicken delivery Denver" and "best Nashville hot chicken near me" keywords. Budget: $500/month. Landing page: a simple one-pager showing their Glovo delivery area and a "Order on Glovo" button that linked directly to their restaurant page on the app.

**Step two:** Set up conversion tracking using Glovo's affiliate link with UTM parameters. This let Sarah see exactly which searches, ad copy, and times of day generated actual orders.

**Step three:** Tested three ad angles over four weeks: "Denver's Best Hot Chicken — Delivered," "Free Delivery on Your First Glovo Order," and "Better Than the Original — Try Our Hot Chicken."

**Results:** The "Best" angle won by a mile. Cost per acquisition: $4.20 per first-time order. Average order value from ad-driven customers: $34 (higher than her $27 average because these people searched specifically for hot chicken delivery — they wanted a meal, not a snack). Monthly spend stayed at $500. Revenue attributed to ads: $3,800 in month two, $4,100 in month three.

**The catch:** This only works if your Glovo profile is optimized (see the existing article). Sarah had already fixed her menu, so new customers found exactly what the ad promised. If your menu is a mess, ads will just accelerate the disappointment.

**Tool:** Google Ads with conversion tracking. Budget $300–$800/month depending on your city's keyword costs. Portland and Denver run cheaper than NYC or LA. Test for 30 days before scaling.

## Yelp + Glovo: The Partnership Nobody Talks About

Here's something most guides skip: Yelp is not a competitor to Glovo. Yelp is a customer acquisition channel for Glovo, if you use it right.

A pizzeria in Chicago — family-run, been around 40 years — was getting killed on delivery platforms. Their Yelp page had 4.2 stars and 300+ reviews, but they weren't directing any of that traffic to Glovo. Customers would find them on Yelp, see their menu photos, then call to order. The owner's wife was answering phones during dinner rush while Glovo orders sat idle.

**The fix:** We updated their Yelp business page to include their Glovo delivery link in three places:
1. The "Order Now" button (Yelp lets you link directly to a third-party delivery page)
2. The business description section
3. A pinned Yelp post: "Order delivery through Glovo — same menu, contactless drop-off"

**The outcome:** In eight weeks, Yelp-referred Glovo orders went from negligible to 23% of their total delivery volume. The owner stopped taking phone orders during peak hours. Average wait time dropped, Yelp reviews actually improved because delivery was faster, and their Glovo ranking climbed because the platform saw increased order frequency from their location.

**The numbers:** $0 spent on Yelp ads. Just redirected existing traffic. Monthly Glovo revenue increased $2,200. Phone order complaints dropped to near zero.

**Why this works:** A 2023 survey of 2,000 US diners found that 67% check Yelp before ordering delivery from an unfamiliar restaurant. If your Yelp page sends them to a dead end (a phone number they have to call), they go to the next option. If it sends them to your Glovo page with one click, you get the order.

**Tools:** Yelp for Business (free tier), Google Business Profile (same logic applies — add your Glovo link there too).

## Email and SMS Retargeting for Glovo Customers

This one requires a workaround because Glovo doesn't give you customer email addresses. But you can build the same system using order inserts and QR codes.

A ramen shop in NYC tried this. Owner Ken was frustrated that 80% of his Glovo customers never ordered again. He had no way to reach them. We designed a simple card that went into every Glovo bag: "Thanks for ordering! Scan this QR code to join our VIP list — get a free side on your next order."

**What happened next:** 14% scan rate. Not amazing, but enough. Over three months, Ken collected 340 email addresses and 280 phone numbers (the form offered both options). Then:

- **Welcome email** with a $5 off code for their next Glovo order (cost Ken $1.20 per redemption, got 22% conversion)
- **Weekly SMS blast** every Thursday: "Weekend ramen alert — order through Glovo by Sunday for a free soft-boiled egg" (cost: nothing, open rate: 68%, redemption rate: 8%)
- **Abandoned cart follow-up** (not possible natively, but Ken used Mailchimp's automation to send a "Haven't seen you in 14 days" email with a $3 off code)

**The numbers:** Ken spent $140 on card printing and $45/month on Mailchimp. In month one, retargeted customers generated $1,800 in additional revenue. By month three, his repeat customer rate on Glovo went from 20% to 38%. His cost per acquired email was $0.41. His ROI on the entire program: 12x in the first quarter.

**The caveat:** This requires you to actually pack those cards. Ken's staff forgot for the first two weeks. He put a laminated checklist by the packing station. After that, compliance hit 90%.

**Tools:** Mailchimp (free tier up to 500 contacts), QR code generator, cheap business card printing from VistaPrint or Moo.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I charge higher prices on Glovo than in-store to cover the commission?**

Yes, and you probably should. I've tested this across eight clients. The sweet spot is 15–20% above your in-store prices. Customers already expect delivery to cost more — they're paying for convenience, not menu parity. A Chicago burrito shop tested 25% markup: orders dropped 30%. Tested 15% markup: no noticeable drop, and their margin went from 4% to 11% on Glovo. Start at 15%, bump to 20% if volume holds.

**Q: Is it worth being on Glovo if I'm in a smaller city with fewer users?**

It depends on population density and how many of your competitors are already on the platform. A salon in Boise tested Glovo for three months. It failed — high delivery fees for customers, low order volume. A coffee shop in a dense neighborhood of Denver did fine. Check how many restaurants in your specific zip code are active on Glovo. If it's fewer than 20, the platform probably doesn't have enough delivery drivers or users to make it work in your area.

**Q: How do I handle bad reviews on Glovo?**

Respond publicly within 24 hours. Don't get defensive. Acknowledge the problem, apologize, offer a specific fix ("We've adjusted our prep time for that item"). Don't offer refunds in the public reply — tell them to contact you through Glovo's support. In my experience, 40% of customers will update or remove their bad review if you respond professionally and actually fix the issue.

**Q: Should I pause Glovo during slow hours?**

Yes, but do it strategically. Pause during hours when orders drop to zero for more than 90 minutes consistently. It's not worth keeping the kitchen open and staff paid for three lunch orders spread over two hours. A bakery in Portland paused Glovo between 2:00 and 4:00 PM when they averaged 0.8 orders per hour. Saved $380/month in labor. Just remember to schedule your menu availability accurately — don't leave customers hanging at 3:15 PM because you forgot to turn the app back on.

**Q: Can I use Glovo data to improve my physical restaurant?**

Absolutely. This is the hidden value. An Austin taco shop noticed their best-selling Glovo item was a vegetarian option that accounted for 8% of dine-in sales but 22% of delivery orders. They added a permanent vegetarian section to their physical menu. Sales of that category in-store doubled. Glovo data told them something their own POS couldn't: delivery customers were different from walk-ins.

**Q: Is it better to go exclusive with Glovo or use multiple delivery platforms?**

Generally, don't go exclusive unless Glovo offers you a significantly lower commission rate (I've seen deals at 18% instead of 27% for exclusivity). The risk: if Glovo's algorithm changes or their driver coverage drops in your area, you lose everything. Most successful restaurants I've worked with run Glovo and one other platform (Uber Eats or DoorDash depending on local market) and optimize each separately.
