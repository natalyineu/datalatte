## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting Everyone Within 50 Miles

A broker in Austin, Texas called me after burning through $4,200 in six weeks. He'd set his radius to 50 miles because he didn't want to miss anyone. What he got was clicks from teenagers searching "mortgage calculator" for a school project, someone in San Antonio who was just curious about rates, and a retiree in Round Rock who clicked his ad by accident.

**What went wrong:** He used broad match keywords without negative keywords and assumed "anyone near Austin wants to buy a home." Most of his budget went to people who had no intention of getting a mortgage in the next year.

**The fix:** I narrowed his radius to 15 miles. We added negative keywords like "refinance calculator," "amortization schedule," "rent to own," and "reverse mortgage." We switched to phrase match and exact match for terms like "buy a house in Austin" and "Austin mortgage pre-approval." I also set dayparting to run ads only between 8am and 8pm Monday through Saturday.

**The outcome:** His cost per click dropped from $8.50 to $3.20. His conversion rate went from 0.8% to 2.4%. Within 30 days, he had 11 qualified leads and closed two loans worth about $480,000 combined. His ad spend for that month was $1,800. The campaign was actually profitable for the first time.

### Mistake 2: Sending All Traffic to the Homepage

A mortgage team in Nashville was spending about $3,000 a month on Google Ads. They were getting clicks, but nobody was filling out their contact form. When I looked at where the traffic was going, it was their main website — the one that says "Welcome to Our Team" and has six navigation options.

**What went wrong:** Their homepage is designed for people who already know them. It has their story, their values, a photo of the office. It does not have a clear next step for someone who just searched "Nashville mortgage rates" and landed there. That prospect doesn't care about their story. They want to know rates or get pre-approved.

**The fix:** I built a dedicated landing page for each ad group. One for "first-time homebuyer Nashville" that talked about FHA loans and down payment assistance. One for "Nashville mortgage pre-approval" that skipped the introduction and went straight to a short form. No navigation bar. No links to other pages. Just the offer and a form.

We tested two versions with Unbounce. Version A had a headline that read "Get Pre-Approved for a Nashville Mortgage." Version B said "Nashville Home Loan Pre-Approval — Apply in 5 Minutes." Version B outperformed by 40%.

**The outcome:** Conversion rate went from 0.9% to 4.1%. Cost per lead dropped from $112 to $38. They spent $2,800 on ads that month and got 74 leads. They closed 8 loans, generating about $12,000 in commission revenue.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Phone Call Conversions

A broker in Portland, Oregon had been running Google Ads for eight months. He was tracking form submissions and email inquiries. But he told me he kept getting calls from people saying "I saw your ad." His analytics showed zero calls from ads.

**What went wrong:** He wasn't tracking phone calls. Google Ads has call extensions and call-only campaigns, but he had neither set up. His website had a phone number, but it wasn't clickable on mobile. About 40% of mortgage inquiries start with a phone call, especially for time-sensitive things like making an offer on a house. He was missing that entire channel.

**The fix:** I added call extensions to his search ads and set up call tracking through CallRail. We created a call-only campaign for mobile users searching "mortgage broker Portland" and "home loan specialist near me." I set the call-only campaign to only show during business hours and forward calls to his cell phone after hours.

We also swapped his website phone number for a trackable number so we could see which ads were generating calls versus direct visits.

**The outcome:** In the first month, he got 23 tracked calls from ads. Nine of those calls turned into appointments. Three closed into loans worth about $340,000 total. His overall cost per lead dropped because phone calls converted at a higher rate than form fills — form leads closed at 15%, phone leads closed at 39%.

### Mistake 4: Letting the Campaign Run Without Negative Keywords

A small team in Denver was spending $2,500 a month on a campaign targeting "mortgage broker Denver." Their click-through rate was decent, but they were spending $1,200 a month on clicks that never converted.

**What went wrong:** They never built a negative keyword list. People searching "mortgage broker salary," "mortgage broker course," "become a mortgage broker," and "mortgage broker jobs Denver" were clicking their ads. Each click cost $7-$10. They were paying for traffic from people who wanted to work *as* a mortgage broker, not hire one.

**The fix:** I pulled the search terms report and found 47 irrelevant queries they were paying for. I added them as negative keywords. I also added broad negatives for "salary," "job," "career," "training," "internship," "salary," "course," "certification," "license," "how to become," and "salary." Then I set the campaign to only show for searches containing "mortgage broker" plus a location modifier.

**The outcome:** Their wasted spend dropped to about $200 per month. Their overall ad spend went from $2,500 to $1,500 because they weren't bleeding money on irrelevant clicks. Their cost per lead went from $95 to $42. They started getting more qualified leads with less budget.

## Beyond Search Ads: Using Display and Retargeting for Mortgage Clients

Search ads are the bread and butter for mortgage brokers, but they have a blind spot: they only work when someone is actively searching. Most people don't search "mortgage broker" until they're already deep in the home-buying process. What about the people who are still in the "I wonder if I can afford a house" phase? Or the people who visited your site once and then disappeared?

That's where display ads and retargeting come in. They're not replacements for search. They're the top-of-funnel and mid-funnel support your search campaign needs.

### Display Ads for Awareness

I ran a test for a broker in Chicago who was frustrated that his search campaign wasn't generating enough leads. He was getting about 15 clicks a day, but that was it. We set up a display campaign targeting in-market audiences — specifically people Google identified as "actively researching mortgages" and "home buyers."

We used responsive display ads with three headlines: "Lock Your Rate Before It Changes," "Thinking About Buying a House in Chicago?" and "First-Time Homebuyer? Let's Talk." The images were simple — a photo of a couple looking at a house, a shot of a "sold" sign, and a graphic of interest rates trending down.

**Budget:** $500/month for display. That's not a big number, but display ads are cheap. We were paying $0.08 to $0.15 per impression and about $0.50 per click.

**Outcome:** In the first 60 days, the display campaign served 340,000 impressions and got 1,800 clicks. About 40 people signed up for a newsletter we set up through Mailchimp where the broker sent weekly tips about Chicago neighborhoods and mortgage rates. Three of those newsletter subscribers eventually converted into clients. Not huge numbers, but the cost per lead from display was $166 — higher than search, but these were people who didn't exist in his funnel before.

### Retargeting People Who Didn't Convert

The same broker was getting about 200 visitors a month to his landing page, but only 5-10 were filling out his form. That's a lot of people who were interested enough to click an ad and read his page, but left without taking action.

We set up a retargeting campaign through Google Ads that showed ads to anyone who visited his landing page but didn't convert. The ad said "Still Looking for a Mortgage in Chicago?" with a link back to the same landing page, but with a slightly different offer — "Get a Free Rate Quote."

**Budget:** $300/month for retargeting. Cost per click was higher than display at about $1.20, but the conversion rate was much better.

**Outcome:** The retargeting campaign brought back about 15% of the abandoned visitors. Of those, 12 people filled out the form. Three closed loans worth about $520,000 total. The retargeting campaign alone generated about $6,000 in commission revenue against a $300 ad spend.

### The Real Lesson

Display and retargeting work best when your search campaign is already running well. Don't start with them. Get your search campaign profitable first. Then layer in display to catch people who aren't searching yet. Then add retargeting to bring back the ones who slipped through.

I've seen brokers try to start with display because it's cheaper per click, and then wonder why nobody converts. Display ads have a 0.5% click-through rate on a good day. The people clicking them aren't ready to buy. You need the lower-funnel search ads to catch them when they actually hit "I need a mortgage now" mode.

## Budget Strategy: Where to Actually Put Your Money

Every mortgage broker I talk to asks some version of "How much should I spend?" The answer is never a fixed number, but I can tell you how to think about it based on what actually moves the needle.

### The $1,000 Minimum

If you're in a mid-sized city like Denver, Austin, or Nashville, you need at least $1,000 a month to make Google Ads work. Below that, the data is too thin to optimize. You'll get a few clicks, maybe one or two leads, and you won't know if the campaign is working or if you got lucky. With $1,000, you can run one solid search campaign with 10-15 keywords and have enough data to make decisions after 30 days.

### Where the Budget Goes

For a $1,500 monthly budget, here's how I'd split it:

- **$1,200** on search ads targeting high-intent keywords: "mortgage broker [city]," "home loan pre-approval [city]," "best mortgage rates [city]," "first-time home buyer [city]"
- **$200** on a retargeting campaign for people who visited your landing page
- **$100** on a display campaign targeting in-market mortgage audiences

Don't put money into video ads unless you have a specific reason. Video ads for mortgage brokers rarely work at small budgets. They require more creative investment and the targeting is less precise. I've seen brokers burn $500 on YouTube pre-roll ads that got 12 clicks and zero leads.

### When to Scale Up

Once your search campaign is generating leads at a cost per lead you can live with — say, under $75 per lead — start increasing the budget by 20% every two weeks. If the cost per lead stays the same or drops, keep going. If it spikes, pause the increase and let the campaign stabilize.

I worked with a broker in Phoenix who was spending $1,200 a month and getting 15 leads. Cost per lead was $80. We increased to $1,500 and his cost per lead dropped to $65 because the higher budget unlocked more auction share on high-intent keywords. We kept scaling to $2,500 a month over three months and his cost per lead stayed around $60. He was getting 40+ leads a month and closing about 8-10 loans.

### Don't Forget the Landing Page

Your ad budget is only as good as what happens after someone clicks. I've watched brokers throw $3,000 a month at Google Ads while sending people to a generic contact page. That's like buying a billboard on the highway and pointing it at a dirt road.

The landing page needs to:
- Load in under two seconds. Test it with Google's PageSpeed Insights.
- Have one clear call to action: "Get Pre-Approved," "Check Today's Rates," "Schedule a Call."
- No navigation bar. No links to your blog. No social media icons. Distraction kills conversions.
- Include a phone number that's clickable on mobile.
- Show trust signals: your license number, Better Business Bureau rating, Google reviews, specific numbers like "Closed 500+ loans in Austin."

I've seen a landing page redesign increase conversion rates from 1.2% to 3.8% for a broker in Kansas City. Same ads, same budget, same audience. The only thing that changed was the page they landed on.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long until I see results from Google Ads?**

Most campaigns need 2-3 weeks to gather enough data to start optimizing. You'll probably get leads in the first week if your targeting is right, but I wouldn't make decisions based on those early results. After 30 days, you'll have a sense of your cost per lead and which keywords are working. After 90 days, you should know whether the campaign is profitable. If you're not seeing progress by day 60, something is wrong — either your keywords, your landing page, or your budget is too small.

**Q: Can I compete with big mortgage companies like Rocket Mortgage or LoanDepot?**

Yes, but not on their terms. You're not going to outspend them on "mortgage rates" — that keyword costs $50+ per click and they have unlimited budget. What you can do is dominate local keywords and niche terms. "Mortgage broker in Austin TX for first-time buyers" costs $8-$12 per click and has almost no competition from the national players. You can also compete on service — your ad copy can say "Talk to a real person, not a call center." That resonates with people tired of automated phone trees.

**Q: Should I use automated bidding or manual bidding?**

Start with manual bidding. Automated bidding like Target CPA or Maximize Conversions works best when you have 30+ conversions in the last 30 days. If you're just starting out, automated bidding will make weird choices — like showing your ad to people who are searching for "how to become a mortgage broker" because it thinks any click is good. Manual bidding lets you control your costs while you learn. After you have enough conversion data, switch to Target CPA and set a realistic target based on your actual cost per lead.

**Q: What if I only have $500 a month to spend?**

$500 is tight but not impossible. You'll need to be very specific. Pick one city neighborhood or one zip code. Use exact match keywords only. Run ads only during business hours. Focus on one ad group with 5-7 keywords. Your landing page needs to convert at 3% or higher to make this work. At $500, you're probably getting 4-8 leads a month. That's enough to test if the channel works for you. If you can't get profitable at $500, scaling to $1,000 won't fix the problem — you'll just lose money faster.

**Q: How do I track phone calls from ads?**

Set up call tracking through a service like CallRail, WhatConverts, or Google's own call tracking (available in Google Ads if you set up call extensions). You need a phone number that only shows on your ads and landing page. When someone calls that number, the system records the call and attributes it to the specific ad and keyword that drove it. Without call tracking, you're blind to calls, and calls convert at 2-3 times the rate of form fills.

**Q: Should I advertise on mortgage rate comparison sites like Bankrate or LendingTree?**

Only if you're willing to pay $20-$50 per lead and compete with dozens of other brokers for the same person. Those platforms are lead generation businesses — they sell leads to multiple brokers and the person you're "buying" has already been contacted by 3-5 other companies. The quality is low and the volume is inconsistent. In most cases, Google Ads will give you better leads at a lower cost because you're targeting people who specifically searched for a broker in your city, not people who filled out a generic form on a comparison site.

**Q: What about local service ads from Google?**

Google Local Service Ads (the ones with the green checkmark at the top of search results) are worth testing if they're available in your city. They charge per lead instead of per click, which means you only pay when someone contacts you. The catch is that Google vets your business — you need a license, insurance, and background checks. For mortgage brokers, the program is rolling out slowly. Check if your city is eligible. If it is, start with a small budget of $500 and see if the lead quality beats your search campaign.
