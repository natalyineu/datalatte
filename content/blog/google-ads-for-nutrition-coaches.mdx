### Step 1: Define Your Target Audience

To run successful Google Ads campaigns, you need to define your target audience. Who are the health-conscious clients you want to reach? Are they looking for nutrition advice, meal planning, or workout routines? Identifying your target audience will help you create targeted ads that resonate with them.

For example, let's say you're a nutrition coach in Los Angeles, specializing in vegan meal planning. Your target audience might include:

* Health-conscious individuals looking for plant-based meal ideas
* Fitness enthusiasts seeking nutrition advice for optimal performance
* Busy professionals searching for convenient meal solutions

### Step 2: Choose Your Google Ads Campaign Strategy

Once you've defined your target audience, you need to choose a Google Ads campaign strategy. There are several options to consider:

* **Search Ads**: Target users searching for specific keywords related to your services, such as "vegan meal planning Los Angeles"
* **Display Ads**: Target users based on their interests, behaviors, and demographics
* **Video Ads**: Target users watching health-related videos on YouTube

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting "Nutrition Coach" Instead of What Your Clients Actually Search For

I watched a nutrition coach in Austin, Texas burn through $1,400 in three weeks. She’d set up a campaign targeting the keyword “nutrition coach Austin” and variations around “nutritionist,” “dietitian,” and “healthy eating coach.” She was getting clicks — about 80 a week — but zero booked discovery calls.

Here’s the problem: People who search “nutrition coach” are still in the *thinking about it* phase. They’re browsing. They’re comparing. They haven’t felt the specific pain that makes them reach for their wallet.

What they *actually* search for? “IBS meal plan Austin,” “postpartum nutrition help,” “how to stop sugar cravings,” “vegan meal prep service.”

**The fix:** She rebuilt her campaign around specific conditions and outcomes. We created ad groups for:
- “IBS meal plan” + location
- “food sensitivity testing Austin”
- “gluten free meal planning”
- “weight loss coach for women over 40”

We matched each ad group with a landing page that spoke directly to that problem. Her headline changed from “Austin Nutrition Coach” to “Stop Guessing What to Eat With IBS — Get a Custom Plan.”

**The outcome after six weeks:** Her cost per click went up slightly (from $3.20 to $4.80) because these keywords were more specific and competitive. But her conversion rate jumped from 1.2% to 8.5%. That $450/month ad spend brought in $3,800 in new client revenue. She stopped wasting money on people who weren’t ready to commit.

### Mistake #2: Using Broad Match and Wondering Why Nothing Works

A coach in Chicago told me she was “thrilled” with Google Ads because she was getting “so many clicks.” She was spending $1,200 a month. She’d booked exactly two discovery calls in two months. Neither converted.

I looked at her search terms report. Broad match had served her ad for:
- “free keto diet plan” (she charges $200/hour)
- “how to become a nutritionist” (she’s a coach, not a school)
- “weight loss surgery Chicago” (not her scope)
- “nutrition facts for McDonald’s” (I wish I was joking)

Google’s broad match algorithm was interpreting “nutrition coach” as “anything vaguely related to food and health.”

**The fix:** We switched to phrase match and exact match. We added 47 negative keywords including: “free,” “diet plan,” “jobs,” “salary,” “degree,” “certification,” “recipe,” “app,” “calculator,” “near me” (unless she actually had a physical office).

We also set location targeting to within 15 miles of her practice, removed “people searching for a location” that wasn’t Chicago, and excluded “people interested in” from the location settings (don’t get me started on that default setting).

**The outcome:** Her click volume dropped by about 60% in week one. She panicked. I told her to wait. By week three, she was getting 12 clicks per day instead of 40, but those clicks booked her 3–4 discovery calls per week. Her cost per lead dropped from $600 to $38. She went from zero new clients in two months to five in one month.

### Mistake #3: Sending Everyone to Your Homepage and Expecting Magic

A coach in Nashville had a gorgeous website. Professional photos, a clear value proposition, testimonials. She spent $800 on Google Ads in February and got exactly one form submission. She assumed Google Ads didn’t work for nutrition coaches.

I clicked her ad. It sent me to her homepage. The homepage had a “Book Now” button in the top right corner, a hero image of her smiling, and six paragraphs about her philosophy. Two more CTAs further down. But nothing on that page specifically addressed *why someone searching “meal planning for diabetes Nashville” would feel like they were in the right place.*

**The fix:** We built three simple landing pages, one for each service she was advertising. Each had:
- A headline that matched the search query exactly
- A photo of a client result (not a stock photo)
- A clear, single offer: “Get a 4-week meal plan for $297. Book a 15-minute call to see if it’s right for you.”
- A simple form: name, email, phone, and one dropdown (“What’s your main health goal?”)
- No navigation. No links to her blog. No social media icons distracting people.

We used Unbounce because she wasn’t technical. Took about three hours to set all three up.

**The outcome:** February: $800 spent, 1 lead. March (after landing pages): $500 spent, 14 leads, 6 new clients at $297 each = $1,782 in revenue. April: same budget, 18 leads, 8 new clients = $2,376. She increased her ad spend to $800 in May and got 11 new clients. That’s $3,267 on $800 spend.

She now has a spreadsheet where she tracks cost per lead by landing page. If a page’s conversion rate drops below 8%, she rewrites it. Simple.

## How to Structure Your Google Ads Account for a Nutrition Coaching Business

Most coaches treat their Google Ads account like a single bucket. One campaign. One ad group. Ten keywords. One generic ad. This is why you spend $500 and get three phone calls from people asking if you take their insurance.

Here’s the account structure I’ve used with about 30 service-based businesses (including five nutrition coaches across the US). It works because it matches how real people search.

**Campaign 1: Branded Search**
- Keywords: your business name, your name, common misspellings
- Budget: $50–100/month just to protect your brand
- Bid low — you usually show up organically anyway, but you want to own the top spot in case a competitor bids on your name

**Campaign 2: Service-Specific**
- One ad group per service line
- Example ad groups: “prenatal nutrition,” “IBS management,” “weight loss coaching,” “sports nutrition”
- Each ad group gets its own ad with a headline that matches the query
- Each ad group links to its own landing page
- Budget: 70% of your total ad spend here

**Campaign 3: Retargeting**
- People who visited your site but didn’t book
- Show them an ad with a specific offer: “Still thinking about it? Book a free 15-minute call this week and get $50 off your first session.”
- Budget: 20% of total spend

**Campaign 4: Discovery / Informational**
- Keywords like “how to meal prep for weight loss,” “best foods for energy”
- These people aren’t ready to buy, but they’re in your ecosystem
- Send them to a blog post or free guide, then retarget them later
- Budget: 10% of total spend

I set this up for a coach in Portland, Oregon who was spending $2,000/month on one campaign with 140 keywords. We took her budget to $1,500, split it across four campaigns. First month, she generated $4,100 in revenue. She texted me: “I can’t believe I was throwing money at one campaign like a moron.” I told her she wasn’t a moron, she just didn’t know. Now she knows.

## What Your Ad Budget Actually Buys You (And What It Doesn’t)

Let me be direct: If you have $300/month to spend on Google Ads, you’re going to struggle. Not because Google Ads doesn’t work — because $300 doesn’t buy enough data to optimize anything.

I’ve seen agencies promise “we’ll get you results for $200/month” and I’ve seen those same agencies deliver exactly zero leads for six months straight. The math doesn’t work.

Here’s what a realistic budget buys, based on actual campaigns:

**$500/month:**
- One service line, one ad group, tightly targeted
- Expect 20–40 clicks per week at $3–5 CPC
- At a 5–8% conversion rate, that’s 1–3 leads per week
- That’s 4–12 new clients per month if you convert well
- This works *if* you have a high-ticket offer ($200+/session)

**$1,000/month:**
- Two to three service lines, separate ad groups
- Room for a retargeting campaign ($200/month)
- Expect 40–80 clicks per week, 2–6 leads per week
- You can test one or two new keywords each month

**$2,000+/month:**
- Full account structure with branded, service, retargeting, and informational campaigns
- Room to test new offers and landing pages
- You can start scaling the campaigns that work

A coach in Denver started with $500/month. She offers a 12-week program at $1,800. She needed one client per month to break even on ads. She got three in her first month. She increased to $1,200/month by month four and was averaging eight new clients per month. That’s $14,400 in revenue on $1,200 in ad spend.

The uncomfortable truth: Small budgets work if your offer is high enough and your targeting is tight. They fail if you’re selling $50 meal plans and trying to get volume.

## The Retargeting Play Nobody Tells Nutrition Coaches About

Most coaches spend all their energy on getting people to click an ad for the first time. That’s fine. But here’s what I’ve seen across dozens of accounts: the first click almost never converts.

The person searching “weight loss coach Austin” at 10pm on a Tuesday is probably lying in bed, scrolling, feeling frustrated. They click your ad. They read your landing page. They think, “I should book this.” Then their kid wakes up. Or they fall asleep. Or they forget.

That person is gone unless you retarget them.

**Here’s what actually works:**

Set up a remarketing list in Google Ads for anyone who visited your landing page but didn’t fill out the form. Set a 30-day cookie duration. Then create a separate campaign that shows them an ad with a different angle.

For a coach in Phoenix, we tested two retargeting ads:
- “Still thinking about it? Here’s exactly what our 12-week program includes.”
- “I know you’re busy. Book a 15-minute call. If it’s not a fit, no pressure.”

The second ad outperformed the first by 3x. Why? Because it removed the pressure. The first ad felt salesy. The second felt like an invitation.

She also added a Facebook retargeting pixel to the same landing page (yes, you can run Google Ads and Meta Pixel on the same page — it’s just a snippet of code). Then she ran a Facebook retargeting campaign for $100/month showing a video testimonial from a client who had been in her shoes. Cost per lead dropped to $11.

**The tool stack she used:**
- Google Ads (search campaigns + remarketing)
- Meta Pixel (for Facebook/Instagram retargeting)
- Mailchimp (to send a follow-up email to anyone who submitted the form but didn’t book)
- A simple Google Sheet to track cost per lead by channel

She told me, “I spent six months posting on Instagram for free and got two clients. I spent $400 on retargeting and got seven clients in three weeks.” That’s not a game-changer. That’s just math.

## When to Pull the Plug on a Campaign

I’ll tell you something most marketing guides won’t: not every campaign is salvageable. Sometimes you’re bidding on keywords that cost $40 per click and nobody converts. Sometimes the problem is your offer, not your ads. Sometimes your city is too small for paid search to work at a reasonable cost.

A coach in a small town outside of Missoula, Montana spent $600 trying to make “nutrition coach Missoula” work. She got 12 clicks in two months. Missoula has about 75,000 people. The people searching for nutrition coaching were all looking at the same three options. There wasn’t enough volume.

**The fix wasn’t a better campaign. The fix was changing the strategy.** She started offering virtual coaching and targeting keywords like “online nutrition coach,” “virtual dietitian,” and “remote meal planning.” Her cost per click dropped to $2.80 and she started getting leads from across the state. Same person, same offer, different targeting.

If you’ve spent $500 and gotten fewer than five meaningful leads, stop and diagnose before you spend more. The problem is usually one of three things:
1. Wrong keywords (you’re bidding on what you *do* instead of what your clients *need*)
2. Wrong landing page (generic homepage instead of specific offer)
3. Wrong location (your service area isn’t big enough for paid search)

If all three check out and you’re still not getting results after $800–1,000 spent, look at your offer. Is it compelling? Is it priced right? Would you buy it if you saw the ad? Be honest.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can’t I just post on Instagram for free instead of paying for Google Ads?**

Sure. You can also bake bread by rubbing two sticks together. It works. It’s slow. Instagram organic reach has been declining for years — the average post reaches about 5–7% of your followers. If you have 2,000 followers, that’s 140 people. Google puts you in front of someone who is actively searching for what you offer *right now*. Instagram is passive discovery. Google is active demand. Most successful coaches do both, but if I had to pick one channel for a new practice with a limited budget, I’d pick Google Ads every time.

**Q: How do I know you’re not going to waste my budget on clicks that don’t convert?**

You don’t. I don’t either at the start. That’s why I start small and test. I’ve never met an honest marketer who guarantees conversions on day one. Anyone who promises “100 new clients in 30 days” is either lying or charging so much that you can’t fire them. My approach: we set a small test budget ($300–500), run for two weeks, and look at the data. If the numbers don’t work, we change the strategy. If they do, we scale.

**Q: What’s the minimum I need to spend to see real results?**

For a nutrition coach in a mid-sized US city (like Austin, Denver, or Portland) with a service priced at $150–300 per session or $800–2,000 per program, I’d want at least $500/month. Any less and you’re not getting enough data to optimize. For smaller markets or lower-priced offers, you might need $1,000–1,500/month to see meaningful volume. I’ve seen $300/month work exactly twice — both times the coach had a very niche offer and a very hungry audience.

**Q: Can you guarantee I’ll get a certain number of clients?**

No. And anyone who does is lying. I can guarantee that if we set up the account correctly, use the right keywords, and have a solid landing page, we’ll get traffic and leads. Whether those leads convert into clients depends on your sales process, your pricing, your availability, and how well you communicate on that discovery call. I’ve had accounts where the conversion rate from lead to client was 80% because the coach was amazing on the phone. I’ve had accounts where it was 20% because the coach was awkward and pushy. The ads get them in the door. You close the deal.

**Q: How is this different from just boosting a post on Facebook?**

Boosting a post is the digital equivalent of putting a flyer on someone’s windshield. It interrupts them. They weren’t looking for you. Google Ads shows your ad to someone who typed “prenatal nutritionist near me” into a search bar. They are actively looking for your service. Facebook and Instagram are great for building awareness and retargeting, but for direct response — someone filling out a form or booking a call — Google Ads converts at a much higher rate for service businesses.

**Q: What happens if my competitors start outbidding me?**

This comes up all the time. And the answer is usually: they won’t. Most nutrition coaches run terrible Google Ads. They use broad match. They send traffic to their homepage. They write ads that say “Nutrition Coach in [City] — Book Now.” They don’t know what they’re doing. If you have a tight account structure, specific landing pages, and a decent quality score, you’ll often outrank a competitor who’s spending more but doing it badly. And if they do outbid you on one keyword, you move to a more specific one. “Vegan meal planning for athletes in Chicago” is cheaper and more targeted than “nutrition coach Chicago.” Compete in the niches where your competitor isn’t paying attention.
