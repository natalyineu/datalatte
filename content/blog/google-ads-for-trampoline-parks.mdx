## Understanding Your Target Audience

To succeed with Google Ads for trampoline parks, you need to understand your target audience: families with young kids. These parents are busy, and their attention is fragmented. They're looking for fun, safe activities that cater to their children's energy levels. Your ads need to speak directly to them, showcasing your park's unique features and offers.

* Identify your ideal customer: families with kids aged 5-12, living within a 20-mile radius
* Research popular activities and attractions in your area to differentiate your park
* Develop a unique value proposition (UVP) that highlights your park's best features

## Setting Up Your Google Ads Campaign

To get started with Google Ads, you'll need to create a campaign that targets your local audience. Here's a step-by-step guide:

* Set up a Google Ads account and link it to your Google My Business profile
* Choose the 'Local' campaign type and select your target location (e.g., city, zip code)
* Set your budget and bidding strategy (e.g., cost-per-click, cost-per-conversion)

## Creating Effective Ad Copy

Your ad copy needs to grab attention, convey your UVP, and entice parents to click. Here are some tips:

* Use attention-grabbing headlines that highlight your park's unique features (e.g., "Trampoline Park with Soft Play Area")
* Include relevant keywords (e.g., "trampoline park", "kids activities", "local events")
* Emphasize benefits, such as "Get ready for a fun-filled day with our trampoline park and soft play area!"

## Optimizing Your Landing Page

Your landing page is critical to converting clicks into customers. Ensure it:

* Matches your ad copy and UVP
* Includes clear calls-to-action (CTAs) and easy-to-use booking or inquiry forms
* Is mobile-friendly and loads quickly

## Measuring and Optimizing Performance

To ensure your Google Ads campaign is effective, you need to track key metrics and adjust your strategy accordingly. Here are some tips:

* Set up conversion tracking to measure bookings, inquiries, and other valuable actions
* Monitor your cost-per-conversion and adjust your bidding strategy as needed
* Use Google Ads' built-in reporting tools to analyze performance and identify areas for improvement

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting "Parents of Toddlers" When Your Real Customers Have 8-Year-Olds

**The story:**

A trampoline park owner in Nashville — let's call him Mark — came to me after spending $1,800 on Google Ads in four weeks. He had one booking. One.

I opened his account and found he'd selected "Parents of toddlers (0-3)" as his primary audience. His ads were showing to people with infants and toddlers who could barely walk, let alone jump on a trampoline. His ad copy said "Fun for the whole family!" which is so generic it might as well say "please click me out of pity."

**What went wrong:**

Mark assumed "parents with young kids" meant everyone from newborns to pre-teens. But there's a massive difference between a family with a 2-year-old (looking for baby gymnastics) and a family with a 7-year-old (looking for somewhere to burn energy for two hours so they can sleep through the night).

Google lets you layer age ranges into your targeting. Mark never did. He also didn't use custom audiences based on search behavior — things like "kids birthday party ideas" or "after school activities for kids."

**The fix:**

I rebuilt his campaign to target parents of kids aged 5-12. We added a custom audience segment for people who'd searched "kids birthday party venue" or "trampoline park birthday" in the last 30 days. We added negative keywords like "toddler," "baby," "indoor playground for babies," and "soft play."

His ad copy changed from "Fun for everyone" to "Birthday parties that don't suck" and "Rainy day? We have 5,000 square feet of trampolines."

**The outcome:**

In the next 30 days, Mark spent $1,200 and generated $4,700 in revenue — a 3.9x return. His cost per booking dropped from $1,800 to $28. He called me and said, "I feel like an idiot, but also I'm thrilled."

You should feel like an idiot too if you're making this mistake. Fix it.

### Mistake #2: Running Ads 24/7 and Wondering Why Wednesdays at 10 AM Don't Convert

**The story:**

A park owner in Austin — family-run, great facility, terrible ad management — was running the same ad schedule for six months. Ads ran 24 hours a day, seven days a week. She was paying for clicks at 2 AM on a Tuesday.

When I asked why, she said, "I didn't know you could turn them off."

**What went wrong:**

Google Ads defaults to "run all the time" unless you manually set an ad schedule. Most small business owners don't know this exists. So they're paying for clicks at 3 AM when the park is closed and no one is booking anything. (Unless you're a 24-hour trampoline park, which you're not.)

I checked her data. Clicks between midnight and 6 AM had a 0.2% conversion rate. That's not a conversion rate — that's a rounding error.

**The fix:**

We pulled 90 days of conversion data and mapped it against time of day. For her Austin location, peak booking times were:

- Weekdays: 3 PM–7 PM (parents booking after school activities for the next day or weekend)
- Weekends: 9 AM–11 AM (parents planning same-day outings before the kids get bored)

We set her ads to run only during those windows. She was skeptical — "Won't I miss people looking at night?" No. People searching at 11 PM are browsing, not booking. Show them ads at 4 PM when they're actually making decisions.

We also added ad scheduling adjustments: +20% bid increase during peak hours, -90% during off-hours (still running but barely spending).

**The outcome:**

Her budget stayed the same — $800/month — but her bookings increased by 35% in the first two weeks. Her cost per conversion dropped from $45 to $19. She stopped paying for 2 AM clicks from people searching for "trampoline near me" while drunk. (Yes, that happens. No, they don't book.)

### Mistake #3: Using "Trampoline Park" as Your Only Keyword

**The story:**

A park owner in Portland told me, with total confidence, "I bid on 'trampoline park' and nothing else. That's the keyword, right?"

He was spending $2,400 a month on a single keyword that had a search volume of 1,600 in his area. His average CPC was $7.50. His conversion rate was 3%.

That sounds okay until you realize he could have gotten 20x more traffic for half the cost by adding related keywords.

**What went wrong:**

"Trampoline park" is a high-intent keyword but also high-competition. Other parks bid on it. Google Shopping ads bid on it. Even inflatable bounce house rental companies bid on it by mistake. You're paying a premium for a keyword that only 1,600 people in Portland search each month.

But those 1,600 people aren't the only ones looking for what you offer. What about:

- "Kids birthday party venue Portland"
- "Indoor activities for kids Portland"
- "Rainy day activities for kids"
- "Kids jump place near me"
- "After school activities for energetic kids"
- "Where to have a kids birthday party in Portland"

These all have lower CPCs because fewer businesses bid on them. And they attract people who are in the same buying mindset — just using different words.

**The fix:**

We built a keyword list of 47 non-branded terms across four categories:

1. Activity-based: "things to do with kids," "indoor playground," "kids jump center"
2. Weather-based: "rainy day activities Portland," "indoor fun for kids"
3. Event-based: "kids birthday party ideas," "where to throw a birthday party"
4. Problem-based: "hyperactive kid activities," "burn kid energy indoors"

We used broad match modified (now phrase match, post-2021 changes) and paid close attention to search terms reports. Within two weeks, we added 12 new high-performing keywords and paused 8 that brought irrelevant traffic (like "trampoline for home," "trampoline repair").

**The outcome:**

His monthly ad spend stayed at $2,400. His monthly bookings went from 32 to 87. His revenue increased from $7,680 to $20,880 in a single month. The cost per booking dropped from $75 to $27.

He said, "I can't believe I spent two years only bidding on one keyword." I can. I've seen it at three different clients.

### Mistake #4: No Negative Keywords (and Wasting 30% of Your Budget)

**The story:**

A park in Denver was getting clicks, but no bookings. They were spending $600/week and getting zero conversions. Zero.

I checked their search terms report. They were showing up for "trampoline for sale," "trampoline removal," "used trampoline," "trampoline replacement parts," and — my personal favorite — "trampoline accident attorney."

They were paying $4–$12 per click for people who wanted to buy a trampoline, remove one, or sue someone related to one. Not a single one of those people wanted to visit a trampoline park.

**What went wrong:**

They never added negative keywords. Google Ads will happily match your "trampoline" keyword to any search containing the word "trampoline" — even if the intent is completely wrong. It took them four months to realize 30% of their budget was going to people who would never book.

**The fix:**

We built a negative keyword list with 80+ terms including:

- For sale, used, cheap, clearance, Amazon, Walmart
- Removal, repair, parts, replacement, assembly
- Accident, injury, lawsuit, attorney, lawyer, claim
- Toddler, baby (unless you specifically target those ages)
- Free, coupon, discount (unless you want to attract deal-seekers who may not convert)

We also set up a weekly search terms review. Every Monday, we'd skim the terms report and add any new irrelevant terms as negatives. This took 10 minutes.

**The outcome:**

In the first week of negative keywords, their wasted spend dropped from $180 to $22. Their cost per conversion dropped from $62 to $14. They started getting actual bookings instead of paying for people researching trampoline accident lawsuits.

## How to Structure Your Campaign for Maximum Efficiency

Most small business owners set up Google Ads like they're throwing spaghetti at a wall. One campaign. One ad group. Every keyword thrown in together. Then they wonder why performance is flat.

Here's the structure I've used for trampoline parks across six different cities. It's not fancy. It works.

### Campaign 1: Brand Search (Your Park Name)

- Budget: $300/month
- Keywords: Your park name, common misspellings, "[city] trampoline park"
- Why: If someone searches your name and sees a competitor's ad instead, you lose that customer. This is defensive spending, and it's cheap because your brand terms have high Quality Scores.
- Typical CPC: $0.50–$1.50
- Conversion rate: 15–25%

### Campaign 2: Non-Brand Activity Search (People Looking for Something to Do)

- Budget: $600–$1,000/month
- Keywords: "indoor activities for kids," "things to do with kids [city]," "rainy day activities," "birthday party venue"
- Why: This is your growth engine. These people don't know you exist yet. They're looking for options.
- Typical CPC: $2–$5
- Conversion rate: 4–8%

### Campaign 3: Event & Party Search (High Intent, High Value)

- Budget: $400–$600/month
- Keywords: "trampoline park birthday party," "kids birthday party packages," "birthday party venue [city]"
- Why: Birthday parties are your highest-value booking. A single party booking can be worth $300–$800. It's worth spending more to capture these.
- Typical CPC: $3–$7
- Conversion rate: 8–12%

Total monthly spend: $1,300–$1,900. If you're spending less than that, you're leaving money on the table. If you're spending more without this structure, you're wasting money.

## Using Google Ads Extensions (And Why Most Parks Skip Them)

I checked 15 trampoline park Google Ads accounts last month. Only three had ad extensions set up. That's 80% of parks leaving free real estate on the table.

Extensions are extra lines of text that appear below your ad. They cost nothing extra. You only pay when someone clicks them. But they increase your click-through rate by 10–15% on average.

Here are the extensions you need for a trampoline park:

### Call Extension
- Shows your phone number directly in the ad
- Mobile users can tap to call instantly
- Set it to show during business hours only (nothing worse than someone calling at 9 PM on a Tuesday)
- Track calls using Google's call reporting — you'll be surprised how many bookings come from phone calls

### Location Extension
- Shows your address, directions, and distance
- Critical for "near me" searches
- Links to your Google Business Profile (which you should have fully optimized — photos, posts, reviews)
- I've seen parks get 20+ extra walk-ins per week just from this extension

### Callout Extension
- Short bullet points: "5,000 sq ft of trampolines," "Open 7 days," "Birthday party packages available"
- These appear as small text snippets below your ad
- Use 4–6 callouts. Test different ones. "Free parking" outperforms "Family friendly" 3:1 in my experience.

### Structured Snippet Extension
- Pre-set categories like "Activities: Dodgeball, Foam Pit, Basketball Dunk, Climbing Wall"
- Gives Google more context about what you offer
- Improves Quality Score because Google understands your relevance better

### Price Extension
- Show specific prices: "General Admission: $14," "Sock Rental: $3," "Birthday Package: $299"
- Filters out people who can't afford you (good) and attracts people who are ready to book (better)
- Test it — some parks see higher conversion rates, others see lower CTR. It depends on your pricing.

Setting up all these extensions takes about 30 minutes. If you haven't done it, you're losing 10–15% of potential clicks for no reason. That's $100–$300 of free traffic per month you're ignoring.

## Why Your Landing Page Matters More Than Your Ads

Here's something most guides skip: your ad gets the click. Your landing page gets the booking. If either is bad, you lose.

I audited a park in Chicago that was spending $1,500/month on ads. Their ad copy was solid. Their landing page was a generic homepage with a "Book Now" button that led to a third-party ticketing site that looked like it was built in 2008.

Their conversion rate: 1.8%.

I built them a simple one-page landing page with:

- A headline matching the ad (if the ad said "Rainy Day? Jump with Us," the headline said the same)
- Three bullet points: "Safe, clean facility," "5,000 sq ft of trampolines," "Socks included with admission"
- A single booking form (name, email, number of jumpers, time slot)
- Trust signals: "4.8 stars on Google, 200+ reviews," photos of happy families, a mention of their safety certification
- Mobile-optimized (60% of trampoline park searches are on mobile)

Cost to build: $500 on a freelancer from Upwork. (You can also use Carrd or Squarespace and do it yourself in an afternoon.)

New conversion rate: 7.2%.

On $1,500/month ad spend, that's the difference between 27 bookings ($6,480 revenue) and 108 bookings ($25,920 revenue) — assuming $240 average booking value.

The ad spend didn't change. The landing page changed.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads in my first month?**

Start with $500. Run a single campaign targeting your top 20 keywords with exact match and phrase match. Track everything. If you see positive ROI within two weeks (cost per booking under $40), scale to $1,000. If not, fix your keywords, ad copy, or landing page before spending more. Don't start at $2,000 and hope it works. It won't.

**Q: Can I target only people within 10 miles of my park?**

Yes. Use location targeting with radius targeting. Set it to 10–15 miles for most suburban parks, 5–8 miles for urban parks (where people have more options). But exclude zip codes that are predominantly commercial or industrial — I've seen parks waste 15% of their budget showing ads to office buildings with no families.

**Q: Should I run Google Ads during the summer when we're already busy?**

Yes, but reduce your budget. Summer is high season — you might fill slots without ads. But competitors will run ads to steal your customers. Run a small brand defense campaign ($200–$300/month) to protect your name. Then shift your main spend to fall and winter when families are looking for indoor activities. One park in Denver saw a 300% ROI increase by moving 60% of their budget from summer to winter.

**Q: What if a competitor bids on my park name?**

It happens. Some parks run "conquesting" campaigns targeting competitor names. Fight back with your own brand campaign. Your Quality Score on your own brand name is 10/10 automatically — you'll pay pennies per click. A competitor's Quality Score on your brand name will be low — they'll pay $3–$6 per click. Outspend them at $0.50 per click and make it unprofitable for them to keep running.

**Q: Do I need a separate campaign for birthday parties?**

Yes. Birthday parties are your highest-value booking. Run a separate campaign with keywords like "trampoline park birthday party," "kids birthday party packages [city]," and "birthday party venue near me." Budget: $300–$500/month. Track party bookings separately from general admission. One park in Phoenix found that birthday party bookings had a 12% conversion rate with a $12 cost per booking — 3x better than their general campaign.

**Q: Will Google Ads work if my park is in a small town (population under 50,000)?**

It can, but you need to adjust. Your search volume will be low — maybe 200–400 searches per month for your top keywords. Run a single campaign with broad match and tight location targeting. Budget: $200–$300/month. Focus on event keywords ("birthday party," "school break camp") where the intent is higher. One park in a town of 18,000 in Ohio generated $3,200/month from a $250 ad budget by targeting birthday parties and school vacation weeks.
