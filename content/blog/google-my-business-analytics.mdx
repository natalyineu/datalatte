**Understanding Google My Business Analytics**

Before we dive into the nitty-gritty of GMB analytics, it's essential to understand the basics. Google My Business is a free tool that helps you manage your online presence, including your business listing, photos, and reviews. GMB Insights is a powerful feature that provides you with valuable data and analytics to help you understand how your business is performing online.

**Getting Started with GMB Insights**

To access GMB Insights, follow these steps:

1. Log in to your GMB account.
2. Click on the "Insights" tab.
3. Choose the data range you want to analyze.

Now that you have access to GMB Insights, let's explore the key metrics you need to focus on:

* **Views**: The number of times your business listing is viewed.
* **Directions**: The number of times users click on the "Get directions" button.
* **Messages**: The number of messages you receive through GMB.
* **Reviews**: The number of reviews you receive.

**Using GMB Analytics to Drive More Customers**

Now that you have a basic understanding of GMB Insights, let's dive into some actionable tips to help you drive more customers to your business:

* **Use GMB to track your website traffic**: By linking your GMB listing to your website, you can track website traffic and understand how users are interacting with your business online.
* **Optimize your business listing**: Make sure your business listing is complete and up-to-date, including accurate hours, address, and contact information.
* **Encourage users to leave reviews**: Reviews are essential for building trust and credibility with potential customers. Encourage users to leave reviews by offering incentives or simply by providing excellent customer service.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

Over the years, I've watched business owners stare at their Google My Business dashboard like it's written in ancient Greek. The data is right there — but most people interpret it wrong. Here are the mistakes I see most often, with real stories from actual clients.

### Mistake #1: Ignoring the "Directions" Request

A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas — let's call her Maria — ran a shop called Brew & Batter on South Congress. She had 300+ reviews, a 4.7-star rating, and she checked her GMB analytics every Monday. She saw she was getting 2,500 searches per month and felt good about it.

Here's what she missed: only 78 of those searches turned into "directions requests." Translation: people saw her listing, thought about coming, and then changed their minds at the decision point.

The fix wasn't complicated. Maria added a "nearby landmarks" section to her business description — "two blocks from the Continental Club, across from the Guero's parking lot." She also uploaded 12 photos of the exterior, including one that showed the bright orange door that matched her awning.

Within 60 days, her directions requests went from 78 to 210 per month. Actual foot traffic? Up 34%. She told me that translated to roughly $1,400 in additional monthly revenue during those months.

What to check instead: Look at direction requests, not just total searches. If you're getting 1,000 searches but only 30 people ask for directions, something is breaking between "interested" and "I'll show up." Fix your location visibility.

### Mistake #2: Treating All Phone Calls the Same

A pet grooming business in Portland, Oregon — Furry Friends Grooming — was getting 45 phone call clicks per month from their GMB listing. The owner, Jason, thought this was great. He was wrong.

I asked him one question: "How many of those calls actually turn into bookings?"

Turns out, 22 of those 45 calls were people asking about pricing for services he didn't offer (nail grinding, which he'd dropped six months earlier). Another 8 were people calling outside business hours, getting the automated message, and never calling back.

The wasted opportunity: Jason was paying $200/month for a local phone number service that tracked calls. He was running Google Ads for $600/month. And his GMB listing still mentioned nail grinding in the services section, even though he hadn't offered it for eight months.

We updated the services list, added "Book Online" button (connected to his Booksy account), and changed his business hours to show when he actually answered the phone versus when he was in the back washing dogs.

Three months later: phone calls dropped to 28 per month — but 24 of them booked appointments. His actual conversion rate went from roughly 10% to 85%. He was spending less time on the phone and generating $3,800 more in monthly revenue.

What to check instead: Download your GMB insights once a month and categorize the phone calls. If you don't know what people are asking, you're flying blind. Use call recording or just ask every caller "how did you find us" for two weeks.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the "Questions & Answers" Section

Here's one that makes me order a second coffee. A fitness studio in Nashville — Sweat Haus on 12th Avenue South — had a GMB listing that looked perfect. Great photos, updated hours, positive reviews. But the Q&A section was a wasteland.

Someone had asked "Do you offer childcare during classes?" — six months earlier. No answer.

Another person asked "What's the parking situation?" — four months earlier. No answer.

The owner, Tanya, had no idea this section existed. She was losing potential clients to an inbox she didn't know she had.

Here's the real cost: when a user asks a question on GMB, that question appears in search results. Other potential clients see the unanswered question and assume the business doesn't care — or doesn't offer the service. Tanya's studio actually had a partnership with a neighboring daycare, but nobody knew because the question sat unanswered.

We spent 45 minutes answering every past question and setting up email notifications for new ones. We also proactively posted the five most common questions and answers ourselves (yes, you can do this).

Within two weeks, two people mentioned the now-visible childcare answer in their first-time inquiries. One became a monthly unlimited member ($150/month). Another bought a 10-class pack ($200).

Total time investment: 45 minutes. Total return: at least $350 in the first month, with ongoing residual benefit.

What to check instead: Go to your GMB listing right now. Scroll down to "Questions & Answers." If you see unanswered questions, answer them today. If you see none, add five common questions yourself. This takes 20 minutes and pays back for months.

### Mistake #4: Obsessing Over Views, Ignoring Actions

A barber shop in Chicago — Lincoln Park Cuts — had a GMB strategy that consisted of "look at the views graph." Every Monday, the owner Dave would check the chart showing how many people viewed his profile. When it went up, he felt good. When it went down, he felt bad.

In 12 months, Dave had never once checked the "actions" section — the part that shows how many people clicked "Call," "Directions," or "Website."

Here's what was actually happening: 2,500 views per month, but only 90 actions. That's a 3.6% action rate. His competitor, three blocks south, had 1,800 views but 240 actions — a 13.3% action rate. The competitor was busier with fewer views, because they were converting attention into action.

The fix was painful but simple. Dave's profile photo was a wide shot of the empty shop interior, taken at an angle that made it look like a hallway. His services listed "men's haircut $35" but didn't mention beard trims or hot towel shaves — his two most profitable services.

We changed the hero photo to a tight shot of a finished fade haircut. We updated the services list to include "Beard Trim + Hot Towel ($15 add-on)" and "Straight Razor Shave ($45)." We added a "Book Now" button connected to his Square Appointments.

Three months later: views dropped to 2,100. Actions increased to 190. Action rate went from 3.6% to 9%. Revenue from GMB-sourced customers increased by $1,800/month.

What to check instead: Divide your "actions" by your "views." If that number is under 10%, your listing isn't converting. Fix your photos, your services list, and your booking flow before you worry about getting more views.

## Why Google My Business Analytics Needs to Talk to Your Other Tools

Here's a scenario I've seen at four different clients: a business owner prints out their GMB insights, prints out their Square sales report, prints out their Mailchimp email report, and puts them in three different piles. Then they wonder why none of them tell a complete story.

GMB analytics on their own are useful. But when you connect them to your other systems, they become something else entirely: a direct line to what's actually driving revenue.

### The Mailchimp Connection

I worked with a boutique in Austin — Raven & Rose on South Lamar. They were sending weekly email blasts to 2,400 subscribers. Open rates were fine, click rates were fine, nobody could complain. But they noticed that every time they sent an email promoting a new dress collection, their GMB "views" spiked by 40–60% within 24 hours.

The email wasn't driving direct online sales. It was driving people to search for the store on Google, then visit the GMB listing, then walk in.

We set up a simple tracking system: for each email campaign, they added a specific promo code that could only be used in-store. Then they cross-referenced the promo code usage with the GMB views spike.

What they found: their email was worth $2.10 per recipient in GMB-driven in-store revenue, even though direct online sales from email were only $0.40 per recipient. They had been judging email performance on the wrong metric for 18 months.

Fixed it: they started including a "Google us and we'll see you soon" CTA in every email, along with a map link. In-store sales from email-triggered GMB visits went from $4,800/month to $7,200/month.

### The Square / PayPal Connection

You can do something similar with your point-of-sale system. Export your daily sales, then overlay your GMB performance data. Match the days where calls spiked with the days where foot traffic spiked.

A fitness studio in Denver — Core24 — did this and found something annoying: their busiest GMB days (Monday and Tuesday) had the lowest conversion rates. People were searching, viewing, and even calling — but not booking. Turns out, Monday was their instructor swap day, and the schedule on GMB didn't match what was actually happening in class.

They fixed the schedule mismatch, and Wednesday — previously their slowest GMB day — became their highest-revenue day within three weeks. The data was hiding in plain sight.

### The Google Ads Connection

This one hurts. A hair salon in Nashville was running $800/month in Google Ads targeting "hair salon Nashville" and "blowout Nashville." Their GMB listing was ranking organically for the same terms. They were paying to show up next to themselves.

I see this constantly. People run Google Ads without checking what their GMB listing is already doing for free. The fix: look at your GMB "search queries" report (it's under the "Performance" section). Write down the 10 terms people use to find you organically. Then pause those exact terms in your Google Ads. You're double-paying.

The salon saved $240/month by pausing the overlapping terms, then redirected that budget to "balayage Nashville" — a term their GMB didn't rank for. That single change brought in 12 new clients in the first month, spending an average of $180 each. $2,160 in new revenue for a $240 ad spend shift.

## What the Search Data Actually Tells You (and What It Doesn't)

There's a metric in GMB called "Queries" — the search terms people used to find your listing. Most business owners look at this and think "great, now I know what keywords to use." That's partially right, but it's missing the point.

GMB search queries tell you something more important: where your customer is in their decision process.

### Direct vs. Discovery Searches

Let me explain with an actual example from a restaurant in Austin. The GMB query report showed two types of searches:

- "Italian restaurant South Congress" (a discovery search — they didn't know the name)
- "Lucciano's South Congress" (a direct search — they knew the name)

For Lucciano's, 70% of their GMB traffic came from direct searches. That means 7 out of 10 people who found them were already looking for them. They were not attracting new customers through search — they were servicing existing demand.

A coffee shop in the same neighborhood had the opposite problem: 80% discovery searches. Tons of new eyes, but almost no repeat business. People were finding them on Google, visiting once, and never coming back.

Neither situation is inherently good or bad. But the fix is completely different.

If you're heavy on direct searches: your GMB listing is a utility, not a growth driver. Focus on reviews, photos, and accurate information. Your investment should go elsewhere — maybe a loyalty program or a referral system.

If you're heavy on discovery searches: your GMB is your storefront. Invest in it. High-quality photos, menu/pricing visible, "Book Now" button, answered Q&As. Every impression is a chance to convert someone who doesn't know you yet.

### The "Device Type" Lie

GMB tells you whether people found you on mobile or desktop. If you're a local business, ignore this breakdown. Seriously. It's irrelevant.

Here's why: someone searching on desktop at 10 AM is probably at work, planning a visit for later. Someone on mobile at 7 PM is probably within walking distance and ready to buy now. The device is a proxy for intent, not for optimization.

Instead of "oh, 80% mobile, better optimize for mobile," ask: "at what time of day are mobile searches happening, and is that when I want people to come?"

A dog groomer in Portland noticed that 45% of her mobile searches happened between 5 PM and 8 PM — exactly when she was closed. She started posting photos of recent grooms (including dogs' names and tags for "dog of the day") at 5 PM. Mobile searchers saw fresh content even when the business was closed. The next day's booking rate jumped by 22%.

## The One Metric That Predicts Revenue (It's Not What You Think)

I've looked at GMB data from about 40 local businesses over the past two years. If I had to pick one metric that correlates most closely with actual revenue, it's not views, not clicks, not even direction requests.

It's the "Website" action rate — how many people click through to your website from your GMB listing.

Here's the logic: people who click your website are deep in the funnel. They've seen your listing, they've decided you're worth investigating, and they want more information. Every other action (call, directions, booking) is a lower-commitment action.

A yoga studio in Denver had 1,200 views per month, 80 website clicks (6.7% website rate), and $5,400 in monthly revenue from GMB-sourced clients.

A competing studio had 2,400 views but only 40 website clicks (1.7% website rate), and $1,800 in monthly revenue from GMB.

More views, less revenue. Why? The second studio's GMB listing was complete but boring. Generic description. No "why us" in the profile. No sense of what made them different. People looked, didn't feel compelled, and moved on.

The fix for the second studio: rewrite the GMB description to answer one question — "Why should I choose you over the 15 other yoga studios within 3 miles?" Then add a "Book a Free First Class" button and a photo of the actual studio during a real class (not an empty room).

Three months later: website clicks went from 40 to 95 per month. Revenue went from $1,800 to $3,400. Not because views increased — they didn't. Because the quality of the click-through improved.

What to track: divide your website clicks by your total views. If that number is under 5%, your GMB listing is a teaser that nobody wants to follow up on. Fix the story you're telling.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need to check GMB analytics every week? I'm already overwhelmed.**

No. Check them once a month. Write down the three numbers that matter: total views, actions taken, and website clicks. Track the ratio between actions and views. If that ratio changes by more than 10% month-over-month, investigate. Otherwise, leave it alone. I've seen business owners waste 3 hours per week looking at data that doesn't change.

**Q: My competitor has more reviews and higher ratings. Should I just give up on GMB?**

No. Reviews matter, but they're not everything. I worked with a coffee shop in Portland that had a 4.2 rating (competitor had 4.7) but was generating 40% more revenue from GMB. Why? Their photos showed actual people drinking coffee, their menu was visible, and their "most relevant review" (the one Google shows first) mentioned how fast the service was. Reviews are a factor. They're not the only factor.

**Q: I'm a plumber. People either call me or they don't. Do GMB analytics matter for service businesses?**

Yes, but differently. Service businesses should focus on call tracking and direction requests. A plumber in Chicago found that 60% of his GMB calls came between 7 AM and 9 AM — before most competitors opened. He started answering his phone at 6:30 AM. His call-to-booking rate went from 30% to 65% in the first month. The data told him when to be available, not just how many people found him.

**Q: Can I compare my GMB analytics to other businesses in my category?**

Google doesn't show you competitor data directly. But you can get close by searching for your category in your city and seeing who shows up in the "local pack" (the three businesses shown at the top). If a competitor has 500 reviews and you have 50, you know where your gap is. If they have 40 photos and you have 6, you know what to fix. This takes 15 minutes and costs nothing.

**Q: I'm paying for Google Ads. Does GMB data matter for my ad strategy?**

Yes, and it might save you money. Pull your GMB query report. Write down every search term people use to find you organically. Go into your Google Ads account and pause any ad groups targeting those exact terms. You're paying to show up for searches where you already appear for free. I saved a client $400/month doing this.

**Q: Should I respond to every GMB review, even the negative ones?**

Yes, but not for the reason you think. Responding to positive reviews does little for the person who left the review — they already like you. It matters for the person reading reviews next week. A thoughtful response shows you care. For negative reviews, respond within 48 hours. Acknowledge the issue. Don't argue. Offer to fix it offline. I've seen a business turn a 1-star review into a 4-star update because they handled the response well.
