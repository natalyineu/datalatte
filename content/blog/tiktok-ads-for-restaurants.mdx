## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched restaurant owners burn through ad budgets on TikTok faster than a fry cook goes through a Friday night rush. The platform is cheap, but cheap doesn’t mean foolproof. Here are four mistakes I keep seeing in the wild.

**Mistake #1: Targeting “Everyone Who Eats Food”**

A barbecue spot in Austin, Texas came to me after spending $1,200 in three weeks with zero reservations. The owner set his targeting to “people interested in food and drink” within 15 miles. That’s basically everyone in Austin. His ads showed brisket shots to vegans, college kids on a ramen budget, and families looking for a sit-down restaurant when he only had counter service.

**What went wrong:** He treated TikTok like Facebook and assumed broad targeting would work. TikTok’s algorithm needs stronger signals than “this person has a mouth.”

**The fix:** We narrowed the audience to people within 5 miles who followed local food bloggers, searched for “Texas BBQ” in the last 30 days, and matched the age range of his actual customers (25–45). We also excluded anyone who engaged with vegan or vegetarian content. Total audience size: 14,000 people. That’s small enough for the algorithm to actually learn.

**The outcome:** $4,600 in revenue from ads over the next month. Cost per conversion dropped from $27 to $9. The owner told me he “didn’t realize you could tell TikTok to ignore people who hate meat.”

**Mistake #2: Silent Videos with Slow Cuts**

A fried chicken spot in Nashville ran ads that looked like a food photography reel. Slow crossfades between shots of chicken, coleslaw, and biscuits. No text. No sound. They spent $800 and got three clicks.

**What went wrong:** TikTok is a sound-on, fast-cut platform. When I worked on agency campaigns, we learned that any pause longer than 1.5 seconds in the first three seconds drops view-through rates by 40%. Silent ads with slow transitions get scrolled past instantly.

**The fix:** We reshot the ad in one afternoon. Twelve seconds. First two seconds: a person biting into the chicken with a loud crunch sound. Next three seconds: quick cuts of the kitchen staff laughing, seasoning chicken, and plating. Last seven seconds: a text overlay reading “Best fried chicken in Nashville — come get it” with a swipe-up button. We added captions because 30% of people still watch without sound.

**The outcome:** 47,000 views in the first week. 22 conversions. Cost per conversion: $4. The restaurant had to hire an extra line cook to handle the lunch rush.

**Mistake #3: Setting a Landing Page That Asks for the World**

A pizza place in Denver ran a killer ad showing cheese stretch across a slice for ten seconds. The ad performed great — 8,000 views in two days. Then people clicked through to a landing page that asked for their name, email, phone number, birthday, and whether they wanted to join a loyalty program. Clicks died. Zero conversions.

**What went wrong:** The owner used the same form he uses for email list signups. He thought more data was better. It wasn’t. Every extra field in a form drops conversion rate by 10–15%. On mobile, that number doubles.

**The fix:** We built a one-field landing page. “Enter your phone number to get this deal.” The deal was a free slice with any drink purchase, good for one week. When the customer showed up, the cashier confirmed the phone number and handed over the slice. No loyalty program pressure. No email opt-in.

**The outcome:** 134 conversions in two weeks. The landing page conversion rate jumped from 1.2% to 11%. The owner collected phone numbers and sent a single text blast the next week. That text brought in another $1,200 in sales.

**Mistake #4: Forgetting to Retarget Anyone**

A coffee shop in Portland, Oregon spent $950 on TikTok ads promoting a seasonal latte. They got 12,000 views and 80 clicks. The latte sold okay. But when I asked if they retargeted people who watched the ad but didn’t come in, the owner looked confused. “You can do that?”

**What went wrong:** Most small business owners think TikTok ads are a one-shot thing. You run it, you wait, you hope. They don’t realize you can show a second ad to the same people who watched the first one.

**The fix:** We created a retargeting campaign for anyone who watched at least 5 seconds of the original ad. The second ad showed a different angle — a customer drinking the latte while reading a book, plus a “Still thinking about it? It’s here until March 1st” text overlay. Budget: $5 per day for two weeks.

**The outcome:** 32 conversions from retargeting alone, at $3.50 per conversion. The shop sold out of the seasonal latte two weeks early. The owner told me she “couldn’t believe people needed to see a coffee twice before drinking it.” Yes. Yes they do.

## How to Pair TikTok Ads with Google Ads (Without Doubling Your Work)

If you’re running TikTok ads, you’re probably also running Google Ads. Or you’re thinking about it. I’ve managed accounts where someone runs Google Ads for “breakfast near me” and TikTok ads for b-roll of pancakes. Same goal, different platforms, usually no coordination. That’s leaving money on the table.

Here’s a structure that works for a restaurant in Chicago I worked with last year. She was a bakery owner spending $900 a month on Google Ads for generic terms like “cupcakes Chicago” and $300 a month on TikTok for video content. She was getting inquiries from both, but she couldn’t tell which one drove the sale.

We changed the approach. TikTok became the top-of-funnel driver. We ran ads showing a five-second clip of someone frosting a cake, then a text overlay: “Order for pickup — 15% off your first order.” The call to action sent people to a landing page with a simple Square checkout. No menu browsing. No “sign up for our newsletter.” Just the discount and a cart button.

Google Ads took the bottom of the funnel. We paused the generic “cupcakes Chicago” keyword — it was eating budget and converting at 1.8%. Instead, we bid on brand terms plus “order cupcakes near me” and “cupcakes for delivery today.” We added a sitelink extension pointing to the TikTok promotion. When someone searched for the bakery by name, they saw the ad with the same offer from TikTok.

The results were not subtle. In the first month, total ad spend across both platforms was $1,500. Revenue from ads: $7,200. The Google Ads cost per acquisition dropped from $18 to $9 because we stopped bidding on worthless keywords. The TikTok ads were cheap leads, and Google Ads closed the ones who were already ready to buy.

You don’t need a complicated attribution model for this. If someone sees your TikTok ad and later searches your bakery name on Google, that’s Google closing a TikTok lead. Run both. Let them do what they’re built for.

If you’re already using Google Ads, look at your search terms report this week. If you see “bakery near me” spending $12 a click with 20% conversion rate, that’s fine. If you see “cake” spending $8 a click with 1% conversion rate, pause it. That budget belongs on TikTok.

## Using Customer Data from Square or Toast to Build Better Audiences

Most restaurant owners I meet already use a POS system. Square, Toast, Clover — something that tracks who buys what and when. That data is sitting there, and you’re probably not using it for anything except reconciling the register.

Here’s what I did with a pizza shop in Brooklyn. They had Square data going back two years. Every transaction recorded: customer name, items ordered, total spent, time of purchase, payment method. They had never exported it.

We pulled the list of customers who had ordered at least three times in the last six months and spent over $50 total. That’s your repeat customer list. Then we uploaded that list as a custom audience in TikTok Ads Manager. The goal was not to advertise to them — they already know you. The goal was to build a “lookalike” audience of people with similar behaviors.

TikTok creates a lookalike audience by finding users whose purchasing behavior, location, and engagement patterns match your existing customers. We set the lookalike size to 1% — the closest match — which gave us about 8,000 people in Brooklyn.

Then we ran ads to that audience. No discount offer, because these people didn’t need a price incentive. Just a simple video of a customer walking in and ordering the same thing they always order. The tagline: “Your regular is ready.”

That campaign ran for three weeks. Budget: $200 total. Revenue attributed to the campaign: $3,400. The owner said people were walking in and saying “I saw that video and it looked like me.” That’s the lookalike working.

You can do this with any POS that lets you export customer data. Most do. Square’s export tool is under Reports > Sales > Export. Toast has a customer data export in the backend. Mailchimp also integrates with Square and Toast, so you can sync your email list directly to TikTok without exporting a CSV.

One warning: don’t upload your email list if you haven’t sent an email in six months. TikTok flags stale lists as low-quality and your lookalike will be weak. Clean your list first. Remove anyone who hasn’t purchased in over a year.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I run a diner in Ohio. My food looks good but not “viral” good. Will TikTok ads still work?**

Yes, because you don’t need your food to go viral. You need it to be noticed by 500 people within 3 miles of your diner who are hungry right now. I’ve seen ads of a guy flipping a burger on a flat top with zero special effects get 10,000 views and 50 conversions. The algorithm cares about relevance and completion rate, not production value. Film your best-looking dish in natural light, add a text overlay with your address and hours, and run it at $5 a day. Test for a week.

**Q: Do I need to post organic TikToks before I run ads?**

No, but it helps. If you have zero organic content, TikTok’s algorithm has less information about your restaurant. That doesn’t mean ads won’t work — it means you need to give the algorithm stronger signals through targeting. Skip organic entirely if you don’t have time. Just make sure your ad content feels native. Don’t use stock footage. Film yourself. That’s enough.

**Q: How much should I spend per day on TikTok ads?**

Start at $10 per day for one week. That’s $70. If you spend $70 and don’t get any conversions, you have a targeting problem or a creative problem, not a budget problem. Scale up to $25 per day only after you see consistent conversions under $10 each. I’ve seen restaurants spend $5 a day and get 15 orders in a week. I’ve also seen restaurants spend $100 a day and get zero because their ad was boring.

**Q: How do I track in-store visits from a TikTok ad?**

TikTok has a “Store Visit” conversion metric, but it requires a business account with a verified address and at least 1,000 store visits in the last 90 days. Most small restaurants don’t qualify. Instead, use a trackable offer. Set up a landing page with a coupon code like “TIKTOK15” and ask customers to show it at checkout. That’s the most reliable way to measure offline conversions. If your staff forgets to ask, put a sign at the register.

**Q: What if my food looks terrible on video?**

Then fix your food. No, seriously — if your dishes don’t look appetizing on camera, they probably don’t look appetizing on the plate either. Get a cheap ring light ($30 on Amazon) and shoot at a 45-degree angle. Use garnishes. Clean the plate edges. If it still looks bad, shift your ad focus to something else: the atmosphere, a fast turnaround time, a customer testimonial. Nobody buys ugly food on TikTok.

**Q: Can I run TikTok ads myself or do I need to hire someone?**

You can run them yourself. The TikTok Ads Manager dashboard is straightforward. Create an account, upload your video, set your budget, pick your targeting, and hit launch. The learning curve is two hours. What you can’t do yourself is consistently improve the ad based on data. Most owners run one ad, get middling results, and give up. The value of working with someone is the iteration cycle — testing different hooks, swapping visuals, adjusting targeting. If you have time to do that yourself, go for it. If you don’t, pay someone who will.
