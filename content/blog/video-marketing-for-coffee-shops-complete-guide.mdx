## What is Video Marketing for Coffee Shops?

Video marketing for coffee shops involves creating short, engaging videos that showcase your business, products, and personality. It's not about producing a Hollywood blockbuster; it's about creating content that resonates with your target audience and drives sales. Here are some ideas to get you started:

* Share behind-the-scenes footage of your baristas at work
* Highlight your unique menu offerings and promotions
* Showcase your cozy atmosphere and inviting decor
* Share customer testimonials and reviews

* Be authentic and engaging: Show your personality and have fun with it.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Posting Without a Strategy — The “Spaghetti at the Wall” Approach

I had a coffee shop owner in Portland, Oregon call me last year. Let’s call her Jenna. She ran a small café in the Alberta Arts District. She’d been posting two Reels a week for four months. Each one got maybe 50 views. She’d bought a ring light, edited in CapCut, added trending audio. And nothing happened.

I asked her what she wanted the videos to do. She paused. “I guess… get more people in the door?”

That’s not a strategy. That’s a wish.

**What went wrong:** Jenna was making content for the algorithm, not for a specific outcome. She was trying to go viral instead of trying to fill tables. She had no call to action, no offer, no way to measure whether a viewer became a customer. She was spending three hours a week on content that produced zero tracked revenue.

**The fix:** I told Jenna to reverse-engineer from the result. She wanted to sell more of her $5.50 lavender lattes on Saturday mornings. So we made three short videos: one showing the latte being made start to finish (15 seconds), one with a customer saying “this is the only thing I get here” (20 seconds), and one with a time-lapse of her Saturday morning line (10 seconds). Every video had the same caption: “Saturdays are for lavender lattes. Show this post at the counter for 10% off your first one. Good through March.” She used Square’s promo code feature to track redemptions.

**The outcome:** In three weeks, she redeemed 47 promo codes. That’s $258.50 in latte sales from three videos — and she kept the customers who came back without the discount. Her Saturday morning revenue climbed 23% over the following month. She stopped making random videos and started making *offers*.

If you’re posting without attaching an offer and a tracking method, you’re just burning time. Pick one product, one day, one discount. Measure it. Then do it again.

### Mistake #2: Filming in a Dead Space — The Atmosphere Problem

A pet groomer in Austin, Texas — I’ll call him Marcus — spent $600 on a videographer friend to shoot his shop. The footage looked clean. Good lighting. Nice shots of the grooming table. But when I watched the videos, I noticed something: no dogs. No owners. No sound of barking. No chaos. It looked like a surgical suite.

He was showing the space, not the *experience* of being in it.

**What went wrong:** Marcus’s videos felt sterile. A groomer’s selling point is trust — people need to believe their anxious golden retriever will be handled with care. You can’t communicate that with a quiet shot of clippers. You need energy. You need a dog wagging its tail. You need the moment a nervous poodle realizes it’s getting a treat.

**The fix:** I had Marcus reshoot using his phone. No hired videographer. He filmed three things over two days: a terrier running through the front door, a groomer scratching a Great Dane behind the ears, and the handoff where an owner saw their freshly groomed dog and laughed. He added text overlays: “We’ve groomed 3,000+ dogs. We know how to handle the nervous ones.” He posted it on Instagram and added a link in his bio to Booksy for booking.

**The outcome:** His booking rate through Instagram increased 40% in six weeks. His cost per acquisition went from $12 to zero — he wasn’t running ads anymore. The videos cost him nothing but his own time. The $600 videographer footage is still sitting on his hard drive.

Your video should make someone feel like they’re already in your shop. If it’s quiet, sterile, and empty, you’re telling people your business is boring. Show the mess. Show the noise. That’s where the trust lives.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the First 3 Seconds — The Scroll Killer

A hair salon owner in Nashville — I’ll call her Rachel — was making solid Reels. Great haircuts. Good lighting. But her views were stuck under 200. She couldn’t figure out why.

I watched her content. Every single video started with a slow pan across the salon — a shot of the chairs, the mirrors, the product shelf. It took four to five seconds before you saw a human. On a platform where people decide to stay or leave in under a second, that’s a death sentence.

**What went wrong:** Rachel was treating her videos like a commercial. She wanted to “build atmosphere” before she showed the haircut. But short-form video punishes slow starts. You have to give the viewer a reason to stay immediately — a person’s face, a dramatic before-and-after, text that says “watch the full transformation.”

**The fix:** I had Rachel start every video with the finished haircut — the reveal — as the first frame. Then she cut to the process. Every video opened with a client looking in the mirror, smiling. She added two-second text: “Before: bleached to death. After: healthy caramel balayage.” Engagement shot up. People wanted to see how the color was done, so they watched through to the end.

**The outcome:** Average views went from 180 to 1,400 within two weeks. One video hit 12,000 views — and she credited that specific post for three new clients in the following week, each booking a color service worth $185–220. She started blocking off a section of her schedule for “video appointments” where she built an extra ten minutes into the booking to film the reveal. That simple change added roughly $1,500 in monthly revenue from new clients who found her through Reels.

Three seconds. That’s all you get. If your video doesn’t hook someone immediately, nothing else matters. Start with the best part. Save the atmosphere for the middle.

### Mistake #4: Posting Without Local Search Intent — The Ghost Content Problem

A fitness studio owner in Denver — let’s call her Sarah — was making excellent workout videos. Her instructors were charismatic. The editing was tight. But she was getting zero leads from them. I asked if she’d ever seen a client say “I found you from a video.” She hadn’t.

**What went wrong:** Sarah’s content was generic. She posted “5-minute ab burner” and “HIIT cardio blast” — content that could have come from any fitness account in any city. Nothing told a viewer in Denver that this studio was five minutes from their apartment. No location tagging. No neighborhood references. No “come try our 7am class in Capitol Hill.” She was competing with the entire internet when she should have been competing with the other three studios within a two-mile radius.

**The fix:** I had her replace one generic workout post per week with a hyperlocal one. “Here’s what a Tuesday morning looks like at our Capitol Hill studio — we’re right off 12th and Broadway.” She tagged the studio location in every post. She added a Google Business Profile post linking to her video. She included the neighborhood name in the caption and the on-screen text.

**The outcome:** Within five weeks, three new members checked in and specifically mentioned seeing the Capitol Hill video. Each signed up for the $179/month membership. That’s $6,444 in annual recurring revenue from one video format change. Sarah now opens every video with “Hey Denver — this is where we train” — and her local discovery rate is up 60%.

If your video content could apply to any city, you’re not building a local business. You’re building a content account. Embed your city into the content itself. Make it impossible for a viewer not to know exactly where you are.

## How to Actually Track Video ROI (Without a Data Science Degree)

Most small business owners I talk to tell me video “feels like it’s working” but can’t point to a single number. That drives me crazy. You wouldn’t run a Facebook ad without checking the cost per click, but you’ll happily spend two hours filming a latte pour without knowing if it ever produced a sale. Let me give you the three ways to track this that don’t require a spreadsheet obsession.

**Method 1: Unique URLs through UTM parameters.**

This is the single most underused tool in small business marketing. When you post a video on Instagram, TikTok, or Facebook, include a link in your bio — but not a link to your homepage. Use a link to a specific page with a UTM code. For example, if you’re promoting your breakfast sandwich, create a link like: `yourwebsite.com/menu?utm_source=instagram&utm_medium=reel&utm_campaign=breakfast_sandwich_oct`. Then check Google Analytics or your dashboard to see how many people clicked that specific link. I did this for a Chicago bagel shop — they posted a Reel of their everything bagel with lox. They used a unique URL. In two weeks, 18 people clicked that link, and 6 of them ordered the lox sandwich. That’s $132 in attributed revenue from a 20-second video. The shop now uses UTMs for every single post. Total time to set up: ten minutes per link.

**Method 2: Square or Mailchimp promo codes.**

If you’re using Square for POS or Mailchimp for email, set up a promo code that’s only shared in your video. “Show this video at the register for 10% off any iced drink.” Or “Use code REEL20 at checkout for $2 off.” The code is unique to the video. You see how many times it’s used. That’s a direct revenue number. A coffee shop in Denver I work with used this to prove that one Reel about their cold brew brought in 34 promo redemptions over ten days — $3.80 average after discount, so about $129 in tracked revenue. Not life-changing, but when they scaled it to one promo video per week, they saw roughly $500 in monthly attributed sales. Without the code, they’d be guessing.

**Method 3: Customer ask.**

This sounds too simple, but it works. “How did you hear about us?” — put that question on your checkout screen, your intake forms, or just train your staff to ask. When a customer says “I saw your Reel,” write it down. Track it on a piece of paper or a quick Google Sheet. A hair salon in Portland did this with a clipboard at the front desk. In two months, 17 new clients credited a video. Average ticket was $140. That’s $2,380 from a clipboard and a question.

You don’t need a dashboard. You need a system. Pick one of these three. Do it for three months. You’ll either prove video is working — or you’ll stop wasting your time on content that isn’t.

## Platform Strategy: Where Your Video Belongs and Where It Doesn’t

I see small business owners trying to be on every platform and doing none of them well. You don’t need to be on TikTok, Instagram, Facebook, YouTube Shorts, and Pinterest. Pick two. Let’s talk about which two actually move the needle for local businesses.

**Instagram Reels: Non-negotiable for most local businesses.**

Instagram’s local discovery algorithm is aggressively good at surfacing content from nearby businesses. If you tag your location and use neighborhood hashtags (#austincoffee, #denverfitness), your Reels show up in the Explore pages of people within a ten-mile radius. For a coffee shop or salon, this is gold. A studio in Nashville I consulted for went from zero to 14% of new customers citing Instagram as their discovery source within eight weeks of consistent Reels. Every single one lived within three miles of the shop. Instagram rewards local relevance, not virality. Stop trying to get a million views. You only need views from the 50,000 people within driving distance.

**YouTube Shorts: Underrated for high-intent searches.**

People don’t search Instagram for “best haircut in Austin.” But they search YouTube. Shorts now show up in YouTube search results alongside long-form videos. A pet groomer in Austin started posting 30-second grooming tutorials on YouTube Shorts — “How to brush a golden retriever at home” and “What to look for in a professional groomer.” Those videos ranked for local search terms. Within three months, she got 22 booking inquiries directly from YouTube. Each booking averaged $85. That’s $1,870 from videos she didn’t think anyone would find. Plus, the videos live forever. An Instagram Reel is dead in 48 hours. A YouTube Short keeps ranking for months.

**TikTok: Only if you have the energy for it.**

I don’t recommend TikTok for most local small businesses unless you genuinely enjoy making short, scrappy, unpolished content. TikTok’s algorithm is less locally focused — it prioritizes entertainment over proximity. You can get a million views from people in India who will never visit your shop. That’s useless. If you do go on TikTok, make sure your bio clearly states your city and your offer. I’ve seen it work for a donut shop in Richmond, Virginia that does $4,000 in weekly revenue from TikTok traffic — but that owner posts three times a day and treats it like a part-time job. If that sounds exhausting, skip TikTok and lean into Instagram and YouTube.

**Facebook: Don’t post video. Boost it.**

Organic reach on Facebook is essentially dead for business pages. Don’t post Reels to your Facebook page and expect anyone to see them. Instead, take your best-performing Instagram Reel, upload it to your Facebook page, and run a small ad — $10 a day for seven days, targeted to people within 15 miles of your shop who like competing brands. A barbershop in Chicago did this with a video of a hot towel shave. The ad cost $70 total. It brought in 12 appointments, each averaging $55. That’s $660 in revenue for $70 in ad spend. Facebook still works — but only as a paid channel, not an organic one.

Choose two platforms. Master them. Ignore the rest.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need to be on video? I’m just a small coffee shop in a small town.**

Honest answer: no one “needs” to do anything. But if you’re competing with a Starbucks or a Dunkin’ or another café that’s producing content, you’re invisible to the 25–45 year olds who decide where to spend their Saturday morning based on what they see in their feed. I’ve watched a bakery in Hudson, New York (population 6,000) grow its Saturday morning line by 30% using one Reel per week. The scale of your town doesn’t matter. The phone in everyone’s hand does.

**Q: How much time should I budget per video?**

If you’re overthinking it, you’re doing it wrong. The best performing videos in my experience are shot in under 15 minutes, edited in under five minutes, and scheduled in under two. That’s 22 minutes per video. Two videos per week is under 45 minutes. If you can’t find 45 minutes a week to generate revenue, video marketing isn’t the problem — your schedule is. Block off 30 minutes on Monday and 30 on Thursday. Film everything in one take. Don’t over-edit.

**Q: I’m not comfortable on camera. Can I still do this?**

Yes, but you need to shift formats. Show your hands making the product. Film the espresso machine from a first-person angle. Use voiceover instead of talking to camera. I coached a florist in Portland who refused to show her face. She filmed her hands arranging bouquets over 30 seconds with a voiceover talking about flower sourcing. That single video got 4,200 views and 11 orders through her website. You don’t need to be the face of the brand. You need to show the craft.

**Q: What equipment do I actually need?**

Your phone. A tripod that costs $25 on Amazon. Natural light from a window. That’s it. I’ve seen professional videographers shoot for a client and produce content that performed worse than the owner’s shaky phone footage because it felt staged. Customers respond to real. Dirty countertops, steam rising from a machine, a barista laughing — that’s content. If you want one upgrade, buy a cheap lapel microphone for audio quality. Bad audio kills videos faster than bad video quality.

**Q: How often should I post?**

Two to three times per week is the sweet spot for local businesses. More than that and you’ll burn out. Less than that and you won’t build enough momentum for the algorithm to serve you locally. The consistency matters more than the quality or the length. Post one mediocre video on Monday and one mediocre video on Thursday, and you’ll outperform someone who posts one perfect video once a month. The algorithm rewards frequency for local discovery.

**Q: What if I try this and it doesn’t work in 30 days?**

Then you pivot. I’ve seen video fail when the product wasn’t right, when the offer was weak, or when the targeting was off. But I’ve rarely seen it fail when the business owner stuck with it for 90 days and iterated based on what the data told them. Give yourself a three-month experiment. Track the promos, the customer asks, the booking source. If after three months you’re not seeing at least a 10% lift in one metric you care about, move your time to another channel. But give it the 90 days. Most people quit in week four.
