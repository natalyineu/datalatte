## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've sat through enough quarterly reviews to know that wealth management firms make the same marketing mistakes over and over. These aren't small errors. These are the kind that drain budgets and push potential clients toward your competitors.

Here are four I see most often.

### Mistake 1: Treating All "High-Net-Worth" Like One Audience

A firm in Austin, Texas — let's call them Hill Country Wealth Partners — came to me after burning through $18,000 in six months on Facebook ads. They were targeting "affluent adults 45–65 in the Austin metro area." That was it.

The problem? "Affluent" in Austin means different things. A tech executive in her 40s with $2 million in liquid assets is not the same as a retired surgeon living off real estate income. Different fears. Different communication styles. Different timetables.

Hill Country's ads showed a generic retiree walking on a beach. The tech execs ignored it. The actual retirees thought it was too basic.

**The fix:** We split the campaign into two distinct offers. One for "wealth accumulators" (ages 35–50, still working, focused on tax strategy and growth) and one for "wealth preservers" (55+, focused on income generation and estate planning). Each got its own landing page, its own ad creative, and its own lead magnet.

**The outcome:** In the next three months, they spent $9,000 and acquired 11 qualified leads worth an estimated $4,200 each in annual fees. That's $46,200 in projected annual revenue from a campaign that cost half of their previous one.

### Mistake 2: Buying Expensive Leads From "Premium" Aggregator Sites

A firm in Nashville signed up for a lead generation service that claimed to deliver "pre-screened high-net-worth prospects." They paid $2,400 per month. In reality, they got people who filled out a form after clicking a random ad — most of whom had net worths under $100,000 and were shopping for mutual funds, not comprehensive wealth management.

I looked at their CRM. Out of 57 leads from this service over four months, exactly two met their minimum threshold. The cost per qualified lead? $4,800. They never closed either one.

**The fix:** Cancel the service. Redirect that $2,400/month into a Google Ads campaign targeting specific search queries like "estate planning attorney Dallas" or "trust administration services Nashville." Then pair it with a retargeting campaign on LinkedIn for people who visited the firm's "Services for Trustees" page.

**The outcome:** After the change, cost per qualified lead dropped to $340. In month three, they had seven meetings worth an estimated $1.5 million in new assets under management.

### Mistake 3: Building a Website That Talks About the Firm, Not the Client

A Chicago firm showed me their new website. It was beautiful. Photos of the managing partners. A timeline of the firm's history. A page explaining their investment philosophy in terms I barely understood.

I asked them what the first thing a prospective client sees. They said "our team photo."

That's the problem. High-net-worth clients do not care about your team photo. They care about whether you understand their specific situation.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the homepage around a single question: "What keeps you up at night?" The sections addressed specific pains — "Worried about estate taxes?" "Unsure if your portfolio will support your retirement lifestyle?" "Tired of being handed off to junior advisors?"

Each pain point linked to a case study that showed how the firm solved that exact problem for someone like them.

**The outcome:** Within six weeks, their contact form submissions tripled. The average time on page went from 42 seconds to 3 minutes 17 seconds. One consultation booked from the new site brought in a $2.8 million account.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Local Search Because "We Don't Need Walk-Ins"

A firm in Denver told me "wealth management doesn't need Google Maps presence. Our clients find us through referrals."

Here's what actually happened: A prospect Googled "wealth manager near Cherry Creek Denver." The firm's Google Business Profile had not been updated in two years. The hours said "closed" on weekends (who cares — but the algorithm cares). There were three reviews, average 3.2 stars, from accounts that looked fake.

The prospect chose the firm that showed up with 47 reviews, a 4.8-star average, regular photo updates, and responses to every review.

**The fix:** Claim and optimize the Google Business Profile. Encourage existing clients (who are happy — most of them are) to leave honest reviews. Respond to every review within 48 hours. Post one useful update per week — a market insight, a community event sponsorship, a profile of a team member.

**The outcome:** Google search impressions increased 340%. The firm got 18 calls in the first month from people searching "financial advisor Denver." Estimated value of the smallest account they closed from those calls? $410,000.

## Why Your Referral Strategy Is Probably Leaving Money on the Table

Most wealth managers I meet say "referrals are our primary source of new business" like it's a badge of honor. It's not. It's a sign you have no systematic approach to getting them.

Here's what I mean.

Referrals happen organically when you do good work. But "organically" means "when your client happens to remember you at the exact moment their friend mentions money." That's unreliable. You want referral volume to be predictable.

### The Two Referral Types You're Ignoring

**Type One: The "Warm" Introduction**

I worked with a firm in Portland, Oregon that had 230 clients. They had never once asked a client for an introduction. When I suggested it, the founder said "I don't want to be pushy."

I showed them a different approach. After a quarterly review where the client expressed satisfaction — we measured this with a simple "On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate our support?" — we asked: "We're looking to serve more people like you. Are there two people in your life who might benefit from a conversation like the one we just had? We're not asking for a sale — just an introduction over coffee."

The key: we asked at the moment of highest satisfaction, not as a random email.

**The fix:** Set up an automated Mailchimp sequence that triggers after a quarterly review email. Three days later, send a request for introductions. Offer a small gift — a $50 gift card to a local restaurant — as a thank-you, whether or not the referral converts.

**The outcome:** In the first quarter, they got 14 introductions. Three became clients within six months. AUM added: $4.7 million. Cost of gift cards: $700.

**Type Two: The "Invisible" Referral Partner**

Your clients aren't your only referral source. Other professionals are. I don't mean the obvious ones (CPAs, estate attorneys) who everyone courts. I mean the overlooked ones.

In Nashville, one firm started building relationships with divorce attorneys and elder law specialists. Divorce attorneys handle high-net-worth clients who often need a new financial advisor once the marriage ends. Elder law specialists work with families managing inheritance.

**The fix:** Identify five professionals in complementary fields. Invite each to coffee once per quarter. Not to pitch them — to understand their clients' needs. Offer to send your clients to them. Over time, they'll return the favor.

**The outcome:** After eight months, the Nashville firm had three divorce attorneys regularly sending them clients. Average account size from those referrals: $1.2 million.

## The Google Ads Strategy That Actually Works (It's Not What You Think)

I see wealth management firms spend thousands on Google Ads targeting keywords like "wealth manager" or "financial advisor [city]." These are expensive and convert terribly.

Here's why: People who search "financial advisor" are often shopping around. They're comparing options. They're price-sensitive. They might book a call but they're not ready to commit.

The real opportunity is different.

### Target Problems, Not Titles

A firm in San Diego was bidding on "San Diego financial planner." Cost per click: $18. Average cost per conversion: $450. They were getting leads, but most were small.

We changed the strategy. We identified three specific problems that high-net-worth individuals face:

1. "How to minimize capital gains when selling a business" — This happens when someone is preparing to exit. They're anxious. They need advice. They're not comparing prices.

2. "Trust administration and executor responsibilities" — This person is likely dealing with an inheritance or estate. They need guidance on a specific, time-sensitive matter.

3. "Retirement income planning for high net worth" — This person has assets and wants to know how to make them last, while minimizing taxes.

Each problem had a dedicated landing page. Each landing page had a specific offer: a free 30-minute "problem review" call, not a "free consultation." Small difference in wording, massive difference in perception.

**The fix:** Spend 70% of the budget on problem-specific keywords. Spend 30% on brand defense (your firm name, competitor names with "vs [your firm]").

**The outcome:** Cost per conversion dropped from $450 to $92. Average initial meeting size went from $350,000 in assets to $1.1 million. Why? Because people searching for specific problems are much closer to making a decision.

### The Retargeting Play

Most wealth managers stop after the first click. The person visits their site, leaves, and never hears from them again.

Use Google Ads retargeting with a specific timeframe. Show an ad to anyone who visited your "Trust Administration" landing page and didn't book a call. The ad should say something direct: "Still figuring out trust administration? We wrote a guide. Download it free."

The guide doesn't sell your services. It educates. It builds trust. After they download it, send them to a second landing page with a case study. Then offer the call.

**The outcome for a Dallas firm:** They spent $800/month on retargeting. Over three months, it generated six qualified leads. One of those was a $3.2 million account from a widow who had been struggling with her late husband's trust for eight months. She booked a call after reading the guide.

## Building an Email Sequence That Gets Open Rates Over 50% (Yes, Really)

Most wealth management email marketing is terrible. It's either a monthly newsletter with market commentary that nobody reads, or it's a sales pitch disguised as helpful content.

Your prospective clients are skeptical. They've already been pitched by every other advisor. They need proof — but more importantly, they need to feel understood.

### The Three-Email Sequence for New Leads

When someone fills out a form on your website, send this:

**Email 1 (Day 0):** "Thanks for reaching out. Here's what happens next" — Brief, professional. Include a calendar link for a 15-minute discovery call. Mention that you'll share a specific case study relevant to their situation. (Not a generic case study — one you selected based on what they clicked on your site.)

**Email 2 (Day 3, if no reply):** "I noticed you were interested in X. Here's a quick look at a similar situation we handled." — Attach a one-page PDF (not a 12-page deck). Show the problem, the solution, the outcome. Include specific numbers. No fluff.

**Email 3 (Day 10, if still no reply):** "One more thing — then I'll stop bothering you." — Share a two-sentence testimonial from a client with a similar profile. End with a direct question: "Would a quick 10-minute call be useful?"

**Outcome for a Charlotte firm:** Open rates of 54% on email 1, 42% on email 2, and 37% on email 3. They booked calls with 18% of leads that went through the sequence. Previous approach? One generic email with a pitch. Response rate: 3%.

### The Nurture Sequence for Leads Who Aren't Ready

Some leads aren't ready to talk. That's fine. But don't lose them.

Set up a 12-week nurture sequence in Mailchimp (or whatever you use). Every two weeks, send one piece of content:

- Week 1: "The #1 mistake we see high-net-worth families make with estate planning"
- Week 3: "How one client saved $127,000 in taxes by restructuring before a business sale"
- Week 5: "Three questions to ask before naming a trust executor"
- Week 7: "Why we don't believe in [common investment advice that's wrong]"
- Week 9: "What to look for in a wealth advisor (hint: it's not their fee structure)"
- Week 11: "Invitation: Attend our quarterly webinar on maximizing charitable giving"

Each email ends with a soft CTA: "Curious? Reply to this email and I'll send you the full case study."

**Outcome:** A Houston firm has 340 people in their nurture sequence. Over 18 months, 22 of those people became clients. Average account size: $890,000.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a solo advisor. I don't have time to manage all these campaigns. What's the minimum viable approach?**

Start with Google Business Profile optimization and one targeted Google Ads campaign focusing on a specific problem (trust administration or business exit planning). That's it. Spend two hours per week on it. If your budget is tight, skip paid ads and focus entirely on the referral system I described. A systematic referral request after quarterly reviews costs nothing but time.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from Google Ads for wealth management?**

It depends on your targeting and landing page quality. I've seen firms get their first qualified lead within a week. But the real results — the $1M+ accounts — take 30 to 90 days. The people searching for "capital gains on business sale Austin" are usually 60 days out from needing to make a decision. They need to trust you before they'll talk.

**Q: Should I be on Instagram? LinkedIn? What about TikTok?**

LinkedIn is where you need to be if you're serious. Not for posting about your lunch — for publishing short, specific posts about problems you solve. "How to handle concentrated stock positions without triggering a massive tax bill" gets read. "Happy Monday everyone" does not.

Instagram and TikTok? Only if you're targeting younger wealth accumulators (ages 30–45) who are still building. If your clients are 55+, skip visual social media.

**Q: All this sounds expensive. What if I only have $1,000/month to spend on marketing?**

I'll tell you what I tell every small business owner: Don't spend it on ads yet. Spend it on fixing your website and getting your Google Business Profile right. Then spend $300 of that $1,000 on a targeted LinkedIn ad campaign that sends people to a landing page with a case study. The rest goes into a nurture sequence in Mailchimp.

If you don't have any content, write one case study. One. That's enough to start.

**Q: I already have clients. Can I just ask them for referrals more often?**

Yes, but do it systematically. Set a recurring task in your calendar (or use your CRM) to ask for referrals from every client who gives you a 9 or 10 on a satisfaction survey. Don't ask randomly. Don't ask when they're frustrated. At the moment of highest satisfaction.

**Q: What's the single most cost-effective thing I can do this week?**

Pick up the phone and call five of your favorite clients. Not to pitch them. To thank them. Then ask if they know anyone struggling with the same problem you solved for them. That's it. Cost: zero. Potential: hundreds of thousands in assets under management.
