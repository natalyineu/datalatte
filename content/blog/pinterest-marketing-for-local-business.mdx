### Setting up a Pinterest Business Account

Getting started with Pinterest is relatively easy. First, create a business account and verify your website to access Pinterest Analytics and other business features. Then, optimize your profile with a profile picture, cover photo, and bio that reflects your brand and location.

<Callout type="tip">Use keywords relevant to your business and location in your bio to improve discoverability.</Callout>

### Creating Engaging Content

Content is king on Pinterest, and local businesses can create engaging content that showcases their products, services, and expertise. Use high-quality images and videos to showcase your offerings, and consider creating boards for different categories or themes.

### Using Hashtags Effectively

Hashtags help your content reach a wider audience on Pinterest, but using them effectively is key. Research popular hashtags in your niche and include them in your pin descriptions to increase visibility.

### Leveraging Pinterest Ads

Pinterest ads can help drive traffic and sales to your store, but it's essential to set a budget and target the right audience. Use Pinterest's targeting options to reach users who are interested in your products or services.

### Analyzing Performance

To maximize the effectiveness of your Pinterest marketing strategy, it's crucial to track your performance. Use Pinterest Analytics to monitor your metrics, such as engagement, clicks, and conversions.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched enough local business Pinterest accounts crash and burn to fill a very depressing spreadsheet. Here are the four most common ones, with real stories from owners who let me share their screw-ups so you don't have to make them yourself.

### Mistake 1: Treating Pinterest Like Instagram

**The story:** Michelle owns a hair salon in Austin, Texas. She spent three months pinning the same content she posts on Instagram — selfies at the chair, mirror shots of blowouts, a photo of her dog sleeping in the corner. She had 47 followers and zero website clicks. She was spending about $500/month on Canva Pro and a scheduling tool, convinced Pinterest "just wasn't for her audience."

**What went wrong:** Pinterest is a visual search engine, not a social platform. Michelle's pins had no keywords in the titles, no location tags, and no context. When someone in Austin searched "blonde balayage Austin," her pins didn't show up because they were titled "Friday vibes 🌸" with a generic photo.

**The fix:** I had her rewrite every existing pin title to include location and service keywords. "Friday vibes" became "Balayage for Blonde Hair — Austin TX Hair Salon | South Lamar." She created dedicated boards for each service — "Austin Hair Color," "Texas Wedding Hair," "Curly Haircuts Austin." She also added her salon address and phone number to her profile bio and linked to her Booksy booking page.

**The outcome:** Three months later, her Pinterest traffic increased 340%. She started getting booking requests directly from the platform — an average of 12 per month. Each booking was worth about $180 in services. That's $2,160 in monthly revenue from a platform she was about to abandon. Her cost: three hours of keyword research and rewriting pin descriptions.

### Mistake 2: No Local SEO in Pin Descriptions

**The story:** Tom runs a coffee shop in Nashville, Tennessee, called East Side Roast. He was posting beautiful photos of latte art, pastries, and his espresso machine. His pins got saves — hundreds of them. But the traffic to his website was basically zero. He was convinced Pinterest was only useful for national brands.

**What went wrong:** Tom's pins were visually great, but they were keyword deserts. A typical description read: "Morning coffee ☕ #coffeelover #latteart #morningvibes." Nobody in Nashville searching for "coffee shop Nashville" or "East Nashville espresso" would find those pins. Pinterest's algorithm couldn't connect his content to local searchers.

**The fix:** I had Tom add city, neighborhood, and service-specific keywords to every pin title and description. "Morning coffee" became "Best Latte Art in Nashville — East Side Roast | East Nashville Coffee Shop." He also created boards like "Nashville Coffee Guide," "Tennessee Roasters," and "East Nashville Brunch Spots." He claimed his Google Business Profile in his Pinterest settings and linked to his Yelp page.

**The outcome:** Within 60 days, his Pinterest impressions from users within 25 miles of Nashville increased by 500%. Website clicks went from zero to roughly 150 per month. He tracked coupon codes from Pinterest referrals — customers who mentioned they found him on Pinterest got 15% off their first drink. In three months, he redeemed 84 coupons, generating $1,260 in new revenue with zero ad spend.

In the first 90 days of the local-focused strategy, she booked $2,200 in new grooming appointments directly attributed to Pinterest. She now spends $150/month on local promoted pins and gets a 4:1 return.

## How to Track Pinterest ROI Without Losing Your Mind

Most small business owners I meet do one of two things: either they ignore Pinterest analytics entirely, or they obsess over vanity metrics like monthly viewers and total saves. Both approaches are wrong, and both will cost you money.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: Pinterest's native analytics are designed to make you feel good, not to tell you what's working. The platform happily shows you "total impressions" and "engaged users" because those numbers go up and up, making you think you're winning. But a million impressions from users in Bangladesh won't fill your coffee shop in Cincinnati.

**What actually matters for local businesses:**

First, track "outbound clicks" not "saves." A save means someone liked your pin. A click means someone visited your website. I had a client in Chicago, a vintage clothing store, who was celebrating 5,000 monthly saves but had zero website visits. Her pins were being saved to "aesthetic fashion" boards by users who had no intention of buying. Once she optimized for clicks instead of saves, her traffic went from zero to 400 visitors per month in six weeks.

Second, set up UTM parameters on every pin. This sounds technical but takes ten minutes. Use Google's Campaign URL Builder to add tags to your pin links — source=pinterest, medium=social, campaign=spring_sale. This lets you see exact Pinterest traffic in Google Analytics. I did this for a bakery in Portland and discovered that Pinterest was driving 30% of their online cake order form submissions — something their Instagram account had never achieved.

Third, use a dedicated landing page or phone number for Pinterest traffic. A dog trainer in Austin added a "Free Consultation — Mention Pinterest" option on his Booksy booking page. In his first month, nine people booked through that link. He knew exactly where they came from and exactly how much each was worth — $75 per initial consultation, with a 60% conversion rate to paid packages averaging $600 each.

**Tools to use for tracking:**

- Google Analytics (free) with UTM parameters — non-negotiable
- Pinterest Tag (free) — install it on your website to track conversions
- Square or Booksy appointment tags — mark bookings as "from Pinterest"
- Manual tracking — create a specific coupon code like PINTEREST10 for 10% off

I've seen too many business owners spend $500/month on Pinterest ads and have no idea whether they're getting $50 or $5,000 in return. Set up tracking before you spend a dollar. It's not glamorous, but neither is losing money quietly.

## Integrating Pinterest With Your Other Local Marketing Tools

Pinterest doesn't exist in a vacuum. The businesses that get real results from it are the ones who connect it to everything else they're already doing. Here's how to tie it into tools you probably already use.

**Mailchimp email sequences:** I worked with a hair salon in Nashville that was already sending weekly emails to 2,000 subscribers. We started embedding Pinterest pins directly into their emails — a "This Week's Best Looks" section with clickable images. The result: email click-through rates went from 1.8% to 4.2%. Subscribers were re-pinning the images, which drove new traffic to the salon's Pinterest profile. The entire integration took one hour to set up in Mailchimp's drag-and-drop editor.

**Yelp and Google Business Profile:** Your Pinterest profile should cross-reference your Yelp and Google listings. A coffee shop in Portland added "Find us on Yelp" to their Pinterest bio and embedded their Google Business Profile review link in board descriptions. When people searched "best espresso Portland" on Pinterest, they saw the shop's pins, clicked through to their profile, and found direct links to 5-star reviews. The owner told me she got three calls in one week from people who said they "found her on Pinterest, checked the Yelp reviews, and decided to come in."

**Square or point-of-sale systems:** If you use Square for transactions, you can track Pinterest-driven purchases directly. Set up a "How did you hear about us?" prompt at checkout with Pinterest as an option. A bakery in Austin added this to their Square register and discovered that 8% of daily transactions came from people who had seen their Pinterest pins — mostly of wedding cakes they'd posted months earlier. That's revenue they would have attributed to "word of mouth" or "passing by."

**Mailchimp abandoned cart emails for service businesses:** This works for more than e-commerce. A pet groomer in Denver started using Mailchimp's abandoned booking flow — if someone clicked a Pinterest pin and started the booking form on Booksy but didn't finish, they got an automated email 24 hours later. The email included the original Pinterest pin they clicked. Recovery rate: 12%. Each recovered booking was worth $85.

**Local ads synergy:** If you're running Google Local Services Ads or Facebook ads targeting your area, Pinterest can support them. A fitness studio in Denver ran a Google Ads campaign for "Denver personal trainer" at $3.50 per click. They created a Pinterest board called "Denver Fitness Before and After" and pinned to it consistently. The organic Pinterest traffic started showing up in their Google Analytics as assisted conversions — people who found them on Pinterest, then searched for them on Google, then clicked the ad. Their Google Ads cost per acquisition dropped by 22% because Pinterest was warming up their audience.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a plumber. Who's searching for plumbing on Pinterest?**

More people than you'd think. Pinterest has over 440 million monthly active users, and a significant portion are homeowners planning renovations, looking for DIY tutorials, or researching maintenance. I had a plumber in Austin create a board called "Bathroom Renovation Ideas Austin" with 50 pins of local projects, before-and-after photos, and a "signs you need a repipe" infographic. He got 12 booking requests from Pinterest in three months. The trick is to think about what your customers are planning before they need you — not what they search for when the pipe bursts.

**Q: Do I need to post every single day? I barely have time for Instagram.**

No. Pinterest rewards consistency, not frequency. Five pins per day is ideal, but you can schedule those in 30 minutes once a week using Tailwind (free trial, then $15/month). If you're really strapped for time, three pins per day will work. The people who fail are the ones who post 50 pins in one afternoon and then disappear for two months. Pinterest's algorithm penalizes inconsistent posting. Set a schedule and stick to it.

**Q: I don't sell products. I'm a service business — dentist, lawyer, therapist. Does this still work?**

Yes, but you have to adjust your content strategy. Lawyers: pin about legal topics your clients search for — "how to create a will in Texas," "divorce checklist," "small business LLC steps." Dentists: "teeth whitening before and after," "what to expect during a root canal," "kids dental care tips." Therapists: "anxiety management techniques," "signs of burnout," "how to find a therapist in Denver." The pins aren't selling a product — they're establishing expertise and trust. When someone in your area searches those topics, they find your pin, visit your website, and book a consultation. A therapist in Portland told me she gets three to five new clients per month from Pinterest, each worth $150 per session.

**Q: How long until I see actual results?**

Realistically, 60 to 90 days for organic traction. Pinterest is a slow-burn platform. Your first month will feel like shouting into a void. Month two, you'll start seeing impressions and saves. Month three, clicks and conversions should appear. If you're running promoted pins, you can see results faster — within two weeks — but you need to have a well-optimized profile first or you'll waste money. I tell clients to commit to 90 days before deciding whether to continue. Most who quit after two weeks are leaving money on the table.

**Q: Should I use video or static images?**

Both, but start with vertical images — 1000 x 1500 pixels, high resolution, with text overlay. Pinterest is rolling out more video features, and Idea Pins (their version of short video) can get high reach, but they take more time to produce. For a local business just starting out, create 30 good static pins first, then add one video pin per week. A coffee shop in Nashville got 4,000 views on a 15-second video of a latte being poured — but their static pin of the same latte with "Best Latte in East Nashville" text got 12 saves and 3 website clicks within a week.

**Q: I tried Pinterest years ago and it didn't work. What's different now?**

Probably nothing if you approach it the same way. The most common reasons older attempts fail: you posted generic content, you didn't use keywords, you gave up after two weeks, or you treated it like Instagram. Pinterest's algorithm has gotten better at serving local content, but the fundamentals haven't changed. If you reposted your Instagram feed, that won't work now either. If you never claimed your website or set up analytics, that's why you saw no results. Try again with proper local SEO, consistent posting, and UTM tracking. Most of my clients who failed the first time succeed the second because they stopped guessing and started treating it like a search engine.
