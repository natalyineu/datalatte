## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched probably fifty-plus photographers, florists, and wedding vendors burn money on Google Ads. Not because the platform doesn't work — because they made the same three mistakes I made myself at GroupM back when I was spending other people's budgets. Let me save you the tuition.

### Mistake #1: Bidding on Your Own Business Name

A wedding photographer in Portland, Oregon — let's call her Sarah — came to me frustrated. She was spending $600 a month on Google Ads and getting maybe two inquiries. She showed me her account. First thing I saw: she was bidding $4.50 per click on her own business name.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: if someone searches "Sarah Johnson Photography Portland," they're already looking for you. You don't need to pay for that click. Google gives you that placement for free through organic search if your site is halfway decent.

**What went wrong:** Sarah had "exact match" and "phrase match" keywords for her business name, her studio name, and her personal brand. She was paying $200–$250 a month for clicks she would have gotten anyway.

**The fix:** I paused all branded keywords. Added her business name as a negative keyword to every campaign so Google wouldn't show her ad when people searched for her directly.

**The outcome:** Her monthly spend dropped to $400. Her actual lead flow from non-branded searches stayed the same — because the people searching for "Portland wedding photographer" were still seeing her ads. She effectively got a 33% cost reduction overnight.

She reinvested that $200 into broader targeting and started getting 8–10 inquiries per week instead of 2.

### Mistake #2: Running Ads All Year Round

A wedding photography studio in Nashville spent $1,200 in January through March. They got three bookings. Total revenue from those bookings: about $4,500. That's a 3.75x return — not bad on paper.

But here's what they missed: January in Nashville is dead for wedding planning. Couples get engaged over the holidays, sure, but most of them don't start seriously searching until February or March. By April, search volume spikes. By June, it's a bloodbath out there.

**What went wrong:** They spread their $400 monthly budget evenly across twelve months. They were paying for clicks in November and December when search volume was low but CPCs were actually higher because fewer photographers were advertising (meaning less competition, but also less intent).

**The fix:** We front-loaded their budget. Concentrated 60% of annual spend into March through June. Cut September through December to maintenance mode — just enough to stay visible if someone was planning a winter wedding.

**The outcome:** Same $4,800 annual budget. But concentrated into peak season, they generated $18,200 in booked revenue. That's a 3.8x ROI instead of break-even. The key insight: you don't need to be everywhere all the time. You need to be visible exactly when people are ready to book.

### Mistake #3: Using Only Broad Match Keywords

A Denver-based wedding photographer told me she was getting 200 clicks a month but "all of them were wrong." She showed me her search terms report. Someone had searched "free wedding photography templates" and clicked her ad. Someone else searched "wedding photographer salary Denver" — not a booking, just somebody researching career options.

**What went wrong:** She had all her keywords set to "broad match." Google interpreted "wedding photographer Denver" as "anything vaguely related to weddings, photography, or Denver, plus anything Google's algorithm thinks is similar." She spent $380 on clicks from people who were never going to book.

**The fix:** Switched to phrase match and exact match. Added a long list of negative keywords: "free," "cheap," "jobs," "salary," "template," "DIY," "courses," "how to become."

**The outcome:** Clicks dropped from 200 to 80 per month. But those 80 clicks generated 12 inquiries, and 4 of those turned into bookings worth $9,600 total. Her cost per booking dropped from $380 (wasted) to $95.

Most guides skip this part — don't. Broad match is a trap for small budgets. It works when you have massive volume and machine learning data. On $500 a month, it's just an expensive way to learn what your customers don't search for.

### Mistake #4: Not Tracking What Actually Happens After the Click

A wedding photography duo in Austin told me they were getting "tons of clicks" but "no bookings." Their Google Ads dashboard showed 150 clicks in the past 30 days. They were convinced the ads were broken.

I asked three questions: What happens when someone clicks? A contact form. Do you have a thank-you page with a tracking pixel? No. Do you call or email every lead within 24 hours? "We try to, but sometimes it takes a few days."

**What went wrong:** They had no conversion tracking beyond the click. Google was reporting "clicks" as success. But Google doesn't know if that click turned into an email, a phone call, or someone closing the tab after seeing their prices.

**The fix:** We set up Google Ads conversion tracking on their contact form submission. Then we integrated it with their CRM (they were using HoneyBook, but you can use anything — Square, 17hats, even a spreadsheet). We also set up a Google Ads call extension so phone calls from ads were tracked separately.

**The outcome:** Real data showed that 150 clicks generated 22 form submissions and 14 phone calls. Of those 36 touchpoints, they actually converted 5 into booked weddings. That's a 14% close rate from ad leads. But they'd been ignoring those leads because they didn't have a system.

They changed their follow-up process: email within 1 hour, phone call within 4 hours. The close rate jumped to 22%. That's an extra $4,200 in revenue per month from the same ad spend.

## How to Structure Your First Campaign (Without Wasting Money)

Most photographers overthink this. Here's the simple structure that works for small studios with budgets under $1,000 month.

**Campaign 1: Capture ready buyers (70% of budget)**
- Keywords: "wedding photographer [city]," "book wedding photographer [city]," "affordable wedding photography [city]," "wedding photography packages [city]"
- Match type: Phrase match
- Ad schedule: Monday through Saturday, 9 AM to 9 PM in the couple's time zone
- Locations: Target within 50 miles of your city, but exclude areas you can't reasonably serve

**Campaign 2: Capture planners and referrals (20% of budget)**
- Keywords: "wedding planner [city]," "event coordinator [city]," "best wedding vendors [city]"
- These searchers aren't the couple themselves, but they influence decisions. A recommendation from a planner is worth gold.
- Use ad copy that says something like: "Vetted wedding photographer for your clients. Preferred vendor pricing available."

**Campaign 3: Protect against competitors (10% of budget)**
- Keywords: "[competitor name]," "[competitor studio name] reviews," "better than [competitor]"
- This is aggressive, but it works. When someone searches for your competitor, you can be there with a direct comparison.
- Just don't mention the competitor by name in your ad copy — Google frowns on that.

A photographer in Chicago used this exact structure with a $500 monthly budget. After three months, their cost per lead dropped from $18 to $7. They booked 8 weddings from those leads. Total ad spend: $1,500. Total revenue from those bookings: $24,000. That's a 16x return.

## Tools That Actually Help (And Which Ones to Skip)

I've tested most of the tools that claim to "automate" Google Ads for small businesses. Here's what works and what doesn't.

**Google's own tools (free, use them):**
- Google Keyword Planner — for research, not for exact CPC estimates (those are always optimistic)
- Google Ads Editor — for bulk changes without messing up your live campaign
- Google Analytics 4 — set up conversion tracking, look at which keywords actually lead to booked calls
- Google Business Profile — this is free and most photographers underutilize it. Make sure your profile links to your booking page, not your homepage

**Third-party tools (some worth the money):**
- **Booksy** if you're doing mini-sessions or engagement shoots alongside weddings — it syncs with Google Ads call tracking
- **Mailchimp** for retargeting people who clicked your ad but didn't book. I worked with a studio in Denver that sent a "we're still thinking about you" email sequence to ad clickers. Their close rate on retargeted leads was 3x higher than cold leads
- **Canva** for ad images — but don't overdesign. A simple photo with your name and city outperforms a heavily styled graphic 80% of the time in my experience

**Tools to skip:**
- Any tool that promises "AI-optimized bidding" without telling you how it works. If it can't explain why it's bidding $5 instead of $3, don't trust it
- "Google Ads automation" services that charge a flat monthly fee. If they're managing your $300 budget for $200, they're not doing anything you couldn't do in an hour
- Most landing page builders — use your own website with a clear booking form. Adding a middle step costs you conversions

## When to Scale (And When to Walk Away)

Here's the conversation I have most often with photographers: "I'm spending $500 a month and getting 3 bookings. Should I spend $1,000?"

The answer depends on one number: **cost per acquisition**.

If you're spending $500 and getting 3 bookings, your CPA is about $167. That's fine if your average booking is $2,500. But if you spend $1,000 and only get 4 bookings (CPA $250), you've hit diminishing returns.

**Scale when:**
- Your CPA is stable or decreasing as you increase spend
- Your close rate from ad leads is above 20% (meaning you're good at converting)
- You have capacity to actually shoot more weddings (this sounds obvious, but I've seen photographers book work they couldn't deliver)

**Walk away when:**
- You're spending more than 40% of your average booking value on ads per sale
- Your close rate is below 10% (fix your website or your pricing first)
- You're in a market where the average CPC is above $5 and you can't afford enough clicks to get statistically meaningful data

A photographer in San Francisco had a $1,200 monthly budget. Average CPC in that market is around $4.50. She was getting 266 clicks, maybe 15 leads, and booking 3 weddings per month. CPA was $400. Average wedding booking was $3,800. That's a 9.5x return — totally worth scaling.

But a photographer in a smaller market like Boise was paying $1.80 per click. Same budget got her 666 clicks, 25 leads, 5 bookings. CPA was $240. Her average booking was $2,200. She was only getting 9x return, but the volume was lower because the market is smaller. She was better off investing in local SEO and word-of-mouth.

There's no universal answer. It depends on your market, your prices, and your conversion skills.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can't I just rely on Instagram and word-of-mouth instead of Google Ads?**
You can, but you're leaving money on the table. Instagram is a discovery channel — people see your work and maybe remember you when they get engaged. Google Ads is an intent channel — people are typing "wedding photographer near me" right now and will book someone within the week. I've seen studios get 50% of their new business from Google Ads while only spending 15% of their marketing budget on it. The other channels feed the top of the funnel; Google captures the bottom.

**Q: I'm a wedding photographer, not a florist. Will this still work for event photography?**
Yes, with a few tweaks. If you shoot corporate events, change your keywords to "corporate event photographer [city]" or "conference photographer [city]." If you shoot elopements, target "elopement photographer [city]" and "intimate wedding photography." The logic is the same: find people who are actively searching, show up with a clear offer, and follow up fast.

**Q: Do I need a big budget to start?**
No. Start with $300 a month. That's $10 a day. At $2.50 per click, that's 4 clicks per day. You need about 100 clicks to get statistically meaningful data on what works. That's 25 days. After a month, you'll know if your keywords are right, if your ads are getting clicks, and if your landing page is converting. If it's working, scale up. If it's not, fix the weak point before spending more.

**Q: How do I know Google isn't just stealing my money with fake clicks?**
Google's invalid click protection catches most of that. But you should still monitor the "search terms" report weekly. If you see clicks from IP addresses that look suspicious (multiple clicks from the same source within minutes), add those as negative placements. Also, use a tool like Lucky Orange or Hotjar to watch session recordings — if a click is from someone who stays for 1 second, that's a bad click. Google will refund you for invalid clicks if you report them within 30 days.

**Q: What if I'm in a small town and nobody searches for wedding photographers?**
Then Google Ads isn't for you. Focus on Google Business Profile, local SEO, and Facebook/Instagram. I worked with a photographer in rural Montana who spent $200 a month on Google Ads and got zero leads. We moved that budget to Facebook targeting engaged couples in nearby cities. She started getting leads within a week. The lesson: use the channel that matches your market.

**Q: Should I bid on keywords for other types of photography?**
Only if you actually do that work. If you're a wedding photographer but also shoot family portraits, create a separate campaign for "family photographer [city]" with its own budget and ad copy. Don't mix them — if someone searching for "senior portraits" sees your wedding ad, they'll assume you don't do seniors. Be specific in your keywords and your landing pages.

**Q: How long until I see results?**
Clicks start within hours. Inquiries start within days. Bookings take weeks — couples typically research 2–4 weeks before booking. Don't panic if you don't get a booking in the first week. Focus on getting leads. If you're not getting leads after 2 weeks, fix your ads or your landing page. If you're getting leads but not bookings, fix your follow-up process.
