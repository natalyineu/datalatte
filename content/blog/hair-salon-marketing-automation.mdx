## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched a lot of small businesses light money on fire with marketing automation. Not because the tools didn't work — because they set them up wrong, then blamed the software. Here are the four most common mistakes I see with hair salons, and what happened when real owners fixed them.

### Mistake #1: Automating Everything Before You Have Decent Data

A five-chair salon in Austin, Texas called "Corte Moderno" had the right instinct. The owner, Maria, bought a fancy CRM with automated email sequences, SMS reminders, and a loyalty point system. She spent about $350/month on the software and another $600 on setup with a freelance tech consultant.

The problem? She had been running the salon for four years using a spiral notebook and the calendar on her personal iPhone. When the consultant asked for email addresses, Maria had maybe 200 from a paper sign-up sheet. No purchase history. No visit frequency. No product preferences. She had her technician load all the data into the CRM anyway.

**What went wrong:** The automation sent generic promotional emails to 200 people who hadn't visited in over a year. "50% Off Color Treatment" went to someone who had moved to Seattle in 2022. Birthday coupons went out on the wrong dates. The salon spent $950 in the first month and got exactly two bookings from it — both from regulars who would have come in anyway.

**The fix:** I told Maria to pause all automation and spend six weeks just collecting clean data. She offered a $10 discount for every client who filled out a digital intake form with email, birthday, preferred services, and how they found the salon. She stopped the CRM from sending any emails. For sixty days, she just recorded.

**The outcome:** She ended up with 1,200 clean client profiles. When she turned the automation back on, segmented by service preference and visit frequency, it cost her $370/month and generated $3,800 in incremental revenue in the first quarter. The birthday campaign alone brought in $1,100 from people who hadn't booked in six months.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Booking Software Integration

A busy salon in Portland, Oregon called "Willow and Blade" had 800 square feet and three chairs. The owner, Jenna, used Booksy for bookings because that's what her stylists were used to. She used Mailchimp for email because it was free. She used Instagram DMs for client communication because it felt personal. And she kept a whiteboard with client notes because one of her senior stylists "didn't trust computers."

**What went wrong:** Jenna was running three separate systems that never talked to each other. When a client booked through Booksy, the email address didn't automatically go into Mailchimp. When a stylist updated a note on the whiteboard, nobody else knew. When Jenna wanted to send a "we miss you" email to clients who hadn't visited in six weeks, she had to manually cross-reference Booksy reports against Mailchimp lists. She told me she spent about eight hours per month doing data entry that should have been automatic.

**The real cost:** Jenna was losing 6–8 recurring clients per quarter because she simply forgot to follow up after their third visit — the exact point where retention usually kicks in. At an average ticket of $85 per visit, that's roughly $5,100 per year in lost revenue.

**The fix:** I pushed her to switch everything to Square Appointments, which integrates with Square's own email marketing tools. It cost her $0 in additional software because she was already processing payments through Square. She set up a single automation: when a client books a second appointment, they automatically get tagged as "repeat," and after their third, they enter a "loyalty" segment that gets a monthly promotion.

**The outcome:** Three months later, Jenna's repeat visit rate went from 38% to 54%. Her monthly email send time dropped from eight hours to zero — she just reviewed the report. The loyalty segment generated $4,200 in additional revenue over six months. And she got rid of the whiteboard.

### Mistake #3: Sending "Personal" Messages to Everyone in Your Database

A well-established salon in Nashville — eight chairs, strong Yelp reviews, been around since 2014 — tried automated re-engagement emails. Their manager, Derek, bought a template from a marketing agency that promised "personalized client outreach at scale." The template used merge fields: *Hey &#123;&#123;First Name&#125;&#125;, we noticed you haven't visited in &#123;&#123;X&#125;&#125; months!*

**What went wrong:** Derek's database had 2,300 contacts accumulated over eight years. About 400 of them were former employees, vendors, and people who had come in once for a wedding updo in 2016. The automation sent "We miss you!" to Derek's ex-wife, who had not been in the salon since the divorce. It sent a "color refresh" offer to a man who only ever got beard trims. And it sent the same message to someone who had literally been in the salon the previous week — because the data sync with the booking system had a 48-hour delay.

**The fix:** Derek and I spent an afternoon cleaning the list. We removed anyone who hadn't booked in 24 months. We added service tags based on actual booking history. Then we set up three different re-engagement sequences: one for haircut clients (offering a $15 discount on their next visit), one for color clients (offering a free gloss treatment), and one for facial/waxing clients (offering a free brow shape). Each had a frequency cap: no more than one email every 30 days.

**The outcome:** The campaign cost $270 in email software and labor to clean the list. It generated $1,800 in bookings in the first three weeks. But more importantly, it stopped annoying the clients who mattered. Derek told me two clients actually called and said, "Thank you for finally sending me something relevant."

### Mistake #4: Setting Up Automation and Never Looking at the Numbers

A Brooklyn salon with six chairs automated their entire client communication system through a service called Zenoti. They set it up in January and by June had no idea if it was working. The owner told me, "The system sends stuff, right?"

**What went wrong:** The automation was sending exactly one campaign: a generic "book now" email to everyone every two weeks. Open rates had dropped to 11% by month three. Click-through rates were under 1%. No one was monitoring this because the owner assumed the software would "just work." They were paying $420/month for a system they had stopped auditing.

**The fix:** We set up a 15-minute monthly review: open rates, click-through rates, booking conversion rate, and unsubscribe rate. When the owner saw the numbers, she finally agreed to change the messaging. She tested a subject line that read "Your stylist asked about you" instead of "Book your next appointment now." She also added a simple "we have last-minute openings" email that went out on Wednesday mornings.

**The outcome:** Within six weeks, open rates hit 34%. The Wednesday morning email produced an average of $520 per send in same-day bookings. The email that had been bleeding unsubscribes started actually working. The owner saved $420/month by downgrading to a cheaper plan after I pointed out she wasn't using 80% of Zenoti's features. She just needed to look at the data.

## The Right Way to Approach Email Automation (Your Highest ROI Channel)

If you only automate one thing this quarter, make it your email. Not social media, not SMS — email. Here's why: email has the highest ROI of any channel for local service businesses, and hair salons in particular leave money on the table by not using it intentionally.

### What Actually Works for Hair Salons

The salons that see real revenue from email treat it less like a newsletter and more like a personal assistant who knows your clients by name. Here's a system that works across three salons I've advised, from a budget shop in Denver to a high-end color studio in Chicago.

**The welcome sequence.** When a new client books their first appointment, they should get three emails: one confirming the booking (day 0), one with preparation tips for their service (day 2), and one asking for feedback (day 5 after service). The prep email is the one nobody sends. It's also the one that builds trust. A Denver salon saw a 22% increase in first-time clients' second visit rate just by adding the prep email. No discount, no coupon — just information about what to expect and how to make the color last longer.

**The "we have an opening" email.** This is the single highest-converting email I've ever seen for salons. Send it Wednesday morning for weekend openings and Friday morning for weekday openings the following week. Use a specific subject line: "Thursday at 2pm just opened up with Jenna." Not "Appointment available." Specificity works because it creates urgency and specificity. A salon in Nashville tested this against a generic "book now" email. The specific opening email had a 41% booking rate. The generic one had 2%.

**The seasonal promotion.** Once per quarter, send an offer tied to a specific service that changes with the season. March: "Winter hair repair treatment, $25 off." June: "Sun damage repair gloss, free add-on." September: "Pre-holiday trim, $10 off." November: "Holiday blowout, buy one get one half off." These don't need to be complicated. The salon in Denver runs exactly four promotion emails per year and they account for 18% of her annual color service revenue.

**The anniversary email.** One year after a client's first visit, send them a note: "A year ago you came in for your first time. Thanks for being part of the salon." Include a $10 credit for their next appointment. Two salons I worked with saw a combined $5,400 in revenue from this single email campaign in 2024. The cost: the software was already paid for, and the $10 credits cost them $240 total.

### What Tools to Use

For most small salons, Mailchimp's free plan covers up to 500 contacts. That's enough for your active clients. If you have more than 500, Mailchimp's Essentials plan costs $13/month. Square's email tool is free if you process payments through Square. Constant Contact is fine if you need better phone support but expect to pay around $20/month.

Here's the rule: do not buy any tool that costs more than $30/month until you have at least 1,000 email subscribers and a conversion rate above 3%. The fancy tools don't matter when you have 14 people on your list.

The one exception to the low-budget rule: Booksy's automation features. If you're already paying for Booksy, use their built-in reminder and follow-up tools before you buy anything else. Most salon owners I talk to are paying for Booksy and paying for another tool on top of it. That's $50–100/month they're lighting on fire.

## Google Ads for Hair Salons: Spend $500, Get $1,800 Back (Or Waste It)

Google Ads terrifies most salon owners. I understand why. The interface is ugly, the terminology is confusing, and one wrong click and you've spent your entire marketing budget on someone searching for "cheap haircut" in a different state.

But here's the thing: Google Ads works very well for hair salons in cities with populations between 50,000 and 2 million. Below that, there's not enough search volume. Above that, the competition pushes costs too high for a single-location salon. If you're in Austin, Nashville, Portland, Denver, or similar-sized metro, ads can work.

### The Only Campaign Structure You Need

Create exactly one campaign. Call it "Salon Services." Target a 10-mile radius around your salon. Use two ad groups:

- **Ad group 1: Service-based keywords.** "Haircut [city]," "color treatment [city]," "blowout [neighborhood]," "balayage specialist [city]." These are people who know what they want. Your ad should say exactly what service you're promoting and the price.
- **Ad group 2: Intent-based keywords.** "Best hair salon [city]," "hair salon near me," "salon that does [specific service]." These are people comparison shopping. Your ad should emphasize reviews, awards, or years in business.

The budget split: 70% on service keywords, 30% on intent keywords. Test for 30 days. If service keywords convert at 5% or higher, move more budget there. If not, shift to intent.

### The Mistake Everyone Makes

They bid on the word "haircut" without a location modifier. This is the fastest way to waste money. Google will happily show your ad to someone in Miami if you're in Chicago. The word "haircut" on its own costs $3–7 per click in most markets. The phrase "haircut Portland" costs $1.50–3 and converts 4x better.

A salon in Austin was spending $1,200/month on Google Ads with a generic budget. They were getting clicks, but the phone wasn't ringing. I checked their search terms report. They had spent $340 on clicks from people searching for "haircut ideas" — blog readers, not buyers. Another $280 on "men's haircut styles" — more blog readers. When I narrowed the keywords to "men's haircut Austin" and "haircut downtown Austin," their cost-per-booking dropped from $28 to $9.

### What to Track

Do not track "clicks." Tracking clicks is like measuring how many people walked past your window. Track bookings. Use call tracking (Google provides this for free with Google Ads) and appointment booking links. If someone clicks your ad and books online within 7 days, that's a conversion. If they call and book, that's a conversion. Everything else is noise.

A salon in Denver ran ads for six weeks without call tracking. They thought ads were underperforming. Once we added call tracking, they discovered that 40% of their bookings happened over the phone from ad clicks. The ads were generating $3,800/month in revenue. They just didn't know it.

### The Real Numbers

After optimizing for location and service keywords, the Denver salon spent $475/month on Google Ads and got $1,820 in direct bookings attributable to ads. The Austin salon spent $390/month and got $2,100. These are not "lifetime value" numbers. These are direct bookings that happened because of the ad.

For a single-chair or two-chair salon, start with $300/month. For three to five chairs, budget $500–800/month. If you're not seeing at least a 3:1 return after 60 days, pause the campaign and fix your keywords or your ad copy.

## Yelp: How to Stop Hating It and Start Using It

Every small business owner I've ever met has a complicated relationship with Yelp. They hate the sales calls. They hate that one bad review from three years ago still shows up. But here's the uncomfortable truth: Yelp drives real business for hair salons, especially in cities where people actually read reviews before booking.

I worked with a salon in Chicago that spent $2,200/month on Yelp advertising. When I audited their account, they had no idea where the money was going. Their Yelp page showed the same photos from 2018. They had 12 unanswered reviews. Their business hours were wrong.

### What to Actually Do on Yelp

First: update your page. Business hours, services, prices, photos. Take new photos. Not iPhone shots of a mirror. Actual good photos of the space, the stylists working, the products you sell. A Portland salon updated their photos and saw a 17% increase in Yelp-driven bookings within three weeks. No ad spend increase. Just better photos.

Second: respond to every review. Every single one. The positive ones get a "thank you, hope to see you again." The negative ones get a genuine response within 48 hours. "I'm sorry your color didn't turn out as expected. Please call the salon at [number] and ask for me directly. I want to make this right." Do not argue. Do not explain. Just fix it.

A salon in Nashville with 238 reviews had a 3.5-star average. They started responding to every review consistently. Within five months, their average rating went to 4.2. Not because the reviews got better — because the negative reviewers who got a personal response often updated their review or came back and gave a second chance.

Third: Yelp ads. I'm not anti-Yelp ads, but I'm anti-spending money without tracking. Start with the "cost per lead" model instead of "cost per click." Yelp will charge you per phone call or per form fill that comes from your ad. This is safer than paying for impressions or clicks that may or may not convert.

### The Numbers

The Chicago salon that was wasting $2,200/month? We dropped them to $500/month on cost-per-lead Yelp ads. Their monthly revenue from Yelp dropped from $3,100 to $2,400 — but their profit went up because they were spending $1,700 less. The salon owner told me, "I wish I had called you two years ago."

A four-chair salon in Portland spends $350/month on Yelp ads and generates roughly $800/month in bookings. Not their biggest channel, but profitable. They keep it running because Yelp users tend to be higher-ticket clients — they book color services at an 11% higher rate than Instagram-driven clients.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a two-chair salon in a small city. Do I really need automation?**

Probably not for the first 12 months. If you have fewer than 300 active clients and you remember most of their names, you don't need software. What you need is a system. Write down when people last visited. Text them yourself. A simple spreadsheet and 30 minutes of manual follow-up per week will outperform bad automation every time. Come back to this article when you hit 400 active clients.

**Q: What if my clients hate automated messages?**

They hate automated messages that feel automated. They don't hate "Hey, your appointment is tomorrow at 10am." They don't hate "We noticed it's been six weeks since your last cut — want to come in?" The difference is relevance. If you send irrelevant messages to people who haven't been in your salon in 18 months, yes, they'll unsubscribe. If you send useful, timely reminders to people who actually come in, they'll appreciate it. I've audited 30 salons' email lists and the average unsubscribe rate for a good automated sequence is under 0.5% per send.

**Q: What's the minimum I should spend per month on marketing automation tools?**

Your first $0 should go toward using free tools: Mailchimp's free tier (up to 500 contacts), Square's free email tool if you process payments through them, and Google's free My Business listing. If you're paying for nothing else, these three will cover 80% of what you need. When you outgrow them, expect to spend $20–40/month on email, $300–800/month on Google Ads, and maybe $200–500/month on Yelp or Instagram ads. Do not buy a $200/month CRM until you have at least 800 active clients.

**Q: How long until I see results from automating my marketing?**

The wrong answer: "It depends on your goals." The real answer: Three to six weeks if you do it right. The setup takes a week. The first automated emails go out in week two. By week six, you should see either an increase in repeat bookings, a reduction in no-shows, or both. If you don't see anything by week eight, something is wrong. Either your data is bad, your offers are weak, or you're sending to the wrong people.

**Q: What about social media automation? Should I schedule Instagram posts?**

Schedule your posts so you're not posting at 11pm. That's fine. But do not automate DMs. Do not automate comments. Do not automate responses to reviews. Social media works for salons because it feels personal. The second a client realizes they're talking to a bot, they're gone. I've seen salons lose clients over automated direct messages that sounded like a telemarketer. Post on a schedule. Respond manually.

**Q: I tried automating my email once and it didn't work. What did I do wrong?**

You probably did one of three things: sent the same message to everyone, sent too often, or didn't clean your list before starting. Most failed automation attempts are caused by bad data, not bad software. Go back and clean your list. Remove anyone who hasn't visited in 12 months. Segment by service type. Send one email per week maximum for the first 90 days. Then see if it's working. If it's still not working after that, you might just need different ad copy or a better offer.
