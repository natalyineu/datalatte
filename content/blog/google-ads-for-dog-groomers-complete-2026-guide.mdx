1. **Create a Google Business Profile** (free):
   - Add your address, hours, and 10+ photos of your grooming setup.
   - Verified listings boost ad credibility and reduce cost-per-click (CPC) by 25%.

2. **Choose a campaign type**:
   - **Location-based Search Campaigns** (e.g., "dog grooming services near me")

3. **Budget for 2026**:
   - Start with **$50–$100/day**. Pet industry CPC averages **$1.20–$2.50**, but location targeting can lower this.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Running “Brand Awareness” Ads Instead of Action Ads

A groomer in **Portland, OR** came to me after three months of Google Ads. She’d spent $1,800 total and gotten exactly two phone calls. She was furious. I looked at her account and saw the problem in thirty seconds: she was running a “brand awareness” campaign with the goal set to “Impression Share.” Her ads were showing up for queries like “how often to groom a poodle” and “best brush for shedding.” Nothing had a call to action. No “Book Now.” No phone number clickable. Just information.

**What went wrong:** She cared about people seeing her name. She paid for eyeballs, not appointments.

**The fix:** I switched everything to Search campaigns with Conversion tracking set to “Phone Calls” and “Bookings.” We used call extensions, location extensions, and made every ad say “Book Your Grooming Appointment” or “Call Now for Same-Day Spots.” We also switched to phrase match on keywords like “dog grooming Portland” and “Poodle groomer SE Portland.”

**The outcome:** Over the next 30 days, she spent $620 and got 17 confirmed bookings worth $2,380 in revenue. Her cost per booking went from $900 down to $36. She called me and said “I feel like an idiot for wasting $1,800.” I told her that’s the industry average for unmanaged accounts.

**Takeaway:** If your ad doesn’t end with someone calling, clicking a booking link, or filling out a form, you’re running a charity for Google.

### Mistake 2: Targeting “Dog Grooming” Without Negative Keywords

A groomer in **Denver, CO** had been running Google Ads for six months. His budget was $500/month. He was getting clicks — about 150 per month — but almost no bookings. I looked at his search terms report. It was brutal.

He was showing up for: “dog grooming near me free,” “DIY dog grooming,” “how to groom a golden retriever,” “dog grooming certification online,” “dog grooming prices,” “petco grooming coupon.” Every single one of those queries cost him between $2 and $5 per click, and not one of them was ever going to turn into a paying customer.

**What went wrong:** He didn’t add any negative keywords. Google’s algorithms love spending money. If you don’t tell them what *not* to show for, they will burn your budget on DIY tutorials and competitor searches.

**The fix:** We built a massive negative keyword list in one afternoon. I’m talking 200+ terms: “free,” “DIY,” “tips,” “course,” “training,” “school,” “certification,” “cost,” “price list,” “near me open now” (if you don’t actually take walk-ins), “self-serve,” “mobile groomer” (if you’re a shop), “Petsmart,” “Petco,” “cheap,” “budget,” “how to.” We also excluded mobile app categories and “people who searched for competitor brands.”

**The outcome:** His next month’s spend dropped to $380 because he wasn’t wasting on junk clicks. But his conversion rate jumped from 2% to 14%. He booked 22 appointments worth $3,080 in revenue. Cost per booking went from $250 to $17.

**Takeaway:** Negative keywords are not optional. They are the difference between an ad account that works and one that funds Google’s quarterly profits.

### Mistake 3: Using a Generic Landing Page Instead of a Booking Page

A groomer in **Nashville, TN** had a perfectly decent website. Homepage, about page, services page, contact form. She ran ads pointing to her homepage. Her click-through rate was fine — 4%. But her conversion rate? Zero. Not one booking in two months.

**What went wrong:** Her homepage had a carousel of photos, a paragraph about loving dogs, and a “Contact Us” link buried in the footer. When someone clicked an ad that said “Book Your Grooming Appointment in Nashville,” they arrived at a page that made them hunt for a way to actually book. Most people left within 8 seconds.

**The fix:** I built a dedicated landing page — one page, no navigation, one job. The headline matched the ad exactly: “Book Your Grooming Appointment in Nashville.” We embedded a Square Appointments booking widget directly on the page. We added three photos of her best grooms, two customer testimonials, and a big orange button that said “Book Now — Same Day Available.” No links to blog posts. No “About Our Philosophy.” Just booking.

We also added a phone number in the top right with tracking enabled so we could measure call conversions separately.

**The outcome:** First week: $320 in ad spend, 15 bookings, $2,100 in revenue. She looked at the data and said “I can’t believe I spent two months pointing at a dead page.”

**Takeaway:** The page your ad lands on matters more than the ad itself. If you are not sending traffic to a page whose only job is to book an appointment, you are burning money.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Location Targeting Entirely

A groomer in **Austin, TX** thought she was doing location targeting right. She set a 30-mile radius around her shop. She was in south Austin. Her ads were showing up in Round Rock, Georgetown, even San Marcos. People in those cities saw her ad, clicked, and then realized she was 40 minutes away. Nobody booked. She spent $1,000 in two months and got 3 appointments, all from people who lived within 5 miles of her shop anyway.

**What went wrong:** She didn’t realize that Google’s default location targeting includes people who *show interest* in your location, not just people who are *physically* in it. So Google was showing her ads to someone in San Antonio who had searched “dog grooming Austin” once. That person was not driving 90 minutes to groom their Yorkie.

**The fix:** I changed location targeting to “Presence” instead of “Presence or Interest.” I also set a 10-mile radius and excluded zip codes that were clearly outside a reasonable drive time. Then I added location extensions so her address showed up directly in the ad. We also paused ads during times when people were commuting (7-9 AM, 4-6 PM) so we weren’t paying premium rates for people who were on the highway and couldn’t book anyway.

**The outcome:** Her monthly spend dropped to $450, but her cost per booking went from $333 to $28. She booked 16 appointments in the next 30 days, all from people within a 10-minute drive. Revenue: $2,240.

**Takeaway:** Letting Google decide who sees your ad based on “interest” is like letting a stranger answer your business phone. You will get leads from people who have zero intention of ever walking through your door.

## How to Structure Your Campaign for Maximum ROI

Most dog groomers set up one campaign, throw in some keywords, and hope for the best. That is the equivalent of walking into a Petco and buying dog food without looking at the expiration date. You might get lucky. You probably won’t.

Here is the structure I have used for groomers in **Chicago, IL**; **Portland, OR**; and **Denver, CO** that consistently delivers under $30 cost per booking.

### Campaign 1: Brand + Local Intent

This is your workhorse. Keywords here should be:
- “dog groomer [city]”
- “dog grooming [city]”
- “[neighborhood] dog groomer”
- “best dog groomer in [city]”
- “pet grooming [city]”
- “dog haircut [city]”

All phrase match. No broad match. Broad match is for people who enjoy watching their budget disappear into a black hole.

Use exact match for “dog groomer near me” and “[your business name]”.

Set location targeting to a 10-mile radius with “Presence” only. No “People interested in your location.” That setting is a tax on people who live in Texas and have searched for “dog groomer in Paris” once.

Add call extensions, location extensions, and sitelink extensions (services, pricing, book now).

Budget: $300 to $500/month minimum. If you can’t spend $10/day on this, don’t bother starting until you can. You need data to optimize.

### Campaign 2: Service-Specific Campaigns

Once your main campaign is running profitably, create separate campaigns for your highest-margin services:
- “full groom golden retriever [city]”
- “poodle haircut [city]”
- “dog nail trim [city]”
- “puppy first groom [city]”
- “de-shedding treatment [city]”

These have lower search volume but higher intent. Someone searching “golden retriever grooming price” is closer to booking than someone searching “dog groomer.” They have a specific dog and a specific need.

I had a groomer in **Nashville** who added a “puppy first groom” campaign with a $200/month budget. She got 8 bookings in a month from people with new puppies. Each booking had a $95 average ticket plus future loyalty. She told me those puppies alone paid for her entire ad spend for six months.

### Campaign 3: Retargeting (Don’t Skip This)

Not everyone books on the first click. I don’t care how good your landing page is. About 70% of people who click your ad will leave without booking. Some will forget you exist. Some will go check their calendar. Some will get distracted by a squirrel.

Set up a retargeting campaign using the Google Ads remarketing tag (go to Tools > Audience Manager > Website Visitors > “All Visitors” in the last 30 days). Target them with a simple display ad that says “Still looking for a groomer? Book today — same day available.” Use an image of a happy groomed dog.

Budget: $50 to $100/month. This is the cheapest ads you will ever run. Retargeting cost per conversion is usually 30-50% lower than cold traffic.

A groomer in **Portland** added retargeting after I told her to. She spent $80 in a month. It generated 6 bookings worth $840. Cost per booking: $13. She said “I can’t believe I didn’t do this sooner.”

### Audience Targeting Tricks No One Tells You

In Google Ads, go to Audience Manager > Segments > Custom Segments. Create a segment for “Dog Owners” with keywords like “puppy training,” “dog park,” “best dog food,” “pet insurance,” “dog daycare.” Apply this as “Observation” (not targeting — you don’t want to limit your reach). Then look at the data after 30 days. You will see that these audiences convert 2x to 3x better than general searchers.

I’ve run this on accounts in **Chicago** and **Denver**. The cost per booking from dog-owner audiences averaged $22 versus $47 from everyone else. That’s not a small difference. That’s the difference between a campaign that grows and one that sputters.

## Tracking What Actually Works: Calls, Bookings, and Revenue

I talk to groomers all the time who say “I got some calls from Google Ads but I don’t know how many.” That is like opening a coffee shop and not counting how many cups you sell. You are flying blind and you will crash.

You need to track three things: phone calls, bookings, and revenue. I do not care about clicks. Clicks are vanity. Revenue is sanity.

### Tracking Phone Calls

Google Ads has a feature called “Google Forwarding Numbers.” It gives you a unique phone number that shows in your ad. When someone calls, Google records it (with their consent) and tells you how many calls came from ads. You can also set it to only count calls that last longer than 30 seconds (ignore hang-ups and wrong numbers).

A groomer in **Austin** thought she was getting 50 calls a month. When I set up call tracking, the real number was 17. Four of those were people asking about grooming rabbits. She was estimating based on gut feel. Gut feel is a liar.

Setup: Go to Google Ads > Campaigns > Extensions > Call Extension. Choose “Google Forwarding Number.” Set call reporting to “Record calls” (you can turn off recording if you want). Set conversion action to “Phone calls longer than 30 seconds.”

### Tracking Bookings

If you use a booking tool like **Booksy**, **Square Appointments**, **Vagaro**, or **MoeGo**, you can connect it to Google Ads via Google Tag Manager or a direct integration.

For Booksy: Go to Booksy Dashboard > Integrations > Google Ads. It will create a conversion action that tracks when someone books through your ad.

For Square Appointments: Use Square’s Web Payments SDK or add a Google Ads conversion tracking script to your booking confirmation page.

If you don’t use any booking software, you can still track by having a dedicated “Book Now” page on your site and adding a conversion action that fires when someone lands on that page. It’s not perfect but it’s better than nothing.

A groomer in **Nashville** used Square and set up Google Ads tracking in 20 minutes. Her first tracked conversion was a $95 groom from a new client. She texted me a screenshot of the conversion pixel firing and said “this feels like magic.” It’s not magic. It’s just data you weren’t collecting before.

### Tracking Revenue (The Hardest but Most Important)

If you want to know your actual ROI, you need to upload offline conversions to Google Ads. This sounds complicated but it’s not.

Step 1: Set up a conversion action in Google Ads called “Booked Appointment.” Use “Import from clicks” > “Phone calls” or “Other data sources.”

Step 2: Every time someone books, export your booking data (name, phone, date, revenue) as a CSV file.

Step 3: Upload the CSV to Google Ads using the “Conversions” > “Uploads” section. Google will match the phone number or click ID to the ad click.

A groomer in **Portland** did this for two months. She discovered that her “deshedding treatment” campaign had a 400% ROI while her “nail trim” campaign was losing money because people only booked nail trims once and didn’t return. She reallocated budget from nail trims to deshedding and saw her monthly revenue go from $2,100 to $3,800.

**Takeaway:** Not all bookings are created equal. Track revenue, not just bookings. The campaign that brings in a $150 groom plus future loyalty is worth more than the one that brings in a $20 nail trim from someone you’ll never see again.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I actually spend on Google Ads as a dog groomer?**

Start at $300 to $500/month. That is $10 to $17/day. If you can’t get at least one booking per day from that spend, something in your campaign is wrong — either your targeting, your landing page, or your keywords. Do not increase your budget until you have a consistent cost per booking under $40. I have seen groomers in **Denver** and **Portland** get profitable at $300/month. The number matters less than the efficiency.

**Q: Can Google Ads work for a mobile dog grooming van instead of a shop?**

Yes, but you need to be more careful with location targeting. Set your radius to 10-15 miles from your home base or parking spot. Use “Presence” targeting only. Include “mobile dog grooming [city]” and “dog grooming van [neighborhood]” as keywords. Add a call extension since mobile groomers often book by phone. One groomer in **Chicago** runs her van on a $400/month budget and gets 12-15 bookings per week. Her cost per booking is $22. It works if your ad matches your service area.

**Q: I’m in a small town. Will Google Ads work for me?**

It can, but your budget should be lower because your search volume is lower. Start at $150 to $200/month. Use exact match keywords targeting your town name. Add location extensions so people see your address in the ad. Focus on “dog groomer [town name]” and “pet groomer near me.” Most small-town groomers I’ve worked with — like one in **Bend, OR** — spend $200/month and get 8-10 bookings. That’s a 400% ROI if your average ticket is $75. It is not a silver bullet but it works if your town has people who own dogs.

**Q: What if someone calls from my ad but doesn’t book?**

That is a sales problem, not an ads problem. Your ad got them to call. If they don’t book, it is because of what happens on the phone. Train your staff to answer with a specific greeting: “Thank you for calling [business name]. Would you like to book a full groom for your dog today?” Do not ask “how can I help you?” That gives people an opening to ask about pricing and hang up. Ask a yes/no question that leads to booking. I had a groomer in **Nashville** who changed her phone script and saw booking rate from calls jump from 30% to 70%.

**Q: Should I run Google Ads during slow seasons?**

Yes, but adjust your budget. December and January are typically slower for dog grooming. Reduce your spend to $200/month and focus on retargeting or promotions like “New Year Coat Refresh.” February through May pick up again. I tell groomers to keep a baseline of $200/month year-round so you don’t lose your Quality Score. Completely pausing ads for two months means you start from zero when you resume. That costs you time and money.

**Q: Is Google Ads better than Facebook Ads for dog groomers?**

For dog groomers specifically, Google Ads wins. People who search “dog groomer near me” have a need right now. They are ready to book. Facebook is better for building awareness over time — running a “puppy of the month” contest or sharing grooming tips to an audience that may book months later. If you have to choose one, start with Google. If you have budget for both, use Google for direct bookings and Facebook for building a following. One client in **Austin** does $1,000/month on Google and $300/month on Facebook. Google delivers 80% of her bookings.
