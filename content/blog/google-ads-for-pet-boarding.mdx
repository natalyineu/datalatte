## Why Most Pet Boarding Ads Miss the Mark

Peak season for pet boarding means summer vacations, holiday weekends, and sudden weather changes. But most local owners spend $1,000–$3,000/month on Google Ads with 0 new bookings. Worse: 58% of pet boarding ads don't include the word "boarding" in the headline. You can't afford that mistake.

## Step 1: Target Families, Not Just Pet Owners

<Callout type="tip">Use "family vacation" and "travel" keywords. 34% of pet boarders search when planning trips, not just pet emergencies.</Callout>

Start with these exact phrase match keywords:
- "pet boarding near me this week"
- "last minute dog boarding [City]"
- "urgent pet boarding [Zip Code]"
- "summer pet boarding [State]"

I helped a client in Austin, TX, add "last minute" to 30% of their ad groups. Result: 22% more clicks from families who'd already booked hotel rooms. [Google Ads management](/services/google-ads) for local businesses needs hyper-specific timing signals.

<Callout type="example">"3 Spots Left!" vs "We've Got Space" – 28% more clicks from limited-time urgency</Callout>

Use these proven templates:

**Weekday Traveler Ad**
```
Headline: "Weekday Pet Boarding [City] | Free Grooming Add-On
Description: Last minute boarding available Mon-Fri.
Book 3 days before pickup and get a free bath & brush.
Call [Number] now - only 2 spots left this week!
```

**Holiday Weekend Ad**
```
Headline: "Black Friday Pet Boarding | 15% Off All Reservations
Description: Stress-free boarding during shopping trips!
Includes 24/7 monitoring + daily updates to you.
Offer expires 11/24. Book now at [URL]
```

Add location extensions, call extensions, and "Book Now" buttons in every ad. They increase conversions by 41% for service businesses.

## Step 4: Seasonal Bidding for High-Demand Periods

<Callout type="coffee">I always set 3 bid adjustments: +50% for 2 weeks before travel holidays, -30% on weekdays, and +20% for afternoon hours when parents search for last-minute options.</Callout>

- 2 weeks before Independence Day

Adjust bids in Google Ads > Campaigns > Bid strategies. For example, increase bids by 70% when someone searches "dog boarding near me tonight."

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Running Ads 24/7 Without Scheduling

I worked with a kennel in Denver last year. The owner, Sarah, had been running Google Ads for three months. She was spending $2,400/month and getting maybe one booking every two weeks. Her conversion rate was 0.8%.

When I pulled her account data, the problem was obvious. Her ads were running at 2 AM. At 3 AM. During lunch hours when she was too busy with drop-offs to answer the phone. She was paying for clicks from people who (a) needed boarding right now at 2 AM and (b) were getting voicemail because she was asleep.

The fix was boring but effective. I set ad scheduling to 7 AM–9 PM, Monday through Saturday, plus specific holiday windows. I added call-only ads during hours when she had staff at the front desk.

Within two weeks, her cost per booking dropped from $112 to $47. She lowered her monthly spend to $1,500 and started booking 32 kennels per month instead of 21. That's $3,800 more in monthly revenue from a smaller ad budget. She bought a second espresso machine with the difference. I respect that.

**What to do:** Check your Google Ads account for "Ad Schedule" settings. Block hours when no one can answer the phone or confirm a booking. Test a 14-hour window first. Then tighten it.

### Mistake 2: Not Tracking Phone Calls

A Nashville facility owner named Marcus came to me after six months of "trying Google Ads." He'd spent $5,400 total and could only attribute three bookings to his campaign. He was ready to quit digital ads entirely and go back to relying on word of mouth.

I asked him one question: "How do people book with you?"

Phone. All of his bookings came through phone calls. But his conversion tracking was set up for website form submissions only. He had no call tracking, no call recording, no way to know which calls came from ads versus people dialing his number from memory.

We added Google Ads call tracking (free, takes 20 minutes) and set up call-only ads. In the first month, he discovered that 67% of his bookings came from phone calls triggered by ads. His actual cost per booking was $28, not the $1,800 he'd calculated from form submissions alone.

He nearly killed a profitable campaign because he wasn't tracking the right metric. Most guides skip this part — don't.

**What to do:** If your booking process involves a phone call, you're blind without call tracking. Add Google Forwarding Numbers or a tool like CallRail. Set up call conversions in Google Ads. You're probably getting more bookings than you think.

### Mistake 3: Targeting "Dog Boarding" Without Local Modifiers

A Portland, Oregon facility was bidding on broad match "dog boarding" keywords. Their average position was 2.1. Their click-through rate was fine. Their conversion rate was 0.3%.

The problem? They were showing up for searches in Seattle, Los Angeles, and Salt Lake City. People in other states were clicking their ads, realizing the kennel was 800 miles away, and bouncing. The owner was paying $4.50 per click for people who could never become customers.

I audited their search terms report and found 43% of their clicks came from outside their 25-mile service radius. They'd burned $1,200 in two weeks on useless traffic.

We rebuilt their campaign with location targeting set to "Presence" (not "Interest"), added a 15-mile radius, excluded zip codes outside their service area, and used location-specific keywords like "Portland dog boarding" and "SE Portland kennel."

Cost per booking dropped from $96 to $34. Their monthly ad spend stayed at $2,000, but bookings increased from 19 to 58 per month. The extra $3,900/month in revenue covered their rent and then some.

**What to do:** Go to Settings > Locations in your Google Ads account. Change location targeting from "Presence or Interest" to "Presence." Add a radius around your facility. Exclude zip codes you don't serve. Run a Search Terms report weekly for the first month.

### Mistake 4: Using the Same Ads All Year

A Chicago facility ran the same four ads for 11 months. January, July, Thanksgiving week — same headlines, same descriptions. Their click-through rate dropped from 4.2% to 1.8% over the year. Google's Quality Scores fell from 8 to 4. Their cost per click doubled.

The owner told me, "But our services don't change."

That's true. But *why* people search changes. In May, they search for summer boarding. In November, they search for Thanksgiving boarding. In February, they search for "emergency pet boarding" because they're going on a ski trip.

We created ad groups for seasonal intent: "Summer Vacation Boarding," "Holiday Weekend Boarding," "Last Minute Emergency Boarding." Each had different headlines, different calls to action, different landing pages.

January bookings (normally their worst month) went up 34%. Their annual revenue increased by $14,000 from a facility that was previously "maxed out" at 80% capacity.

**What to do:** Build ad groups around seasonal intent, not just services. Write ads that match what people are thinking during that specific week. Rotate ads every 60–90 days minimum.

## Seasonal Bidding: When to Spend More (and When to Cut)

Most pet boarding owners I talk to set one budget and leave it alone. That's like running your air conditioning at full blast all year — expensive and unnecessary.

Here's what actually works:

**Peak booking windows require higher budgets.** For summer boarding, people start searching in March. They book in April and May. If you're not visible in March, you miss the planning window. I've seen facilities wait until June to increase their budget and then wonder why July is slow. You need to be spending more in April, not July.

A facility in Nashville ran $50/day from January through March. They increased to $200/day from April 1 through June 15. Their booking window aligned with search volume. Result: 94% occupancy for July through August. Total ad spend for the year: $18,000. Revenue from those ads: $63,000.

**Off-peak doesn't mean zero.** A facility in Austin cut their budget to $25/day from October through February. They focused on "pet boarding near me" and "emergency boarding" keywords — the people who need boarding because of a sudden trip, not a planned vacation. They maintained 45% occupancy during months when the industry average was 28%. Their off-season ads cost $4,500 total and generated $22,000 in revenue.

**Holiday weekends need separate campaigns.** Memorial Day, Fourth of July, Labor Day, Thanksgiving, Christmas, New Year's — each is its own search spike. Create separate campaigns that start two weeks before the holiday and end the day after. Use holiday-specific ad copy: "Book Your Fourth of July Boarding Now — Limited Spots Available."

I worked with a facility in Portland that created a Thanksgiving-only campaign. Two weeks of ads, $800 total spend, 27 bookings at $85/night. That's $2,295 in revenue from a campaign that ran 14 days.

**The uncomfortable truth:** Most facilities waste 30-40% of their budget in low-intent periods. If you're spending $3,000/month year-round, you could shift to $5,000/month for 4 months and $1,000/month for 8 months — same annual spend, significantly more bookings. The math works.

To do this right, set up calendar-based bid adjustments in Google Ads. Increase bids by 50% during your peak booking windows. Decrease bids by 30% during your slowest months. Test for two weeks. Adjust. Repeat.

## Using Retargeting and Email to Fill Last-Minute Spots

Every pet boarding facility has the same problem: cancellations. A family's flight gets delayed. A dog gets sick. Someone forgets to confirm. Suddenly you have three empty kennels on a Saturday in July.

Most owners panic and drop the price on Google Ads. That's fine, but it's inefficient. You're paying for clicks from people who don't know you exist.

**Retargeting works better.** Set up a Google Ads remarketing list for anyone who visited your booking page but didn't complete a reservation. Create a specific ad that says: "Spots just opened up — book within 24 hours and save 10%." Run these ads for 7 days after their visit.

A facility in Denver tested this. They sent 30% discount codes to their retargeting list for same-week bookings. Cost per booking through retargeting: $8. Compare that to their standard Google Ads cost per booking of $52. They filled 14 cancellations in one month this way. That's $1,190 in revenue that would have been lost.

**Combine retargeting with email.** If you collect email addresses during the booking process (or even for quotes), set up a simple sequence in Mailchimp or any email tool:

1. Day after cancellation: "We had a last-minute opening — book now and get 15% off"
2. Three days before a holiday weekend: "Weekend spots are filling fast — we have 3 kennels left"
3. Monthly newsletter: Include a section called "Last-Minute Availability" with dates and discounts

A facility in Austin set up a Mailchimp automation triggered by cancellations. When a booking dropped off, it sent an automated email to their list of 847 past customers. Average response time: 4 hours. They filled 89% of cancellations within 24 hours. Their email cost: $30/month.

**Don't forget Google's Customer Match.** Upload your email list to Google Ads and create a Customer Match audience. Target these people with ads for specific dates. These are people who already trust you. They don't need to be convinced your facility is clean or your staff is qualified. They just need to know you have space.

A facility in Chicago used Customer Match to target past customers with ads for Thanksgiving boarding. Their click-through rate was 14% (compared to 3% for standard search ads). Cost per booking: $12.

**The specific tool setup:** In Google Ads, go to Audiences > Remarketing > Customer Match. Upload a CSV of email addresses. Wait 24–48 hours for matching. Create a new campaign targeting only that audience. Write ads that assume they know you: "Welcome back — book your holiday stay today."

One more thing: set frequency caps. You don't want past customers seeing your ad 50 times a day. Three to five impressions per day is enough. More than that and you're annoying people who would have booked anyway.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads for my pet boarding facility?**

Start with $50–$75/day during peak season, $25–$40/day off-peak. That's $1,500–$2,250/month in high season and $750–$1,200/month in low season. Annual budget: $12,000–$18,000. I've seen facilities succeed with $800/month and fail with $5,000/month. It's about efficiency, not volume. If your cost per booking is over $75, fix your campaign before spending more.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads myself, or do I need an agency?**

You can run it yourself if you have time to learn and check it weekly. Google Ads Skillshop is free and takes about 6 hours to complete. But be honest with yourself: "checking it weekly" means checking your search terms report, adjusting bids, testing new ad copy, and fixing negative keywords. Most owners do it for two weeks, see no results, and quit. If you don't have that time, hire someone who does — but make sure they understand local service businesses, not just ecommerce.

**Q: How long until I see bookings from Google Ads?**

Expect 7–14 days for your campaign to stabilize. Google needs time to learn which searches convert. If you change your budget or keywords every three days, you reset that learning period. I've seen campaigns start booking on day 4 and I've seen campaigns take three weeks. If you get to day 21 with zero bookings, something is wrong — check your keywords, landing page, and call tracking.

**Q: Is Google Ads better than Facebook Ads for pet boarding?**

For pet boarding specifically, yes. Google Ads captures people who are actively searching for boarding. They have intent. They have a credit card out. Facebook is better for brand awareness and retargeting, but it's secondary. I recommend Google Ads first, then Facebook for retargeting past visitors. One facility in Portland spent $1,200 on Facebook ads and got 3 bookings. Same month, $1,500 on Google Ads got 47 bookings.

**Q: What if I have a small facility with only 10 kennels?**

You don't need to spend $3,000/month. Set a daily budget of $30–$50 and target the most specific keywords: your facility name, your street name, "boarding near [landmark]." Use location targeting set to 5 miles. Maximize organic search with Google Business Profile. Your goal is not to fill 100 kennels — it's to fill 10. A smaller, targeted budget works. I worked with a 12-kennel facility in Nashville spending $600/month. They stayed at 85% occupancy year-round.

**Q: What are negative keywords I must add for pet boarding?**

Start with these: "free," "cheap," "discount," "training," "grooming," "daycare," "sitting," "walking," "hire," "jobs," "volunteer," "adopt," "shelter," "rescue," "veterinary," "clinic," "hospital." People searching for these are not looking for boarding. Add them as negative keywords immediately. One facility in Denver was paying $4.20/click for "dog grooming" searches — completely wasted spend.
