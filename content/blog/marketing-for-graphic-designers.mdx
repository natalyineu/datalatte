## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Designing for Everyone — Charging Like Nobody

I watched a graphic designer in Austin, Texas — let's call her Sarah — spend six months building a website portfolio that featured logos for a vegan bakery, packaging for a craft brewery, social media templates for a realtor, and a brochure for a dental clinic. Her "About" page said she specialized in "brand identity for small businesses." Which sounds like "I'll take anything."

She landed exactly two clients in that period. One paid $250 for a logo. The other negotiated her down to $300 for a three-page website. Total revenue: $550.

When I asked her who her best client had ever been — the one who paid on time, didn't ask for five revisions, and actually used her work — she said a specialty coffee roaster in East Austin. She'd rebranded their bags and packaging. They paid $1,800 and referred her to two other roasters.

The fix was painful but simple: she cut her portfolio to only show coffee and food packaging. She changed her website headline from "Brand identity for small businesses" to "Packaging design for specialty coffee roasters in Austin." She stopped pitching general small business owners and started emailing coffee shops with outdated bags.

Outcome: In the next three months, she booked four projects at an average of $1,600 each. One client flew her to Portland for a shoot. Her pipeline went from empty to consistent because she became the person you call when your coffee bag looks like it was designed in 2009.

**What to do instead:** Pick one industry you actually enjoy working with. Remove every portfolio piece that doesn't serve that industry. Then write website copy that makes someone in that industry say "finally, someone who gets it."

### Mistake 2: Treating Your Pricing Like a Negotiation

A pet groomer in Nashville hired a graphic designer to redesign her logo and storefront signage. The designer quoted $800. The groomer asked if he could do it for $500. He said yes. Then the groomer asked for "just a few small changes." Then a few more. Then a second round. By the end, the designer had spent 24 hours on a $500 project — effective rate: $20.83/hour.

He told me this story while we were both waiting for coffee, and I could see the defeat in his face. He'd done the work. The groomer was thrilled. But he'd trained her to expect logo work at $500, and when she needed social media templates three months later, she asked, "Can you do those for $200 like last time?"

The fix: He went back to every client he'd discounted and sent a polite email — "My rates have changed to reflect the quality of work I deliver. Starting next month, logo packages begin at $1,200." Two clients left. The ones who stayed paid more. The others who left came back within six months after getting cheap work from someone else.

**What to do instead:** Quote your real rate the first time. Don't negotiate. If someone asks for a discount, say, "I understand budgets are tight. I can offer a smaller scope — we could skip the secondary logo and brand guidelines — but my hourly rate doesn't change." The clients who respect you will adjust their scope. The ones who just want cheap work will go elsewhere. That's good.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Reviews and Referral Systems

A fitness studio owner in Portland — yoga, Pilates, the whole vibe — hired a designer for a full website rebuild. The designer delivered solid work: clean, fast, mobile-optimized. The studio owner was happy. But three months later, the designer was back to cold-emailing strangers. The studio owner had never left a review, never posted about the designer on Instagram, never mentioned her to the other studio owners she met at networking events.

The designer had also never asked. Not once.

I asked her why. She said, "I don't want to be pushy."

Here's what I've seen: at GroupM, when we ran referral programs for Fortune 500 clients, the average referred customer was worth 25% more than a cold lead. They converted faster, stayed longer, and referred more people. Small businesses are no different.

The fix: The designer created a simple email sequence for project completion. Day 1: "Your files are ready. Here's a link to leave a Google review." Day 7: "If you know anyone who needs design work, I offer a 10% referral fee on their first project." Day 30: "Quick check-in — how's the website performing?"

Outcome: Over the next six months, she got 12 Google reviews, four direct referrals (two of which turned into $2,400+ projects), and one yoga studio owner who brought her in to brand a second location in Seattle. The cost of the email sequence: zero dollars.

**What to do instead:** Build a referral system before you need it. Ask for reviews while the client is still happy (day of delivery, not a week later). Offer something concrete for referrals — a discount, a free add-on, actual cash. And track where every client comes from so you know what's working.

## How to Price Yourself Out of the Commodity Trap

Most graphic designers price by the hour. This is the fastest way to cap your income at whatever your local coffee shop's barista makes per hour, because the math is brutally simple: you have limited hours, and clients will always compare your $75/hour to some kid on Fiverr doing it for $15/hour.

I've seen this play out at three different clients across the US. A Chicago-based designer was billing $65/hour and barely scraping by. She was fast — she could complete a logo in eight hours flat. But clients didn't know that. They saw her quote: "$520 for a logo." Then they'd find someone on Upwork offering a logo for $150, and she'd lose the job.

The fix was counterintuitive: stop pricing by time and start pricing by value.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

A Denver hair salon needs a redesigned logo, menu boards, and social media templates. The designer calculates:
- Logo: 6 hours
- Menu boards: 4 hours
- Social templates: 3 hours
- Revisions: 3 hours
- Total: 16 hours at $75/hour = $1,200

Instead, she quotes a flat fee: $2,500 for a "salon brand refresh package."

The salon owner doesn't think about hours. She thinks: "This logo will be on my storefront for five years. It will be in every Instagram post. If it helps me increase my prices by even $5 per haircut — which it will — that's an extra $10,000 a year. $2,500 is cheap."

The designer charged $2,500, spent 16 hours, and made $156/hour. The salon owner got a logo that increased her perceived value. Both won.

To make this work, you need three things:

1. **A defined package.** "Logo refresh" is not a package. "Brand identity for coffee shops: logo, color palette, font selections, one packaging design, and social media templates" is a package. Name it, price it, list what's included.

2. **A justification that ties to their revenue.** Before you quote, ask: "What would it mean for your business if your branding helped you raise prices by 10%?" They'll answer. Then you say: "My package helps you get there. It costs $X."

3. **The discipline to not break scope.** When they ask for "just one more version," you say: "That's outside the package. I can add it for $Y." And you charge $Y.

I tested this with a group of five designers in a workshop I ran in 2023. All five raised their project minimums within 60 days. The smallest increase was 40%. The largest was 180%. None of them lost clients — they lost tire-kickers, which is a win.

## How to Use Google Ads Without Burning Your Budget

Most small business owners I meet have a Google Ads horror story. They spent $500, got three clicks, and swore off digital advertising forever. But the problem wasn't Google Ads — it was running Google Ads for the wrong thing.

If you're a graphic designer, running ads for "graphic designer" or "logo designer" is throwing money into a dumpster fire. Those keywords are bid on by giant agencies, overseas freelancers, and Canva (yes, Canva runs ads). You'll pay $15–$30 per click and get people who want a $50 logo.

Instead, target specific local businesses that need your services.

**Real example:** A designer in Nashville decided to run Google Ads for "hair salon branding Nashville." She set a budget of $600/month — $20/day. Her ad said: "Nashville salon owners: Get a brand identity that makes clients pay $10 more per appointment."

The first month: 42 clicks at an average of $14.28/click. Total spend: $600. She got 3 leads. One turned into a $3,800 project (logo, signage design, menu boards, social templates). ROI: 6.3x in month one.

The second month: She optimized the landing page, added a client testimonial video, and focused the ad copy on "salon rebranding." Spend: $600. Leads: 5. One became a $5,200 project for a chain of three salons.

To do this yourself:
- Use Google's Keyword Planner to find search terms like "graphic designer for [your niche] [your city]"
- Target a radius around your city — 10–15 miles is usually enough
- Direct the ad to a specific landing page, not your homepage. The page should say: "I design brands for [niche] businesses in [city]. Here's how I work. Here's my pricing."
- Track every lead with a Google Click ID (gclid) so you know which ads actually generated revenue

The tools you need: Google Ads account (start with $300–$600/month budget), a landing page (can be a subdomain of your portfolio), and Google Analytics to track conversions.

One warning: If you're not comfortable spending at least $300/month on ads, don't bother. Below that, the data is too thin to optimize, and you'll just get angry.

## The Client Retention System That Actually Works

I spent six years at GroupM working on retention strategies for Fortune 500 clients. The dirty secret they don't tell you: retaining a client costs roughly one-fifth of acquiring a new one. But most graphic designers have zero retention strategy. They deliver the project, send an invoice, and disappear until the client emails them again.

A designer in New York — let's call him Marcus — was stuck in this cycle. He'd do a $2,000 project, the client would be thrilled, and then he wouldn't hear from them for eight months. By then, they'd forgotten his name and hired someone else.

I suggested a simple quarterly touch system using Mailchimp (free for up to 500 contacts):

**Quarter 1:** Email past clients. "Hey, I'm offering a free 30-minute brand audit this month. I'll look at your current materials and give you three quick wins." Marcus did 12 audits. Seven of those turned into paid projects averaging $1,400 each.

**Quarter 2:** Email with a case study of a recent project — not a general "look at this cool design," but a breakdown: "Here's how a [industry] business in [city] increased their average ticket by 15% after a brand refresh." Marcus sent this to 85 past clients. Three replied asking for meetings. Two booked projects.

**Quarter 3:** Send a "what's new" email. New services, new portfolio pieces, new testimonials. No hard sell. Just visibility.

**Quarter 4:** Send a holiday email with a small gift — a free template, a checklist, something useful. Marcus sent a "social media post template pack for coffee shops" and got four replies from clients who wanted custom versions.

Results after one year: Marcus's repeat client rate went from 15% to 48%. His average revenue per client went from $1,800 to $3,200. He spent maybe two hours per quarter on these emails. His annual revenue from past clients went from roughly $5,400 to $23,040.

Tools: Mailchimp (free), a simple spreadsheet tracking client name, project date, industry, and email. That's it. You don't need a CRM. You need consistency.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why would I pay a graphic designer $2,000 when I can get a logo on Fiverr for $100?**

Because you're not buying a logo — you're buying a brand that lets you charge more. I've seen it firsthand: a coffee shop in Nashville that spent $150 on Fiverr got a logo that looked like a clip art clip. They rebranded six months later with a local designer for $2,400. Their average ticket went up $2.50 per customer. On 200 customers a week, that's $26,000 a year. The $2,400 logo paid for itself in five weeks. The Fiverr logo cost them $150 plus the revenue they left on the table.

**Q: How do I know if a graphic designer actually understands my business?**

Ask them questions about your industry. Not "what's your design style" but "what's the biggest branding mistake coffee shops make?" A good designer will have a specific answer — not a "it depends" non-answer. They'll name cities, give examples, mention actual metrics. If they can't do that, they're a template pusher, not a partner.

**Q: Can't I just use Canva? Why do I need a real designer?**

You can use Canva for internal documents and quick social posts. But Canva templates are being used by thousands of other businesses. Your customers see the same layouts on every other local coffee shop's Instagram. A designer creates something unique to you — fonts, colors, hierarchy, spacing that signals professionalism. One fitness studio in Chicago switched from Canva to a designer's custom templates and reported a 22% increase in online bookings within 30 days. The common denominator: they looked like they invested in their business, so customers trusted them more.

**Q: What should I look for in a portfolio?**

Don't look at the pretty pictures. Look for results. A good portfolio will say: "I redesigned the packaging for a Portland coffee roaster. They reported a 15% increase in wholesale orders within three months." If the portfolio only shows logos without context or outcomes, the designer is selling art, not strategy. You want strategy.

**Q: How long does a brand identity project actually take?**

Four to six weeks for a full project — logo, color palette, typography, packaging or signage, social templates. Anyone promising faster is either working off templates or not doing the strategic work. The fastest projects I've seen that actually worked took three weeks, but that was for a single logo with no brand guidelines. Expect a proper process.

**Q: What's a fair retainer arrangement?**

For ongoing work — social media graphics, email templates, seasonal materials — a monthly retainer of $1,000 to $3,000 is normal depending on volume. You get priority access, consistent pricing, and they learn your brand deeply. The designer gets predictable income. Everybody wins. Get it in writing: what's included, how many revisions, what happens if you need extra work.
