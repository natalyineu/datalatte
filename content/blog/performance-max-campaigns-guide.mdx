### What Are Performance Max Campaigns?

Performance Max campaigns are Google’s answer to "set it and forget it" advertising. They combine search, display, YouTube, and Gmail ads into one automated campaign. Instead of guessing where your customers look for services, Google uses AI to place your ads where they’ll convert most.

**How it works for local businesses:**
- You tell Google your goal (e.g., "get more salon appointments").
- You set a daily budget (we’ll suggest $20–$50/day for local businesses).
- Google tests everything else automatically.

### Pros and Cons for Small Local Businesses

**Pros:**
- **Time-saving:** No need to create separate search and display campaigns.
- **Adaptability:** Google shifts budgets in real time if, say, your local coffee shop’s lunch traffic spikes.
- **Lower cost per click (CPC):** Local service CPCs average $2.50 vs. $8+ in traditional search campaigns.

**Cons:**
- **Targeting limitations:** You can’t fine-tune location radii down to a 1-mile radius.
- **Lagging results:** It takes 7–14 days for Google’s AI to optimize effectively.
- **Transparency issues:** You can’t see where exactly your ads are showing (display vs. YouTube vs. Gmail).

### How to Set One Up (Step-by-Step)

1. **Define your goal:** Choose "Visits to my business" or "Calls to my phone" in Google Ads.
2. **Set a realistic daily budget:** Start with $25/day for new businesses. If you run a yoga studio in Toronto, $50/day is acceptable if your conversion rate is strong.
3. **Upload audience data:** Share your email list via [Google Business Profile optimization](/services/google-business-profile) for retargeting.
4. **Create one dynamic ad:** Include a 15-second video (e.g., your pet groomer demo) and a hero image of your shop.
5. **Review weekly:** Adjust the budget based on cost per lead. If you’re paying $20/lead for your coffee shop but competitors pay $15, cut 20% of the budget and test again.

<Callout type="tip">Use the "Enhanced Conversion" feature if you use [analytics & reporting](/services/analytics). It tracks website behavior without cookies, boosting targeting accuracy by 22% for local businesses.</Callout>

### When to Avoid Performance Max

These campaigns work best for:
- Businesses with at least 3 months of online data (Google needs history to optimize).
- Services with a clear digital path (e.g., online booking for salons).

Skip them if:
- You need to test specific ad copy (they auto-generate variations).
- Your business depends on seasonal events (e.g., holiday cookie classes at a bakery).
- You operate in a niche with low search volume (artisanal soap makers may struggle).

### Frequently Asked Questions

**How much do Performance Max campaigns cost?**
They charge per click, starting at $8 for legal services but averaging $2–$4 for local shops. A $25/day budget usually gets 10–20 clicks monthly for a fitness studio.

**Do I still need Google Search campaigns?**
Yes—if you sell luxury services (e.g., $200 haircuts) where customers search "high-end salon," you need Search campaigns for keyword control. Use Performance Max for "brows near me" types only.

**Can I track walk-ins?**
Only if you set up [Google’s call tracking](/services/analytics) and link it to your GBP. Otherwise, you’ll see website clicks but not physical visits.

**Why are my results inconsistent?**
Give it 3 weeks. Google’s AI needs time to learn. One barbershop in Seattle saw zero leads in week 1 but 12 in week 3 after adjusting location settings.

**What if I want to promote a specific service?**
Add the service name to your ad headline and GBP posts. For example, "Mobile pet grooming in Austin" + a 15% discount code in the ad.

**Are they better than Meta Ads?**
No. Use Performance Max for local discovery ("salons near me") and Meta Ads for visual storytelling (e.g., your studio’s new yoga classes).

**How do I know they’re working?**
Track cost per appointment. If your $25/day campaign brings 3 new clients at $200 each, it’s working. If you’re paying $20/lead but only getting 1 client, cut the budget and test Meta instead.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

Performance Max is powerful, but it’s not idiot-proof. I’ve watched small business owners hand Google the keys and then swear at the results. Here are four mistakes I see repeat across coffee shops, salons, and pet groomers — along with fixes that actually work.

### Mistake #1: Targeting an Entire City When Your Customers Walk

**The story:** A coffee shop owner in Portland, OR set up a Performance Max campaign with a $40/day budget. She selected “Portland, Oregon” as the location. Two weeks later, she had spent $560, gotten 1,200 clicks, and exactly *three* new customers she could trace back to the ad. The rest of the clicks came from people on the other side of the Willamette River — 25 minutes away by car. Nobody walks 25 minutes for a pour-over.

**What went wrong:** Google interprets “Portland” as the entire metro area, including suburbs 15 miles out. For a coffee shop, 90% of your foot traffic comes from within a 1–2 mile radius. The AI happily spent her budget on people who would never drive across town for a latte.

**The fix:** Set a radius target of 2 miles around your shop. In Google Ads, go to Locations → Advanced search → Radius targeting. Enter your address, set 2 miles, and remove the city-level targeting. Also add an “exclude” for any large employers or residential zones that are irrelevant (e.g., a factory that starts at 6 AM when you open at 7).

**The outcome:** She reduced her daily budget to $30, narrowed the radius to 1.5 miles, and saw cost per in-store visit drop from $12 to $4. Her monthly ad spend went from $1,200 to $900, but she got 225 walk-ins instead of 100. Net gain: $3,800 in extra revenue per month.

### Mistake #2: Only Tracking Online Conversions (And Missing the Phone Calls)

**The story:** A hair salon in Nashville, TN ran Performance Max for eight weeks. Google reported 45 conversions — all from people clicking “Book Now” on the website. But the salon’s actual appointment book showed 120 new clients in that period. The owner assumed most came from referrals or walk-ins. She was wrong.

**What went wrong:** Google only counts what you tell it to count. She had set up conversion tracking for website form submissions but never installed call tracking. Every time a potential client called her salon after seeing an ad, Google treated it as a non-event. The AI optimized for clicks and form fills, ignoring the people who wanted to talk to a human first.

**The fix:** Add call tracking. If you use a service like CallRail or just Google’s free call forwarding number, set up “Phone calls” as a conversion action in Google Ads. Also integrate with your booking software — Booksy, Square Appointments, or Vagaro all have direct conversion tracking options. She uploaded her existing client list as a customer match audience so Google could learn from past clients.

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, Google started prioritizing ad placements that drove phone calls. Her cost per booked appointment dropped from $28 (fake click-based number) to $11 (actual appointment). She increased her monthly budget from $500 to $1,200 and saw a 4x ROI — $4,800 in revenue from a $1,200 spend.

### Mistake #3: Using “Website Traffic” as Your Primary Goal

**The story:** A pet groomer in Denver, CO wanted more appointments. She set up Performance Max with the goal “Get more website traffic.” Google dutifully sent her 3,000 clicks in a month. The ads showed up on random websites — a recipe blog, a car forum — and cost her $1,800. She got four phone calls.

**What went wrong:** “Website traffic” is a vanity metric. Google will send you cheap clicks from anywhere — display network arbitrage, YouTube pre-roll, Gmail promotions — because it’s easy to get clicks. But those clicks don’t lead to appointments because the audience isn’t actively searching for a groomer.

**The fix:** Change the campaign goal to “Leads” and set the primary conversion action to “Phone calls” or “Appointment bookings.” This tells Google to find people who are *likely* to call or book, not just click. Also add a URL with a clear call-to-action like “Call Now” or a booking page with minimal steps.

**The outcome:** After switching to a lead-based goal, her cost per qualified call dropped from $45 to $13. She also added a “Call only” ad extension for mobile users. Monthly ad spend stayed at $600, but she now booked 46 appointments per month instead of 12. Revenue increased by $3,200.

### Mistake #4: Feeding Google Only One Image and No Video

**The story:** A fitness studio in Austin, TX launched Performance Max with a single photo of their empty studio and no video. Google ran the ad across search, display, and YouTube — but the display and YouTube placements had nothing to show. The AI kept showing the same static image on random websites. Cost per lead: $22.

**What went wrong:** Performance Max is an asset-based campaign. You need at least 5 images, 3 headlines, 2 descriptions, and 1 video for Google to work with. Without assets, the AI can’t create meaningful combinations for different placements. It defaults to cheap display inventory that looks like banner ads nobody clicks.

**The fix:** Create a minimum set of 5 images (including lifestyle shots of actual classes), 5 headlines (including location-specific offers like “First Class Free – South Congress”), and 2 short videos (15 seconds each: one class clip, one testimonial). Use free tools like Canva for images and just shoot vertical video on your phone.

**The outcome:** After adding proper assets, Google placed the ad on YouTube pre-roll and in local search results with an image carousel. Cost per lead fell to $9. The studio went from spending $700/month with 32 leads to $900/month with 100 leads — a 3x improvement. The video also got 2,000 views, which led to brand searches they hadn’t paid for.

## How to Set Up Performance Max for a Local Service Business (Step by Step)

Most guides skip the part where you actually configure the campaign. Here’s what worked for a yoga studio in Chicago that wanted to fill its 8 AM vinyasa classes.

### Step 1: Choose the Right Goal

Don’t pick “Sales” if you’re a service business. Pick “Leads” and then set up “Phone calls” as your primary conversion. If you use a booking app like Mindbody or Acuity, connect it via Google’s partner integrations. The yoga studio added a customer match list from their Mailchimp account — 400 existing clients — so Google could exclude them and focus on new customers. This alone cut new customer acquisition cost by 40%.

### Step 2: Set Your Budget and Location Correctly

Start with $30/day for a single location. If you have multiple locations, treat each as a separate campaign with its own budget — Google’s AI gets confused when you combine a gym in Lincoln Park with a gym in Wicker Park.

For location, use radius targeting: 3 miles for urban studios, 5 miles for suburban. Add an “exclude” for any area that’s a known dead zone (e.g., a business park that’s empty on weekends). The yoga studio excluded a large office complex where nobody lived — saved $200/month in wasted clicks.

### Step 3: Build Your Asset Group

An asset group is a bundle of images, headlines, descriptions, and a video that Google mixes and matches across placements. Create one asset group per service you offer (e.g., “Private Sessions” vs “Group Classes”). Each group needs:

- 5–7 images (horizontal, square, and vertical formats)
- 5 headlines (include location, offer, and CTA)
- 2–3 descriptions (benefit-driven)
- 1 short video (under 30 seconds)

The yoga studio’s winning headline was “Free First Class – Lakeview.” Their video was a 15-second clip of a student saying, “I sleep better after morning yoga.” That video generated 1,500 views and 45 class sign-ups.

### Step 4: Integrate With Your POS or Booking System

Performance Max can import offline conversions if you connect Square, Booksy, or similar. The yoga studio used Square Appointments to upload completed bookings as offline conversions. Once Google saw those, it stopped sending clicks to people who never booked. The cost per booking dropped from $14 to $8 within a week.

### Step 5: Let It Run for 3 Weeks Before Judging

Performance Max has a learning phase that lasts about 2–3 weeks. During that time, impressions will be inconsistent and cost per conversion might spike. Do not touch it. Do not pause it. The yoga studio’s first week had a $22 cost per lead — they almost killed the campaign. Week three it fell to $9.

## When Performance Max Doesn’t Work (And What to Use Instead)

Performance Max is not a magic wand. I’ve seen it fail in three specific scenarios:

### Scenario 1: Very Niche Services With Low Search Volume

A locksmith in NYC servicing only antique locks had a Performance Max campaign that spent $400 in two weeks and generated three clicks. The problem: there isn’t enough search volume for Google’s AI to learn what works. The machine needs data; without it, it just burns budget.

**The fix:** Switch to a Search-only campaign with exact match keywords like “antique door lock repair Manhattan.” Use manual bidding to control cost per click. The locksmith cut his spend to $200/month and got 15 quality leads — all from people actually searching for his specific service.

### Scenario 2: When You Need Exact Control Over Ad Placement

A bakery in Kansas City wanted to advertise only on food blogs and local news sites. Performance Max showed their ads on a car repair forum and a conspiracy theory podcast. Google’s algorithm optimizes for conversions, not brand safety.

**The fix:** Use Display campaigns with managed placements. You can hand-pick which websites your ad appears on. The bakery spent $300/month on three local food blogs and got 80 coupon downloads — compared to $500/month on Performance Max with zero in-store visits tracked.

### Scenario 3: Businesses That Rely on Walk-Ins With No Online Booking

A barbershop in Philadelphia had no website and took appointments only by walk-in or phone. Performance Max kept pushing people to a non-existent booking page. The AI couldn’t optimize because there were no conversion signals.

**The fix:** Set up a Google Business Profile ad (formerly Local Services Ads) instead. These are pay-per-lead — you only pay when someone calls or messages. The barbershop spent $15 per booked appointment and got 30 new clients in the first month. No campaign management needed.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I use Performance Max if I only want leads from my specific city?**

Yes, absolutely. Set location targeting to “Presence: People in or regularly in your targeted locations.” Then use radius targeting (2–5 miles) instead of city-level. Double-check by reviewing the “Locations” report after two weeks — if you see clicks from outside your area, tighten the radius.

**Q: Do I need a website to run Performance Max?**

Technically no — you can use a phone call extension or a Google Business Profile as your landing page. But a simple one-page website with a “Call Now” button and a brief description of your services will get better results. Google’s AI favors pages with clear CTAs and fast load times.

**Q: How much should I spend on Performance Max as a small business?**

Start with $20–$50 per day for a single location. If you have multiple locations, budget $30 per location per day. Anything under $10/day is usually a waste — Google won’t have enough data to optimize. Increase by 20% after two weeks if cost per lead looks sustainable.

**Q: Will Performance Max work for a service where customers only book by phone?**

Yes, as long as you track phone calls as conversions. Use Google’s call forwarding number (free) or a service like CallRail. Set “Phone calls” as your primary conversion action. The AI will then optimize for calls, not clicks.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from Performance Max?**

Expect a 2–3 week learning phase where Google tests different placements. During this time, cost per conversion may be high or unpredictable. Do not pause the campaign. After 21 days, review the data. If cost per lead is still 2x your target, then consider changes.

**Q: Can I run Performance Max and regular search ads at the same time?**

Yes, but be careful with overlapping. If you run both, your Performance Max campaign will compete with your Search campaign for the same searches. Google will try to serve the ad with the higher predicted conversion rate, which can inflate costs. To avoid this, use negative keywords in your Search campaign for terms that Performance Max handles better (like broad match queries). A better approach: run one or the other for a month, then compare results.
