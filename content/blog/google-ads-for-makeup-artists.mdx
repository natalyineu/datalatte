### Target Brides with Location-Based Keywords
Brides aren’t just searching for "makeup artist." They’re typing *"bridal makeup near me"* or *"event glam in Dallas TX."* Start with these 3 keyword strategies:

1. **Geo-modify high-intent terms**: Add city names, counties, or neighborhoods to phrases like "wedding makeup artist" or "prom glam package."
2. **Use long-tail keywords**: "Bridal makeup trial + [your city]" gets 10x fewer bids but converts better.
3. **Negative keywords**: Block terms like "cheap" or "party makeup" if you specialize in luxury services.

Example: *Sarah, a Dallas-based artist*, boosted her trial bookings by 70% after targeting "bridal makeup Dallas TX within 50 miles." She paired this with [Google Business Profile optimization](/services/google-business-profile) to rank #1 on maps.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've audited enough Google Ads accounts for service businesses to spot the same problems recurring. Here are the three I see most often with makeup artists — along with fixes that actually work.

### Mistake #1: Letting Google Decide Where Your Ads Show

A bridal makeup artist in Austin, Texas came to me after spending $850 in 11 days with zero bookings. She'd set up her campaign in 20 minutes, selected a 50-mile radius around her location, and let Google's "optimized targeting" loose.

Here's what happened: Her ads showed to people in San Antonio (80 miles away), Houston (160 miles), and even Oklahoma. She got clicks from users searching "makeup tutorials" and "cosmetology school." Google spent her budget on cheap clicks that had nothing to do with booking a bridal trial.

**The fix:** I restricted the radius to 15 miles (her actual service area in Austin), switched to "presence only" instead of "presence or interest," and added 47 negative keywords in one sitting. Terms like "how to," "DIY," "school," "jobs," "products," and "supplies" were blocked immediately.

**The outcome:** Her cost per lead dropped from $42 to $11 within two weeks. She booked three bridal trials in the first month — $1,200 in revenue against a $400 ad spend. The uncomfortable truth is Google's default settings are designed to spend your budget, not grow your business.

### Mistake #2: Bidding on Your Own Business Name

A makeup artist in Nashville was spending $380/month bidding on "Nashville bridal makeup [her business name]." She ranked organically at position #1 in Google searches for her own name. Every click on that ad cost her $4.50 for something she was already getting for free.

This is the digital equivalent of paying someone to tell people your phone number when it's already printed on your door. I see this constantly with small business owners who follow YouTube tutorials telling them to "cover all your bases."

**The fix:** Pause the branded keywords. If you're worried about competitors bidding on your name (which rarely happens at local level), set a $10/day max bid for branded terms — not $30.

**The outcome:** She reallocated that $380/month into keywords like "Nashville bridal makeup trial" and "wedding makeup artist Tennessee." Her total bookings went from two per month to six within six weeks. That's roughly $3,800 in additional revenue against the same budget.

### Mistake #3: Running the Same Ads Year-Round

A makeup artist in Portland, Oregon was running "bridal makeup" ads in November and wondering why her conversion rate tanked. Wedding season in Portland runs April through October. She was spending $600/month during off-season with nothing to show for it.

She had the right keywords. She had good ads. She just had terrible timing.

**The fix:** I set her ad schedule to run March through October only, with a heavy push in January-February for wedding show season (brides book 8-14 months out, but they start researching in January). During off-season months, we shifted her budget to "event makeup" and "holiday glam" keywords.

**The outcome:** Her annual ad spend dropped by 35% ($2,520 saved), and her conversion rate during active months went from 2.1% to 5.8%. She used the off-season to focus on her Google Business Profile and organic content. Her ROI for the year improved by 140%.

### Mistake #4: Not Differentiating Trial vs. Event Bookings

A makeup artist in Denver was running one campaign for "bridal makeup" and getting frustrated that people were clicking but not booking. When I looked at her search terms, most clicks came from brides searching "bridal makeup trial near me" — but her ad copy only mentioned "event day glam."

The mismatch cost her. A bride looking for a trial is comparing artists and wants to test the experience. A bride looking for event day service is often further along and ready to book. She was targeting both with the same message.

**The fix:** Split into two ad groups. One ad copy: "Book Your Bridal Trial — $75 Consultation Fee Applied to Your Event Booking." Another: "Wedding Day Makeup — From $250. Inquire About Availability." Different landing pages were ideal, but even different ad headlines improved the match.

**The outcome:** Trial bookings increased 60% in 30 days. Event day bookings stayed the same, but her total revenue went up because trials converted to bookings at a higher rate when brides had already experienced her work. The cost per trail booking was $13. The cost per event booking dropped to $24.

## How to Structure Your Campaign Budget for Maximum ROI

I've managed accounts where $200/month outperformed accounts spending $2,000/month. The difference wasn't the amount — it was how the money was allocated.

### The 70/20/10 Split That Works

After testing across 40+ local service businesses, here's what consistently works:

**70% to your best performers.** Identify your top 2-3 converting keywords from the last 90 days. Pour money into those. For most makeup artists, that's "bridal makeup [city]" and "[city] bridal makeup artist." Keep bidding manual until you have 30+ conversions in 30 days.

**20% to testing new angles.** This is where you experiment with "maternity shoot makeup," "quinceañera makeup packages," or "prom makeup 2025 [city]." These often convert at lower volume but higher margins because there's less competition.

**10% to retargeting.** Set up a Google Ads retargeting campaign for anyone who visited your booking page but didn't convert. Show them a specific ad: "Still comparing artists? Book your trial — we'll save your date for 48 hours."

### When to Raise (or Cut) Your Budget

If your cost per lead is below $20 and you're getting fewer than 10 leads per week, raise your budget by 20% every 7 days until you hit the ceiling where cost per lead jumps above $30.

If your cost per lead is above $40 and you're not seeing at least 3 leads per week, pause the campaign. Something's broken. It could be your keywords, your ad copy, or your landing page — but throwing more money at a broken system won't fix it.

### The One Number That Matters More Than Cost Per Click

Everyone obsesses over cost per click. I've had clients panic over a $12 CPC, then book a $2,500 wedding package from that click. Click cost means nothing on its own.

Track **cost per booking** and **revenue per booking**. If your cost per booking is $30 and your average event booking is $350, you can afford higher CPCs than you think. If your cost per booking is $80 and your average is $150, you have a problem that isn't fixed by cheaper clicks.

One Chicago makeup artist I worked with had a $4.50 CPC, which she thought was "too expensive" based on general Google Ads advice. But her conversion rate was 9%, and her average booking was $420. Cost per booking was $50. She was making $370 on every booking. That's an 8.4x return. The CPC wasn't the problem — her ad copy was, and we fixed that instead.

## Retargeting Brides Who Don't Book Immediately (How to Recover Lost Leads)

Here's something most Google Ads guides skip: most brides won't book the first time they visit your site. They're comparing three to five artists. They're checking your portfolio. They're asking their wedding party for opinions.

I worked with a makeup artist in NYC who was getting 200+ site visits per month from ads but only booking 4-5 events. That's a 97% abandonment rate. Most business owners would look at that and say "my ads don't work."

The reality: her ads worked fine. Her follow-up didn't exist.

### The Two-Email Retargeting Sequence

Set up a Google Ads retargeting campaign that shows your ad to anyone who visited your booking page but didn't book. The ad should say something specific: "Saw you checking availability — here's 10% off your bridal trial if you book within 72 hours."

But ads alone aren't enough. Pair this with an email sequence using Mailchimp or Klaviyo. Here's the sequence that recovered $4,600 in lost bookings for that NYC artist in three months:

**Email 1 (24 hours after visit):** "You looked at my bridal makeup packages — here's a real answer to the question every bride asks me." Include a 90-second video showing a bridal trial transformation. No hard sell. Just proof.

**Email 2 (72 hours after visit):** "I have three Saturday dates open in June. First to book gets a complimentary false lash application ($45 value)."

**Email 3 (7 days after visit):** "Still deciding? Here's a sample timeline for your wedding day so you know exactly what to expect." Remove the urgency. Give value. Close the loop.

### The Google Business Profile Connection

Set up a retargeting campaign based on people who viewed your Google Business Profile but didn't click through to your website. Target them with a simple display ad: "See more bridal work on Instagram @[yourhandle]." This works because they already saw your star rating and location — they just need one more nudge.

The NYC artist booked two additional weddings ($3,200 combined) from this Instagram retargeting tactic alone.

### How to Set This Up in Google Ads (Step-by-Step)

1. Install the Google Ads pixel on your booking confirmation page (not just your site — specifically the "thank you" page after booking).
2. Create an audience of "All visitors except those who reached the booking confirmation page."
3. Set a frequency cap of 3 impressions per day per user (any more feels desperate).
4. Run for 30 days. Pause after that — if they haven't booked in 30 days, they've chosen someone else.

## When to Use Yelp Ads vs. Google Ads (and When to Skip Both)

I get asked this constantly. Here's the honest answer based on actual spend, not general advice.

### Yelp Ads for Makeup Artists: The Numbers

Yelp works well for one specific scenario: brides looking for "makeup artists near me" who want to read reviews before anything else. The problem is Yelp's business model. They want you to keep spending to maintain visibility.

A makeup artist in Denver spent $350/month on Yelp ads for four months. She got 14 leads and booked 3 events. Cost per booking: $466. Her average event booking was $350. She was losing $116 per booking.

Compare that to Google Ads, where her cost per booking was $38.

**When Yelp Ads make sense:** If you have 20+ reviews with a 4.5+ star rating and you're in a market where Yelp dominates search results for your service type (this varies by city — check before committing).

**When Yelp Ads don't make sense:** If you're just starting to build your review base, Google Ads will outperform Yelp in almost every scenario for local service businesses.

### The Only Yelp Strategy Worth Trying

Don't run Yelp ads until you have 15+ organic reviews. Respond to every single one. Then test with $150/month for exactly 30 days. If your cost per lead is under $25, scale. If it's over $50, cut it and redirect to Google Ads.

### What About Instagram Ads?

Instagram ads work for building awareness, not booking brides directly. The conversion rates are lower than Google Ads because Instagram users aren't in active search mode — they're scrolling.

A Nashville makeup artist spent the same amount ($500) on Instagram ads and Google Ads in the same month. Google Ads: 8 leads, 3 bookings ($960 revenue). Instagram Ads: 47 link clicks, 0 bookings ($0 revenue).

Instagram is where brides find you for engagement photos or inspiration. Google is where they book. Use Instagram for portfolio discovery and build your email list. Use Google for immediate bookings.

### The Booksy Connection

If you use Booksy or Square Appointments for booking, make sure your Google Ads link goes directly to your booking page — not your homepage. Every extra click between the ad and the booking form costs you 15-20% of potential conversions based on what I've seen across 30+ accounts.

Set up conversion tracking in Google Ads using Booksy's booking confirmation page as the conversion event. Without this, Google's algorithm is optimizing for clicks, not bookings. You're flying blind.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads per month as a new makeup artist?**

Start with $300-500/month for the first 60 days. That's enough to gather data on 4-5 keyword groups without burning cash. After 60 days, you'll know which keywords convert and can scale or cut accordingly. I've seen artists book 3-4 events per month on $400 budgets when their keywords and ad copy are tight. Do not start at $2,000/month — you'll waste at least half learning what works.

**Q: I'm getting clicks but no bookings. What's broken first?**

Check three things in this order: (1) Your ad copy promises "bridal makeup" but your landing page shows portfolio work from 2019. Update it. (2) Your contact form asks for 17 fields including "wedding party size" and "preferred palette." Brides will abandon at 5 fields. (3) Your pricing isn't visible anywhere. I've tested this — "request a quote" forms convert 40% worse than pages with clear package pricing and one "book now" button.

**Q: Should I include pricing in my ads?**

Yes, if you're competitive. "Bridal makeup from $250" filters out tire-kickers and attracts people who can afford you. If you're premium ($500+), test "luxury bridal makeup" without a price and see which converts better. A Charlotte artist tested both and found the priced ad got fewer clicks but more bookings — quality over quantity.

**Q: Is Google Ads worth it if I'm already fully booked from referrals?**

Not right now. If you're turning down clients, put your ad budget into a referral program (discounts for past brides) and update your Google Business Profile instead. Revisit ads when your capacity opens up. Most artists only need ads to fill 2-3 slow months per year.

**Q: What if a bride books a trial but cancels the wedding date?**

This happens. Don't put your ad budget at risk for one booking. Set a non-refundable trial fee ($50-100 depending on your market) that's applied to the event booking if they proceed. This protects you from "shopping around" brides who have no intention of booking. A Chicago artist lost $1,200 in 2023 to brides who booked trials, cancelled events, and never paid. The trial fee fixed it.

**Q: How do I compete with makeup artists who have 500 reviews when I'm just starting?**

You don't compete on reviews. You compete on specificity. Bid on "maternity shoot makeup [city]" or "elopement makeup [city]" — phrases established artists ignore because they're too focused on "bridal." You'll pay less per click and face 80% less competition. Build your credibility through before/after photos in your ad extensions and a clean landing page. One Atlanta artist built a full roster in 8 months targeting only "elopement makeup" with zero reviews — because she was the only person running ads for it.
