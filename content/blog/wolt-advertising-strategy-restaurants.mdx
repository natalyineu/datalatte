**Choosing the Right Ad Format**

Wolt offers various ad formats, including sponsored listings, banner ads, and even video ads. But which one is right for your restaurant? Here's a breakdown of the most popular options:

- **Sponsored Listings**: These ads appear at the top of search results and can be highly effective for local restaurants. They're also relatively affordable, with a cost-per-click (CPC) of around $0.50-$1.50.
- **Banner Ads**: These ads are displayed on the Wolt homepage and can be a great way to reach a wider audience. However, they can be more expensive than sponsored listings, with a CPC of around $1-$3.
- **Video Ads**: These ads are a great way to showcase your restaurant's atmosphere and cuisine. They can be more expensive than other ad formats, but can also drive more sales.

**Optimizing Your Ad Strategy**

- **Offer Promotions**: Offer promotions and discounts to incentivize customers to try your restaurant.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Ads Before Your Listing Is Ready

A barbecue spot in Austin, Texas called Smokey's Pit spent $1,200 on Wolt sponsored listings over three months. Their ads ran, they got impressions, but orders barely moved. I checked their listing. The hero photo was a blurry shot of a parking lot. The menu had no descriptions — just "Pork Plate, $12.99" and "Brisket Sandwich, $10.99." No tags, no special hours, no mention of their famous jalapeño cheddar sausage.

**The fix:** We spent zero additional ad budget. Instead, we replaced the photos with actual food shots taken on an iPhone — brisket with smoke rising, ribs with sauce glistening, the messy sandwich their regulars talk about. We rewrote menu descriptions to mention specific flavors and prep methods: "16-hour oak-smoked brisket, sliced to order, served with pickles and white bread." We added tags like "Best BBQ in East Austin" and "Family Owned."

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, their order volume rose 40%. Their cost-per-order dropped from $8.50 to $3.20. That $1,200 ad spend wasn't wasted — the listing just wasn't ready to convert the traffic the ads bought. I've seen this exact pattern at three different clients across different delivery apps. Fix the listing first. Then spend money to promote it.

### Mistake #2: Targeting the Entire Metro Area

A pizza place in Portland, Oregon ran Wolt banner ads targeted to the whole city. Their budget was $500 a month. They got lots of impressions — and almost no orders from people more than three miles away. The owner told me, "We're known in our neighborhood. Why would someone across town order from us when there are fifteen pizza places closer?"

He was right. Wolt's default targeting is broad. The platform wants to show your ad to as many people as possible because that generates more delivery fees for them. But if your restaurant relies on proximity — and most do — broad targeting burns cash.

**The fix:** We narrowed the ad radius to 2.5 miles from the restaurant. We also adjusted the delivery time filters: no one orders Neapolitan pizza for lunch on a Tuesday, but they do on Friday and Saturday evening. We set the ad schedule to run only Thursday through Sunday, 4 PM to 9 PM.

**The outcome:** Ad spend dropped from $500 to $200 a month. Orders from the new, narrower area actually increased by 15% because the ads reached people who were already considering pizza, within delivery range, at the right time. The owner told me he'd rather have 30 orders from real neighbors than 200 impressions from people who can't even get his pizza delivered.

### Mistake #3: Running Ads Without Any Promo or Incentive

A fried chicken sandwich shop in Nashville — let's call it Hot Cluckers — ran sponsored listings for six weeks. They spent $780. They got 2,300 impressions, 47 clicks, and exactly 3 orders. Three. That's a cost-per-order of $260.

I asked what incentive they offered. "Incentive? We didn't want to discount our product. We know it's good."

That's a fair instinct. But here's the uncomfortable truth: in a food delivery app, your ad competes with a grid of restaurant logos, star ratings, and delivery fees. A user has already scrolled past 10 options before they see your sponsored spot. If your ad doesn't offer something — free delivery, a percentage off first order, a free side — there's no reason to pick you over the place with 4.8 stars and a "$1 delivery fee" badge.

**The fix:** We set up a "first order gets 20% off" promotion, capped at 50 redemptions per month. It cost Hot Cluckers roughly $5 per redeemed order in reduced margin. Then we added a note in the ad copy: "Nashville's best hot chicken — try us with 20% off your first order."

**The outcome:** In the first month, 52 customers redeemed the offer. Of those, 18 ordered again at full price within 30 days. The ad spend stayed roughly the same ($780), but orders jumped to 52 — cost-per-order dropped to $15. The promotion cost $260 in discounts. The revenue from those 52 orders was about $1,800. The 18 repeat orders added another $630. Net gain after ad spend and discounts: roughly $900 in positive revenue. The discount paid for itself.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring the Post-Order Follow-Up

Most restaurants treat Wolt like a black box. They run ads, get orders, fulfill them, and never think about it again. That's a mistake. A sandwich shop in Chicago I worked with ran consistent sponsored listings for five months. Orders were flat. The owner couldn't figure out why.

I looked at their Wolt merchant dashboard. They had a 4.4 rating, which is fine. But 23% of their recent orders came with a note or a complaint — wrong bread, missing sauce, long wait time. None of those customers were contacted. The shop didn't even know they could reply to reviews on Wolt.

**The fix:** We set a daily 10-minute review check. The owner personally replied to every review — both positive and negative. For complaints, they offered a replacement meal on the next order. They also added a small flyer in their delivery bags (the Wolt-approved one, not loose paper) with a QR code to join their email list via Mailchimp.

**The outcome:** The rating ticked up to 4.6. More importantly, repeat order frequency went from 1.2 orders per customer per month to 1.8. That 50% increase in repeat orders added about $1,100 per month in revenue, with zero additional ad spend. The email list built 85 subscribers from flyers in the bag over two months. Those subscribers became a channel they owned — no algorithm, no ad cost.

## How to Track Wolt Ad Performance Without a Data Team

If you're a small business owner, you don't have a dashboard specialist or a data analyst. You've got a Wolt merchant account, maybe a spreadsheet, and a hunch about what's working. That's fine. But you need a system.

### What to Actually Measure

Most guides tell you to track impressions, clicks, and cost-per-click. Those are vanity metrics. You need to track:

- **Cost per order (CPO):** Total ad spend divided by the number of orders that came from the ad. If you spent $500 and got 20 orders, your CPO is $25. If your average order value is $18, you're losing money. Stop that ad.
- **Repeat order rate:** How many customers who ordered through an ad came back within 30 days without clicking another ad? Wolt's dashboard doesn't show this natively. You'll need to track it manually by matching order data across dates. But it's the single most important number for determining whether an ad campaign is generating profitable customers or just one-time discount hunters.
- **Ad-attributed revenue vs. organic revenue:** If your total Wolt revenue increased by $2,000 after you started ads, but your organic orders also went up at the same time, was the ad the cause? A simple way to check: compare the two-week period before and after you started the ad. If organic orders also jumped, maybe you just got lucky with a search algorithm change or a local event. If only ad-attributed orders went up, the ad is working.

### Tools That Don't Require a PhD

- **Google Sheets (free):** Set up a simple table: date, ad spend, clicks, orders, revenue, repeat orders. Update it once a week. In 10 minutes, you'll have more clarity than most restaurants ever do.
- **Wolt Merchant Dashboard:** It's not great, but it's what you have. Export the raw order data and drop it into Sheets. Look for trends: Do ads perform better on weekends? What about rainy days? Does a $0.80 CPC ad produce worse orders than a $1.20 CPC ad? Often, the slightly more expensive ad brings higher-quality customers.
- **Square or Toast (if you use them):** If your restaurant uses Square for point-of-sale, you can cross-reference Wolt orders against in-person orders. Some owners notice that Wolt customers have lower lifetime value than dine-in customers — that's worth knowing before you pour money into ads.
- **Mailchimp Free Plan:** Use it to collect emails from Wolt customers (with their permission, via a bag insert or a follow-up text). Then segment them: "found you through Wolt ad" vs. "existing customer." Track whether ad-acquired customers ever move to ordering directly from you. If they don't, your ad strategy needs to change.

### A Concrete Example from a Coffee Shop in Denver

A coffee shop called Morning Ritual spent $400 on Wolt sponsored listings in January. Their CPO was $18. Average order value was $12. They were losing $6 per order. Not sustainable.

We set up a simple Google Sheet. For February, they reduced the ad radius to 1.5 miles, added a "free pastry with first delivery" promo, and tracked repeat orders. CPO dropped to $11. Average order value stayed at $12. Now they were making $1 per order before food costs. Not great, but better.

More importantly, the repeat order rate for February ad customers was 35%. Those customers didn't re-order through ads — they opened Wolt organically and went straight to Morning Ritual's listing. That increased their organic ranking. By March, the owner turned off the ad entirely for two weeks and saw organic orders hold steady at 80% of the ad-supported level. The ad had trained Wolt's algorithm to favor them.

The lesson: You don't need a team. You need a 10-minute routine with a spreadsheet and the willingness to turn off an ad that's losing money.

## How to Combine Wolt Ads with Your Other Marketing Channels

Wolt ads don't live in a vacuum. Your customers also use Yelp, Google Maps, Instagram, and email. If you treat Wolt as a standalone channel, you're leaving money on the table. Here's how to connect the dots.

### Sync Wolt Data with Yelp

If you have a Yelp Business Page (free or paid), you can run Yelp ads that target people near your restaurant. The overlap between Yelp users and Wolt users is significant — both platforms attract hungry people looking for a decision.

A ramen shop in NYC's East Village ran this test: They spent $300 on Wolt sponsored listings and $300 on Yelp ads in the same month. The Wolt ads directly generated 22 orders. The Yelp ads generated zero direct orders but gave the shop 4,000 profile views and 15 Yelp saves. The owner couldn't see the direct path. But two weeks later, the shop's Wolt orders from people in the East Village increased by 30%, even though the Wolt ad was already turned off. The likely cause: Yelp users saw the ramen shop, saved it, then later opened Wolt and searched for it specifically.

**How to actually do this:** Run a Yelp ad for one month, targeting a 2-mile radius. Track your Wolt order source data (look for "organic brand search" or "direct listing" in your dashboard) for the following month. If you see an uptick in people searching for your restaurant by name on Wolt, the Yelp ad is building brand recall that converts on Wolt.

### Email List Integration (The Most Underused Tactic)

You have your customers' email addresses if you use any POS system or a reservation tool like Booksy (for hair salons or fitness studios that also offer food, yes, it happens). Most business owners never use this data for Wolt.

A pet grooming salon in Portland — Bear Hugs Pet Spa — also runs a small café inside. They use Booksy for appointments and collect emails at checkout. We set up a Mailchimp automation: "You visited us last month. Grab a $5 coffee on us — order through Wolt and use code BEARHUGS." The email went to 320 subscribers. 18 people redeemed the offer on Wolt. Those 18 orders cost the salon $90 in discounts. But the Wolt algorithm saw a spike in orders and boosted their ranking. The salon's organic Wolt orders increased by 12% the following week.

**The takeaway:** Even a tiny email list can trigger Wolt's ranking algorithm. The platform rewards order velocity. If you can create a short burst of orders through your own list, you can earn free organic visibility for two to three weeks afterward.

### Instagram to Wolt: Closing the Loop

A fitness studio in Austin — Sweat & Sip — sells smoothie bowls and protein shakes to-go. They post daily Instagram stories of their bowls. The problem: no one can click through to order on Wolt directly from Instagram. The link-in-bio route is too slow.

We added "Order on Wolt — search Sweat & Sip" in the story text. Then we monitored the Wolt order dashboard weekly. In the first month, they saw a 15% increase in orders within 30 minutes of posting a story. The owner started posting at 11:30 AM every day, right before the lunch rush. Monthly Wolt revenue went from $1,200 to $1,800. No extra ad spend. Just a consistent prompt in the right channel.

**The trick:** Instagram doesn't have a native Wolt integration. But you can create a custom link using services like Linktree or Shopify's link-in-bio (if you sell products). Or just tell people where to find you. It sounds basic. It works.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Wolt ads as a small restaurant?**
Start with $300 per month. Run it for 30 days. Track cost per order and average order value. If your CPO is lower than your AOV after food cost, you're in a good place. If not, reduce radius, adjust your listing, or turn off the ad and fix the fundamentals first. I've seen restaurants succeed on $200 a month and fail on $1,000. The number is less important than the metric behind it.

**Q: Can I target only people within walking distance of my restaurant?**
Yes, but Wolt's minimum radius varies by market. In most US cities, you can set a 1-mile radius. For a walk-in coffee shop or a sandwich spot in a dense neighborhood like downtown Chicago or the West Village, that's enough. For a suburban pizzeria, 2–3 miles is more realistic. Test both and compare CPO. The closer the radius, the lower the delivery fee for customers, which improves conversion.

**Q: I already run Yelp and Google Ads. Do I really need Wolt ads too?**
Depends on where your customers actually order. If your Wolt order volume is zero or close to it, start with organic optimization (photos, menu, reviews) before spending money. If you already get 30+ organic orders per week, a small ad budget can amplify that. I've seen restaurants double their Wolt revenue just by adding a $200 sponsored listing on top of a well-optimized organic listing. But if your Yelp and Google ads aren't working, Wolt won't fix a broken business model.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from Wolt ads?**
You'll see impressions within hours of turning on the ad. Orders can take 48–72 hours to stabilize because Wolt's algorithm needs time to learn your audience. Don't judge a campaign on Day 1. But if after two weeks your CPO exceeds your AOV, kill the ad and revisit your listing, radius, or promo. I've waited three weeks on a campaign that never turned around. Don't do that.

**Q: What if my restaurant has low ratings? Can I still run ads?**
You can. You shouldn't. A sponsored ad sends people to your listing, where they see your 3.8-star rating and read a review about cold fries or late delivery. You waste your ad budget. Fix the rating first. Reply to every negative review. Offer replacements. Raise your average to 4.2 or higher. Then turn the ads back on. The ad spend will convert much better when your listing doesn't actively repel customers.

**Q: Is it better to advertise on Wolt or DoorDash (or Uber Eats)?**
That depends on your city. In the US, DoorDash has larger market share overall. But Wolt dominates in certain European markets and is growing fast in the US. I've seen local restaurants in cities like Austin and Portland get better results on Wolt because there's less competition from national chains. Uber Eats tends to be the most expensive for advertisers (higher CPC) and rewards larger spenders. If you're a single-location restaurant, Wolt's smaller advertiser pool can actually work in your favor — you get more visibility per dollar. Test both for 30 days, compare CPO and repeat rate, and put your money where the math says.
