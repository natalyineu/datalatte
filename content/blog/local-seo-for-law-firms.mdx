## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've reviewed Google Business Profiles for over 40 law firms in the past two years. The same mistakes keep showing up. Here are four that cost real money.

### Mistake #1: Writing Your Google Business Profile Description Like a Law Firm Website

A family law practice in Nashville, TN had a GBP description that read: *"Comprehensive legal representation for divorce, child custody, alimony, property division, and prenuptial agreements. Over 15 years of combined experience. Client-centered approach."*

That's not a description. That's a menu.

Google gives you 750 characters. This firm used 178 of them to list practice areas. They were ranking on page 3 for "Nashville divorce lawyer" and getting maybe two calls a week from organic search.

**What they did instead:** We rewrote the description to answer the question a person has when they're searching at 10 PM on a Tuesday. *"You're considering divorce in Tennessee and don't know where to start. We handle the paperwork, the court appearances, and the difficult conversations so you can focus on your kids and your next chapter. Same-day consultations available. We serve Davidson and Williamson counties."*

**The outcome:** In six weeks, they moved to the local pack for "Nashville divorce lawyer." Monthly calls from GBP went from 8 to 47. They signed three new retainer clients at $3,500 each within the first month. That's $10,500 in new revenue from a paragraph rewrite.

**The fix:** Write your description like you're talking to one person who's having a bad day. Use natural language. Include your service area. Don't list practice areas — that's what the service menu is for.

### Mistake #2: Treating Reviews Like a To-Do List, Not a Trust Signal

A personal injury firm in Austin, TX had 23 reviews with an average rating of 4.7 stars. That sounds fine. But 14 of those reviews were from 2021 and earlier. The most recent was eight months old. And the owner's responses? "Thank you for your feedback" on every single one. Copy and paste.

They were ranking decently for "Austin personal injury lawyer" but losing at the click. People would see the old reviews, see the generic responses, and go to a competitor who looked active.

**What they did instead:** I told them to implement a review request system. After every case closing, the paralegal sends a text message link (using a service called Widewail) asking for a review. They also started responding to every review like a human. *"Thanks for the kind words, Sarah. I remember your case was stressful with the insurance company dragging their feet. Glad we could get you that settlement before the holidays."*

**The outcome:** They added 12 new reviews in 60 days. Average response time dropped from "never" to under 24 hours. Their click-through rate from GBP to their website increased from 3% to 11%. Estimated additional revenue: $3,800/month from new case inquiries.

**The fix:** The number of reviews matters less than their recency and the quality of your responses. Respond within 48 hours. Reference something specific from their experience. New reviews signal to Google that your business is active. Active businesses rank higher.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the "Services" Section (The Most Underused Feature)

A criminal defense attorney in Portland, OR had a GBP that was technically "complete." Photos? Yes. Hours? Yes. Description? Yes. But the Services section listed only three categories: "Criminal Defense," "DUI Defense," and "Drug Crimes."

Someone searching "Portland assault lawyer" or "Portland theft lawyer" or "Portland restraining order lawyer" would not see this firm as a direct match. Google's algorithm looks for keyword alignment between the search query and your GBP services. They were missing dozens of relevant searches.

**What they did instead:** We expanded the Services section to include every practice area they actually handled: Assault, Theft, Domestic Violence, Restraining Orders, Probation Violations, Juvenile Crimes, Expungement, and White Collar Crime. All within Google's category limits. We also added the specific counties and cities they serve.

**The outcome:** In 45 days, their impressions from "near me" searches increased by 340%. They started getting calls for cases they used to miss entirely. One new client came in from a "Portland expungement lawyer" search — a service they'd been offering for years but never listed. That single case generated $2,200 in fees.

**The fix:** Open your GBP. Go to Services. List every specific practice area you actually handle. Include all geographic areas you serve. This is free traffic you're leaving on the table.

### Mistake #4: Using the Same Categories as Every Other Law Firm

An estate planning firm in Chicago had their primary category set to "Lawyer." That's it. No secondary categories. Google's category system has over 3,000 options. "Lawyer" is the most generic, most competitive category possible.

**What they did instead:** We changed their primary category to "Estate Planning Attorney" (which is a real category in Google's system). Added secondary categories: "Probate Lawyer," "Wills, Trusts & Estates Lawyer," and "Business Planning Attorney."

**The outcome:** They went from ranking on page 2 for "Chicago estate planning" to the local pack in three weeks. Their calls from GBP increased from 12 per month to 34 per month. They attribute two new retainer clients ($4,800 total) directly to this change.

**The fix:** Don't use "Lawyer" or "Attorney" as your primary category unless you're a general practice firm with no specialty. Find the most specific Google category that matches your primary practice area. Then add up to 9 secondary categories that cover everything else you do.

## Why Your Website Is Killing Your Local SEO (And What to Do This Week)

Your Google Business Profile gets you in the door. Your website closes the deal — or loses it entirely.

I looked at 30 law firm websites last month. Most of them had the same problem: they were designed for the attorney, not the client.

### The "About Us" Problem

Law firms love writing about themselves. "Mr. Smith graduated from Harvard Law in 1998. He clerked for Judge Rodriguez. He's a member of the American Bar Association."

Great. Here's what your potential client wants to know:

- Can you help me with my specific problem?
- How much does it cost?
- How long will this take?
- What do I do next?

Put your practice area pages front and center. Not your biography. Not your firm's history. The pages that answer "Can you help me?" should be one click from the homepage.

### Location Pages That Actually Work

If you serve multiple cities or counties, you need individual location pages. But not the kind that say "We serve Austin" and then repeat the same paragraph 15 times with different city names. Google has gotten very good at detecting those, and they don't rank.

Here's what works: A unique location page for each city with:

- A real street address (a co-working space or virtual office counts)
- A local phone number with that area code
- Embedded Google Map showing your service area
- 300+ words of original content about that specific market
- Testimonials from clients in that area
- Photos of that location (real photos, not stock)

A family law firm in Denver built eight location pages for surrounding suburbs. Each page was unique. They added citations from local chambers of commerce and bar associations. Traffic from organic search increased 180% over four months.

### Speed Is a Ranking Factor (And Your Site Is Probably Slow)

Google uses page speed as a ranking factor for local searches. If your site takes more than three seconds to load on mobile, you're losing rankings and clients.

I ran a test on a personal injury firm's site in Dallas. Load time on mobile: 8.2 seconds. They had giant hero images, five different font files, and a chat widget that loaded before the main content.

**What they did:**
- Compressed all images (saved 2.1 MB)
- Removed two font families they didn't need
- Lazy-loaded the chat widget
- Moved to a faster hosting provider ($29/month instead of $8/month)

**Outcome:** Load time dropped to 2.4 seconds. Organic traffic from "Dallas car accident lawyer" increased 40% in 60 days. They estimate this added roughly $6,000/month in new case revenue.

Run your site through Google's PageSpeed Insights. If your mobile score is under 70, fix it. If your Core Web Vitals don't pass, fix them. This isn't optional anymore.

## Local Citations: The Boring Work That Actually Pays

Citations are mentions of your law firm's name, address, and phone number on other websites. They're one of the top three local ranking factors. But most law firms get them wrong.

### The Consistency Problem

A probate firm in Atlanta had their address listed as "123 Peachtree St Suite 400" on their website, "123 Peachtree Street, Suite 400" on their GBP, and "123 Peachtree St., Suite 4F" on Yelp. This inconsistency made Google unsure which address was correct. Their local rankings were inconsistent — ranking #1 for some searches, not showing up at all for others.

**The fix:** Audit every citation — at least 20 sources. Use a tool like BrightLocal or Whitespark (both under $40/month). Find every inconsistency. Fix them all to exactly match your GBP.

They found 17 citations with errors. Fixed them over two weeks. Within 30 days, their local pack presence stabilized. They went from ranking in the top 3 for 4 keywords to 12 keywords.

### Where to Build Citations (Not Where You Think)

Most guides tell you to go after Avvo, FindLaw, and Justia. Those are important. But here are three citation sources most law firms ignore:

**Your local Bar Association directory.** If your state or city bar has a member directory, get listed. It's a high-authority .org link that Google trusts.

**Local Chamber of Commerce.** Costs $200-500/year to join, depending on your city. You get a directory listing, networking opportunities, and often a backlink from a .org domain.

**Local business directories.** Not the spammy ones. I mean the legitimate local directories your city runs. Cities like Austin, Nashville, and Denver have "Open Data" portals with business directories. Get your firm listed there.

An immigration law firm in NYC added citations to 12 local directories they hadn't used before. Three months later, their local search visibility index (measured by BrightLocal) went from 42 to 78. That's a measurable improvement from free or low-cost directory listings.

### What to Do With Yelp

Yelp is the most controversial citation source for law firms. Some attorneys hate it. Here's my take: Yelp reviews show up in Google search results. If you have a Yelp page with 4+ stars and recent reviews, it helps your overall local presence. If you have a Yelp page with 2.5 stars and one review from 2019, it hurts.

**The fix:** Claim your Yelp page if you haven't. Encourage satisfied clients to leave reviews there (but never offer incentives — that violates Yelp's terms). Respond to negative reviews professionally. If you have a problematic Yelp history, focus your energy on other platforms instead.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it take to rank for "lawyer near me" searches?**

If you're starting from zero — no Google Business Profile, no citations, no reviews — expect 3-6 months to see meaningful results. If you already have a profile but it's not optimized, 4-8 weeks. I've seen firms jump to the local pack in two weeks after fixing basic errors, but that's rare. The firms that rank fastest are the ones that fix everything at once: GBP optimization, reviews, citations, and website speed. Doing one thing alone takes longer.

**Q: Do I need to spend money on Google Ads for local SEO to work?**

No. Local SEO and Google Ads are separate channels. A well-optimized GBP and website will drive organic traffic without paid ads. That said, Google Local Services Ads (the ones with the green checkmark that say "Google Screened") work extremely well for law firms. I've seen cost-per-lead between $30-80 for personal injury and family law, depending on the city. But you don't need to run ads to rank organically. Just don't expect results overnight.

**Q: What if I have bad reviews? Should I delete my Google Business Profile?**

Absolutely not. Deleting your GBP removes your biggest local SEO asset — the thing that gets you into the local pack. Bad reviews are manageable. Respond to every negative review professionally. Address the specific issue. Offer to make it right offline. One or two negative reviews among 20+ positive ones won't hurt you. But a pattern of unresolved negative reviews with no owner response? That's a problem.

**Q: Can I do local SEO myself, or do I need to hire someone?**

You can do the basics yourself in about 10-15 hours. Claim and optimize your GBP. Get 10-15 reviews. Fix your website speed. Build citations on the top 10 directories. That will get you to page 1 for your city and practice area in most markets. If you're in a competitive city (NYC, LA, Chicago) or a competitive practice area (personal injury, divorce), you'll need ongoing work: content creation, link building, review management. At that point, hiring someone who knows local SEO for law firms is worth the $500-1,500/month they'll charge.

**Q: Do I need a separate website for each city I serve?**

No. You need dedicated landing pages within your main site. A separate domain for each city creates more work — you'd need separate GBPs, separate citations, separate content strategies. One site with well-optimized location pages is cleaner and easier to manage. But those location pages need to be unique, not copy-pasted. Write 500+ words about that specific city, include a local phone number if you have one, and embed a Google Map.

**Q: What's the one thing I should do today that will make the biggest difference?**

Claim and fully optimize your Google Business Profile. Fill in every section: services, hours, photos, description, Q&A, posts. Then get three new reviews this week. Text your last five clients and ask them to leave a review. That single action — a complete GBP plus fresh reviews — will move you up in rankings faster than anything else.
