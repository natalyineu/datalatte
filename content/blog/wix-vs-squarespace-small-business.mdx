## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Picking a Template Before You Understand Your Customer Flow

A coffee shop in Nashville called Grind + Grace spent three weeks beautifying a Squarespace template. They picked the most visually expensive option — full-screen video background, parallax scrolling, the works. The owner, Sarah, told me she wanted her website to "feel like the shop looks."

It looked great. It also loaded in 6.2 seconds on mobile.

Here's what happened: Sarah's Google Ads campaign was driving about 400 visitors per month to her site. Those ads cost her roughly $500/month. But her bounce rate was 72%. That means 288 people left before the page finished rendering. She was burning $360/month on ads that went nowhere — just to show off a video of someone pouring latte art.

**The fix:** I told Sarah to strip it back. Choose a template that prioritizes the three things a coffee shop visitor actually wants: hours, menu, and the order button. Nothing else matters on the first visit. She switched to Wix's "Café" template, which loads in under 2 seconds on mobile. Then she moved her video to a secondary page called "About Our Roast."

**The outcome:** Load time dropped to 1.8 seconds. Bounce rate went from 72% to 34%. Her monthly ad spend of $500 started booking an average of $3,800 in online orders and in-store pickups. That's a 7.6x return on ad spend, up from basically zero.

**What to do instead:** Before you pick a template, write down exactly what you want visitors to do on the *first* visit. For a coffee shop, that's usually "order a drink" or "find directions." For a pet groomer, it's "book a slot." For a salon, it's "see pricing and book." Choose a template that puts that action front and center — not your brand manifesto.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring the Booking Integration Until It's Too Late

A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon, named Jenna picked Wix because she liked the templates. To switch to Wix's booking, she'd either need to migrate every client manually — no automated tool for that — or run two separate booking systems side by side.

She chose the latter. Bad call.

Running two systems meant double entry. When a client booked through her Wix site, Jenna had to manually add it to Booksy. She missed three appointments in the first month. Then a client double-booked (one through each system) and Jenna had to ask them to reschedule. That cost her $180 in lost revenue and an irritated review on Yelp.

She eventually hired a virtual assistant for $200/month to reconcile the two calendars. That ate into her margin.

**The fix:** I asked the most boring question first: "What tools do you already use every day?" Jenna used Booksy for booking, Square for payments, and Mailchimp for email reminders. Wix could handle the website and email, but Booksy was non-negotiable.

We rebuilt her site on Squarespace, which integrates directly with Booksy. The client books on the website, it syncs to Booksy automatically, and Square handles the payment. Same monthly cost ($22/month for Squarespace Business), zero double-entry.

**The outcome:** Jenna cancelled the virtual assistant ($200/month saved). Booking errors stopped. Her online booking rate went from 40% of clients to 68% within six weeks — because there was no friction. The additional booked hours generated roughly $1,200/month in new revenue.

**What to do instead:** Map your tech stack before you choose a platform. List every tool you use: booking, POS, email marketing, payment processing, accounting. Check Wix's App Market and Squarespace's Extensions catalog for each one. If the integration doesn't exist or requires a workaround, walk away from that platform. The prettiest website in the world doesn't matter if you're manually copying client data into three different systems.

## What About Integrations: Wix vs Squarespace for Your Real Tech Stack

A website isn't a standalone thing. It's the front door to whatever tools you're already paying for. Here's how Wix and Squarespace stack up against the software a typical small business in the US actually uses.

### For Coffee Shops and Cafes

You probably use Square for payments, Toast for the kitchen, or Clover for in-person orders. You might use Shopify for branded merchandise or a custom ordering widget from Olo.

**Wix:** Wix's App Market has a direct integration with Square POS, which means your online orders can sync directly with your in-store payment system. It also integrates with Shopify if you sell beans online. Toast integration requires a third-party app called "Connex."

**Squarespace:** Squarespace integrates with Square but only for basic payment processing — not full POS sync. No native Toast or Clover integration. If you run a hybrid coffee shop with both in-person and online ordering, Wix wins here.

**Real scenario:** A coffee shop in Austin with a $45,000/year Square processing volume. On Wix, they sync automatically. On Squarespace, they'd need to manually reconcile online orders — roughly 40 orders/week — at 3 minutes per order. That's 2 hours/week of administrative time, or $2,080/year at a $20/hour assistant rate. Not catastrophic, but not nothing.

### For Salons and Barbershops

You probably use Booksy, Vagaro, Mindbody, or Boulevard for booking. You might use Square for payments and Mailchimp for reminders.

**Wix:** Wix has native booking built in, but it doesn't integrate with Booksy or Vagaro. If you switch to Wix, you're committing to their booking system. That's fine if you're starting from scratch. Painful if you have 500 clients in Booksy.

**Squarespace:** Squarespace has direct API integrations with Booksy and Vagaro. A salon in Chicago with 300 active clients on Booksy can connect their site in 15 minutes. No migration, no double entry.

**Real scenario:** A barbershop in Phoenix with $8,000/month in bookings. They're on Booksy, paying $29/month. Squarespace integrates directly. Wix doesn't. If they choose Wix, they either keep Booksy separately (and clients book through Booksy, not the site) or migrate to Wix Booking. Migration would take 3-4 hours of manual work per client. Not worth it.

### For Pet Groomers and Boarding

You probably use Gingr or KennelSoft for scheduling, and you might use Rover or Wag for extra bookings.

**Wix:** No native integration with Gingr or KennelSoft. You'd need a custom API setup or Zapier bridge. This is doable but requires $30-50/month for a Zapier plan and some technical setup time.

**Squarespace:** Same issue — no native pet industry integrations. Neither platform is built for the specific workflows of pet businesses. In this case, the platform matters less than finding a scheduling tool that works. If you choose Wix or Squarespace, expect to use whichever booking system your kennel software integrates with and link from the website.

**Real takeaway:** The tool you use every day — your booking system, your POS, your email platform — should dictate which website builder you choose. Not the other way around. I've watched pet groomers spend $800 on custom Squarespace development only to realize their kennel software doesn't integrate. That's $800 in the trash.

## Migration: Switching From Wix to Squarespace (and Vice Versa)

I worked with a yoga studio in Santa Monica that had been on Wix for 18 months. They had 200 pages of content — class descriptions, instructor bios, blog posts, event pages. They wanted to move to Squarespace because the booking integration was better.

Here's the part most guides skip: migration is not easy. You can't "export" a Wix site and "import" it into Squarespace. There's no button for that.

**What actually happens:** You export your blog posts and maybe some images. You lose your page layouts, your custom styling, your SEO metadata, and your backlinks. You rebuild everything from scratch. For the yoga studio, that meant 40 hours of work — the owner's time plus a freelance developer — at a total cost of about $2,400.

**Is it worth it?** Sometimes. The yoga studio's new Squarespace site integrated directly with Mindbody, saving the owner 5 hours/week in manual scheduling. At $50/hour opportunity cost, that's $250/week saved. The migration paid for itself in 10 weeks.

**But here's the catch:** You lose your SEO history. The old Wix pages had 18 months of link equity. Those links now point to dead pages. You need to set up 301 redirects for every single page — manually — to preserve your Google rankings. If you miss even one, you lose that traffic.

**The outcome for the yoga studio:** They set up 182 redirects. It took 6 hours. They lost 12% of their organic traffic during the transition — roughly 80 monthly visitors — but recovered within 8 weeks. The booking efficiency gain made up for it.

**If you're considering migration:** Do it only if you're gaining a critical integration or feature. Don't migrate because you like how the other platform's templates look. The hidden costs — your time, lost SEO value, potential traffic dip — usually outweigh the aesthetic benefit. If your current site works, keep it. Optimize what you have instead of starting over.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I switch from Wix to Squarespace later without losing everything?**

Technically, yes. Practically, no. You can export blog posts and images, but page layouts, custom code, SEO metadata, and internal links don't transfer. You're rebuilding the site from scratch. Budget 30-50 hours of work and expect a 10-20% temporary traffic drop unless you set up 301 redirects for every old page. I've seen business owners abandon migration halfway through because they underestimated the time commitment. Decide carefully the first time.

**Q: What happens if I need to take online payments on both platforms?**

Both Wix and Squarespace charge transaction fees on top of payment processor fees. Wix charges 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction on all plans except Business and eCommerce plans (which drop to 2.6% + $0.30). Squarespace charges 3% on the Personal plan and no extra fee on Business plans or higher. For a coffee shop processing $10,000/month in online orders, that difference is $40/month — not huge, but it adds up. Square integration is better on Wix. Stripe is native on both.

**Q: Which platform is better for getting found on Google?**

Neither platform has a meaningful SEO advantage out of the box. Wix used to have terrible SEO; they fixed it in 2020. Both now allow you to edit title tags, meta descriptions, alt text, and URL structures. The difference comes down to whether you actually use those settings. A properly optimized Wix site will outrank a neglected Squarespace site every time. I've seen both perform well for local searches with the right effort.

**Q: I'm a pet groomer. Can Wix or Squarespace handle my booking system?**

It depends on your booking software. If you use Gingr or KennelSoft, neither platform integrates natively. You'll need a Zapier bridge or a direct link from your site to your booking portal. If you use a general booking tool like Acuity or Calendly, both platforms integrate easily. The workaround: add a "Book Now" button on your site that opens your booking software in a new tab. It's not seamless, but it works and costs nothing extra.

**Q: Which platform costs less over 12 months including hidden fees?**

For a basic site with a custom domain and no e-commerce: Wix Combo plan at $12/month = $144/year. Squarespace Personal at $16/month = $192/year plus $12/year for the domain = $204/year. Wix is cheaper by $60/year.

For a site with online booking and payments: Wix Business plan at $27/month = $324/year. Squarespace Business at $23/month = $276/year plus domain = $288/year. Squarespace is cheaper by $36/year.

The difference is small — $60 either way. I wouldn't pick a platform based on this gap. Pick based on which integrations you need and which editor drives you less crazy.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads with either platform?**

Yes. Both Wix and Squarespace let you connect Google Ads, Facebook Ads, and Bing Ads. Neither has meaningful restrictions. The more important question is whether the platform's built-in analytics give you clean conversion data. Wix's analytics are better for tracking form submissions and button clicks. Squarespace's analytics are cleaner but less customizable. In either case, you should install Google Tag Manager and Google Analytics 4 directly — don't rely on the platform's built-in reporting.

**Q: What happens if my business grows and I outgrow these platforms?**

Both Wix and Squarespace have limitations. Wix caps sites at 100 GB of storage. Squarespace doesn't have storage limits but restricts file types and custom code. If you hit $500k in revenue and need advanced inventory management, custom CRM workflows, or a headless CMS, you'll want Shopify or Webflow or a custom WordPress build.

That said, 90% of small businesses never get there. Don't build for a future you don't have yet. Pick a platform that works for $50k to $500k in revenue. If you hit $1M, you can afford the migration.
