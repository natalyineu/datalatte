## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched a lot of small business owners light money on fire with email marketing. Not because they're bad at their jobs — they're excellent at cutting hair. But because someone sold them on email marketing as a set-it-and-forget-it solution. It's not. Here are the mistakes I see most often, with real examples from actual salons.

### Mistake #1: Buying an Email List

A salon owner in Austin, Texas called me after spending $800 on a "targeted email list" of women aged 25–45 within a 10-mile radius. She sent her first campaign — a "20% off your first visit" offer — and got exactly 12 clicks out of 5,000 sends. Zero bookings. Sendinblue flagged her account for high bounce rates. She had to build a new domain reputation from scratch.

**The fix:** Build your own list. Every client who sits in your chair has an email address. Every person who walks in for a consultation has one. Every person who follows you on Instagram and fills out a contact form — you guessed it. Add a checkbox to your booking confirmation page. Offer 10% off their next service for signing up. At 30 new clients per week, you'll have 1,500 opted-in subscribers in a year. That list will convert at 20–30x the rate of a purchased list.

**The outcome:** She scrapped the bought list, rebuilt from scratch over 6 months, and now sends to 800 opted-in subscribers. Her last campaign drove $3,800 in booked appointments. Zero dollars wasted on bad data.

### Mistake #2: Sending the Same Email to Every Client

A salon in Portland, Oregon had been open for seven years. They sent a monthly newsletter to their entire list — about 2,000 people. Every email was the same: "Book now! New color special!" Their open rate hovered around 18%. Their booking rate was under 1%. They were losing clients who wanted different things.

One client wanted balayage touch-ups every 8 weeks. Another came in for a quick men's cut once a month. A third only booked before weddings. They were all getting the same message.

**The fix:** Use Sendinblue's segmentation. Split your list into at least three groups: color clients, cut clients, and special occasion clients. Color clients get reminders at 6 weeks. Cut clients get reminders at 3 weeks. Special occasion clients get a "planning your wedding look?" series starting 4 months out. Send different offers to each group. It takes 20 minutes to set up the segments.

**The outcome:** After segmentation, their open rate hit 34%. The color client segment alone drove $2,100 in additional bookings in the first month. The owner told me, "I can't believe I was sending the same email to everyone for two years."

### Mistake #3: No Mobile Optimization

A salon in Denver sent a beautiful email with a giant image of their new platinum blonde service. The image took 8 seconds to load on mobile. The call-to-action button was half the size of a fingertip. Their desktop open rate was fine — 22%. Their mobile open rate was 71% — which is typical for beauty services — but the click-through rate on mobile was 0.4%. Nobody could book from their phone.

**The fix:** Open every email you send on your own phone before you schedule the send. Check three things: does the CTA button take up at least 44x44 pixels? Does the text render without pinch-zooming? Does the booking link open in a browser, not an app that requires a login? Sendinblue's templates are mobile-responsive out of the box, but only if you don't upload a 4,000px-wide image.

**The outcome:** They redesigned their template for mobile-first. Same offer, same audience. Mobile click-through rate went from 0.4% to 4.2%. That one change added about $1,500 in monthly bookings from existing clients who hadn't been able to book from their phones.

### Mistake #4: Only Sending When You Have a Sale

A salon in Nashville was sending emails once every three months — only when they ran a promotion. The rest of the time, silence. Their list went stale. People forgot who they were. When a "buy one get one 50% off" email finally arrived, half the list didn't open it because they didn't recognize the sender.

**The fix:** Send something useful every two weeks, even if there's no special offer. A blog post about winter hair care. A photo of a new styling product they now carry. A client spotlight with a before-and-after. A reminder that summer humidity is coming and here's what works. Keep the relationship warm. When you do run a promo, it lands in a familiar inbox, not an "oh yeah, that place" inbox.

**The outcome:** After switching to biweekly sends — promotional every other email, useful content in between — their list churn dropped from 5% per month to under 1%. Promotional emails now get 3x the bookings they did before. No additional spend. Just consistency.

## How to Use SMS Alongside Email (Without Annoying Anyone)

Email is for the relationship. SMS is for the urgency. That's the rule I've seen work across every salon I've consulted with.

Here's a concrete example from a salon in Chicago. They were using email to send appointment reminders — which worked okay, but about 15% of clients never opened the emails. No-shows were costing them roughly $700 per month in lost appointment slots.

They added SMS reminders through Sendinblue's built-in SMS feature. Here's the setup:

**Email:** Sent 72 hours before the appointment. Includes the booking details, a map link, and a "preview our new color menu" button. No urgency, just information.

**SMS:** Sent 24 hours before the appointment. Three lines max: "Hey [Name], your cut & color with Sarah is tomorrow at 2 PM. Reply YES to confirm or call us to reschedule. — [Salon Name]"

That's it. No marketing. No "check out our new products." Just a confirmation request.

**The result:** No-show rate dropped from 12% to 3%. That's about $480 in recovered revenue per month. The SMS cost about $0.02 per message, so total cost was roughly $30 per month.

A second example: A salon in Los Angeles used SMS blast for a flash sale. They had a Thursday afternoon opening that hadn't filled in three weeks. They sent an SMS to their top 50 clients — the ones who'd booked at least 4 times in the last 6 months — offering 30% off any service booked for that Thursday. Within 2 hours, all 6 available slots were filled. Revenue from those slots: $1,080. Cost of the SMS campaign: $1.25.

**What not to do:** Don't send SMS blasts more than once a month unless it's appointment-related. Don't send marketing SMS to clients who haven't opted in specifically for SMS. Don't send SMS at 8 AM on a Saturday. Don't use SMS to deliver content that belongs in an email. Your clients do not want to read a 500-word promotional message on their phone like it's a ransom note.

**The tools:** Sendinblue handles both email and SMS in one platform, so you don't need to juggle two systems. Segment your list by who's opted into SMS — not everyone will, and that's fine. Use SMS for three things only: appointment reminders, last-minute fill-ins, and time-sensitive offers (48 hours or less). Everything else goes to email.

## What to Actually Put in Your Welcome Sequence

Most salons send one welcome email and call it done. That's a missed opportunity. A proper welcome sequence can convert a first-time visitor into a regular client within 30 days.

Here's the sequence I've seen work best, based on what a salon in Brooklyn tested over 90 days.

**Email 1 (Day 0): The confirmation.** Sent immediately after someone signs up. No pitch. No coupon. Just: "You're on the list. Here's what you get: booking links, service menu, and occasional good stuff." Simple. Quick. Establish trust.

**Email 2 (Day 3): The introduction.** A short email from the owner or lead stylist. "I'm [name], I've been cutting hair in Brooklyn for 8 years. Here's why I started this salon." Include a photo of the team. No discount. People want to know who they're trusting with their hair before they care about a deal.

**Email 3 (Day 7): The offer.** Now you can give the discount. "First visit? Here's 15% off any service. Use code WELCOME15 when you book." This is where most salons start and stop. Don't.

**Email 4 (Day 14): The social proof.** A before-and-after photo with a client testimonial. "See what [client name] said about their balayage." Link to your Google Reviews or Yelp page. This email alone drove 22% of the total bookings in the test.

**Email 5 (Day 21): The reminder.** "Your 15% off expires in 7 days. Here's the link to book." Creates urgency without being pushy.

**The results from the Brooklyn test:** 38% of people who received all 5 emails booked within 30 days. Average order value: $145. Cost per acquisition from the email sequence: essentially zero — they were already on the list. Compare that to Google Ads, where the same salon was paying $35 per booked appointment.

**One note:** Don't automate the welcome sequence and then forget about it. Review the performance every 3 months. If your open rates drop below 30% on any email, rewrite the subject line. If click-through rates drop below 3%, rewrite the body. The sequence is never "done."

## How to Track ROI So You Know What's Working

Here's the uncomfortable truth: most salon owners I've worked with have no idea which marketing channel actually drives bookings. They run Google Ads, post on Instagram, send emails, and maybe run a Yelp promotion — but they can't tell you which one brought in that $200 color service last Tuesday.

You don't need a complicated attribution system. You need three things:

**1. Unique phone numbers.** Use a tool like CallRail or even a free Google Voice number for each channel. Your email newsletter gets one number. Your Google Ads get another. Your Instagram bio gets a third. When someone calls, you know exactly where they came from. A salon in Seattle did this and discovered their email list was driving 4x more bookings than Instagram — despite having half the followers. They shifted $500/month from Instagram ads to email list building and saw a 40% increase in bookings within two months.

**2. UTM parameters on every link.** Every link in your emails should have tracking codes. Sendinblue does this automatically if you enable it in settings. If you're copying and pasting links without UTM tags, you're flying blind. A salon in Nashville found that their "balayage special" email was driving appointments, but their "men's cut" email was driving mostly website visits with no bookings. They killed the men's cut email, replaced it with a "recommend a friend" campaign, and saw a 15% lift in overall revenue.

**3. Booking source fields.** If you use Square Appointments, Booksy, or Vagaro, most of them have a "how did you hear about us?" field. Make it required, but give them options, not an open text box. Options: Email, Google, Instagram, Friend, Walk-in, Other. A salon in Austin ran this for three months and learned that 34% of new clients came from email, 28% from Google, 22% from Instagram, and 16% from referrals. That data changed their entire marketing budget allocation.

**The dollar example:** A salon in Portland was spending $1,200/month across Google Ads, Instagram, and email (via Mailchimp). After setting up basic tracking for 90 days, they found:

- Google Ads: $600/month, 18 bookings, cost per booking: $33
- Instagram: $400/month, 9 bookings, cost per booking: $44
- Email: $200/month (Mailchimp plan), 32 bookings, cost per booking: $6.25

They moved $200 from Google Ads and $200 from Instagram into email. Total spend stayed the same. Bookings went from 59 per month to 74 per month. That's 15 additional bookings at an average ticket of $130 — about $1,950 in additional monthly revenue. For zero additional spend.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How many times per month should I email my list?**

At minimum, twice. At maximum, once per week — but only if every email has something genuinely useful. I've seen salons that email once a month and salons that email four times a month. The ones who email twice per month tend to get the best balance of engagement and unsubscribes. More than four times per month and you'll see churn jump unless you're running a specific promotion.

**Q: Should I use Mailchimp or Sendinblue for a hair salon?**

Both work. I recommend Sendinblue because their SMS integration is built in, their pricing is based on sends rather than subscriber count, and their automation workflows are easier to set up for small teams. Mailchimp gets expensive fast as your list grows. A salon with 2,000 subscribers will pay about $45/month on Mailchimp versus about $25/month on Sendinblue for the same volume. The SMS feature alone saves them from paying for a separate service like Twilio.

**Q: What if I have no email list and no website?**

Start with a simple landing page on Booksy or Square. Then add an email signup form to your booking confirmation page. Every person who books through those platforms has an email address — ask permission to send them updates. Within 3 months, you'll have a list of a few hundred people. That's enough to start sending. You don't need a fancy website to send effective email.

**Q: Can I really get 25% more bookings from email marketing?**

I've seen it happen at three different salons. The 25% figure is real — but it requires a consistent send schedule, segmentation, and tracking. If you send one email per quarter with no segmentation, you'll get a 2% lift. If you send biweekly segmented emails with appointment reminders, you'll land closer to 25%. The salons that hit that number were sending between 8 and 12 emails per month per segment.

**Q: Should I offer a discount in every email?**

No. If every email has a discount, you train your clients to only book when there's a sale. They'll stop booking at full price. Limit discount emails to one out of every three. The other two should be content, tips, client stories, or service spotlights. A salon in San Francisco tested this: they sent discount emails once a month for three months, then switched to one discount email per three sends. Their overall revenue went up 11% because clients started booking at full price in between promotions.

**Q: How do I handle unsubscribes?**

Don't take it personally. A 0.2% to 0.5% unsubscribe rate per send is normal. People change, move, or decide they don't want salon emails anymore. That's fine. A smaller engaged list is worth 10x more than a large disengaged one. If you're seeing unsubscribe rates above 1%, you're sending too often or your content isn't relevant. Check your segmentation.
