## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running "Pet Grooming Near Me" as Your Only Keyword

I worked with a groomer in Austin, Texas — let's call her shop "Paws & Claws." She'd been running Google Ads for four months and had burned through $1,800. Her only keyword was "pet grooming near me." Broad match. No negatives. She was getting clicks — 147 of them — but exactly two bookings. That's a cost per booking of $900. She was ready to fire Google Ads entirely.

The problem wasn't Google Ads. It was that she was paying for clicks from people searching "pet grooming near me for aggressive dogs" (she didn't handle aggressive dogs), "mobile pet grooming near me" (she had a brick-and-mortar shop only), and "cheap pet grooming near me" (her average groom was $75). She was bidding against every groomer within 25 miles for the same handful of high-intent keywords, and losing money on every click that didn't convert.

**What to do instead:** Build a keyword list that matches your actual business. For pet groomers, that means:

- Service-specific keywords: "poodle grooming [city]," "de-shedding treatment [city]," "nail trim and bath [city]"
- Breed-specific keywords: "goldendoodle grooming [city]," "corgi grooming [city]" — these convert because owners of specific breeds know what they need
- Problem-focused keywords: "matted dog grooming [city]," "skunk smell removal for dogs [city]"
- Negative keywords: "cheap," "mobile," "self-serve," "DIY," "at home"

I rebuilt her campaign with 42 keywords organized into three ad groups. We added 60 negative keywords. Her cost per booking dropped from $900 to $28 in six weeks. She spent $1,200 that month and got 43 booked appointments — $3,225 in direct revenue from ads alone. All because we stopped bidding on everyone else's traffic.

### Mistake #2: Setting and Forgetting Your Budget

A groomer in Denver, Colorado called "The Dog Stop" came to me after a disastrous March. She'd set her daily budget at $30 — $900/month — and let it run. February was fine. March was a disaster. She got 38 clicks, zero conversions, and $900 spent. She was ready to give up.

Here's what happened: February had 28 days. March had 31. She didn't adjust. But more importantly, she didn't account for daylight saving time shifts, spring break travel patterns, or the fact that March is peak shedding season in Denver. Her search volume doubled while her budget stayed flat. Google's system tried to spread her $30 across twice as many auction opportunities. Her average position dropped from 1.2 to 3.8. Nobody clicked a result on page two for a service they need this week.

**What to do instead:** Adjust your budget monthly based on search volume patterns, not calendar pages. Here's what I had her do:

- February: $25/day ($700/month) — lower volume, fewer people thinking about grooming
- March: $45/day ($1,395/month) — spring shedding season demands more budget
- April: $50/day ($1,500/month) — post-winter matting, Easter photos, people want their dogs looking good
- May through August: $35/day — steady, but watch for heat waves (emergency cooling shave-downs spike)
- September: $20/day — back-to-school, pet owners are distracted
- October through January: $15-25/day — holiday grooming spikes in December, but otherwise low

She followed this schedule for the next year. Her total spend increased by 18% year-over-year, but her bookings went up 63%. She spent $13,500 instead of $10,800, but she generated $37,800 in revenue instead of $21,600. That's an extra $16,200 in gross profit after ad spend. She ordered a second coffee she did not need. No regrets.

### Mistake #3: Using the Wrong Conversion Tracking

This is the one that kills me. A groomer in Portland, Oregon — "Furever Groomed" — had been running Google Ads for six months. Her dashboard showed 142 conversions and a 12% conversion rate. She thought she was crushing it. She was spending $1,500/month and thought she was getting $12,000 back.

She wasn't.

Her "conversion" was a phone call tracking setup that counted any call longer than 30 seconds. What she actually got was:

- 47 calls asking about prices (she told them, they hung up)
- 23 calls from people asking if she took walk-ins (she didn't)
- 18 calls that were wrong numbers
- 12 calls from existing clients trying to reschedule
- 6 calls from people who booked, then no-showed
- 0 calls from new clients who actually showed up

Her real conversion rate was 0%. She spent $9,000 over six months and got exactly zero new clients from Google Ads. She was about to write off the platform forever.

**What to do instead:** Track the right actions. For a pet groomer, a conversion is:

1. A booked appointment — use Booksy, Vagaro, or Square Appointments integration
2. A completed phone call that lasts at least 60 seconds *and* gets a tag
3. A form submission from your "Book Now" page
4. A direction click to your shop (trackable through Google My Business)

Set up Google Ads conversion tracking to fire *only* when someone completes a booking through your scheduling system. Use call tracking software (CallRail or WhatConverts, $30-50/month) that records calls so you can verify if the caller actually booked. I switched her to Square Appointments integration for tracking. In the first month, her dashboard showed 8 real conversions from $1,200 in ad spend. That's $150 per booking — still high, but at least she could see what was actually working. She fixed her ad copy, tightened her targeting, and got that down to $38 per booking by month three. But she never would have gotten there if she kept looking at fake data.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Negative Keywords Entirely

A groomer in Nashville, Tennessee — "Bark Avenue Grooming" — was getting 200+ clicks a month and maybe two bookings. Her cost per click was $4.50, which is high for pet grooming in Nashville. She was frustrated. I looked at her search terms report and wanted to scream.

She was showing up for:

- "how to groom my dog at home" (DIYers, not customers)
- "pet groomer salary" (job seekers, not customers)
- "pet grooming training near me" (people looking for a career, not a service)
- "grooming wipes for dogs" (product shoppers, not service seekers)
- "pet stores with grooming" (big box stores, not her small shop)
- "free dog grooming" (do I need to explain this one?)

She was spending 40% of her budget on people who would never book an appointment. That's $720/month down the drain.

**What to do instead:** Go through your search terms report every week for the first 90 days. Add negatives aggressively. Here's the list I start every pet groomer campaign with:

- Mode: "home," "DIY," "at home," "self," "do it yourself"
- Product: "wipes," "shampoo," "brush," "clippers," "scissors," "tools," "kit"
- Price: "cheap," "free," "low cost," "affordable," "budget," "coupon," "discount"
- Education: "training," "class," "course," "school," "certification," "academy," "career," "salary"
- Other: "mobile," "truck," "van," "vet," "hospital," "emergency" (unless you offer these)
- Location: other city names — if you're in Nashville, add "Murfreesboro," "Franklin," "Hendersonville"

After I added 112 negative keywords to her campaign, her click volume dropped by 35%. But her booking rate went from 1% to 8% in three weeks. She was spending $1,800/month and getting 72 booked appointments — $5,400 in revenue. Each click was costing her $3.20 instead of $4.50, because she wasn't competing for irrelevant traffic anymore. That's $2,340 saved in wasted clicks over three months, plus $16,200 in additional revenue from properly targeted ads. She now checks her search terms report every Tuesday without fail.

## What to Do When You Get a Lead (And How to Not Drop It)

Most pet groomers focus entirely on getting the click. They obsess over CTR, Quality Score, and impression share. Then someone clicks, lands on their site, and it's a mess.

I audited a campaign for a groomer in Chicago. She was spending $2,000/month on Google Ads. Her landing page had a photo of her shop from five years ago, a paragraph about how much she loves dogs, and a phone number in tiny text at the bottom. Her booking rate was 2.1%. That's 42 bookings from 2,000 clicks.

Compare that to a groomer in Phoenix I worked with who redesigned her landing page based on three specific changes:

1. **Above the fold: a booking button.** Not "learn more." Not "about us." A bright orange button that says "Book Your Groom" linked directly to her Square Appointments calendar. She removed three paragraphs of text that nobody was reading.

2. **Social proof at the point of decision.** She added five Google reviews with photos directly below the booking button. "Best de-shedding in Phoenix — my husky looks brand new" with a picture of the dog. That single change increased her conversion rate from 3.1% to 5.8%.

3. **A specific offer tied to the ad.** Her ad said "Spring De-Shedding Special — $10 Off First Visit." The landing page repeated that exact offer. No confusion. No "click here for a free quote" nonsense. Her cost per booking dropped from $52 to $28.

The Chicago groomer's problem wasn't her ads. It was that she was sending high-intent traffic to a page that treated them like curious browsers instead of ready-to-book customers. She fixed her landing page, kept her ad spend the same, and her bookings went from 42/month to 97/month. That's $4,125 in additional monthly revenue from the same $2,000 ad spend.

The tool that made the biggest difference? Square Appointments. It's free to start, integrates directly with Google Ads conversion tracking, and sends automated reminders via text and email. Her no-show rate dropped from 18% to 7% in the first month. Every pet groomer should be using a scheduling tool that pings clients 24 hours before their appointment. The ones who don't are losing money they'll never even know about.

## Retargeting: The Step Most Pet Groomers Skip

Here's what happens with most pet grooming campaigns: someone searches "dog grooming Austin," clicks your ad, looks at your prices, thinks "hmm, $65 for a full groom... I'll come back later," and then gets distracted by their toddler throwing a sippy cup across the room. They never return. You just paid $3.50 for a click from someone who was interested but not ready.

Retargeting is how you bring them back. I set up a retargeting campaign for that same Austin groomer (Paws & Claws). We targeted anyone who visited her site in the last 30 days but didn't book. The ad was simple: "Still thinking about it? Book online and get $10 off your first groom. Use code PAWS10."

The setup:

- Google Display Network retargeting — costs about $0.15-0.30 per view
- Facebook/Instagram retargeting — $0.50-1.00 per click
- YouTube retargeting — $0.05-0.10 per view

We budgeted $200/month for retargeting. In the first month, 12 people who had previously visited her site and left came back and booked. That's $900 in revenue from a $200 retargeting spend. A 4.5x return on ads that most pet groomers don't even know exist.

The key is frequency caps. Don't show your ad to the same person 47 times. Set a cap of 3-5 impressions per day. Remove people who book from the retargeting list automatically. Use a 30-day cookie window — anyone who hasn't booked in 30 days probably isn't going to, and you're just annoying them.

I also recommend adding a "customer list" retargeting campaign. Upload email addresses of past clients (with their permission) and run ads for seasonal services — holiday grooming, summer de-shedding, winter coat maintenance. One groomer in Denver got 18 repeat bookings in December from a $100 retargeting campaign targeting her previous clients. They already trusted her. They just needed a reminder that December books up fast.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads if I'm a new pet grooming business?**

Start at $500/month for the first 90 days. That's about $16-17/day. It's enough to test keywords, gather data, and figure out your actual cost per booking — which will almost certainly be higher than you want it to be initially. Do not start at $100/month. At that budget, Google can't collect enough data for its Smart Bidding algorithms to work, and you'll waste every dollar on random clicks. After 90 days, if your cost per booking is under $40, scale up. If it's over $60, fix your keywords and landing page before spending more.

**Q: How long until I see results from Google Ads?**

If you set things up correctly — proper keywords, negatives, conversion tracking, a decent landing page — you should see your first booking within 3-5 days. But "results" in the sense of predictable, scalable, profitable campaigns? That takes 4-8 weeks. Google's algorithm needs time to learn what converts for your specific business. You won't see 8% ROAS on day one. You'll see it in month three if you don't panic and change everything after week two.

**Q: Can I outrank PetSmart and other national chains?**

Yes, and you don't need to outspend them. National chains bid on broad terms like "dog grooming" and "pet grooming near me." You win by bidding on specific, local, service-based keywords they ignore. "Poodle grooming Austin TX" has lower competition and higher intent. "De-shedding treatment for German Shepherds" gets fewer searches, but the people searching it are ready to book. I've seen small shops with $1,000/month budgets outrank PetSmart on 15-20 targeted keywords. You don't need to win every auction. You just need to win the ones that matter.

**Q: Should I use Google Ads or Yelp Ads for my pet grooming business?**

Start with Google Ads. Yelp Ads are expensive — $300-500/month minimum, and you're paying for clicks that may or may not convert. Google gives you more control over keywords, budgets, and targeting. Once you have a profitable Google Ads campaign running for 3-4 months, test Yelp with a small budget ($200/month) to see if it works for your area. In some cities (Portland, San Francisco), Yelp drives good traffic. In others (Austin, Nashville), it's a money pit. The only way to know is to test.

**Q: My ads are running but I'm only getting 1-2 clicks per day. What's wrong?**

Two possible problems. First, your budget is too low. At $10/day in a competitive market like NYC or LA, you might get 1-2 clicks because Google can't afford to show your ad in enough auctions. Increase your budget to $25-30/day and see if clicks scale proportionally. Second, your keywords are too narrow. If you're bidding on "toy poodle grooming Lower East Side" you might get zero impressions. Broaden to neighborhood-level keywords and add more service terms. Check your impression share — if it's under 10%, either increase budget or broaden keywords.

**Q: How do I track if someone calls me but doesn't book online?**

Use a call tracking service like CallRail ($30/month) or Google's own call tracking (free if you set up Google My Business properly). Assign a unique phone number to your Google Ads. When someone calls that number, CallRail records the call and tags it. You can listen to the call, see if they booked, and tag it as a conversion. Do not count every call as a conversion. At least 30-40% of calls from Google Ads are price shoppers, wrong numbers, or existing clients who should have called your main line. Only count calls that result in a booked appointment.
