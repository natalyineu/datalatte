## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Using Broad Match Keywords Because “More Traffic Is Better”

A flooring company in Austin, Texas — let’s call them Heritage Floors — came to me after six months of Google Ads with nothing to show but a $4,800 credit card bill. They were getting 2,000 clicks a month. Zero conversions. The owner, a guy named Derek who installed hardwood for 15 years before starting his business, was ready to swear off online advertising forever.

I looked at his account. The problem was obvious in about thirty seconds: every single keyword was broad match. He was paying for searches like “flooring,” “hardwood prices,” “how to install laminate,” and my personal favorite — “flooring near me in Miami.” Heritage Floors operated exclusively in Austin.

Here’s what actually happened: Google’s algorithm saw “flooring” and decided that meant anyone anywhere searching for anything floor-adjacent was fair game. Derek was spending $4–6 per click on people looking for DIY tutorials or competitors in other states. His click-through rate was 4% on the high end, but his conversion rate was 0%.

**The fix:** I switched every keyword to phrase match and exact match. Then I added negative keywords like “how to,” “DIY,” “install yourself,” “cost calculator,” “home depot,” “lowe’s.” We narrowed his targeting radius to 25 miles from his Austin showroom. I also made sure his ads included “Austin” in headlines so people knew he was local.

**The outcome:** In month one after the switch, his clicks dropped to 180. But his conversion rate went to 9%. He got 16 phone calls and 3 form submissions. He booked $12,700 in jobs from that month. His cost per lead went from infinity to $37. Derek sent me a photo of his first project from those ads — a beautiful white oak install in a South Austin bungalow — with a note that said, “I should have called you first.”

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Location Targeting Radius

A carpet and tile specialist in Nashville — I’ll call them Music City Floors — was spending $1,200 a month and getting leads from places nobody would drive to. They got calls from people in Knoxville (three hours away), Memphis (four hours), and once from someone in Bowling Green, Kentucky. The owner, a woman named Sarah, was frustrated because she’d turn down 40% of the leads from her ads.

She had set her location targeting to “United States.” Not Tennessee, not Nashville, not a radius. The entire country.

**What went wrong beyond that:** Sarah didn’t realize Google’s location targeting defaults to “people who are regularly in or have shown interest in your targeted location.” That means if someone in Kentucky searched “carpet Nashville” because they were planning a trip next month, Google counted them as in-market. Sarah paid for that click.

**The fix:** I changed the location setting to “people in or regularly in your targeted location” and unchecked the “people interested in” box. I drew a 30-mile radius around her showroom. I also added her physical address as a location extension so Google could verify she was actually in Nashville.

**The outcome:** Her ad spend dropped to $800 a month because she stopped paying for clicks from people in Arkansas. Her lead quality improved dramatically — 85% of calls were from people within a 20-minute drive. Her close rate went from 20% to 55%. She ended up spending less per month but booking $3,800 more in monthly revenue. Sarah told me she wished she’d spent that first $7,200 on literally anything else.

### Mistake 3: Not Using Call Extensions (And Missing 60% of Your Leads)

A flooring company in Portland, Oregon — let’s call them Rose City Hardwood — was running ads that only sent people to a contact form. Their conversion rate was 2%. The owner, a guy named Mike, thought that was normal. “That’s just how online ads work,” he told me.

It’s not. And it’s not.

I checked his call tracking data from a separate service he had but wasn’t using. In the same three months he ran those ads, his business phone rang 140 times from people who saw his ad but didn’t click. That’s 140 missed opportunities. He had no call extension enabled, so Google never showed his phone number on the ad. People had to go to his website, find his phone number, and call separately. Most didn’t.

**The fix:** I added a call extension with his actual business phone number. I also set up a Google forwarding number so I could track which calls came from ads vs. organic listings vs. his Yelp page. I made sure the extension showed during business hours only, with a “Call Now” button on mobile.

**The outcome:** In the first month with call extensions, Rose City Hardwood got 47 tracked phone calls from their ads. 22 of those turned into in-home estimates. 12 booked jobs. Total revenue from those call-tracked leads: $31,400. Mike’s total ad spend that month was $1,850. That’s a 17x return on ad spend from call extensions alone. He told me, “I feel like an idiot for not doing this sooner.” I didn’t disagree.

### Mistake 4: Running One Campaign for Every Service and Location

A commercial and residential flooring company in Chicago — Midwest Floors & Tile — was running a single campaign that combined keywords for vinyl plank, ceramic tile, hardwood, carpet, epoxy garage floors, and commercial carpet tiles all in one ad group. Their ads said “Premium Flooring – Call Today.” That’s it.

The owner, a guy named James, was spending $3,500 a month. His cost per click was $5.50 on average. He was getting about 15 leads a month, but only 2–3 were actually relevant. Someone searching for “epoxy garage floor Chicago” saw an ad for “Premium Flooring” with no mention of epoxy, assumed it wasn’t relevant, and clicked on a competitor instead.

**The fix:** I split his account into five separate campaigns: hardwood, vinyl/laminate, tile, carpet, and commercial. Each campaign had its own ad group with keyword themes, its own ads that mentioned the specific service, and its own landing page. I also separated residential and commercial by campaign so James could see which was actually profitable.

**The outcome:** His cost per click dropped to $2.80 because Google’s Quality Score improved — the ads and landing pages matched the search intent. His conversion rate went from 2% to 8%. His monthly leads went from 15 to 43, and 36 of them were relevant. His monthly ad spend stayed at $3,500, but his revenue from ads jumped from about $18,000 to $52,000 in the first 60 days. James sent me a bottle of bourbon. I don’t drink bourbon, but I appreciated the gesture.

## Why Local Service Ads (LSAs) Are Worth Trying (But Not a Replacement)

You’ve probably seen Local Service Ads — the ones at the very top of Google search results with the little green checkmark that says “Google Guaranteed.” They look like a cheat code, and for some flooring companies, they are.

Here’s what actually happened when I tested LSAs for a flooring client in Denver. The company specialized in luxury vinyl plank and laminate, mostly for suburban homeowners. They were spending $2,000 a month on standard search ads and getting decent results — about 20 leads at $100 per lead. I ran LSAs in parallel for three months to compare.

**The good:** LSAs cost per lead was $65 for the first two months. That’s 35% cheaper than search ads. The leads were also higher intent — people who clicked on a Google Guaranteed listing were ready to book an estimate, not just browse. In month one, they got 12 LSA leads, and 8 booked estimates. 5 turned into jobs worth a total of $14,200.

**The not-so-good:** Google took a bigger cut. LSAs charge per lead, not per click, and you pay whether or not the lead converts. In month three, the quality dropped — they got 18 leads but only 5 were serious buyers. Google charged for all 18. That month, cost per acquisition jumped to $180 per job booked. Worse than search ads.

**The real problem:** LSAs don’t let you control your keywords. Google decides which searches trigger your ad. If you do residential and commercial flooring, Google might show you for both — and you’ll pay for leads you can’t serve. My Denver client did residential only, but Google showed their LSA for a commercial warehouse inquiry. They paid $55 for a lead they had to turn down.

**My recommendation:** Use LSAs as a supplement, not a replacement. Start with a small budget — $500 a month — and track lead quality religiously. If your cost per acquisition stays below what you’d pay in search ads, scale it. If it creeps up, pull back. LSAs aren’t a set-it-and-forget-it tool. They need attention, just like everything else.

## The Retargeting Strategy Most Flooring Companies Skip

Here’s a scenario I see all the time: someone searches for “hardwood flooring Austin,” clicks on your ad, browses your site for four minutes, looks at your white oak collection, and then leaves. They’re not ready to buy. Maybe they’re comparing prices. Maybe they’re checking if you serve their neighborhood. Maybe they just got distracted by their toddler.

Most small business owners let that person disappear forever. Big flooring chains don’t. They follow that person around the internet for two weeks with ads that say “Still thinking about hardwood? Save 10% this month.”

You can do the same thing for about $200 a month.

**How I set this up for a floor store in Dallas:** First, I installed the Google Ads remarketing tag on their website. This takes ten minutes — you copy a code, paste it into your site’s header, and you’re done. Then I created a remarketing campaign that targeted anyone who visited the “hardwood” or “vinyl plank” pages but didn’t submit a contact form or call.

I set the audience to 14-day lookback — anyone who visited in the last two weeks sees your ad. The ad itself said something simple: “Still shopping? Visit us for a free in-home estimate. Mention this ad — $200 off installation.” Direct. No fluff.

**The tools you need:** Google Ads for the remarketing campaign. If you use a CRM like Mailchimp or HubSpot, you can also create a Facebook remarketing audience from your email list. But for Google Ads, you just need the tag and a simple display ad.

**The outcome in Dallas:** The client spent $220 on remarketing in month one. They got 1,200 impressions and 38 clicks. 6 of those clicks turned into form submissions. 3 booked estimates. Total revenue from those three jobs: $8,900. Cost per acquisition: $73. That’s better than their standard search ads, which were running at $110 per lead.

**Why this works for flooring:** Flooring is a considered purchase. People don’t buy the day they see your ad. They browse, they compare, they think about it for a week, and then they act. Remarketing catches them in that “thinking about it” phase. If you’re not doing it, you’re leaving money on the table.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I actually spend on Google Ads to start?**

Start with $500–$1,000 per month. That might feel like a lot, but anything less than $500 usually gets you so few clicks that you can’t tell if the campaign is working or not. At $500/month, you’ll get roughly 150–200 clicks at $2.50–$3.00 per click in most US markets. That’s enough data to know if your ads are landing. If you’re in a higher-cost market like NYC or San Francisco, expect $4–$6 per click. Also — run the campaign for 30 days before you make any decisions. One week is not enough.

**Q: Will Google Ads work if I only do commercial flooring?**

Yes, but the budgets are bigger and the keywords are more specific. Commercial flooring clients usually need multiple bids, longer decision cycles, and higher-touch sales. You’ll pay more per click for terms like “commercial vinyl flooring contractor Chicago” — expect $5–$8. But your average job value is higher. I had a commercial client in Phoenix who spent $2,000 a month and booked a single $45,000 job from one ad click. That 22.5x return made the $8 cost per click look like nothing. Just know that commercial campaigns take 2–3 months to stabilize.

**Q: Why aren’t my ads showing up at all?**

Three most common reasons: low Quality Score, small budget, or wrong location targeting. Check your Quality Score in the Keywords tab — anything below 6/10 will make Google charge you more and show your ad less. If it’s low, rewrite your ad to match the keyword more closely and send people to a page that actually mentions that specific service. Second — if your daily budget is $20 and you’re in a competitive market, your ads might show once every three days. Raise it or narrow your audience. Third — double-check your location targeting. I’ve seen people accidentally target Zimbabwe. It happens.

**Q: Can I track phone calls from my Google Ads?**

Yes, and you should. Set up a Google forwarding number in your Google Ads account under Extensions > Call extensions. Google will generate a unique phone number that forwards to your real number. You’ll be able to see in Google Ads exactly which ads and keywords generated phone calls, how long each call lasted, and whether it was answered or missed. Cost: free. Setup time: 10 minutes. I call it the highest-ROI task you’ll do all month.

**Q: My competitor is in every ad slot. Can I still compete?**

Yes, as long as you’re not trying to outspend them. Instead, out-target them. If your competitor is bidding on “flooring” broadly, focus on specific service keywords like “engineered hardwood installation Denver” or “pet-friendly vinyl plank Austin.” Those are lower competition, lower cost per click, and higher intent. Also — use ad extensions that your competitor isn’t using. Call extensions, location extensions, and sitelink extensions all improve your Quality Score and make your ad more likely to show. Most small flooring businesses skip these. Don’t be most.

**Q: How long until I see results?**

With proper setup and a $1,000+ budget, you should see leads by day 3–5. Full optimization takes 4–6 weeks. Google’s algorithm learns over time — it needs about 30 conversions to start optimizing on its own. So in month one, expect to spend $1,000 and get maybe 10–12 leads. In month two, with data and refinements, that same $1,000 could get 18–22 leads. By month three, if you’ve added negative keywords, tested ad copy, and improved your landing pages, you could be at 30+ leads. Patience is not optional.
