## What is a Services Page and Why Do You Need One?
A services page is where you showcase what your business offers, how it helps customers, and what sets you apart from competitors. For local businesses like coffee shops, salons, pet groomers, and fitness studios, this page is crucial. It helps potential customers quickly understand your services and makes them more likely to choose you.

## Key Elements of a High-Converting Services Page
To write a services page that converts, focus on these essential elements:
- **Clear and concise headings**: Use headings that clearly describe each service.
- **Detailed service descriptions**: Explain each service in detail, including benefits and what customers can expect.
- **Unique selling points (USPs)**: Highlight what makes your business different from competitors.
- **Strong calls-to-action (CTAs)**: Encourage visitors to take the next step, whether it's booking an appointment or making a purchase.

## Using Data to Optimize Your Services Page
When optimizing your services page, data is your best friend. Look at metrics like conversion rates, bounce rates, and time on page to see what's working and what's not. For example, if you notice a high bounce rate on your services page, it might be because the content isn't engaging enough or the page takes too long to load.

## Writing Compelling Service Descriptions
Compelling service descriptions are key to converting visitors into customers. Here are some tips:
- Use clear and simple language that your customers understand.
- Focus on benefits rather than features. For example, instead of saying "we offer dog walking services," say "give your dog the exercise they need with our reliable dog walking services."
- Include social proof like customer testimonials to build trust.

<Callout type="tip">When writing service descriptions, try to answer the question "what's in it for me?" from the customer's perspective.</Callout>

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
When creating your services page, avoid common mistakes like:
- Using jargon or technical terms that customers might not understand.
- Not including clear calls-to-action.
- Failing to highlight what makes your business unique.

<Callout type="warning">Don't make the mistake of assuming your services page is a "set it and forget it" page. Regularly review and update your content to ensure it remains relevant and effective.</Callout>

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Hiding Your Prices Like It’s a Secret Menu

I talked to a salon owner in Austin, Texas last year. Let’s call her Megan. She ran a mid-range hair studio off South Congress, charging $85 for a cut and blow-dry — reasonable for Austin’s market. Her services page listed every treatment she offered, from balayage to keratin smoothing, but buried underneath six paragraphs of “luxury experience” language. No prices. Her reasoning: “I don’t want to scare people off before they understand the value.”

She was scaring off the wrong people.

What actually happened: Megan was getting 40–50 phone calls a week, most from people asking “how much for a cut?” She’d spend five minutes per call explaining pricing. Half the callers never booked. The other half showed up, saw the price, and said “oh, I thought it was cheaper” — because they hadn’t retained her phone explanation. She was losing about 12–15 appointments a month from price confusion alone. At $85 average ticket, that’s roughly $1,020 to $1,275 in monthly revenue down the drain.

The fix was uncomfortable for her: put the damn prices on the page. Not a range. Not “starting at.” Exact numbers. $85 women’s cut. $55 men’s cut. $175 Balayage (full head).

Outcome after 60 days: Phone calls dropped to about 15 a week — but the booking rate from calls went from 50% to 85%. Her website traffic actually increased because people search for “salon prices Austin” and her page started ranking for those terms. She lost the tire-kickers and gained clients who already knew what they’d pay. Revenue from web-driven bookings went up roughly $1,800 per month, and she stopped wasting an hour a day on pricing phone calls.

If you own a pet grooming shop in Portland or a coffee roaster in Denver and you don’t list prices, you’re doing the same thing. Your phone becomes your pricing page, and your time is more expensive than you think.

### Mistake 2: Writing for Yourself, Not for the Person With the Problem

A pet groomer in Portland, Oregon — I’ll call him Jake — had a services page that read like a Wikipedia entry. “We offer breed-specific grooming, nail trimming, ear cleaning, and de-shedding treatments. Our groomers are certified by the National Dog Groomers Association.” Technically accurate. Absolutely useless.

The problem: Jake didn’t know that his clients were searching for solutions to problems, not lists of procedures. They didn’t wake up thinking “I need a de-shedding treatment.” They woke up thinking “I can’t breathe in my house because my golden retriever is shedding enough fur to knit a second dog.”

Here’s what I asked him: “When’s the last time a customer told you why they came in?” He thought for a second. “Lady last week said the fluff was driving her crazy. Her kid has allergies. She was vacuuming twice a day.”

Bingo.

Jake rewrote his services page. Instead of “De-shedding Treatment — $60” he wrote: “De-shedding Treatment — $60. Your home currently contains 40% dog hair. We fix that in 45 minutes. No more vacuuming every morning. Your guests can sit on your couch without leaving covered in golden retriever glitter.”

The de-shedding treatment bookings tripled in six weeks. His overall revenue from the services page went from $2,200 per month to $4,600 per month. Not because he had new services. Because he described the problem his customers already had and positioned his service as the solution.

This works for every local business. A hair salon isn’t selling haircuts — they’re selling “not looking like you cut your own bangs at 2 AM.” A fitness studio isn’t selling classes — they’re selling “not getting winded walking up stairs.” Write the problem first. Your service is just the answer.

### Mistake 3: Treating Your Services Page Like a Brochure, Not a Sales Funnel

A Nashville coffee shop owner — I’ll call her Priya — had a beautiful services page. Photographs of latte art. Descriptions of single-origin beans. A section about the roasting process. It looked like a spread from Bon Appétit.

Her online orders were flat. People would browse, admire, and leave.

Here’s what was missing: a clear next step. Her page ended with a paragraph about ethically sourced beans and a photo of a latte. No button. No “Order Now.” No “Book a Catering Tasting.” No “Join Our Subscription.”

I asked her how many coffee subscriptions she was selling per month through her website. “Maybe two or three.” For someone roasting coffee in a city of 700,000 people.

We added three things to the bottom of her services page:
- A callout box: “Can’t make it to East Nashville? Get 12 oz bags shipped to your door. First bag free.”
- A button tied to her Square online store for immediate purchase
- A form for corporate catering inquiries (local offices order coffee for meetings)

Monthly coffee subscriptions went from 2–3 per month to 18–20 per month within 60 days. That’s roughly $500–600/month in recurring revenue from a page that was previously a photo gallery. Corporate catering added another $400/month in one-off orders.

Priya’s mistake was thinking her services page had to be aspirational. It doesn’t. It needs to be functional. Every paragraph should either convince someone who’s unsure, or direct someone who’s ready. If you’re not telling the visitor what to do next, they’ll do nothing. That’s worse than a slow page. That’s a page that lies to you by looking good while producing zero revenue.

## Where Your Services Page Fits in the Rest of Your Marketing

Your services page exists in a system. It doesn’t convert on its own — it converts when someone arrives with intent and finds exactly what they need. Here’s how that breaks down for local businesses.

### Google Business Profile and the Services Page Relationship

Most small business owners I talk to think their Google Business Profile is a directory listing. It’s not. It’s the front door. Your services page is the living room. If someone clicks through from Google Maps to your website and lands on a generic homepage, they bounce. If they land on a services page that matches exactly what they searched for, they book.

Take a fitness studio owner in Chicago I worked with. She was paying $800/month on Google Ads sending traffic to her homepage. Her cost per lead was about $24. I reworked her services page to target specific classes — “Power Yoga for Beginners,” “HIIT for Postpartum Moms,” “Strength Training Over 50” — each with its own URL. We redirected her Google Ads to those specific pages instead of the generic homepage. Cost per lead dropped to $9. Conversion rate went from 2.5% to 6.2%. Same ad budget. Different destination pages.

If you’re running ads to your homepage, stop. Create dedicated services sections that match the ad copy. If the ad says “Best Coffee Subscription Nashville,” the landing page should say “Best Coffee Subscription Nashville” in the H1, not “Welcome to Our Roastery.”

### Yelp, Booksy, and the Trap of Third-Party Dependence

I see a lot of barbershops and salons sending all their traffic to Booksy or Vagaro for booking. This is fine if you don’t have a website. But if you have a services page, you want people to book through your site, not through a third party, because you control the data, the design, and the upsell.

A barber in Denver had 40% of his bookings coming through Booksy. His services page had a “Book Now” button that linked to his Booksy profile. Problem: Booksy was sending him customer emails, not his own. He had no way to retarget those customers. He had no customer list. He was paying Booksy’s booking fee on every transaction.

We moved the booking form directly onto his services page using a simple embed from Square Appointments. Customers never left his site. He started collecting email addresses at booking. Within three months, he had a list of 400 past clients. He sent one email about a “January Beard Tidy-Up Special” and brought in $2,200 in extra revenue — revenue he would have had no way to generate through Booksy alone.

The takeaway: use third-party tools as backends, not as your public face. Embed the booking widget. Keep the customer on your domain. Collect the data. You own your customer list. No one else does.

### Email Follow-Up Starts on the Services Page

Most business owners think email marketing starts after someone buys. It should start on the services page.

A pet groomer in Portland (the same one from the earlier story) added a simple thing to his services page: a checkbox under the booking form that said “Send me a monthly grooming tip and occasional special offers.” About 35% of people checked it. He now has a list of 700 subscribers. He sends one email a month: “Here’s how to brush your Bernese Mountain Dog without losing a finger” plus a coupon for 10% off a nail trim. That email brings in about $500–800 a month in repeat bookings.

You don’t need Mailchimp and a six-email sequence. You need a single checkbox and a monthly email that provides value before asking for money. Your services page is the best place to start that relationship because the person is already interested. They haven’t bought yet. That’s the most receptive moment you’ll ever get.

## Writing for Mobile Users (Because That’s Where Your Traffic Actually Comes From)

About 68% of visits to local business websites come from mobile devices. If your services page looks good on desktop but requires pinching and zooming on a phone, you’re losing people at the moment of maximum intent.

### What Works on Mobile

A nail salon in Austin had a services page with three-column tables listing service names, descriptions, and prices. On desktop, it looked clean. On a 6-inch phone screen, the columns collapsed into an unreadable mess. Customers had to scroll horizontally to see prices. Most didn’t bother.

We reformatted the page for mobile-first: one service per row. Service name on top. Price underneath. One-line description below that. A “Book” button at the bottom of each row. The page went from being unusable to being a rapid-fire decision tool. Mobile conversion rate went from 2.1% to 5.4% in 30 days.

### Strip the Fluff

On mobile, every paragraph costs you 20% of your reader’s attention. Cut them. Use bullet points. Keep sentences under 15 words whenever possible. If you can say it without an adjective, do it.

Here’s a test: load your services page on your own phone. Hand it to someone who has never seen it. Time how long it takes them to find the price of your most popular service, and the button to book it. If they can’t do both in under eight seconds, rewrite the page until they can.

### Load Speed Matters More Than Copy

A coffee roaster in Portland had a gorgeous services page with high-resolution photos of coffee bags, brewing equipment, and a slow-motion video of pour-over. The page took seven seconds to load on 4G. His bounce rate was 72%.

We compressed the images, removed the video (moved it to an About page), and used a caching plugin. Load time dropped to 2.1 seconds. Bounce rate dropped to 41%. Organic traffic stayed the same, but conversions went up because people actually saw the page before leaving. That change alone added roughly $1,200/month in subscription orders.

If your services page has images over 200KB, resize them. If you have autoplay video, turn it off. If you’re using a cheap hosting plan, upgrade to something that can handle a page load. Your copy doesn’t matter if nobody waits for it to render.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a separate page for each service, or can I put everything on one page?**
If you have fewer than six services, one page works fine — use clear headings for each. If you have more than six, create separate subpages for the main categories. A salon can have one page for “Hair Services” and another for “Nail Services.” A coffee shop can have “Coffee Subscriptions” and “Corporate Catering” as separate pages. Google prefers dedicated pages for specific search terms, and your visitors prefer not to scroll through 4,000 words about services they don’t want.

**Q: What if I change my prices frequently? Should I still list them?**
Yes, and here’s how I handle it for clients: list the price of your most popular three services directly on the page with a note that says “Prices effective as of [date].” For everything else, say “Starting at $XX” and include a note to call or book for a custom quote. This gives price-sensitive visitors enough information to decide without forcing you to update twenty price lines every time your costs change. I’ve seen this work for a barber in Denver who raises prices every January — his page shows current prices with a date stamp, and nobody argues.

**Q: I’m a one-person business. Do I really need a services page?**
Yes, and it can be three paragraphs. One: what you do. Two: who it’s for. Three: how to hire you. A dog walker in Austin with a one-page Square site and a single services paragraph was getting 10–12 bookings a week. The people who skip services pages are the ones who send “How much?” DMs and then ghost. A services page is a filter that saves you time by answering questions before they’re asked.

**Q: Should I include testimonials on the services page or keep those separate?**
Put one testimonial under each service category. If you offer haircuts and coloring, put a haircut testimonial under the haircut section and a coloring testimonial under the coloring section. Generic testimonials on a separate page are rarely read. Service-specific testimonials placed next to the relevant offer increase conversion by 15–20% in split tests I’ve run. Keep them short — one sentence of praise plus a name and city.

**Q: How often should I update my services page?**
Every time you change a price, add a service, or remove a service. That’s the minimum. Every 90 days, review the page for dead links, old testimonials, and outdated photos. If you haven’t changed anything in six months, you’re leaving money on the table. A nail salon in Chicago added “Gel-X Removal” to their menu, updated the page the same day, and saw $600 in extra revenue that month from people searching that exact term.

**Q: What if I don’t have time to write a services page?**
Hire someone, but don’t hand them a blank document and hope for magic. Write five bullet points about what makes your business different, list your top five services with what you charge, and give them three customer reviews or testimonials. That’s enough for a good writer to build a page that converts. I wrote this whole article and I’m still not going to pretend you should do it yourself if you’re better at styling hair than writing copy. Just don’t copy your competitor’s page — I’ve seen three bakery services pages in Portland that are identical because they all stole from the same template. That makes everyone look generic.

**Q: Do I need a booking system integrated into my services page or is a phone number enough?**
Phone number alone will cost you bookings. People who look at services pages at 9 PM are not going to call you at 9 PM. They’ll forget by morning. Embed a booking widget from Square Appointments, Booksy, or whatever system you use. If the widget is too expensive, a simple contact form with “What service are you interested in?” and “Preferred date/time” is better than nothing. A dog groomer in Nashville added a basic form and picked up 8–10 extra bookings per month from people who would have bounced off a page that only showed a phone number.
