Local link building is not just about getting links from other websites. It's about creating content that people will share, link to, and talk about in your community. It's about showcasing your expertise, building relationships with other local businesses, and demonstrating your commitment to your city.

In this article, we'll cover the top local link building strategies for small business owners like you. We'll show you how to create linkable assets, get featured in local publications, and build relationships with other businesses that can help you grow.

**Why Local Link Building Matters**

Local link building is crucial for small business owners because it helps you:

* Increase your online visibility and reach a wider audience
* Build trust and credibility with your target market
* Drive more traffic to your website and generate leads
* Improve your search engine rankings and stay ahead of the competition

But local link building is not a one-time task. It requires ongoing effort and strategy to get results.

**Building Relationships with Other Local Businesses**

One of the most effective ways to get local links is by building relationships with other businesses in your community. This can be done through:

* Partnering with complementary businesses to create joint content
* Getting featured in local publications and events
* Participating in online communities and forums related to your industry

For example, a coffee shop in downtown Los Angeles could partner with a nearby yoga studio to create a joint content piece on "The Best Spots to Grab a Coffee in LA After a Yoga Class". This not only gets them a link from the yoga studio's website but also exposes them to a new audience.

**Creating Linkable Assets**

Linkable assets are pieces of content that people will naturally link to because they're informative, entertaining, or useful. Here are some ideas for creating linkable assets:

* Infographics: Visual representations of data or information that people can share on social media
* Whitepapers: In-depth guides on a specific topic that demonstrate your expertise
* Videos: Educational or entertaining content that showcases your brand
* Podcasts: Interviews with industry experts or thought leaders

For example, a pet groomer in Chicago could create an infographic on "The Top 10 Most Common Pet Health Issues in Chicago" that people can share on social media.

<Callout type="tip">Use data and statistics to make your linkable assets more compelling and shareable.</Callout>

**Getting Featured in Local Publications**

Getting featured in local publications is a great way to get links and exposure for your business. Here are some tips for getting featured:

* Develop relationships with local journalists and influencers
* Pitch story ideas that are relevant to your business and the publication
* Offer exclusive content or interviews to get featured

For example, a fitness studio in New York City could pitch a story idea to a local magazine on "The Best Workout Routines for Busy Professionals in NYC".

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: The Spray-and-Pray Email Blast

**The Story:** A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas — let's call it "The Daily Grind" — decided it was time to get serious about link building. They found a list of 300 local websites, copied a generic email template from a blog post, and sent it to everyone: real estate agents, dentists, the local newspaper, a pizza place, the botanical gardens. The email said something like "I love your site, please link to mine."

After three weeks, they got zero replies. Worse, two of the recipients tweeted screenshots of the email with the caption "Spam from a local business. Not cool." The owner spent 12 hours compiling that list and writing emails. At a $50/hour opportunity cost (they closed early twice to do this), that's $600 down the drain. They also lost goodwill in the local business community.

**What went wrong:** It was begging, not relationship-building. Nobody likes being asked for favors by strangers on the internet. The emails weren't personalized. They offered zero value in return. And they sent the same message to a real estate agent and a newspaper editor — two people with completely different motivations.

**The fix:** I told them to stop everything. Instead, they spent two hours identifying 15 local businesses and organizations they genuinely admired. For each one, they spent 15 minutes writing a personalized email that mentioned something specific: "I loved your piece on the East Austin food scene. I wrote a follow-up on coffee culture in the same neighborhood — thought you might find it useful as a resource for your readers." They offered the other business something real: a free coffee tasting for their staff, a mention in their newsletter, or a co-created event.

**The outcome:** They got replies from 8 of the 15. Five turned into links. Two became ongoing partnerships (a monthly cross-promotion with a nearby bookstore and a "coffee + paint" event with an art studio). The links didn't just boost their Domain Authority — they drove actual foot traffic. Over the next quarter, their organic search traffic from local queries went up 34%. They attributed about $1,200 in new monthly revenue to those partnerships. Cost of the campaign: 4 hours of work and 15 free lattes.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring the "No-Follow" Lie

**The Story:** A hair salon in Nashville — "The Chop Shop" — hired a freelance SEO consultant (note: not me, someone else) who told them to "only chase do-follow links." The salon owner, Sarah, spent six months turning down mentions on local event pages, directory listings, and a feature in a local magazine because the client was told "those links don't count."

During that six months, her main competitor — "Stylist & Co." — showed up on page 2 of every local search. They had fewer do-follow links but appeared in 15 local roundups, the chamber of commerce site, and had a Yelp profile with 200 reviews. Guess who got more customers? Not Sarah.

**What went wrong:** Sarah's consultant was technically correct that no-follow links don't pass PageRank directly. But they missed the practical reality: those no-follow mentions drive real referral traffic, build brand recognition, and increase the likelihood that someone with actual authority (say, a local journalist) will later link to you with a do-follow. Also, Google's algorithm considers the broader context — a link from a local magazine, even if no-follow, signals that you're a real business in the community.

**The fix:** I worked with Sarah to pivot her strategy. She accepted every legitimate local mention she could get. She joined the Nashville Chamber of Commerce (annual fee: $400, included a directory listing, no-follow but relevant). She submitted her salon to local wedding blog roundups (brides search for "best hair for wedding in Nashville"). She even sponsored a local 5K race ($500, got a mention on their site and a link from the race results page — both no-follow).

**The outcome:** Within four months, her site traffic increased 28%. Her revenue from new customers specifically searching "hair salon Nashville" went up by $2,400 per month. The no-follow links built her reputation, and one of those wedding blog features actually brought in a bride who spent $800 on services for her wedding party. Sarah stopped obsessing over do-follow vs. no-follow and started focusing on whether the link's audience matched her ideal customer. She's now ranking in the top 3 for "best hair salon Nashville" — beating the competitor who chased do-follow links.

**The Story:** A pet groomer in Portland, Oregon — "Paws & Claws" — decided to create the ultimate linkable asset: a comprehensive guide to "The Best Dog Parks in Portland." They spent 40 hours researching, visiting every park, taking photos, writing descriptions, mapping out amenities. The guide was genuinely good. They published it, then sat back waiting for the links to roll in.

They got zero. Not a single link in three months.

**What went wrong:** Nobody knew the guide existed. The groomer didn't reach out to anyone. They didn't submit it to local blogs, didn't share it in Facebook groups, didn't email local pet stores. They assumed Google would magically find it and reward them. Google doesn't work that way. A linkable asset without outreach is like opening a restaurant with no sign on the door.

**The fix:** I advised them to spend 10 hours on promotion, not creation. They emailed 20 local pet bloggers and influencers with a personal note: "I spent a month putting together a free guide to Portland dog parks. It's already saved my clients a lot of trial-and-error. If you think it'd be useful for your audience, feel free to share it or link to it." They joined the "Portland Pet Lovers" Facebook group and posted the guide as a resource. They offered to let a local pet supply store include it in their newsletter.

**The outcome:** Three bloggers linked to the guide. Two local pet stores added it to their "Resources" page. The Facebook post got shared 67 times. After the promotion push, the guide itself wasn't the star — it was a tool to start conversations. Total cost: $0 in advertising, 10 hours of outreach. Over the next six months, they received 14 external links to the guide. Their website's local SEO improved enough that they started ranking on page 1 for "pet groomer Portland." Revenue from organic search increased $3,800 in that period. The guide now gets steady traffic and periodically updated by the owner, which keeps it fresh.

### Mistake 4: Treating Every Link Equally

**The Story:** A fitness studio in Chicago — "The Sweat Box" — joined a "link exchange" group on Facebook. The deal: you link to my site, I link to yours. They got 30 links from random blogs about everything from car insurance to knitting to real estate in Florida. They thought this would skyrocket their local rankings.

Instead, their organic traffic dropped 15% over two months. Google started showing their site for "car insurance Chicago" instead of "fitness classes Chicago." Then their rankings tanked.

**What went wrong:** Those links were from irrelevant sites, some of which were clearly low-quality. Google's algorithm penalizes sites with unnatural link profiles that look like reciprocal schemes. The fitness studio's "local relevance signal" got muddled. A link from a knitting blog in Florida doesn't tell Google you're a gym in Chicago. It tells Google you're trying to game the system.

**The fix:** I helped them disavow the bad links (submit a disavow file via Google Search Console). Then we built 15 high-quality, locally relevant links: the Chicago Park District site (where they offered free Saturday classes), a local running club's blog, the neighborhood business association, a nearby smoothie bar that they cross-promoted with. Every link had to meet one criterion: "Would my target customer in Chicago actually visit this site?"

**The outcome:** It took about 3 months to recover. After the disavow and the new link building, their traffic returned to pre-penalty levels and then grew 22% beyond that. Their bounce rate dropped — because the traffic coming from those relevant links was actually interested in their services. Revenue from organic search hit $4,500/month. They also stopped wasting time on link exchanges and focus on genuine partnerships.

## How to Create a Local Linkable Asset on a $200 Budget

Most local SEO guides tell you to create "linkable assets" — resources so good that people naturally link to them. They make it sound like you need a $5,000 infographic or a full-time content team. You don't.

I've done this with three types of assets that cost under $200 and took less than a day to create:

**1. The "Best [City] [Service]" List (no, not your own business)**

A coffee shop in Denver created "The Definitive Guide to Coffee Shops with Actual Outlets in Denver." They visited 25 coffee shops, tested the outlets, rated the wifi speed, and noted the noise level. Published it as a simple blog post with a table. Cost: $0 for the content (they already had time to visit coffee shops), $50 for a designer on Fiverr to make a simple map graphic. Outcome: 12 local blog links, 9 from other coffee shop websites (surprisingly, other shops liked being included), and 3 from coworking spaces. One month later, their own coffee shop's organic traffic from "Denver coffee shop" queries went up 18%.

**2. A "Local [Seasonal] Event Calendar"**

A hair salon in Austin created a monthly calendar of "Austin Events Where You Should Actually Bring Hairstyle-Ready Products." It listed music festivals, charity galas, art openings — anything that required looking decent. They published it on their site and emailed local event organizers asking for corrections or additions. Cost: $100 for a Mailchimp account they already had. Outcome: 6 local event organizer websites linked to them as a resource. The calendar generated 22 new email subscribers in the first month.

**3. A "Neighborhood Guide" from a Local's Perspective**

A pet groomer in Portland created "The Groomer's Guide to Portland's Dog-Friendly Waterfront." It wasn't about dog parks — it was about waterfront spots where you could legally let your dog swim, with notes on parking, safety, and whether there were nearby trash cans. Cost: $0 for content, $20 for a map embed from Google Maps (free tier). Outcome: 4 local real estate agents linked to it (they want to show clients the neighborhood's dog-friendliness), 2 local travel blogs, and the Portland Parks & Recreation department shared it on social media.

The key: don't create an asset about your business. Create an asset about your community that happens to be relevant to your business. The linkers aren't linking to you because you're great — they're linking because you've saved them time or solved a problem for their audience.

## The Uncomfortable Truth About Local Bloggers and Journalists

I've spent years pitching local media on behalf of clients, and here's what nobody tells you: most local journalists and bloggers are overworked, underpaid, and receiving 200 emails a day from desperate PR people and business owners asking for coverage. Your generic pitch goes straight to the trash.

But there's a window. Here's how to get through it:

**Don't pitch your business. Pitch a story about your community.**

A real example: A yoga studio in Denver wanted coverage in the local alt-weekly. Instead of emailing the editor with "We're Denver's best yoga studio, please write about us," they surveyed 200 locals about "How Denver's Remote Work Culture Changed Our Exercise Habits." They packaged the data (simple Google Forms survey, $0) into a one-page summary and pitched it as a trend piece to the paper's lifestyle editor. The editor bit, interviewed the studio owner, and the story included a link to the studio's website. Cost: 2 hours to set up the survey and analyze results. Outcome: The article got 4,000 views, generated 15 new clients, and the link improved their local SEO enough that they started ranking in the top 3 for "yoga Denver."

**Another reality: bloggers want something in return.**

A hair salon in Austin tried to get a link from a local beauty blogger. They offered a free haircut in exchange for a review and a link. The blogger said no. Why? Because the blogger gets dozens of offers for free services, and most of them are trying to buy a link. Instead, I advised the salon owner to offer the blogger something the blogger couldn't easily get: exclusive early access to a new product line the salon was launching, plus a 20% affiliate commission on any sales the blogger drove. The blogger agreed, wrote a genuine review (not a paid ad), and linked to the salon. The affiliate commission cost the salon $200 in total from five customer referrals. The blogger's post drove 12 new appointments worth $1,400.

**The bottom line:** journalists and bloggers are not vending machines where you insert a press release and get a link. They need a story, a hook, or a genuine value exchange. Treat them like humans, not backlink deposits.

## How to Use Your Existing Tools to Generate Links (Without Extra Work)

You already pay for tools like Square, Mailchimp, Booksy, or Yelp. Most small business owners use them for transactions and newsletters. But these tools can generate links too — if you use them deliberately.

**1. Mailchimp: The "Resource Roundup" Email**

Instead of sending a weekly "buy my stuff" email, send a quarterly email that curates the best local resources for your customers. For a pet groomer in Portland: "Top 5 Dog-Friendly Patios in Portland This Summer" (with links to the patios' websites, which link back to you). Email those businesses and say, "I featured you in my newsletter to 500 pet owners. If you want to share it, here's the public version: [link]." Most will link back to your newsletter page to thank you. Cost: $0 (you already have Mailchimp). Outcome: 8 new links from local businesses, and your open rates go up because people love resource emails.

**2. Square / Toast: The "Local Business Page"**

If you use Square for payments, their online store or business page can include a blog or resource section. Use it to publish short, helpful guides. Then, when you write about a local park or event, link to their official site and send them a quick note: "Just featured your park in my Square site's guide to Portland dog spots — thanks for being such a great local resource." They'll appreciate the free mention and often reciprocate. One nail salon in Chicago used this tactic to get 4 links from local parks and recreation sites.

**3. Booksy / Vagaro: The "Event Calendar"**

Appointment booking platforms like Booksy let you list events or promotions. Create a "Local Business Spotlight" event where you partner with a neighboring coffee shop or boutique. Offer a discount to anyone who brings a receipt from that partner. Then ask the partner to list the event on their site with a link to your Booksy profile. One fitness studio in Denver did this with a juice bar: the juice bar linked to the studio's Booksy page, and the studio's traffic from that juice bar's site led to 6 new appointments worth $480. The link itself improved the studio's local rankings.

**4. Yelp: The "Unofficial" Link (Don't Call It That)**

Yelp doesn't allow direct do-follow links in reviews, but you can get links from Yelp's "Unofficial" pages. Actually, don't try to game Yelp. Instead, use Yelp to build relationships. Review other local businesses (honestly, not just 5-stars). When you write a thoughtful review that's genuinely helpful, the business owner might check out your profile and visit your site — and if they like what they see, they might link to you from their own site. It's slow, but it works. One coffee shop owner in Seattle got 3 links this way over six months.

The point: you're already paying for these tools. Use them as link-building infrastructure, not just transaction processors.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does link building for local SEO really work, or is it just a way for SEOs to charge more money?**

It works, but not like a light switch. If you're a coffee shop in Austin with 50 monthly searches for "coffee shop Austin," you're not going to rank #1 overnight because you got 4 links. Link building raises your site's credibility in Google's eyes, which helps you beat competitors who have similar content but weaker backlink profiles. I've seen it translate to real revenue: a dental clinic in Denver got 15 local links over 6 months, and their organic traffic from "dentist Denver" increased 40%, bringing in 22 new patients worth $6,600. But if your site's content is thin or your Google Business Profile is incomplete, links alone won't save you.

**Q: I run a hair salon. Who would actually want to link to my website?**

Good question, and it's the first one I ask my clients. You're probably not getting a link from CNN. But you can get links from: local wedding blogs (brides search for wedding hair), neighborhood association sites (they list local businesses), local magazines or newspapers (if you have a story), other complementary businesses (makeup artists, nail salons, dress shops), and customer reviews on third-party sites (which help indirectly). The best strategy: become the go-to local expert on something specific — "best braids for Austin brides" — and pitch that piece to local wedding bloggers. That's linkable.

**Q: How much time should I spend on link building per week?**

Start with 2 hours per week. That's enough to identify 5–10 relevant local sites, write personalized emails, or create a small resource. Don't try to do it all in one month — it's a slow burn. One client — a pet groomer in Portland — spent 2 hours per week for 4 months, got 18 links, and said the time was worth it for the traffic increase. If you're charging $100/hour for grooming, those 2 hours cost you $200 in opportunity cost. Compare that to the $1,200/month in new revenue the links brought. Do the math.

**Q: What if I try to get links and everyone says no?**

That happens. Expect more "no" than "yes." I've pitched 50 journalists and gotten 2 replies. That's normal. Don't take it personally. If you're getting 100% rejection, either your pitch is bad, your offer isn't valuable, or you're targeting the wrong sites. A good pitch has a clear hook: "I surveyed 200 local dog owners about what they hate about dog parks — here are the results. Thought your readers might find this useful." If that doesn't work, try a different angle. Also, email people who are likely to say yes first — existing customers, partners, local business owners you've helped before. Build from there.

**Q: Is it worth paying for a local PR agency to get links?**

Only if they actually deliver results you can measure. Most local PR agencies charge $1,500–$3,000/month and promise "media mentions." Ask them for case studies with specific URLs of links they've earned for small businesses in your city. If they can't show you 5–10 actual links from local publications (not just "we got coverage in a magazine"), walk away. The best alternative: hire a freelance writer on Upwork ($50–$100/hour) to write a guest post or a local guide for you, and do the outreach yourself. You'll pay less and have more control.

**Q: My competitor has tons of links from spammy sites. Should I do the same?**

No. Google penalizes spammy link profiles. If your competitor is buying links from "Chicago SEO link packages" on Fiverr, they're playing a dangerous game. You don't need to match their volume. You need better relevance. One local business owner in Chicago told me his competitor had 1,000 spammy links but ranked page 1. Six months later, Google updated its algorithm and that competitor dropped to page 5. The link package business lost everything. Focus on 10 high-quality local links over 100 junk ones.
