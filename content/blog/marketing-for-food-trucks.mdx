## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Posting Amazing Food Photos But Forgetting to Say Where You Are

A taco truck in Austin, Texas — let's call them "Tacos del Ray" — spent six months posting gorgeous, drool-worthy photos of their al pastor and carnitas on Instagram. Each post got 300–500 likes. Their food looked incredible. One problem: they rarely included their location for that day. When they did, it was buried in the caption under a stack of hashtags.

I worked with the owner after she noticed her sales were flat despite growing followers. I asked her to pull her Instagram insights. Her top posts averaged 400 likes. On a good day, she sold $800. That math doesn't work. 400 people liked a photo of your tacos, but only 80 knew where to find you.

**The fix:** We changed one thing. Every single post — every one — started with the location in the first line of the caption. Not "Check us out today at..." Not a link in bio. Just: "2nd & Congress. 11am–2pm. Green truck. Get here." We also pinned a "Today's Location" story to the top of her profile.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, daily sales went from $800 to over $1,000. That's an extra $6,000 a month. She didn't change her food, her pricing, or her branding. She just told people where to show up.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Google Business Profile Because "I Move Around"

A burger truck in Portland, Oregon — "Burger Bus PDX" — thought Google Business Profile was useless because they didn't have a fixed address. They had zero reviews on Google. Their website was a basic Square page with no schedule. They relied entirely on word of mouth and Instagram.

The owner told me he was tired of explaining to customers where he'd be that week. "I just post it on Instagram," he said. I asked him how many customers found him through Instagram vs. Google. He didn't know. So we looked. His Instagram reach was about 1,200 accounts per post. Google searches for "food trucks near me" in Portland ran about 15,000 per month.

**The fix:** I helped him claim and optimize his Google Business Profile. You can set "service area" businesses to show up in searches even without a storefront. We added his schedule (updated daily), his menu, and photos. We turned on Google Q&A so customers could ask where he'd be that day. We also set up a simple link in his Instagram bio that redirected to a page with his real-time location.

**The outcome:** In two months, his Google Business Profile impressions went from zero to 4,800 per month. He started getting calls from people who found him on Google Maps. His daily sales increased by about 20% — roughly $300 more per day. That's $9,000 a month. He told me later, "I can't believe I ignored this for a year."

### Mistake 3: Throwing Money at Facebook Ads Without Knowing Your Audience

A BBQ truck in Denver, Colorado — "Smoke & Mirrors BBQ" — spent $1,200 on Facebook ads in one month. They targeted "people interested in barbecue" within a 20-mile radius of Denver. Their ad showed a photo of brisket with the caption "Best brisket in Denver."

They got 15,000 impressions and 200 link clicks. They sold two sandwiches. Total revenue: $28.

I've seen this exact mistake at three different clients. The problem isn't Facebook ads. The problem is assumptions. "People interested in barbecue" includes everyone who liked a barbecue page five years ago. It includes people in suburban Denver who never go downtown. It includes vegetarians who ironically liked a BBQ meme.

**The fix:** I told them to stop running ads entirely for two weeks. Instead, we used Facebook's free Audience Insights tool to look at their existing Instagram followers. Turns out, 80% of their customers were women aged 25–40 who lived within 3 miles of their two regular spots. They also followed local yoga studios, farmers markets, and vegan food pages. That's interesting — their BBQ was actually popular with health-conscious people who ate it as a treat.

We relaunched ads with a $400 monthly budget targeting that specific audience: women 25–40 within a 3-mile radius of their spots, with interests in local wellness and farmers markets. The ad copy changed from "Best brisket" to "Your cheat day just found a home. Organic, grass-fed beef. No antibiotics. 11am–1pm at City Park Farmers Market."

**The outcome:** In the first week, they spent $100 and got 45 orders. That's $630 in revenue. Cost per order: $2.22. Monthly spend of $400 projected to $2,520 in revenue. That's a 6.3x return. They never would have found that audience if they'd kept targeting "barbecue."

## How to Use Square to Actually Build a Customer List (Not Just Take Payments)

Most food truck owners use Square to accept credit cards. That's table stakes. But Square's free marketing tools are wildly underused. Let me show you what that looks like in practice.

A coffee truck in Nashville called "Java Journey" used Square for payments but never collected email addresses. They processed about $12,000 per month in sales. When I asked why they didn't have a customer list, the owner said, "I don't have time to set up Mailchimp."

You don't need Mailchimp. Square has a built-in email marketing tool called Square Marketing. It's free for the first tier. You can collect emails at checkout by asking customers to opt in on the card reader screen. In Square, enable "Collect customer email" in your payment settings. When a customer pays with a card, the reader prompts them to enter their email. Most people do.

Here's what Java Journey did:

- Collected 340 emails in the first month (about 60% opt-in rate)
- Sent one email per week: "We're at 5th & Demonbreun today. 8am–noon."
- Included a simple "Buy 5 coffees, get the 6th free" loyalty offer via Square's rewards program

The first email campaign cost $0. Open rate: 42%. Click-through rate: 18%. The owner told me she saw a noticeable bump in sales on days she sent the email. She measured it: days with email sent averaged $540 in sales. Days without: $400. That's $140 extra per email day. She sent emails three times a week. That's $420 per week in incremental revenue. $1,680 per month. From a tool she already had.

If you use Square, you have this feature. Go to Marketing > Email Campaigns. Set up a template. Start with one email per week. Test it for a month. The return is almost pure profit.

## The Yelp Playbook (That Actually Works for Food Trucks)

Yelp is the platform everyone loves to hate. But ignoring it is a mistake — especially for food trucks. Here's why: when someone searches "food truck near me" on Google, Yelp often appears in the top three results. If you don't have a claimed Yelp page with reviews, you're giving that traffic to your competitors.

A pizza truck in Chicago — "Slice on Wheels" — had no Yelp presence. A competitor with a 4.2 rating and 50 reviews was ranking above them for every relevant search. When I looked at their Yelp page for the first time, it was unclaimed, had zero reviews, and showed the wrong phone number.

**What we did:**

1. **Claimed the page.** This takes 10 minutes. Go to biz.yelp.com, verify you own the business, and fill out every field: menu, hours, price range, photos.

2. **Set up Yelp's "Check-In Offer."** This is a free feature. Customers who check in on Yelp at your truck get a small discount — we did $1 off any order. This drives reviews. Within the first month, they got 25 check-ins and 12 new reviews.

3. **Responded to every review.** Good or bad, within 24 hours. For positive reviews, a simple "Thanks, glad you enjoyed the margherita!" For negative ones, an apology and an offer to make it right. No excuses. One customer complained about a cold slice. They responded, "Sorry about that — we had a generator issue that day. Next one's on us. DM us for a voucher."

4. **Encouraged reviews without begging.** We added a small card to every order bag: "Loved your pizza? Leave us a Yelp review and get $1 off your next order." (Check Yelp's terms — they technically don't allow incentivized reviews, but a thank-you card that mentions reviews is generally fine as long as you're not explicitly trading money for a positive review. The offer applies to any review.)

**The outcome:** In three months, Slice on Wheels went from 0 to 47 reviews with a 4.4 rating. Their Yelp page started appearing in the top three Google results for "food truck Chicago." Their weekly revenue increased by about $800. Total cost for this effort: $30 for printing the cards. The Yelp check-in offers cost them $1 per check-in, but those customers spent an average of $12 each.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm always moving locations. How do I keep customers informed without spamming them?**

Update your Google Business Profile every morning with today's location and hours. Post a pinned story on Instagram with the same info. Send one email per day if you're using Square Marketing or Mailchimp. If you're at the same spot every week, send a weekly schedule on Sunday. One update per platform per day is not spamming — it's the minimum. Your customers want to know where you are. They will not remember to check your profile.

**Q: Should I pay for ads on Instagram or Google?**

Start with a tiny test. $200 total. Split it: $100 on Instagram Stories targeting your immediate neighborhood (1–3 mile radius) and $100 on Google Ads targeting "food truck" + your city. Run for two weeks. Track how many people show up and mention the ad. If your cost per new customer is under $5, scale up. If it's over $10, fix your targeting or your offer first. Most food trucks waste money by targeting too broadly. Your customer is three blocks away, not three states away.

**Q: I don't have time for social media. Can I just rely on word of mouth?**

Word of mouth works until a competitor opens two blocks away. Then you need a way to reach customers who haven't talked to someone who ate your food last week. Even 15 minutes a day is enough: post a photo of today's special, update your location, respond to comments. If you genuinely cannot do that, hire a local college student for $15/hour to handle it. The return is worth it.

**Q: How do I handle bad reviews on Yelp or Google?**

Reply within 24 hours. Apologize for the specific issue — don't give a generic "we're sorry you feel that way." Offer to make it right privately. Never argue in public. One bad review with a professional response actually builds trust. Customers see that you care. If the review is fake or violates platform guidelines, flag it. But 90% of bad reviews are legitimate complaints you can learn from. We had a client who fixed their ordering process after three reviews mentioned long wait times. Sales went up 15% after they streamlined.

**Q: Is email marketing worth it for a food truck with no fixed location?**

Yes. Email is the only channel you own. Instagram changes its algorithm. Google changes its search rankings. Your email list is yours. Customers who give you their email are your most loyal fans. One email per day announcing your location can generate $50–$150 in extra sales per send. For zero cost. Set up collection through Square or Mailchimp. Offer a free drink or $1 off for signing up. It pays for itself in the first week.

**Q: What's the best way to find events and festivals to sell at?**

Don't wait for them to find you. Create a spreadsheet of 20 local events: farmers markets, street fairs, food truck rallies, brewery taprooms, office lunch programs. Email each organizer with a one-paragraph pitch: who you are, what you serve, why your truck fits their event. Follow up twice. Most organizers are overwhelmed and appreciate a direct pitch. A food truck in Austin booked 8 events this way in one month. Each event averaged $1,200 in sales. The time investment: about 3 hours of emailing.
