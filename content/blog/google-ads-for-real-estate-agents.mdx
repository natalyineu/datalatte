## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve spent enough time inside agency meeting rooms to know that most Google Ads advice for real estate is either too vague to be useful or actively wrong. Here are four mistakes I’ve seen repeat across clients in different cities — along with what actually fixed them.

### Mistake #1: Bidding on “houses for sale” and nothing else

A broker in Nashville came to me after burning through $1,400 in six weeks. His campaign targeted keywords like “Nashville homes for sale” and “houses in Nashville TN.” On paper, those look relevant. In practice, he was paying for clicks from people who had already toured five properties, already had an agent, and were just checking listings on their own.

That $1,400 generated exactly two leads. One was someone who said “just browsing.” The other wanted a property that had been sold three months earlier.

**What to do instead:** Segment your keywords by intent, not just geography.

- **Buyer intent, early stage:** “first time home buyer Nashville,” “moving to Nashville from out of state,” “Nashville real estate agent for relocation”
- **Buyer intent, active:** “Nashville condos under $400k,” “homes for sale in East Nashville with acreage” — these usually come in via the Google Ads Search Partner Network if you’ve disabled it, by the way
- **Seller intent:** “sell my house fast Nashville,” “Nashville home value estimate,” “how to sell a house without a realtor in Tennessee”

**The result:** After we rebuilt his campaigns around intent segmentation and paused the generic terms, he spent $1,200 the next month and got 11 qualified leads — five sellers and six buyers. Cost per lead dropped from $700 to $109.

### Mistake #2: Sending all traffic to your homepage

A team in Austin ran Google Ads for six months and kept telling me the leads were “low quality.” I asked to see where the ads were sending people. To their homepage. Their homepage — which listed 40+ listings, a team photo, three contact forms, a blog link, and a pop-up newsletter signup.

Prospective clients landed there, got confused, and left.

**What to do instead:** Build a dedicated landing page for every ad group. It doesn’t need to be complicated. A single page with:
– One headline that matches your ad (“Sell Your Austin Home in 14 Days”)
– A short bullet list of what makes you different
– A single form asking for name, email, phone, and property type
– No navigation menu — don’t give them a reason to leave

You can build this with Unbounce, Leadpages, or even a clean WordPress page. I’ve seen good results from people who just used a stripped-down version of their MLS IDX page — but stripped-down is the key word.

**The result:** The Austin team swapped their homepage link for a seller-specific landing page. Within three weeks, their contact form submission rate went from 1.2% to 6.8%. They closed two listing appointments worth roughly $4,200 in commission in the first month — on $900 in ad spend.

### Mistake #3: Not excluding your own sphere

A real estate agent in Portland called me frustrated that she was spending $800 a month on her own name. Literally. She was running ads on “[her name] real estate” and “[agency name] Portland.” She thought that would capture people who searched for her directly. Instead, it captured past clients who already had her number — and Google charged her $12–$18 per click for the privilege.

Over six months, she had spent roughly $2,400 on people who could have called her for free.

**What to do instead:** Add your own brand name and your agency name as negative keywords. Also add competitor names you don’t want to pay for. And add common misspellings of your own name — yes, really.

**The result:** She saved roughly $300 a month by cutting those branded clicks. She redirected that budget to seller intent keywords. In month two, she booked a full-price listing from a seller who found her via “sell my home Portland OR.” One listing alone paid for six months of ads.

### Mistake #4: Setting the location targeting to “Presence or Interest”

This one kills campaigns quietly. A Chicago team targeting “Chicago real estate” had their ads showing to someone in New York who had just searched for Chicago apartments once. That click cost them $22. The user never converted.

**What to do instead:** In Google Ads, change location targeting to “Presence” only. This means the ad only shows to people who are physically in your target area. Then layer in a radius around your city — 10 to 20 miles is usually enough for real estate. For sellers, you can keep it tight. For buyers relocating from another state, you may want a separate campaign that targets “presence” in other cities but uses keywords like “relocating to Chicago.”

**The result:** The Chicago agent adjusted her targeting in 90 seconds. Her click-through rate stayed the same, but her lead quality improved noticeably. She stopped getting calls from people in Indiana who “were just thinking about moving someday.” Her cost per lead dropped 34% over the next month.

## How to Structure a Real Estate Google Ads Account That Doesn’t Leak Money

Most real estate agents set up one campaign, throw in 50 keywords, and call it done. That works — poorly. A cleaner structure saves you money and makes optimization actually possible.

### Build separate campaigns for buyers and sellers

Buyers and sellers search differently. They have different timelines, different objections, and different costs to acquire.

For a buyer campaign, target terms like:
- “homes for sale in [city]” (broad match modifier — not exact, not broad)
- “[city] real estate listings”
- “condos in [neighborhood] [city]”
- “houses under [price] in [city]”

For a seller campaign, target:
- “sell my house [city]”
- “home valuation [city]”
- “sell my house fast [city]”
- “comparative market analysis [city]”

Each campaign gets its own budget. If you set a single budget and mix them, seller terms (which usually convert faster) will get starved by buyer terms that generate more clicks but fewer conversions.

**Example from Denver:** One agent had a combined campaign spending $1,800/month. Buyer terms ate 78% of the budget. Sellers got the rest. After splitting into two campaigns with $900 each, the seller campaign was spending the full $900 on terms that closed in 14 days. She booked three seller consultations in the first week. The buyer campaign kept running at $900/month but now with cleaner data.

### Use call-only ads for sellers

Google call-only ads show up on mobile and let users tap to call directly. This works well for seller leads because they tend to want immediate answers — not a form that gets answered in 24 hours.

Set up a call-only ad group within your seller campaign. Use a tracking number from CallRail or Google Voice so you can count calls as conversions. Set your bid to “target CPA” and start with $25. If you get no calls, raise it. If you get calls but they’re not qualified, lower it.

**In practice:** A real estate team in Kansas City ran call-only ads for 30 days. They got 47 calls. Of those, 12 turned into listing appointments and 4 closed. Total ad spend: $1,100. Commission on four listings (average home value $310k at 3%): roughly $37,200.

### Keep search partners off

Google’s search partner network is a black box. I’ve seen clicks from sites I can’t identify, with bounce rates above 90%, costing $8–$15 each. For real estate, those clicks almost never convert. Turn search partners off in every campaign. The setting is under “Networks” in campaign settings. Uncheck the box.

### Set up conversion tracking before you spend a dollar

This sounds obvious. About 40% of the agents I talk to haven’t done it. They’re looking at clicks and impressions and guessing which ads work. You need:

- Google Ads conversion tracking (set to “contact” or “form submission”)
- Imported calls from call tracking
- If you use a CRM like Follow Up Boss or kvCORE, link it via Google Ads integrations or a manual import

Without this data, you’re spending money on what looks good instead of what works.

**One real example:** An agent in Phoenix told me her campaign was “working great” because CTR was 4.2%. I asked her conversion rate. She didn’t know. We set up tracking. Her conversion rate was 0.8%. She was spending $1,600 a month to get maybe two leads. After restructuring around tracking data, she cut spend to $800 and got six leads.

## Landing Page Must-Haves for Real Estate Google Ads

Your landing page matters more than your ad. A brilliant ad sending people to a slow, confusing page will fail every time. Here’s what I’ve seen work across dozens of campaigns.

### One question per page

If you run ads for buyers, your landing page should ask: “What type of home are you looking for?” Not: “Are you buying or selling — or both — or maybe just curious?” That’s three questions. Confusion kills conversions.

For sellers, the landing page should ask one thing: “What’s your property address?” That’s it. You can collect name and email after they enter the address.

A real estate agent in Scottsdale changed her seller form from seven fields to three (address, name, email). Her lead volume doubled. She thought she’d get lower quality — nope. Sellers who give their address are serious. The extra fields were just barriers.

### Load in under two seconds

Mobile users in real estate are often driving, standing in front of a house, or sitting in a car. They don’t wait. If your page takes more than two seconds to load, they leave.

Use Google PageSpeed Insights. If your page scores below 70 on mobile, fix it before you spend ad dollars. Common fixes: compress images, remove autoplay video tours (those things are heavy), and use a lightweight theme.

**Numeric example:** An agent in Charlotte had a landing page that loaded in 4.7 seconds. Her conversion rate was 1.9%. After optimizing images and removing a background video, load time dropped to 1.8 seconds. Conversion rate rose to 4.1%. Same ad spend, same keywords — just a faster page.

### Include social proof that matches the ad

If your ad says “top agent in Tampa,” your landing page better show that badge from a recognized source. If you claim “sold 50 homes last year,” list a specific number. Vague praise from a past client with no context is less effective than a real quote with a name and location.

Even better: embed a Google Business Profile widget showing your recent reviews. People trust those more than testimonials you wrote yourself.

### Use one clear call-to-action

Don’t give people three buttons: “Get a home value,” “Browse listings,” “Contact us.” Pick one. For seller ads, the call-to-action is “Get Your Home Value.” For buyer ads, it might be “View Available Homes” or “Get Pre-Approved.”

A broker in Seattle had two buttons on his landing page — “Listings” and “Get Valuation.” Click-through to form dropped because users hesitated. He removed the listings button and saw a 22% increase in form completions.

### Collect phone numbers, not just emails

Emails go to spam. Phone calls get answered. Make the phone number field mandatory. You can still offer email as an option, but require a phone number.

In some cases, a simple callback request form works even better: “Enter your phone number and we’ll call you in 10 minutes.” A team in San Antonio tried this and saw a 40% higher conversion rate on their seller ad group. Sellers liked not having to wait.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads as a real estate agent?**

Start with $500–$1,000 per month for a single market. That’s enough to test buyer and seller campaigns with decent data. If you spend less than $500, you probably won’t get the volume needed to know what works. If you have a bigger market (like NYC or LA), expect to spend $1,500–$3,000 because cost per click is higher. Do not start with $100 and expect results — that’s rent money, not ad budget.

**Q: Google Ads vs. Zillow — which is better for lead generation?**

They’re different. Zillow leads are mostly buyers who want to see listings. They can convert, but they’re expensive and you don’t get exclusivity — other agents can see the same lead. Google Ads gives you more control over who sees your ads and when. For sellers, Google Ads wins every time. For buyers, it depends on your local market. I’ve seen agents who rely on Zillow alone and spend $3,000/month with a 2% close rate. Meanwhile, a well-structured Google Ads campaign at $1,000/month can close at 8–12%. Test both and compare your cost per closed deal — not cost per lead.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads on a $500 budget?**

Yes, but be smart about it. At $500, you can’t compete for the most expensive keywords in your market. Focus on long-tail keywords with lower competition: “sell my house in [city] without paying commission” or “first time home buyer [city] grants.” Also, make sure your location radius is tight — five to ten miles max. And disable search partners and display network. $500/month can work, but you have to track everything.

**Q: Should I only target buyers or only sellers?**

Start with sellers if you need fast cash flow. A seller lead can close in 30 to 60 days and generate thousands in commission. Buyer leads can take six months or more. The ideal structure is separate campaigns for each with different budgets. If you can only do one, pick sellers. The ROI is faster, and the ad cost per conversion is usually lower because seller intent keywords have less competition from other agents.

**Q: How long before I see results from Google Ads?**

If you target sellers, you could get a lead within the first week and close within a month. But that’s the best case. Realistically, give it 60 to 90 days to generate enough data to know what’s working. Don’t make big changes in the first two weeks — let the campaign run and collect conversion data. If you change everything every three days, you’ll never get clear answers. After 90 days, you’ll know which keywords, landing pages, and ad copy drive actual leads.
