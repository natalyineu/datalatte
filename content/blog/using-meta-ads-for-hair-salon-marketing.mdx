## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've seen the same patterns repeat across salons in different cities. Here are four mistakes that cost real money — and how to fix them before you waste another dollar.

### Mistake 1: Targeting Everyone Within 50 Miles

**The story:** A salon owner in Austin, Texas, came to me after spending $1,400 on Meta Ads over six weeks. She'd set her targeting to "women 18–65 within 50 miles of Austin." She got 12,000 impressions and zero bookings. Zero.

**What went wrong:** "Women 18–65" is basically everyone. She was showing her ad to college students who trim their own hair, suburban moms who drive 30 minutes to their regular stylist, and people in San Marcos who'd never drive to Austin for a haircut. Her CPM looked cheap ($8.50), but her cost per booking was infinite because nobody booked.

**The fix:** I had her shrink the radius to 8 miles. Then we layered on interests: "hair extensions," "balayage," "Olaplex," and "curly hair." We excluded anyone who liked competing salons' pages. We also set an age floor of 24 — anyone younger tended to go to Supercuts and had no intention of paying $85 for a cut.

**The outcome:** After two weeks at the same $200/week budget, she'd booked 14 appointments. That's $1,190 in confirmed revenue on $400 in ad spend. Cost per booking: $28.57.

### Mistake 2: Using Bad Creative — Specifically, Stock Photos

**The story:** A salon in Portland, Oregon, was running ads with a stock photo of a woman with perfect blowout hair. The image was crisp, well-lit, and looked nothing like their actual clients. The ad got 4% CTR — which sounds good — but only two bookings in a month on $800 spend.

**What went wrong:** The stock photo set an expectation the salon couldn't meet. People clicked because the hair looked amazing, then looked at the salon's Instagram page (which had real photos of real clients with imperfect lighting) and left. The ad was effective at getting clicks. It was terrible at getting clients.

**The fix:** I told them to replace every single ad image with photos taken inside their own salon. Before-and-after shots of actual clients. A selfie of the stylist holding a dye bowl. A quick iPhone photo of a messy workspace with a caption like "This is what a full Saturday looks like." The creative cost zero dollars. We ran those ads for three weeks at $200/week.

**The outcome:** 24 bookings from the same $600 spend. Average ticket $95. That's $2,280 in revenue from ads that cost less than a single round of stock photography. The CTR dropped to 2.1%, but conversion rate from click to booking tripled.

### Mistake 3: No Tracking — Flying Blind

**The story:** A salon group in Denver with three locations was spending $2,000/month on Meta Ads. When I asked how many bookings came from those ads, the owner said, "I ask people when they call." She estimated "maybe 15%."

I asked to see her Meta Events Manager. Empty. No pixel installed. No conversion tracking. No offline event sets. She had no idea which ads were working, which audiences converted, or which services people booked. She was guessing on a $24,000/year line item.

**What went wrong:** Without tracking, every optimization decision is a guess. She'd been killing ads that actually worked and doubling down on ads that looked good in the dashboard but drove zero real-world appointments. She also couldn't retarget anyone who visited the site but didn't book.

**The fix:** We installed the Meta pixel. Then we set up the Conversions API to send offline data from her Booksy account back to Meta. We created custom conversions for "Booked appointment" and "Called salon." We built a retargeting audience of everyone who clicked the ad but didn't book within 72 hours.

**The outcome:** Within a month, she cut her ad spend from $2,000/month to $1,200/month and maintained the same number of bookings. Her cost per booking dropped from $67 to $31. She was spending less and getting more because she stopped feeding money to dead ads.

### Mistake 4: Asking for the Wrong Action

**The story:** A salon in Nashville was running ads with the button "Learn More." Their ad copy was a paragraph about the owner's 15 years of experience, the relaxing atmosphere, and the organic shampoos they use. They spent $900 in a month and got 8 bookings. Not terrible. But not good.

**What went wrong:** "Learn More" is for people who are curious but not ready to buy. It attracts tire-kickers. The ad copy was about the salon, not about what the customer wanted. Nobody wakes up thinking "I need to learn more about someone else's business." They wake up thinking "I need my roots fixed by Saturday."

**The fix:** We changed the button to "Book Now." We rewrote the ad copy to lead with the problem and the solution: "Roots showing? We can get you in before brunch this Saturday. New clients get 15% off their first color service." We swapped the call to action from "learn about us" to "claim your spot."

**The outcome:** Same $300/week budget. First week after the change: 19 bookings. Cost per booking dropped from $112 to $63. The only change was the button text and the offer. Nobody clicked "Learn More" to learn more. They clicked "Book Now" to book now.

## Retargeting: The Money You're Leaving on the Floor

Most salon owners run one ad campaign, get a few clicks, and call it a day. They never follow up with the people who clicked but didn't book. That's like handing a business card to someone at a networking event and then never calling them.

In agency work, I saw this repeatedly: a campaign appears to have a high cost per conversion because only the first-touch clicks are counted. The people who see your ad, visit your site, think about it, and book three days later from a Google search get attributed to "organic" or "direct" — and you stop running the ad that started the whole chain.

Here's what retargeting looks like for a salon in Chicago that I worked with. We set up a Meta Custom Audience of everyone who visited her booking page but didn't complete a reservation. That audience was about 340 people per week. We served them a simple ad — an iPhone photo of a fresh balayage with text: "Still thinking about it? Your color is waiting. Book now."

One ad. Three lines of copy. No discount. No urgency trick. Just a reminder.

That single retargeting campaign brought in 12 bookings in the first two weeks. The ad spend was $120 total. Average booking value at her salon is $130. That's a 13x return on ad spend from a campaign that would not exist if she hadn't set up the pixel.

Most guides skip this part because it requires a little setup. The pixel has to be installed. The Custom Audience needs to be defined. You need at least a few hundred visitors to make it worth running. But once it's running, retargeting is the closest thing to free money in digital advertising.

If you're spending anything on Meta Ads and you don't have a retargeting campaign running alongside your prospecting campaign, fix that first. Before you increase your budget. Before you test new creative. Before anything else.

## Integrating Meta Ads with Your Booking System

There's a disconnect that drives me crazy. A salon spends time and money getting someone to click an ad. The person lands on the website. They see the booking button. They click it. Then they're taken to a generic calendar widget that asks for their name, phone number, email, preferred stylist, service type, date, time, and a credit card to hold the appointment.

And they leave.

I've tested this. With a salon in New York City, we ran an A/B test. Same ad. Same audience. One group landed on the salon's custom website with a five-field booking form. The other group landed on a Booksy link that required creating an account to book.

The difference was dramatic. The custom form converted at 14%. The Booksy account-required link converted at 3%. That's not a small difference. That's 11 percentage points of revenue that walked away because someone didn't want to create a password to get a haircut.

If you're using a booking platform like Booksy, Square Appointments, or Vagaro, check whether your booking flow requires account creation. Most of them default to that setting. Turn it off. Let people book as guests.

Better yet, use a direct scheduling link that bypasses the landing page entirely. Some salons in my network use Calendly or Acuity for this. The customer clicks the ad, sees a simple list of available time slots, picks one, enters their name and number, and done. No extra screens. No account. No friction.

The second integration worth doing is offline conversion tracking. If you're using Square Appointments, you can set up a Zapier connection that sends booking data back to Meta when an appointment is marked as completed and paid. This does two things: it tells Meta which ads actually generated revenue (not just clicks), and it feeds that data into Meta's algorithm so it can find more people like your best customers.

I had a salon in San Diego do this. She was spending $800/month on Meta Ads and attributing about $2,400 in monthly bookings to them. After connecting Square Appointments to Meta via the Conversions API, the attributed revenue jumped to $4,100. The actual bookings hadn't changed — she was just finally tracking all of them. She immediately increased her budget to $1,200/month because she could see the real return.

If you're not sending offline conversion data back to Meta, you're flying blind. And you're probably under-valuing your ads by 40–60%.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Meta Ads as a new salon?**

Start with $200–300 per week. That's enough to generate meaningful data without risking your rent. Run for three weeks. If you can't get at least 5–8 bookings from that $600–900, don't increase the budget — fix the creative, targeting, or offer first. More money won't fix a broken ad.

**Q: Can't I just boost posts on my salon's Facebook page? Isn't that the same thing?**

No. Boosting a post is Facebook's way of taking your money with minimal setup and minimal control. You can't target specific audiences, set a conversion goal, run A/B tests, or retarget people who engaged. Running ads through Meta Ads Manager gives you full control. The boost button is for people who don't want to learn how advertising works. You're a business owner. Learn how it works.

**Q: Should I target women only, or include men?**

Include men. I've seen salons in Dallas and Seattle run ads targeting women only and miss 20–30% of their potential client base. Men get haircuts. Men get beard trims. Men get color services. The assumption that only women book salon appointments is outdated. Set your gender targeting to "All" and let the creative do the filtering. A photo of a men's fade will attract men. A photo of highlights will attract women. No need to restrict your audience.

**Q: How do I know if my ads are actually bringing people through the door?**

Three ways. First, install the Meta pixel and set up the "Purchase" or "Lead" conversion event when someone books. Second, use call tracking — assign a unique phone number to your ad campaign so calls from ads are tracked separately. Third, start asking every new client how they found you. That last one is low-tech but catches people who clicked an ad, left, Googled your salon later, and called. If 3–5 clients per week say "I saw your ad on Instagram," your ads are working.

**Q: My salon is in a small town. Will Meta Ads work for me?**

Depends on your town's population. If you're in a town of 5,000 people, Meta Ads might struggle because the audience is too small to optimize properly. You'd be better off with a Google Local Services ad or a yard sign. If you're in a town of 20,000 or more, Meta Ads can work — but keep your radius tight (5–8 miles max) and your daily budget low ($15–20). One salon owner in Bozeman, Montana, runs ads to a 6-mile radius at $15/day and books 6–8 appointments per week from it. Small towns work if you're patient.

**Q: What's the best offer to put in my Meta ad for a salon?**

A specific, time-limited offer that requires booking. Not "10% off" — that's vague. Try "New clients: $15 off your first cut and style. Must mention this ad when booking." Or "Free consultation and scalp treatment with any color service booked this week." The offer should have a clear action and a clear deadline. Don't run an evergreen offer. Run offers for the week or the month. Scarcity works for a reason.

**Q: Do I need a professional photographer for ad images?**

No. I've tested this multiple times. In Austin, a salon's professionally shot photos (cost: $600/session) performed identically to an iPhone photo of a stylist mid-blowout holding a comb. In some cases, the iPhone photo performed better because it looked authentic. Use good lighting — natural window light is best — and make sure the photo is in focus. That's it. Your own phone is fine.
