## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting "Everyone in Town" (The Austin, TX Disaster)

I worked with a salon owner in Austin last year who was furious that her Meta ads were "useless." She'd spent about $1,200 over three months and gotten maybe two appointments she could directly attribute to the ads. When I looked at her ad account, I understood why immediately.

She had set her targeting to "women aged 18–65 within 30 miles." That's roughly 600,000 people. She was showing an ad for a $450 balayage service to college students, retirees, and everyone in between. Her click-through rate was 0.3%. Her cost per click: $2.80. She was paying top dollar to show her ad to people who would never book a $450 service.

**The fix:** We narrowed her audience to women aged 25–50 within 8 miles, with interests in "hair color," "balayage," "salon," and "beauty." We also uploaded her existing client list from Booksy and built a lookalike audience from it. Then we split her budget: 70% to the lookalike, 30% to the interest-based targeting.

**The outcome:** Her cost per click dropped to $0.74. Her cost per booking went from roughly $200 down to $38. In the next 60 days, she generated $3,800 in revenue from a $600 ad spend. The difference wasn't luck. It was not showing a Ferrari ad to people looking for a bus pass.

### Mistake #2: Using Bad Photos (The Nashville Cautionary Tale)

A men's grooming lounge in Nashville called me after running ads for six weeks with zero bookings. Their ads had great copy — solid offers, clear call-to-action. The problem was the creative. They had used a stock photo of a model with perfectly styled hair that looked nothing like their actual clients. The photo was also dark, poorly lit, and looked like it was shot in a basement.

The owner told me, "But we don't have good photos of our work." So I asked him to take out his phone, book three clients for the next morning, and take photos in natural light by the window. Before and after shots. Client smiling. Clean background. He did.

**The fix:** We replaced the stock photo with real client photos. We also added a 15-second video of the actual barbershop — people talking, clippers buzzing, the front door opening. The video cost zero dollars to make.

**The outcome:** The same ad with real photos got a 4.2% click-through rate. The stock photo version had been at 0.8%. The video ad outperformed both at 6.1%. In the first week, they booked 12 new clients. The owner told me later that he had wasted about $800 on the original campaign. The only thing that changed was the photos.

### Mistake #3: Running the Same Ad for Six Months (The Denver Blunder)

A salon in Denver was proud that they'd had the same ad running "successfully" since January. It was September when I looked at it. The ad had a 2.1% CTR in January. By August, it was 0.4%. Their cost per result had tripled.

This is called ad fatigue. Meta's algorithm had shown that ad to every person in the Denver metro who might possibly care about haircuts. Twice. Possibly three times. The audience was exhausted. The salon owner didn't want to change it because "it was working before."

**The fix:** We archived the old ad and created three new ones:
- One featuring a seasonal service (fall hair color trends)
- One featuring a new stylist with a short introduction video
- One with a limited-time offer (15% off for new clients, expires in 10 days)

We set a frequency cap of three impressions per person per week. We also scheduled the ad set to refresh creative every 21 days.

**The outcome:** CTR jumped back to 2.8%. Cost per booking dropped 45%. They booked 22 new clients in the first month of the new campaign. Total cost for the month: $450. Revenue from those new clients: approximately $4,400 in first-visit services alone. Repeat visits? That's where the real money is.

## When to Use Google Ads Instead of Meta Ads

I run into a lot of salon owners who think Meta is the only option. It's not. There are times when Google Ads is a better bet, and knowing the difference saves you money.

Think of it this way: Meta is for people who don't know they need a haircut yet. Google is for people who are actively searching "best salon near me" or "balayage Chicago" right this second. Those are two very different mindsets.

**When Google wins:** A salon in Portland was spending $800/month on Meta ads and getting decent results — about 10–12 new clients per month at roughly $70 per booking. The problem was that most of those clients were browsing, not buying. They'd see the ad, maybe click, maybe save the post, and then forget.

We added a Google Search campaign targeting phrases like "hair salon Portland OR," "women's haircut Portland," "color specialist Portland." The cost per click on Google was higher — about $4.50 — but the conversion rate was 8%. Compare that to the Meta campaign that had a 1.8% conversion rate from click to booking.

**The numbers:** Google cost her $540 that month. It generated 14 bookings. That's about $38 per booking — nearly half the cost of her Meta campaigns. But here's the important part: the Google bookings were higher intent. Those clients were ready to book. They showed up. They spent more on average ($185 vs $135) because they came in for a specific service they'd searched for.

**The reality:** Most small business owners don't need both. If you have $1,000/month total, I'd put 60% into Google and 40% into Meta. If you're a new salon with zero reputation, put 80% into Google because people need to find you when they search. If you're an established salon trying to fill slow Tuesday afternoons, put 80% into Meta with a limited-time offer.

**Free tool you should use:** Google's Keyword Planner is free. Type in your city and "hair salon" and see what people are actually searching for. You'll be surprised. In Chicago, "affordable haircut near me" gets 10x the volume of "luxury salon Chicago." If you charge $35 for a cut, that's your audience. If you charge $120, target "precision haircut" or "master stylist."

## How to Track Results Without Losing Your Mind

Here's something most guides skip: how to actually know if your ads are working. I've seen salon owners who think their ads are failing because they track the wrong numbers.

A salon in Phoenix told me their Meta ads were "a waste of money" because they only got 50 clicks in a week. I asked: how many of those clicks turned into bookings? She didn't know. She was looking at vanity metrics.

**What to actually track:**

1. **Cost per booking (CPB):** This is the only number that matters. If you spend $500 and get 10 bookings, that's $50 per booking. If those clients spend an average of $150 per visit, you're looking at $1,500 in revenue from $500 in ad spend. That's a 3x return. Not bad.

2. **Return on ad spend (ROAS):** Total revenue from ads ÷ total ad spend. A 3x ROAS is decent. 5x is good. 8x+ is fantastic. But be honest about attribution. If someone saw your ad, didn't click, then Googled you a week later and booked, most ad platforms won't count that. It's still a result of your ad.

3. **New vs. returning clients:** If your ads are only bringing in people who would have booked anyway, you're wasting money. Use a booking tool like Booksy or Square Appointments that can tag new clients. If 70%+ of your ad-driven bookings are new clients, the ads are working.

**The tracking setup that actually works:**

- Use Meta's pixel (it's free, install it on your booking page)
- Use UTM parameters on all your ad links (Google's Campaign URL Builder is free)
- In your booking software, ask every new client: "How did you hear about us?" Train your front desk staff to ask this. It's the simplest tracking method that never lies.

**A real example:** A salon in Atlanta ran a $300 Meta campaign. They tracked 18 new clients from it. Those clients spent $2,700 in their first visit. The salon owner thought that was the end of it. But 11 of those 18 clients came back within 60 days, spending another $2,100. Total revenue from a $300 ad spend: $4,800. That's a 16x ROAS. But only if you track the lifetime value, not just the first visit.

**The mistake I see most often:** Salon owners run ads for two weeks, see no immediate bookings, and turn them off. That's like planting a seed and digging it up to check if it's growing. Give it 30 days minimum. And track everything.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I only have $300/month for ads. Is that enough to even bother?**

Yes, but you have to be smart about it. With $300, you can't afford to target everyone in your city. Narrow your radius to 3–5 miles. Use a specific offer ("$20 off your first cut" not "visit our salon"). And run the ad only during your slowest days — Tuesday through Thursday, for example. One salon in Cleveland did exactly this and generated $1,400 in revenue from a $300 campaign in the first month. It's not about the budget size. It's about how precisely you aim.

**Q: How do I compete with salons that have way bigger ad budgets?**

You don't compete on budget. You compete on specificity. A big salon with a $5,000/month budget is probably targeting "everyone." You can target "women aged 30–50 within 5 miles who follow [local influencer] and have shown interest in curly hair techniques." That's a niche they're ignoring. Also, use Google Ads for high-intent searches. A smaller budget goes further when someone is already looking for what you offer. And never underestimate the power of before-and-after photos. Big budgets can't buy authenticity.

**Q: Should I use a promotional offer in my ads, or will that attract bargain hunters?**

Use an offer, but make it strategic. If you advertise "$10 off any service," you'll get price shoppers who never return. If you advertise "Free consultation + $25 off your first color service," you attract people who are already considering color. That's a higher-value client. I've seen salons test both approaches. The "free consultation" offer produced clients with a 45% return rate. The "$10 off" offer produced a 12% return rate. Same ad spend. Different lifetime value.

**Q: What's the first thing I should do if my ads aren't working?**

Pause them. Don't throw more money at a broken campaign. Then look at three things: your audience (too broad?), your creative (bad photos?), and your offer (is it compelling?). Change one thing at a time and test for a week. A salon in Boston was getting zero results. I looked at their ad — they were running a generic "book now" ad with no offer. We added "First-time clients: 15% off any service." Same audience. Same creative. Bookings started within 24 hours. The offer was the missing piece.

**Q: How often should I change my ads?**

Every 3–4 weeks, or sooner if you see CTR drop below 1%. Meta punishes stale ads. But don't change everything at once. Keep the winning element (maybe your headline is working) and change the photo or the offer. A Denver salon ran the same ad for 18 days before CTR dropped from 2.1% to 0.8%. They swapped the photo but kept the copy. CTR went back to 1.9%. You don't need to reinvent the wheel. Just rotate the tire.

**Q: Should I run ads on Instagram, Facebook, or both?**

Both, but separate the audiences. Facebook users tend to be older (30–55) and more likely to click a link and book. Instagram users are younger (18–35) and more likely to engage but less likely to book immediately. Use Instagram for brand awareness and retargeting. Use Facebook for direct response and offers. A salon in San Diego ran the same ad on both platforms. Facebook had a 3.2% conversion rate. Instagram had a 1.1% conversion rate. But Instagram generated 4x more saves and shares. Both have a place — just for different jobs.
