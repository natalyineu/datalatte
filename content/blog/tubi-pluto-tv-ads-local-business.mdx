While Netflix, Hulu, and Disney+ dominate the streaming conversation, the fastest-growing segment of streaming in 2026 is FAST — **Free Ad-Supported Streaming Television**.

Tubi (owned by Fox) and Pluto TV (owned by Paramount) are the two largest FAST platforms in the US. Combined, they reach over **100 million monthly users** who watch free streaming content funded entirely by advertising.

The opportunity: these platforms have significant viewership, surprisingly good targeting capabilities, and CPMs that are 40–60% lower than Netflix or Hulu — making them one of the most accessible entry points into TV advertising for local businesses.

## What FAST Platforms Actually Are

FAST stands for Free Ad-Supported Streaming Television. Unlike Netflix or Disney+ (subscription, limited or no ads) or Hulu (mixed model), FAST platforms are entirely free to users — exactly like traditional broadcast TV, but delivered over the internet.

**Tubi** is the largest FAST platform by US users. It offers a massive library of movies and TV shows — older content, cult classics, reality shows, documentaries — all free. Users sign up with an email, no credit card required. Tubi has over **80 million monthly active users** in the US.

**Pluto TV** takes a slightly different approach: it offers both on-demand content and live "channels" that run programming like traditional cable (you tune in and watch whatever is currently playing). Pluto TV reaches **35+ million monthly active users** globally, with strong US penetration.

Both platforms serve ads to all users — there's no ad-free tier. This means 100% of their viewership sees advertising, unlike subscription platforms where ad exposure depends on subscriber tier.

## Who Watches FAST (And Why It Matters for Local Advertisers)

FAST's audience skews differently from premium streaming:

**Age:** Slightly older — FAST's "free" model appeals to cost-conscious consumers and cord-cutters who have dropped cable but won't pay for streaming subscriptions. Strong 35–65 demographic.

**Income:** Mixed, but with meaningful representation of middle-income households that premium subscription platforms under-index on.

**Geography:** Strong in rural and suburban markets — areas where cable was previously the primary TV source and where streaming subscription penetration is lower.

**Viewing behaviour:** FAST viewers often watch for longer continuous sessions than subscription platforms — they're leaning back into a "what's on TV" passive viewing mode rather than actively selecting content.

For local businesses targeting value-conscious, middle-income households — home services, insurance, QSR restaurants, auto services, regional retailers — FAST's demographic is genuinely valuable and often under-priced relative to reach.

<Callout type="tip">
FAST platforms are particularly strong in rural and smaller US markets where premium streaming penetration is lower. If your local business serves a suburban or rural area, Tubi and Pluto TV likely reach a higher proportion of your target customers than their national user numbers suggest.
</Callout>

## How to Buy FAST Advertising

FAST advertising is primarily purchased programmatically — through DSPs — rather than direct self-serve platforms.

**Programmatic access via DSPs:**
- The Trade Desk, DV360, Amazon DSP, and Basis all carry Tubi and Pluto TV inventory
- You can target by geography, demographics, content genre, and device type
- Typical minimum spend through programmatic: $1,000–2,000 to generate meaningful data

**Tubi's direct advertising:**
Tubi offers a direct advertising programme for larger brands. For most small businesses, programmatic is the more accessible route.

**Pluto TV's direct channels:**
Pluto TV's "live channel" model creates an interesting opportunity: brands can sometimes sponsor or partner with specific Pluto TV channels relevant to their audience. A pet product brand sponsoring Pluto's animal content channel is a good example — though this is managed service territory.

**Third-party CTV platforms:**
Platforms like Simpli.fi, StackAdapt, and AdRoll offer FAST inventory (including Tubi and Pluto) with lower minimums and simpler self-serve interfaces designed for small businesses. Minimums can be as low as **$500–1,000** per campaign.

FAST's CPMs are dramatically lower than premium streaming — typically **$8–18** compared to $25–50 for Hulu or Peacock. This makes FAST one of the most efficient ways to buy TV impressions.

The trade-off: content quality and viewer engagement are lower on average than premium platforms. A viewer watching a 2005 B-movie on Tubi is in a different mindset than someone watching an HBO prestige drama. For awareness and reach, FAST is highly efficient. For premium brand positioning, the content context matters.

For most local businesses focused on reach over prestige, the efficiency advantage wins.

## FAST Advertising Strategy for Local Businesses

**Layer FAST on top of premium CTV, don't replace it.** Use Hulu or Roku for quality brand-building impressions. Add Tubi and Pluto TV to extend reach at lower cost. This combination reaches premium streaming households *and* FAST households — a much larger total market than either alone.

**Genre targeting on Pluto TV is underutilised.** Pluto TV's channel structure lets you buy adjacency to specific content types. A pet groomer buying Pluto TV's animal and nature channels is reaching an audience self-selecting for pet content. A home services company buying home improvement channels gets relevant context at FAST prices.

**Use FAST for frequency building.** If your Google and Meta campaigns are building awareness, FAST can cost-effectively drive additional exposures to the same households — reinforcing your message across multiple screens.

<Callout type="warning">
Don't assume FAST viewers are less valuable than premium streaming viewers. The income and age demographics are different, but for many local businesses — particularly home services, insurance, regional retail, and QSR — FAST audiences are highly relevant. Evaluate performance by your specific audience profile, not platform prestige.
</Callout>

## Creative Requirements for FAST

The technical requirements are similar to other CTV platforms:
- Video file: MP4 or MOV
- Resolution: 1920×1080 minimum
- Length: 15 or 30 seconds
- Audio: Stereo, 48kHz
- No text-only static frames — motion throughout

One FAST-specific consideration: because FAST viewers are often in long passive viewing sessions, 30-second ads can work better than 15-second formats. The viewer is less task-oriented and more receptive to a longer brand story.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is FAST advertising right for a single-location local business?

Yes — if your budget allows. The low CPMs mean you can reach meaningful local audiences with budgets of $1,000–2,000/month. Focus geographic targeting on your immediate service area (DMA or zip code cluster). FAST's reach in suburban and rural markets makes it particularly relevant for businesses serving those areas.

### What's the difference between Tubi and Pluto TV for advertisers?

Both are FAST platforms with broadly similar demographics. Tubi is on-demand focused (choose what to watch) with a slightly younger skew. Pluto TV's live channel model creates more passive, longer viewing sessions. For most local advertisers, running on both through programmatic buys makes sense — you're buying the audience, not the specific platform.

### Can I track whether FAST ads lead to store visits?

Physical store visit attribution for FAST advertising requires a third-party location intelligence provider (like Foursquare or NinthDecimal) integrated with your DSP. This is generally available at minimum spend levels of $5,000+ per campaign. For smaller campaigns, use website traffic and brand search as proxies.

### How does FAST compare to linear local TV advertising?

Traditional local TV ad spots in a mid-sized market typically run $500–3,000 per spot with minimum frequency commitments. FAST offers more granular audience targeting, better attribution, and lower CPMs — but without the massive simultaneous reach of a popular live news or sports broadcast. For audience-targeted campaigns, FAST is superior. For broad local market saturation (important for political campaigns or high-urgency local events), linear local TV still has advantages.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting "Everyone in the Metro Area" Instead of Your Actual Service Zone

A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas—let's call him Mike—came to me after spending $1,500 on Tubi ads over two months. His results: three website visits and zero in-store traffic that he could trace to the ads.

The problem? He targeted "Austin metro area" because that's what the platform's default geo-targeting suggested. Austin metro covers over 800 square miles. A person 40 minutes away in Round Rock is not driving to your coffee shop on South Congress for a pour-over, even if your ad is charming.

What I actually did: I pulled his customer list from his Square POS system, mapped the zip codes where 80% of his transactions happened, and set his Tubi and Pluto TV campaigns to target only those specific zip codes plus a 3-mile radius around his shop. I also added a time-of-day restriction: 7–10 AM (morning commute) and 12–2 PM (lunch break), when people are making decisions about where to grab coffee.

The outcome: His Tubi campaign went from $750/month to $500/month (we cut the waste). His attributed in-store visits climbed to 47 in month one. At an average ticket of $6.50, that's roughly $3,800 in monthly revenue from $500 in ad spend. His actual lift was higher than the attribution showed—people don't always say "I saw your ad" when they walk in.

### Mistake #2: Running a Generic National-Style Commercial for a Local Business

A pet groomer in Nashville spent $1,200 on Pluto TV ads with a polished 30-second spot that showed fluffy dogs running through a field to upbeat music. It looked professional. It got 0 calls. Zero.

The problem: nothing in that ad told a Nashville viewer why they should drive to her specific shop in East Nashville rather than the PetSmart down the street. The ad could have run for any groomer in any city.

The fix was uncomfortable for her—she had to film herself. I had her record a 15-second vertical video on her iPhone: standing in her actual shop, holding a freshly groomed schnauzer, and saying "I'm Sarah, I've been grooming dogs in East Nashville for eight years. We take walk-ins Tuesdays and Thursdays. Bring your muddy mutt in, and mention this ad—first groom is 20% off." We slapped a simple title card at the end with her address and phone number.

We launched that ad on Pluto TV targeting Davidson County and the three adjacent counties. Within three weeks, she'd booked 22 appointments she could directly attribute to the ad (people mentioned it when they called). At an average groom price of $65, that's $1,430 in revenue on $400 in ad spend. More importantly, repeat business from those 22 clients has been over 60% in the six months since.

### Mistake #3: Not Having Any Way to Track Offline Conversions

A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon, told me she "knew" her Tubi ads were working because she was busier. But she couldn't prove it to herself or justify the $600/month spend to her bookkeeper husband.

She had no promo code, no phone number tracking, no "mention this ad" system. She just ran the ad and hoped.

The fix was dead simple: I set up a Google Voice number with Portland's area code (503) that forwarded to her salon's main line, and she added "Call 503-XXX-XXXX for same-day appointments" as the ad's call-to-action. She also started asking every new client how they found her and trained her receptionist to log the answer in Booksy.

In month one of using the tracked number, she got 14 calls directly from the ad. Three resulted in same-day bookings. Month two: 22 calls, 7 bookings. The $600/month spend was generating about $1,100 in direct bookings—but the real story was that the ad was also driving walk-ins who didn't call first, which we couldn't perfectly track. She kept the campaign running because even the conservative data showed positive ROI.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Frequency Caps (And Annoying Your Potential Customers)

A fitness studio owner in Denver ran Pluto TV ads for six weeks with default settings. By week four, a regular client told him, "I've seen your squat ad seventeen times. I get it. You exist."

The problem: Pluto TV's default frequency settings will happily serve your ad to the same user every commercial break if you don't set a cap. That's not brand awareness—that's brand annoyance.

The fix: We set a frequency cap of three impressions per user per week. Then we rotated two different creative versions—one focused on morning classes, one on evening classes. This kept the ad fresh and reduced the odds of anyone feeling stalked by a squat rack.

Within two weeks of the change, the studio's website traffic from Pluto TV increased 44%, and they had 11 new memberships that month (up from 5 the month before) at $149/month each. The ad was still reaching people—it just wasn't burning them out.

## How to Set Up Your First Tubi or Pluto TV Campaign (The Steps Most Guides Skip)

Most articles will tell you to "go to the ad platform and create a campaign." That's like telling someone to "just bake a cake" without mentioning you need an oven, ingredients, and a recipe. Here's the actual process, including the parts that agencies normally don't share.

### Step 1: Know What You're Actually Buying

Tubi and Pluto TV both sell their inventory through programmatic ad platforms. You don't buy from Tubi directly unless you're spending six figures. Small businesses buy through demand-side platforms (DSPs) like The Trade Desk, Amazon Ads, or even some self-serve platforms that have sprung up specifically for local businesses.

The platforms that actually work for small budgets ($500–$3,000/month): Tubi has a self-serve beta program for local advertisers, but it's invite-only as of this writing. Pluto TV inventory is available through Amazon Ads' self-serve platform, which is where most of my local clients start. You can set a $500/month budget, target by zip code, and turn it on within an hour.

### Step 2: Build a 15-Second Spot, Not a 30-Second One

I see small businesses blow their whole budget on 30-second ads that cost twice as much to produce and don't perform twice as well. On FAST platforms, attention span is even shorter than on YouTube because people are "watching TV" while scrolling their phones.

The 15-second format works better for three reasons: it's cheaper to produce (one clear message, no "story arc" needed), it costs less per impression (CPMs are based on ad length), and viewers are more likely to watch the whole thing.

I tested this with a family-owned pizza place in Chicago: we ran a 30-second version and a 15-second version at the same time on Pluto TV, targeting the same zip codes. The 15-second version had a 37% higher completion rate and a 22% lower cost-per-visit to their website. The shorter ad outperformed on every metric.

### Step 3: Use a Specific Call-to-Action, Not "Visit Our Website"

Your CTA needs to be trackable and urgent. "Visit our website" is not a CTA—it's a wish. "Call 555-0123 and mention this ad for a free slice with your first order" is a CTA. You now have a way to measure whether the ad worked.

I helped a dentist in Philadelphia set up a Pluto TV campaign with the CTA "Text 'DRYMOUTH' to 555-0123 for a free whitening kit." They got 43 texts in month one. Twenty-three of those people booked an appointment. At an average revenue of $400 per new patient, that's $9,200 in attributed revenue on a $750 ad spend. The text-in method worked better than a phone number because people are less anxious about texting than calling a dentist.

## How to Measure Whether Tubi and Pluto TV Ads Are Actually Working

The uncomfortable truth: TV advertising—even digital TV—is harder to measure than Facebook ads. You cannot get a perfect "click-to-sale" attribution because most people don't watch TV on the same device they order from. Here's what I actually use with clients.

### The Three-Bucket Attribution Method

**Bucket 1: Direct response.** Things you can track with a promo code, unique phone number, or text keyword. This is your "I know this ad caused this sale" data. Most of my clients see 2–4x ROAS from direct response alone within 60 days.

**Bucket 2: Lift measurement.** Compare your sales data from the two months before you started running ads to the two months after. If you're a pet groomer doing $8,000/month in revenue and you're now doing $9,500/month with no other changes, the ad is likely responsible for part of that lift. This isn't perfect—weather, holidays, and local events matter—but it's directional.

**Bucket 3: Brand search lift.** Track how many people search for your business name on Google. If you weren't getting any branded search traffic and now you're getting 50 searches per week, that's awareness working. Google Ads gives you this data for free under "Search terms."

I had a chiropractor in Denver who ran Pluto TV ads for six weeks. His direct response (people who mentioned the ad) showed $1,200 in new patient revenue on $600 spend. His monthly revenue lift comparing pre- and post-campaign was $3,800. His branded search volume went from 12 searches/month to 89. All three buckets told the same story: the ads were working, just at different levels of precision.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much do I actually need to spend to see results?**
I've run successful campaigns for as little as $500/month in a single zip code. For a local business with a tight geographic radius (3–5 miles), $500–$1,000/month is enough to test. You'll see data within two weeks on whether it's worth scaling. Anything under $300/month is too thin—the platforms need minimum volume to optimize your targeting.

**Q: Can I target people in my specific neighborhood?**
Yes, but the minimum geo-targeting size varies by platform. Pluto TV through Amazon Ads lets you target by zip code, which is about as granular as most local businesses need. Tubi's targeting through most DSPs allows down to a 1-mile radius. The key is to start tight and expand if performance is good—don't start wide and hope to narrow down later.

**Q: How is this different from running video ads on Facebook or YouTube?**
Facebook and YouTube are pull channels—people can skip your ad, block it, or scroll past it. Tubi and Pluto TV are push channels—the ad plays during a commercial break and viewers have to actively change the channel to avoid it. Completion rates on FAST platforms are 85–95% for 15-second ads. On Facebook, you're lucky to get 30% view-through. The downside is less granular targeting than Facebook. The upside is a captive audience that can't easily ignore you.

**Q: Do I need to make a professional commercial?**
No. I've seen iPhone-shot ads outperform produced spots for local businesses because they feel authentic. Film yourself or an employee in your actual space, with natural lighting, and a clear offer. Polish matters less than specificity. A slightly blurry video of a florist saying "I'm Maria, and every bouquet at Bloom on Broadway comes with a handwritten note" will outperform a slick ad with generic flower footage.

**Q: How do I know if my audience is even on Tubi or Pluto TV?**
Tubi's audience skews slightly older (25–54) than Pluto TV (18–49), but both platforms cover a broad demographic. Tubi has strong audiences in Hispanic and African American households. Pluto TV has good reach with cord-cutters aged 35–55 who want a TV-like experience without paying for cable. If your customers watch any streaming TV at all, they're likely on at least one of these platforms. The test budget for a local business in 2026 is around $1,000—you'll know within 30 days whether your specific audience is there.

**Q: What happens if my ad runs during a movie nobody watches?**
The platforms don't charge you by show or movie—they charge per thousand impressions. If your ad runs during low-traffic content, you get fewer impressions overall. The platforms optimize toward inventory that delivers views within your targeting parameters. Setting a modest budget and letting the algorithm run for 7–10 days usually smooths this out. If you're still getting impressions at 3 AM for a business that closes at 6 PM, add a time-of-day restriction.
