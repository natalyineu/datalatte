## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Posting Whenever You “Feel Like It”

A coffee shop in Portland, Oregon, hired a friend-of-a-friend to “do social media.” She posted three times one week, then nothing for two weeks, then a random meme about Mondays. The owner told me he was “tired of wasting money on Instagram” and asked me to look at their account. What I saw was a ghost town — a feed with no rhythm, no anticipation, no reason to follow.

**What went wrong:** They had zero content calendar. No themes for specific days. No advance planning around seasonal demand (cold brew in July, gingerbread lattes in December). Customers didn’t know when to expect a post, so they stopped looking.

**The fix:** I built a simple 30-minute-per-week calendar. Tuesday posts featured a drink spotlight with a video of the barista making it. Thursday posts ran a limited-time offer (buy one, get one 50% off any pastry before 10 a.m.). Saturday posts showcased customer photos tagged at the shop. Everything was scheduled on Sunday using Meta’s free scheduling tool.

**The outcome:** Within 60 days, their Reel views went from 200 average to 4,100. In-store check-ins from Instagram increased 23%. That week of ghost-town posting? It had cost them roughly $800 in missed foot traffic per month — they just hadn’t noticed because nobody tracks what *didn’t* happen.

### Mistake #2: Using the Same Offer in Every Month

A hair salon in Austin, Texas, ran “$20 off your first visit” as their only promotion for eight straight months. They put it on Google Ads, Facebook, and a sign in the window. When I asked the owner why, she said, “It works, so why change it?”

**What went wrong:** The offer had a short half-life. The first month it worked — 14 new clients. By month four, the same ad got 1 click per $20 spent. Worse, the salon was training customers to only book during a discount. No one wanted to pay full price because the owner had accidentally created a permanent sale culture.

**The fix:** We built a rotating offer calendar instead. January: New Year haircut package (wash, cut, style + scalp treatment, $85 value for $65). March: “Spring refresh” with a free blow-dry on any color service. June: Referral bonus — bring a friend, you both get 20% off. October: Halloween hair sparkle (temporary color application for $15 add-on). Every offer ran for exactly three weeks, then disappeared.

**The outcome:** New client acquisition cost dropped from $48 to $22 per person over six months. Average ticket price increased by $18 because clients stopped waiting for a generic discount — they booked specific packages they actually wanted. The owner told me she made an extra $1,900 in May alone, and she stopped feeling like she was running a clearance sale.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Google Business Profile Updates

A pet groomer in Denver, Colorado, had a solid website, decent Yelp reviews, and a loyal customer base. But she couldn’t figure out why new clients kept telling her, “I didn’t know you offered nail grinds” or “Do you do cats? Your website doesn’t say.” She had a well-maintained Google Business Profile with accurate hours, but she hadn’t posted anything on it in fourteen months.

**What went wrong:** Google’s algorithm favors businesses that post fresh content — photos, updates, offers, service changes. By leaving her profile dormant, she was invisible in the Local Pack (the top three results that appear before the map). A competitor three blocks away, who posted twice a week on their profile, was getting all the new search traffic even though her shop had better reviews and more five-star ratings.

**The fix:** We set up a rotation: Monday post (photo of a dog before/after grooming), Wednesday post (quick tip: “why we use stainless steel combs”), Friday post (available same-day appointment slot — limited to one per day to create urgency). Each post took three minutes. We scheduled them using the Google Business Profile mobile app.

**The outcome:** In 90 days, her profile appeared in the Local Pack for “pet grooming Denver” seven out of ten searches (up from two out of ten). The phone rang from new clients — she went from averaging 8 new client calls per week to 22. That single change added an estimated $3,800 in monthly revenue, with zero ad spend. The competitor? Still posting twice a week, but she had them on pace.

## How To Build a Calendar You’ll Actually Use (Not a Spreadsheet You’ll Abandon by February)

Most business owners I meet start January with a beautiful Google Sheet. Four columns: date, channel, offer, status. By mid-March, half the cells are blank. By June, the sheet is in your Google Drive trash.

Here’s the problem: you’re building a *planning* document, not an *execution* system. A marketing calendar isn’t useful if it requires you to log in, check dates, cross-reference offers, and then write a post from scratch. That’s homework. You already have a business to run.

Instead, build your calendar around *templates* and *recurring themes*. Here’s the three-piece system I’ve seen work across 30+ local businesses in Chicago, Nashville, and Minneapolis:

**Step 1: Lock in your weekly themes.** You need exactly four. Example for a fitness studio: Monday (success story — client transformation photo + quote), Wednesday (tip — “how to avoid injury during deadlifts”), Friday (offer — “bring a friend for free this weekend”), Saturday (community — photo from the morning class). That’s it. Every week, same structure. You just swap the asset.

**Step 2: Create 90 days of offers upfront.** Don’t wait until the week before Valentine’s Day to think of a promotion. Sit down once, write out one offer per month tied to something real: January (New Year special), February (Valentine’s couples session), March (spring cleaning — new membership enrollment), April (tax refund — $150 off annual), May (Mother’s Day gift card bonus), June (summer kickoff — outdoor class series). Each offer gets one line in your calendar. Done.

**Step 3: Batch content on Sunday night.** You need 45 minutes. Open your phone, film three Reels (each under 30 seconds), take four photos, write the captions (use the same proven hook structure every time: problem → solution → offer), schedule everything in Meta Business Suite. Done for the week.

I had a barber shop in Nashville follow this system. The owner spent one hour every Sunday. Within 6 weeks, his Instagram Reels averaged 8,000 views per post. He booked out for three weeks straight. His calendar wasn’t a spreadsheet — it was a checklist.

## The One Metric That Matters More Than Engagement

Local business owners love tracking likes, comments, and saves. I get it — they feel good. But I’ve seen a coffee shop with 10,000 Instagram followers struggle to break even on a $200 Facebook ad, while a laundromat in Chicago with 1,200 followers grew 18% month over month.

The difference? The laundromat tracked something specific: *in-store redemptions of one specific offer per week.*

Here’s the uncomfortable truth: engagement metrics are vanity numbers. A like doesn’t pay your rent. A comment doesn’t book an appointment. The only thing that matters is whether someone walks through your door (or calls, or clicks to book) and says the phrase you put in your post.

If you run a “10% off any haircut” offer on Instagram, you need to ask every single person who redeems it: “How did you hear about us?” Track those answers in a spreadsheet. After 30 responses, you’ll know whether Instagram is actually driving business or just generating digital noise.

I worked with a pizza place in Denver that had 400 Instagram followers but ran a weekly email campaign to 900 subscribers. They tracked offers by using unique coupon codes — “PIZZA10” on Instagram, “EMAIL10” in their newsletter. Email consistently drove 7x more redemptions per dollar spent. If they had only looked at Instagram analytics, they would have doubled down on the wrong channel. Instead, they cut Instagram posting to twice a week and invested in a Mailchimp upgrade.

Track the offer code. Ask the question. Ignore everything else until you have 100 data points.

## Seasonality: The Months Most Local Businesses Forget to Plan For

You already know to market in December (holiday gift cards) and January (New Year resolutions). But you’re probably ignoring three hidden opportunity windows:

**February 1–15 (post-holiday slump).** December and January are exhausting for most business owners. You ran promotions, you dealt with crowds, you’re tired. Then February hits and everything goes quiet. Most businesses run zero marketing in February — which means any ad or email you send faces drastically less competition. A dog groomer in Chicago ran a simple “February pick-me-up” — 15% off any groom, no expiration. She sent one email to her list of 600 and got 24 bookings in two days. Cost: $0 in ad spend. Revenue: $1,080.

**Late August (back-to-school for adults).** Everyone talks about back-to-school for kids. Nobody talks about how August is the month parents realize they have more free time during the day. A hair salon in Portland ran a “Mom’s morning off” special — early appointments with a complimentary coffee. They promoted it only to their email list. They booked 19 appointments in one week.

**Third week of October (slowdown before November).** October is surprisingly strong for local service businesses if you don’t fixate on Halloween. People are settled into fall routines. They’re not yet thinking about holiday spending. A personal trainer in Nashville ran a “Get strong before holiday travel” 4-week mini program starting October 15. He sold 12 spots at $150 each. Total effort: three posts on Instagram, one email to his list, and a 30-second video. Net profit: $1,800.

The pattern is simple: when everyone else is quiet, you show up. That’s the advantage of having a calendar — you don’t forget these windows because they’re already written down.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I have no time to plan 12 months in advance. Do I really need a full-year calendar?**

No. You need 90 days. Anything beyond that will change anyway. Sit down for two hours on a Sunday, write out offers and themes for the next three months, build your weekly template, and schedule the first four weeks of content. When you hit month two, add month four. Keep it rolling. A 12-month calendar is aspirational — a 90-day calendar is actionable.

**Q: What if I don’t have a budget for ads? Is a calendar still useful?**

Yes, but skip the paid promotion rows. Your calendar should focus on organic tactics that cost time, not money: Google Business Profile posts, Instagram Reels, email to your existing list, referral incentives, in-store signage. I’ve seen a yoga studio in Austin grow from 60 to 140 clients with zero ad spend — they just consistently emailed their list every Tuesday and ran a “bring a friend and you both get a free class” offer once per month. The calendar kept them consistent.

**Q: Should I post every day? That feels like too much.**

Posting every day is a recipe for burnout and low-quality content. Post three times per week if you can manage it. Twice per week is fine. Once per week works if that’s all you can sustain — but it needs to be the same day and time every week so customers learn to expect it. A carpet cleaner in Denver posted every Sunday at 10 a.m. for eight months straight. His audience grew slowly (from 300 to 800), but his conversion rate was 11% because his followers knew Sunday was “tip day.” Consistency beats frequency.

**Q: Do I need separate calendars for Facebook, Instagram, email, and Google?**

No. One master calendar with columns for each channel. The same weekly theme gets adapted across channels — different formats but same message. Your Monday email subject line matches your Monday Instagram caption matches your Monday Google post. It takes less time than creating four different calendars, and your audience sees a coherent brand instead of four separate voices.

**Q: What if my business has seasonal lulls that are impossible to fix?**

Most seasonal lulls aren’t impossible — they’re under-marketed. A landscaping business in Minneapolis assumed winter was a dead zone. They pivoted to selling holiday light installation and snow removal packages starting October 1. They promoted it to their existing client list via email. That winter brought in $14,000 in additional revenue. If your business truly can’t operate during certain months, use that time to market for the upcoming season — send a postcard in January offering early-bird spring bookings at 20% off. Plan for it on your calendar.

**Q: How do I know if my calendar is actually working?**

Track one number per month: total new customers. That’s it. If that number is going up, your calendar is working. If it’s flat or dropping, look at which channel drove the most new business (using offer codes or asking “how did you find us?”) and invest more time there. Don’t complicate it. I tell every client to write their monthly new customer count on a sticky note above their desk. By month three, you’ll know exactly what’s working and what isn’t.

Then add one more. By the time you’ve built momentum, you won’t need a 12-month calendar — you’ll have a rhythm that carries the business forward on its own.
