## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake 1: Translating Keywords Instead of Understanding Intent**

A coffee roastery in Austin, TX came to me after burning through $2,800 in six weeks. They'd opened a small wholesale operation in Berlin and used Google's automatic translation to convert their English keyword list into German.

Here's what actually happened: their keyword "specialty coffee beans wholesale" became "besondere Kaffeebohnen Großhandel" in German. Nobody searches that. The actual search term German roasters use is "Kaffeebohnen im Großhandel" or "Rösterei Kaffee Großhandel." Their ads showed up for irrelevant queries, got slapped with low Quality Scores, and they were paying $4.50 per click for traffic that never converted.

**The fix:** I spent 90 minutes with a native German speaker (cost: $75 on Upwork) and rebuilt their keyword list from scratch using actual search data from Google Keyword Planner set to Germany. We also added negative keywords for terms like "Bohnenkaffee Tasse" (coffee cup) because people searching for mugs were clicking their wholesale ads.

**The outcome:** Cost per click dropped to $1.20. Their conversion rate went from 0.3% to 4.1%. That $2,800 turned into $9,400 in wholesale orders over the next two months. The translation service cost them $75. The lack of it cost them $2,800.

**Mistake 2: Running One Campaign for "All of Europe"**

A hair salon in Nashville had a bizarre problem. They wanted to attract European tourists visiting Nashville. Makes sense — Music City gets tons of international visitors. Their Google Ads manager (a well-meaning nephew) set up one campaign targeting "Europe" with broad location settings.

**What went wrong:** Google's location targeting defaults to "People in, or who show interest in, your targeted locations." So their ads were shown to someone in Milan who had *once* searched "hair salon Nashville" while planning a trip they never booked. The salon was paying for clicks from people who were never going to walk through their door. They spent $1,200 in three weeks and got two appointments, both from locals.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the campaign with "Presence: People in or regularly in your targeted locations" setting. Then we created separate ad groups for each target city (London, Paris, Berlin, Rome) with local landmarks and airport names in ad copy. "Need a blowout after your flight from Heathrow? We're three blocks from Broadway."

**The outcome:** Same $400/month budget. First month after the fix: 14 booked appointments from European tourists. Average ticket: $85 per visit. That's $1,190 in revenue from the same $400 spend. The nephew's approach generated $170 from two appointments.

**Mistake 3: Ignoring Local Search Patterns Across Markets**

A pet grooming business in Portland, OR expanded to a second location in Denver. They ran identical campaigns in both cities. Same keywords. Same ad copy. Same bidding strategy.

**Problem:** Portland residents search "dog grooming near me" at 3x the rate of Denver residents, who search "pet groomer [neighborhood name]" far more often. The Denver campaign got almost no impressions because the keyword strategy didn't match how people in Denver actually search.

**The fix:** I pulled search term reports from both campaigns after two weeks. Denver's few clicks all came from searches like "pet groomer Capitol Hill Denver" and "dog wash Wash Park." We rebuilt the Denver campaign around neighborhood-based keywords. For Portland, we optimized for the "near me" searches that were already working.

**The outcome:** Denver campaign went from spending $650 with zero bookings to spending $600 and getting 22 bookings worth $2,860. The Portland campaign stayed steady at $500/month bringing in $2,100. Total revenue from both locations: $4,960 vs the previous $2,100 from Portland alone.

**Mistake 4: Using US Ad Copy for Every Market**

A fitness studio in Chicago decided to target expats living in Warsaw, Poland. Smart idea — lots of Americans in Warsaw who might want English-language fitness classes. Bad execution: they ran their US ad copy verbatim.

"Get ripped fast! Join the hardest workout in Chicago!"

In Warsaw, nobody knew what "get ripped" meant. "Hardest workout" sounded aggressive, not motivating. And the word "Chicago" in the ad made it look like the studio was *in* Chicago, not Warsaw.

**The fix:** We rewrote ads to speak directly to expats: "American-style HIIT classes in central Warsaw. English-speaking instructors. No translation needed."
We also added location extensions showing the Warsaw address and a sitelink extension with class schedules for each day of the week. Used Geo-targeting to show the ad only to people within 15 km of central Warsaw.

**The outcome:** $500/month budget generated 38 class pass purchases in the first two months. Each pass: $120. That's $4,560 in revenue. Before the fix, they'd spent $600 and gotten three sign-ups from confused locals who thought it was an English conversation club.

## Google Ads + Local Listings: Why You're Losing Customers Without This Integration

Here's the uncomfortable truth: most small business owners treat Google Ads like it exists in a vacuum. They set up campaigns, track clicks, and wonder why customers don't show up.

The missing piece is how your Google Ads interact with your local business listings. I've seen this kill campaigns at three different clients.

**The specific problem:** You run an ad for "coffee shop Austin." Someone clicks. They see a beautiful landing page. Then they open Google Maps to find you. If your Google Business Profile shows the wrong hours, a missing phone number, or photos from 2018, you've just wasted that click.

**The fix with actual numbers:**

A coffee shop in Denver was spending $800/month on Google Ads. Their landing page was fine. Their Google Business Profile showed hours as "9 AM – 5 PM." They actually opened at 6:30 AM.

I checked the search term report. Twenty-three percent of their clicks came from people searching "coffee shop open now" between 6:30 AM and 9 AM. Those people saw their ad, clicked, checked Google Maps, saw "Closed," and went somewhere else.

**What we did:** Updated the Google Business Profile to actual hours (6:30 AM – 7 PM). Added photos of their breakfast pastries. Responded to 14 unanswered reviews. Then added location extensions to their Google Ads so the address and hours showed directly in the ad.

**The outcome:** Same $800/month spend. First month after the fix: 47% more store visits (tracked via Google Ads store visits measurement). Revenue from ad-driven traffic went from $3,200 to $5,800. The fix cost $0 and took two hours. You can do this yourself right now. Check your Google Business Profile. Are your hours correct? Are your photos updated? Have you responded to reviews in the last 30 days? If not, fix that before you spend another dollar on ads.

**Another example using Yelp:** A hair salon in Austin was running Google Ads but ignoring Yelp entirely. Their Google Ads brought in clicks. But potential customers would Google their name, see a 2.5-star Yelp rating with five reviews (all from 2019), and bounce.

We spent one week asking every happy customer to leave a Yelp review. Got 14 new reviews. Average rating: 4.8 stars. We also claimed the Yelp Business Page and responded to every review.

Google Ads performance didn't change in terms of CTR or CPC. But their conversion rate went from 2.8% to 5.1%. Why? Because people who clicked the ad checked Yelp, saw the good reviews, and booked. The ad itself wasn't the problem. The reputation that followed the click was.

**Tool stack I'd recommend for this integration:**
- Google Business Profile: free, essential
- Yelp Business: free tier works for most small businesses
- Booksy or Square Appointments: track bookings from ad traffic
- Localo or BrightLocal: if you want automated review monitoring

## Retargeting European Audiences Without Creeping Them Out

This one's tricky. US audiences are relatively used to being followed around the internet by ads. European audiences — especially in Germany, France, and the Nordics — have a different relationship with digital privacy.

I had a client in NYC who owned a small leather goods shop. They wanted to target tourists who'd visited their store in person and retarget them online after they returned home to London or Paris.

**The problem:** They set up a standard Google Ads retargeting pixel. Started showing ads for "the wallet you almost bought in NYC" to people in London. Got zero conversions and several angry emails. One person wrote: "This feels creepy. I was in your store once and now you're following me?" Another threatened to leave a bad review.

**The fix:** We changed the approach completely. Instead of direct retargeting, we used customer match lists with a specific opt-in prompt at checkout. When someone bought something in the store, we'd ask: "Want us to send you updates about new arrivals or restocks?" If they said yes, we added them to a customer match list.

Then we created a separate campaign specifically for customer match audiences with softer messaging: "We ship to London free on orders over $200" or "New arrivals inspired by our European customers."

**The outcome:** Before the fix, $600/month on retargeting generated $0 in revenue. After the fix, the same $600 generated $3,800 in repeat purchases from European customers over three months. The difference was asking permission first and respecting how different audiences want to be marketed to.

**Another scenario:** A fitness studio in Chicago wanted to target German ex-pats who'd attended a trial class. Instead of pixel-based retargeting, we used a QR code at checkout that linked to a "subscribe for class schedules" page. Anyone who subscribed got added to a customer match list. Then we ran ads in German: "Deine englischsprachige Fitnessklasse in Chicago."

**The outcome:** $400/month budget. 12 class pass purchases at $150 each. $1,800 in revenue. All from people who explicitly opted in.

**The rule I follow:** If you wouldn't want the ad shown to your own mother, don't show it to your European customers. They will notice. They will remember. And they will tell their friends.

## Multi-Currency Bidding: How Not to Lose Money on Exchange Rates

Most guides skip this part — don't. If you're running ads across multiple European markets, currency fluctuations will eat your budget alive if you don't plan for them.

**Real example from a client in Austin:** They sold handmade leather journals to customers in the UK and EU. Budget was $1,000/month split 50/50 between UK (GBP) and Germany (EUR). They set both campaigns to the same bid in USD.

**What actually happened:** Over three months, the GBP weakened against the USD. Their pound-denominated bids effectively became higher in dollar terms. Their UK campaign spent 40% faster than planned and ran out of budget by the 18th of each month. Meanwhile, the EUR campaign was under-spending because the euro strengthened, making their bids too conservative.

**The fix:** Instead of setting bids in USD and letting Google convert, we set bids in local currency for each campaign. UK campaign: £3.50 max CPC. Germany campaign: €4.00 max CPC. We also set separate daily budgets in local currency and checked exchange rates every two weeks.

Then we created a budget rule in Google Ads that paused any campaign that hit 90% of spend before the 25th of the month. This prevented one market from cannibalizing the other's budget.

**The outcome:** Both campaigns started spending evenly across the month. UK campaign went from spending $500 in 18 days to spending $500 over 30 days. Germany campaign went from spending $500 and getting $1,200 in revenue to spending $500 and getting $1,900 in revenue because we could properly optimize bids.

**The tool:** Google Ads now lets you set budgets and bids in local currency per campaign. Use that feature. Don't let Google's auto-conversion silently drain your budget.

**A second example with a different currency problem:** A pet supply store in Portland started shipping to Switzerland. They set bids in USD. Google converted to CHF at their internal rate, which had a 3% markup. Over six months, that 3% markup on $4,800 in spend meant they lost $144 to hidden currency conversion fees.

**The fix:** Set the campaign to bid in CHF directly. Added a negative keyword list for "billig" (cheap) because Swiss customers searching for cheap pet supplies had a 90% bounce rate.

**The outcome:** $144 saved in conversion fees. Plus an additional $600 in revenue from focusing on quality-conscious Swiss buyers who actually converted.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I just run one campaign for all of Europe and target "English speakers"?**

That depends on whether you like wasting money. If you target "English speakers in Europe," your ads will show to people in Sweden who speak English and people in Malta who speak English and people in London who speak English. The problem is someone searching for "dog grooming London" has completely different intent than someone searching for "dog grooming Stockholm." They need different keywords, different ad copy, different landing pages. One campaign cannot serve four different markets properly. I've tested this. It fails. Always. Separate campaigns per market or don't bother.

**Q: How much should I spend to test a new European market?**

Start with whatever amount you'd be okay lighting on fire. For most small businesses, that's $300–$500 per month per market. Run for 60 days minimum. If you can't get at least 100 clicks and 5 conversions by day 60, either your offer doesn't fit that market or your campaign setup is wrong. Don't spend more until you know which.

**Q: Do I need to translate my landing pages or just my ads?**

Both. Here's why: if someone clicks your French ad and lands on an English page, they bounce within 3 seconds. Google tracks this as a low-quality experience. Your Quality Score drops. Your cost per click goes up. You pay more for worse results. I've seen campaigns where fixing the landing page translation dropped CPC by 40% and doubled conversion rate. Use a service like OneHourTranslation or a native speaker from Upwork. Do not use Google Translate for landing pages. It will sound like a robot wrote it, because a robot did write it.

**Q: How do I track conversions across different currencies?**

Set up conversion tracking in your local currency per campaign. Use Google Ads conversion tracking with values set in the same currency as your campaign budget. If you're selling $50 products to UK customers, set the conversion value in GBP. Google will handle the reporting in your dashboard. For e-commerce, use Google Analytics 4 with enhanced e-commerce tracking. It handles multi-currency natively. If you're using Shopify or WooCommerce, the Google channel apps usually handle this automatically.

**Q: Will Google Ads work for a service business targeting multiple European cities? I'm a plumber in NYC who wants to attract European visitors.**

Yes, but with caveats. Service businesses that require physical presence (plumbers, electricians, cleaners) can only target people who are actually in your service area. You cannot serve a German tourist if they need a plumber in Berlin. You're in NYC. This only works for businesses where the customer comes to you: hotels, restaurants, hair salons, fitness studios, retail stores. If you're a plumber, stick to your local market and target European tourists who own vacation homes in NYC. That's a real niche. I've seen it work for a property management company in Miami.

**Q: What's the biggest mistake small business owners make when expanding to European markets?**

Assuming European customers behave like American ones. They don't. Germans want detailed product specifications. French customers want beautiful visuals and emotional copy. British customers want humor and understatement. Swedish customers want sustainability claims backed by actual data. If you run the same ad in Paris, Berlin, and London, you will fail in at least two of those markets. I've seen this happen at three different agencies. The clients who succeed are the ones who adapt their messaging market by market, not the ones who try to save $500 on copywriting by running one generic ad.
