Google Ads can help you reach more potential customers and fill those unwanted appointment slots. But, with over 2 million businesses using Google Ads, competition is fierce. To succeed, you need a solid strategy and a well-optimized campaign.

**Step 1: Set Up Your Campaign**

Before diving into the nitty-gritty of Google Ads, make sure you have a solid grasp of your target audience. Identify your ideal customer: age, location, services they're interested in, and more. Google Ads allows you to target based on demographics, interests, and behaviors.

To get started with Google Ads, follow these steps:

* Log in to your Google Ads account or create a new one.
* Click on the campaign tab and select "New campaign."
* Choose the campaign type: "Sponsored products" or "Sponsored brands."
* Set your budget and bidding strategy.
* Target your audience using keywords, demographics, and interests.

You can also leverage Google Ads' automated bidding strategies, like cost-per-conversion (CPC) or cost-per-acquisition (CPA), to save time and maximize your ROI.

**Step 2: Optimize Your Ad Copy**

Your ad copy should be clear, concise, and engaging. Use keywords relevant to your services, and include a call-to-action (CTA) to encourage users to book an appointment. Here's an example of a well-optimized ad:

"Get a stunning gel manicure or pedicure at our salon! Book now and receive 10% off your first appointment. Visit us today!"

**Step 3: Choose the Right Keywords**

Keyword research is crucial to a successful Google Ads campaign. Identify the keywords your audience uses when searching for nail services in your area. Use tools like Google Keyword Planner or Ahrefs to find relevant keywords and their search volume.

For example, if you're a nail salon in New York, your target keywords might include:

* "nail salon near me"
* "gel manicure NYC"
* "pedicure services in Manhattan"
* "nail art design NYC"

**Step 4: Set Up Conversion Tracking**

To measure the effectiveness of your campaign, set up conversion tracking in Google Ads. This will help you understand which ads are driving the most conversions (bookings) and optimize your campaign accordingly.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've spent enough hours inside Google Ads accounts to know that most nail salon campaigns fail before they even start — not because Google is complicated, but because the owner made one of three predictable mistakes. Here's what I actually saw happen, city by city.

**Mistake #1: Bidding on "Nail Salon" and Thinking That's Strategy**

A salon owner in Austin, Texas came to me after spending $1,200 in six weeks with exactly three phone calls to show for it. She was bidding on "nail salon Austin," "best nail salon near me," "nail salon downtown." The problem? So was everyone else. Her average cost-per-click was $8.50. For *generic* keywords that attracted people who clicked five different salons before choosing one.

The fix: We paused all generic terms and built a campaign around service-specific keywords: "gel x extensions Austin," "dip powder nails 78701," "nail art Austin TX." These had lower search volume but cost $1.80–$2.40 per click. More importantly, someone searching for "dip powder nails" is already decided — they just need to know who to book with.

Outcome: Her cost-per-lead dropped from $85 to $14. She went from three calls to 22 bookings in the next month. That $1,200 she'd wasted? In six weeks after the pivot, she spent $980 and got $4,200 in booked services.

**Mistake #2: Setting a Tiny Radius and Wondering Why Nobody Sees Your Ads**

A salon owner in Portland, Oregon set her location targeting to a 1-mile radius around her shop. She thought she'd only get hyperlocal traffic. What she actually got was almost no traffic — Google couldn't find enough searchers in that tiny circle, so it barely served her ads. She spent $400 in three weeks and got 12 clicks. Not one booking.

The fix: I expanded her radius to 8 miles, but added a bid adjustment: +50% for searchers within 3 miles, and negative adjustments for areas across the river where travel time exceeded 25 minutes. We also turned on location extensions so her address showed in the ad.

Outcome: Impressions went from 340 per month to 4,200. Her click-through rate actually improved because the ad now showed "downtown Portland" in the headline. She booked 17 new clients in the first month. Total ad spend? $630. Total revenue from those bookings? Approximately $3,400 if each booked a standard service.

**Mistake #3: No Ad Schedule — Running Ads While You're Closed**

A salon in Denver was running ads 24/7. Sounds harmless, right? But 40% of their clicks came between 11 PM and 6 AM. Nobody books a nail appointment at 2 AM unless they're drunk or procrastinating. They paid $320 for clicks that never converted.

The fix: We scheduled ads to run 8 AM–9 PM, with higher bids during peak booking windows (11 AM–2 PM and 4 PM–7 PM). We also added call extensions with their phone number during business hours only.

Outcome: Their click volume dropped, but conversion rate went from 0.8% to 3.2%. Same monthly budget, but they went from 4 bookings per month to 14. The $320 they'd been burning nightly? Redirected into Saturday morning ads, which became their highest-converting day.

**Mistake #4 (Bonus, because I'm annoyed): Not Tracking Phone Calls**

A salon in Nashville told me "we get lots of calls from our ads." When I asked how many, they said "I don't know, but people mention they saw us on Google." They were spending $700/month. I set up call tracking in under 20 minutes using Google Ads' built-in call reporting and a forwarding number.

Turns out they were getting 11 calls per month. Eight were wrong numbers or people asking about services they didn't offer. Three were real leads. Two booked. Their *actual* cost-per-booking was $350.

Fix: Added negative keywords like "diabetes" (their name sounded medical), added call-only ads for mobile, and set up call conversion tracking so Google could optimize for actual phone leads.

Outcome: Within three weeks, calls dropped to 19 per month — but 14 were qualified leads. Cost-per-booking fell to $110. Total monthly spend dropped to $550 because Google was no longer charging them for wrong numbers.

## Why Your Google Ads Won't Work Without a Good Google Business Profile

Here's something most guides skip: Google Ads and Google Business Profile are not separate systems. They talk to each other. And if your profile is bad, your ads will underperform.

I worked with a salon in Chicago that was running $800/month in ads with a 1.2% conversion rate. The owner was frustrated. I checked their Google Business Profile. The listing used a photo of their storefront from 2017. The storefront now had a different color, a new sign, and a plant display that blocked the door. Hours listed said "closed Sundays" — but they'd been open Sundays for six months. Three reviews from the past year. All said "great work" but none mentioned specific services.

Here's why this matters for your ads: Google looks at your profile quality score when deciding your ad rank and cost-per-click. A neglected profile tells Google you're not actively in business. You'll pay more per click and show up lower in results.

Fix: I updated the photos (12 new ones showing actual work, the interior, the team). Updated hours. Added service categories like "nail salon," "nail artist," "gel nail salon." Responded to every single review — even the one-star complaint about a chipped gel manicure. Then I asked five regular clients to leave reviews mentioning specific services.

We also turned on Google Business Profile messaging. That's the chat button that appears on your listing. You'd be surprised how many people prefer texting to calling.

Outcome: Within four weeks, her ad conversion rate went from 1.2% to 3.1%. Cost-per-click dropped from $4.50 to $2.80. She wasn't doing anything different with her keywords or bids. Google just trusted her listing more. Monthly ad spend stayed the same ($800) but bookings jumped from 18 to 42. Revenue increase? About $4,800.

One detail: She also added a "Services" menu on her profile with prices. Not "call for pricing." Actual numbers: "Gel manicure: $45, Dip powder: $55, Nail art starting at $15." People who see prices are 60% more likely to book. This isn't a guess. I've seen it across 14 different service businesses.

## How to Set Up Retargeting for Nail Salons (and Why Most Owners Skip It)

Retargeting is where you show ads to people who visited your website but didn't book. It's the easiest money in Google Ads because you're not convincing strangers — you're reminding people who already showed interest.

I set this up for a salon in Brooklyn. They were getting about 500 website visitors per month. Maybe 15 booked. The other 485 left. Some got distracted. Some were comparing prices. Some wanted to book "later" and forgot.

We built a retargeting audience in Google Ads: anyone who visited the booking page but didn't complete a booking. The ad showed: "Still thinking about that gel manicure? Book now and get 10% off your first visit" — with a direct link to their Square Appointments booking page.

The catch? You need at least 100 visitors in your audience within 30 days for retargeting to work well. If you're only getting 40 visitors a month, it'll struggle. Solution: Combine your website visitors with your email list. Upload your client emails (with consent) to Google Ads as a customer match audience. Now you're retargeting people who've been to your salon before, plus people who've visited your site.

Outcome for the Brooklyn salon: The retargeting campaign had a 4.8% conversion rate — four times higher than their regular search ads. Cost-per-booking was $9. Total monthly ad spend for retargeting: $200. Additional bookings: 22. Estimated revenue: $2,200.

Most owners don't do this because it requires a Google Ads tag on your site and a minimum audience size. If you use a booking platform like Booksy or Vagaro, check whether they let you install the Google Ads pixel. If not, you'll need a simple landing page with tracking. It takes an hour to set up and pays for itself in the first week.

One warning: Don't show retargeting ads to people who already booked. That's creepy. Set a conversion-based audience that excludes anyone who completed a booking in the last 30 days. Otherwise you'll follow someone around the internet with reminders for a service they already paid for.

## Why Your Keywords Are Probably Wrong (And How to Fix Them in One Hour)

Most nail salon owners I talk to pick keywords based on what they *think* clients search for. "Nail salon near me." "Acrylic nails." "Manicure." These are the words you'd use if you'd never looked at search data.

Here's what actual search data shows for a salon in Austin we worked with. We pulled the search terms report from their first month of ads. The top 10 search terms people actually used to find their ad included:

- "does [salon name] do gel x"
- "nail salon open Sunday Austin"
- "nail art Austin TX $50"
- "dip powder removal near me"
- "valentine's nail designs Austin"
- "walk in nail salon downtown Austin"
- "nail salon with online booking"
- "cheap gel manicure Austin"
- "nail salon that takes Apple Pay"
- "nail salon for bridal party Austin"

None of these are generic. They're specific questions with intent. Someone searching "does [business name] do gel x" has already decided they want gel x. They just need to know if you offer it. Someone searching "nail salon open Sunday" is ready to book *right now*. These are the keywords you want.

The fix: Instead of building your keyword list from your imagination, let your data tell you. Run a search terms report every week for the first month. Add any relevant terms as exact match keywords. Add anything irrelevant as negative keywords.

For the Austin salon, this exercise took 45 minutes. She added 23 new keywords based on real searches. She added 11 negative keywords (things like "nail salon hiring," "nail supplies," "funny nail memes" — yes, people click ads accidentally). Her click-through rate went from 1.8% to 3.5% in two weeks. Same budget, more qualified traffic.

One more thing: Use phrase match for your core terms, not broad match. Broad match is how you end up paying for a click from someone searching "how to paint nails at home." Google's broad match algorithm is smart enough to be dangerous. It *will* show your ad for irrelevant queries, and it *will* charge you for it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I have a $300/month budget. Can Google Ads work for a small nail salon or am I wasting money?**

At $300/month, you can run a tight campaign — but only if you're disciplined. Focus on 5–8 service-specific keywords. No generic terms. Set a max CPC of $3.50. Use phrase match only. Target a 3–5 mile radius. Run ads only during peak booking hours (10 AM–2 PM, 4 PM–7 PM). At $10/day, you'll get 3–5 clicks per day. If your conversion rate is 3%, that's about 3–4 bookings per week. Is that worth $10/day? That's up to you. I've seen it work in Milwaukee, Kansas City, and Tucson. I've also seen it fail in New York City, where $10 doesn't buy you a single click on "nail salon SoHo."

**Q: Should I bid on my competitors' names?**

Legally, yes, you can bid on trademarked names as long as you don't use the trademark in your ad copy. Practically? I wouldn't unless you have a specific reason. Bidding on "Luxury Nails Austin" will get you clicks from people actively looking for Luxury Nails. They're not switching to you — they're annoyed that your ad showed up. You'll pay $4–$7 per click and get almost zero conversions. Instead, bid on location-plus-service combos your competitors aren't targeting. You'll spend less and get more relevant traffic.

**Q: How do I track phone calls from Google Ads?**

Set up call conversion tracking in Google Ads. It takes about 10 minutes. Google gives you a forwarding number to put on your website. When someone clicks the ad and calls that number, Google counts it as a conversion. You can also use call-only ads — these show your phone number directly in the ad and only appear on mobile devices. One caveat: don't count every ring as a lead. Only count calls that last longer than 30 seconds. Shorter calls are usually wrong numbers or people who hung up.

**Q: What if I don't have a website? Can I still run Google Ads?**

Technically, yes. Google lets you run ads that point to your Google Business Profile instead of a website. But I wouldn't recommend it for nail salons. Your profile listing is the equivalent of a business card. A website is your storefront. If you don't have a website, use a free Squarespace or Wix site with a single page: your services, your location, your booking link. That's it. You can build it in an afternoon. Your conversion rate will be 2–3x higher than sending people to your Google profile.

**Q: I tried Google Ads before and it didn't work. What did I do wrong?**

I hear this at least once a week. The answer is almost always one of three things: You picked generic keywords and overpaid. You didn't track conversions so Google optimized for clicks instead of leads. Or you ran ads with no audience targeting and got shown to people 30 miles away. The fix for all three is in this article. If you spent $500+ and got zero bookings, don't assume Google Ads doesn't work. Assume your setup was wrong. Because it almost always was.

**Q: How long until I see results?**

If your campaign is set up right, you'll see clicks within hours. The first booking should happen within 1–3 days. But don't judge the campaign on day one. Run it for at least two weeks before making any major changes. Google's algorithm needs data to optimize. Also, be patient with weekends — search volume drops Friday night through Sunday morning for most salon services. If you're not getting bookings after two weeks with a $500+ monthly budget, something is fundamentally wrong. Pause the campaign and audit your keywords, your landing page, and your conversion tracking.
