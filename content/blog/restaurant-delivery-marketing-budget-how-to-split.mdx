## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating Every Delivery Platform the Same

I worked with a barbecue spot in Austin, Texas — let's call it "Smoke Ring BBQ" — that was hemorrhaging $2,100 a month on marketing across DoorDash, Uber Eats, and Grubhub. They split their budget evenly: $700 per platform. Sounded fair, right?

Here's what was actually happening: 73% of their delivery revenue came through DoorDash. Uber Eats brought in 18%. Grubhub was generating $340 in monthly revenue against $700 in ad spend. They were losing over $350 a month on Grubhub alone, month after month, because they assumed "more platforms = more customers."

**The fix:** I had them pull 90 days of platform-specific data — not just orders, but *new customer acquisition cost* per platform. For Smoke Ring BBQ, a new DoorDash customer cost $8.50 to acquire. On Uber Eats, it was $14. A new Grubhub customer cost $47. They cut Grubhub marketing entirely. That freed up $700/month, which they redirected into DoorDash boost spending and a simple "order direct" flyer in carryout bags.

**The outcome:** Total monthly delivery revenue went from $8,900 to $11,400 over the next 60 days. Their effective ad spend dropped from 23% of delivery revenue to 14%. The owner told me, "I was paying $47 to get a customer who ordered once for $22. I feel like an idiot." He's not an idiot. He just never looked at the per-platform math.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Google My Business as a Delivery Driver

A pizza place in Portland, Oregon was spending $1,500 a month on DoorDash promotions — "free delivery," "$5 off first order," the works. They were getting orders but losing money on almost every one because the commission plus the promotion ate 38% of the ticket.

Meanwhile, their Google My Business listing was a mess. Wrong hours. No photos of their actual pizza. The "order online" button pointed to a broken link for three months. Nobody caught it because nobody was checking.

**The fix:** We cleaned up the GMB profile, uploaded 12 photos of their actual food (not stock images), set accurate hours, and linked their direct online ordering system (Square Online, $0/month extra since they already used Square for POS). Then we reallocated $500 of that DoorDash promo budget into local Google Ads targeting "pizza delivery [neighborhood name]" — five specific Portland neighborhoods within 3 miles.

**The outcome:** Direct orders through Google increased from 14 per month to 89 per month over six weeks. Average order value was $32 on direct orders versus $24 on DoorDash. The $500/month in Google Ads generated $4,100 in direct delivery revenue. That's an 8.2x return. Their DoorDash spend dropped to $1,000/month, and their total delivery profit actually went up by $1,900/month because they kept more of each transaction.

The painful part? They had the direct ordering system the whole time. It just wasn't connected to anything customers could find.

### Mistake #3: Advertising to People Who Already Order

A coffee shop in Denver — three locations, solid regulars — was running "new customer" promotions on DoorDash at $600/month. They assumed it was bringing in fresh faces.

I asked them to check one thing: how many of those "new customer" promo codes were being used by accounts that had ordered before. DoorDash doesn't make this obvious, but you can request a customer acquisition report from your DoorDash account manager. Turns out 41% of their promo redemptions were from repeat customers using new accounts or family members' accounts to get the discount again.

These were existing customers they were paying to acquire twice. Three times. Some had cycled through four different email addresses to keep getting the $8 off.

**The fix:** Stop running general promos. Instead, redirect that $600 into a targeted DoorDash "new-to-restaurant" campaign — which DoorDash actually offers but doesn't advertise well. (You have to ask your rep to enable it.) Also set up a loyalty program through Square that gave regulars a free drink after 8 purchases, delivered via text message through their existing Square Marketing account. Total monthly cost: $50 for the texting credits.

**The outcome:** New-to-restaurant DoorDash orders stayed roughly flat — 112 per month before, 108 per month after — but the promo cost dropped from $600 to $240. The $360 saved went into a print run of 5,000 double-sided flyers with a QR code to their direct ordering site. That generated 47 new direct customers in the first month, averaging $27 per visit. Net gain: approximately $1,270 in additional monthly profit from what was previously wasted promo spend.

### Mistake #4: Confusing "Marketing Budget" with "Commission Fees"

A bakery in Nashville was telling me their "delivery marketing budget" was $3,800/month. I asked for the breakdown. $2,400 was Uber Eats commission fees. $800 was DoorDash commission fees. $600 was "promotions." They had zero dollars going to anything that would actually bring in customers who weren't already browsing the app.

They were paying 30% commissions and calling it marketing. It's not marketing. It's a cost of sale. Commission is what you pay to process the transaction. Marketing is what you spend to get someone to choose your bakery over the one three blocks away.

**The fix:** Treat commissions and marketing as separate line items. Commission is unavoidable within the delivery app ecosystem. Marketing is where you differentiate. We restructured their budget: kept commissions where they were, moved the $600 "promotions" into Instagram geotargeted ads within 2 miles of their location, and built a simple email capture popup on their website offering 10% off first direct order.

**The outcome:** The Instagram ads cost $580/month and generated 34 direct orders in the first 30 days. Total revenue from those orders: $1,870. More importantly, they now had 29 email addresses from people who opted in. Those people got a "free cookie on your next visit" email two weeks later. Twelve of them showed up. Average in-store spend: $14. That's $168 in additional revenue from a $0 email send.

The commission fees didn't change. But the $600 that was previously just subsidizing the platform now actually builds something the bakery owns.

## Building an Off-Platform Marketing Engine That Actually Works

If your entire customer acquisition strategy relies on delivery apps, you're renting your customers. The platform owns the relationship. They own the data. They can change their algorithm tomorrow and your orders drop 40%. I've seen this happen to three different clients in the last 18 months.

Here's how to build a direct pipeline that insulates you from platform drama.

**Start with the checkout experience.** Every single delivery order — whether it comes through DoorDash, Uber Eats, or your website — should include a physical insert inviting the customer to order direct next time. Not a QR code on a thermal receipt that fades in three hours. A real card. I've tested this with a sandwich shop in Chicago: 4x6 card, one sentence ("Skip the fees — order from us directly and get a free drink"), QR code to a dedicated ordering page. Cost: $85 for 2,000 cards printed at a local shop. Result: 63 direct orders traced to those cards in the first month.

**Capture phone numbers, not just emails.** Email open rates for restaurant marketing average around 18-22%. SMS open rates are 95%+ within three minutes. Tools like Postscript or Klaviyo integrate with most POS systems (Square, Toast, Clover) and let you send automated texts. A bagel shop in NYC added a "text us your order number for a free bagel next time" prompt at checkout. They collected 340 phone numbers in 45 days. Cost: $0 to set up the prompt, $45/month for the SMS tool. They send one text per week. Average redemption rate: 31%. Average spend per redemption: $11.50.

**Use Google Business Profile posts.** Most restaurant owners set up their GMB listing and never touch it again. Google gives you a free billboard: you can post updates, photos, and offers directly in search results. A taco truck in Los Angeles posts their daily special at 10am every morning. Takes 3 minutes. They track which posts generate calls using Google's built-in insights. Their "today's special" posts drive an average of 17 extra phone orders per day. The cost is zero.

**Run hyperlocal Facebook ads with a specific offer.** Not "20% off your order." Too generic. Try "Free guacamole with any burrito ordered before 2pm — show this ad." Use the 1-mile radius targeting. A burrito shop in Phoenix spent $200 on this exact campaign. They set a $10 daily budget for 20 days. Results: 143 ad clicks, 89 coupon redemptions (they asked customers to say "Facebook," which is hacky but works), and $2,140 in revenue attributed to the ad. Cost per acquisition: $2.25.

**Make it stupidly easy to reorder.** If a customer has to log in, find their password, search for their past order, and re-enter their payment info, they're going back to Uber Eats. Use a POS that supports saved customer profiles. Toast and Square both let customers reorder in two taps if they've ordered before. Remind them via email or text. A diner in Austin added a "one-tap reorder" button to their weekly Friday email. Open rate was 22%. Click-to-order rate was 41%. Average order value on those reorders was $19. That email cost nothing to send.

The point isn't to abandon delivery platforms. They're a valid channel. The point is to stop treating them as your only channel. Every dollar you put into direct ordering infrastructure compounds. Every dollar you put into platform ads needs to be spent again next month.

## How to Structure a $2,000 Monthly Delivery Marketing Budget (A Real Template)

Small business owners ask me for a number. Here's one. This is based on what actually worked for a fried chicken spot in Charlotte, North Carolina. Your numbers will vary, but the structure is transferable.

**Total monthly budget: $2,000**

**$700 — DoorDash and Uber Eats "new customer" ads.** Split $350 each, focusing on new-to-restaurant campaigns only. No general promotions. Target audience radius: 2 miles. Expected ROI: 3-4x on DoorDash, 2-3x on Uber Eats.

**$500 — Google Ads (Local Search + "Order Online").** Target 5-7 specific neighborhoods within 3 miles. Use keyword "delivery [neighborhood name]" and "food near me." Link directly to your website's ordering page, not your DoorDash page. Expected cost per click: $2.80-$4.50. Expected conversion rate: 8-12%.

**$300 — Google Business Profile management.** This includes someone actually checking your listing weekly, responding to reviews, updating hours for holidays, and posting offers. If you can't do it yourself, hire a VA in the Philippines for $150/month or use a service like BrightLocal. The remaining $150 goes into GMB photo updates and posts.

**$250 — SMS marketing (two sends per week).** One Wednesday "order for the weekend" text. One Friday "lunch special" text. Use Postscript for a restaurant-specific SMS platform. Cost: $45/month base plus about $15 in messaging fees per 1,000 contacts. The remaining $190 is buffer for testing offers.

**$150 — Instagram/Facebook hyperlocal ads.** One campaign, $5/day, targeting people within 1 mile who follow restaurant accounts. Offer: specific item, specific time window ("Chicken sandwich + fries for $12, 11am-2pm, show this ad").

**$100 — Monthly flyer reprint (5,000 inserts for delivery bags).** Cost includes design (Canva, free) and printing at a local shop. Include QR code to direct ordering with a "free side" offer.

**Total: $2,000**

This budget structure assumes you already have a functioning direct ordering website and a POS that captures customer data. If you don't, start there before you spend a dime on ads.

The Charlotte chicken spot went from $5,800/month in delivery revenue (all platform) to $9,200/month (platform + direct) in 90 days using this exact framework. Their platform commissions actually dropped as a percentage of total revenue because direct orders carry zero commission.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I just drop DoorDash and Uber Eats entirely?**
Probably not. They're still the largest source of new customers who are actively looking to order delivery. The mistake isn't using them. The mistake is using them as your only acquisition channel and calling it a marketing strategy. Keep the platforms that generate positive ROI. Cut the ones that don't. Run the math every quarter.

**Q: How do I track whether a customer came from DoorDash versus my website?**
You can't perfectly track this on the platform side — DoorDash doesn't share that data. But you can track it on your end. Use unique phone numbers (Google Voice numbers cost $0), unique promo codes per channel, or ask customers how they found you during checkout. Imperfect is fine. You're not running a clinical trial. You're running a restaurant.

**Q: What if I don't have a website that takes orders?**
Get one. Square Online costs $0/month if you already use Square for payments. Toast has a similar free tier. If you're paying $500/month for a custom website built in 2018 that nobody can find, replace it with a $29/month template on Shopify or Framer and connect it to your POS. This is not a technology problem. This is a decision problem.

**Q: How long should I test a platform before deciding it's not working?**
Eight weeks minimum. Some campaigns take time to optimize. But if you're losing money on a platform after 60 days and you've tested at least three different ad creatives or offers, cut it. You can always come back later. The money you save can go into something that works.

**Q: Do I need a social media manager?**
Not if your budget is under $3,000/month total marketing spend. Post photos of your actual food on Instagram Stories. Reply to comments. That's enough. A social media manager at $800/month will eat a third of your budget before you've spent a dollar on anything that actually brings someone through the door. Hire one when your monthly marketing budget hits $5,000 and you're already profitable on every other channel.

**Q: What's the one thing I should do today that costs zero dollars?**
Claim your Google Business Profile if you haven't. Update your hours. Add your current menu. Upload photos that a customer took of your food — not professional shots, the ones that look like someone actually ate it. Then respond to every review from the last three months. Google ranks businesses that actively manage their profile. This takes 45 minutes and costs nothing.
