### Setting Up Google Ads for Restaurants

When it comes to setting up Google Ads for your restaurant, the first step is to create a campaign. You can choose from several campaign types, including Search, Display, and Shopping. For restaurants, Search campaigns are the most effective, as they allow you to target customers who are actively searching for restaurants like yours.

<Callout type="tip">Make sure to set up Google My Business (GMB) before creating your Google Ads campaign. GMB is essential for restaurants to manage their online presence and get more customers.</Callout>

### Crafting the Perfect Ad Copy

Your ad copy is what sets you apart from the competition and makes customers choose your restaurant. Here are some key elements to include in your ad copy:

*   A compelling headline that grabs attention
*   A clear and concise description of your restaurant and its offerings
*   A call-to-action (CTA) that encourages customers to take action
*   A sense of urgency or scarcity to drive sales

<Callout type="warning">Avoid using generic ad copy that can be applied to any restaurant. Create unique ad copy that speaks to your restaurant's brand and values.</Callout>

### Targeting the Right Audience

Targeting the right audience is crucial to getting more reservations. With Google Ads, you can target customers based on their location, language, and search history. Here are some targeting options to consider:

*   Location targeting: Target customers in a specific geographic area, such as a city or zip code.
*   Language targeting: Target customers who speak a specific language.
*   Search history targeting: Target customers who have searched for restaurants like yours.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've managed enough Google Ads accounts over the years to have a collection of screw-ups that still make me wince. Here are the ones I see restaurant owners making most often — and what actually works instead.

### Mistake #1: Bidding on your own restaurant name

A steakhouse owner in Austin, Texas came to me frustrated. He was spending $1,200 a month on Google Ads and getting almost nothing for it. When I pulled up his account, the first thing I saw was that 40% of his budget was going to clicks on his own branded terms — searches for "[restaurant name] Austin."

Here's the thing: if someone is already searching for your restaurant by name, they're probably going to find you anyway. Your Google My Business listing shows up. Your website is on page one organically. You're paying Google for something you'd get for free.

**The fix:** I paused all branded keywords and moved that budget to non-branded searches like "best steak in Austin" or "romantic dinner downtown Austin." Within two weeks, his cost per reservation dropped from $18 to $6. After a month, he was getting 40 more reservations per week on the same budget. That's about $3,800 in incremental monthly revenue from shifts that took me 15 minutes.

If you're bidding on your own name, stop. Take that money and go after people who don't know you exist yet.

### Mistake #2: Using broad match keywords without negative keywords

A pizza place in Denver had a Google Ads account set up by someone who clearly read a blog post from 2018 and called it a day. They were running broad match on "pizza Denver" and wondering why their phone was ringing off the hook with people asking about delivery options that didn't exist and gluten-free crusts they'd never made.

Broad match in 2024 is smarter than it used to be, but it's still dumb enough to show your ad to someone searching "pizza Denver recipe" or "vegan pizza Denver" when you're a meat-lovers joint. That client burned $900 in two weeks on clicks from people who were never going to walk through their door.

**The fix:** I added 47 negative keywords in one session — things like "delivery only," "gluten free," "vegan," "recipe," "frozen," "buffet," "cheap," and competitor names. Then I switched the campaign to phrase match and exact match only. Their click-through rate went from 1.8% to 4.2%, and their cost per call dropped by 60%. They spent $500 less per month and got more actual customers.

You can find your negative keywords by looking at the search terms report in Google Ads every week for the first month. Anything irrelevant gets added to the negative list. Do this before you spend another dollar.

### Mistake #3: Sending all traffic to a homepage

A barbecue joint in Nashville was running ads for "Nashville BBQ catering" — a high-intent search if I've ever seen one. But the ads were sending people to their general homepage. Customers had to hunt for the catering page, find the form, fill it out, and hope someone called them back.

Their conversion rate was 0.8%. On a $2,000 monthly budget, that's roughly 3 catering inquiries. They were losing money and didn't know why.

**The fix:** I built a dedicated landing page for catering. One headline, one photo of their brisket platter, a short form (name, email, date, guest count), and a button that said "Get a Quote." That's it. No navigation. No menu PDF downloads. No distractions.

Conversion rate went to 6.2%. Their cost per catering lead dropped from $47 to $9. They booked $5,400 in catering orders in the first month after the change. The page took me about 90 minutes to build with a simple landing page builder.

Your ad promises something specific. Your landing page should deliver exactly that. If you send someone looking for "Sunday brunch reservations" to a page about your dinner menu, they're leaving.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring location targeting radius

A coffee shop in Portland was running ads that showed up for people 30 miles away. Their shop was in Southeast Portland. Most of their customers walked or biked there. The owner was paying for clicks from people in Beaverton and Gresham who were never going to drive 40 minutes for a latte.

**The fix:** I set the targeting radius to 2 miles. Then I added location exclusions for areas that were too far. Their impressions dropped by 70%, but their foot traffic increased 25%. The budget that was previously wasted on irrelevant clicks now went to people who could actually visit. Their cost per visit dropped from $4.50 to $1.80.

Set your radius tighter than you think. For a coffee shop or casual restaurant, 1-3 miles is usually right. For a destination restaurant, maybe 5-10 miles. Anything beyond that is charity to Google.

## Smarter Bidding: When to Use Maximize Conversions vs. Manual CPC

Most restaurant owners I talk to set their Google Ads budget and let the platform decide how to spend it. That's fine if you like surprises. I don't.

The two main bidding strategies you'll choose between are Maximize Conversions and Manual Cost-Per-Click (CPC). Here's when each one actually works.

**Maximize Conversions** tells Google to spend your budget to get as many conversions as possible. Sounds great, right? The problem is that Google defines "conversion" based on whatever you've set up — and if you haven't set up conversion tracking properly, it's basically guessing. I've seen accounts where Google decided that "time on site over 30 seconds" counted as a conversion. That's not a reservation. That's someone who couldn't find the menu.

Maximize Conversions works well when:
- You have a clear conversion action (online reservation, call tracking, form submission)
- You have at least 30 conversions in the past 30 days
- Your budget is stable and predictable

A Thai restaurant in Chicago used Maximize Conversions after I helped them set up proper call tracking. Their cost per reservation dropped from $14 to $8 in three weeks. But when they tried the same strategy on a new campaign with zero conversion history, Google spent $400 with one reservation to show for it.

**Manual CPC** gives you control over how much you're willing to pay for each click. It's more work, but it's better for:
- New campaigns with no conversion data
- Small budgets under $1,000/month where every dollar matters
- Restaurants with specific margin math (you know a reservation is worth $22 to you, so you won't pay more than $5 for a click)

A bakery in New York City was spending $600/month on Manual CPC with bids set at $3.50. Their cost per online order was $11. I switched them to Maximize Conversions, and within a month, their cost per order hit $7. But that only worked because they had clean tracking and 50+ orders per month to train the algorithm.

**My recommendation:** Start with Manual CPC until you have 30-50 conversions in your account. Then test Maximize Conversions with a small portion of your budget — maybe 20%. Compare the results for two weeks. If it works, scale it. If it doesn't, go back to manual.

And for the love of good coffee, set a max CPC bid limit even when using automated bidding. I've seen Maximize Conversions spend $12 on a single click because Google thought it was "high intent." That click ordered a $5 coffee. The math doesn't work.

## Getting Reservations from Your Google My Business Listing: The Missing Link

Your Google My Business (GMB) listing is free real estate that most restaurant owners treat like a digital phone book. They fill out the basics and move on. That's a mistake.

I worked with a brunch spot in Portland that was getting 200+ calls a month from their GMB listing. The problem? They were missing half of them because the owner was in the kitchen during peak hours. Each missed call was a potential reservation walking to a competitor.

Here's what I set up that actually moved the needle:

**1. Direct booking links in GMB**
Google lets you add a reservation button that links directly to your booking system — OpenTable, Resy, Toast, or even a simple form on your site. Most restaurants don't bother. The ones that do see 15-25% more online bookings. It takes 10 minutes to set up in your GMB dashboard.

**2. Call tracking numbers**
I use CallRail or a similar tool to track calls from GMB separately from other traffic. A BBQ joint in Austin discovered that 60% of their GMB calls were asking about hours and location — not reservations. They added those details to their GMB profile in bullet points. Call volume dropped 30%, but reservation calls went up 20% because people who actually wanted to book weren't competing with people who just needed to know if they were open on Mondays.

**3. Responding to reviews (the right way)**
Every review response is a chance to show up in local search. Google rewards businesses that engage with reviews. But most owners write "Thanks for coming!" and move on. Instead, respond to positive reviews by mentioning a specific dish or experience. "Glad you enjoyed the brisket tacos — come back for happy hour, they're half off on Tuesdays" tells Google you're active and tells customers you care. A coffee shop in Denver started doing this and saw their local search impressions jump 18% in two months.

**4. Photos that actually get people to book**
GMB listings with photos get 42% more requests for directions and 35% more clicks to website. But most restaurants upload 3 blurry photos from 2019. Upload 20+ photos — food shots, interior, exterior, staff, the parking lot if it's tricky. Update them every quarter. A bakery in New York City saw a 12% increase in online orders after adding photos of their seasonal menu items.

Your GMB listing is running 24/7. If it's not optimized, you're paying for ads that have to work harder to make up for it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads for my restaurant to see results?**
$500 to $2,000 per month is typical for a single-location restaurant, depending on your city and competition. In a mid-sized market like Austin or Denver, I'd start at $800. In a high-cost market like New York or San Francisco, you'll probably need $1,500+. Anything under $300 is unlikely to generate enough data to know what works. I've seen restaurants waste $200/month for six months because they never had enough budget to actually test anything.

**Q: Can't I just rely on Yelp and word of mouth instead of paying for ads?**
You can, but you're leaving money on the table. Yelp is great for people who already know they want your type of food. Google Ads catches people who are searching "what's open near me right now" or "best brunch in [neighborhood]" — that's pure demand you're not capturing otherwise. A taco spot in Portland ran a $600/month Google Ads campaign alongside their existing Yelp presence and saw a 30% increase in new customer reservations within six weeks. Word of mouth is slow. Ads are fast.

**Q: How long does it take for Google Ads to start working?**
You'll see clicks within hours of launching a campaign. But meaningful results — actual reservations or calls — usually take 2-4 weeks. The first week is data collection. Week two, you start optimizing. By week three or four, you should have a clear picture of your cost per reservation. If you're not seeing a positive return by week six, something is wrong with your setup, not your restaurant.

**Q: Do I need to hire an agency or can I do this myself?**
You can absolutely do it yourself if you're willing to learn the basics and check the account weekly. The minimum setup takes about 3-4 hours: campaign structure, keyword research, ad copy, landing page, conversion tracking. After that, 30 minutes per week to review search terms and adjust bids. But if you're spending more than $1,000/month and don't have time to learn, an agency or freelancer will probably save you money by not wasting it. I've seen DIY accounts with $1,500 budgets where 60% was going to irrelevant keywords.

**Q: What's the biggest waste of money in restaurant Google Ads?**
Running ads without conversion tracking. I've audited accounts where the owner thought they were getting 50 reservations a month. When we actually tracked calls and bookings, it was 7. Without tracking, you're flying blind. Second biggest waste: bidding on your own restaurant name (covered above). Third: sending all traffic to a generic homepage instead of a dedicated page for what the ad promises.

**Q: Should I run ads during slow hours to fill seats?**
Only if you have a specific offer and can track it. A happy hour ad that runs 3-5 PM Monday through Thursday can work if you have a compelling reason to come in — half-price appetizers, live music, whatever. But just running general ads during slow times rarely works because the demand isn't there. People aren't searching for dinner at 3 PM. They're searching at 11 AM for lunch or 5 PM for dinner. Focus your ad schedule on when people are actually looking.
