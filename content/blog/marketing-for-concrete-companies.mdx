## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Relying Entirely on Referrals Without a Digital Presence

A concrete company in Denver had been in business for 12 years. They did excellent stamped patios and driveway overlays. Their entire marketing strategy was: do good work, hope someone calls. That worked fine until 2021, when three new competitors opened within five miles and started running Google Ads.

The owner called me frustrated. "I'm losing bids I used to win in my sleep." He showed me a $12,000 driveway job he'd quoted. The customer chose another company because "we couldn't find you online." They found the competitor through a Google search. The competitor didn't have better work — they just existed where customers were looking.

**The fix:** We built a basic five-page website in two weeks. Nothing fancy. Homepage, services, project gallery, about, contact. Claimed and optimized his Google Business Profile with 40 before-and-after photos. We set up a simple Google Ads campaign targeting "concrete contractor Denver" and "stamped concrete Denver" with a $600 monthly budget.

**The outcome:** In the first month, he got 14 leads through his website and GBP. Six turned into signed projects worth $4,300 in tracked revenue. Within 90 days, his monthly leads from digital sources exceeded his referral leads for the first time. Total cost: $1,800 in setup and three months of ads. Total attributed revenue: roughly $50,000.

### Mistake 2: Using Terrible Photos on Everything

A concrete crew in Nashville had great work. I mean, genuinely impressive driveways with colored borders and custom stamp patterns. But their website and Google profile had photos taken with a phone from 2016 — dark, blurry, taken at bad angles, showing half-finished work.

A potential customer told them, "Your photos look like you do cheap work." That hurt because their prices were mid-range and their quality was high. They just looked low-end.

**The fix:** I told the owner to spend $500 on a real photographer for one afternoon. They shot five completed projects — wide shots, detail shots, before-and-after pairs. We swapped all photos on the website, Google profile, and Facebook page within a week.

**The outcome:** Their website conversion rate went from 2% to nearly 6% in six weeks. More importantly, they started getting asked about premium stamped options more often. The photos communicated quality without a single word. One customer later said, "I picked you because your driveway photos looked like something I'd want to park on." That job alone was $8,500. The photography paid for itself on the first call.

### Mistake 3: Throwing Money at Google Ads Without Understanding Keywords

A concrete contractor in Austin thought Google Ads was a magic button. He set a $1,200 monthly budget, turned it on, and waited. He got a lot of clicks. Very few calls. He was furious about "wasting money."

I looked at his campaign. He was bidding on "concrete contractor Austin" — fine. But also on "how to pour concrete," "concrete cost per yard," "cement mixer rental," and "DIY patio ideas." Those clicks were people researching, not buying. He'd spent $800 on people who wanted to do it themselves.

**The fix:** We paused every keyword that didn't indicate purchase intent. Kept only "concrete contractor Austin," "driveway replacement Austin," "stamped concrete patio Austin," "concrete repair Austin." Changed match types from broad to phrase match. Added negative keywords: "how to," "diy," "cost of materials," "rental," "calculator." Set his ads to run only 7am-6pm weekdays, since homeowners call during work hours.

**The outcome:** Monthly cost dropped from $1,200 to $750. Leads per month went from 5 to 22. Cost per lead dropped from about $60 to about $15. The owner called me after two weeks and said, "I can't answer the phone fast enough." He booked $28,000 in new work that month.

## What Actually Works for Driveway and Patio Marketing

Let me be specific about what generates actual calls for concrete businesses. Not theory — what I've seen work in practice across 15+ concrete and hardscape clients.

**Google Business Profile is your front door.** I don't care if you have the best website in the world. If your GBP is incomplete or unclaimed, you're invisible. Concrete customers search "concrete contractor near me" or "driveway replacement [city]" on their phones. Google shows them a map with three local results. You need to be one of those three.

Here's what I've seen work consistently: post to your GBP once a week. A photo of a finished project with 2-3 sentences about what you did. "Completed a 500sqft stamped concrete patio in South Austin. Client chose a custom brown color to match their limestone exterior." That's it. Companies that post weekly get 2x the GBP views compared to companies that post once a month.

**Facebook ads work for concrete — but only the right ones.** Don't run ads to "everyone in a 20-mile radius." That's how you spend $1,000 and get three likes from your aunt. Instead, create a custom audience of homeowners aged 35-65 within specific zip codes. Target home values above $300,000 if you're doing patios and decorative work. Target older homes (built before 1990) if you focus on driveway replacement.

I had a client in Portland who spent $450/month on Facebook ads targeting homeowners with properties built in the 1970s. He specialized in driveway tear-outs and replacement. That campaign generated 18 leads in two months. Seven closed at an average of $4,200 per job. Revenue from those campaigns: $29,400. Ad spend: $900. That's a 32x return.

**Yelp is a dumpster fire but you still need to claim your page.** I've had clients spend $2,000/month on Yelp ads with zero measurable return. I don't recommend paying Yelp anything. But you need to claim your free page, add photos, and respond to reviews — good and bad. Why? Because some customers still check Yelp. If your page is unclaimed and has three 1-star reviews from 2018, you look dead.

**Seasonal targeting matters more than you think.** Concrete work is seasonal in most US markets. In Chicago, you're slow November through March. In Phoenix, you're slow July through August. Don't spend money advertising when you can't pour. Instead, run remarketing campaigns during off-seasons to stay top-of-mind. Offer early booking discounts for spring work. One client in Minneapolis offered 10% off for all projects booked and deposited before March 15. He booked $34,000 in spring work during January and February. That cash flow kept his crew employed and his equipment paid for.

## How Reviews Actually Impact Your Concrete Business

Reviews are not just "nice to have." They directly determine whether you get a call or get ignored. Here's what I've seen working with concrete companies across the US.

**The number of reviews matters more than the average rating.** A company with 4.8 stars and 12 reviews loses to a company with 4.5 stars and 80 reviews almost every time. Volume signals trust and legitimacy. Customers want to know you've done this before, more than once.

I worked with a concrete contractor in Chicago who had 22 reviews and a 4.9 average. A competitor had 137 reviews and a 4.4 average. Guess who got more calls? The competitor. Why? Because 137 people vouching for you beats 22 people every time, even with a slightly lower score.

**Here's the specific ask that works:** When you finish a job and collect final payment, send follow-up text within 24 hours. "Hey [name], we finished your driveway today. If you're happy with the work, could you leave us a quick Google review? Here's the link: [GBP link]." That's it. No pressure. No complicated process.

One client in Charlotte did this consistently for six months. They went from 15 reviews to 94. Their close rate on estimates went from about 30% to about 50%. The owner told me, "People mention the reviews at every single estimate now. I don't even have to sell anymore."

**Negative reviews are not the end of the world.** You will eventually get a bad review. It happens. The worst thing you can do is ignore it or argue publicly. Here's what to do: respond within 48 hours. Apologize for their experience. Offer to make it right offline. "We're sorry the job didn't meet your expectations. We'd like to come back and fix the issue at no charge. Please call [owner's name] directly at [number]."

I've seen this turn a 1-star review into an updated 4-star review more than a dozen times. Customers respect transparency. They also watch how you handle problems. If you argue or deflect, you look like someone they don't want to hire.

**Review generation tools are worth the money.** I've used GatherUp and Birdeye with concrete clients. They automate the review request process and make it easy. Cost is roughly $100-200/month. One client in Atlanta generated 50+ reviews in three months using GatherUp. They calculated that each new review was worth roughly $400 in closed business. That's $20,000 in revenue from a $600 investment.

## Running Google Local Services Ads for Concrete Companies

If you're a concrete contractor and you're not on Google Local Services Ads (LSAs), you're leaving money on the table. I'm not exaggerating.

**LSAs are different from regular Google Ads.** They appear at the very top of search results, above regular ads. You pay per lead instead of per click. Google vets your business, checks your license and insurance, and gives you a "Google Screened" badge. That badge alone increases trust dramatically.

I set up LSAs for a concrete company in Phoenix. They were already running regular Google Ads for $900/month and getting decent results. Adding LSAs cost an additional $300-400/month in lead costs but generated 15 additional calls per month. Those calls closed at a higher rate because the "Google Screened" badge removed the skepticism upfront.

**The catch:** You need to be licensed and insured. Google verifies this. They also run a background check on the business owner. If you're operating without proper licensing — and I know many small concrete crews do — you won't qualify. That's not my problem. Get licensed or accept that you're limiting your growth.

**Pricing structure matters.** LSAs use a bidding system. You set a maximum cost per lead. Google shows your ad based on your bid and your proximity to the searcher. Start with a lower bid ($20-30 per lead) and increase as you see what converts. One client in Dallas started at $25 per lead, got 8 leads in the first week, closed 3 of them. That's $200 in lead cost for roughly $12,000 in signed work. He raised his bid to $35 after that.

**Respond to leads immediately.** LSAs track your response time. If you don't respond within 24 hours, your ad performance drops. I tell clients to set up notifications on their phone and call every lead within 30 minutes. The contractor who calls first often gets the job. I've seen companies lose bids because they waited three hours to return a voicemail.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a website, or can I just use Facebook and Google My Business?**

You need a website. Google My Business is critical, but you still need a place where customers can see your full portfolio, read about your process, and get a sense of who you are. Facebook pages look unprofessional for service businesses. A simple one-page site with 10-15 photos costs $500-1,000 to build and pays for itself on the first job.

**Q: How much should I spend on marketing each month?**

Start with $500-1,000/month total. Split it: $300-500 on Google Ads or LSAs, $200-300 on your GBP maintenance and review generation, and the rest on a simple website hosting and maybe some targeted Facebook ads. Do that for three months. Track every lead and every dollar. If you're not getting at least 3x return on ad spend, adjust or stop.

**Q: What if I get a bad review from a customer who was unreasonable?**

Respond politely and professionally anyway. Other customers will see your response. I've seen a contractor in Nashville get a 1-star review from someone who demanded free work. The owner responded calmly explaining what happened. That response got 47 helpful clicks. The customer looked unreasonable. The contractor looked professional. He got three new clients from people who read that exchange.

**Q: Should I offer discounts to get more reviews?**

No. Never pay for reviews. It's against Google's terms and it devalues the feedback. Instead, ask for reviews as part of your standard finish process. I mentioned the 24-hour text method above. That works better than offering $50 off because it gets real, unbiased feedback.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from online marketing?**

If you do everything right — good GBP, targeted ads, consistent reviews — you should see calls within the first week. Real pipeline-building takes 60-90 days. The first month is testing. The second month is optimizing. By month three, you should have predictable lead flow or you need to change your approach.

**Q: Do I need to be on Instagram? I don't have time for social media.**

Only if you take good photos. A concrete company in Austin posts one finished project photo per week on Instagram. That's it. No stories, no reels, no hashtag strategy. Those photos get shared in local Facebook groups and generate 3-4 leads per month on their own. But if you're not taking good photos, skip Instagram entirely. Bad photos hurt more than no photos.

Just showing up where customers are looking.
