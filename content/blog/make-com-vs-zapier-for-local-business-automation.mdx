## What is Make.com?
Make.com, formerly known as Integromat, is a powerful automation platform that lets you connect over 1,000 apps—everything from Shopify and Mailchimp to QuickBooks and Google Sheets—into visual, drag‑and‑drop workflows. With Make.com you can automate data entry, email marketing, and social media posting, freeing up more time for high‑leverage activities. Its built‑in routers and iterators enable complex branching logic, so you can, for example, pull a CSV of inventory, filter out out‑of‑stock items, and automatically generate purchase orders in QuickBooks—all in a single scenario.

## What is Zapier?
Zapier is another popular automation tool that enables you to integrate different web applications, automating tasks and workflows across multiple platforms. Zapier offers a wide range of pre‑built integrations, known as "Zaps," making it easy to get started with automation. Whether you run a coffee shop or a fitness studio, Zapier can help you save time and increase efficiency by connecting your favorite tools—like posting a new Instagram photo whenever you add a product to your Shopify store.

<Callout type="tip">When choosing between Make.com and Zapier, consider the specific needs of your business. If you require more advanced automation features and customization, Make.com might be the better choice. However, if you prefer a more user-friendly interface and a wider range of pre-built integrations, Zapier could be the way to go.</Callout>

## Comparing Make.com and Zapier
Both Make.com and Zapier offer robust automation features, but there are some key differences to consider. Make.com is known for its advanced customization options and flexibility, while Zapier excels at providing a vast library of pre-built integrations. In terms of pricing, Make.com offers a more tiered pricing structure, with plans starting at $9 per month, whereas Zapier's plans start at $19.99 per month.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Automating the Wrong Step and Making Things Worse

A pet grooming studio in Portland, Oregon, was drowning in no-shows. The owner, Maria, had read about automation and decided to set up a Zapier zap that automatically sent a confirmation email 24 hours before each booking. Sounded smart. Booking software was Booksy, email was Mailchimp. She set it up in 20 minutes.

No-shows went *up*.

Why? She’d automated the confirmation message but hadn’t disabled Booksy’s own built-in reminder. Clients were getting two identical emails — one from Booksy, one from Zapier — and started ignoring both. The second email felt like spam. Her no-show rate jumped from 12% to 19% in two weeks. She lost about $480 in missed appointments during that period (average groom $60, 8 additional no-shows).

**The fix:** Audit existing triggers before layering on new ones. I’ve seen this at three different clients — someone sets up a beautiful Make.com scenario that runs alongside a default notification from Square or Vagaro, and customers get triple messages. The fix is almost always: turn off the native reminders first. Or use the native tool alone, because it’s already fine.

Maria disabled Booksy’s original reminder, kept her Zapier confirmation, and added a second follow-up for reviews. No-show rate dropped to 8%. That’s $264 in saved revenue per month she was leaving on the table.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring the “What If It Breaks” Scenario

A coffee shop in Nashville — two locations — decided to use Make.com to automate inventory alerts. When a certain syrup or cup size hit a threshold, it would email the supplier and log a purchase order in QuickBooks. Great idea. The owner, Tom, spent an afternoon building the scenario. It worked perfectly in testing.

Two weeks later, he ran out of oat milk on a Saturday morning. The scenario had been running fine, but on Wednesday the supplier’s email system bounced — their server changed something. Make.com didn’t tell Tom. It simply marked the action as “error” and stopped. Tom assumed everything was fine because he didn’t check the dashboard. Lost sales that weekend: roughly $1,200. Plus the annoyance of telling 30 customers they couldn’t have a latte.

**The fix:** Always set up an error notification. In Make.com, you can add a second path that sends you a text message or a Slack alert if any step fails. Zapier has similar features. Most people skip this because they’re excited about the automation. Don’t. Also, build in a weekly sanity check — have the automation send you a summary: “This week I ran 47 times, failed 2 times, here’s what happened.” Tom now has that and says it’s saved him three separate disasters since.

The uncomfortable truth: automation doesn’t eliminate the need for oversight. It shifts where you need to pay attention.

### Mistake #3: Underestimating the Learning Curve and Giving Up

A fitness studio in Denver — owner named Jenna — wanted to automate her new client follow-up sequence. She signed up for Zapier, connected Mindbody and Mailchimp, and tried to set up a “new client booked a class” trigger. Three hours later, she had a zap that only worked half the time, sent duplicate welcome emails, and she was frustrated enough to swear off automation entirely.

She spent the next six months manually copying names from Mindbody into a spreadsheet and sending individual emails. That’s about 5 hours per week. At her own billing rate (she values her time at $75/hour when she could be coaching or marketing), that’s $375 per week in lost opportunity cost. Over six months, roughly $9,000 in essentially wasted time.

**The fix:** Start with the simplest possible workflow. Jenna should have started with a single-step zap: “When new client added in Mindbody, send welcome email from Mailchimp.” That’s a 5-minute setup. Once that worked for a week, she could add a second step: “Wait 3 days, send follow-up email with referral link.” Then a third: “After 2 weeks, send request to leave a Google review.”

Most small business owners try to build the Eiffel Tower on day one and end up with a pile of spaghetti. You don’t need branching logic and routers and iterators for a 30-client email list. You need a reliable simple chain. Once you trust the machine, add complexity.

Jenna started over with a single Zapier zap. It took 8 minutes. She now runs a sequence of four emails and it’s cost her $30/month in Zapier fees. She reclaimed 5 hours per week. That’s a 24:1 return on her time.

### Mistake #4: Overpaying for Features You Don’t Need

A hair salon in Chicago (different from the Austin example — this is a different owner, Lucia) signed up for Make.com’s Pro plan at $99/month because she thought she needed the higher task limits and premium apps. She was using exactly two apps: Square Appointments and Gmail. That’s it. No routers, no iterators, no custom webhooks. She was paying $99 to send appointment reminders and thank-you emails to about 80 clients per month.

Her total automation needs fit comfortably inside Make.com’s free plan (1,000 operations per month). She would have paid $0. Instead she spent $1,188 over the course of a year before her accountant asked about the recurring charge.

**The fix:** Start with the free tier. Both Make.com and Zapier have generous free plans for small volumes. Make.com’s free plan gives you 1,000 operations per month — that’s enough for a solo operator sending confirmations, follow-ups, and review requests. Zapier’s free plan is 100 tasks per month, which is tight but workable for a micro-business. Only upgrade when you hit the limit and the business case is clear.

Lucia switched to the free plan, saved $99/month, and her automation didn’t miss a beat. She used the extra money to run a Google Ads campaign that brought in 12 new clients.

## When to Pick Make.com Over Zapier (And Vice Versa)

The short answer: it depends on how you think. But let me give you something more useful than that.

### Make.com Is Better When…

You need complex logic. If your automation involves conditional branches — “if client spent over $100, put them in loyalty list; if under, put them in re-engagement” — Make.com’s visual editor makes that far easier to build and debug. I’ve built scenarios that route data through four different paths based on customer behavior. In Zapier, that requires nested paths and a lot of clicking. In Make.com, you just drag lines.

You also deal with data transformations. Make.com has built-in functions to parse JSON, reformat dates, split text, and perform calculations. For example, a pet sitter in Austin wanted to calculate a service fee based on the number of pets plus a distance surcharge. Zapier would have required a separate webhook or a premium app. Make.com did it with a few formula fields.

You’re on a tight budget. For high-volume automation (think thousands of tasks per month), Make.com’s pricing is significantly cheaper. At 10,000 operations per month, Zapier’s Team plan is $69/month. Make.com’s Pro plan is $99/month but gives you 15,000 operations. For 50,000 operations, Zapier is $119/month (Starter plan for teams), Make.com is $189/month for the Teams plan with 50,000 ops. It scales differently — you need to compare your specific usage.

### Zapier Is Better When…

You want something that just works. Zapier has a wider app directory (over 6,000 apps to Make.com’s 1,500+). For local businesses, that means you can connect obscure POS systems, reservation tools, and industry-specific CRMs. A brewery in San Diego needed to connect a niche inventory system called BrewTarget with Mailchimp. Zapier had a direct integration. Make.com did not.

You don’t want to think about logic. Zapier’s interface is linear: when this happens, do that. It’s hard to mess up. For a simple “new Square transaction → add to Google Sheets” zap, Zapier is perfect. I’ve set these up in under two minutes during a phone call with a client. Make.com’s visual canvas can be overwhelming if you just want to send a confirmation email.

You need reliability at the expense of flexibility. Zapier has been around longer and their error handling is more straightforward. When something breaks, Zapier emails you immediately. Make.com’s error handling is more powerful but requires configuration. For business owners who can’t babysit their automations, Zapier’s simpler fail-safes are usually the better choice.

### A Real-World Decision

I helped a chiropractor in Denver choose between the two. She used Jane (a practice management tool) and wanted to:
- Send a booking confirmation
- Send a reminder 24 hours before
- Send a follow-up after the visit with a survey
- Auto-generate a receipt in QuickBooks

Zapier had a direct integration with Jane for the trigger, and the receipts could be done with a QuickBooks zap. Total: three zaps, two minutes each. Cost: $19.99/month on Zapier’s Starter plan. She was done in 20 minutes and hasn’t touched it since.

If she had needed conditional logic (e.g., “if patient didn’t book a follow-up after 7 days, send a special offer”), Make.com would have been better. But she didn’t. She went with the simpler tool and saved herself the headache.

So: if your automation is a straight line, use Zapier. If it’s a tree, use Make.com.

## How to Choose the Right Triggers and Actions for Your Local Business

Most automation guides tell you “you can automate anything.” That’s not helpful. Here are the three highest-ROI automations I’ve seen work for coffee shops, hair salons, pet groomers, and fitness studios in 2025 and 2026.

### Booking Confirmations + Pre-Visit Reminders

This is the lowest-hanging fruit. A local bakery in Portland (the one from mistake #1? No, different bakery) used Square Appointments and had a 14% no-show rate. They set up a Make.com scenario that triggered when a booking was created: send a text confirmation immediately, then send an email reminder 24 hours before with a link to reschedule or cancel. No-shows dropped to 6%.

The financial impact: average order $35, 150 bookings per week. That’s 21 no-shows per week at 14%, or 9 at 6%. Difference: 12 saved appointments per week, or $420 in weekly revenue. Monthly: $1,680. Yearly: over $20,000. The automation took 30 minutes to set up.

**Trigger:** New booking in Square / Booksy / Vagaro  
**Action:** Send SMS (Twilio or built-in in Zapier) + email (Mailchimp or Gmail)  
**Pro tip:** Add a cancellation link. Fewer no-shows, and those who cancel can be replaced in real time.

### Post-Visit Review Requests

Most local businesses forget to ask for reviews. A pet groomer in Austin (yes, another one — Austin is full of automated groomers apparently) was stuck with 12 Google reviews after two years in business. She set up a Zapier zap that triggered 24 hours after an invoice was marked paid in Square. It sent a text message with a direct link to her Google Business profile. She also added a filter: only send if the transaction was over $40 (her sweet spot for high-value customers who were more likely to write a good review).

Within 90 days, she had 48 new Google reviews. Her average rating stayed at 4.8 stars. She told me her call volume from new customers increased noticeably — she attributed about 30% of that directly to the new reviews. Hard to quantify exactly, but she added roughly $800/month in new recurring clients during that period.

**Trigger:** Invoice paid in Square / Stripe / PayPal  
**Action:** Send SMS or email with review link  
**Pro tip:** Use a URL shortener and UTM parameters so you can track which reviews came from the automation.

### Low Inventory Alerts

I mentioned the Nashville coffee shop earlier, but let me give a better example. A smoothie bar in Chicago used Make.com to monitor their Square inventory for three key ingredients: frozen berries, protein powder, and coconut water. When any item dropped below a set threshold, the system sent a text to the owner AND emailed the supplier with a pre-written order. The owner told me it saved him about 45 minutes per week in manual inventory checks. More importantly, he stopped running out of protein powder on Monday mornings (the busiest day). Lost sales from out-of-stocks dropped by 80%. Estimated recovery: $600/month.

**Trigger:** Stock count update in Square / Shopify / other POS  
**Condition:** If quantity < threshold  
**Action:** Send SMS + email with purchase order template

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I try both tools for free before committing?**
Yes. Make.com’s free plan gives you 1,000 operations per month — no time limit, so you can test as long as you want. Zapier’s free plan gives you 100 tasks per month. Both let you explore the interface and set up basic workflows without a credit card. I recommend building the same simple automation in both to see which feels more intuitive. Most people decide within 20 minutes.

**Q: What if my local business only has 10–20 customers a month? Is automation worth it?**
Probably not with a paid plan. But the free tiers of both tools can handle tiny volumes. If you’re sending two booking confirmations and one follow-up per day, that’s about 90 operations per month — comfortably inside Make.com’s free plan. The time saved is minimal (maybe 15 minutes per week), but the consistency is valuable. A single missed reminder that leads to a no-show could cost you $30–$80 depending on your business. So yes, worth setting up, but don’t pay for it.

**Q: Will automation make my emails look spammy?**
Only if you set it up badly. Use plain text, your real business name, and a clear sender address. Avoid “Dear Valued Customer” templates. I’ve seen businesses use the exact same wording they would type manually — the customer has no idea it’s automated. If you send a text, don’t include links to “opt out” unless you have a legitimate SMS marketing consent. Short, direct messages work best.

**Q: What happens if the automation breaks while I’m on vacation?**
If you’re using Zapier, you’ll get an email notification of the error (by default). With Make.com, you need to set up that notification manually — I showed how above. But even if you do nothing, the automation simply stops. You won’t lose data; the next step just won’t execute. When you get back, you’ll see a list of failed operations. The real risk is that you’re missing new leads or confirmations during that time. For critical automations (booking confirmations), I recommend setting up a backup manual process — like checking your booking app once a day even if automated. That’s not defeat; that’s common sense.

**Q: Can I integrate with Yelp?**
Yelp is famously closed — it doesn’t offer native webhook triggers or actions in either Make.com or Zapier. However, you can automate *responding* to Yelp reviews by using a third-party tool like ReviewTrackers that sends a webhook to your zap or scenario. Or you could set up a Make.com scenario that periodically checks a Google Sheet where you manually paste new Yelp reviews, then triggers an email to you. Not perfect, but it works. Yelp wants you to use their own tools, so automation is limited.

**Q: Will automation replace my staff?**
No. It replaces the boring, repetitive stuff that your staff hates doing anyway. A hair salon owner I worked with in Denver had her front desk person spending an hour each morning calling to confirm appointments. After automation, that hour turned into 20 minutes of handling special requests and building client relationships. The employee was happier and the salon retained her. Automation kills busywork, not jobs.
