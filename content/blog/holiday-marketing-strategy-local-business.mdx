**Developing a Winning Holiday Marketing Strategy**

To stand out from the competition and maximize sales during the holiday season, you need a solid marketing strategy in place. This includes a mix of online and offline tactics that will drive foot traffic, increase sales, and keep your customers engaged.

### Step 1: Review Your Holiday Marketing Goals

Before you start planning your holiday marketing strategy, it's essential to define your goals. What do you want to achieve during the holiday season? Do you want to increase sales, drive more foot traffic to your store, or strengthen your brand reputation? Having clear goals will help you create a focused and effective marketing strategy.

<Callout type="tip">Set specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals for your holiday marketing strategy.</Callout>

### Step 2: Optimize Your Online Presence

Your online presence is crucial during the holiday season, as it's often the first point of contact for potential customers. Ensure that your website, social media, and Google Business Profile are up-to-date and accurately represent your business.

<Callout type="warning">Don't forget to include holiday-themed content and offers on your website and social media channels to keep customers engaged and informed.</Callout>

### Step 3: Leverage Email Marketing and SMS

Email marketing and SMS can be powerful tools for driving sales and engaging customers during the holiday season. Use them to send targeted promotions, exclusive offers, and reminders about upcoming events.

<Callout type="example">For example, a local pet groomer could send an email campaign with discounts on holiday-themed pet grooming services, such as a "Pup's Christmas Pamper" package.</Callout>

### Step 4: Run Targeted Ads

Targeted ads can help you reach new customers and drive sales during the holiday season. Use platforms like Google Ads and Meta Ads to target customers based on their interests, behaviors, and demographics.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Generic Facebook Ads to a Cold Audience

A boutique gift shop in Portland, Oregon spent $1,500 in November on a Facebook ad campaign featuring a generic holiday graphic with "Shop Local This Season" plastered across it. They targeted a 50-mile radius, all adults 25-65. The result? A 0.3% click-through rate. They sold exactly zero gift cards. The owner told me she felt like she'd thrown the money into a fireplace.

**What went wrong:** The ad looked like every other holiday ad running in Portland. No specific offer. No urgency. No reason for someone to click instead of scrolling past. The audience was so broad that the algorithm had no idea who to show it to.

**The fix:** I restructured the campaign around one specific product — a $25 mystery gift box — with a clear deadline. We narrowed the audience to people within 5 miles who had shown interest in local shopping or competing stores. We ran the ad for ten days, not thirty. Total budget: $400 instead of $1,500.

**The outcome:** The gift shop sold 152 mystery boxes in those ten days. That's $3,800 in revenue from a $400 ad spend. Not bad for two weeks of work. The owner has since canceled her automated "boosting" of every single post, which she admitted was bleeding about $200 a month for no measurable return.

### Mistake #2: Not Updating Holiday Hours on Google Business Profile

A hair salon in Austin, Texas had a $12,000 December every year. Then one year, they closed on Christmas Eve at 2 PM instead of 6 PM. They didn't update their Google Business Profile hours. Seven clients showed up between 3 PM and 5 PM to locked doors. Three of them left one-star reviews. Two of them never came back. The salon lost an estimated $1,200 in missed appointments that day alone, but the real cost was the reputation damage during the busiest week of the year.

**What went wrong:** The owner assumed a sign on the door was enough. It wasn't. Google serves your Business Profile hours to anyone searching "hair salon near me" or "Austin hair salon open today." If those hours are wrong, customers don't try again — they go to the next result.

**The fix:** Set up holiday hours in Google Business Profile six weeks before Christmas. This takes about four minutes. Add special hours for any day you're closing early or opening late. Then write a Google Post announcing the schedule change. The salon also added a text message to their Booksy booking confirmations that read: "We're closing at 2 PM on Christmas Eve. Reschedule by Dec 22 if you need a different time."

**The outcome:** No more locked-door complaints. The salon saw a 97% on-time arrival rate for appointments during Christmas week. Their December revenue hit $13,800 — higher than any previous year — because they also captured overflow from competitors who weren't answering phones or had confusing schedules.

### Mistake #3: Sending One Generic Email to Your Whole List

A pet groomer in Nashville sent a single December email to all 1,400 subscribers: "Happy Holidays! Book your grooming appointment now!" Open rate: 12%. Unsubscribe rate: 4%. The owner said she felt like she'd just annoyed a chunk of her customer base for no reason.

**What went wrong:** She sent the same message to someone who had come in three weeks ago and someone who hadn't visited in eighteen months. The first group needed a reminder. The second group needed a reason to come back. Neither group got what they actually needed.

**The fix:** Split the list into three segments: customers who visited in the last 60 days, customers who hadn't visited in 6-12 months, and customers who hadn't visited in over a year. Each got a different email:
- Active customers: "Your regular appointment window is coming up — book your December slot before they fill."
- Lapsed 6-12 months: "We miss you. Here's a $10 off coupon for any grooming service before Dec 31."
- Lapsed 12+ months: "We've updated our grooming packages. First visit back is 20% off."

**The outcome:** Open rate jumped to 29%. The coupon redemption rate hit 18% for the lapsed segments. Total revenue from that single email campaign: $2,400. The groomer now uses Mailchimp's free tier and segments every email she sends. She told me she wishes she'd done this three years ago. I don't disagree.

## Holiday Email Sequences That Actually Get Opened

Most small business owners send one holiday email during the first week of December and then disappear until January. That's a mistake, and I've seen it cost businesses thousands in repeat sales.

A coffee shop in Denver started running a three-email holiday sequence through Mailchimp at the end of November. Here's what they sent:

**Email 1 (Nov 28):** "Your Holiday Coffee Gift Packs Are Available Now" — This was an early access announcement with a photo of the actual product. No discount. Just scarcity: "We make 200 of these and they sell out by Dec 10 every year."

**Email 2 (Dec 10):** "Last Call for Gift Packs" — The coffee shop had sold 160 by this point. This email showed remaining inventory with a countdown. It also included a "buy one for yourself, get one for a friend" deal.

**Email 3 (Dec 18):** "We're Still Roasting, But These Ship By Dec 22" — Last-minute option for procrastinators. Also included digital gift card options for anyone who had missed the physical deadline.

The coffee shop had 800 subscribers on their list. Total cost of the email software: $0 (Mailchimp free tier). Total revenue from these three emails: $5,600. The owner admitted she had been worried about "emailing too much." I told her that's the same worry that keeps a lot of small businesses from making money they're leaving on the table.

The emails also drove in-store traffic. About 40% of the gift pack purchases were for in-store pickup, which meant those customers bought a drink while they were there. That's another $12-15 per visit that never shows up in your email analytics.

If you're using Square, you can pull customer purchase history directly into Mailchimp or Klaviyo without any manual exporting. This takes about 30 minutes to set up and then runs itself. The coffee shop owner spent that time while closing up one evening and hasn't touched the automation since.

## Paid Search for Local Businesses During the Holidays

A lot of small business owners tell me they tried Google Ads once and "it didn't work." When I dig into it, the campaign was usually set up wrong — broad match keywords, no location targeting, a generic landing page. That's not Google Ads failing. That's the setup failing.

A pet grooming business in Chicago had $300 to spend on holiday advertising. Instead of Facebook, where most of their local competitors were fighting for attention, I had them try a Google Search campaign targeting very specific phrases: "dog grooming Christmas Chicago," "pet grooming holiday appointments," and "Chicago dog groomer open December."

**Why this worked:** Someone searching those phrases is actively looking to book. They're not scrolling through TikTok. They have a credit card out and a problem to solve. The click-through rate on these ads was 8%, which is high for local search. The cost per click averaged $2.40. That meant $300 bought about 125 clicks.

**Conversion rate:** 14% of those clicks booked an appointment. That's 17 new clients from a $300 ad spend. Their average ticket is $85 per grooming session. So that's $1,445 in revenue. Plus, about half of those clients booked a second appointment before they left, adding another $722.

Total return on a $300 investment? About $2,167. That's a 7x return. The owner now runs Google Ads year-round but increases the budget from $200 to $500 during November and December. She tracks everything through a simple spreadsheet and her Square dashboard. No fancy attribution software required.

A few things that mattered for this campaign:
- Location targeting was set to a 5-mile radius around the shop — not the whole city
- Ad copy included the price for a standard grooming session ($85)
- The landing page was the booking page, not the homepage
- Negative keywords excluded "cheap," "discount," and "DIY"

If you're setting this up in Google Ads for the first time, start with phrase match keywords only. Broad match will burn through your budget showing your ad to people searching for "Christmas ornaments" because your keywords have the word "Christmas" in them. I have seen this happen at least four times. It hurts every time.

## Local SEO: The Free Traffic You're Leaving on the Table

I mentioned the Google Business Profile hours mistake earlier, but there's more you can do there that costs nothing except time.

A fitness studio in Nashville focused on three things in November and saw their December foot traffic increase by 34% without spending a dime on advertising.

**1. Holiday-specific Google Posts:** They created a Google Post every Thursday in November announcing a new holiday class schedule. Each post had a photo, a call-to-action button ("Book Now"), and a deadline. Google posts show up directly in search results and take about five minutes to create. Most local businesses ignore this feature entirely.

**2. Holiday-related Q&A:** They searched for the most common questions people ask about fitness studios during the holidays in Nashville: "Are you open on Christmas?", "Do you have New Year's Day classes?", "Can I buy a gift membership?" They answered each one on their Google Business Profile Q&A section. Those answers now show up in search results when someone asks those questions.

**3. Category and attribute updates:** They added "holiday hours available" and "gift cards" as attributes in their Google Business Profile. They also updated their primary category from "Gym" to "Fitness Center" when they noticed competitors using that category were ranking higher for local searches.

The total time invested: About two hours over four weeks. The result: Their Google Business Profile impressions went from 2,100 in November to 3,800 in December. Website clicks from Google search went up 40%. Class bookings from first-time visitors increased by 28%.

A yoga studio across the street didn't do any of this. Their December traffic was flat compared to November. The difference wasn't their classes or their prices — it was showing up in the places customers actually looked.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I have a $500 monthly marketing budget. How much should I spend on holiday-specific advertising?**

Put $200 into Google Ads targeting local search terms related to your business during the holidays. Put $150 into Facebook or Instagram ads with a specific offer and a deadline. Keep $150 for anything unexpected — a last-minute promotion, a local event sponsorship, or extra inventory. Do not spend the entire $500 on one channel. I've seen a bakery spend $500 on Facebook ads alone and get 0 sales because nobody searches for "holiday cookies near me" on Facebook — they search Google.

**Q: Is Google Business Profile really worth updating? I haven't touched mine in two years.**

Yes, and I say this with the full weight of having watched a pizzeria in Philadelphia lose $4,000 in December because their Google Business Profile still said "temporarily closed" from a 2020 COVID closure. Update your hours, add holiday photos, and post once a week. It takes less time than it takes to drink one of the coffees I'm currently drinking at 9:47 PM.

**Q: Should I offer holiday discounts, or does that cheapen my brand?**

Offer something specific, not a blanket percentage off. A bakery that offers "20% off everything" looks desperate. The same bakery that offers "Free dozen cookies with any December catering order over $75" looks strategic. One devalues your product. The other drives a larger average order. The second approach generates about 35% more revenue per customer based on what I've seen across six different food-service clients.

**Q: What's the most underrated marketing tool for local businesses during the holidays?**

Text message. Not email. Not social media. SMS open rates average 98%. Most small businesses don't use it at all. A hair salon in Denver sent one text message to their list of 400 clients on December 15: "We have five cancellation spots on Dec 23. First to text back gets them." All five spots filled within 12 minutes. The salon used a free trial of SimpleTexting. They've since converted to a $25/month plan.

**Q: I don't have an email list. Is it too late to start one in December?**

No, but you're in a hurry. Put a sign-up sheet at your register or a tablet at the counter. Offer a $5 discount or a free add-on for joining. Start collecting today. Even 50 emails are enough to send a "last-minute gift cards available" message that could bring in $500-1,000 in revenue. A coffee shop in Austin started collecting emails on December 10 and had 80 subscribers by December 20. They sent one email about gift card bundles and sold 45 of them in two days.

**Q: How do I measure if my holiday marketing is actually working?**

Pick one number. Just one. If you're a hair salon, it's appointments booked. If you're a coffee shop, it's gift cards sold. If you're a pet groomer, it's new clients acquired. Track that number for three weeks before the holidays and then for three weeks during. Everything else is noise. I've watched business owners drown in vanity metrics like social media likes while their actual revenue stayed flat. Don't be that person.
