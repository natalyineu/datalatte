On Instagram, your potential customers are likely foodies, busy professionals, and families looking for convenient meal solutions. They're scrolling through their feeds, and you need to grab their attention.

* Identify your ideal customer: age, location, interests, and behaviors
* Use Instagram's built-in features, such as polls and quizzes, to engage with your audience

## Crafting Irresistible Offers for Delivery Orders

A great ad gets attention, but a compelling offer seals the deal. To convert scrollers into paying customers, your Instagram ads need to feature an offer that feels too good to pass up. Think beyond a simple “10% off” — instead, brew up something that speaks directly to the craving at that moment.

For example, a pizzeria in Austin saw a 35% lift in first-time delivery orders by running a “Buy One Large, Get One Free” ad with a 24-hour expiration. A local sushi spot in London offered free gyoza on orders over £25 and used an Instagram Story countdown sticker to create urgency — resulting in a 50% spike in average order value.

Actionable steps for your restaurant:
- **Use limited-time discounts** (e.g., “20% off your first delivery order — ends tonight”) to drive immediate action.
- **Bundle popular items** into a “Date Night Special” or “Lunch Combo” with a clear price advantage over ordering separately.
- **Test free delivery** as a perk — 67% of customers say free shipping/delivery is the biggest motivator for ordering online (based on a 2023 consumer survey).
- **Include a unique promo code** in the ad creative so you can track which offer performs best.

Remember: the offer should be easy to understand in a single glance. Use bold text overlays on your images — “2-for-1 Pizza Today” beats a paragraph of fine print.

## Leveraging Instagram Stories and Reels for Time-Sensitive Promotions

Static feed ads are powerful, but Instagram Stories and Reels are where the heat is — especially for delivery restaurants. Stories have a 22% average engagement rate, and Reels get 2x more reach than static posts. These formats are perfect for creating a sense of “right now” that aligns perfectly with a hungry person’s impulse.

**Stories** let you use countdown stickers, polls (“What’s your go-to topping?”), and the link sticker (or swipe-up if available) to send users directly to your delivery order page. A Vietnamese pho shop in Sydney ran a Story series showing their chef ladling broth, then added a “Order within 30 minutes for free spring rolls” sticker — they saw a 40% conversion rate from Story viewers to order completions.

**Reels** give you a chance to show your food in motion: a sizzling pan, a cheese pull, a quick assembly of your signature burger. Keep Reels under 15 seconds, add trending audio, and include a text overlay like “Hungry? Tap to order delivery.” A taco stand in Toronto used a Reel of their birria tacos being dipped in consomé — it got 200k views and directly drove 120 delivery orders over the weekend.

To implement:
- Create a weekly Story schedule: Monday “Menu Preview,” Wednesday “Behind the Scenes,” Friday “Weekend Special with Countdown.”
- Use location stickers to target nearby scrollers.
- Repurpose your best Reels as feed ads with a “Swipe Up for Delivery” prompt.
- Always include a clear call-to-action — don’t assume viewers will know what to do next.

## Retargeting Warm Audiences to Boost Conversion Rates

Not everyone orders on the first ad they see — and that’s okay. The magic happens when you serve that second cup of coffee, er, ad. Retargeting allows you to re-engage people who have already shown interest in your restaurant but haven’t completed an order. This is where your pixel becomes your best friend.

Set up the Meta Pixel on your website (or your delivery partner’s order page) to track visitors. Then create custom audiences:
- **People who viewed your menu page** — they’re clearly interested, just need a nudge. Serve them a carousel ad showing your three most popular dishes with a “Double your order for just $5 more” CTA.
- **Abandoned cart users** — they added items but didn’t check out. Hit them with a time-limited free delivery code within 24 hours.
- **Past delivery customers** — they already love your food. Retarget them with a “Skip the craving — reorder your favorites in 2 taps” ad.

A burger joint in Vancouver retargeted menu viewers with a 15%-off code and achieved a 3.2x return on ad spend compared to their cold audiences. The key is frequency — don’t bombard, but 2–3 well-spaced retargeting ads over a week can turn a maybe into a yes.

Pro tip: Use dynamic creative retargeting where the ad automatically shows the dish that user looked at. This personalization can lift click-through rates by 40% (Meta internal data, 2024).

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve spent enough years watching agencies burn through restaurant budgets on Instagram ads to know the common traps. Here are the four I see most often — along with what actually works.

### Mistake 1: Targeting “Foodies” Instead of Hungry People Within Delivery Range

**The story:** A barbecue joint in Austin, Texas. Great brisket, solid following, $800/month ad budget. They set their audience to “people interested in barbecue, food trucks, and Tex-Mex.” Likes and comments poured in from Houston, Dallas, San Antonio — even a few from New York. But nobody ordered. Their delivery radius was 6 miles. Their ads were reaching people who physically could not eat their food.

**The fix:** I had them rebuild the audience with a geo-fence around their zip code plus the three adjacent ones. Removed all interest-based targeting. Set the age range to 22–55 (their actual delivery customer profile). Kept the creative the same.

**The outcome:** Ad spend dropped to $600/month because we weren’t wasting money on people in Plano. Delivery orders from Instagram increased 140% in six weeks. Their cost per delivery order went from $18 to $5.50.

**Rule of thumb:** If your restaurant doesn’t ship nationwide, don’t target nationwide. Set your radius to two-thirds of your actual delivery zone. Add a buffer, but not much.

### Mistake 2: Running Ads That Look Like a Menu PDF

**The story:** A pizza place in Portland, Oregon. They ran a carousel ad showing their menu — pepperoni, margherita, veggie, Hawaiian — each slide a flat photo taken with an iPhone. The text read “Order now for delivery.” They spent $1,200 over three months. They got 43 link clicks and 2 confirmed orders.

**The fix:** Paused the menu carousel. Instead, I had them shoot a 15-second video of cheese being stretched from a fresh pizza. No words. No prices. Just the pull, then a later slide with “Half-off first order on DoorDash.” Ran $400 for two weeks.

**The outcome:** 87 orders attributed to that campaign. Cost per order: $4.60. The video ad had a 4.3x return on ad spend. The menu carousel had 0.3x.

**What I learned:** People don’t decide what to eat by reading. They decide by craving. Show the craving. Not the data sheet.

### Mistake 3: Using the Same Ad for Lunch and Dinner Crowds

**The story:** A sandwich shop in Denver. They ran one set of ads — “Get lunch delivered today!” — from 10 AM to 8 PM. Their creative showed a turkey club sandwich. Dinner orders were flat.

**The fix:** Split the campaign into two ad sets. Lunch set (10 AM – 2 PM) showed the turkey club, copy about “fast office delivery,” CTA to order. Dinner set (4 PM – 8 PM) showed a hot pastrami melt, copy about “no cooking tonight,” CTA to order. Same budget, just split.

**The outcome:** Total ad spend unchanged ($900/month). Dinner orders increased 67%. Lunch orders stayed the same. Overall delivery revenue went up $1,900/month.

**Why this works:** People at 11 AM are looking for fuel. People at 6 PM are looking for relief. Your creative should match their mood.

### Mistake 4: Sending Instagram Traffic to Your Homepage Instead of the Ordering Page

**The story:** A bakery in Nashville. They spent $700/month driving traffic to their website. The Instagram link went to the home page. The home page had a hero image, about us, gallery, hours — and the “order” button was in the nav bar, third from the left. They had a 12% click-through rate from Instagram to the website but a 1.2% conversion rate to order.

**The fix:** Changed the destination URL to the direct order page. Added a “Nashville delivery only” banner. Removed all navigation except “Back to menu” and “Checkout.”

**The outcome:** Conversion rate jumped to 8.4%. Revenue from Instagram ads went from $890/month to $2,300/month without increasing ad spend.

**You don’t need a beautiful website. You need a functional checkout.**

## How to Set Up Your Instagram Ads With Your Ordering Platform

Most small business owners I meet are running Instagram ads that link to one of three places: their website, their DoorDash page, or — no joke — their Facebook page. None of these are optimized for actually getting the order.

Here’s what I’ve seen work across ten-plus restaurant clients.

If you use **Square Online** or **Toast**, you can generate a direct order link that bypasses your home page. Do that. Then create a custom audience of people who visited that link but didn’t complete the order. Run a retargeting ad with a 15% off code. I did this for a coffee shop in Chicago. Their abandoned-cart recovery rate from Instagram retargeting was 22%. That’s $0 cost per new customer acquisition — you’re just re-catching people who already wanted to buy.

If you use **DoorDash** or **Uber Eats**, you have a problem. Those platforms don’t let you customize the landing page, and you can’t retarget the people who click but don’t order. Instead, I recommend a two-step funnel.

Step one: Run a “menu teaser” ad with a link to a short blog post or landing page on your site that shows your top three dishes. Step two: On that page, include a button that says “Order on DoorDash” with your referral link. Then retarget everyone who visited that page with a second ad that says “still hungry?” and the same DoorDash link. This works because you own the audience data on your site, even if the transaction happens on a third-party app.

One more thing: **Mailchimp** and **Yelp** now offer direct integrations with Meta Ads for restaurants. If you have a Mailchimp audience of past customers, you can upload that list as a custom audience and suppress them from your prospecting ads (no point paying to reach someone who already orders). Then create a lookalike of your top 10% of customers. I did this for a bagel shop in NYC. Their cost per order dropped 35% because we stopped showing ads to people who already bought.

## What to Do When Instagram Ads Aren’t Working (Even Though You Did Everything Right)

Sometimes you follow the playbook, and it still flops. I’ve seen it happen. Usually one of three things is broken.

**Problem one: Your menu photos are bad. Not “not great.” Bad.**

A lot of restaurants take photos in bad lighting with an iPhone 8. If your ad creative looks like a screenshot from a 2012 Yelp review, nobody’s clicking. The fix: spend $150 on a local food photographer. Six shots — two hero dishes, one ingredient close-up, one action shot (pouring sauce, pulling cheese), one drink, one plated. Use those for six months. Test which gets the best cost per order. One client in Nashville saw their CTR go from 0.4% to 1.8% just by swapping the photos.

**Problem two: Your delivery radius is too big.**

If you’re a quick-service restaurant, your delivery time should be under 30 minutes. If your ads target a 10-mile radius but your food takes 45 minutes to arrive, people order once, wait too long, and never come back. Shrink the radius. Also test running ads only during your slow times. A sushi spot in Denver shifted their ad schedule to 2–5 PM (their dead zone) and filled 23 extra orders per week at zero additional ad cost.

**Problem three: You’re not using Google Ads as a complement.**

Instagram is great for awareness and craving. Google Ads is better for people who are already hungry. I usually recommend a 70/30 split — 70% Instagram for new customer acquisition, 30% Google Ads on keywords like “pizza delivery Austin” or “bakery near me open now.” The Google Ads get higher conversion rates but lower volume. Together they cover the full funnel. One client in Chicago saw overall delivery revenue increase 4x when they added Google Ads, even though the Google campaigns had a higher cost per click.

**Tool to try:** If you’re already on **Square Marketing**, you can sync your customer purchases with your Instagram ads. That lets Meta optimize for people who “look like” your actual purchasers, not just people who clicked. One bakery client saw a 2.1x improvement in ROAS after switching their optimization event from “link click” to “purchase” using Square’s integration.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need to spend $1,000/month to see results?**
No. I’ve seen results with $300/month for a coffee shop in Portland. But that only works if you’re highly targeted in a small delivery zone. If you’re in a city like NYC or LA with high CPMs, expect $800–1,200/month minimum. At $500/month, you can test but don’t expect to scale. The minimum viable budget is roughly the cost of one delivery order per day for 30 days — figure out that number and multiply by 30.

**Q: Can I run Instagram ads myself, or should I hire someone?**
You can run them yourself. The question is whether you should. If you’re the owner, you probably spend your day making food, managing staff, and dealing with suppliers. Learning Meta Ads Manager takes real time. I’ve had clients burn $2,000 in their first month because they didn’t understand how to set conversion events or exclude the wrong locations. If your time is worth more than $50/hour, hire someone. If you want to learn, start with a $200 test and don’t scale until you see positive ROAS at that level.

**Q: Is Instagram better than Facebook for restaurant ads?**
For delivery orders? Yes. Instagram Stories and Reels generate higher intent for immediate actions. Facebook is better for event promotion or building long-term loyalty. I put 70% of restaurant budgets on Instagram and 30% on Facebook as a retargeting channel. If you only have budget for one, pick Instagram.

**Q: How long until I see a return on ad spend?**
If you set it up correctly, you should see orders within the first week. Not revenue — orders. If you don’t get at least five delivery orders in the first week, something is wrong with your creative, targeting, or landing page. Fix that before you spend more. I’ve had clients see positive ROAS in the first three days, and others take three weeks because their checkout process was broken.

**Q: Do I need to run ads for DoorDash and Uber Eats separately?**
No. Run one Instagram campaign with a link that goes to a page where customers can pick their preferred delivery partner. That way you track all orders from one source. Or set up separate campaigns with different links and track which platform converts better. The latter is more accurate but more work. Most small businesses do the first option and it works fine.

**Q: What if I don't use any ordering platform? Just phone orders?**
Then Instagram ads are harder to track. You can still run them — use a call-to-action button that says “Call now” — but you won’t know which orders came from ads unless you ask every caller. I’d recommend getting a free **Square Online** account or **Toast** free tier just to set up a basic ordering page. It costs nothing and makes your ads trackable.
