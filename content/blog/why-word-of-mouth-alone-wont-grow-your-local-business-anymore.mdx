## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating Every Negative Review Like a Personal Attack

I watched a salon owner in Nashville, Tennessee burn $1,200 a month on Google Ads and still lose customers. She had great targeting, decent copy, and a clean landing page. The problem was hiding in plain sight: her 2.8-star Google rating with fifteen unanswered one-star reviews.

She told me she "didn't want to argue with crazy people online." Fair instinct. But those reviews were the first thing potential clients saw after clicking her ad. One review said "stylist was 20 minutes late and didn't apologize." Another: "asked for a specific cut, got something completely different." No salon owner response. No attempt to make it right.

**What went wrong:** She treated her Google Business Profile like a vanity page instead of a conversion tool. Every unanswered negative review was a silent salesperson telling prospects "this business doesn't care."

**The fix:** I made her do three things. First, respond to every existing negative review within 48 hours — apologize, explain what happened, and offer a specific resolution (e.g., "Please call us at 615-xxx-xxxx for a complimentary corrective appointment"). Second, set up a simple text-message follow-up system through Square Appointments asking every client for a Google review after their visit. Third, train her front desk to ask happy clients in-person "would you mind leaving us a quick Google review?" and hand them a card with a QR code.

**The outcome:** Three months later, her rating climbed to 4.3 stars with 87 new reviews. Her ad click-to-call conversion rate went from 1.8% to 4.1%. She didn't increase her ad spend. She just stopped sabotaging it. Estimated annual revenue gain from that change alone: roughly $8,500 from appointments that came through Google Ads and direct searches.

### Mistake #2: Running Ads to Your Whole City When You Only Serve Three Neighborhoods

**The fix:** We rebuilt her campaign around a 2-mile radius around her shop. That's roughly a 10-minute walk or a 5-minute drive. We excluded any area across the Willamette River. We added location extensions so her address showed in the ad. We set her budget to $300/month — $150 less than she was spending — and allocated it only to peak hours (7 AM–10 AM weekdays, 8 AM–12 PM weekends).

**The outcome:** Her cost per click dropped from $3.80 to $1.10. Her conversion rate went from 0.9% to 4.2%. She started getting 12–15 calls per week from people who said they saw her ad and walked over. She saved $1,800 a year in ad spend and gained an estimated $6,200 in incremental revenue from new customers who never would have seen her old, city-wide campaign.

## How to Turn Review Volume Into Revenue (Without Begging)

Most local business owners treat reviews like a popularity contest. They want a high rating because it feels good. But a high rating alone doesn't pay your rent. What matters is how many reviews you have, on which platform, and whether you're using them to close the deal.

Here's the math I've seen work across three different clients:

**The review density threshold.** Google's local algorithm favors businesses with recent, frequent reviews. Not just five-star ones. Any review. A 4.2-star rating with 200 reviews will outrank a 4.8-star rating with 12 reviews in most local searches. Why? Because Google trusts volume and recency more than perfection. A business with 200 reviews is clearly active. A business with 12 reviews might have closed six months ago.

**The Yelp trap.** I've had clients obsess over their Yelp rating while ignoring Google. Yelp has its own traffic, but Google has local search. For a coffee shop or salon, Google Maps is where discovery happens. Yelp is where people with strong opinions go to write novels. Prioritize Google, then Yelp, then industry-specific directories (Booksy for salons, Mindbody for fitness studios).

**The review-to-appointment pipeline.** A review is not a vanity metric. It's an asset you can use to build trust before the first conversation. Here's what I tell clients to do:

- Print your best Google reviews on a small card and put them on every table or counter.
- Add a "what our clients say" section to your website with 3–5 specific reviews. Not "she was great" — use reviews that mention a specific service, outcome, or detail.
- Reply to every review — positive and negative — within 48 hours. For positive reviews, say thank you and mention something specific they enjoyed. For negative reviews, apologize, acknowledge the issue, and offer to make it right offline.
- Use review snippets in Google Ads. If someone wrote "best haircut I've had in years," you can turn that into an ad extension.

A pet store in Denver did this exact playbook. In 90 days, they went from 23 Google reviews to 104. Their monthly calls from Google Maps went from 40 to 112. They didn't increase ad spend. They just started treating reviews like inventory rather than decoration.

## Why Your Google Business Profile Isn't Working (And What Will Fix It)

I look at a lot of Google Business Profiles, and most of them are digital billboards for a business that forgot to update the sign. The basics are fine — name, address, phone number — but everything else is either missing or wrong.

Here are the three things I fix first:

**1. Your primary category is wrong.** Google lets you choose one primary category and up to nine secondary categories. Most business owners pick the closest match and move on. But the primary category determines which search queries you show up for. A salon that selects "Beauty Salon" instead of "Hair Salon" might not appear for "haircut near me." A coffee shop that selects "Café" instead of "Coffee Shop" might miss "coffee near me." Go into your GBP dashboard, click "Info," and check your primary category. Change it if it's even slightly off.

**2. You have no products or services listed.** Google's inventory feed is not just for ecommerce. You can list actual services with prices. A nail salon in Los Angeles added "Gel Manicure — $45" and "Acrylic Full Set — $60" to their GBP. Their click-to-call rate increased by 30% in two weeks. Why? Because searchers saw a price and knew they could afford it. Remove the friction of "I don't know how much this costs."

**3. You haven't posted in 60 days.** GBP posts disappear after 7 days, but they signal recency to Google's algorithm. Post once a week minimum. A photo of a new drink. A limited-time offer. A staff spotlight. A holiday schedule change. Each post is a signal that your business is active. A diner in Tucson posted their daily specials every morning for three months. Their "get directions" clicks doubled.

**The fix that pays for itself:** Add a booking button. If you use Square Appointments, Booksy, Vagaro, or Mindbody, you can link your GBP to your booking system. A massage therapist in Denver added a booking button to her GBP and stopped taking calls entirely. Her booking rate increased by 40% because customers could book at 2 AM without waiting for a human to answer.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long do I have to wait for Google Ads to work for my local business?**

You should see calls or form submissions within the first week if your targeting and keywords are correct. If you've spent $200 and gotten zero conversions after 7 days, something is wrong — wrong keywords, wrong location radius, wrong landing page, or your Google Business Profile is incomplete. Fix those first. Do not throw more money at a broken campaign hoping it will fix itself. I've seen business owners spend $2,000 before admitting their ad was showing to people in the wrong zip code.

**Q: Do I really need both Google Ads and a Google Business Profile?**

Yes, and they work together. A fully optimized GBP will get you organic visibility for free. Google Ads gets you placement above the organic results. When someone clicks your ad, they see your GBP information — reviews, hours, photos. If your GBP is weak, your ad converts poorly. If your GBP is strong, your ad gets a quality score boost and costs less per click. You can spend $300 on ads with a strong GBP and outperform someone spending $800 with a neglected profile.

**Q: I'm in a small town. Does any of this apply to me?**

It applies more, not less. In small towns, Google Maps is often how people discover new businesses because they don't have the directory options of a big city. If you're the only coffee shop in a town of 8,000 people, you need to be the one that shows up when someone searches "coffee." That means a claimed GBP, updated hours, and a handful of reviews. I've worked with a pet supply store in a town of 3,500 that got 30% of their business from Google Maps searches. Small towns aren't immune to digital discovery.

**Q: Can I just run Facebook ads instead of Google Ads?**

You can, but you'll reach people who are browsing, not people who are looking for what you offer. Facebook is interruptive. Google is responsive. Someone searching "haircut [neighborhood]" has intent. Someone scrolling Facebook at 10 PM has a thumb and time. For a local business with a limited budget, Google is usually the better first bet. Use Facebook for retargeting — show ads to people who visited your website but didn't book. Don't make Facebook your primary acquisition channel.

**Q: How many reviews do I actually need to rank on Google?**

There's no magic number, but I've seen a consistent pattern: 25–50 reviews gets you on the first page for competitive local searches in a medium-sized city. 100+ reviews often pushes you into the top 3 map listings. 200+ reviews with consistent recency makes you difficult to displace. The key is recency. A business with 200 reviews from 2021 will be outranked by a business with 40 reviews from the last 3 months. Get reviews weekly, not annually.

**Q: I keep getting 5-star reviews but nobody leaves a written comment. Does that matter?**

Google cares about the text content of reviews for relevance purposes. If someone writes "great haircut" and someone else writes "best gel manicure in Brooklyn," Google can connect the second review to searches for "gel manicure Brooklyn." Ask your customers to mention one specific thing they liked — a service, a product, an experience. A 5-star rating with no text is better than nothing. A 5-star rating with 20 words about what you do well is better than a 4-star review with a novel about a parking issue.
