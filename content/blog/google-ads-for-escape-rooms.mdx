## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting "Escape Room" as Your Only Keyword

**The story:** An escape room owner in Nashville called me after spending $1,400 on Google Ads in six weeks with exactly two bookings — and one was from his cousin. He had one campaign. One ad group. One keyword: "escape room."

**What went wrong:** He assumed everyone searching for "escape room" was ready to book. That keyword gets traffic, but most of it is research — people comparing prices, reading reviews, or looking for themes. He was paying $4–6 per click for people who hadn't decided to book yet. On top of that, his ad was showing in searches for "escape room near me" without any location modifiers, so he was paying for clicks from people 40 miles away who weren't driving to his neighborhood.

**The fix:** We restructured his campaign into three ad groups:

- **Intent-driven keywords:** "book escape room Nashville," "escape room birthday party," "escape room gift certificate" — these had lower search volume but conversion rates above 12%
- **Theme-specific keywords:** "zombie escape room Nashville," "prison break escape room" — captured people who knew what experience they wanted
- **Competitor keywords:** specific competitor names + "vs" + his business name

We added negative keywords like "escape room online," "escape room game download," "escape room tips." We set location targeting to a 15-mile radius with a location bid adjustment of +20% for people physically in Nashville.

**The outcome:** His cost per booking dropped from $350 to $47. Monthly spend stayed at $1,000. Monthly bookings from Google Ads went from basically zero to 21 bookings worth $3,780 in ticket revenue.

### Mistake #2: One Generic Ad for Every Audience

**The story:** An escape room in Austin was running one ad that said "Best Escape Room in Austin — Book Now!" It wasn't terrible copy, but it was trying to appeal to everyone. The owner was spending $1,800/month and getting a 1.2% click-through rate with a 2% conversion rate. Below average for their market.

**What went wrong:** Their audience isn't monolithic. A group of 25-year-olds looking for a Friday night activity doesn't respond to the same message as a corporate manager booking a team-building event. The generic ad didn't speak to anyone specifically. The owner also wasn't using ad extensions at all — no call button, no sitelinks, no pricing callouts.

**The fix:** I had them build four separate ad groups with dedicated ads:

- **"Friends Night Out"** ad: Headline — "Escape With Your Crew | $32/Person | Austin's Top-Rated Escape Room" | Description focused on difficulty levels, group sizes, and a discount code for groups of 4+
- **"Corporate Team Building"** ad: Headline — "Corporate Escape Room Packages | Private Bookings Available | Team Building That Doesn't Suck" | Description mentioned waivers, flexibility with timing, and a food package option
- **"Birthday Parties"** ad: Headline — "Birthday Escape Room Party | Ages 10+ | Private Room Included" | Description called out cake-and-game combo
- **"Date Night"** ad: Headline — "Two-Player Escape Room | Something Different for Date Night | Book Your 60-Minute Challenge"

Each ad group had its own landing page (simple but specific). We added call extensions, sitelink extensions pointing to their booking page, pricing page, and FAQ page.

**The outcome:** Click-through rates jumped to 4.1%. Conversion rates hit 9.8%. Cost per booking dropped from $42 to $18. Monthly spend stayed at $1,800, but bookings nearly doubled. That specific "$32/Person" pricing callout in the friends' ad alone cut their cost per click by 22% — people who see a price upfront and click anyway are much more likely to book.

### Mistake #3: No Conversion Tracking (Or Only Tracking the Wrong Thing)

**The story:** A Chicago escape room owner had "conversion tracking" set up. Technically. He was tracking clicks on the "Call Now" button. That's it. He thought he was getting solid data. When I looked at his account, he was spending $2,200/month and thought his cost per conversion was $8. His actual business was losing money on ads.

**What went wrong:** Clicks on "Call Now" are not bookings. His team answered the phone maybe 60% of the time. Of the calls they did answer, about 40% ended in a booking. So a $8 "conversion" was actually a $8 click on the phone button, not a paying customer. The real cost per booked room was closer to $33, but he didn't know because he wasn't tracking the actual transaction.

His other problem: he had no way to track which campaigns were driving online bookings. His booking software (a custom WordPress plugin) wasn't connected to Google Ads at all. So even when people booked online, Google Ads got zero credit.

**The fix:** We did three things:

1. Set up Google Ads conversion tracking on the actual "booking confirmed" page — not the contact page, not the pricing page, the post-purchase "thanks for booking" page
2. Connected his booking system (he switched to Square Appointments during this process) to Google Ads via Google Tag Manager
3. Set up call tracking through CallRail with per-campaign tracking numbers and call recordings

Within two weeks, we had real data. The campaign targeting "escape room for birthday party" was converting at 14%. The campaign targeting "escape room near me" was converting at 4%. We shifted budget from the broad campaign to the birthday campaign and saw immediate improvement.

**The outcome:** Monthly spend dropped to $1,500 (we cut the waste). Cost per actual booking dropped to $19. Monthly bookings went from roughly 45 to 78 because we weren't throwing money at ads that looked good in Google Ads but didn't actually bring in people. The owner also found out his team was consistently mishandling the "corporate team building" calls — they were booking those groups into their hardest room instead of their easiest one. Fixing that one call-handling issue increased corporate repeat bookings by 35%.

### Mistake #4: Running Ads Year-Round Without Adjusting for Seasonality

**The story:** An escape room in Portland OR was running the same campaigns, same budgets, same ads every month of the year. December and January were brutal. February looked okay. They couldn't figure out why some months performed well and others didn't.

**What went wrong:** Escape rooms have clear seasonal patterns. November through January is slow (people are spending money on holidays, not entertainment). February through March picks up. April through June is strong. July through August drops (vacations, outdoor activities, good weather competing for attention). September through October is peak season for corporate team building and Halloween-themed events.

This owner was spending $1,200/month in February and getting 40 bookings. He was spending $1,200/month in December and getting 12 bookings. Same spend, three times the cost per booking.

**The fix:** We created a seasonal budget structure:

- **Peak season (Sept–Oct):** $1,800/month — focused on corporate team building and Halloween events
- **Good season (Feb–June):** $1,200/month — balanced across audience groups
- **Slow season (Nov–Jan):** $600/month — focused only on gift certificates and birthday parties
- **Shoulder months (July–Aug):** $900/month — focused on tourist traffic and two-player bookings

We also changed ad copy seasonally. In November, every ad highlighted gift certificates. "Give the gift of adventure — digital escape room gift certificates delivered instantly." In September, every headline tested "Corporate team building without the trust falls."

**The outcome:** Total annual spend dropped from $14,400 to $12,900. Total annual bookings went up from 310 to 440. The key was that during slow months, he wasn't burning cash on low-intent traffic. And during peak months, he pushed harder when people were actually looking. The gift certificate campaign in December had a 22% conversion rate and a $5 cost per acquisition — his best-performing campaign all year, but only because he stopped trying to sell full-price bookings when nobody was buying them.

## How to Build an Escape Room Ad That Actually Converts

Most escape room ads look the same. "Best escape room in [city]! Book now!" Every competitor has this ad. Yours won't stand out unless you say something specific.

Here's what I've seen work across 20+ escape room accounts.

**The headline structure that consistently wins:** Specific theme + specific offer + specific price. Example: "Zombie Lab Escape Room | $28/Person | Book Your 60-Minute Challenge" beats "Escape Room [City] | Fun for Groups | Book Now" by about 40% on click-through rate.

**Why it works:** People searching for escape rooms usually have a rough idea of what they want. They're comparing themes, prices, and availability. If your ad tells them exactly what you offer and how much it costs, they self-select before they click. The ones who click already know they're interested in zombies and are okay with $28/person. You're filtering out the tire-kickers.

**The description formula:** One sentence about the experience. One sentence about what makes you different. One sentence about the offer. That's it. Example: "Navigate a contaminated zombie lab to find the cure before time runs out. Rated [city]'s #1 escape room on TripAdvisor. Use code ESCAPE10 for 10% off your booking."

**The landing page rule:** Your ad should point to the specific experience you're selling, not your homepage. If your ad says "Zombie Lab Escape Room," the page should be about the Zombie Lab, not a page that lists all seven of your rooms. This sounds obvious. I've audited accounts where the exact ad I just described pointed to a general booking page. Conversion rate went from 4% to 9% just by fixing that.

**What to test first:** The single most impactful thing you can test is whether your ad includes a price. Run two identical ads — one with a price, one without. Track which one gets more bookings, not just more clicks. The ad with the price almost always has a lower click-through rate but a higher conversion rate. That's fine. You want conversions, not vanity metrics.

**What not to waste time testing:** Headline capitalization. Ad color schemes (that's display network thinking, not search). Whether to use "escape room" or "escape game." Test price, offer, and call to action. Everything else is noise.

## Tracking What Actually Works (So You Don't Fly Blind)

Most small business owners I talk to have some version of this problem: they know they're spending money on Google Ads, but they're not sure if it's actually driving bookings. They look at the Google Ads dashboard, see "conversions: 47," and assume everything is fine.

Here's how to actually know if your ads are working.

**Step one is tracking the right action.** Not page views. Not button clicks. Bookings. If your booking system doesn't feed data back to Google Ads, you're guessing. The fix is usually a 15-minute job with Google Tag Manager. You place a tracking snippet on your "thank you for booking" page. Google Ads sees that fire and counts it as a conversion. If you can't do it yourself, any freelance web developer can set this up in under an hour. It's the best $100 you'll spend on your ad account.

**Step two is UTM parameters on every ad.** This is free, takes five minutes, and gives you data that your booking software can read. When someone clicks your ad, the UTM tells your booking system exactly which ad, campaign, and keyword brought them in. If you use Square Appointments, Booksy, or Mindbody, you can set up reports that show which Google Ads campaigns generate the most revenue. Without UTMs, you're flying blind.

**Step three is phone call tracking.** This is one of those things nobody tells you about until you've wasted $2,000 on phantom calls. People book escape rooms over the phone. If you're not tracking which calls came from Google Ads, you're only seeing half your conversions. I use CallRail with most clients. It's about $30/month. Each Google Ads campaign gets its own phone number. When someone calls that number, CallRail logs it and sends a conversion signal back to Google Ads. You can also record calls, which is great for training your staff on how to handle booking inquiries.

**Real example:** A Denver escape room was running ads for their corporate team building packages. Google Ads showed zero conversions from that campaign. When we set up call tracking, we found that campaign was actually generating 15–20 calls per week, and about half of those turned into bookings. The campaign was profitable — they just had no way to see it because people picked up the phone instead of booking online. Their cost per booking was actually $12, not "infinite."

**What you actually need to check weekly:** Number of bookings from Google Ads. Cost per booking. Revenue per booking. That's three numbers. If cost per booking is higher than your margin per booking, you're losing money. If it's lower, you're winning. Don't get distracted by click-through rate, quality score, or impression share. Those are intermediate metrics that tell you something about your ads but nothing about your business.

**Monthly audit checklist:**
- Are there search terms that are eating budget without converting? Add them as negative keywords.
- Is one campaign consistently underperforming? Lower its budget or pause it.
- Are your best-performing campaigns getting enough budget? Increase it.
- Are the ads getting stale? Refresh the copy if click-through rate has dropped below 2% for more than two weeks.
- Did any competitors change their pricing or offers? Adjust yours if needed.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: "How much should I spend on Google Ads per month for my escape room?"**

Start with $500–$1,000/month if you have one location. That's enough to test 2–3 campaigns and see real data within 30 days. I've seen escape rooms succeed on $400/month in smaller markets like Boise or Omaha, and fail on $2,000/month in New York because they didn't have the conversion tracking set up. The number matters less than what you do with it. A well-run $600 campaign will outperform a sloppy $2,000 campaign every time. After 60 days, you'll know your cost per booking and can scale accordingly.

**Q: "Isn't everyone already searching for escape rooms anyway? Why do I need ads?"**

Not everyone, and not consistently. On your best day, maybe 200 people in your metro area search for escape room–related terms. Google Ads puts you in front of the ones who are actually ready to book. Without it, you're relying on word of mouth, your Google My Business listing, and organic search rankings that take months to build. During slow seasons — November, January — paid ads are often the only reliable way to fill rooms.

**Q: "How do I compete with the big escape room chains that have huge budgets?"**

You don't need to outspend them. You need to out-target them. Chains run broad campaigns and pay for every click. You can run tight campaigns that only show up for people searching for your specific neighborhood, your specific theme, or your specific offer. An escape room in Chicago's Wicker Park neighborhood can target "escape room wicker park" and pay $2/click while the chain downtown pays $6/click for "escape room Chicago." Your conversion rate will be higher because the person searching for your neighborhood wants your location. Chains also rarely change their ad copy. Update yours monthly. Fresh copy with local references consistently outperforms stale chain ads.

**Q: "What happens if no one books? Am I just burning money?"**

If you set up conversion tracking before you launch, you'll know within two weeks whether a campaign is working. If nobody books after 14 days and $300 spent, pause the campaign and change something — your keywords, your ad copy, your landing page, or your targeting. Don't throw more money at a broken campaign hoping it will fix itself. That's the most common mistake I see. Small businesses lose $1,000–$2,000 running bad campaigns for three months before they finally stop. If you test and optimize, your odds of success go up dramatically.

**Q: "Should I run ads for every room theme I have?"**

Start with your top two or three most popular rooms. Run separate campaigns for each. Track which theme's campaign has the lowest cost per booking. After 30 days, shift more budget to the winner. The other campaigns will still be useful, but you want your best-performing offers to get the most traffic. In my experience, one theme usually dominates — often the one with the best reviews or the most approachable difficulty level. The "hard for experts" rooms rarely convert well in ads.

**Q: "Do I need a separate landing page for Google Ads, or can I just link to my homepage?"**

Separate page. Every time. Your homepage has too many options. People who click a Google ad want one thing: to book the experience they just clicked on. Give them a page that shows that specific room's description, a photo, a price, and a booking button. That's it. No navigation bar. No links to other rooms. No blog posts. One focused page converts at 8–12%. A homepage with the same traffic converts at 2–4%. The difference is worth the 30 minutes it takes to build the page.
