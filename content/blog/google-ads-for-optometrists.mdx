## Measuring the Success of Your Google Ads Campaign

Once your campaign is up and running, it's essential to track its performance and make adjustments as needed. Here are some metrics to focus on:

* Conversion rate: The percentage of users who complete a desired action on your website
* Cost-per-conversion: The cost of acquiring one conversion
* Return on ad spend (ROAS): The revenue generated by your campaign compared to the cost

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Too Wide a Radius

A practice in Austin, TX came to me after spending $1,200 over two months with exactly three new patients to show for it. The owner was ready to swear off Google Ads entirely. I looked at their campaign settings and found the problem in about thirty seconds: they had set a 30-mile radius with no location exclusions. Their ads were showing to people in Round Rock, Georgetown, and San Marcos — suburbs where residents already had optometrists half the distance away.

The fix: I tightened the radius to 10 miles, enabled location bid adjustments to prioritize people physically near their office, and added three location exclusions for areas where they knew patients wouldn't travel. I also added location extensions so their address and phone number showed right in the ad.

The outcome: The next month they spent $600 — half their original budget — and booked 14 new patient appointments. At an average revenue of $180 per exam (before glasses or contacts upsells), that's $2,520 against $600 in ad spend. A 4.2x return instead of throwing money at ZIP codes that were never going to drive foot traffic.

### Mistake #2: Using Broad Match Keywords Without Negatives

An optometrist in Nashville let me look at their account. They had one keyword: "eye exam" set to broad match. Google helpfully showed their ads for searches like "cheap eye exam coupon," "free eye exam for seniors," and "eye exam price." They were paying $4.50 per click to people looking for the absolute lowest price — not the quality care this practice was known for. Their conversion rate was 0.5%. That's five clicks before a single conversion. On a good day.

The fix: I switched all keywords to phrase match. I added negatives like: cheap, free, discount, coupon, low cost, affordable, Walmart, Costco, 2020. I also added high-intent keywords: "comprehensive eye exam Austin," "eye exam with insurance," "optometrist near me accepting new patients." We set up a separate ad group for branded terms.

The outcome: Cost per click dropped to $2.80. Conversion rate climbed to 3.2%. They started booking patients who actually wanted comprehensive care, not people price-shopping between five different offices. Their monthly spend stayed at $800, but they went from 4 conversions to 18.

### Mistake #3: Not Tracking Phone Calls

A practice in Chicago was relying entirely on website form submissions to measure campaign performance. They were seeing two forms a week and assuming the ads were underperforming. What they didn't know: their number was on the ad, and patients were calling directly. I set up Google Ads call extensions with a forwarding number and installed call tracking through CallRail (which integrates directly with Google Ads for about $30/month).

The fix took 20 minutes. Once I had the data, I discovered 40% of their conversions happened over the phone. The campaign was actually delivering 20% more leads than they thought — they just weren't measuring them.

The outcome: We increased the budget by 30% based on the real conversion data. Over the next quarter, total appointments jumped 60%. The practice went from $1,000/month ad spend to $1,300, and their revenue from new patients went from $3,600 to $5,760 per month. That's a return on ad spend of 4.4x — all because they started measuring what was already happening.

### Mistake #4: Running Ads 24/7 (When You're Only Open 9 to 5)

A practice in Portland OR was running ads around the clock. They were getting clicks at 2 AM and 3 AM from people searching for emergency eye care — which this practice didn't offer. The owner thought more clicks equaled more patients. In reality, they were burning $500/month on people who couldn't book with them even if they wanted to.

The fix: I set ad scheduling to run 7 AM to 7 PM weekdays, 8 AM to 2 PM Saturdays. I also created a separate emergency care ad group with different copy and a clear disclaimer about after-hours services, scheduled only during their actual emergency coverage hours.

The outcome: They saved $500/month in wasted spend. Their conversion rate actually improved because the clicks they got during business hours were from people who could immediately book an appointment. Same number of bookings, half the spend. That $6,000 annual savings went straight to their bottom line.

## Using Local Service Ads to Get in Front of High-Intent Searchers

If you've searched for a plumber recently, you've seen Google's Local Service Ads — the ones with the green checkmark and "Google Guaranteed" badge. These work differently than regular search ads. You pay per qualified lead, not per click. For optometrists, this is often more efficient than traditional search ads, especially if you're in a competitive market.

I helped a practice in Manhattan, NY set up Local Service Ads alongside their existing search campaign. They were spending $2,000/month on search ads with a $32 cost per click. Their conversion rate was decent, but the cost per booked appointment was hovering around $180. That's not bad, but it wasn't great for Manhattan margins.

The Local Service Ads setup required a background check and license verification — Google's "Google Guaranteed" program — which took about two weeks. Once active, the cost per lead was a flat $45 per qualified phone call or message. No paying for accidental clicks. No paying for people who bounce after five seconds.

In the first month, they received 40 leads through Local Service Ads. Of those, 25 booked appointments. At $180 average revenue per exam, that's $4,500 in revenue against $1,800 in lead costs. Their search ads were still running, but the LSAs were delivering a 2.5x return with zero wasted clicks.

Here's what you need to know: LSAs work best for high-intent searches like "eye exam near me" or "optometrist accepting new patients." They don't work well for informational queries or brand-building. You also need to respond to leads within 24 hours or Google penalizes your ranking. If you're a one-person practice who doesn't answer the phone, this might not be for you.

The tool itself is built into Google Ads under "Local Services." Setup takes about an hour if your credentials are ready. You set your weekly budget (I recommend starting at $500/week), and Google automatically pauses your ad once you've hit your lead target for the week. No overspend, no surprise bills.

One downside: you can't control the ad copy. Google uses a template based on your business category. You can add a few custom lines, but mostly you're buying the "Google Guaranteed" badge and the pay-per-lead model. For my clients in dense urban areas — think Chicago, NYC, San Francisco — this trade-off is worth it. For rural practices, regular search ads usually perform better because there's less competition.

## Building a Remarketing Funnel for Hesitant Patients

Here's a pattern I've seen at four different optometry accounts: someone visits your website, looks at the services page, maybe clicks "Book Appointment," then leaves. They're interested but not ready. Maybe they want to check their insurance. Maybe they're comparing you against another practice. Maybe they just got distracted by a phone call.

Remarketing brings them back. And for optometry, it's criminally underused.

A practice in Denver, CO was seeing a 62% abandonment rate on their booking form. People would fill out their name, email, and phone number, then stop at the insurance dropdown. That's frustrating, but it's also a signal: they're almost ready.

I set up a remarketing campaign in Google Ads targeting anyone who visited the booking page but didn't complete it. The list duration was 30 days. The ad creative was simple: "Still looking for an eye doctor? Book now and get $20 off your copay." The landing page was the same booking form with the offer pre-applied.

Cost of the remarketing campaign: $220 over three weeks. Results: they recovered 18% of the abandoned bookings — 22 new appointments worth $3,960 in revenue. That's an 18x return on the ad spend.

You can layer email on top of this. Export the same audience list to Mailchimp and send a three-email sequence: (1) "We saved your spot" reminder 24 hours after abandon, (2) "Meet our doctors" with staff bios, (3) "Book by Friday and skip the waiting room" urgency play. The combination of ads plus emails typically recovers 25-30% of abandoners.

One tool that makes this easier: use a Google Ads audience list called "All Visitors" and create separate lists for "Booking Page Visitors (not submitted)" and "Services Page Viewers." The more specific your audience, the cheaper your clicks and the higher your conversion rate. Running a generic "people who visited my site" campaign is better than nothing, but segmenting by page type doubled my client's remarketing conversion rate from 4% to 8%.

## Tracking Offline Conversions with Your Practice Management Software

Most optometrists use some version of practice management software — Practice Fusion, EyeCarePro, RevolutionEHR, or a simple Square Appointments setup. Here's the thing: Google Ads has no idea what happens after someone clicks your ad unless you tell it. If you're relying on form submissions alone to measure success, you're probably undercounting your conversions by 40-60%.

I worked with a multi-location practice in Dallas, TX that was running $3,500/month in Google Ads. They had form submissions tracked, but they kept telling me the campaign "felt better" than the data showed. I set up offline conversion import. Here's how it works:

1. Export appointment data from your practice software to a CSV file (date, time, phone number, name)
2. Match it against the click IDs or phone calls tracked in Google Ads
3. Upload the file to Google Ads as offline conversions

The process took about two hours to set up the first time, then 15 minutes weekly to maintain. The result: they discovered that 52% of their new patient appointments came from Google Ads — not the 28% their form data showed. The campaign was performing nearly twice as well as they thought.

More importantly, they could see which keywords drove actual appointments, not just clicks. "Comprehensive eye exam" was generating 12 appointments per month at a cost of $18 per appointment. "Best optometrist in Dallas" was generating 2 appointments at $150 per appointment. They cut the second keyword, reallocated that budget, and saw a 22% increase in total appointments over the next three months.

The tool you need depends on your software. Square Appointments has a direct Google Ads integration that imports conversion data automatically. For Practice Fusion and RevolutionEHR, you'll likely need a CSV upload or a third-party connector like Zapier to bridge the gap. Budget $50-100/month for the integration if you go the Zapier route.

One warning: don't skip the call tracking piece. If you're not tracking phone calls as conversions, you're flying blind. I've seen clinics where 60% of their bookings come by phone. If you're only tracking form fills, you're optimizing for 40% of your actual conversions. Use CallRail, WhatConverts, or even Google's free call tracking through call extensions. That $30-50/month is the cheapest optimization you'll ever make.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What's a reasonable starting budget for a single-location optometry practice with no prior Google Ads experience?**

Start at $500-1,000/month. In a smaller market (think 50,000-100,000 population), $500 is usually enough to get meaningful data. In a competitive city like Austin or Nashville, $1,000 gives you enough clicks to optimize within 4-6 weeks. Do not go below $300/month — you won't get enough data to make any real decisions, and you'll conclude that Google Ads doesn't work when really you're just underfunding the test.

**Q: How do I know if the calls and appointments are actually coming from my ads?**

Use call tracking with unique phone numbers in your ads (CallRail, WhatConverts, or Google's built-in call extensions with forwarding numbers). For web traffic, set up Google Ads conversion tracking on your thank-you page after someone books. Then ask every new patient "How did you hear about us?" for the first three months. Correlate that with your ad data. You'll quickly see if there's a gap between what the platform reports and what's actually happening.

**Q: Should I do Google Ads or Yelp Ads for my optometry practice?**

Google Ads, nine times out of ten. Yelp Ads can work for certain service businesses, but I've seen practices waste $1,000+ on Yelp with zero measurable conversions. Yelp's click costs are often higher, their data transparency is worse, and people searching for eye doctors are overwhelmingly using Google (not Yelp) to find them. If you have budget to test both, put 80% into Google Ads and 20% into Yelp. But if you're picking one, pick Google.

**Q: Can I run this myself or do I need to hire someone?**

You can run it yourself if you're willing to learn the basics. Google Ads has a steep learning curve, but the fundamentals are manageable for a business owner who's comfortable with spreadsheets and data. The mistakes people make — broad match keywords, no negatives, no call tracking, no location targeting — can be avoided by reading a few good guides and spending 30 minutes per week on optimization. If you're not getting results after 4 weeks, hire someone who specializes in local service ads. I've fixed campaigns in an hour that were losing $800/month.

**Q: How long until I see actual appointments?**

You'll see clicks within 2-3 days of launching. Real appointments usually start within 7-14 days, depending on how quickly patients call or book online. Give it 30 days before you judge performance. A campaign with 500 clicks and zero conversions after two weeks likely has a targeting or offer problem. A campaign with 50 clicks and zero conversions is too small to judge — wait for more data.

**Q: Do I need a separate website page for each service I advertise?**

Yes, and this is where most optometrists drop the ball. If you're bidding on "contact lens fitting," send people to a page specifically about contact lens fitting — not your homepage, not your general services page, not a generic "schedule an appointment" form. I've seen conversion rates double just from creating dedicated landing pages for the three highest-volume services: comprehensive eye exams, contact lens fittings, and medical eye exams (dry eye, glaucoma, etc.). Use a simple page builder like Unbounce or even a dedicated page on your Squarespace site. The investment is a few hours of work for months of better performance.
