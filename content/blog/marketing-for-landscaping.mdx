Email marketing is an excellent way to nurture leads and retain existing clients. As a landscaper, you can use email campaigns to promote your services, share tips and advice, and offer exclusive discounts.

Here are some tips to get you started:

*   **Build your email list**: Collect email addresses from your website, social media channels, and in-person interactions.
*   **Create engaging content**: Write compelling subject lines and email content that resonates with your audience.
*   **Segment your list**: Divide your list into segments based on demographics, interests, and behavior to create targeted campaigns.

### Local SEO for Landscaping Companies

Local SEO is critical for landscaping companies that want to attract local clients. By optimizing your website and online presence for local search, you can increase your visibility and reach new customers.

Here are some tips to get you started:

*   **Claim your Google My Business listing**: Verify your business and complete your profile to increase your visibility in local search results.
*   **Optimize your website**: Use relevant keywords and meta tags to optimize your website for local search.
*   **Build local citations**: Get listed in local directories and citations to increase your visibility and credibility.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched landscaping companies throw money at marketing strategies that were never going to work. Not because the strategies were bad in theory, but because they weren’t built for how landscaping actually sells. Here are the three most expensive mistakes I see — and what to do instead.

**Mistake 1: Running Google Ads for “lawn care” in January in Chicago**

I worked with a landscaping company in Naperville, Illinois — about 40 minutes outside Chicago. They had a decent website, a solid fleet of trucks, and the owner, Mike, had been in business for eight years. In January, he noticed his phone wasn’t ringing. So he threw $1,500 into Google Ads targeting “lawn mowing service” and “landscaping near me.”

Here’s what actually happened: He spent $1,200 in two weeks. Got 12 clicks. Zero calls. The problem wasn’t his ad copy or his landing page. The problem was that nobody in Illinois is looking for lawn mowing in January. His ads were showing up for searches that weren’t happening. The few people who did click were either competitors checking him out or someone who misclicked while looking for snow removal.

**The fix:** I had him pause all seasonal services and run ads only for snow removal and winter tree pruning through February. Starting March 1, we flipped to spring cleanup and aeration. We allocated $800/month to snow removal in January, $500 to early-bird spring packages in February, and $1,200/month to full landscaping services from April through October.

**The outcome:** His February ads for early-bird spring cleanups brought in 28 leads and 19 booked jobs at an average ticket of $475. That’s $9,025 in booked revenue from $500 in ad spend. His January mistake cost him $1,200. His February fix made him $9,025. The difference was just timing.

**What to do instead:** Match your Google Ads to what people are actually searching for right now. If you’re in a cold climate, nobody wants lawn care in December. They want snow shoveling, holiday lighting removal, or consultations for spring projects. Use Google’s keyword planner to check search volume by month. If the volume isn’t there, don’t spend the money.

**Mistake 2: Ignoring Google Business Profile because “I get enough word-of-mouth”**

A company in Austin, Texas — let’s call them GreenScapes — had been in business for 12 years. The owner, David, told me he didn’t need online reviews because 80% of his business came from referrals. He had four reviews on Google. Two were from 2018. One was a complaint about a broken sprinkler head that he never responded to.

Here’s what happened: A potential client in the Mueller neighborhood in Austin was looking for a landscaping company. She had two friends recommend GreenScapes. She Googled them. The first thing she saw was a 3.2-star rating with an unresolved complaint and no recent activity. She clicked on the next company — one with 47 reviews, a 4.8 average, and photos of recent projects. She booked with them. That job was worth $3,200 for a full backyard redesign.

David didn’t know he lost that client. But I saw it happen three times in one month with different prospects. He was losing $8,000–$10,000 in potential revenue every month because his online presence made him look like a company that didn’t care.

**The fix:** We cleaned up the profile. Added current photos. Responded to every review — good and bad. Set up a simple system: after every completed job, the crew sent a text to the client with a direct link to leave a review. We offered no discounts or bribes. Just a clean ask: “If you’re happy, please leave us a review. It helps us keep the lights on.”

**The outcome:** In 90 days, GreenScapes went from 4 reviews to 38. Rating went from 3.2 to 4.7. Their Google Business Profile impressions from search increased by 340%. Phone calls from Google listings went from 2 per week to 14. David tracked $23,000 in revenue directly attributed to calls from his Google profile over the next five months.

**What to do instead:** Claim your Google Business Profile. Update photos every month. Respond to every review within 48 hours. Set up a review request system — there are tools like Widewail or even a simple text automation through your CRM. If you have fewer than 20 reviews in 2024, you’re leaving money on the table.

**Mistake 3: Using a generic email newsletter that nobody opens**

A landscaping company in Nashville — 10 employees, $1.2M annual revenue — was sending a monthly email newsletter to 1,400 subscribers. They used Mailchimp. The open rate was 14%. The click rate was under 1%. The owner told me he’d been doing it for three years and “it just doesn’t work for landscaping.”

I asked to see the last three emails. Every single one was the same format: a photo of a lawn, a paragraph about why lawn care matters, and a “call us for a quote” button. No subject line creativity. No segmentation. No offers. No personality.

Here’s what was actually happening: His customers were getting the same email every month. They stopped opening it because it never gave them a reason to. His unsubscribe rate was low only because people didn’t care enough to unsubscribe. They just deleted it.

**The fix:** We segmented the list into three groups — current customers, past customers who hadn’t booked in 12+ months, and leads who had requested a quote but never booked. We rewrote the content so each segment got something relevant.
- Current customers got maintenance reminders and early access to seasonal services. “Your aeration is due next month. Here’s a link to book before slots fill up.”
- Past customers got a “we haven’t seen you in a while” offer with a specific discount. “It’s been 18 months since we last serviced your yard. Here’s $50 off your first visit back.”
- Leads got educational content and a limited-time incentive. “Spring bookings open March 1. First 10 clients get 15% off a full yard cleanup.”

We changed the subject lines from “March Lawn Care Tips” to “Your lawn needs help in March. Here’s what we’re seeing.” We sent the emails from the owner’s name, not the company name.

**The outcome:** Within two months, open rates went from 14% to 38%. Click rates went from 0.8% to 7.2%. The past-customer segment alone generated 14 bookings worth $6,300 in revenue. The lead segment converted 5 people who had ghosted them months earlier — worth another $2,900. Total attributed revenue from two email sends: $9,200. Cost: the Mailchimp subscription.

**What to do instead:** Stop sending the same thing to everyone. Segment by behavior. Send offers, not tips. Use a real person’s name as the sender. Write subject lines that create curiosity or urgency. If your open rate is under 25%, your email list is a liability, not an asset.

## Using Google Local Services Ads Instead of Regular Search Ads

Most landscaping companies start with Google Search Ads. That’s usually a mistake. Search Ads show up at the top of results, but they also show up below maps, below Local Services Ads, and below shopping results. By the time your ad appears, the client has already seen four other options.

**What Local Services Ads do differently**

Local Services Ads (LSAs) are the ones that appear at the very top of Google search results with a green checkmark and “Google Guaranteed.” They show your business name, rating, phone number, and business hours. The client clicks directly to call you or send a message. You pay per lead, not per click.

I set up LSAs for a landscaping company in Portland, Oregon. They had been spending $2,400/month on regular Search Ads. Average cost per click was $6.50. Average cost per lead — meaning someone who actually called or filled out a form — was $47. They were getting around 50 leads per month, but the quality was inconsistent. Lots of tire-kickers. People asking for quotes on tiny jobs they didn’t want to pay for.

We switched their budget to LSAs. The setup took about 30 minutes. Google verified their license, insurance, and background check. Cost per lead dropped to $22. The leads were warmer because LSAs show your rating, your response time, and whether you’re available now. People who click LSAs are closer to buying.

**The numbers:** With $1,800/month in LSAs, they got 82 leads. Of those, 31 booked a job. Average job value was $620. That’s $19,220 in booked revenue from $1,800 in ad spend. Their old search ads were generating about $11,000 in booked revenue from $2,400 in spend. The LSAs outperformed by 80% on ROI.

**How to set this up:** Go to Google Local Services Ads. Set your service areas by ZIP code. Set your budget per week. Google will ask you to pass a background check and verify your insurance. Once approved, your ad goes live. You can pause it during off-season and restart when demand picks up. There’s no long-term commitment.

**Who this works for:** Landscapers in any metro area where LSAs are available — most US cities over 50,000 people. It works best if you have a 4.0+ rating and respond to calls quickly. If you don’t answer your phone, LSAs will route the lead to someone else.

## Building a Referral System That Actually Pays You

Every landscaping company says they get referrals. Few of them have a system for generating them. They rely on word-of-mouth, which is unreliable and impossible to scale. Here’s a system that works, based on what I’ve seen work across three landscaping clients.

**Step 1: Make the ask specific and timely**

Don’t say “tell your friends about us.” Say: “If you know anyone within three blocks of your house who needs lawn care, mention our name and we’ll give you $50 off your next visit.” People refer when the ask is concrete and the reward is immediate.

**Step 2: Use a simple tool**

Mailchimp can send automated referral requests. Yelp has referral tracking if you run ads through them. Booksy, which is popular with service businesses, has a built-in referral program. If you want something dead simple, use a Google Form and a spreadsheet. The tool doesn’t matter. The process does.

**Step 3: Offer something worth referring for**

I worked with a company in Denver that offered $75 off for every referral that booked a job. They also gave the referred client $75 off their first visit. Average job was around $500. The $150 total cost was a no-brainer because the referred client’s lifetime value was around $2,400.

In six months, this system generated 43 referrals. 38 of them booked. That’s 38 new clients at a cost of $5,700 in discounts. Those 38 clients spent an average of $1,800 each in their first year — $68,400 in revenue. The cost to acquire them: $5,700.

**Step 4: Track everything**

If you don’t track where referrals come from, you won’t know what’s working. Ask every new client: “How did you hear about us?” Track the answer in your CRM or even a notebook. Look at the data quarterly. If referrals from existing clients are your highest-converting channel, double down. If Yelp referrals are expensive and low-quality, cut them.

**Step 5: Follow up with referral givers**

Send a thank-you note. A real one. Not an email. A postcard or a handwritten card. In a world of automated messages, a physical note stands out. One client in Austin sent handwritten thank-you cards to every referral source. His referral rate went up 60% over the following quarter. People remembered how it felt to be appreciated.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on marketing each month as a landscaping company?**

It depends on your revenue and goals. A safe starting point is 8–12% of your projected revenue. If you expect to do $200,000 this year, allocate $16,000–$24,000 for the year — roughly $1,300–$2,000 per month. But don’t spend evenly across months. Put more money into March through June and September through October. Spend less in January and February unless you offer snow services. I’ve seen companies spend $500/month and grow steadily. I’ve also seen companies spend $5,000/month and barely break even because they were targeting the wrong things. Start small, track everything, and scale what works.

**Q: Is Yelp worth it for landscapers?**

Yelp can work, but it’s expensive and unpredictable. I’ve seen landscaping companies pay $2–$8 per click on Yelp ads with no guarantee of a booking. The bigger issue is that Yelp’s algorithm favors businesses that advertise. If you don’t pay, your reviews get pushed down and negative reviews get highlighted. If you have a solid Google Business Profile with 30+ reviews and a 4.5+ rating, I’d put your money there first. If you want to test Yelp, start with a $300/month budget and track lead quality closely. Most landscapers I’ve worked with get better ROI from Google Local Services Ads and direct referrals.

**Q: Do I really need to post on social media? I don’t have time to make videos.**

Short answer: No, you don’t need to. Social media is a nice-to-have, not a must-have, for most landscaping companies. Your clients aren’t scrolling Instagram looking for a landscaper. They’re searching Google. That said, before-and-after photos on Instagram or Facebook can reinforce your credibility when someone looks you up. One photo per week with a caption like “Before and after — 3 hours of work in Brentwood” takes five minutes. If you want to skip social entirely, focus on Google, email, and referrals. That’s where the actual bookings come from.

**Q: Should I offer discounts to get new clients?**

Yes, but strategically. Don’t offer blanket discounts to everyone. Offer limited-time discounts for early-bird bookings in spring. Offer referral discounts. Offer a discount to past clients who haven’t booked in over a year. The goal is to use discounts to trigger action, not to lower your prices permanently. I’ve seen a “book by March 15 and save 10%” campaign generate 22 bookings worth $11,000. The discount cost $1,100. That’s a good trade.

**Q: How do I handle the slow season? I can’t afford to lose money for three months.**

You have three options. One: add winter services — snow removal, holiday lighting, tree pruning, indoor plant maintenance, consultations for spring projects. Two: run retargeting ads to past clients with offers for early-bird spring bookings. I’ve seen companies book 30% of their spring work by the end of February using this approach. Three: use the slow months to fix your website, update your Google profile, collect reviews, and plan your marketing for the busy season. Most companies ignore these tasks during peak season. The ones who use winter to get organized come into spring with a massive advantage.

**Q: Can I do this myself or do I need to hire someone?**

You can do most of it yourself if you have the time and patience. Google Local Services Ads takes 30 minutes to set up. A Mailchimp email list takes an hour to build. A referral system is a spreadsheet and a text message. The problem isn’t setup — it’s consistency. Most small business owners start strong, then get busy, and the marketing stops. If you can commit to one hour per week on marketing, you can run it yourself. If you won’t stick to that, find someone who will. Even a part-time VA at $15/hour can handle review requests, basic emails, and ad monitoring.
