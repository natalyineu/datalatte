## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched auto body shops throw away thousands of dollars on marketing that didn't work. Here are the mistakes I see most often — and what actually fixes them.

### Mistake #1: Trying to Be Everything to Everyone

**The story:** A 3-bay shop in Nashville called Music City Collision spent $1,800/month on Google Ads targeting "auto body shop Nashville," "car repair Nashville," "paintless dent repair," *and* "classic car restoration." The owner wanted to capture every possible lead. After four months, he'd burned through $7,200. His average cost-per-click was $6.50, his conversion rate was 0.8%, and he'd booked exactly 23 appointments — most of them for minor scratches that didn't fill his bays.

**What went wrong:** He was competing against 15 other shops on broad terms. Google happily served his ads to people looking for oil changes and tire rotations. His daily budget evaporated on clicks that would never convert.

**The fix:** I had him build three separate ad campaigns:
- One for collision repair with insurance keywords ("accident repair," "insurance claim body shop")
- One for retail paint work ("bumper repair," "scratch removal")
- One for his tow truck partnership (local referral terms)

He cut his daily budget from $60 to $35, focused it on "collision repair near me" and "insurance claim body shop Nashville," and added negative keywords like "oil change" and "tire."

**The outcome:** His cost-per-lead dropped from $78 to $31. He booked $12,400 in insurance work in the first month. His total ad spend that month was $1,050. The owner told me, "I was paying for people to ignore me before."

### Mistake #2: Ignoring the Google Business Profile (GBP)

**The story:** A shop in Austin called South Congress Auto Body had been at the same location for nine years. They had a Google Business Profile but hadn't logged into it since 2019. Their listing showed old hours, a defunct phone number, and zero photos. When I pulled their GBP insights, they were getting 47 views per month. Their competitor three blocks away was getting 1,200.

**What went wrong:** When someone in Austin searches "auto body shop near me," Google surfaces shops with complete, active profiles. South Congress Auto Body looked like it might be closed. Customers were calling the wrong number and leaving one-star reviews about not being able to reach anyone.

**The fix:** We updated everything:
- Hours of operation (they were open Saturdays — nobody knew)
- New photos of finished jobs (20 before-and-after shots)
- Services list with insurance accepted (State Farm, Allstate, Progressive, GEICO)
- Posts every Tuesday and Thursday (repair tips, vehicle spotlights)
- A structured process for asking every satisfied customer to leave a review

**The outcome:** Within 90 days, their GBP views went from 47/month to 890/month. Their review count went from 12 to 67. Their average rating stayed at 4.7. Phone calls from the profile went from zero to 18 per week. They attributed $3,800 in new monthly revenue directly to the change.

### Mistake #3: Marketing to Insurance Companies Like They're Customers

**The story:** A shop in Portland called Hawthorne Auto Body spent $500/month running ads on LinkedIn targeting insurance adjusters. The ads said things like "quality repairs" and "customer satisfaction guaranteed." The owner hosted two lunch-and-learns for local adjusters. After six months, he'd booked exactly zero insurance DRP (Direct Repair Program) jobs from the effort.

**What went wrong:** Insurance companies don't care about your marketing copy. They care about cycle time, supplement frequency, and audit scores. The owner was selling "quality" to people who are measured on speed and accuracy. He didn't know that USAA requires shops to submit supplements within 24 hours, or that State Farm tracks touch time per repair.

**The fix:** I had him do three things:
1. Pull his own data: average cycle time, first-time repair rate, supplement percentage
2. Invite the State Farm and Progressive adjusters to tour his facility *without* a sales pitch
3. Send a simple monthly email to adjusters with actual data: "Last month we averaged 8.3 days cycle time with a 12% supplement rate"

**The outcome:** Within two months, a Progressive adjuster started sending him overflow work. Within four months, he was on the DRP list for State Farm. That program alone added $4,200 in monthly revenue. The owner admitted, "I was marketing like I was selling to a customer, not a claims department."

## How to Actually Target Insurance Jobs (Without Wasting Money)

Most guides tell you "build relationships with adjusters" and stop there. That's like telling someone to "make money" and walking away. Here's what actually works.

### Step 1: Get Your Data Clean

Insurance companies measure everything. Before you pitch a single adjuster, pull these numbers:
- Average cycle time (repair time from authorization to completion)
- Supplement rate (how often you need more money after starting the job)
- Audit score (if you've ever had one)

USAA, State Farm, and Allstate all publish their minimum requirements for DRP partners. If your cycle time is over 10 days or your supplement rate is above 20%, fix that first. I've seen shops in Denver lose DRP contracts because they were averaging 14-day cycle times while the insurer wanted 8. No marketing budget can fix operational problems.

### Step 2: Make It Easy for Adjusters to Send You Work

The adjuster doesn't care about your Instagram feed. They care about:
- Can you pick up the phone on the first ring?
- Will you email photos within 2 hours of arrival?
- Do you submit supplements before noon?

Set up a dedicated phone line for insurance calls. I worked with a shop in Chicago that put a separate number on their DRP paperwork. That line went straight to the estimator's desk — no voicemail tree. The adjusters started sending work because they knew they'd talk to a human immediately.

### Step 3: Use the Right Tools

- **CCC One** or **Mitchell**: These are the estimating platforms insurers use. If you're not on the same system, you're invisible to adjusters who batch-send assignments. That's $1,200/year for most shops.
- **RepairShopr** or **Tekmetric**: These shop management systems can auto-generate the reports adjusters want. I'm talking cycle time, labor hours, parts sourcing time. When an adjuster asks "how fast can you do this?" you pull up the numbers in 30 seconds.
- **Google Ads with insurance keywords**: Bid on "State Farm approved body shop" and "[insurer name] direct repair." These terms convert at 4-5% because the customer is literally looking for a shop that works with their insurance company. Budget $300-500/month. Track everything.

### Step 4: Use the Monthly Check-In (Not the Quarterly Pitch)

I've seen shops in Phoenix land DRP contracts by sending a two-sentence email every month:
"Last month: 8.1 days cycle time, 11% supplement rate, 0 audits failed. Open for referrals."
That's it. No graphics. No testimonials. Adjusters forward these emails to their managers. One shop owner told me his State Farm rep said, "You're the only shop that sends me numbers I can actually use."

## Retail Client Marketing: The Stuff That Actually Gets Cars in the Bays

Insurance jobs pay the bills. Retail work is your profit margin. Here's how to get people who pay cash and don't argue about parts pricing.

### Google Ads for Retail Work

Stop bidding on "auto body shop." That's a $8-12 click in most cities, and you're competing with 20 other shops. Instead:

- **"Bumper repair [city]"** : $3-4/click. Someone with a scratched bumper doesn't need insurance. They want it fixed today. Cost-per-lead usually runs $25-35.
- **"Scratch removal [city]"** : $2-3/click. Low competition. High intent.
- **"Dent repair [neighborhood]"** : Target your specific ZIP codes. I had a shop in San Diego that ran ads for "dent repair Pacific Beach" and got a 9% CTR because nobody else was doing it.

Budget $400-600/month. Run a separate campaign for each service. Track conversions via calls (use a tracked number) or form fills. Cut any keyword that costs more than $50 per conversion after two weeks.

### Google Business Profile Posts (Free Traffic)

This is the easiest money in local marketing. Here's a post schedule that works:

- **Monday**: Repair tip ("How to tell if that bumper crack needs replacement vs. repair")
- **Wednesday**: Before/after photo (no logo overlay — let the work speak)
- **Friday**: Offer ("$50 off any deductible up to $500")

A shop in Denver started doing this consistently. Their GBP views went from 600/month to 2,400/month in eight weeks. They booked 12 extra retail jobs from posts alone. Zero dollar ad spend.

### Yelp — Yes, Actually Yelp

I know Yelp gets a bad reputation. But auto body is one of the few categories where Yelp reviews actually drive bookings. Here's why: when someone's car is damaged, they're stressed. They want the safest option. Yelp reviews signal safety.

The strategy:
- Claim your page
- Upload 30 photos (finished work, your shop bays, your waiting area)
- Respond to every review — positive and negative — within 24 hours
- Do NOT buy Yelp ads until you have 20+ reviews with a 4.0+ rating

A shop in Portland spent $300/month on Yelp ads after getting to 45 reviews with a 4.5 rating. They got 8 calls in the first month, booked 5 jobs, and grossed $4,900 from Yelp traffic. The key was waiting until they had social proof.

### Email Follow-Ups (The Missed Opportunity)

Most auto body shops take a customer's email for the invoice and never use it again. That's leaving money on the floor. Here's a simple sequence using **Mailchimp** (free up to 500 contacts):

1. **Day 1**: "Thanks for choosing us" with a photo of their repaired car
2. **Day 7**: "How's the repair holding up?" with a link to leave a review
3. **Day 90**: "It's time for that paint touch-up you mentioned" (if they mentioned one)
4. **Day 180**: "Winter is coming — here's how to protect your paint from salt"

A shop in Nashville set this up in one afternoon. After six months, they'd sent 1,200 emails with a 38% open rate and 12% click rate. They booked 19 repeat jobs from people who'd forgotten to come back for that touch-up. Each job averaged $380 in retail margin.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why should I bother marketing for insurance jobs if I'm already busy?**

Because "busy" and "profitable" are different things. Insurance jobs pay better per bay hour and they're more predictable. If you're only doing retail work, you're one slow season away from losing money. Every shop I've worked with that added insurance volume saw their profit per bay increase by 20-35% within six months.

**Q: Do I really need Google Ads, or can I just rely on word of mouth?**

Word of mouth takes 6-12 months to build enough volume to fill a shop. Google Ads gets you calls this week. I had a shop in Austin that was "busy enough" on word of mouth. They ran a $500/month Google Ads test. In the first month, they booked $3,200 in new work. Word of mouth is great. Paid ads are a multiplier, not a replacement.

**Q: How do I track whether my marketing is actually working?**

Phone calls and form fills. That's it. Set up a tracked phone number for each campaign (Google Ads, Yelp, GBP). Use a CRM like RepairShopr or HubSpot's free tier. If you can't tell me your cost-per-lead and cost-per-customer for each channel, you're guessing. Stop guessing.

**Q: Yelp called me and offered me a deal. Should I take it?**

Not until you have 20+ organic reviews with a 4.0 or higher. Yelp ads work *if* you have social proof. If you have 6 reviews and a 3.8 rating, Yelp ads will just spend your money and show people that you only have 6 reviews. Wait until you have the proof, then consider it.

**Q: I'm a one-person shop. I don't have time for this. Can't I just hire someone?**

You can, but you shouldn't until you've proven the channel works. Start with one thing: either Google Ads ($500/month for one campaign) or GBP posts (30 minutes per week). Run it for 60 days. If it works, hire a part-time VA for $15-20/hour to handle the rest. Most shops waste money hiring a marketing person before they even know what works.

**Q: What about social media — Instagram, Facebook, TikTok?**

Nice to have, not need to have. Auto body is a search-driven business. People search for you when they need you. They don't browse Instagram and decide to get their bumper repaired. If you have time, post before/after photos on Instagram. But don't spend money on social ads until Google Ads and GBP are dialed in. I've never seen an auto body shop succeed on Instagram ads. I've seen dozens that made money from search.
