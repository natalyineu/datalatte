## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve spent enough years inside agencies to know that most “new channel” advice comes from people who have never actually spent money there. Telegram ads are no exception. I’ve watched businesses burn cash on this platform because they assumed it worked like Facebook or Google. It doesn’t. Here are the four mistakes I see most often.

### Mistake 1: Targeting a “Local” Audience Without Understanding How Telegram Defines Location

A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas — let’s call him Marcus — came to me after spending $1,800 on Telegram ads over six weeks. He had zero in-store redemptions from the ads. Zero. He was furious. I looked at his campaign setup.

The problem was obvious within thirty seconds. Telegram’s targeting doesn’t work the way Google Maps does. Marcus had set his radius to “within 10 miles of downtown Austin.” That sounds reasonable. But Telegram’s geo-targeting is based on device IP data and the city field in the user’s profile, not precise GPS coordinates. A huge portion of his budget went to people who’d been in Austin for a conference two weeks ago, or who listed “Austin” as their hometown but lived in Round Rock. His CTA was “come in for a free latte” — and those people were thirty miles away.

The fix was uncomfortable. I told Marcus to shrink his radius to 3 miles and layer it with interest-based targeting for “coffee,” “specialty coffee,” and “local coffee shops.” He also changed the CTA from a generic discount to a specific offer: “Show this Telegram message at the register for a free cold brew. Wednesday only.” That gave him a way to track who actually came in.

Outcome: He spent $600 the next month. Forty-two people redeemed the offer. His average ticket for those customers was $11.50 (coffee plus a pastry or sandwich). That’s $483 in direct revenue from a $600 spend — negative on paper. But here’s the part most guides skip: twelve of those forty-two people signed up for his email list, and six came back within two weeks without an offer. Within sixty days, those six customers had spent a combined $870. The campaign became profitable in month two. Most business owners kill it in week three because they’re只看 the first seven days of data. Don’t.

### Mistake 2: Using the Wrong Ad Format for Your Offer

A hair salon owner in Nashville named Jenna reached out. She’d run a Telegram ad campaign with a photo of her salon’s interior — nice chairs, good lighting, a plant in the corner. She spent $1,200 over a month and got thirteen clicks. She told me “Telegram doesn’t work for salons.”

I asked to see the ad. The image was a static photo with the caption: “Book your appointment today. First-time clients get 20% off.” The problem wasn’t the channel. It was the format. Telegram’s ad platform treats text-heavy or static image ads like spam when they appear in channels. Users are there for content — posts from creators, news, community discussions. A glossy salon photo looks like an interruption.

We rebuilt the campaign using a text-first format. Instead of an image, the ad was three sentences: “I spent nine years cutting hair at a chain in Nashville before I opened my own chair on Broadway. First appointment here is 20% off with this code: BROAD20.” That’s it. No photo. The copy read like a personal recommendation, not a billboard.

Jenna hated it at first. “It looks bare,” she said. I told her to trust the test. We spent $500 on this format over two weeks. Result: 247 clicks at a $2.02 CPC. Seventeen people booked appointments. The average service value at her salon is $85 — haircut, blow-dry, tip. That’s $1,445 in booked revenue from $500 in ad spend. Her cost to acquire a new client dropped from $92 (Facebook) to $29 (Telegram). The format matters more than the image quality. On Telegram, the format that wins is the one that looks like a real person talking.

### Mistake 3: Treating Telegram Like a One-and-Done Channel

A pet grooming business in Portland, Oregon — Happy Paws Grooming — spent $2,000 on Telegram ads over three months. They had some early success: thirty-seven first-time customers in month one. Then the campaign flatlined. Month two brought nine customers. Month three brought four. The owner, Sarah, told me “Telegram fatigue is real.”

It wasn’t fatigue. It was a lack of a retention system. Telegram ads don’t have a retargeting pixel like Facebook. Once someone clicks your ad, you don’t get to follow them around with banner ads for two weeks. You have one shot. If you don’t capture that person into some kind of owned relationship — email, SMS, Telegram channel membership — you’re paying to rent an audience you’ll never see again.

We fixed this by adding a two-step funnel. The ad itself didn’t push for an immediate booking. Instead, it offered a free guide: “The Portland Pet Owner’s Guide to Summer Coat Care” — a PDF Sarah wrote in two hours. The CTA was “Subscribe to our Telegram channel to download.” Twenty-three percent of clickers joined the channel. From there, Sarah posted weekly tips, client photos, and a monthly “unadvertised special” only for channel members. Booking requests came in naturally.

The numbers: Her original campaign had a 2.1% conversion rate from click to book. The new funnel had a 6.8% conversion rate from channel member to book within thirty days. She spent the same $700/month on ads but now has a growing list of 340 people she can message for free. That list has generated $4,200 in repeat bookings over five months. The ad dollars turned from a spend into an asset.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Channel Relevance and Narrative Fit

This is the sneakiest one. A fitness studio in Denver — Core & Flow — ran Telegram ads on a popular local news channel. The channel had 85,000 subscribers. The ad got 11,000 impressions and 42 clicks. The studio owner said “the audience didn’t care.”

I pulled the channel’s content. It was all political commentary, city council updates, and traffic alerts. The audience there is looking for opinion and information, not wellness offerings. The channel’s narrative is about Denver’s problems and politics. A yoga class ad inserted into that feed feels like a cold call.

We moved the budget to two much smaller channels: a Denver hiking and outdoors channel (12,000 subscribers) and a local “Denver Eats & Wellness” channel (8,000 subscribers). The CTA changed from “Try our hot yoga” to “Recover from your weekend hike — Sunday morning stretch session. $10 for first-timers.” The narrative alignment was obvious. People in the hiking channel think about recovery. The ad read like a service to them, not a sales pitch.

Outcome: $400 spent across both channels. 890 clicks. $0.45 CPC. Fifty-six class bookings. At $25 per class (first-timer pricing), that’s $1,400 revenue. Fourteen of those people bought a 10-class pass ($180 each) within two weeks. Total revenue: $1,400 + $2,520 = $3,920 against a $400 spend. The channel selection was the only variable we changed.

## Telegram Ads vs. Google Ads for Local Service Businesses

I run this comparison every time I talk to a local business owner because the math is rarely what they expect. Google Ads feels like the safe bet — show up when someone searches “plumber near me” or “coffee shop Austin.” But safe doesn’t always mean efficient.

Here’s what happened with a real client. A locksmith in Chicago — let’s call his business Windy City Locks — was spending $3,200/month on Google Ads. His average cost per click was $9.80. For a service where the average job is $150, that’s a 6.5% cost of acquisition before you even talk about tools or dispatch. His conversion rate from click to job was 12%. So each new customer cost him $81.67 in ad spend.

I suggested a Telegram test. He laughed. “My customers are searching for me. Telegram is for teenagers and crypto people.” I didn’t argue. I asked for $500 of his monthly budget — just fifteen percent — to run a six-week test on Telegram.

The ad went into three Chicago-specific Telegram channels: two neighborhood community groups and one “Chicago Small Business Owners” channel. The copy was direct: “Locked out of your car in Lincoln Park? I’m usually there in 18 minutes. Send this message for a $20 discount on the service fee.” The CTA was a simple “Reply to this ad with LOCKOUT.”

Results: $500 spent. 312 replies. 94 turned into actual jobs — people who were genuinely locked out and ready to pay. At $150 average job, that’s $14,100 in revenue from $500 in ad spend. His cost per new customer on Telegram: $5.32. Compare that to $81.67 on Google.

I’m not saying fire your Google Ads manager. Google Ads captures demand that already exists. But Telegram creates demand by being present in spaces where people are already talking, already bored, already scrolling. The locksmith succeeded because his offer matched a moment of urgency. People who are locked out of their car don’t always search — they panic-text a friend, post in a neighborhood group, or look for someone who seems trustworthy in a channel they already read.

The practical takeaway: Use Google Ads for people who know what they want. Use Telegram for people who are in the right context but haven’t decided yet. If your average ticket is under $100, Telegram’s lower CPC makes the math work faster. If your ticket is over $500, Google Ads still wins because the intent is higher. Most local businesses fall in the $30–$200 range. That’s Telegram’s sweet spot.

## How to Structure a Telegram Campaign That Doesn’t Waste Money

Most guides tell you to “test different creatives.” That’s like telling someone to “drive better.” Useless without specifics. Here’s an actual campaign structure I use for small local businesses.

**Step 1: Budget allocation.** Start with $400–$600/month for one city. Anything less and you won’t get enough data. Anything more and you haven’t earned the scaling. I’ve seen businesses burn $2,000 in a week because they didn’t set a daily cap. Set a daily cap of $20–$25.

**Step 2: Channel selection.** You need three to five channels minimum. Don’t put all your money into one channel. Use Telegram’s native channel search or tools like TGStat to find channels in your city. Look for channels with 5,000–50,000 subscribers. Below 5,000, the engagement is usually too low to matter. Above 50,000, the audience becomes too broad and the CPM spikes.

**Step 3: Offer design.** Do not make your offer a percentage discount. “20% off” sounds generic. Make it specific: “Free cold brew with any sandwich purchase. Show this message.” Or “$10 off your first cut. Code: DENVER10.” Specific offers get remembered. Generic offers get scrolled past.

**Step 4: Tracking.** Telegram doesn’t have a pixel. You need a manual tracking method. Use unique discount codes, dedicated phone numbers (Google Voice works fine), or a landing page with UTM parameters. I prefer the “mention this ad” approach for physical businesses because it creates a conversation at the register. “How did you hear about us?” becomes a data point, not a formality.

**Step 5: Measurement window.** Do not judge a campaign in the first ten days. Telegram audiences need to see your ad three to five times before they act. That’s normal. The mistake is killing a campaign on day seven because you only got two clicks. Give it three weeks minimum. After that, if your cost per action is more than 30% of your average ticket value, pause and rethink the channel or the offer.

**Step 6: List building.** Every Telegram ad campaign should have a secondary goal. The primary goal is the sale. The secondary goal is capturing that person into an email list, SMS list, or Telegram channel you own. I use Mailchimp for email and a simple Telegram channel for ongoing communication. If you don’t capture them, you have to pay to reach them again. That’s not a business model. That’s renting.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn’t Telegram just for scammers and crypto bros? I don’t want my brand associated with that.**

You’re not wrong that Telegram has a reputation problem in certain corners of the internet. But so does email — remember the Nigerian prince scams? The question isn’t “what is the platform’s worst use case?” It’s “is my actual target customer on this platform?” For local businesses in the US, the data shows Telegram usage has grown 40% year over year in 2023 and 2024, driven by regular users, not just crypto enthusiasts. The audience skews 25–45, urban, and tech-comfortable. If your customers own iPhones and use WhatsApp or Venmo, they’re probably on Telegram too. You’re not running ads in dark web channels. You’re running ads in your city’s neighborhood chat, the local hiking group, or the “Austin Food Lovers” channel. The context matters more than the platform’s overall reputation.

**Q: How much money do I need to start without wasting it?**

Absolute minimum: $400/month for one month. Anything under that and you won’t get enough impressions or clicks to know if the channel works for you. Most small businesses I work with start at $500–$800/month and scale to $1,500–$2,000/month if the cost per acquisition stays under 20% of their average ticket. Do not start with $100 and expect to learn anything. You’ll have three data points and a bad opinion.

**Q: Can I run Telegram ads myself without an agency or a freelancer?**

Yes, if you’re comfortable with a moderately ugly advertising dashboard. Telegram’s ad platform isn’t as polished as Google Ads or Meta Ads Manager. It feels a little clunky — think 2015-era ad tool. But the basics are straightforward: choose your channels, set your budget, write your copy, upload your image. The hard part isn’t the setup. It’s the channel selection and copywriting. If you can write a decent email newsletter, you can probably handle Telegram ads. If you struggle to write a Facebook post that gets comments, hire someone for the copy at least.

**Q: How do I know my ad is actually being seen by real people, not bots?**

This is the question I get most often, and it’s fair. Telegram does have bots. But the ad platform shows placements in real channels with real moderators, and your ad goes through a review process. I’ve run over fifty Telegram campaigns for clients. The bot traffic on Telegram ads is lower than what I see on Facebook or Twitter. The bigger risk isn’t bots — it’s running your ad in a channel where the subscribers are inactive. Check the channel’s post engagement before you buy. If a channel has 50,000 subscribers but gets 12 likes on a post, don’t advertise there. That’s a dead audience.

**Q: What kind of local business gets the worst results on Telegram?**

Anything with a long sales cycle or a high-ticket item that requires trust before purchase. I tried running Telegram ads for a $5,000 kitchen renovation service in Austin. The cost per click was low — $0.80 — but the conversion rate was 0.2%. People on Telegram aren’t shopping for major home renovations in their feed. They’re scrolling for quick entertainment or local news. The businesses that win on Telegram are the ones where the decision can happen in under five minutes: coffee, food, haircuts, car washes, pet services, urgent services (locksmiths, plumbers, electricians). If your customer needs to think about it for a week, Telegram ads probably aren’t your best channel.

**Q: What happens if my competitor starts using Telegram ads before I do?**

Then they’ll pay slightly higher CPMs for the channels you both want. But Telegram’s ad inventory isn’t scarce the way Facebook’s is. There are thousands of local channels in any mid-sized US city. Your competitor can’t “lock up” Telegram the way they can bid up keywords in Google Ads. The window for early adoption is real — you’ll get cheaper rates and better placement now than you will in two years. But it’s not a zero-sum game. Start now, but don’t panic.
