## Segmentation Secrets: How to Send the Right Grooming Tips to the Right Pet Owner

Segmentation isn’t just about avoiding mistakes—it’s the engine that turns a generic email list into a revenue-generating machine. Let’s get practical. Here are three segmentation strategies that pet groomers can implement in one afternoon using any email platform that supports tags or custom fields.

### Strategy 1: Behavioral Segmentation Based on Visit Recency

Every client falls into one of three buckets: active (visited within the last 90 days), lapsed (90–180 days since last visit), or lost (over 180 days). Create separate email flows for each group.

- **Active clients:** Send a “pre-booking reminder” 10 days before their typical appointment date. If you know Poodle Patron visits every 6 weeks, schedule an email to drop at week 5 saying, “It’s almost time for Patron’s next trim—here’s a link to book your preferred time slot.” Add a personal touch: mention the groomer’s name last worked on the dog.
- **Lapsed clients:** Send a “We miss you” series that includes a photo of the pet from their last visit (if you have it), a testimonial from another happy client, and a limited-time discount (e.g., $15 off next groom, valid for 14 days). This series performs best when spaced 3–4 days apart, with the discount offer in the second or third email.
- **Lost clients:** Send a single “We’d love to see you again” email every 60 days, but with a higher-value incentive—say, 20% off a full groom. If they still don’t respond after two attempts, move them to a “cold list” and stop mailing to avoid spam complaints.

### Strategy 2: Breed‑Specific Content Based on Known Data

If you collect breed information at check-in (even manually), you can create micro‑segments that feel hyper‑personal. For instance:

- **Double‑coated breeds** (Huskies, Golden Retrievers, Aussies, Shelties): Send seasonal de‑shedding tips, warnings about matting behind the ears, and reminders for undercoat raking. Include a photo of a recent de‑shedding package result.
- **Short‑haired breeds** (Labradors, Boxers, Beagles): Focus on nail care, ear cleaning, and skin health. Offer a discounted “nail trim only” add-on.
- **Toy and small breeds** (Chihuahuas, Yorkies, Shih Tzus): Tear‑stain removal and dental hygiene tips work well here. You can also promote “puppy’s first groom” packages.

Breed‑specific emails don’t need to be long—200 words with a single photo and a booking button. But when a chihuahua owner receives “Tips for Keeping Your Chihuahua’s Eyes Clean,” they feel seen. One groomer in Chicago reported a 41% click‑through rate on breed‑specific grooming tips compared to 12% on her general newsletter.

### Strategy 3: Lifecycle Stage Segmentation Based on Pet Age

Pets go through distinct grooming needs as they age. You can ask for the pet’s age on your intake form and tag clients accordingly.

- **Puppy clients (under 1 year):** Send a series about socializing the puppy to grooming, step‑by‑step at‑home brushing, and when to start professional grooming. Include a “Free Nail Trim with First Full Groom” offer.
- **Adult clients (1–7 years):** Focus on maintenance: seasonal shedding, tick/flea prevention, and coat conditioning. Send reminders for semi‑annual health checks.
- **Senior clients (8+ years):** Highlight senior‑friendly grooming—gentle handling, mobility support, and shorter sessions. Offer a “Senior Pet Comfort Package” with a massage or extra brushing time. Including a testimonial from another senior pet owner builds trust.

By aligning your content with the pet’s life stage, you demonstrate expertise and empathy. Clients reward that by staying loyal—and referring friends.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve sat through enough agency reviews to know that most small business email strategies fail for the same three reasons. Here’s what I’ve seen pet groomers do — and what actually fixed it.

### Mistake #1: Only sending emails when you need a booking

A groomer in Austin, Texas — let’s call her shop “Aussie Cuts” — had 340 clients on her email list. She sent one email every six weeks: “We have openings next Thursday.” Open rate was fine, but bookings barely moved. She couldn’t figure out why nobody responded.

**The problem:** She only emailed when she wanted something. No value. No relationship. Just a request.

**The fix:** I had her set up a three-email sequence that ran automatically for new clients over the first 60 days. The first email was a welcome note with a photo of their dog from their first visit. The second was a short article on how often to bathe a Goldendoodle (with a link to rebook at the bottom). The third was a “we miss you” note after six weeks of no visit.

**The outcome:** Within 90 days, her rebooking rate went from 22% to 41%. That translated to roughly $1,400 in additional monthly revenue from clients who had previously fallen off. She spent exactly zero extra dollars on ads.

### Mistake #2: Sending the same email to everyone

I worked with a groomer in Nashville who had a list of 600 people — but her emails had a 34% unsubscribe rate over six months. That’s brutal. When I looked at her Mailchimp account, she was sending “25% off all grooms” to every single person. Including people who had brought their dog in three weeks ago. Including people who only did nail trims.

**The problem:** She was treating all clients like they had the same needs. A client who just had a full groom doesn’t need a coupon. A client who only does nail trims probably doesn’t want a full-price ad for a complete cut.

**The fix:** We segmented her list into three buckets based on Booksy history: new (one visit only), regular (monthly), and lapsed (no visit in 90+ days). New clients got a 20% off second visit offer. Regulars got a reminder plus a referral bonus. Lapsed got a “we miss your dog” with a stronger incentive.

**The outcome:** Unsubscribes dropped to 4%. Revenue from email campaigns went from roughly $200/month to $1,100/month. Same list, same number of emails, just different messages.

### Mistake #3: No automation at all

A groomer in Portland, Oregon told me she was manually typing out “thanks for booking with us” emails at 10pm after closing. She had 50+ clients a week and was spending hours on something a $15/month tool could do.

**The problem:** She was burning time she could have spent grooming or marketing. And because she was inconsistent, some clients never got a reminder and simply didn’t show.

**The fix:** I set her up with Square’s free automation — appointment reminders 48 hours before, a thank-you email the day after, and a rebook prompt two weeks later for clients who hadn’t scheduled their next visit.

**The outcome:** No-shows dropped from 18% to 6%. She saved about 8 hours a week on manual emails. And the automated rebook prompt brought in an additional 12 bookings per month — roughly $900 in revenue she had been leaving on the table.

## What to Send (And When to Send It)

Most email guides tell you to “send valuable content.” That’s not helpful. Here’s what actually works for pet groomers, based on what I’ve seen work across 40+ small businesses.

### The 3-email cycle that works

You don’t need a weekly newsletter or a daily deal. You need three types of emails, sent on a predictable schedule:

1. **The reminder** (48 hours before booking) — Just the date, time, and a link to reschedule. No fluff. Clients forget. This is your highest-value email.

2. **The post-visit thank-you** (24 hours after) — One photo of their dog, a short note, and a request to leave a Google review. Don’t ask for a booking here. They just handed you $80. Give them a day.

3. **The rebook prompt** (3–4 weeks later, depending on breed) — “It’s been three weeks since Max’s last groom. Dogs with double coats typically need a visit every 4–6 weeks. Here’s a link to book.” No discount needed if the timing is right.

I tested this cycle with a groomer in Chicago who had 200 clients. In the first month, 32 people rebooked through the prompt email alone. At an average ticket of $75, that’s $2,400 in revenue that would have been lost without the automated sequence.

### When to send

Tuesday or Wednesday mornings, between 9am and 11am local time. That’s when small business owners I’ve worked with see the highest open and click rates for pet services. Saturday emails get ignored. Monday afternoons get buried. Thursday evenings occasionally work, but mornings are consistently better.

### What about holidays?

Send a “book now for holiday grooming” email three weeks before Christmas, two weeks before Thanksgiving, and one week before any major holiday that gets people thinking about their dog’s appearance. I’ve seen groomers fill their entire holiday schedule with a single email sent at the right time. One shop in Denver added $3,800 in December revenue from one well-timed email to 400 people.

## Getting People on Your List Without Being Annoying

A common objection I hear: “My clients won’t give me their email.” That’s usually a sign you’re asking the wrong way.

### The checkout ask

Every single transaction is a chance to get an email address. When a client pays with Square or a card terminal, say: “I’ll send you a photo of Fido after his groom — what’s the best email for that?” You’re not asking for a newsletter. You’re offering a photo of their dog. Nobody says no to that.

Then you send the photo via email, and now you have a warm contact. That’s how you build a list without ever saying “sign up for our mailing list.”

### The Yelp and Google integration

If you have a Yelp or Google Business profile, add a link to your email signup in the booking confirmation. Most groomers I work with don’t even know this option exists. Go to your Google Business profile, add a “book online” button through Booksy or Vagaro, and in the confirmation email that tool sends, include a line at the bottom: “Want grooming tips and reminders? Add your email here.” You’ll pick up another 10–15% of clients who would never give you their email in person.

### The referral bonus

Here’s a specific setup I’ve seen work at a shop in Atlanta: When a client refers a friend, both get $10 off their next groom. Instead of running a manual system, they used a simple Mailchimp integration with a referral link. Existing clients got an email with their unique link. When the new client booked through it, the discount was auto-applied.

That shop got 47 referrals in three months from a list of 280 people. At $65 per groom, that’s over $3,000 in new revenue. The cost was $940 in discounts. Net gain: roughly $2,000.

### The “we take photos” hook

This is my favorite low-cost growth tactic. Put a small sign at your front desk: “We take a photo of every dog after their groom. Want a copy? Drop your email here.” Then have a dedicated iPad or tablet (even a cheap $50 one) with a simple Google Form. Every client who leaves their email gets the photo sent within an hour. You get a warm contact. They get a cute picture of their dog. Both sides win.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Will my emails just end up in spam?**

Not if you’re sending them to people who actually opted in. The biggest reason small business emails hit spam is because they bought a list or added people without permission. If you ask for emails at checkout, send from a real domain (not Gmail), and keep your send volume reasonable (no more than 1–2 per week), you’ll be fine. I’ve managed lists for pet groomers with 99% deliverability for over two years.

**Q: I only have 50 emails. Is it worth even doing this?**

Fifty engaged customers is worth more than 5,000 cold ones. If those 50 people are your actual clients, you can generate $1,000–$2,000 a month from them with the right automation. One groomer in Seattle started with 38 emails and added $800 in monthly revenue within 60 days. Start with what you have, build from there.

**Q: Can’t I just use Instagram or Facebook? Why email?**

You can, but you don’t own those audiences. Instagram changes its algorithm, your posts stop being seen, and you’re competing with everyone else. Email is something you own. Your list is yours. If Instagram goes down tomorrow, you still have a way to reach paying customers. I’ve seen too many shops lose half their business because a social account got suspended.

**Q: How much time will this take to set up?**

If you use Mailchimp’s free plan and Square’s automation, you can set up the three-email cycle I described in about 90 minutes. After that, maybe 15 minutes a week to check the list and write one new email per month. The time you save on manual reminders alone will pay back the setup time within two weeks.

**Q: Do I need a separate tool for this, or can I use what I already have?**

Check your current booking software first. Booksy, Vagaro, and Square Appointment all have built-in email features. Most people are paying for these tools and not using half the functionality. If your current tool doesn’t support it, Mailchimp’s free tier works fine for up to 500 contacts. You don’t need an expensive platform.

**Q: What if my clients are older and don’t check email?**

I hear this a lot, and it’s usually wrong. The average email user in the US checks their inbox 15 times a day, including people over 60. The real issue is usually that the email content isn’t relevant. Send a photo of their dog and a simple reminder, and they’ll open it. I’ve worked with a groomer whose average client age was 58 — open rates were 68%.
