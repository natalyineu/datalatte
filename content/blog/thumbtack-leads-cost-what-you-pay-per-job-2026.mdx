If you run a home service business — plumbing, cleaning, electrical work, landscaping — Thumbtack is probably on your radar. And the first question every business owner asks is the same: how much do leads actually cost?

The honest answer: between **$3 and $75 per lead**, depending on your trade, your city, and how competitive your category is. That range is wide on purpose — a house cleaning lead in rural Kansas costs very differently from an HVAC lead in Manhattan.

This guide breaks down real Thumbtack lead costs by trade, explains what drives prices up or down, and tells you how to get more value from every dollar you spend.

---

## How Thumbtack Pricing Works

Thumbtack does not charge a monthly subscription. You pay per lead — specifically, each time a potential customer either contacts you directly or you send them a quote that they open.

There are two main ways leads come in:

**Direct leads**: A customer finds your profile and messages you. You're charged automatically when this happens. You don't get to screen the lead before paying.

**Opportunity leads**: You browse available jobs and decide which ones to quote. You pay when the customer opens your quote. If they never open it, you pay nothing.

This matters because direct leads are riskier — you pay whether or not the job is a good fit. Opportunity leads let you filter before spending.

## What Drives Your Lead Cost Up

**Location**: New York, LA, San Francisco, Chicago — expect to pay 40–60% more than national averages. Competition is higher, demand is higher, Thumbtack charges accordingly.

**Time of year**: Landscaping leads spike in spring. HVAC leads spike in summer and winter. Thumbtack adjusts pricing dynamically based on demand.

**Your profile completeness**: A profile with 5+ reviews, photos, and a full bio gets better lead quality and sometimes lower costs. Thumbtack favors active, complete profiles in its algorithm.

**How quickly you respond**: Pros who respond within 5 minutes get priority placement. Slower response = fewer leads or higher cost to get visibility.

**Targeting radius**: The tighter your service area, the fewer leads available — which can mean either lower costs (less competition for that zone) or zero leads at all.

## How to Reduce Your Cost Per Acquired Customer

A $35 plumbing lead sounds expensive until you realize you're converting 1 in 4 into a $400 job. Your real cost per customer is $140 for a $400 return — that's fine. The math matters more than the lead cost.

Here's how to make the math work better:

**Respond within 5 minutes.** Thumbtack data shows pros who respond first win the job 50% of the time. Set up Thumbtack notifications on your phone and treat every new lead like a phone call you can't miss.

**Only quote jobs you can win.** If you see an HVAC job 45 minutes away in an area where you have no reviews, skip it. Use opportunity leads to filter ruthlessly. Bad-fit jobs waste lead budget.

**Build reviews as fast as possible.** After every completed job, text the customer: "Hey [Name], glad we could help today. If you have a minute, a quick review on Thumbtack would really help our small business — [link]." Aim for 20+ reviews in your first 60 days.

**Set your budget and track it weekly.** Thumbtack lets you set a weekly lead budget. Start at $100/week. Track how many leads you get, how many you convert, and what the jobs are worth. Adjust after 30 days.

**Use the Thumbtack app, not just the website.** The app gives you faster notifications and easier quote management. Most pros who complain about slow leads are checking email instead of the app.

## Is Thumbtack Worth It?

For home service businesses — cleaning, landscaping, handyman, plumbing, electrical — yes, if you have the bandwidth to respond fast and follow up.

For businesses that rely on repeat clients (salons, fitness studios, coffee shops) — no. Thumbtack is project-based, not relationship-based. You'll pay for leads that never come back.

The sweet spot is a service business with $300–$1,000 average job value, where one converted lead covers 5–10 leads' worth of cost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does Thumbtack charge me even if I don't get the job?**

Yes — for direct leads (when a customer contacts you), you're charged when they reach out, not when you win the job. For opportunity leads (when you send quotes), you're charged when the customer opens your quote. Neither type guarantees you'll win the work. This is why tracking your conversion rate matters — not just your lead count.

**Q: Can I get a refund on a bad Thumbtack lead?**

Yes. Thumbtack has a lead credit policy. If a customer gave false information, asked for a service you don't offer, or the job location was outside your stated service area, you can request a credit. Go to the lead in your app → tap "Get help with this lead" → explain the issue. Credits are issued within 3–5 business days. I've seen businesses recover 15–20% of their monthly lead spend through legitimate credits.

**Q: How long does it take to see results on Thumbtack?**

Expect 2–4 weeks before your profile gains traction. The first week you'll get few leads. The algorithm is learning your trade and location. After you accumulate 5+ reviews and have a complete profile, lead volume typically increases 40–60%. Don't judge Thumbtack in the first two weeks.

**Q: Should I use Thumbtack and Google Local Services Ads at the same time?**

Yes, if budget allows. They serve the same audience but through different paths. Google LSA captures people actively searching right now. Thumbtack captures people planning a project and comparing options. Running both gives you coverage across both intent stages. I usually recommend starting with Google LSA (higher intent = higher conversion) and adding Thumbtack once that's profitable.

**Q: What's the minimum budget to test Thumbtack effectively?**

$200–$300/month for a 30-day test. Less than that and you won't get enough lead volume to know if it's working. Set your weekly budget to $50–$75, respond to every lead within 5 minutes, and track which ones convert. At the end of 30 days, calculate your cost per customer and compare it to what that customer is worth. That math tells you whether to scale up or move on.
