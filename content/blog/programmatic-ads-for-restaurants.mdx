## What are Programmatic Ads for Restaurants?
Programmatic ads for restaurants are a type of online advertising that uses automation to buy and place ads in front of hungry customers. This approach allows you to target specific audiences, increase brand awareness, and drive sales.

Programmatic advertising works by using data and algorithms to identify and target potential customers. For restaurants, this means targeting people who have shown interest in food, dining out, or ordering takeout. You can also target customers who have visited your website or interacted with your brand online.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting Too Broad a Radius

A BBQ joint in Portland, Oregon — let's call them Smoky Trails — was spending $1,800/month on programmatic display ads. The owner told me, "We're getting impressions, but nobody's showing up." I pulled their campaign data. They were targeting anyone within a 15-mile radius of their location.

In Portland, 15 miles covers Beaverton, Gresham, Hillsboro, and about six other suburbs. People in those areas don't drive past ten other BBQ spots to get to Smoky Trails. The ads were showing to people who had no business being targeted.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the targeting to a 3-mile radius with a custom heat map for lunch traffic, and a 5-mile radius for dinner. Then we added a conversion pixel from their online ordering system (they used Toast) to track who actually ordered.

**The outcome:** Within 30 days, their cost per click dropped from $2.40 to $0.85. Their online orders increased 34% in the first month. That $1,800 budget went from generating about $3,200 in attributable revenue to $6,100. The owner's exact words: "I should have fired my last agency sooner."

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Time-of-Day Targeting

A breakfast-and-lunch cafe in Nashville called Biscuit & Co. ran display ads 24/7. Their logic: "More impressions must mean more customers." They were spending $900/month on programmatic ads through a self-serve platform.

The problem: Their ads were showing at 2 AM to people scrolling their phones in bed. Those same ads ran at 3 PM — long after their kitchen closed at 2:30 PM. They were literally paying to advertise when they couldn't serve customers.

**The fix:** We paused ads between 8 PM and 6 AM. Then we built two dayparting blocks: 6 AM–10:30 AM for breakfast (push mobile banner ads with coffee and biscuit imagery), and 11 AM–2 PM for lunch (push ads with combo meal offers). We used Google Ads' automated bidding with dayparting rules.

**The outcome:** Their ad spend dropped to $600/month because we eliminated six hours of useless impressions. But their in-store redemptions increased 28%. Their cost-per-redemption went from $12 to $4.50. They stopped getting angry calls from people showing up at 3:30 PM thinking they were still serving lunch.

### Mistake 3: Using Generic Creative With No Offer

A pizza spot in Chicago's Wicker Park neighborhood — let's call it Slice House — ran programmatic ads with a photo of a cheese pizza and the headline "Best Pizza in Chicago." That's it. No offer. No call to action beyond "Order Now."

They spent $1,500/month. They got 4,300 clicks in January. They attributed maybe $2,000 in sales to those ads. That's a terrible return.

**The problem:** Nobody needs another generic pizza ad. Every pizza place in Chicago claims they're the best. The ad gave people no reason to choose Slice House over the place two blocks away.

**The fix:** We tested three creative variations with specific offers:
- "First order? 20% off your online order. Code: WICKER20"
- "Buy a large, get a free 2-topping. Enter FREE2TOP at checkout."
- "Free delivery on orders over $25. Code: NODELIVERYFEE"

We used a simple A/B test with Google's Display & Video 360. Each variant ran for two weeks on a $500/month budget.

**The outcome:** The "Buy a large, get a free 2-topping" ad crushed the others. It had a 5.2% click-through rate — more than double the original ad. Their cost per acquisition dropped from $18 to $6. They tracked 214 redemptions of that code in the first month alone, generating $4,280 in revenue on $500 in ad spend.

### Mistake 4: Not Retargeting Website Visitors

A taco shop in Austin, Texas — Torchy's Taco Trailer (not the chain, a local spot) — had a website that got about 1,200 visitors a month. They were running programmatic ads to cold audiences only. Nobody who visited their site and left without ordering was ever followed up on.

The owner told me, "I figured if they didn't order, they weren't interested." That's wrong. Most people browse restaurant menus multiple times before ordering. They're comparison shopping. They're checking prices. They're seeing if delivery is available.

**The fix:** We installed a Facebook pixel and a Google Ads retargeting tag on their website. Then we built a retargeting campaign that showed ads specifically to people who visited the menu page but didn't order. The ad showed photos of their three most popular tacos with a "Hungry yet? Order now" button.

**The outcome:** In six weeks, retargeting accounted for 22% of their total online orders. The cost per retargeted conversion was $4.20, compared to $11.80 for cold audience ads. That $300/month retargeting spend generated $2,100 in revenue. The owner stopped thinking of website visitors as "lookers who won't buy" and started treating them as "people who need one more nudge."

## How to Set Up Your First Programmatic Campaign (For Under $500/Month)

Most guides tell you programmatic advertising requires enterprise budgets. That's true if you're trying to buy NBC homepage takeovers. It's not true for local restaurant ads.

Here's exactly how you set up a campaign for $400–$500/month. I've done this with three small businesses this year. It works.

**Step 1: Choose your platform.** For sub-$1,000 budgets, don't use a demand-side platform (DSP) like The Trade Desk or Amazon DSP. You'll pay $1,000–$2,000 in minimums before you even buy an ad. Instead, use Google Ads' Display campaigns or a simplified platform like Choozle or Simpli.fi. They have no minimums and let you start small.

**Step 2: Set up conversion tracking.** This is boring. Do it anyway. Connect your Google Ads account to your website's ordering system. If you use Toast, Square Online, or ChowNow, they all have Google Ads integration guides. If you don't track conversions, you're guessing. I've seen too many restaurant owners say "we got a lot of clicks" while their revenue stayed flat. Clicks don't pay rent.

**Step 3: Build your audience layers.** You want three audience segments running simultaneously:
- **People in your delivery zone** who searched for food delivery in the last 7 days (use Google's in-market audiences)
- **People who visited your website** in the last 30 days (use your retargeting pixel)
- **People who are within 2 miles of your location** right now (use location targeting with a 2-mile radius)

**Step 4: Set a reasonable budget.** Start at $15–$20/day. That's $450–$600/month. Run for 30 days. If you don't see at least a 3:1 return on ad spend by day 21, pause and change your creative or targeting. Don't let it ride for three months hoping it gets better.

**Step 5: Use frequency caps.** Set a limit of 3 ads per person per day. More than that, and you're annoying people. I had a client in Denver who was showing the same ad 14 times per day to the same people. Their click-through rate was 0.2%. After capping at 3, it went to 1.8%.

I used this exact setup for a coffee shop in Brooklyn that had a $500 monthly budget. In their first month, they got 47 online orders directly attributed to programmatic ads. Each order averaged $16. That's $752 in revenue on $500 in spend — not amazing, but profitable. By month three, after optimizing, they hit $1,340 in monthly revenue from the same budget.

## Integrating Programmatic Ads With Your Other Marketing

Here's something most articles skip: programmatic ads work best when they're not the only thing you're doing. They're a layer on top of everything else.

**Email + programmatic = better results.** I worked with a yoga studio (not a restaurant, but the principle applies) in Santa Monica that had a Mailchimp list of 2,400 people. They sent a weekly newsletter. Open rates were fine — about 22%. But they weren't converting to class bookings.

We added programmatic display ads targeting anyone who opened their email but didn't click through. The ads showed a "Your mat is waiting" message with a link to book. In six weeks, class bookings from email-engaged audiences increased 41%. The studio spent $350/month on those display ads and saw $1,600 in additional revenue.

**For restaurants, here's what that integration looks like:** Your email list is gold. People who signed up for your newsletter or loyalty program are already warm. Run programmatic ads to them — they're people who like you but forgot about you. The email open is a signal of intent. The display ad is the reminder.

**Yelp and programmatic work together, not separately.** A lot of restaurant owners tell me, "I'm already on Yelp, why do I need programmatic?" Because Yelp shows your ad to people actively searching for restaurants in your area. Programmatic shows your ad to people who haven't searched yet but are likely to be hungry.

A Thai restaurant in Seattle's Capitol Hill neighborhood ran both simultaneously. Yelp ads cost them $400/month and drove about 15 walk-ins per week. The programmatic campaign (also $400/month) targeted people within 2 miles during lunch and dinner hours. The programmatic ads drove 22 online orders per week on average. The two channels didn't cannibalize each other — they reached different stages of the customer journey.

**Square and Toast data is a goldmine.** If you use Square for POS or Toast for online ordering, you have transaction data. You know what people ordered, when they ordered, and how much they spent. Export that data and use it to build lookalike audiences in your ad platform.

One coffee shop in Denver did this. They exported their top 200 customers by order frequency from Square, then built a lookalike audience in Google Ads. They targeted that audience with display ads offering "Your first drink is on us." The campaign generated 89 coupon redemptions in 30 days, 52 of which became repeat customers within two months.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why should I use programmatic ads instead of just Facebook or Instagram ads?**

Because they catch people who aren't on social media. Programmatic display ads show up on news sites, weather apps, recipe blogs, and review platforms — places your customers visit throughout the day. I've seen plenty of restaurants get great results from social ads. But if your entire strategy depends on people scrolling Facebook at the right moment, you're missing everyone who doesn't. Programmatic fills that gap. You can run social and programmatic together. That's usually the right answer.

**Q: Can I run programmatic ads if I don't have a website?**

Technically, yes. Practically, no. You need somewhere to send people. If you don't have a website, use a direct link to your Toast or Square Online ordering page. Or send people to your Google Business Profile. But you need a landing page with a clear action — "order now" or "get the offer." Without that, you're paying for eyeballs with no conversion path. That's a waste.

**Q: How much do I need to spend to see results?**

For a single-location restaurant, start at $400–$600/month. Some restaurants see results at $300/month if they're in a dense urban area with good targeting. But don't expect miracles at $200/month. Programmatic works on volume and frequency. Too small a budget and you won't show up enough times to anyone. I've seen $250/month campaigns generate exactly three clicks over 30 days. That's not enough data to optimize.

**Q: How do I know if programmatic ads are actually working?**

Set up conversion tracking before you launch. Measure orders, coupon redemptions, or phone calls — not just clicks. If your ad platform says you got 900 clicks but your online orders didn't budge, something's wrong. Also use promo codes unique to your programmatic campaign. "Use code DISPLAY10 for 10% off" lets you track exactly how many people came from those ads. Don't rely on attribution models alone. They're wrong half the time.

**Q: Will programmatic ads work for a coffee shop or a small cafe, or is this only for restaurants with $50,000 budgets?**

Yes, they work for coffee shops. A coffee shop in Portland ran programmatic ads for $350/month targeting people within 1 mile during morning hours (6:30 AM–10 AM). They offered a "free pastry with any drink" coupon. In four weeks, 62 people redeemed the coupon. 19 of those became regulars who came back at least twice per week. Their total ad spend was $350. The revenue from those 62 redemptions was about $480 in drink sales. The long-term value of those 19 regulars? Probably over $5,000 each across the year. Yes, it works for small shops.

**Q: What if I've tried online ads before and they didn't work?**

Ask yourself three questions: Did you track conversions? Did you target the right radius? Did you use an actual offer, not just a logo and a tagline? Nine times out of ten, when someone tells me online ads didn't work, the answer is one of those three. I had a client in Austin who swore programmatic ads were a scam. Turned out they were targeting a 25-mile radius with no offer and no conversion tracking. After fixing all three, their second attempt generated a 4:1 return. The ads weren't broken. The setup was.
