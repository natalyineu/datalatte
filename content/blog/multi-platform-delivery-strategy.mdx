### 1. Choose the Right Platforms

The first step in creating a multi-platform delivery strategy is to choose the platforms that are right for your business. Not every platform is a good fit for every restaurant, so it's essential to do your research and choose the ones that will give you the best return on investment.

For example, if you're a coffee shop, you may want to focus on platforms like Glovo and Wolt, which are popular among coffee lovers. If you're a restaurant, you may want to focus on platforms like Uber Eats and DoorDash.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Signing Up for Every Platform “Just to See”

A coffee shop in Austin, Texas — let’s call it Buzz & Bean — decided they needed to be everywhere. They signed up for Uber Eats, DoorDash, Grubhub, Postmates, and Wolt in the same month. The owner told me “we wanted maximum exposure.” What they got was five separate tablet rentals ($50–$75 each per month), a stack of tangled cables, and five different notification sounds going off at the same time.

After three months, they pulled the data. Two platforms accounted for 88% of their total delivery revenue. One platform had exactly four orders. Another had zero. They were paying $325/month in tablet fees alone for platforms that generated a combined $187 in revenue over the entire period. The fix was brutal but simple: drop the two dead platforms, keep the two that worked, and test a third only if there was clear customer demand (e.g., customers asking for it at the counter). They also added a link on their website to the two active platforms so customers didn’t have to search.

Outcome: They saved $325/month in tablet fees and cut the time spent managing orders by about eight hours a week. The two remaining platforms got more attention — optimized photos, better descriptions, faster response to reviews — and revenue on those platforms grew 18% in the next two months. Total monthly profit improvement: about $1,100.

**What to do instead:** Run a 60-day test on no more than three platforms. Rank them by actual orders, not by brand recognition. Drop any platform that brings in less than 10% of your total delivery revenue unless there’s a strategic reason (e.g., it reaches a neighborhood your other platforms don’t).

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Commission Structures When Pricing

An Italian restaurant in Portland, Oregon — Mama’s Kitchen — launched on Uber Eats and DoorDash with the same menu prices they used in-house. The owner thought this was “fair” to customers. What she didn’t account for was that Uber Eats charged a 30% commission on the subtotal, while DoorDash charged 25% but with a lower per-order fee. After three months, she ran the numbers: average order on Uber Eats was $18. That meant she paid $5.40 per order in commission. Her food cost was $6.50. Packaging added another $1.20. She was losing money on every single Uber Eats order.

She had two choices: raise prices on the platform or shut it off. She tested a 22% price increase on Uber Eats only. Orders dropped 8% — customers noticed but didn’t abandon entirely. The new average order was $21.96. Commission became $6.59. Food cost stayed $6.50, packaging $1.20. Now she was making $7.67 gross profit per order before overhead — positive for the first time. On DoorDash, she kept prices closer to in-store but added a 5% “delivery surcharge” in the notes (DoorDash allows it in some markets). Volume stayed flat.

Outcome: Monthly profit on Uber Eats went from -$1,200 to +$600. On DoorDash, profit went from -$200 to +$150. Combined gain: $1,950/month. She also learned that customers on delivery platforms are less price-sensitive than you think — they’re paying for convenience, not a deal.

**What to do instead:** Calculate your breakeven price per platform. Include commission, packaging, payment processing fees (another 2–3% typically), and the time it takes to bag the order. Mark up your menu on high-commission platforms by at least 15–20%. Test it for two weeks. If orders drop more than 15%, adjust. If they don’t, you’ve found your pricing sweet spot.

### Mistake 3: Not Integrating Delivery with Your POS

A sushi restaurant in Denver, Colorado — Rocky Mountain Roll — was processing all delivery orders manually. The server would see an order come in on a tablet, punch it into the Square POS, then type the details on a sticky note for the kitchen. Mistakes happened constantly. Wrong rolls, missing spicy mayo, orders sent to the wrong address. They were issuing refunds and remakes on about 10% of all delivery orders. At $18 per average order, that was $1.80 lost per order — and they did 450 delivery orders per month. That’s $810/month in straight waste, plus the time spent dealing with angry customers.

The fix was a middleware tool called Chowly. It connects the delivery platform directly to the Square POS, so when an order comes in on DoorDash, it appears as a ticket in the POS automatically. No manual entry. No sticky notes. The installation took an afternoon, and the cost was $99/month.

Outcome: Refunds dropped to under 1% of orders. Waste went from $810/month to roughly $80/month. Staff saved about 12 hours per week on data entry and corrections. And customer ratings on the platforms went from 4.0 stars to 4.6 stars within six weeks. Fewer negative reviews meant higher visibility in search results — they saw a 22% increase in delivery order volume in the next three months.

**What to do instead:** If you’re using Square, Toast, or Clover, check if your POS has a native integration with the platforms you’re on. If not, use a middleware like Chowly, Ordermark, or Cuboh. Don’t try to “save money” by skipping this — the cost of errors is higher than the subscription fee. Also, train one person on your team to handle the integration setup so you’re not the only one who knows how it works.

### Mistake 4: Treating All Delivery Platforms Like They’re the Same

A breakfast-and-lunch spot in Nashville, Tennessee — Biscuit Barn — signed up for Wolt and Grubhub at the same time. They used the same menu, same photos, same description. After two months, Wolt orders were averaging $14 with a 20% commission. Grubhub orders were averaging $22 with a 28% commission. The owner assumed Grubhub was “better” because the average ticket was higher. But Grubhub’s commission plus a higher chargeback rate (customers disputing orders) meant the net margin on Grubhub was actually lower.

Once they dug into the data, they realized the platforms attracted different customer profiles. Wolt users were more likely to order single items (a biscuit and coffee) — lower ticket, lower cost to serve. Grubhub users ordered full meals with sides and drinks. The fix was to tailor the menu to each platform. On Wolt, they pushed the “Quick Breakfast” combo ($9.99) with a higher margin because it used cheaper ingredients. On Grubhub, they promoted a “Family Feast” ($34.99) that included a premium protein.

Outcome: Within six weeks, Wolt ticket rose to $12.50 because customers started adding the combo. Grubhub tickets stayed around $24 but margin improved because the family feast had a 68% margin vs. the previous 55%. Total monthly profit on both platforms combined increased by $1,450.

**What to do instead:** Create a platform-specific menu for any platform that accounts for more than 20% of your delivery revenue. Use the platform’s own data (most dashboards show top-selling items, average order size, and time of day) to decide what to feature. Don’t copy-paste the same menu everywhere.

## Using Marketing Channels to Drive Delivery Orders Without Paying Platform Ads

Platforms will happily sell you ad placements — sponsored listings, promoted items, push notifications. And sometimes that makes sense. But for most small businesses, the highest-leverage move is to use your own channels to push customers to the delivery platforms. Here’s what I’ve seen work with real numbers.

**Email campaigns.** A bakery in Nashville — Buttermilk Biscuit Co. — had a Mailchimp list of 1,200 subscribers from their in-store loyalty program. They sent a simple weekly email: “Craving a biscuit? Order on Uber Eats today and get a free lemonade with code FREEDRINK.” The code was set up in the platform’s promo section. The email cost them $0 to send (they were on a free Mailchimp tier) and took 20 minutes to write. In the first month, they drove 87 delivery orders that wouldn’t have happened otherwise, generating $1,530 in incremental revenue. The free lemonade cost them about $0.20, so total giveaway cost was $17.40. Net gain: $1,512.

**SMS marketing.** A coffee shop in Portland, Oregon — Daybreak Roasters — used Square’s built-in SMS tool (part of Square Marketing) to send a text blast to their 500 opted-in customers every Friday. The message: “We’re open for delivery on DoorDash until noon. Today’s special: latte + pastry bundle for $8.50.” Open rates on SMS were 85–90%. They averaged 35 additional orders per Friday, at an average ticket of $9.50. That’s $332.50 per week, or $1,330 per month in extra revenue. Square’s SMS tool cost $10/month for that volume.

**Yelp integration.** A pizza place in Denver — Slice City — added a “Order Delivery” button on their Yelp Business page, which linked directly to their DoorDash storefront. Yelp drives about 6,000 visits per month to their page. Previously, visitors had to search for the restaurant on DoorDash manually. After adding the button, 12% of Yelp visitors clicked through to order. That translated to roughly 180 orders per month, with an average worth $16. That’s $2,880 in monthly revenue that they attribute directly to Yelp. The cost? Free (Yelp Business includes that feature on the free tier).

**Instagram stories.** A pet groomer in Austin — Pampered Paws — doesn’t sell food, but they do sell grooming products and pet supplements. They use Postmates (now part of Uber) to deliver orders from their shop. They post a weekly Instagram Story showing a new product with a “Get it delivered” sticker linking to their Uber Direct page. With 2,500 followers, they get about 100 story views and 8–12 clicks per week. Average order value is $35. That’s $6,000–$8,000 per month in delivery revenue from a channel that costs $0.

**What to watch out for:** Don’t send these campaigns every single day. Two to three times per week max. And track every campaign with a unique promo code or URL so you know exactly which channel drove the order. If you can’t track it, you’re guessing.

## When to Build Your Own Delivery vs. Relying on Platforms

Every small business owner I’ve worked with has asked this at some point: “Should I hire a driver and do delivery myself?” The answer depends on your volume, average ticket, and geography. Here’s a framework I’ve used with clients, backed by real numbers.

**When platforms make sense:** You do fewer than 20 delivery orders per day, or your average order is under $25. At those volumes, hiring a driver costs more than the commission you pay. A driver in Austin costs about $18/hour including wages, taxes, and insurance. If you have 20 orders spread across an 8-hour shift, each order costs $7.20 in labor alone, not counting the vehicle cost. A platform taking 25% of a $20 order takes $5.00. The platform is cheaper by $2.20 per order. Plus, they handle the customer complaints and the routing.

**When in-house delivery wins:** You’re doing 40+ delivery orders per day, your average ticket is above $35, and your delivery radius is small (say, 3–5 miles). A sandwich shop in Chicago — The Gobbler — was doing 55 delivery orders per day via Uber Eats with an average ticket of $32. They were paying 28% commission, or $8.96 per order. They hired a part-time driver for $20/hour, spent $300 on a branded decal for their car, and used Square’s online ordering system to take direct orders. Their cost per order dropped to $4.50 (labor + fuel + packaging). They saved $4.46 per order, times 55 orders per day, times 26 days per month — that’s $6,378 per month in savings. But they also lost the discoverability that Uber Eats provided. Their direct orders grew slowly, and after three months they were at 35 orders per day. Net profit: they saved money on each order but had fewer orders total. Overall, they ended up about $2,000/month better off.

**The hybrid approach:** Use platforms for discovery and in-house for repeat customers. A bakery in New York City — Morning Bun — uses Uber Eats for new customers who find them through search, but puts a card in every delivery bag saying “Next time, order direct at our website and get 10% off.” Within six months, 30% of their delivery orders came through their own site. They gradually shifted the mix to reduce platform dependency.

**Tools to run your own delivery:** Square Online (free with Square POS), Toast Takeout & Delivery (for restaurants), and ChowNow (flat fee platform, no commission). For routing and dispatching, use Route4Me (starts at $149/month) or Onfleet (custom pricing). Don’t try to build a custom solution — these tools handle the hard parts.

**The uncomfortable truth:** Most small businesses should start with platforms and only move to in-house delivery once they have consistent demand and a loyal customer base. Trying to do both at the same time without data is a recipe for burnout. I’ve seen it happen too many times.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I be on every delivery platform available?**

No. Most small businesses do well on two platforms maximum, sometimes three if your city is large enough. More platforms mean more tablets, more fees, more menu updates, and more chaos. Start with the two that have the most users in your zip code. You can check this by searching your address on each platform and seeing how many restaurants are listed — if there’s only one other place, that platform probably isn’t big in your area. If you’re in a smaller city, sometimes only one platform matters.

**Q: Can I pass the commission cost to customers by adding a “delivery fee”?**

You can, but be careful. Some platforms cap the total delivery fee, and customers can see it before checkout. If you add a $2.50 fee and the platform already charges $3.50, the total might scare people off. A better approach is to raise menu prices by 15–20% on the platform and keep the delivery fee at $0. That way the cost is hidden in the item price. Test it — I’ve seen orders drop by only 5% when prices are raised 15%, and profit jumps.

**Q: What’s the best POS integration for delivery?**

It depends on your POS. If you’re on Square for Restaurants, use Chowly or Cuboh. If you’re on Toast, use their native integration (it’s built in for most platforms). If you’re on Clover, use Ordermark. The goal is automatic order sending with no manual entry. Don’t accept a free tablet from a platform if it means double-entry — that “free” tablet will cost you in errors and labor.

**Q: How do I handle delivery driver tips?**

If you use platforms, tips are handled automatically. If you run your own delivery, make sure your online ordering system (Square Online or Toast) includes a tip prompt before checkout. Studies show that customers tip 20% more when the tip is suggested as a percentage rather than a fixed dollar amount. Also, pay your drivers a base wage plus tips. I’ve seen businesses lose drivers because they tried to pay a flat rate and pocket the tips — that ends badly.

**Q: What if a platform deactivates my account or suspends me?**

It happens more often than you think. Usually it’s because of a sudden spike in order volume (the platform thinks it’s fraud) or a complaint from a customer. Keep a backup. Don’t let one platform become 80% of your delivery revenue. Also, respond to all customer messages within 24 hours, even the ones that say “great service” — platforms track response rates. If you get suspended, call the platform’s merchant support line (not email) and ask to speak to a tier-2 agent. Tier-1 will read a script. Tier-2 can actually reinstate accounts.

**Q: How do I measure if delivery is actually profitable?**

Calculate your gross profit per order on each platform. Formula: (average order amount – commission – packaging cost – food cost) per order. Then subtract any platform-specific costs (tablet rental, marketing spend, extra staff time). If the number is negative, you’re losing money on that platform. If it’s positive but less than your in-store margin, decide if the volume is worth it. I use a simple Google Sheet for this — track it weekly for the first three months, then monthly.
