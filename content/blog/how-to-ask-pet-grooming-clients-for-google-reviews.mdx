## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Asking at the Wrong Moment

A groomer in Austin, Texas — let's call her shop "Paws & Claws" — was losing clients faster than she could book them. She was doing great work. Her Instagram had 2,000 followers. But she had seven Google reviews. Total. Over four years.

Every morning, as clients picked up their freshly groomed pups, she'd hand them a business card and say, "If you like our work, please leave us a review on Google!" She was asking at the exact worst time. The dog is excited. The client is wrestling a leash and a credit card. The card goes in a pocket and gets forgotten within an hour. She'd get maybe one review every three months.

I told her to shift the ask by 24 hours. She started sending a text message the next morning with a direct Google review link. No card. No awkward conversation. Just a simple text: "Hope Max is enjoying his new haircut. If you have a moment, we'd love to hear your feedback."

The result: 23 new reviews in the first month. That's not a typo. Within six months, her total review count went from 7 to 112. Her organic traffic from Google Maps increased by 40%. She told me she was filling two extra appointment slots per week — worth about $1,200 in monthly revenue — directly from new clients who found her via those reviews.

**The fix:** Ask after the transaction, not during it. Use text. Make it one tap. Don't be there to watch them do it.

### Mistake #2: Begging for "5 Stars Only"

A pet groomer in Nashville, "The Dog House," had a different problem. Her average rating was 4.9. But she was terrified of anything less than perfect. She'd print out signs saying "If you can't leave 5 stars, please tell us in person instead." She'd whisper to new clients, "We only ask happy customers to review us."

Google caught on. They flagged her page for suspicious review activity. Several reviews were removed. Her ranking dropped. She lost the "local 3-pack" listing she'd held for three years.

I've seen this at three different clients across different niches. Google does not like review gating. It's against their policies. When they detect it, they don't just remove the fake reviews — they suppress your entire listing for weeks.

The fix: Ask all clients to leave a review. Happy, neutral, unhappy — everyone. If you're doing good work, the 5-stars will drown out the occasional 3-star. And a 3-star with a thoughtful response from you actually builds more trust than a page of five identical "great service!" reviews.

After The Dog House stopped gatekeeping and asked everyone, they got 14 new reviews in three weeks. Their average rating settled at 4.7. Their local ranking returned to the top spot within a month. A 4.7 with 85 reviews beats a 4.9 with 12 reviews every single time in the algorithm.

## Where to Ask: The Four Best Moments

The timing of your ask matters more than the wording. Here are the four moments I've found work best, ranked by success rate:

**1. The morning-after text.** Send a photo of the groomed pet with a short note. "Charlie looks amazing. If you're happy with the result, please leave us a Google review here: [link]." This works because the client has had a night to appreciate the fresh groom. Their dog looks great. They're in a good mood. They're on their phone.

**2. The booking confirmation page.** Use Booksy or Square to add a review request to your confirmation page. "First time with us? See what other pet parents say" or "Had a great experience? Leave a review." This catches clients who are already engaged with your business.

**3. The post-appointment email.** Mailchimp or any email tool. 48 hours after the appointment. Not 24 hours (too soon) and not seven days (too late). 48 hours gives them time to notice the quality of the groom. Two days later, they're still thinking about how good their dog looks.

**4. The in-person ask with a prop.** At checkout, point to a small sign or card with the QR code. Don't ask verbally. Just gesture and say, "That would be awesome." The prop does the work. You're not pleading. You're just showing them a simple option.

Avoid: Asking while they're paying, asking while they're wrangling a wet dog, asking on social media DM unless they've specifically complimented you there.

## How to Handle Negative Reviews Without Panicking

Every business owner I've worked with has the same fear: "What if someone leaves a bad review?"

They will. It's not a sign your business is failing. It's a sign you exist.

I worked with a groomer in Brooklyn who got a 1-star review from a client whose dog had a bad reaction to a new shampoo. The client was angry. They claimed the groomer "ruined their dog's coat." The groomer wanted to delete the review, respond angrily, or just ignore it.

We did none of those things.

Here's what we wrote: "We're sorry Bruno had a reaction to our shampoo. We take skin sensitivities seriously and have switched to a hypoallergenic option for all dogs going forward. We've reached out privately to make this right. Thank you for bringing this to our attention."

That response did two things: It showed future clients that the groomer takes feedback seriously and fixes problems. And it actually resolved the situation — the client updated their review to 4 stars after the groomer refunded the appointment and offered a free re-groom with the new product.

Negative reviews are not a liability. They're a trust-building tool — if you respond well. No response = you look like you don't care. A defensive response = you look like a jerk. A calm, specific response that names the problem and the fix = you look like a professional.

Never: Argue, make excuses, blame the client, or ask Google to remove a review unless it violates their policies (fake, spam, off-topic, or contains personal info).

Always: Respond within 48 hours. Thank them. Acknowledge the specific issue. State what you've done to fix it. Offer to resolve it privately.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I offer a discount or free service in exchange for a Google review?**

No. That's against Google's policies. They can remove all your reviews — even the positive ones — if they catch you doing this. I've seen it happen. A doggy daycare in Dallas lost 80 reviews overnight because they ran a "free nail trim for a 5-star review" promotion. They spent six months rebuilding. Don't do it.

**Q: What if a client leaves a fake negative review from a competitor?**

Google does remove fake reviews if you report them. Go to your Google Business Profile, find the review, click the three dots, and select "Flag as inappropriate." Include a short explanation of why it's fake. It usually takes 3-7 days. But here's the real answer: If you have enough positive reviews, one fake negative doesn't matter. Focus on volume, not removing the occasional bad actor.

**Q: How many reviews do I need to show up in the local 3-pack?**

There's no magic number, but from every case I've tracked, the jump happens around 50-100 reviews. I've seen groomers with 40 reviews rank below ones with 120 reviews and a lower average rating. Google prioritizes both quantity and recency. Fresh reviews (last 90 days) carry more weight than old ones. If you have 150 reviews but none from the last six months, a competitor with 50 recent reviews will outrank you.

**Q: Should I only ask happy clients for reviews?**

No. That's against Google's terms. And it hurts you in the long run. Google's algorithm can detect patterns — if all your reviews are 5-stars and come from the same type of client, they may suppress your visibility. A mix of 4 and 5-star reviews with an occasional 3-star looks authentic. Authentic pages rank higher.

**Q: Does it matter if reviews come from mobile vs. desktop?**

Yes. Mobile reviews carry slightly more weight in local search because Google assumes the reviewer is a local searcher using Maps. Most of your clients will leave reviews on their phones anyway, so make your review link mobile-friendly. A QR code that opens Google Maps directly is ideal.

**Q: Can I ask a client to edit a review if they made a mistake?**

Yes. If a client left a 3-star review because they were confused about your pricing, and you've clarified it with them, you can politely ask them to update it. Say: "I'm glad we cleared that up. If you feel it's accurate now, would you mind updating your Google review to reflect our conversation?" Most people will do it. But don't ask them to lie or fabricate a better experience.
