## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Bidding on “Urgent Care Near Me” Without Negatives

A clinic in Austin, Texas, came to me after burning through $1,200 in three weeks with exactly two booked appointments to show for it. They were running broad match on “urgent care near me.” Sounds logical, right?

The problem was Google interpreted “urgent care” broadly. Their ads showed up for people searching “urgent care for pets,” “urgent care dental,” and — my personal favorite — “urgent care for sprained ankle home remedies.” Those clicks cost them between $4 and $8 each. Every single one was wasted.

**The fix:** Switch to phrase match and add negative keywords. For urgent care, your negative list should include: “vet,” “animal,” “dental,” “home remedy,” “wait time” (if you aren’t advertising wait times), and “reviews” (if you have fewer than 50 reviews and don’t want looky-loos).

We rebuilt their campaign with exact and phrase match for “urgent care Austin,” “walk-in clinic Austin,” and “emergency room Austin.” Added negative keywords. Tightened location targeting to a 5-mile radius. Changed ad copy to include “Open 7 days” and “Board-certified physicians.”

**Outcome:** Their cost-per-click dropped from $6.50 to $3.80. Appointment bookings went from 2 to 11 in the next three weeks. They spent $900 less for five times the results. The owner told me he wished he’d called someone who’d done this before instead of watching YouTube tutorials. I don’t disagree.

### Mistake #2: Running Ads 24/7 Without Checking When People Actually Need You

An urgent care chain in Nashville was advertising around the clock. They had the budget, so they figured more hours meant more patients.

What they didn’t account for was the 3 AM click. Between midnight and 6 AM, people aren’t typically searching for urgent care unless they have a genuine emergency — and those people usually go straight to the ER or call 911. What they were getting at 3 AM were insomniacs clicking ads out of curiosity, or people who searched “urgent care” while half-asleep and never showed up.

Over three months, this cost them roughly $2,400 in overnight clicks that converted at exactly zero appointments.

**The fix:** Dayparting. Look at your actual appointment data. For most urgent cares, the sweet spot is 7 AM to 9 PM, with heavier ad spend between 10 AM and 2 PM (when parents realize their kid’s cough isn’t going away) and 4 PM to 7 PM (when people leave work and decide to get checked).

We paused their ads from 10 PM to 6 AM entirely. Shifted 70% of their daily budget into those two peak windows. Wrote ad copy that said “Walk-ins welcome until 8 PM” and “Evening hours available.”

**Outcome:** Same monthly budget. But their cost-per-acquisition dropped from $47 to $22. Weekend appointments went up 28% because they were actually showing up when people were making weekend decisions. The owner said he felt stupid for not checking the data. I told him most people don’t.

### Mistake #3: Generic Landing Pages That Don’t Mention Location or Hours

A Denver urgent care had a landing page that could have been for any clinic in any city. It said “Compassionate care, short wait times, board-certified staff.” Fine. Boring. Useless.

Their ad was sending people to their homepage, which had a general “About Us” section, a photo of the waiting room, and a contact form buried at the bottom. No mention of what insurance they accepted. No mention of current wait times. No phone number at the top.

I tracked one user session. Someone in Denver searched “urgent care Denver open now,” clicked the ad, landed on the homepage, spent 12 seconds scrolling, and left. They didn’t call. They didn’t book. They probably went to the chain two blocks away that had “Open until 8 PM” in its ad copy.

**The fix:** Build dedicated landing pages for each ad group. If you’re running an ad for “urgent care Denver,” the landing page needs to have:

- The clinic name and address at the top
- A click-to-call button (medical appointments are often phone bookings)
- Insurance logos they accept
- Current hours (with today’s date if possible)
- A map with a “Get Directions” link
- A simple form: name, phone, brief symptom, and preferred time

We built them three landing pages — one per location. Each page had a testimonial from a real patient (with permission, and anonymized if needed). We added a pop-up that said “Same-day appointments available” triggered by exit intent.

**Outcome:** Their conversion rate (defined as phone call + form submission) went from 11% to 34%. That’s three times more people taking action. Their cost-per-lead dropped from $38 to $14. The owner told me he’d spent $800 on a web designer who built a “beautiful” homepage that didn’t convert. Beautiful doesn’t book appointments.

## How to Track What Actually Matters: Phone Calls and Offline Conversions

Google Ads will happily tell you that you got 200 clicks last week. That number is almost useless for an urgent care center. What matters is: how many people called, and how many of those calls turned into actual patients.

Here’s what most guides skip.

You need call tracking. Google Ads has a built-in call extension, but it only tracks calls that come through the ad itself. If someone sees your ad, remembers your number, and calls you an hour later from their work phone — that call doesn’t get counted.

**The fix:** Use a dedicated call tracking number for your Google Ads traffic. Services like CallRail or WhatConverts cost around $30–$50 per month. They give you a local number that forwards to your clinic. You can see the full call recording, duration, and whether the caller actually booked an appointment.

I worked with a clinic in Portland, Oregon, that thought they were getting 40 calls a week from their ads. Call tracking revealed it was actually 68 calls — 28 of them were coming from organic search, direct visits, or people who remembered the number from a billboard. They were attributing those calls to their ads incorrectly. That changed how they allocated their $2,500 monthly budget.

**The second part:** Offline conversion tracking. This is the step almost no urgent care does.

You need to connect your ad data to your actual appointment system. Square Appointments, Practice Fusion, or even a Google Sheet can work. When someone submits a form or calls and books an appointment, that data needs to go back into Google Ads.

Here’s how it works in practice:

- A patient clicks your ad and submits a form
- That click gets a Google Ads click ID (GCLID)
- The patient books an appointment, shows up, and gets treated
- You upload that appointment data (including the GCLID) back to Google Ads
- Google now knows: this keyword + this ad + this landing page = a real patient who walked through the door

A clinic in Chicago started doing this with a $1,500 monthly ad spend. They discovered that their “walk-in clinic” keyword was driving lots of calls but very few actual appointments. People were calling to ask “Do I need an appointment?” and then hanging up. Meanwhile, their “urgent care open now” keyword was converting at 23% to actual visits. They shifted 60% of their budget to that keyword and saw their monthly patient volume increase by 35% — without increasing their ad spend.

Most agencies will tell you this is too complicated for a small business. It’s not complicated. It’s tedious. There’s a difference. If you can export a CSV from your appointment software and upload it to Google Ads, you can do offline conversion tracking.

## Integrating Google Ads with Your Other Patient Acquisition Channels

Google Ads works best when it’s not working alone. Urgent care centers that treat Google Ads as their only marketing channel are leaving patients on the table.

Here are three integrations that actually move the needle.

**Yelp integration.** Yelp still drives a significant number of walk-ins for urgent care centers, especially in cities like Los Angeles and San Francisco where people Yelp everything. The problem is most clinics run Google Ads and a Yelp page separately, with no coordination.

What works: Run a small Yelp ad campaign (even $100–$200 per month) that targets “urgent care” in your city. Then use UTM parameters on your Yelp ad link to track which patients came from Yelp versus Google. A clinic in San Diego found that 18% of their daily walk-ins came from Yelp, but they were spending 80% of their budget on Google Ads. They rebalanced — put 15% of budget into Yelp ads — and saw a 12% lift in total patient volume without increasing spend.

**Email follow-up with Mailchimp or Constant Contact.** This is underused. When someone submits a contact form through your Google Ads landing page, they are actively looking for care. That’s a warm lead. Most clinics send one generic confirmation email and never follow up.

Better approach: Set up an automated email sequence. Day 1: confirmation of their appointment request. Day 3: a short email about what to bring (insurance card, ID, list of medications). Day 7: a request to leave a Google review. Day 14: a “seasonal health tip” email (flu shots, allergy season, school physicals).

A clinic in Phoenix did this with Mailchimp’s free tier. They collected 47 email addresses from Google Ads form submissions in one month. Their seven-day automated sequence resulted in 12 Google reviews and 9 repeat visits within two months. The repeat visits alone generated roughly $2,700 in revenue — from zero additional ad spend.

**Booksy or Square Appointments for same-day booking.** Some urgent cares are now offering same-day online booking directly from the ad. If you use Square Appointments or Booksy, you can link directly to a booking page in your ad copy.

A clinic in Atlanta tried this: their ad said “Book your same-day appointment online” with a direct link to their Square booking page. Their conversion rate jumped from 14% to 29% in two weeks. The owner told me people don’t want to call — they want to tap and confirm. That booking page cost them zero extra dollars to set up. It took one afternoon.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I budget for Google Ads?**
Start with $500–$1,500 per month, depending on your city. In a mid-sized city like Charlotte or Columbus, $500 will get you meaningful data. In a competitive market like NYC or LA, you’ll need at least $1,200 to see results. I’ve seen clinics spend $300 and get nothing because the minimum bids were too high. If you can’t afford at least $500, put that money into your Yelp page and local SEO instead.

**Q: What if my competitors are already running ads? Is it too late?**
No. But you need to be smarter. Most urgent cares run generic ads with boring copy. If every urgent care in your city is bidding on “urgent care near me,” you can win by targeting specific symptoms: “strep throat testing Tulsa” or “sprained ankle Tulsa.” Those keywords are cheaper and more likely to convert because the person has a specific need. I’ve seen clinics get 30% lower CPCs using symptom-based keywords versus generic “urgent care” keywords.

**Q: What’s the most important thing I can do to improve my ad performance?**
Fix your landing page. I can’t overstate this. I’ve seen clinics double their conversion rate just by adding a click-to-call button and listing accepted insurance companies. Your ad gets them in the door. Your landing page gets them to take action. If your landing page is your general homepage, you are wasting money.

**Q: How do I know if my ads are actually working?**
Track phone calls and booked appointments. Not clicks. Not impressions. Not “engagement.” If you can’t tell me how many phone calls came from your ads and how many of those calls turned into actual patients, you are flying blind. Use call tracking software. Upload offline conversions to Google Ads. It takes one afternoon to set up and changes everything.

**Q: Should I use automated bidding or manual bidding?**
For a $1,000/month budget, start with manual CPC. Automated bidding (Target CPA, Maximize Conversions) works better at higher spend levels where Google has enough data to learn. At $500–$1,000 per month, manual bidding lets you control exactly where your money goes. Switch to automated once you’re spending $1,500+ and have at least 30 conversions per month.

**Q: What’s a reasonable cost-per-acquisition for urgent care?**
Between $15 and $40 per booked appointment, depending on your city and competition. If you’re paying $60+ per appointment, something is off — either your keywords are too broad, your landing page is weak, or you’re in a hyper-competitive market. I’ve seen clinics in Phoenix get CPAs of $12 and clinics in San Francisco pay $55. The difference is usually targeting and landing page quality, not market size.

**Q: Do I need to advertise year-round or just during flu season?**
Year-round, but adjust your budget. During flu season (October–February), bump your budget by 30–50%. That’s when people are actively searching. During summer, you can reduce spend. But don’t pause entirely — you want to maintain your Quality Score and ad rank. Pausing for three months means you’ll pay more to restart. A clinic in Minneapolis reduced to $300/month in July and August, then bumped to $1,000 in November. That worked fine.
