## How Much Do Spotify Ads Cost (2026)

Spotify runs ads through its own DSP (demand-side platform), and the cost per 1,000 impressions (CPM) typically falls in the **$15-$35 range**.

But here's the kicker  -  your actual cost depends heavily on:

- **Audience targeting**: The more specific your targeting, the higher the cost.
- **Ad format**: Bumper ads are cheaper than skippable or pre-roll.
- **Ad length**: 15-30 seconds are standard and cheapest.
- **Campaign objective**: Brand awareness campaigns cost more than lead generation or sales-focused.
- **Location and language**: Ads in smaller towns or less competitive markets are cheaper.

## How to Target Local Audiences on Spotify

Spotify offers powerful **geo-targeting** and **interest-based targeting**, which is perfect for local businesses.

Here's how to set up a geo-targeted ad:

1. Go to the Spotify Ad Studio.
2. Set your campaign objective (e.g., "Brand Awareness").
3. Choose your location or ZIP code.
4. Set your budget and targeting preferences (age, gender, interests).
5. Upload your ad and launch.

Pro tip: Use **custom audiences** if you have an email list or CRM data. You can upload phone numbers or emails to target people who've engaged with your brand before.

## Frequently Asked Questions

Yes, Google Ads is worth it if you're targeting people actively searching for your service. For example, a local hair salon can target keywords like "haircut near me" and get high-intent leads.

### Is $10 a day enough for Google Ads?

$10/day is enough to test Google Ads, but it may not generate meaningful results. You'll need at least $30-$50/day for 30 days to see a reliable performance trend.

### Is $100 enough for Google Ads?

$100/day is a solid budget for Google Ads. It gives you a good volume of impressions and clicks, and you can test multiple keywords and ad variations.

### Is $20 a day good for Google Ads?

$20/day is a minimum for Google Ads if you're targeting highly competitive keywords. If you're in a niche market, you can get away with a lower budget.

### How much should a small business spend on Google Ads?

Aim for **$50-$200/day** depending on your goals. For brand awareness, spend more. For lead generation or sales, aim for at least $100/day.

Yes, $10/day is enough to test Spotify ads, especially for bumper or voice ads. Just make sure you're targeting the right audience and tracking the right metrics (impressions, CTR, conversions).

### Is $20 a day good for Spotify Ads?

$20/day is a great budget for Spotify ads if you want to run multiple ad formats and test different audiences. It gives you more flexibility and reach than a $10/day budget.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Too Broadly

**The story:** A coffee roaster in Portland, Oregon came to me after spending $2,400 on Spotify ads over three months. Their CPM was $18 — reasonable. Their CTR was 0.3% — terrible. They'd targeted "coffee lovers" and "Portland metro area" and assumed that was enough.

**What went wrong:** "Coffee lovers" on Spotify includes anyone who listened to a Starbucks playlist once. In Portland, that's roughly everyone with ears. Their ad was competing for attention against national brands with 10x their budget. Their $40/day was getting lost in noise.

**The fix:** We rebuilt targeting to three layers:
- Location: specific ZIP codes within 5 miles of their two storefronts
- Behavior: users who streamed at least 30 minutes of music during morning commute hours (6-8am)
- Interest: listeners of indie folk and jazz playlists (their actual customer base, per their POS data from Square)

**The outcome:** CPM jumped to $28 (tighter audience always costs more), but CTR went from 0.3% to 2.1%. Their cost per visit dropped from $14 to $3.80. They started running ads only on weekdays from 6-9am and 11am-1pm — their two busiest windows. Monthly spend stayed at $800, but they saw 210 incremental visits in the first month. At an average ticket of $12, that's $2,520 in revenue on $800 in ad spend. ROI: 3.15x.

### Mistake #2: Using the Wrong Ad Format for Your Goal

**The story:** A hair salon in Nashville spent $1,600 on Spotify video ads over six weeks. The owner wanted new clients. She got 14,000 impressions and exactly zero booked appointments.

**What went wrong:** Video ads on Spotify are skippable after 5 seconds. Most people are driving, working out, or cooking — they aren't watching. The salon's ad showed a 30-second montage of haircuts set to music. Cute concept. Terrible for conversions.

**The fix:** We switched to audio-only ad spots (15 seconds) with a clear call-to-action: "Text HAIRCUT to [number] for $20 off your first appointment." We used Spotify's clickable call-to-action card that appears on screen during the ad. The audio script was direct: "Nashville, I'm a hairstylist in East Nashville. I have openings this Thursday. Text HAIRCUT to 555-0100 for $20 off. That's HAIRCUT to 555-0100."

**The outcome:** First week: 38 texts. Second week: 52 texts. Conversion rate to booked appointment: 72%. Total ad spend over six weeks: $1,600 (same budget). Appointments booked: 186. Average service price: $85. Revenue generated: $15,810. The owner told me she'd "never touch another video ad" — which is too extreme, but I understood the sentiment.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Frequency Caps

**The story:** A dog grooming business in Austin, Texas ran a two-week campaign with a $50 daily budget. They targeted pet owners within 3 miles. By day four, their CTR had dropped to 0.1%. By day seven, they were getting complaints from customers who said they "kept hearing the same ad."

**What went wrong:** Spotify's default frequency cap is 3 impressions per user per day. That doesn't sound bad until you realize their audience pool was only about 4,000 monthly active users in their radius. At $20 CPM, $50/day buys 2,500 impressions. With 4,000 people in the pool, most were hearing the ad 3-4 times per day by day two.

**The fix:** I set a hard frequency cap of 1 impression per user per day. We also rotated three different ad scripts — one for nail trims, one for full grooming, one for "puppy's first groom." Each ran on a 3-day rotation.

**The outcome:** CPM went up slightly (frequency caps reduce available inventory, so costs rise), but CTR tripled to 0.9%. More importantly, the complaints stopped. Cost per new client dropped from $27 to $11. The owner went from "Spotify doesn't work" to running ads every month. Year-one client acquisition cost through Spotify: $9.40 average.

### Mistake #4: Not Tracking Offline Conversions

**The story:** A fitness studio in Chicago spent $3,000 on Spotify ads over four months. The owner told me "Spotify doesn't work for us." When I asked how he was tracking results, he showed me Spotify's dashboard — 0.4% CTR, 12 link clicks total. He'd never asked new members how they found the studio.

**What went wrong:** Spotify's click-through tracking is notoriously unreliable. Many listeners hear an ad while driving or working out and visit the business later — but Spotify can't track that. The studio was judging success purely on Spotify's internal metrics, which miss the majority of real conversions.

**The fix:** We implemented three tracking methods:
1. A unique phone number for Spotify ads (Google Voice number, $10/month)
2. A custom URL (thestudio.com/spotify) that redirected to their main site
3. A 10% discount code only mentioned in Spotify ads ("SPOTIFY10")

We also added one question to their intake form: "How did you hear about us?" with Spotify as a checkbox option.

**The outcome:** Over the next two months, 47 new members checked "Spotify" on the intake form. The unique phone number got 22 calls. The discount code was used 31 times. Total attributed new members: 78. Average membership value: $89/month. Within three months, those members generated $6,942 in monthly recurring revenue. The ad spend was $1,500 over those two months. The owner's take: "I was about to cancel this. I would have lost $6,000 a month."

## How Spotify Ads Compare to Other Local Ad Platforms

If you're a small business owner in the US, you're probably already running ads somewhere — Google, Meta, maybe Yelp. Here's how Spotify stacks up against the alternatives, based on what I've seen across client accounts.

**Google Ads:** Average cost per click for local service businesses in the US: $4-9. A coffee shop in Denver was paying $5.80 per click for "coffee near me" and converting about 8% of those clicks into visits. That's $72.50 per visit. Spotify, for the same business, cost $3.80 per visit. The trade-off: Google captures intent (someone actively searching), while Spotify captures attention (someone who might not have been thinking about coffee until they heard your ad). Both have a place. If you can only afford one platform, start with Google if you need customers today. Start with Spotify if you need to build awareness for a new location or promotion.

**Meta Ads (Facebook/Instagram):** Meta's CPMs for local targeting in US cities range $12-25 — similar to Spotify. But Meta's click-through rates for local businesses average 0.6-1.2%, while Spotify's audio ads average 0.3-0.7%. The difference: Meta users are scrolling and can click immediately. Spotify users are often in motion. Meta wins for direct response. Spotify wins for brand recall. A pet groomer in Denver tested both: Meta got 3x more clicks, but Spotify got 2x more actual appointments booked. Why? People heard the ad during their commute, remembered it when they got home, and booked online. The click wasn't tracked, but the appointment was real.

**Yelp Ads:** I've run Yelp campaigns for six clients. I've never seen one break even. Yelp's CPMs are $25-40 for local targeting, and their conversion tracking is opaque at best. One bakery in San Francisco spent $800/month on Yelp for four months and got 14 inquiries. Cost per inquiry: $228. Spotify, same bakery: $18 per visit. Avoid Yelp unless you have a specific reason to be there (e.g., you're a restaurant and Yelp reviews drive your business regardless).

**Spotify + Email (Mailchimp):** This combo works better than you'd think. A yoga studio in Portland ran Spotify ads promoting a free week of classes. The ad directed listeners to a Mailchimp landing page. They collected 340 emails in two weeks at a cost of $0.47 per email. Then they sent a 5-email nurture sequence. Conversion rate to paid membership: 22%. Total ad spend: $160. Revenue from those members: $6,800 in first-month dues. The key was using Spotify to capture attention and email to close the sale.

**Spotify + Square:** If you use Square for POS, you can target ads to people who live near your location and have spending patterns similar to your existing customers. Square's audience data is surprisingly good — they know what people actually buy, not just what they say they like. A bakery in Austin used Square's lookalike audience feature to target Spotify ads to people with similar purchase behaviors to their top 20% of customers. CPM was $32 (higher than usual), but cost per visit dropped to $2.10.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I run Spotify ads with a budget under $100/day?**

Yes. $50/day is the minimum I'd recommend for testing. Below that, you won't get enough data to optimize. I've run campaigns at $30/day, but they took 3-4 weeks to gather statistically significant results. At $50/day, you can test two ad variations for a week and have actionable data. At $100/day, you can test three audiences simultaneously.

**Q: How do I track if someone hears my ad and visits my store later?**

You can't track this perfectly — Spotify doesn't have foot traffic attribution. The workarounds: unique discount codes, dedicated phone numbers (Google Voice or Grasshopper), and asking every customer "how did you hear about us?" on your intake form or POS system. I've seen businesses capture 60-70% of Spotify-driven visits this way. The rest are invisible, which means your actual ROI is probably higher than what you can measure.

**Q: Is Spotify better than Google Ads for my local business?**

It depends on what you need. If you need customers who are actively searching for what you offer right now, Google Ads wins. If you need to build awareness for a new business, location, or promotion, Spotify wins. The best approach I've seen: run Google Ads for immediate conversions and Spotify for top-of-funnel awareness. A bakery in Chicago did this — Google got them the "I need a cake today" customers, Spotify got them the "I didn't know you existed" customers. Combined, they grew revenue 34% in six months.

**Q: What's the minimum ad length I should use?**

15 seconds. Anything shorter (6-second bumper ads) doesn't give you time to build enough context or urgency. Anything longer (30 seconds) has higher drop-off rates. I've tested 10-second, 15-second, and 30-second versions of the same ad for a client. The 15-second version had the best cost per conversion. The 30-second version had slightly higher CTR but 40% higher CPM. The 10-second version was forgettable.

**Q: Can I target people who are near my business right now?**

Not in real-time, no. Spotify doesn't offer geofencing or proximity targeting. The closest option is targeting by ZIP code or radius (minimum 1 mile). You can schedule ads during specific hours (e.g., morning commute for a coffee shop), but you can't target someone who's currently standing outside your store. For that, use Google Ads location extensions or Meta's proximity targeting.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from Spotify ads?**

With a $100/day budget and tight targeting, you should see measurable results within 7-10 days. The first 3-4 days are data collection — Spotify's algorithm learns who responds to your ad. By day 7, you'll have enough data to judge CTR and cost per conversion. By day 14, you should know if the campaign is viable. If you're not seeing a positive ROI by day 21, kill the campaign and try different targeting or ad copy.

**Q: Do I need to create a video ad, or can I just use audio?**

Audio-only ads work better for most local businesses. Video ads require production costs ($500-2,000 minimum for something decent), and most listeners aren't watching their screens. I've run audio-only campaigns for 30+ local businesses and only two needed video (a gym that wanted to show workout clips and a restaurant that wanted to show food shots). For everyone else, a well-written 15-second audio script outperformed video.
