## Understanding Your Membership Site Goals
Before diving into marketing strategies, it's essential to define what you want to achieve with your membership site. Are you looking to increase revenue, improve customer retention, or build a community around your business? Knowing your goals will help you create targeted marketing campaigns that drive results. For example, a coffee shop in Portland might aim to increase revenue by offering exclusive discounts to loyalty program members.

## Choosing the Right Membership Model
There are several membership models to choose from, each with its pros and cons. You might consider:
* **Subscription-based**: Members pay a recurring fee for access to exclusive content or services
* **Tiered**: Members can choose from different levels of access or perks based on their subscription level
* **Freemium**: Basic content or services are free, while premium content or services require a subscription

## Creating Valuable Content for Members
To keep members engaged and retained, you need to create valuable content that resonates with them. This might include:
* **Exclusive discounts or offers**: Members get access to special deals or promotions
* **Early access to new products or services**: Members get to try new things before anyone else
* **Behind-the-scenes content**: Members get a glimpse into your business's inner workings

## Marketing Your Membership Site
To grow your membership site, you need to get the word out. Here are some marketing strategies to consider:
* **Social media marketing**: Use platforms like Facebook and Instagram to promote your membership site
* **Email marketing**: Send targeted campaigns to your email list to encourage sign-ups
* **Influencer partnerships**: Partner with local influencers to promote your membership site

## Retaining Members and Reducing Churn
To keep members engaged and retained, you need to continually deliver value and make it easy for them to manage their subscriptions. Consider:
* **Regular communication**: Send regular updates and newsletters to keep members informed
* **Feedback mechanisms**: Make it easy for members to provide feedback and suggestions
* **Personalization**: Use data and analytics to personalize the member experience

<Callout type="warning">Be careful not to overwhelm members with too many emails or notifications - it's a delicate balance between communication and spam.</Callout>

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've spent enough years watching agencies burn client money on membership programs that never took off. Here are the four most common mistakes I've seen at small businesses across the US — and what actually fixed them.

### Mistake 1: Building a "Loyalty Program" and Calling It a Membership

**The story:** A coffee shop in Austin, Texas — let's call it Brew & Bloom — launched a $19/month membership. For that price, members got a free drink each visit plus 10% off everything else. Sounded good on paper. The owner put up a few flyers, posted on Instagram twice, and waited.

Six weeks later: 12 members. Revenue from memberships: $228/month. The shop was giving away roughly $400/month in free drinks to those 12 people. Net negative before you factor in the labor or the cost of the marketing they didn't do.

**What went wrong:** They treated a membership like a punch card with a monthly fee. There was no community, no exclusivity, no reason to stay beyond the math. And the math was bad for them.

**The fix:** I had them restructure. Keep the $19/month, but add:
- A weekly "members-only" 30-minute coffee tasting before opening (costs them maybe $3 in beans)
- A private Signal group where members get first dibs on limited roasts
- A quarterly "member blend" — they'd vote on the next single-origin offering

They also started asking every member "why did you join?" at checkout. The answers told them what to build next.

**The outcome:** Within 90 days, membership hit 87. Monthly revenue from memberships: $1,653. The cost of goods sold on member perks stayed under $400. They added 14 new members from referrals alone after the first tasting event.

**The lesson:** A membership isn't a transaction. It's a backstage pass. If you're not giving people something they can't buy a la carte, you're just running a bad discount program.

### Mistake 2: Pricing Based on "What Feels Right"

**The story:** A hair salon in Nashville — Studio East — wanted to launch a membership for frequent color clients. The owner said she wanted it to be "accessible." She settled on $29/month for one gloss treatment and 15% off color services.

She ran the numbers in front of me on a napkin. A gloss treatment costs her $4 in product and 20 minutes of chair time. At $29, she was making $25 in gross profit per member per month — if they used exactly one gloss and nothing else.

But here's the part she missed: The members who signed up were already her highest-frequency clients. They were coming in every five weeks for full color ($180+ a visit). After joining, they started skipping one of their regular appointments because "I got the gloss last week, so my color still looks good."

She was cannibalizing her own revenue.

**What went wrong:** She priced the membership to attract people who were already paying full price. Instead of growing revenue, she gave her best clients a reason to spend less.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the membership around the clients she *wasn't* serving well — the ones who came in twice a year for a haircut but wanted to maintain their color between visits. The new tier:
- $49/month: One gloss treatment, 10% off all services, priority booking (no more waiting three weeks for an appointment)
- Targeted the offer via email to clients who hadn't booked in 60+ days

**The outcome:** First month: 38 new members, average ticket for members increased by 22% because add-on services went up. The cannibalization problem disappeared because these clients weren't regulars to begin with. Monthly recurring revenue from the membership: $1,862. Net profit after product and labor: about $1,400.

**The lesson:** Price your membership for the customer you want to acquire, not the one you already have. If you're giving a discount to someone who would have paid full price anyway, you're leaving money on the table.

### Mistake 3: Making the Signup Process a Chore

**The story:** A pet grooming studio in Chicago — Paws & Claws — had a $39/month membership. Unlimited nail trims, 15% off grooms, a free bath every quarter. Great value. Their Instagram had 4,200 followers. They got 10 signups in the first month.

I asked to go through the signup process myself. Here's what happened:
1. Click the link in bio (fine)
2. Lands on a generic Squarespace page with no pricing (annoying)
3. Scrolls down, finds a "Learn More" button
4. Clicks through to a PDF (a PDF. In 2023.)
5. PDF says "Call us to sign up"
6. Calls during business hours, gets voicemail
7. Leaves a message. Gets a call back four hours later
8. Over the phone, they take down credit card info manually. Takes seven minutes.
9. Receives a confirmation text with a link to download an app that doesn't work on Android

I wanted to give them money. They made me work for it.

**What went wrong:** The owner designed the membership from her perspective — "If they really want it, they'll go through the steps." She didn't account for friction. Every extra click, every minute of delay, every confusing page was a reason to abandon.

**The fix:** We streamlined to exactly three steps:
- Link in bio goes to a one-page checkout via Square (they already used Square for POS, so the integration was free)
- Customer enters email, phone, card. Done in under 90 seconds.
- Auto-enrolled in the booking system with a "Member" tag. First appointment booked automatically via text.

We also added a post-checkout screen: "Share this with a friend — if they join, you both get next month's nail trim free." Took 15 minutes to set up in Square's loyalty tools.

**The outcome:** Signups went from 10 in the first month to 47 in the second month. The friction audit cost nothing to fix. Monthly recurring revenue went from $390 to $1,833. The referral mechanic added 11 members in month three.

**The lesson:** Every step between "I want this" and "I've paid for this" is a leak. Map your signup process like a funnel. Test it on someone who's never heard of your business. If they hesitate at any point, you've got a problem.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring the "I Forgot I Was Paying" Problem

**The story:** A fitness studio in Denver — Lift & Lunge — had a $99/month unlimited class membership. They were doing fine on acquisition. The problem was churn. Every month, 15-18% of members dropped off. Most cited "I just wasn't going enough."

But the data told a different story. When I looked at the drop-off timing, 70% of cancellations happened between day 28 and day 35 of the billing cycle. These people weren't canceling because they didn't like the classes. They were canceling because the charge hit their account and they had a moment of "Wait, I didn't go last week either. This is wasted money."

**What went wrong:** The membership had no engagement trigger between the billing dates. Members paid on the 1st, then heard nothing until the 1st again. If they missed a week, there was nothing pulling them back in. The billing reminder became the only touchpoint, and it was a negative one.

**The fix:** We added two things:
- A mid-cycle "You've got credits waiting" email. Members had unlimited classes, but framing it as "credits they'd lose" created a mental account. (Behavioral economics: loss aversion > gain framing.)
- A text notification 48 hours before billing: "Your next class is on us if you bring a friend this week." This shifted the billing association from "here's a charge" to "here's a free thing."

**The outcome:** Churn dropped from 16% to 9% in two months. Monthly recurring revenue stabilized. The bring-a-friend mechanic added 23 new non-member trial attendees in the first 60 days, 7 of whom converted to full members.

**The lesson:** If you only talk to members when you're charging them, you're training them to associate your membership with a cost. Give them reasons to remember why they signed up — and do it between billing cycles.

## The Pricing Sweet Spot: How to Set Your First Membership Price

Most small business owners guess at their first membership price. They pick a round number — $19, $29, $49 — based on what competitors charge or what "feels fair." You can do better with three data points you already have.

### Data Point 1: Customer Lifetime Value, But Make It Honest

Calculate your average customer's total spend over 12 months. If you're a pet groomer and your average client spends $65 per visit and comes every 6 weeks, that's about $565/year. Your membership should cost no more than 30-40% of that annual figure per year, or roughly $14-$19/month.

Why 30-40%? Because membership pricing at 50% or more of annual spend creates a mental barrier. People will do the math and realize they're paying close to what they'd spend a la carte. That triggers comparison shopping. You want them thinking "this is a deal" not "does this pencil out?"

### Data Point 2: What Do Non-Members Spend vs. Members?

This is the one most businesses skip. Pull your last 90 days of transactions. Separate customers into two groups: those who visited more than once a month and those who visited less. The "more than once" group is your base. They're already behaving like members. Your job is to formalize the relationship and give them a reason to commit.

Set your price so that the member's average monthly spend (membership fee + any additional purchases) is 10-15% *more* than what a non-member spends per month. If non-members spend $40/month on average, your members should be spending $44-$46/month including the membership fee. This ensures you're not leaving money on the table.

### Data Point 3: The "Uncomfortable Question" Test

Before you launch, ask yourself: If someone joins today and never comes back, am I still happy with the transaction?

If the answer is no, your price is too low. A coffee shop charging $19/month for five drinks is paying to acquire a member — they need that person to show up and buy pastries to break even. A fitness studio charging $99 is fine with a member who attends zero classes, because the margin covers the acquisition cost.

Run this test. If you're uncomfortable with the idea of a "ghost member" paying you every month, you're pricing for their behavior, not your business.

### Real Example: Yoga Studio in Portland, OR

A client had a $79/month unlimited yoga membership. Average class attendance for members: 8 visits per month. Cost per class to the studio: about $3 (instructor split, space, cleaning). At 8 visits, they were making $55 gross profit per member.

But non-members paid $22 per drop-in. If they came 8 times a month, they were spending $176. The membership was a steal — and the studio was leaving $97/month on the table for every member who would have paid drop-in rates.

We raised the membership to $109/month. Lost 11% of members in the first month. Retained 89%. Revenue from the remaining members went up 38%. The members who left were the ones attending 4 or fewer classes per month — exactly the segment that was costing them money.

**The result:** Net monthly revenue from memberships increased by $2,100. Not bad for changing one number.

## Retention Mechanics That Actually Work (Beyond "Send a Newsletter")

Every guide tells you to "engage your members" and "send valuable content." That's like telling a chef to "make good food." Technically correct. Uselessly vague.

Here are three retention strategies I've seen work in actual small businesses, not marketing textbooks.

### Strategy 1: The Pre-Billing Win-Back

Most businesses handle churn reactively. Someone cancels, you send a "We'll miss you" email, maybe offer 20% off for three months. That's fine, but you're fighting a battle you already lost.

Instead, identify the at-risk member *before* they cancel. The two most predictive signals are:
- 14-day gap in engagement (no visits, no bookings, no logins)
- A "soft cancel" behavior (downsizing from premium to basic, skipping a month)

Build a simple automation in whatever tool you use — Mailchimp, Klaviyo, HubSpot free tier — that triggers when someone hits a 14-day gap. The message should not be "We miss you." That's generic and feels automated. Instead:

"Hey [Name], your next [product/service] is reserved for [day] at [time] unless you tell us otherwise."

You're assuming they're coming back. You're making it easy to come back. You're not asking them to do anything — you're telling them you've already done the work.

**Real example:** A barbershop in Brooklyn used this via their Booksy integration. Members who hadn't booked in 14 days got a text saying their usual barber had a slot open Thursday at 4pm. Click once to confirm. 22% of at-risk members rebooked within 24 hours. Churn dropped by 31%.

### Strategy 2: The "You're Leaving Money on the Table" Upsell

Memberships create a psychological effect called the sunk cost trap — but you can flip it. If someone is paying for a membership but not using the full value, they're primed to cancel because the perceived value is low.

Don't wait for them to figure that out. Proactively show them what they're missing. Every month, send a personalized summary:

"You had [X] credits unused this month. Here's what other members used theirs on: [specific thing]. Want us to book that for you?"

The key is specificity. "Other members used their credits on a deep conditioning treatment" is better than "Check out our services." Make it a single click to redeem.

**Real example:** A hair salon in Seattle used their Square appointment data to identify members who hadn't used their monthly gloss treatment in two months. They sent a text: "Your gloss treatment expires in 5 days. Want us to add it to your next appointment?" 44% of recipients added the treatment. Average ticket for those appointments increased by $38 because the stylist had a natural upsell opportunity during the service.

### Strategy 3: The Referral That Actually Converts

Most referral programs give the referrer a discount and the friend a discount. That works okay. But membership referrals have a specific friction: the friend has to commit to a recurring payment.

Remove that friction by making the first month free *for the friend* — but not for the referrer. Instead, give the referrer a month free if their friend stays for 60 days. Now the referrer has a real incentive to help the friend stick around.

**Real example:** A fitness studio in Austin ran this exact mechanic. Referrer gets a free month if the friend is still a member after 60 days. Friend gets the first month free. Conversion rate on referred signups went from 12% to 31%. The average referred member stayed 40% longer than organic signups. Cost per acquisition via referral: $0 upfront, $0 if the friend churned.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a solo barber. I don't have time to manage a membership program. Is this realistic?**

Honest answer: It depends on your setup. If you're using a tool like Booksy or Square Appointments, membership management takes about 30 minutes a month to set up and maybe 15 minutes weekly to maintain — mostly checking the "at-risk" list and sending a few texts. If you're trying to run it on spreadsheets and Venmo requests, it'll eat your life. Use the tools you already have. Most POS systems have loyalty and membership features you're not using. Go into settings, find the tab, turn it on. That's half the work.

**Q: Won't a membership program just train my customers to expect discounts?**

Only if you position it as a discount. If your membership is "pay $29 to get 15% off," then yes, you're training them to wait for a sale. If your membership is "pay $49 to get early access to new services, a monthly exclusive product, and priority booking," then you're selling access, not a discount. The difference is in the framing. Never lead with the savings. Lead with what they get that non-members can't have.

**Q: What if nobody signs up? I put in the work and get 3 members.**

Three members is data. That's not failure — that's a test result. You learned that your offer didn't resonate, your price was off, or your targeting was wrong. Talk to those three people. Buy them coffee. Ask them: "What almost stopped you from signing up?" and "What would make this a no-brainer?" Then iterate. I've seen businesses relaunch a membership three times before it stuck. The ones who quit after the first attempt never found out what would have worked.

**Q: Do I need a separate website or app for the membership?**

No. God, no. Do not build an app. Do not build a membership site on a separate platform. Use what you already have. If you use Square for payments, their loyalty program handles memberships. If you use Mailchimp for email, they have a basic member management feature. If you use Instagram, use a link-in-bio tool to a simple checkout. The fanciest membership setup I've seen at a small business was a Google Form + Square invoice. The owner sent it via text. It worked fine. Complexity is the enemy of execution.

**Q: How do I handle cancellations without it being awkward?**

Make it easy. No exit surveys, no "are you sure?" popups, no pleading emails. Let them cancel with one click. Here's why: If you make it hard to cancel, people will cancel their *payment method* instead — they'll block your number, report spam, or switch to a different card. Then you've lost not just the membership but the relationship. I've seen businesses recover 60% of canceled members within six months because they let people leave gracefully. The cancellation email says: "Sorry to see you go. Your membership ends [date]. If you change your mind, come back — your first month is on us." No guilt. No friction. Door stays open.

**Q: What's the minimum number of customers I need before launching a membership?**

If you have fewer than 50 repeat customers, don't launch a membership yet. You don't have enough data to know what to build or enough potential members to make the math work. Instead, survey those 50 people. Ask them: "If we offered a membership with [benefit A, B, C], what would you pay for it?" You're looking for at least 20% saying "yes, definitely" before you build anything. Less than that means your offer needs work. Run the survey, get the answers, then launch.
