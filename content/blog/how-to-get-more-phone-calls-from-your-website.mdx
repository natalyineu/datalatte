## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Hiding Your Phone Number Behind a Contact Form

**The story:** A pizza place in Austin, TX — "Tony's Original" — had been running since 1994. They spent $1,200/month on Google Ads driving traffic to a "Contact Us" page with a form that asked for name, email, phone number, and a message. Tony wanted calls for large catering orders. In three months, that page generated exactly seven form submissions. Two were spam. One was from his mother.

I sat down with Tony and asked: "Where is your phone number on this page?" He pointed to the footer. In 10pt gray text. Below the privacy policy link. On mobile, you had to zoom in to read it.

**The fix:** We moved his phone number to the top of every page — header, not footer. Made it clickable (tel: link) on mobile. Changed the Contact page to a prominent phone number in 28pt bold font with a "Call Now for Catering" button above the fold. We kept the form below for non-urgent inquiries.

**The outcome:** In the first month after the change, Tony received 47 inbound calls from website visitors. Twelve of those turned into catering orders averaging $380 each. That's $4,560 in new revenue from a $0 change to his website. His mother still texts him orders.

**What to do:** Put your phone number in the header of every page. Not the footer. The header. Make it clickable on mobile. Test it yourself — tap it on your own phone.

### Mistake #2: Having a Google Business Profile That Contradicts Your Website

**The story:** A hair salon in Denver, CO — "Blonde & Beyond" — listed their hours as Monday–Saturday, 9am–7pm on Google Business Profile. Their website said Monday–Friday, 10am–6pm. A potential client named Sarah drove 25 minutes across Denver to arrive at 5:30pm on a Saturday. The salon was locked. Sarah left a one-star review that said "Google says open but doors are locked. Wasted my Saturday."

The owner, Jenna, told me she hadn't updated her website hours since she changed them six months prior. She hadn't touched the Google listing in two years.

**The fix:** We did three things. First, updated both the website and Google Business Profile to match exactly — Monday–Friday 10am–7pm, Saturday 10am–4pm, closed Sunday. Second, set up a recurring monthly reminder (calendar event) to check both listings for accuracy. Third, added a temporary banner to the website for holiday hours changes.

**The outcome:** The one-star review was removed after Google verified the discrepancy was a listing error. More importantly, the salon stopped getting calls at 8pm from people asking if they were still open. Jenna estimated she was losing 5–8 calls per week from frustrated customers who couldn't find consistent information. That's roughly 20–32 missed booking opportunities per month at $80 average ticket. Call volume increased 22% within two weeks of standardizing the information.

**What to do:** Open your Google Business Profile and your website side by side. Check hours, phone number, address, and services. If anything differs, fix it immediately. Then set a monthly calendar reminder to check both again.

### Mistake #3: Making the User Scroll Through a Novel Before They Can Call

**The story:** A pet grooming business in Portland, OR — "Paws & Relax" — had a beautifully designed website. Full-screen hero image of a fluffy golden retriever. A 200-word story about how the owner, Mark, started the business after adopting his rescue dog. Animated icons for services. A drop-down menu with 14 options. And somewhere, buried under three layers of navigation, a "Book Now" button that linked to a third-party scheduler.

Mark was spending $800/month on Facebook ads. He was getting 1,200 website visits per month. He was averaging 4 phone calls per week. His conversion rate was 0.08%.

**The fix:** I told Mark to pretend he was a customer with a golden retriever covered in mud who needed an appointment today. How fast could he find a phone number on his own site? He timed himself. It took 22 seconds. We redesigned the mobile layout: sticky phone button at the bottom of the screen that follows you as you scroll. One prominent "Call Now" button above the fold on desktop. Removed the three-paragraph origin story from the homepage.

**The outcome:** Phone calls jumped from 4 per week to 18 per week in the first two weeks. Mark's average booking value is $65. That's $910/week in new calls versus $260/week before — a 250% increase. The origin story is still on an "About" page for people who care. But it's not blocking the people who just need their dog washed today.

**What to do:** Put a phone button in the sticky footer on mobile. Make the phone number one of the first things a visitor sees on desktop. Then time yourself. If it takes more than 3 seconds to find your number, you've made it too hard.

## How to Set Up Google Ads That Actually Generate Calls (Not Just Clicks)

Most local business owners I meet have the same Google Ads story: they spent $500, spent $1,000, got a bunch of clicks, and zero phone calls. Then they swore off Google Ads forever. That's like buying a fishing rod, casting it into a swimming pool, and declaring fishing doesn't work.

Here's what actually works for local businesses that want phone calls.

**Use Call-Only Ads, Not Search Ads**

Google has a campaign type called "Call-only ads." These ads show up on mobile devices with a big "Call" button. When someone taps it, Google connects them directly to your phone. There's no landing page visit, no website bounce, no "I'll call later" that turns into never.

I set this up for a coffee roaster in Nashville, TN — "Eastside Roast." They were running standard search ads to their website, getting 200 clicks per month at $1.50/click, and maybe 3 calls per month. We switched to call-only ads targeting people searching "coffee beans Nashville" and "local coffee roaster near me." Budget stayed at $600/month.

The result: 41 calls in the first 30 days. Average cost per call was $14.63. The roaster's average order value over the phone was $38. The numbers work: $600 in ad spend generated roughly $1,558 in revenue from phone orders alone. That's a 2.6x return in the first month.

**Set Up Call Tracking Before You Start**

This is the part most guides skip. If you run call-only ads without call tracking, you have no idea which calls came from ads versus your website versus someone walking past your store. Google Ads has free call forwarding numbers you can set up in the platform. Use them. If a call comes through that number, you know the ad generated it.

I worked with an HVAC company in Chicago that was running ads for two years. They had no idea if the calls were from ads or their Yelp listing. Two years of data, completely useless. Once we added call tracking, they discovered 68% of their calls came from ads they thought were underperforming. They doubled the budget on those campaigns. Revenue from ad-driven calls went from $4,200/month to $9,800/month.

**Bid on "Near Me" Keywords**

A fitness studio in Denver, CO — "South Broadway Strength" — was bidding on "personal training Denver." Expensive keyword, broad, lots of competition from big gyms. We added "personal trainer near me," "fitness studio near me," and "Denver gym near me" as exact match keywords. Cost per click dropped from $4.50 to $1.80. Call volume increased 340% in six weeks. Turns out people searching "near me" are ready to sign up today, not browsing.

## Make Your Website Mobile-First for Calls (Or Lose 70% of Your Customers)

Here's a number that should scare you: 63% of local business searches happen on a mobile device. And 76% of people who search for something nearby visit a business within 24 hours. If your website treats mobile like an afterthought, you're losing calls from people who were ready to buy.

**The Tap Target Problem**

I audited a family dentistry practice in Charlotte, NC. Their phone number on mobile was 14 pixels tall and located directly above their social media icons. Every time a user tried to tap the phone number, they accidentally hit the Instagram icon. The practice was paying $500/month for local SEO, driving traffic to a site where the phone number was literally un-tappable.

We increased the tap target to 48x48 pixels (Google's recommended minimum), added 12px of padding around it, and moved the social icons to the footer. Phone calls increased 35% with zero other changes.

**Page Speed Kills Calls**

A locksmith in Austin, TX had a WordPress site with 14 plugins, six unoptimized images, and a loading time of 8.2 seconds. His bounce rate on mobile was 73%. A customer locked out of their car at 11pm is not going to wait 8 seconds for your site to load. They're going to call the next locksmith on the list.

We compressed his images, removed five plugins he wasn't using, and switched to a lightweight theme. Loading time dropped to 2.1 seconds. Bounce rate dropped to 41%. Phone calls increased 52% in the following month. The fix cost him $0 and took about three hours of work.

**What to actually check on your mobile site:**

Open your website on your phone. Not your nice iPhone with perfect WiFi. Turn off WiFi, go to the parking lot, and load it on 4G. Then answer these questions:
- Can you tap the phone number without zooming in?
- Does the phone number appear within the first two seconds of page load?
- Is there a click-to-call button that stays visible as you scroll?
- Does the contact page load in under three seconds?
- If you cover the top third of the screen with your thumb, can you still find the phone number?

If the answer to any of these is no, fix it today. Not next week. Today. Every day you wait is a day customers are calling your competitor instead.

## Tracking Calls: The Only Way to Know What's Actually Working

I have a theory: most local business owners spend more time deciding what to order for lunch than they do tracking which marketing channels generate phone calls. I'm not judging — I've done it too. But it's costing you money.

**The Free Way to Track Calls**

If you're not ready to pay for call tracking software, you can do this right now: get a second phone number from Google Voice (free) or a service like Grasshopper ($26/month). Use that number on your Google Business Profile. Use your main number on your website. Use a different number on Yelp. Then you know which platform drives which calls.

I did this with a bakery in Brooklyn, NY. They had a Google Voice number on their GMB, their main number on their site, and a third number on their Instagram bio. In one month, they tracked 22 calls from GMB, 8 from their website, and 14 from Instagram. They were spending $400/month on Instagram ads and $300/month on Google Ads. The data showed GMB was their highest-performing channel for calls. They shifted $200 from Instagram to local service ads on Google. Calls increased 40% without spending more money.

**The Paid Way (That's Worth Every Penny)**

CallRail or Convirza cost around $30–$100/month. They give you dynamic number insertion — the number on your website changes based on how the visitor got there. Someone clicks a Facebook ad, they see one number. Someone clicks an organic search result, they see another. You can record calls, see which keywords drove them, and even get transcriptions.

A plumber in Seattle, WA used CallRail for three months. He discovered that 60% of his calls were coming from his Google Business Profile, not his website. He was spending $1,200/month on SEO trying to rank his website higher. He cut SEO to $400/month and put $800/month into local service ads and GMB optimization. His call volume went up, not down. And his cost per call dropped from $18 to $6.

**Listen to Your Own Calls**

This is uncomfortable but necessary. Listen to five inbound calls per week for one month. You'll hear patterns: customers asking the same questions, getting confused by your menu, frustrated by your voicemail. One of my clients — a massage therapist in Boulder, CO — realized after listening to calls that half her callers were asking about pricing. Her website didn't list prices. She added a price page. Calls dropped by 12% (people self-filtering out), but the calls that came through converted at 85% instead of 40%. Her revenue from calls actually went up.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I own a carpet cleaning business. Will more phone calls actually help if my team is already busy?**

Yes, but you need to think about which calls you want. If you're fully booked, you don't need more calls — you need better calls. Add a "starting from" price to your website and GMB to pre-qualify callers. Track which jobs generate the highest profit and start turning away the low-value ones. I had a carpet cleaner in Phoenix add a $95 minimum to his website. Call volume dropped 20%, but average job value went from $130 to $210. He made more money from fewer calls.

**Q: Won't putting my phone number everywhere make me get spam calls?**

It will attract some spam, but less than you think. Your GMB listing already has your number if you've claimed it. Spammers scrape GMB, not individual websites. The bigger risk is missing real customer calls because you made your number hard to find. Use a call screening service if spam becomes a problem. I've tested this across 30+ local business clients — the ratio is about 1 spam call per 50 legitimate calls. The revenue from those 50 calls far outweighs the annoyance of one spam call.

**Q: Do I really need call tracking, or can I just ask customers how they found me?**

You can ask, but the data will be unreliable. Studies consistently show that 60–70% of customers either don't remember how they found you or will say "Google" regardless of whether it was a search ad, a map listing, or a review they read. Relying on what customers say is like navigating with a compass that points to whatever direction you're already facing. Spend the $30/month on call tracking for three months. The insights will pay for itself in the first week.

**Q: My website was built five years ago. Do I need a whole new site, or can I just add a phone number?**

You almost certainly don't need a new site. I've seen 10-year-old websites generate excellent call volume because they had a prominent phone number, fast loading speed, and clear navigation. The mistake is thinking you need a $5,000 redesign when all you need is a sticky phone button and compressed images. Try the low-cost fixes first. If calls don't improve, then consider a rebuild.

**Q: What's the single fastest way to get more calls starting tomorrow?**

Fix your Google Business Profile. Claim it if you haven't. Verify it. Make sure the phone number matches your website exactly. Add photos. Respond to every review. Set up Google's free call tracking so you can see how many calls come from your listing. Most local businesses I audit have an incomplete or inaccurate GMB. Fixing this takes two hours and generates results within a week.

**Q: I already get calls but they don't convert. How do I fix that?**

This is a sales problem, not a website problem. Record your own phone calls (with the customer's consent) and listen to them. You'll hear if you're talking too much, not answering the question they actually asked, or failing to ask for the booking. I worked with a personal trainer in Austin who was spending $800/month on ads but converting only 15% of calls. After listening to three calls, I noticed he was giving a 3-minute explanation of his training philosophy before the customer could say what they wanted. We wrote a simple 4-sentence phone script. Conversion rate went from 15% to 40% in two weeks. No change to the website.
