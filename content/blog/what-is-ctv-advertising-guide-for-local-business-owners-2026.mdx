## What Is CTV Advertising?

CTV advertising refers to video ads served on connected TV devices like Roku, Apple TV, Fire TV, and even smart TVs. Unlike traditional TV advertising, where you pay for a time slot regardless of who watches it, CTV allows for targeted, measurable ad delivery.

Key characteristics of CTV advertising include:

- **Targeted ads:** You can target by location, interests, demographics, and even behavior.
- **Measurable performance:** You get data on impressions, clicks, conversions, and more.
- **Connected devices:** Ads are served on streaming platforms like Hulu, YouTube TV, and Peacock.

For local businesses, this means you can reach your ideal customer  -  someone in your service area who's interested in your offerings  -  at exactly the right moment.

## How CTV Advertising Works (Without the Jargon)

You don't need a marketing degree to understand CTV advertising. Here's how the process works in simple terms:

1. **Choose your platform:** You'll need to run your CTV ads through a Demand-Side Platform (DSP). Two popular options are DV360 and The Trade Desk.
2. **Set your targeting:** Decide who you want to see your ads. This includes location, age, gender, interests, and more.
3. **Create your ad:** CTV ads are typically 15-30 seconds long. Make sure the message is clear and ties directly to a local offer.
4. **Set your budget and schedule:** Decide how much you want to spend per day and when you want your ad to run.
5. **Track and optimize:** Use performance data to tweak your targeting, messaging, and budget.

## CTV Advertising vs. Traditional TV: The Real Difference

You might be asking, "What's the big deal about CTV compared to regular TV ads?"

Let's compare:

| Feature | Traditional TV | CTV Advertising |
|---------|----------------|------------------|
| Targeting | Broad, based on time/day | Hyper-targeted by location, interests, and behavior |
| Cost | Expensive per minute | More cost-effective for local businesses |
| Measurability | Hard to track | Detailed metrics on clicks, conversions, etc. |
| Ad Relevance | Low | High  -  ads are shown to people who care |
| Flexibility | Limited | Easy to pause, adjust, and reallocate budgets |

For example, a local pet groomer in Chicago can run a CTV ad campaign that only shows to people who've searched for "dog grooming near me" in the last 30 days. That kind of precision just isn't possible with traditional TV.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched local businesses throw money at CTV advertising and wonder why their cash register isn't ringing. Here are three mistakes I've seen more times than I'd like to admit.

### Mistake #1: Targeting Too Broad and Burning Through Budget

**The story:** A coffee shop in Austin, Texas — let's call them Brew Crew on South Congress — decided to try CTV ads. They set up a campaign targeting "Austin metropolitan area" with a $1,500 monthly budget. Their ad featured their new seasonal latte and a cozy shot of their interior. 

Two weeks in, they had spent $750. Their ad had been served to 47,000 households. They got exactly 12 people through the door who mentioned the ad. That's roughly $62.50 per customer. For a $5 coffee.

**What went wrong:** They were buying impressions across the entire Austin metro — 2.1 million people. Their actual delivery radius for a coffee shop is about 2–3 miles. They paid to show ads to people in Round Rock and Cedar Park who would never drive 25 minutes for a latte, no matter how good it looked.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the campaign with a 2-mile radius around their location, targeting only "coffee drinkers" and "morning commuters" behavioral segments. We added a frequency cap of 2 per household per day and only ran the ad between 6 AM and 10 AM on weekdays.

**The outcome:** Monthly spend dropped to $400. They reached 3,200 households — all within their actual delivery zone. Foot traffic from CTV went from 12 people to 54 people in the next month. Cost per customer dropped to $7.40. They tracked this by offering a QR code on the ad for "free add-on shot" redeemable in-store.

### Mistake #2: Using the Wrong Conversion Goal

**The story:** A hair salon in Nashville — The Mane Studio in East Nashville — ran a CTV campaign with a "website visit" goal. Their ad showed stunning before-and-after transformations of balayage and precision cuts. People watched. People clicked. Nobody booked.

They spent $800 in one month. Got 340 website visits. Zero bookings.

**What went wrong:** The ad drove traffic to their homepage, which had a "book now" button buried below three paragraphs about their philosophy. The booking flow required creating an account, selecting a stylist from a list of names they didn't know, and picking a time 30 days out. On mobile, it was unusable. People landed, got frustrated, and left.

**The fix:** We set up a direct booking link using Booksy (the tool they already paid for but never integrated with advertising). The CTV ad included a QR code that went straight to a simplified booking page with three available stylists, their photos, and the next three available appointment slots. We changed the conversion goal from "website visit" to "booking form completion."

**The outcome:** The next month they spent $900. They got 87 booking form completions. Average ticket for those bookings was $180. That's $15,660 in potential revenue from $900 in ad spend. The bookings came in within the same week the ad ran — no "I'll think about it" delay.

### Mistake #3: No Retargeting and Wasting Upper Funnel Intent

**The story:** A fitness studio in Denver — Peak Performance Gym in the RiNo district — ran a CTV campaign promoting their 30-day transformation challenge. The ad was great. It showed real members, real results, and a genuine coach talking to camera. They spent $1,200. They got 4 sign-ups. Cost per acquisition: $300.

**The problem wasn't the ad.** The problem was that after someone saw the ad on their TV, there was nothing else. No follow-up. No retargeting. No email. No nothing.

**What went wrong:** CTV is great for awareness. It's terrible at closing by itself. People watch the ad, think "that's interesting," then their partner asks what they want for dinner, and the commercial is forgotten. This studio had zero retargeting infrastructure in place.

**The fix:** We set up a three-layer approach. First, the CTV ad ran with a pixel that captured IP addresses. That audience was then retargeted through Google Ads with a mobile-friendly landing page. Second, we added a QR code to the ad that led to a Mailchimp sign-up form offering a free consultation. Third, anyone who signed up got a text message within 2 hours using a simple Square integration with automated reminders.

**The outcome:** Spend stayed at $1,200. Sign-ups went from 4 to 23. Cost per acquisition dropped to $52. The gym kept 18 of those 23 members past the 30-day challenge, generating $8,640 in ongoing monthly membership revenue.

## What a Realistic CTV Budget Looks Like for a Local Business

Most guides will tell you "it depends on your goals." That's useless. Here's what actually works based on what I've seen work across dozens of local businesses.

For a single-location business in a US city, plan for $500–$1,000 per month. That buys you roughly 50,000 to 100,000 impressions on a targeted campaign. If you're in a high-cost market like NYC or San Francisco, bump that to $1,000–$1,500.

**Don't spend more than $1,500 per month until you've proven the channel works.** I've seen too many businesses dump $3,000 into CTV in month one, get discouraged by slow returns, and abandon the channel. Start small. Prove the math. Then scale.

Here's the budget breakdown I typically recommend for a local business:

- **$500–$700:** Ad spend on a streaming platform like Hulu's self-serve or YouTube TV
- **$200:** Creative production — use a smartphone with good lighting, or hire a local videographer for a simple 15-second spot
- **$100:** Retargeting budget (Google Ads or Facebook retargeting of CTV viewers)
- **Optional $50/month:** A basic Mailchimp or Square integration to capture leads from CTV

Total: $850–$1,050 per month. If your average customer lifetime value is under $100, this channel might not be for you yet. If you sell a $40 haircut and want customers returning monthly, the math can work.

## How to Track CTV Ads Without Losing Your Mind

Here's the thing about CTV advertising that nobody tells you: the tracking is messy. Your ad plays on a Roku. The customer sees it, then searches for your business on Google on their phone three days later. How do you connect those dots?

You can't perfectly. But you can get close.

**Use a unique phone number.** Set up a Google Voice number or a line through RingCentral that only appears in your CTV ad. Track how many calls that number gets. I worked with a pet groomer in Portland, Oregon who did this. They spent $600 on CTV, got 17 calls to their dedicated number, and 14 of those turned into bookings. Average grooming visit: $75. Revenue from CTV: $1,050. Net after ad spend: $450. Not life-changing, but profitable month one.

**Create a custom landing page.** Don't send CTV traffic to your homepage. Use a tool like Carrd or Unbounce to build a single-page site with a URL like "yourbusiness.com/tv." Include a clear next step: book a consultation, claim a discount, or sign up for a reminder. Track visits and conversions on that specific page.

**Use QR codes that go somewhere useful.** Most people won't scan a QR code while watching TV. But some will. Make sure the destination is mobile-optimized and gives them something immediate — not your "about us" page. A discount code, a free consultation, a downloadable guide. Something with perceived value.

**Set up Google Ads retargeting.** This is the most reliable bridge. When someone watches your CTV ad, their IP address or device ID gets captured. You can then serve them a Google Display or YouTube ad within 24 hours. The click-through rates on these retargeted ads are 3–5x higher than cold ads. I've seen local businesses get 8–12% conversion rates on retargeted traffic from CTV.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn't CTV advertising just for big brands with huge budgets?**

No. You can start a CTV campaign for $500 a month. The self-serve platforms from Hulu, Roku, and YouTube TV let you set daily caps and pause anytime. The minimums are lower than Facebook ads in many cases. I've run campaigns for a single-location yoga studio in Chicago for $350 a month that brought in 8 new members.

**Q: How is this different from a YouTube ad?**

YouTube ads play before videos someone actively chose. CTV ads play within streaming TV content someone is watching like traditional TV. The viewing mindset is different — people lean back and watch CTV content, rather than leaning in to search for something specific. This makes CTV better for brand awareness and emotional connection, while YouTube can be better for direct response.

**Q: What if nobody scans my QR code during the ad?**

That's normal. QR code scan rates on CTV average 1–3%. The real value of CTV is the top-of-mind awareness it creates. When that person later searches for "coffee shop near me" on Google, your name is the one they remember seeing on TV. The QR code is a bonus, not the main event.

**Q: Can I target people who are watching a specific show or channel?**

You can target by content genre — sports, news, comedy, drama — but not specific shows on most self-serve platforms. Hulu's self-serve lets you target by "comedy fans" or "sports viewers." If you want to appear on a specific show, you need a programmatic buying platform and a much larger budget. For local businesses, targeting by genre is good enough.

**Q: How do I know if CTV is worth continuing after the first month?**

Look at cost per lead and cost per customer, not impressions. If you're spending $800 and getting 5 new customers who spend an average of $100 each, that's $500 in direct revenue. That's not good. But if those customers come back four times a year, that customer lifetime value is $400 each — suddenly the math works. Calculate based on lifetime value, not first purchase.

**Q: Do I need to make a professional video, or can I use my phone?**

Use your phone — but use it well. Steady shot, good lighting, natural sound. Show your product or service in action, not someone talking into a camera from a desk. I've seen a $0-production video of a baker in Brooklyn pulling fresh croissants out of an oven outperform a $5,000 professionally produced ad for a competing bakery. Authenticity matters more than production value on CTV.
