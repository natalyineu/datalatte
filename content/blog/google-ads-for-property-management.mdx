## Why Google Ads Work for Property Management

Your ideal tenant isn’t searching "apartments for rent." They’re looking for "1BR near downtown Austin" or "pet-friendly units in Seattle." Google Ads let you meet them at this exact moment—when they’re ready to act.

Unlike broad Facebook ads, Google’s search and maps ads target people actively looking for properties. For example, a property manager in Dallas used location-based search ads to fill 12 vacancies in 3 weeks by bidding $2–$4 per "apartment rentals [City Name]" query.

Start with these 3 steps:
- Set a daily budget of $15–$50 based on your vacancy urgency
- Use the "Lead Generation" campaign type for property listings
- Add location exclusions to avoid overspending in saturated areas

[Google Ads management](/services/google-ads) helps automate these optimizations while you focus on tenant onboarding.

<Callout type="tip">**Use negative keywords** like "buy," "investment," and "commercial" to avoid irrelevant clicks from homebuyers or landlords.</Callout>

## Building High-Conversion Campaigns

Your Google Ads campaign should mirror how tenants search. Start by listing all your available units with clear pricing and photos. For example, a 3-unit property in Chicago created separate ad groups for "1BR downtown," "2BR family-friendly," and "studio near campus" to target distinct searchers.

## Budgeting for Vacancy Growth

You don’t need a six-figure budget to see results. A 5-unit property in Toronto filled vacancies 40% faster with just $300/month by following this split:

- 50% to search ads (new leads)
- 30% to maps ads (local visibility)
- 20% to remarketing (re-engaging past viewers)

Cost-per-click varies wildly by location. In competitive markets like Los Angeles, expect $10–$20 per click for high-demand keywords. In smaller towns, $2–$5 CPC is standard. Use Google’s Keyword Planner to estimate costs before launching.

## Avoiding Common Mistakes

Google Ads for property management is not a set-and-forget tool. Three mistakes drain budgets fast:

1. **Not updating listing photos:** Outdated images cost 20% of potential leads
2. **Ignoring ad scheduling:** Most units get viewed 8am–5pm weekdays
3. **Skipping call tracking:** 60% of ad traffic converts through phone calls, not website forms

Review your campaigns weekly using [analytics & reporting](/services/analytics) to spot underperforming keywords. For instance, if "luxury apartments [City]" has a 10% click-through rate but no conversions, pause it and test "affordable housing [City]" instead.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched property management companies burn through ad budgets like kindling. The mistakes are predictable. Here are the ones I see most often, along with what actually works.

### Mistake #1: Targeting Too Wide a Geographic Area

A property manager in Nashville came to me after spending $1,800 in six weeks with exactly zero lease signings. She had set her targeting to a 50-mile radius around the city. She figured more eyeballs meant more leads.

What happened: Her ads showed to people in Clarksville, Murfreesboro, and even as far as Bowling Green, Kentucky. Those people weren’t moving to Nashville. They were just scrolling. She paid for clicks from people who had no intention of renting her units. Her cost per click averaged $3.40. Her cost per lead was $112. And none of those leads converted.

The fix: I narrowed her radius to 5 miles around each property. I set location targeting to "People in or regularly in your targeted area" instead of "People interested in your targeted area." That second option shows your ads to anyone who *might* move to the city—not just the people already living there. We also added her properties as specific location targets in Google Maps.

The outcome: In the next four weeks, she spent $620, got 15 tour requests, and filled 4 units. Cost per lead dropped to $41. Cost per lease was $155. She cut her vacancy loss by roughly $4,800 in that first month alone.

**What to do instead:** Set radius targeting to 3–10 miles max for suburban properties. For downtown or urban properties, keep it under 3 miles. Use the "target locations" option that restricts to people physically in the area. Add your property addresses as location extensions so your ad shows up when someone searches "apartments near me" while standing two blocks away.

### Mistake #2: Bidding on Your Own Brand Name

I worked with a property management company in Austin that had 40 units split across three properties. They were spending $800 a month on brand keyword campaigns—bidding on their own company name. They thought it was necessary to dominate search results.

What happened: The company name was unique enough that nobody was searching for competitors under that name. The only people searching "Austin Property Solutions" were people who already knew who they were. They were paying $2.80 per click to show up first for their own name. Organic search already put them at position one. They were paying for traffic they were getting for free.

The fix: I paused all brand campaigns. I took that $800 and put it into non-brand campaigns targeting "2BR apartment Austin" and "dog-friendly rentals downtown Austin." I set up a call tracking number so we could measure exactly which campaigns generated phone calls.

The outcome: Brand search traffic didn't drop. Organic still handled those clicks. The non-brand campaigns generated 28 new leads in the first month. They filled 8 units from those leads. The $800 shifted from a cost to an investment with a measurable return.

**What to do instead:** Check your Google Search Console to see if you already rank first for your brand name organically. If you do, pause brand campaigns. If you're a new company with no organic presence, run brand campaigns at a low budget ($5/day max) just to establish presence. Otherwise, that money belongs in non-brand campaigns where you're reaching people who don't know you exist yet.

### Mistake #3: Using Bland Ad Copy That Says Nothing

A property manager in Denver was running ads with headlines like "Luxury Apartments Available" and "Spacious Units for Rent." She had three properties with different price points and different amenities. All three got the same generic ad.

What happened: Her click-through rate was 0.8%. Industry average for property management is around 3–5%. She was invisible. The people who did click landed on a generic page and bounced. Her quality score was 4/10, which meant Google charged her more per click. She was paying premium prices for garbage performance.

The fix: I wrote three separate ad groups, one for each property. Each ad included specific details: price range, available move-in dates, unique amenities ("rooftop lounge with skyline views," "fenced dog park," "in-unit washer/dryer"). I added callouts like "No application fees through Friday" and "24-hour maintenance response." I used the exact keywords people search for in the headlines.

The outcome: Click-through rate went from 0.8% to 4.2%. Quality score hit 8/10. Cost per click dropped from $3.10 to $1.90. Her cost per lease went from $380 to $165. She filled the vacancies in 18 days instead of the previous average of 52 days.

**What to do instead:** Write ads that sound like a real listing, not a corporate brochure. Include price, location, move-in date, and one specific amenity. Test at least three headlines per ad group. Use every ad extension Google offers—callouts, sitelinks, location, call. The more specific you are, the better your quality score gets and the less you pay.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Negative Keywords

A property management company in Portland was running ads for "studio apartments Portland." Their ads showed up for searches like "free apartment listings," "apartment rentals no credit check," and "rooms for rent $300." They were paying for clicks from people searching for things they couldn't provide.

What happened: Their conversion rate was 1.2%. They were spending $1,200 a month and getting maybe one or two tours. The problem was obvious when I looked at their search terms report. Half their spend went to people looking for sub-$500 housing or short-term rentals, which they didn't offer. They were burning money on irrelevant traffic.

The fix: I added negative keywords: "free," "no credit check," "short term," "weekly rental," "cheap," "$300," "$400," "$500," "roommate," "sublet," "temporary," "furnished" (if they only offered unfurnished), and specific competitor names. I set up a weekly search terms review to catch new irrelevant queries.

The outcome: Spend dropped to $680 the next month. But leads increased. Their conversion rate went from 1.2% to 4.8%. They filled 5 units in 30 days. Cost per lease dropped from $400 to $136. The wasted spend was eliminated, and their ads only showed to people who could actually afford and qualify for their units.

**What to do instead:** Pull your search terms report weekly for the first 60 days. Add every query that isn't directly relevant to what you offer. Common negative keywords for property management include: "free," "cheap," "short term," "vacation," "weekly," "monthly only," "no credit check," "section 8," "low income," "sublet," and specific price points below your minimum. This is the single easiest way to reduce wasted spend.

## How to Structure Campaigns for Different Property Portfolios

Not every property management company runs the same kind of business. A company managing 300 units across 20 buildings needs a different approach than a company managing six single-family rentals. Here's how to match your campaign structure to your portfolio.

### Single Property or Small Portfolio (1–10 Units)

You don't need a complex campaign structure. Create one campaign with one ad group per property. Each ad group should target the specific neighborhood or area where the property sits. Use exact-match keywords like "2BR apartment [neighborhood name]" or "studio rental [cross street]."

Budget: $15–$30 per day total. You're not trying to capture broad demand. You're trying to capture the handful of people searching for exactly what you have in that specific location.

Example: A property manager in Chicago with four units in Logan Square ran one campaign with four ad groups. She spent $20/day. Each ad group targeted specific street names and nearby landmarks. She filled all four units in 14 days. Cost per lease: $105.

### Medium Portfolio (10–50 Units)

Create separate campaigns for each property type or price tier. If you have luxury units and affordable units, don't lump them together. The person searching for a $3,000/month penthouse doesn't want to see a $1,200 studio ad.

Set up one campaign for standard units and one for premium units. Within each campaign, create ad groups by neighborhood or building. Use location extensions extensively. Include sitelinks for floor plans, virtual tours, and move-in specials.

Budget: $30–$75 per day. You have enough inventory that you need consistent lead flow, but not so much that you need broad reach. Focus on intent-based keywords with high conversion potential.

### Large Portfolio (50+ Units)

You need a full-funnel approach. Set up a branded campaign for people searching your company name. Set up non-branded campaigns for general rental searches. Create a retargeting campaign for people who visited your site but didn't submit a lead.

Within your non-branded campaigns, separate by property type, price range, and location. Use broad match with smart bidding to capture a wider range of searches. Test Discovery campaigns to reach people who haven't searched yet but match your renter demographic.

Budget: $75–$250+ per day. At this scale, you're competing with other large management companies and corporate landlords. You need volume. You also need a CRM integration so leads flow directly into your leasing system.

A property manager in Los Angeles with 200+ units ran three separate campaigns: one for luxury units, one for standard units, and one for short-term corporate rentals. Each campaign had different budgets, different ad copy, and different landing pages. She spent $4,500/month and maintained a 95% occupancy rate. Cost per lease averaged $215 across all campaigns.

## Tracking What Actually Matters

Most property managers track the wrong numbers. They look at total impressions or total clicks and think that means something. It doesn't. Here's what you should actually measure.

**Cost per lead:** How much you pay for each phone call, form submission, or email inquiry. This is your baseline efficiency metric. If it's over $50 for a standard apartment rental, your targeting or ad copy needs work.

**Cost per tour:** How much you pay for each scheduled property tour. This tells you if your landing page or call handling is working. If cost per lead is $30 but cost per tour is $150, your lead qualification process is broken.

**Cost per lease:** The only number that matters in the end. If you spend $500 to get one lease signed, and that lease generates $18,000 in annual rent, your ROI is 3,600%. If cost per lease is $2,000 and you're barely breaking even, something is wrong.

A property manager in Denver used a simple spreadsheet to track these three numbers. She ran Google Ads for six properties across two campaigns. Her cost per lease averaged $178. Her average monthly rent was $1,850. She calculated that each lease signed through ads generated $22,200 in annual revenue. Her advertising cost was less than 1% of that revenue. She quadrupled her ad budget the next month.

Use call tracking numbers (I recommend CallRail or WhatConverts) to capture phone leads. Set up conversion tracking in Google Ads for form submissions and click-to-call. If you're not tracking conversions, you're flying blind.

**Tools to use:**
- **Google Ads conversion tracking** (free, mandatory)
- **CallRail** ($45/month, tracks phone calls back to specific keywords)
- **Google Analytics 4** (free, tracks website behavior)
- **ProfitWell** (free, calculates customer lifetime value if you want to get fancy)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I manage 30 units and my monthly ad budget is $500. Is that enough?**

Probably, if you're targeting correctly. At $500/month, you're spending about $17/day. That's enough to capture high-intent searches in a specific neighborhood. The risk is spreading that budget too thin across multiple ad groups. Focus on your highest-priority vacancies and pause campaigns for units that have pending applications. You'd rather fill three units fast than have six units trickle in over three months.

**Q: Won't Google Ads just show my rental to people who are browsing and not serious?**

Not if you set it up correctly. Use search campaigns, not display campaigns. Searc shows your ad when someone types a specific query into Google, like "2BR apartment downtown Portland." That person is actively looking. Display campaigns show banner ads to people browsing websites, which is where you get tire-kickers. Stick with search. Add audience targeting to show your ads only to people in-market for rental properties. That filters out most of the non-serious traffic.

**Q: Should I use Google's "maximize conversions" bidding or manual CPC?**

Start with manual CPC for the first two weeks. You need to understand what your keywords actually cost. If you let Google bid automatically without conversion data, you'll overpay for low-quality clicks. After you've collected 20+ conversions, switch to target CPA bidding and set a cost per lead that makes sense for your margins. I usually set the target at 70% of my current cost per lead and let the algorithm optimize from there.

**Q: My properties are in a small town with 15,000 people. Will Google Ads even work here?**

It depends on the search volume. If 200 people a month search for "apartments" in your town, that's not enough volume for Google Ads to be efficient. In that case, put your budget into Facebook Marketplace, local Facebook groups, and Craigslist. Google Ads works best in markets with consistent search volume. If you're in a town of 15,000, your best bet is word-of-mouth and local classifieds. Google Ads for a small market is like using a fire hose to water a houseplant.

**Q: How long before I see results?**

If you set up conversion tracking correctly, you should see leads within the first week. I've seen campaigns generate calls within two hours of launching. The first lease might take two to three weeks because people need to see your ad, click, tour the property, think about it, and then sign. If you haven't had a tour request after 10 days and your campaign is spending, something is wrong with your targeting, ad copy, or landing page. Fix it.

**Q: Do I need a separate landing page or can I just send people to my website's listings page?**

Send them to the specific listing page for the unit in the ad. If you're advertising a 1BR in Austin, the landing page should be the page for that exact unit, not your general "search all listings" page. People get frustrated when they click an ad and have to dig for the apartment they saw in the ad. Specific landing pages improve quality scores and conversion rates. I've seen a 40% increase in tour requests just by switching from general pages to unit-specific pages.
