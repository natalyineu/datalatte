## What are Local SEO KPIs?
Local SEO KPIs are metrics that help you understand how well your website and online presence are performing in local search results. They're essential for measuring the success of your local SEO efforts and making data-driven decisions to improve your online visibility.

## Tracking Online Visibility
To measure local SEO success, you need to track your online visibility. This includes metrics such as:
* Google My Business (GMB) listing visibility
* Local search rankings
* Online reviews and ratings
* Website traffic from local search

<Callout type="tip">Make sure to claim and optimize your Google My Business listing to improve your online visibility.</Callout>

## Measuring Conversions
Conversions are the ultimate goal of local SEO. You want to track how many customers are visiting your business, making a purchase, or booking an appointment. Key conversion metrics include:
* Phone calls and form submissions
* Website conversions (e.g., online bookings, sales)
* In-store visits and sales

## Analyzing Local Search Rankings
Local search rankings are a critical component of local SEO. You need to track your rankings for target keywords and locations. Use tools like Ahrefs or SEMrush to monitor your rankings and adjust your SEO strategy accordingly.

## Understanding the Impact of Online Reviews
Online reviews play a significant role in local SEO. They can improve your visibility, increase trust, and drive more customers to your business. Track metrics such as:
* Review volume and frequency
* Review rating and sentiment
* Response rate and quality

<Callout type="warning">Respond promptly to all online reviews, both positive and negative, to show customers you value their feedback.</Callout>

Benchmarks can help you understand how well your local SEO efforts are performing compared to industry averages. For example:

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Tracking Rankings But Not Phone Calls

A bakery in Austin, TX — let's call them "Flour Power" — came to me after spending six months and roughly $4,200 on local SEO. Their rankings were beautiful. They showed me a spreadsheet with Position 1 for "custom cakes Austin" and Position 3 for "birthday cakes near me."

Their revenue had not moved.

I asked to see their call tracking setup. They looked at me like I'd asked about their childhood pet hamster. They had none. Not even a separate phone number on their website.

Here's what was happening: People searched for "custom cakes Austin," clicked the Google Business Profile listing, called the number on the profile — which was a mobile phone the owner answered at 4 AM while frosting a cake. He missed calls during business hours. He didn't return voicemails. He had no idea how many calls came from Google.

**The fix:** I set up a free Google Voice number (took 17 minutes) and swapped it into their GBP. Added call tracking through the Google Business Profile dashboard too. Then I connected Google Ads call extensions when they ran a local campaign.

**The outcome:** Over the next three months, Flour Power received 148 calls they could track. 42 converted to orders. Average order value: $85. That's $3,570 in directly attributable revenue. The SEO work itself was fine — it just needed a bridge to the cash register.

If you are not tracking phone calls, you are flying blind. Google Business Profile gives you call data for free. Use it. If you're running local Google Ads, turn on call extensions and use a tracking number. Do not rely on your personal cell phone and hope.

### Mistake #2: Focusing on Review Quantity Over Quality

A dental practice in Denver — "Mile High Smiles" — had 187 Google reviews. Their average rating: 4.1 stars. They were proud of the count. The owner told me they asked every single patient to leave a review, no exceptions.

They had a problem. Four of those reviews were one-star rants from 2020 about a scheduling error, and three more detailed a specific dentist who'd left the practice. Those seven reviews sat at the top of their review sort because Google defaults to "Most Relevant" — which prioritizes recent, long, or high-engagement reviews. The negative ones had multiple replies and dozens of thumbs-up reactions from people who found them "funny" or "helpful."

A 4.1 average sounds fine until you realize a competitor three blocks away had a 4.8 from 93 reviews. That competitor's phone rang constantly.

**The fix:** We stopped asking every patient for a review. Instead, we targeted the happy ones — the ones who thanked the front desk or complimented a hygienist. We sent a follow-up email within two hours of their appointment (when satisfaction peaks) with a direct link to leave a review. For the old negatives, the owner wrote professional, brief responses acknowledging the issues and explaining changes made since. Google doesn't remove bad reviews, but a visible response signals you're not ignoring problems.

**The outcome:** Over four months, their rating climbed to 4.6 from 42 new reviews. Their call volume increased by roughly 35%. They stopped paying $800/month for a third-party reputation management tool they'd never needed.

More reviews is not automatically better. More *good* reviews from actual recent customers who represent your current service — that's better.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Local Link Signals

A hair salon in Nashville — "Cuts & Confessions" — had done everything right on-page. GBP optimized. Reviews managed. Posts every Tuesday and Friday. Their organic traffic from local search was solid.

But they couldn't crack the top three for "hair salon Nashville" even though their Google Business Profile was fully loaded.

Their website had one backlink: a directory listing they'd paid $99 for. That was it. Nobody was linking to a hair salon's website. Why would they?

**The fix:** I looked at what local organizations and businesses a salon could realistically partner with. We got listed on the Nashville Chamber of Commerce site (free). They sponsored a small charity event and got a link. They wrote a 400-word guide to "What Nashville Brides Should Ask Their Hair Stylist" and the local wedding blog published it. They started trading links with a wedding photographer and a dress shop — entirely relevant, local businesses.

**The outcome:** Four months in, "hair salon Nashville" went from Position 8 to Position 4. "Nashville hair extensions" hit Position 2. Cost: roughly $200 (event sponsorship) and time. Revenue from new clients who mentioned finding them through search increased by about $1,800/month.

Local link building is tedious. It is also effective. Skip the spammy directories. Find real organizations and adjacent businesses in your city.

### Mistake #4: Treating Mobile and Desktop as the Same

A pet groomer in Portland, OR — "Furry Godmother" — noticed their bounce rate was 62%. They assumed it was a content problem. Their site was slow, they figured. They paid $1,200 for a site redesign and a faster host.

Bounce rate stayed at 60%.

I pulled up their Google Analytics and filtered by device. Desktop bounce rate: 38%. Mobile bounce rate: 74%. Their mobile site was a nightmare — text too small, buttons too close together, a booking form that required seven fields to scroll through on a 5-inch screen. Their new design hadn't fixed any of it because the designer only checked it on a laptop.

**The fix:** We moved their booking system to Booksy (which handles mobile scheduling natively) and replaced their contact form with a simple tap-to-call button. Shrunk the header images. Made the call-to-action button — "Book Your Groom" — sticky at the bottom of the screen.

**The outcome:** Mobile bounce rate dropped to 42% over six weeks. Mobile bookings increased 55%. Revenue from mobile visitors went from about $1,400/month to $2,600/month. The total cost was the Booksy subscription (around $35/month) and two hours of a freelancer's time to reformat the site.

If you're only looking at your site on a desktop, you're optimizing for 2020 behavior. Most local searches happen on a phone. Check your analytics. If you don't know your mobile bounce rate, start there.

## How to Track the Full Local Search Funnel (Not Just the First Click)

Most small business owners track the wrong thing: a ranking. A ranking is a position on a list. It is not a customer.

The full local search funnel looks like this: Search → Click → Action (call, visit, book) → Revenue.

Each stage needs its own metric.

**Stage 1: Visibility (Rankings + GBP Impressions)**

I use a simple spreadsheet. Every two weeks, I check 10–15 keyword phrases that actually describe what a business sells. "Dog grooming Austin" gets recorded. "Pet groomer 78701" gets recorded. I track Position 1–5, 6–10, and 11+. Anything past page one is noise for a local business.

Google Business Profile provides "Search Queries" data under Performance. This shows exactly what people typed to find your listing. Check it once a week. If someone searched "hair salon open Sunday Denver" and you didn't show up, that's a specific fix — add Sunday hours to your GBP.

**Stage 2: Engagement (Clicks, Calls, Direction Requests)**

GBP also tracks this. The three numbers that matter: Website Clicks, Phone Calls, and Direction Requests. If calls are low but impressions are high, your phone number might be wrong or your business hours are confusing. Don't guess — check.

I had a client in Chicago whose direction requests dropped by 80% in one month. Turns out Google had changed their pin location to a neighboring apartment building. Took me three days to notice because I was busy looking at rankings. Never again.

**Stage 3: On-Site Action (Bookings, Form Fills, Call Duration)**

This is where tools like Google Analytics (free), Google Tag Manager (free), and a call tracking service (Google Voice or a paid one) come in. Track button clicks, not just page views. If someone clicks "Book Now" and leaves — why? The form might be too long or the available times might be confusing.

For calls, I look at duration. A call under 30 seconds is usually a wrong number or a hang-up. Over 60 seconds is a real inquiry. Track those.

**Stage 4: Revenue (Closed Sales)**

This is the hardest and most important. If you use Square for payments, link your online bookings to actual purchases. If you use Mailchimp for email, tag subscribers who came through a local search source. If you're a salon using Booksy, that platform will tell you exactly which services were booked and by which channel.

I worked with a fitness studio in NYC that was spending $750/month on local SEO. The owner had no idea if a single new client came from search because she accepted walk-ins and took payments via Venmo. After I persuaded her to start asking every new client "How did you find us?" and tracking it in a Google Sheet, she discovered that 70% of new clients came from a combination of Yelp and Google Search. She dropped the SEO spend to $400/month and put $350 into optimizing her Yelp presence instead. Revenue didn't drop — it actually went up slightly.

That's the point of tracking the funnel. You stop paying for things that don't work.

## What Your Competition's Local SEO Actually Looks Like (And How to Outpace Them)

Every small business owner I've ever spoken to believes their competitors have some secret advantage. They don't. They're probably making the same mistakes you are.

Here's an exercise that costs nothing and takes 45 minutes. It's honestly more useful than most paid audit tools.

**Step 1: Map your local competitors.**

Search Google Maps for your service + your city. You get a map with 20 businesses. That's your list. Not the websites that rank nationally — the ones on the map. Those are your real competitors.

**Step 2: Audit their Google Business Profile.**

Click each one. Look for:
- Do they have complete categories selected? (Not just "Hair Salon" but also "Hair Extensions Service" and "Balayage Service.")
- Are they posting regularly? Look at GBP posts.
- Do they respond to reviews? Both positive and negative.
- How many photos do they have? A profile with 40 photos generally outranks one with 8.
- What's their Q&A section look like? Did they populate it with common questions, or is it empty?

**Step 3: Check their website for local signals.**

View the page source of their homepage. Search for their city name. Is it in the title tag? The H1? The meta description? If not, they're leaving easy signals on the table.

I did this for a coffee shop in Portland that was stuck in Position 6 behind a chain. The chain's GBP had 200 photos. The local shop had 12. That was basically the entire difference. The local shop spent a week posting 40 photos — interior, exterior, menu boards, staff, latte art — and moved to Position 3 in about three weeks. No new links. No content changes. Just photos on Google Business Profile.

**Step 4: Look at their reviews strategically.**

Don't just look at star counts. Read the negative reviews. What do people complain about? "Parking is terrible." You can address that on your own profile. "They never answer the phone." That's a fix you can implement. "The website booking tool is broken." That's a competitive advantage if yours works.

**Step 5: See where else they show up.**

Search their business name plus a common misspelling or abbreviation. Do they have listings on Yelp, Facebook, Yellow Pages, Nextdoor, Angi? If they're on five directories and you're on two, they're getting referral traffic and backlinks you aren't.

I had a client in Austin (a pet groomer) who was only on Google and Yelp. Her main competitor was on Google, Yelp, Nextdoor, Facebook Marketplace, and a local "Austin Pets" directory. We got her listed on Nextdoor and the local directory within a week. Cost: $0 for Nextdoor, $50/year for the directory. Within two months, her website traffic from non-Google sources went up 30%.

This isn't complicated work. It's tedious. But it works consistently because most small business owners never do it. They assume their competition has a secret sauce. The secret is they filled out more fields on a form, took more photos, and answered a few more review responses.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm spending $500/month on local SEO. How long until I see results?**

Depends on where you're starting. If your Google Business Profile is claimed and optimized, your category is correct, and you have at least 10 recent reviews, you might see ranking improvements in 4–6 weeks. If your GBP is unclaimed and your website has no local content, expect 3–6 months. I've seen one client — a plumber in Chicago — jump from page four to page one for "emergency plumber Chicago" in 11 days because nobody else in the area had correct hours posted. That's rare. Budget for three months before you decide whether the investment is working.

**Q: Do I actually need to pay for a Google Business Profile tool?**

No. Google Business Profile is free. The tools that charge you are either managing multiple locations (not relevant for most single-location businesses) or automating posts and review responses. You can do both manually in 15 minutes per week. If you're paying $100/month for a tool you use to schedule one GBP post and respond to three reviews, cancel it.

**Q: Yelp vs. Google — which one should I prioritize?**

Google, by a wide margin. Google controls roughly 92% of search engine market share and owns the maps experience. Yelp matters if you're a restaurant, a hair salon, or a service that people actively search on Yelp (which is still a thing in major cities). I'd spend 80% of your time on Google and 20% on any secondary platform that drives traffic in your industry. For a fitness studio in NYC, that might be Yelp. For a dog walker in Austin, it might be Nextdoor.

**Q: My competitor has 500 reviews and I have 30. Am I screwed?**

Not if those 500 reviews are from three years ago and their recent ones are complaints. Google weights recency. A steady trickle of recent positive reviews outperforms a massive pile of old ones. Focus on getting 3–5 new reviews per month from actual customers. The competitor with 500 reviews probably got them by asking every customer for years. Start now. You don't need 500. You need 40–50 recent ones that show you're currently good at your job.

**Q: Can I track local SEO without Google Analytics?**

You can, but it's harder. Google Business Profile gives you performance data — impressions, searches, calls, direction requests — for free, no analytics account needed. Square, Booksy, and similar booking platforms track your conversions themselves. What you lose is the ability to see what people do on your *website* after they click through from Google. I'd set up Google Analytics and Google Search Console. They're free. Install them once and ignore them for two months, then check. You don't need to live in the dashboard.

**Q: Do online reviews actually affect my rankings, or is that a myth?**

Yes, they affect rankings. Google has confirmed review signals are a factor in local search. More importantly, reviews affect conversion. I've seen a business with a 4.7 rating and 60 reviews outrank a business with a 4.1 and 200 reviews for the same keyword. The 4.7 shop had recent reviews, a complete profile, and photos. The 4.1 shop had old reviews and a sparse profile. Reviews are part of a larger package. Ignore them and you're working with one hand tied behind your back.
