## Creating Effective Email Marketing Templates
Now that you have your audience and email list, it's time to create effective email marketing templates. Here are 5 templates you can use:
* Template 1: Welcome email - This email is sent to new subscribers and welcomes them to your list. It can include a brief introduction to your business, a special offer, and a call-to-action to book a service.
* Template 2: Promotion email - This email is used to promote a special offer or discount. It can include a sense of urgency, a clear call-to-action, and a reminder of the benefits of using your services.
* Template 3: Educational email - This email is used to educate your audience about pet grooming and health. It can include tips, advice, and resources, and can help establish your business as an authority in the industry.
* Template 4: Abandoned cart email - This email is sent to customers who have abandoned their cart or haven't booked a service in a while. It can include a reminder of the services they were interested in, a special offer, and a call-to-action to book a service.
* Template 5: Loyalty email - This email is sent to loyal customers and rewards them for their loyalty. It can include a special offer, a discount, and a thank you message.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've reviewed dozens of pet grooming email programs over the years. Some were quietly printing money. Most were quietly bleeding it. Here are the four patterns I see most often, along with the fixes that actually work.

### Mistake #1: Treating Every Email Like a Coupon Blast

A groomer in Austin, Texas — let's call her shop "Happy Paws" — was sending the same email every two weeks: "15% off this week only." She had 1,400 subscribers on Mailchimp. Open rates hovered around 11%. Click rates were under 2%. She told me she felt like she was "shouting into a void."

I looked at her send history. The problem wasn't the discount. The problem was that she had *never* sent anything else. No introduction emails. No grooming tips. No photos of before-and-after transformations. Nothing that built a relationship. Just coupon after coupon.

People's brains learn to filter that out. You become spam before you ever hit send.

**The fix:** We built a 4-email welcome sequence that introduced her, showed her work, and explained her pricing philosophy before she mentioned a single discount. Then we capped promotional emails to one per month, max. The other emails became valuable: how to manage shedding season, what to do about matted fur, when to bring a puppy in for their first groom.

**The outcome:** Over three months, her open rate climbed from 11% to 27%. Click-through rate went to 6.4%. But the real number was revenue per email sent: it tripled. She went from generating about $320 per campaign to $980. She emailed less and made more. That's the uncomfortable truth most guides skip.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Your Existing Clients to Chase New Ones

A groomer in Nashville had a thriving practice. Four employees, fifty-plus dogs per week, booked solid. But she was terrified of losing momentum. So she spent $800/month on Facebook ads pushing "New Client Special: $25 off First Groom."

Meanwhile, her existing clients — the ones who booked every three weeks like clockwork — were getting nothing. No loyalty offers. No birthday emails for their dogs. No "we miss you" messages if they hadn't booked in six weeks.

One client told her: "I thought you were full. I didn't want to bother you." She was losing clients not to competition, but to silence.

**The fix:** We redirected $500 of that ad budget into a loyalty sequence. Existing clients who booked at least four times in the last six months got a "skip the line" email: book your next appointment before we open public slots, plus a free nail trim. We also built a re-engagement sequence for anyone who hadn't booked in eight weeks or more.

**The outcome:** The loyalty sequence reactivated 18% of her lapsed clients within the first month. That alone brought in $1,400 in new bookings. Meanwhile, her new-client acquisition numbers barely dipped — because word-of-mouth from happy existing clients was doing more work than the ads ever did. She cut her ad spend to $300/month and netted $1,200 more per month overall.

### Mistake #3: Sending Everything From a "No Reply" Address

This one drives me up a wall. A groomer in Portland, Oregon had a Mailchimp account with 2,800 subscribers. She was sending solid content — seasonal grooming tips, reminders about flea season, cute client photos. But she was sending from "noreply@happypaws.com."

She told me she didn't want to deal with replies. I asked her: "How many people have tried to reply and gotten a bounce-back?"

She didn't know. So we switched her sender to her actual name — "Sarah from Happy Paws" — with a real reply-to address. I told her she didn't have to answer every email immediately. But when clients reply, they're giving you permission to have a conversation. That's literally the point.

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, she had 47 replies. Ten were thank-yous. Eight were appointment requests. Four were complaints (which she resolved and turned into repeat clients). The rest were questions about services and pricing. She booked an extra $680 in appointments directly from email replies in that first month. That's money that was bouncing into the void before.

### Mistake #4: Sending on a Random Schedule Based on "Feeling"

A groomer in Chicago sent emails whenever she "had time." Sometimes twice a week. Sometimes once a month. She used Gmail, not an email service provider. No segmentation. No tracking beyond what Gmail shows (which is almost nothing useful).

She had no idea what was working. She'd send a "10% off senior dogs" email and then a "puppy packages available" email a day apart, then nothing for five weeks. Her list was stagnant because people forgot who she was between sends.

**The fix:** We moved her to Mailchimp's free plan and set up a simple schedule: send every Tuesday at 10 AM. One educational email per month, one promotional email per month, and an automated welcome sequence for new subscribers. That was it. Consistent, predictable, trackable.

**The outcome:** After eight weeks, her list grew from 340 to 520 subscribers — because the welcome sequence had an incentive ($5 off first groom) she was actually promoting. Her open rate stabilized at 31%. And for the first time, she could see which emails drove bookings. She told me: "I spent two years guessing. Now I know."

## The Welcome Sequence That Does the Work for You

Most pet groomers set up a welcome email and call it done. One email. Maybe two. But your welcome sequence is the highest-engagement moment you'll ever have with a new subscriber. The open rates on those first few emails average 40-60%. If you waste that slot with a generic "thanks for signing up," you're leaving money on the table.

Here's what I've seen work across multiple grooming businesses, using tools you probably already have.

**Email 1 (immediate):** A short intro from you. Your name, your shop's story, a photo of you with a dog. No offers yet. Just: "Here's who I am and why I'm obsessed with making dogs look ridiculous in the best way." One groomer in Denver used a photo of her own pug wearing a bow tie after a groom. That email alone had a 58% open rate. People want to know who they're trusting with their pet.

**Email 2 (24 hours later):** A clear explanation of your services and pricing. Not a wall of text — bullet points or a simple table. Include what's included in each service (brushing, nail trim, ear cleaning) and what's extra. A groomer in Seattle discovered that her "basic groom" was being compared to a competitor's "deluxe groom" — and she was losing price-conscious clients because they didn't realize her basic included things the competitor charged extra for. She added a comparison chart to her welcome sequence and saw a 22% increase in basic groom bookings from new clients.

**Email 3 (48 hours later):** Social proof. Three to five client photos with brief testimonials. If you're using Booksy or Square Appointments pull real review excerpts. "Best groom we've had in Denver — our goldendoodle actually wags his tail when we pull up." That kind of thing. One groomer in Los Angeles added a "Meet the Regulars" section with repeat client stories and saw a 19% increase in first-time bookings.

**Email 4 (72 hours later):** The offer. But make it weirdly specific. Not "15% off." Something like: "$10 off your first full groom when you mention this email. Expires in 14 days." Specific offers convert better because they feel like a mistake — like you accidentally sent a coupon meant for someone else. Psychologically, people want to take advantage of a small window.

I've watched this four-email sequence increase first-time booking conversion rates from under 10% to over 30% within the first month. And once it's built in Mailchimp or Constant Contact, it runs on autopilot. You set it once and it works while you're washing a labradoodle.

## What to Send When a Client Goes Silent (Re-engagement Sequences)

Here's a number that surprised me: most pet grooming businesses lose between 25% and 35% of their active clients every year. Not because the grooming was bad. Usually because life got busy. Someone switched jobs, moved neighborhoods, or their dog died. And they never told you.

Most groomers do nothing when a client goes silent. They just hope the client comes back. That's a low-probability strategy.

A groomer in Dallas had 1,200 clients in her Square database. About 400 of them hadn't booked in four months or longer. She was sending her regular monthly newsletter to everyone — including those 400 — and wondering why her overall open rates were dropping. The engaged clients were getting lost in the noise.

**The fix:** We built a re-engagement sequence that activated when a client hit the 60-day mark without a booking. Here's what it looked like, using Mailchimp's automation tools:

**Email 1 (60 days since last booking):** "We haven't seen [dog's name] in a while. No judgment — life happens. But here's something that might help." Then link to a blog post about in-home grooming tips or seasonal shedding advice. No sell. Just useful. Open rates on this email ran about 38%.

**Email 2 (75 days since last booking):** "Quick heads up: our appointment slots for [next month] are opening up soon. If you've been meaning to book, now's a good time." Short. Direct. No coupon. This one drove a 12% rebooking rate among the 400 lapsed clients.

**Email 3 (90 days since last booking):** The nuclear option. "We miss you. Here's $15 off your next groom — just for coming back." If they don't respond to this, they're likely gone for good. But about 8% of the remaining lapsed clients booked after this email.

**The outcome:** Over three months, 92 of the 400 lapsed clients rebooked. Average spend per visit was $72. That's $6,624 in recovered revenue from a sequence that took two hours to set up and cost nothing beyond her existing Mailchimp subscription.

The kicker? She set a rule: anyone who hit the third email without responding was moved to a "cold" list and removed from regular sends. Her deliverability improved. Her open rates went up. And she stopped paying for subscribers who were never going to open another email.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I barely have time to groom the dogs I have. How am I supposed to write emails too?**

You're not writing them from scratch every week. You're setting up sequences once — the welcome sequence, the loyalty sequence, the re-engagement sequence. That takes an afternoon. Then you're sending one or two emails per month that take 20 minutes each. Use photos you already take of clients' dogs. Write like you talk. If you absolutely cannot find 20 minutes a month, hire a high school student to draft them and you approve. The ROI on that hour of work is absurd.

**Q: Will people actually unsubscribe if I send more than one email a month?**

Yes. And that's fine. If they unsubscribe because you sent a grooming tip and a booking reminder in the same month, they weren't going to book anyway. You want a list of people who actually open and click. A smaller engaged list outperforms a huge dead list every single time. I'd rather have 200 subscribers with a 40% open rate than 2,000 with a 5% open rate. The first one makes you money. The second one hurts your deliverability.

**Q: Do I need to be on Mailchimp, or can I use something cheaper?**

Mailchimp's free plan works up to 2,000 subscribers. That covers most independent groomers. If you're bigger than that, you're already making enough to justify $15-30 per month. Constant Contact is fine too. The tool matters less than whether you're actually sending. I've seen groomers on Gmail BCC lists outperform groomers on expensive platforms — because they were actually sending consistently. Pick one, set it up, and go.

**Q: What about those pet grooming apps like Booksy? Isn't that enough?**

Booksy is great for booking and reminders. It is not email marketing. Your text messages should be transactional: "Your appointment is tomorrow at 2 PM." Your emails should be relational: "Here's what [dog's name] looked like after their last groom, and why you should book before the summer rush." They serve different purposes. Use both.

**Q: How do I know if my emails are working?**

Look at three numbers: open rate (should be above 25% for pet grooming), click-through rate (above 3% is solid), and most importantly — bookings attributed to email. If you're using Square, you can add UTM tracking to your email links. If you're using Booksy, ask new clients how they heard about you. Track it. If you can't measure it, you're guessing. And I don't like guessing with your money.

**Q: What if I'm in a small town and only have 150 clients? Is this worth it?**

Yes. A groomer in rural Wisconsin had 97 clients. She set up a welcome sequence. Within two months, 12 of those 97 had referred a friend or family member who then booked. That's a 12% referral rate from a sequence that took her 90 minutes to set up. The math works at every scale because email is the cheapest form of marketing you'll ever run. Even a small increase in repeat bookings or referrals pays for the time.
