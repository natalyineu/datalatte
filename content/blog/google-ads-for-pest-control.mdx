## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Bidding on Broad Match Keywords and Burning Budget on Tire Kickers

A pest control company in Austin, Texas came to me after running Google Ads for four months. They were spending $1,800/month and had gotten exactly two jobs — both small ant treatments. The owner was ready to swear off Google Ads forever.

I looked at their account. They were running broad match keywords like "pest control" and "bugs in house." Their ads were showing for people searching "how to get rid of ants naturally," "pest control jobs near me" (people looking for employment), and "bug exterminator salary." They had zero negative keywords. Their budget was bleeding out to people who would never, ever hire them.

**The fix:** I restructured their campaign to use phrase match and exact match keywords only — "rodent removal Austin," "ant exterminator Austin TX," "emergency pest control 78701." I added 47 negative keywords including "jobs," "career," "salary," "how to," "DIY," "natural," "homemade." I set their location targeting to a 15-mile radius around their service area and turned off "people interested in your location" to avoid showing ads to someone in Seattle researching Austin pest control.

**The outcome:** Same $1,800/month budget. In the first 30 days after the changes, they got 14 booked jobs at an average ticket of $320 — that's $4,480 in revenue. Their cost per acquisition dropped from $900 to $128. The owner called me and said, "I thought Google Ads was a scam. Turns out I was just doing it wrong."

### Mistake 2: No Call Tracking — Flying Blind on Conversions

A Nashville pest control company had been running Google Ads for six months. They told me they were "getting tons of calls." I asked how many turned into jobs. They didn't know. They were relying on the ad rep's word that the campaign was "performing well."

When I looked at their account, they had no call tracking setup and no conversion tracking at all. Google Ads was reporting "phone calls" based on a click-to-call button on their mobile ads — but they had no way to know if those calls were real customers or wrong numbers or people asking for directions.

I asked to see their phone bill compared to the ad platform's call numbers. They were paying $1,500/month for ads, and Google reported 45 calls the previous month. Actual inbound calls to their business number? 12. The other 33 "calls" either never connected or rang for three seconds and dropped.

**The fix:** I set up call tracking using CallRail (integrates directly with Google Ads). Each call gets a unique forwarding number so it tracks back to the specific keyword and ad that triggered it. I also set up Google Tag Manager to track form submissions and booked appointments. Then I imported those conversions back into Google Ads as primary conversion actions and told the algorithm to optimize for actual booked jobs, not "clicks."

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, they knew exactly which keywords generated paying customers. "Emergency pest control Nashville" had a 34% close rate. "Pest control near me" had a 2% close rate — mostly people price-shopping five different companies. We shifted 70% of the budget to the keywords that actually made money. Monthly revenue from Google Ads went from roughly $3,800 (best guess, no data) to a verified $6,200. They spent $1,500 on ads and $79/month on CallRail. Worth every penny.

### Mistake 3: Using a Generic Landing Page That Google Punishes

A Denver pest control company had a beautiful website. Professional photos. Clean design. But their Google Ads were sending traffic to their home page — which had a hero image, a "learn more about us" button, and zero mention of emergency pest control. The page loaded in 6.2 seconds on mobile. Their Quality Scores were 3s and 4s across the board.

For context: a Quality Score of 3 means you're paying roughly 400% more per click than a competitor with a Quality Score of 8. This company was spending $2,200/month and getting outbid on their own keywords because Google didn't trust their landing page.

**The fix:** I built dedicated landing pages for each service type. Rodent removal got a page that said "Rodent Removal in Denver — Same Day Service" in the headline. Ant control got its own page. Bed bugs got its own page. Each page had:
- A headline matching the search query
- A click-to-call button visible without scrolling
- Three bullet points about response time and guarantees
- A simple contact form with four fields
- Mobile load time under 2 seconds (used Google PageSpeed Insights and compressed images)

I also switched their ad copy to include the service name in the headline, not just the "Pest Control" umbrella term.

**The outcome:** Quality Scores went from 3s and 4s to 7s and 8s. Cost per click dropped from an average of $8.50 to $3.25. Same $2,200 budget now generated 57% more clicks. But more importantly — conversion rate on those landing pages was 18.4% compared to their old home page conversion rate of 3.1%. Booked jobs went from 9 per month to 31 per month. Revenue increase: roughly $7,000/month. All from changing one page and rewriting ads.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Negative Days and Times

A pest control company in Portland, Oregon was running their ads 24/7 and wondering why they got calls at 2 AM from people who "just had a question." They also weren't scheduling their ads around their actual availability. They only answered phone calls from 7 AM to 8 PM, but their ads ran constantly.

Google's data showed they were spending $400/month on clicks between 8 PM and 6 AM. Those clicks generated 12 phone calls over 30 days — all voicemails. Zero booked jobs from overnight calls because their voicemail message didn't even mention emergency services, and follow-up calls the next day usually found someone who already hired a different company.

**The fix:** I looked at their call data from the previous six months and found a clear pattern: 85% of their booked jobs came between 8 AM and 6 PM, with a spike between 10 AM and 2 PM (people spotting roaches at lunch) and another spike at 4-6 PM (people getting home and finding evidence). I set ad scheduling to run 7 AM to 8 PM daily, with higher bids during the peak windows. I also added ad schedule bid adjustments: +25% for 10 AM-2 PM, +15% for 4-6 PM, and standard bids the rest of the day.

**The outcome:** Their monthly ad spend dropped from $2,800 to $2,100 — that $400 overnight waste was gone, and they also reduced hours with lower intent. But their booked jobs actually increased from 28 to 34 per month because the budget was concentrated when people actually called and answered. Cost per booked job dropped from $100 to $61. The owner told me, "I can't believe I was basically lighting money on fire while I was sleeping."

## What To Do When Yelp and Google Ads Both Want Your Money

If you're running a pest control business, you've probably gotten the Yelp sales call. "We have 50 million monthly visitors. You need to be advertising here." And maybe you already have a Yelp Business page with a few reviews.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: Yelp ads for pest control can work — but only if you've already got Google Ads dialed in. I worked with a Chicago pest control company that was splitting $3,000/month across both platforms: $1,500 on Google Ads, $1,500 on Yelp. Their Yelp ads were generating leads at $87 each. Their Google Ads were generating leads at $34 each. They were paying 2.5x more per lead on Yelp for lower-intent searchers.

Why lower intent? Someone on Yelp is browsing — comparing reviews, reading about bed bug treatment costs, looking at photos. Someone on Google searching "emergency rat removal Chicago" has a rat in their kitchen. They're not browsing. They're hiring.

I'm not saying ditch Yelp entirely. Yelp's value is in your organic reviews — a 4.5-star Yelp rating with 30+ reviews builds trust when someone googles your business name. But paid Yelp ads? For pest control in 2025, I've seen better returns from those dollars going into Google Ads with a strong local service ads setup.

Google's Local Service Ads (LSAs) are the real hidden gem for pest control. These are the "Google Guaranteed" ads that show above regular search results with a green checkmark. You pay per lead, not per click. In my experience, LSA leads for pest control close at 40-60% because customers see the Google Guarantee badge and trust it. I've had clients in Phoenix and Las Vegas see LSA lead costs of $25-40 per booked job — cheaper than regular Google Ads and way cheaper than Yelp.

If your budget is under $2,000/month, put every dollar into Google Ads and LSAs before touching Yelp paid ads. Once you're spending $3,000+ and your Google Ads are optimized, test Yelp with $500/month max and measure actual booked jobs — not Yelp's reported "leads."

## How To Use Mailchimp and Text Marketing To Recover Lost Leads

Here's something most pest control guides skip: a lot of your Google Ads traffic doesn't convert on the first visit. Someone clicks your ad at 11 PM, sees your click-to-call button, thinks "I'll call tomorrow," and then calls a competitor at 8 AM because your number isn't at the top of their mind.

I fixed this for a pest control company in Charlotte, North Carolina using a two-step process that cost them $49/month.

**Step 1: Capture emails and phone numbers from non-converting visitors.** I set up a simple exit-intent popup on their landing pages using Sumo (free tier was enough). When someone moved their cursor toward the browser bar or back button, a popup appeared: "Need pest help now? Get $25 off your first treatment — just enter your number and we'll text you a coupon." The popup captured 34 phone numbers in the first month.

**Step 2: Automated follow-up sequence.** I connected Mailchimp's free plan to their landing page form and set up a three-email sequence and an SMS follow-up:
- Email 1 sent 30 minutes after the popup: "Here's your $25 off coupon code. Ready to book? Here's our same-day service number."
- SMS sent 4 hours later (if no call was booked): "Hey [Name], just checking if you need pest help. Reply BOOK and we'll call you in 15 minutes."
- Email 2 sent 24 hours later: "Three signs you might have rodents (and why waiting costs more)."

The email-to-SMS handoff used TextMagic ($10/month for 400 messages). The SMS message looked like a personal text from the business owner — because it was.

**The outcome:** Out of 34 numbers captured in month one, 11 people booked jobs via the SMS or email follow-up. Average job value: $275. That's $3,025 in revenue from people who would have otherwise disappeared into the internet void. Cost of the setup: $59/month (Mailchimp free plan, TextMagic $10, Sumo free tier). One-time setup time: about 2 hours.

Most pest control businesses spend all their energy on getting the click and nothing on following up with people who almost booked. Those "almost" leads are worth real money.

## Seasonal Budget Pacing: Don't Run Ads the Same Way in January and July

Pest control is seasonal. Roaches and rodents are year-round problems in most of the US, but ant calls spike in spring, wasp calls peak in August, and rodent calls jump in October when it gets cold. I see small business owners make the mistake of setting a flat monthly budget and never adjusting.

A pest control company in Atlanta asked me why their ads were "getting worse" in March. They were spending $2,500/month year-round with the same bids. In January, they were getting 2-3 jobs per week — decent for winter. In March, ants started coming out and their competitors turned up their bids. Their ads stopped showing because the same $2,500 bought fewer clicks during peak season.

**Here's the fix I use with my clients:**

Look at your business data from the past 12 months. Identify your three lowest-spend months and three highest-spend months. Set your base monthly budget at your average. Then create a "seasonal adjustment" calendar in Google Ads using the "seasonality" adjustments in your shared library:

- Peak months: Increase budget by 20-30% and raise bids by 15%. You want to be aggressive when demand is highest.
- Slow months: Drop budget by 20% and lower bids by 10%. Keep running ads to capture the urgent searchers who exist year-round, but don't bleed money trying to compete with the same cost per click as spring.

For the Atlanta company, we shifted to $2,000/month in December-February, $3,000/month in March-May, $2,500/month in June-August, and $3,000/month again in September-November (rodent season). Total annual spend stayed about the same — $30,000 — but revenue increased because they were winning more auctions in high-demand months when customers were ready to book same-day.

Also important: update your ad copy seasonally. Running "ant control" ads in January is a waste. Running "rodent-proof your home for winter" in October speaks directly to what people are searching for. I use Google Ads' customizer feature to automatically swap headlines based on the month. Takes 20 minutes to set up, runs on autopilot.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is Google Ads really worth it for a small pest control business with a $1,000/month budget?**

Yes, if you do it right. I've seen a $1,000/month budget generate $4,000-6,000 in revenue for pest control businesses in midsized US cities like Knoxville or Boise. The key is hyper-local targeting — set a 10-mile radius, use exact match keywords like "rat removal [city name]" and "ant exterminator [zip code]," and make sure your landing page converts. If you're in a major metro like NYC or Los Angeles where cost per click is $12-18, $1,000/month won't get you far. In that case, you need at least $2,000-3,000/month or focus on LSAs.

**Q: How do I compete with Terminix and Orkin on Google Ads?**

You don't try to beat them on brand searches. You beat them on speed and specificity. Big companies respond in 24-48 hours for non-emergency calls. You can respond in 2 hours. Use ad copy that says "Same-day service — we answer our own phones" and "Local family business — not a national call center." Also, run LSAs where you can get a Google Guaranteed badge without spending millions. I've seen local pest control companies out-close Terminix in Denver and Austin because customers hate waiting on hold and scheduling for next week.

**Q: Should I use broad match keywords now that Google says they're "smart"?**

No. Google's broad match has gotten better at understanding intent, but for pest control — where "roach exterminator" and "DIY roach spray" are very different intents — broad match still wastes money. I tested broad match on a client's account in Nashville for 30 days. 37% of clicks went to irrelevant searches including "pest control training" and "bug spray Walmart." Use phrase match and exact match. Monitor your search terms report weekly. Add negatives like a nervous chef adds salt.

**Q: Can I generate leads without running search ads — just using YouTube or display ads?**

Technically yes. Realistically no for most pest control businesses. YouTube and display ads work for building brand awareness over months. Pest control is an urgent need. Someone with termites doesn't watch a YouTube preroll and think "I should call that company." They search "termite treatment near me." Search ads capture that intent. Display and YouTube are for retargeting — showing an ad to someone who visited your site but didn't call. That's a valid tactic but only after your search campaign is profitable.

**Q: How do I know which keywords are actually making me money?**

Set up conversion tracking that tracks calls and form submissions as separate conversion actions. Then use Google Ads' "conversions" column sorted by keyword. I connect Google Ads to a tracking tool like CallRail or just use Google's built-in call reporting with a forwarding number. If a keyword has high clicks and low conversions after 30 days and 50+ clicks, pause it. If a keyword has a 10% conversion rate, increase its bid. Data, not gut feelings.

**Q: What's the biggest waste of money you see pest control businesses make on Google Ads?**

Running ads without conversion tracking. I see it constantly. Business owners spend $1,500-3,000/month and have zero idea which keywords, ads, or audiences generate revenue. They rely on Google's default reporting which counts any click as a "success." I worked with a company in Columbus, Ohio that thought they were getting 50 leads per month from Google Ads. After setting up proper tracking, it was 14 real leads — the other 36 were accidental clicks, misdials, and people who hung up before the first ring. Set up tracking before you spend a dollar.
