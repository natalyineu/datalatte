Digital billboards, transit screens at bus stops and subway stations, screens in gyms and retail stores, digital signage at airports and gas stations — all of this out-of-home inventory can now be bought programmatically, in real time, targeted to specific audiences, and measured for effectiveness.

This is **Digital Out-of-Home (DOOH) advertising**, and Vistar Media is the platform that made it accessible beyond the traditional billboard buying world.

For local businesses, programmatic DOOH opens possibilities that traditional billboard advertising never offered: the ability to run campaigns that only activate when your target audience is nearby, to pause ads during rainy weather (if you're a patio restaurant), or to show different creative by time of day.

## What Vistar Media Actually Is

Vistar Media is a **DOOH demand-side platform (DSP)** — a technology platform that connects advertisers to digital out-of-home screen inventory from hundreds of publishers (billboard owners, transit systems, retail screen networks, gym screen networks) across the US.

Instead of calling Clear Channel to negotiate a specific billboard for a specific period of time, Vistar lets you define your audience and geographic parameters, set a budget, and serve ads across any qualifying screens — automatically, in real time.

**Screen types available through Vistar:**
- Roadside digital billboards (traditional billboard format, digitised)
- Transit screens (bus shelters, subway stations, train platforms)
- Retail media screens (gas stations, convenience stores, grocery checkout)
- Fitness and wellness venue screens (gym lobbies, studio waiting areas)
- Restaurant and QSR screens
- Office building lobbies and elevators
- Airport and travel venues

This combination creates reach across the physical environment at a targeting sophistication previously impossible in outdoor advertising.

## What Makes Programmatic DOOH Different from Traditional Billboard Buying

Traditional outdoor advertising: Call a billboard company, pick a specific location, pay for 4-week exclusive ownership, get one creative. No targeting, no flexibility, limited measurement.

Programmatic DOOH through Vistar: Define your audience, set geographic triggers, run for as long as you choose, change creative dynamically, measure reach and frequency across all screens. Your ad shares the screen loop with other advertisers (typically 4–8 ads per loop), so your "ownership" is a share of impressions across many screens, not exclusive control of one.

**Practical implications:**
- You don't buy a specific screen — you buy audiences and geographic contexts
- Your ad appears on any qualifying screen in your target area
- Minimum buys are much lower than traditional outdoor ($500–2,000 vs. $5,000+ for a single billboard)
- Creative changes can be made during the campaign without reprinting
- Weather, time of day, and event triggers can dynamically activate or pause your ads

<Callout type="tip">
Programmatic DOOH is particularly powerful for day-parting — showing your coffee shop ad only on morning commute screens (6–10am), or your restaurant ad only at lunch and dinner hours (11am–1pm, 5–8pm). Traditional billboard buying doesn't allow this time flexibility.
</Callout>

## DOOH Targeting Capabilities

Vistar's targeting goes beyond simple geography:

**Location-based:** Radius targeting around your business, specific neighbourhoods, transit corridors, competitor locations.

**Audience-based:** Mobile device data shows which demographic audiences frequent certain screen locations. You can target screens frequented by your ideal customer segment.

**Contextual environment:** Choose screen types that match your audience. A gym equipment retailer targeting gym lobby screens. A coffee brand targeting office building elevator screens at 8am. A pet groomer targeting pet store digital displays.

**Weather triggers:** Activate ads only when weather conditions are relevant. A rainwear shop runs ads when rain is forecast. A patio restaurant ads pause when temperatures drop below 60°F.

**Event triggers:** Ads activate near specific events — sports games, concerts, festivals — when foot traffic spikes.

**Proximity to your competitors:** Target screens near your competitors' physical locations to reach their customers.

**1. Through a programmatic advertising agency**
Most efficient. An agency with Vistar DSP access can execute local DOOH campaigns with a minimum of $2,000–5,000 per campaign. They handle creative specs, campaign setup, and reporting.

**2. Through StackAdapt, The Trade Desk, or similar DSPs**

**4. Place Exchange, Broadsign, or similar alternative platforms**
Vistar is the market leader but not the only DOOH DSP. Place Exchange and Broadsign offer competitive alternatives with slightly different screen network compositions.

## Creative Specifications for DOOH

DOOH creative is a static image or short video displayed on outdoor screens. Key requirements:

- **Format:** Static image (JPEG, PNG) or video (MP4, maximum 15 seconds)
- **Resolution:** Varies by screen type — typically 1920×1080 for standard digital billboards, other ratios for transit and retail screens
- **Text:** Keep to minimum — viewers have 1–3 seconds of attention. One headline, one visual, one CTA
- **No audio:** DOOH screens rarely have speakers (except some indoor placements)
- **Bold, high-contrast visuals:** Must be readable at distance and in varying light conditions

The most common local business DOOH mistake: treating a billboard like a print ad with lots of text and detail. On a roadside screen at 50mph, you have 2 seconds of attention. One image, your logo, five words.

<Callout type="warning">
DOOH creative must be simple. "Beautiful coffee, 2 blocks ahead. [Logo]" is a better billboard ad than a complex layout with your address, hours, menu highlights, and phone number. Save the details for digital ads where the viewer has time to engage. DOOH plants the seed — your Google ad or store sign closes the deal.
</Callout>

## DOOH Measurement for Local Business

Measuring DOOH effectiveness is improving but still less precise than digital channels:

**Footfall attribution:** Location intelligence providers (Foursquare, NinthDecimal) measure device presence near DOOH screens and correlate with store visits. Available but requires sufficient campaign scale.

**Mobile retargeting:** Devices that were near DOOH screen exposures can be retargeted with mobile ads — and the conversion rate from that retargeting pool indicates DOOH's awareness impact.

**Brand search lift:** Track Google searches for your business name during and after DOOH flights.

**Promo codes:** "Mention this ad for 10% off" — simple but effective for in-store attribution.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the realistic minimum budget to start with DOOH for a local business?

Through a managed service or agency, $2,000–5,000 per campaign is a practical minimum to get meaningful reach in a local market. Self-serve access through DSPs requires existing platform relationships. Below $2,000, the reach is generally too thin to make assessment meaningful.

### How long should a DOOH campaign run?

Minimum 2–4 weeks for a local business — shorter than that doesn't generate enough frequency to build brand recall. 4–8 weeks is the typical campaign length for local awareness campaigns.

### Can I specifically target screens near my competitor's business?

Yes — this is possible and commonly used. Geographic targeting can be set to a small radius (even 500 meters) around a specific address. This lets you serve ads on screens visible to people at or near a competitor's location.

### Is DOOH better than traditional print billboard advertising for a local business?

For flexibility, targeting, and measurement: yes. For sheer visual dominance of a single high-traffic location: a premium static billboard in the right spot can outperform programmatic DOOH. The ideal is both — use programmatic DOOH for audience-targeted efficiency, and a premium static placement for flagship visibility at your most important location.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Buying DOOH Like a Traditional Billboard

*Where it went wrong:* A BBQ spot in Austin, Texas, called Smokey's Pit — they'd been advertising on a static billboard on I-35 for three years. $1,800/month for one fixed location. When they heard about DOOH, the owner figured he'd do the same thing but digitally. He told the Vistar rep to run his ad "on all digital billboards within 10 miles of downtown" Monday through Sunday, 6am to 10pm.

*The actual outcome:* $1,200 spent in month one. The ads ran everywhere — including screens near office parks during lunch, screens in gyms at 7pm, and transit shelters in neighborhoods where nobody was driving to a BBQ joint. His cost per foot-traffic (measured via a QR code offer) came out to roughly $47 per person who walked in. That's worse than buying a radio spot in 1998.

*The fix:* We pulled back the targeting to only show ads within a 1.5-mile radius of his location, between 11am and 2pm on weekdays, and 10am to 8pm on weekends. We added a weather trigger — no ads when it rained (patio-dependent business). We also segmented creative: "Friday Lunch Special" on transit screens near offices, "Saturday Family Platter" on roadside billboards.

*The result:* Month two, same $1,200 budget, but foot traffic attributed to the campaign jumped to 93 scanned offers. Cost per visitor dropped to under $13. The incremental revenue from those 93 people? Roughly $3,800 in average ticket size. The owner's quote: "I feel like an idiot for not doing this sooner."

### Mistake 2: Running Static Creative on a Dynamic Medium

*Where it went wrong:* A yoga studio in Portland, Oregon — Align & Flow — spent $800 on a DOOH campaign for their new "Hot Workshop" series. They uploaded a single JPEG: text-heavy, small font, no call to action except "visit our website." It ran on transit shelters and gym screens across the Pearl District for three weeks.

*The actual outcome:* Zero attributable sign-ups. The studio owner tracked using a custom URL (alignflow.com/hotworkshop) and a promo code "HOT25" for their booking system (Booksy). Neither produced a single conversion. The owner told me, "I thought the screen would do the work."

*The fix:* DOOH screens average about 8–12 seconds of view time. You cannot run a print ad on a digital screen and expect results. We created three variations:
- Version A: 4-second loop — instructor photo, "Hot Workshop — New Beginnings," Booksy QR code
- Version B: 6-second loop — class scene, "7 Days Free," tap-to-call button
- Version C: 2-second teaser rotating with time-based offers — "Sunrise Flow @6am" shown mornings, "Evening Unwind @7pm" shown afternoons

*The result:* Month two on the same $800 budget, the studio saw 44 QR code scans and 17 actual bookings via Booksy. Average class fee is $22 — that's $374 in direct-attributed revenue. Not huge, but the cost per acquisition dropped from "infinite" to $47, and the studio ran it again the next month. The owner now updates creative every two weeks.

### Mistake 3: Not Integrating DOOH With Everything Else You're Already Doing

*Where it went wrong:* A pet grooming business in Nashville — Paws & Suds — ran a DOOH campaign targeting dog owners near five popular dog parks. $600/month on transit screens and park-adjacent digital billboards. The ad was solid: "First groom 20% off — use code PAWS20." They got 23 code redemptions in month one.

*The actual outcome:* That's a decent start. But when I asked what they were doing on Google Ads and Yelp during those same three weeks, the owner said "nothing different." They hadn't paused their broad-match Google Ads campaign that was pulling in clicks from people searching "cheap cat grooming" 30 miles away. They also hadn't updated their Yelp listing to mention the DOOH promo. So 60% of the people who saw the digital billboard and searched their business name on Google were greeted by a generic Yelp page and a competing ad showing "same-day booking" for a groomer two blocks away.

*The fix:* We synchronized three things:
1. Google Ads — paused all non-essential keywords, added "Paws20" to the ad copy, bid up on "Nashville dog grooming [neighborhood]" keywords during DOOH flight times
2. Yelp — added the promo offer, updated photos to match the DOOH creative, responded to recent reviews
3. Square (their booking system) — created a specific "DOOH-Dog" service category so they could track redemptions separately from walk-ins

*The result:* Month two, same $600 DOOH spend, but code redemptions rose to 67. Total attributed revenue: roughly $3,350 in grooming services. Google Ads spend actually dropped 22% because they stopped paying for irrelevant clicks. The owner's feedback: "I was basically fighting myself before."

### Mistake 4: Budgeting Like It's Facebook Ads

*Where it went wrong:* A coffee roastery in Denver — High Altitude Brew — wanted to test DOOH for their new downtown café. They set a $300/month budget on Vistar, targeting screens within a 0.5-mile radius of the shop. They expected it to work like Facebook Ads where $300 buys you measurable reach and frequency.

*The actual outcome:* The campaign barely ran. Most DOOH inventory has a CPM floor of $10–$25 depending on screen type and location. At $300/month, their budget bought roughly 12,000–30,000 impressions — but with frequency caps and minimum daily spend requirements, the system couldn't actually compete for premium inventory. Their ads appeared on 14 screens across the month, but the median person saw the ad maybe 1.2 times. Nobody remembers a digital billboard they saw once while driving 35mph.

*The fix:* We consolidated the budget. Instead of spreading $300 across every screen type, we picked one: the digital screens inside six downtown coffee shops (a network called IndoorDirect). CPM was $18, minimum daily spend $10. At $300/month, that bought 16,667 impressions across six screens, with an average frequency of 4.8 per person. We ran the creative for two weeks, then paused, then ran again.

*The result:* $300 spent across two weeks of concentrated frequency. The café tracked foot traffic via a "mention the billboard" prompt at the register. In the two weeks, 22 people mentioned the ad. Average spend per customer: $7.50 for a latte and pastry. That's $165 in direct-attributed revenue — not a positive ROAS yet, but the lifetime value of coffee shop customers (3x/week visits) changes the math. They increased the budget to $600/month. By month three, regulars from the campaign were spending $40–$60/month each.

## Integrating DOOH With Your Other Advertising Channels

DOOH works best when it's not the only thing you're doing. If you treat it as an island, you're leaving money on the floor. Here's how to connect it to the tools you probably already use.

**Google Ads and location extensions.** When someone sees your digital billboard and searches your business name on their phone, their first result should be a Google Business Profile with a clear offer and directions. Set up a location extension. Run a parallel search campaign targeting "near me" queries in the same geographic footprint as your DOOH flight. At a friend's salon in Chicago, they ran DOOH for "Eyebrow threading — $12" on transit screens near three train stops. Simultaneously, they boosted their Google Ads bid for "eyebrow threading Chicago [station name]" by 30% during commute hours. The DOOH ad made people aware; the Google ad caught them when they searched. Combined cost per booking: $9. Separately, it was $18 on Google alone and unmeasurable on DOOH alone.

**Social media retargeting.** Most people who see your billboard won't act immediately. But platforms like Meta and TikTok let you upload location data from Vistar's audience segments (people who passed through a geo-fenced area where your ad ran) and retarget them on their phones. A bike shop in Portland ran a DOOH campaign on screens near the Springwater Corridor trail. They exported the audience segment, uploaded it to Meta, and ran a $200 retargeting campaign for "show this ad to see our Labor Day sale." The retargeting campaign generated $2,200 in bike sales directly attributed to Meta conversions. Was that the DOOH or the Facebook ad? Honestly, it was the combination — the billboard built recognition, the Facebook ad closed the sale.

**Email and SMS with Mailchimp.** If you have a customer list, segment it by ZIP code and send a message before your DOOH flight starts: "If you see our ad on the bus shelter near 5th and Main, show us a screenshot for a free drink." A bagel shop in NYC did this. They got 19 screenshots in the first week. That's 19 people who were looking for the ad. Those 19 people became regulars. One of them worked two blocks away and had never walked in before despite passing the shop daily — the ad made her feel like she was "in on something."

**Yelp and Booksy integration.** If you're a service business (salon, groomer, fitness), your DOOH ad should direct people to a specific booking offer via Yelp or Booksy, not a generic website. A barber in Nashville used a unique Booksy link for each DOOH creative version. Version A (transit screens) linked to a $10 haircut offer. Version B (gym screens) linked to a beard trim + shampoo. They tracked which version drove more bookings. The transit screens won 3:1. That data told them to shift 70% of budget to transit for the next month.

## Measuring DOOH: What Actually Matters for a Local Business

Most DOOH platforms will happily send you a report with "impressions delivered" and "reach" and "frequency." These numbers are useful in the same way that knowing the temperature in Dubai is useful when you're standing in a snowstorm — technically true, irrelevant to your decision.

Here's what I actually track for small businesses.

**Foot traffic lift via WiFi or Bluetooth data.** If your business uses a Square Terminal or a Toast POS, you can set up a simple WiFi captive portal that asks for an email before connecting. Cross-reference the devices that connected during your DOOH flight against the geographic zones your ads served. A cafe in Austin did this. They saw a 14% increase in new device connections during campaign weeks compared to the four weeks prior. That's not foolproof, but it's directional.

**Unique QR code scans.** Vistar supports QR codes on digital screens. Use a different code for each creative variation and each screen type. A furniture store in Denver ran five codes: one for transit shelters, one for gym screens, one for roadside billboards, one for airport screens, one for "other." They tracked 312 total scans. Transit shelters drove 47% of scans. Airport screens drove 8%. They killed the airport spend and doubled down on transit.

**Promo code redemption.** This is the most reliable metric. Use a unique code for DOOH only. Don't use the same code you put on your website or your Instagram bio. A pizza place in Portland used the code "BILLBOARD20" for a month. They got 44 redemptions. Average order value with the code: $24. That's $1,056 in direct-attributed revenue against a $500 DOOH spend. Every penny of that spend was justified.

**Customer survey.** This feels low-tech, but it works. For two weeks, ask every customer: "How did you hear about us?" and track responses manually. A pet groomer in Chicago did this. Over 14 days, 317 customers answered. 41 said "the digital sign at the bus stop." That's 13% attribution. Her total revenue for those two weeks was $6,400. If 13% is correct, DOOH contributed $832 against a $400 spend. Positive ROAS by a comfortable margin.

**Cost per acquisition (CPA).** Take your total DOOH spend (including creative production and any retargeting costs). Divide by the number of tracked conversions. If your CPA is below your average customer lifetime value, the campaign is working. If it's above, adjust targeting or creative. There is no universal "good" CPA. For a coffee shop with a $5 average ticket and 3x weekly visits, a $25 CPA is fine. For a furniture store with a $2,000 average sale, a $200 CPA is fine.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I really target specific people with a billboard? Isn't that impossible?**

Not in the way you're thinking. You can't say "show this ad only to Sarah who works at 123 Main St." But you can target geographic zones, time of day, weather conditions, and even audience segments based on mobile device data (people who recently visited a competitor, people who live in a certain income bracket, people who frequently visit gyms). Vistar also offers "foot traffic attribution" that correlates ad exposure with visits to your location. It's not deterministic — it's probabilistic — but it's accurate enough to make budget decisions.

**Q: What's the minimum spend to make this worth my time?**

For a local business testing DOOH, I'd recommend at least $500–$1,000 per month. Below that, you won't achieve sufficient frequency for people to remember your ad. One-off $200 tests rarely work because the system can't optimize with that little data. Run the same budget for at least two months. Month one is data collection. Month two is optimization.

**Q: How do I know if anyone actually saw my ad?**

You'll never know exactly who looked at the screen. But you can measure downstream actions: QR code scans, promo code redemptions, foot traffic lift, website visits from the geographic area during the ad flight. Vistar and other DSPs also offer "visual attention" metrics that estimate how many people looked at the screen based on camera data from the publisher side — but this is still a surrogate for true attention. Use it as a directional signal, not a guarantee.

**Q: Can I run an ad on just one specific billboard I like?**

Not through a programmatic platform like Vistar. Programmatic DOOH is designed for networks, not single placements. If you want one specific billboard, you need to call the owner directly and negotiate a static or digital buy. Programmatic DOOH works best when you're okay with your ad appearing across multiple screens in a defined area. You trade exact placement control for scale and efficiency.

**Q: What happens if a screen is broken or the ad doesn't display?**

DOOH networks have automated monitoring. If a screen goes dark or shows a static image, the publisher typically doesn't charge for those impressions. Vistar's platform also provides verification reports showing exactly which screens displayed your creative, for how long, and whether there were any technical issues. In my experience, on a well-managed network, 95%+ delivery is the norm. You can request a make-good if delivery falls below 90%.

**Q: Why should I use DOOH instead of just putting that money into more Google Ads?**

Because they solve different problems. Google Ads catches people who are already searching for what you offer. DOOH creates awareness among people who didn't know you existed. If you've maxed out your Google Ads ROI — meaning you're bidding on every relevant keyword and still have budget left — DOOH is a logical next step. If your Google Ads campaigns are running at a 2x ROAS, fix those first. DOOH is an expansion channel, not a replacement.
