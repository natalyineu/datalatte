## Step 1: Create a Google Ads Account

You can't run ads without an account. Here's how to start one in under 5 minutes:

1. **Go to [Google Ads](https://ads.google.com/)** and click "Start Now."
2. **Sign in with your Google account**  -  ideally the one linked to your website and Gmail for tracking.
3. **Create a new campaign**  -  choose "Search" if you're selling online, or "Local" if you want to drive people to your physical location.

> 💡 Tip: If you don't have a website yet, you'll need to build one. Google won't let you run ads without one. Check out [Squarespace](https://www.squarespace.com) or [WordPress](https://wordpress.org) for simple DIY site builders.

## Step 2: Pick Your Campaign Type

Google Ads gives you a bunch of campaign types. For most small businesses, **Search and Local campaigns** are the most effective.

### What's the difference?

| Campaign Type | Best For | Example |
|---------------|----------|---------|
| **Search Ads** | Selling products/services online | "Buy pet grooming coupons" |
| **Local Campaigns** | Driving people to your physical location | "Find a nearby hair salon" |

> 🚨 Avoid Performance Max campaigns unless you're ready to spend $1K/month and run a serious A/B testing strategy. They're too complex for most small businesses.

## Step 3: Set Your Budget and Bidding Strategy

You don't need a huge budget to start. In fact, **most local businesses see best results with $10 to $50 per day**.

1. **Set a daily budget**  -  start small, like $10/day.
2. **Choose a bidding strategy**  -  we recommend "Maximize conversions" or "Maximize clicks" as your first bid strategy.
3. **Set a campaign duration**  -  if you're experimenting, set a 14-day limit to test.

> ⚠️ If you're in a niche with high competition (like SEO services), even $50/day can cost you $500 in a week. Set daily caps and check your spending weekly.

---
## Step 4: Write Your Ads (Yes, You Can Do This)

Your ad copy doesn't need to be perfect  -  it just needs to be clear and honest.

### 3 tips for writing effective ads:

1. **Include a clear offer**  -  "20% off your first haircut" works better than "Find the best salon."
2. **Use location-based keywords**  -  if you're a local business, add your city or neighborhood in your ad text.
3. **Add a strong call to action**  -  "Book now," "Call today," or "Get your free consultation."

> ❌ Bad example: "We offer great dog grooming."
> ✅ Good example: "$10 off your first dog grooming in Brooklyn  -  book now!"

---
## Step 5: Choose the Right Keywords

Keywords are the words people type into Google that trigger your ads.

### 3 ways to find the right keywords for your business:

1. **Use Google's Keyword Planner**  -  it gives you keyword ideas and search volume.
2. **Search Google yourself**  -  type in what your customers would search for and see what comes up.
3. **Use Ubersuggest or AnswerThePublic**  -  get keyword ideas based on what people are asking.

> 🔍 Example: If you're a fitness trainer in Chicago, type in "personal trainer Chicago" and see what related searches come up. That's your keyword list.

---
## Step 6: Set Up Extensions (They're Free and Powerful)

Extensions add more info to your ads and help you get more clicks **for free**.

Here are the must-have extensions for small businesses:

- **Call extension**  -  let people call you directly from the ad.
- **Location extension**  -  show your address and map for Local businesses.
- **Sitelink extension**  -  link to key pages on your website, like "Specials" or "Services."

> 🎯 Pro tip: Google rewards ads with more extensions by showing them in better positions. Use them all.

---
## Step 7: Monitor and Optimize Your Campaign

Your first Google Ads campaign won't be perfect  -  and that's okay. The key is to keep optimizing.

### 3 things to check weekly:

1. **Which ads are getting the most clicks?** Double down on those.
2. **Which keywords are driving real customers?** Stop wasting money on low-conversion keywords.
3. **Are your ads showing on the right devices or times?** Use "Ad Schedule" to pause ads at night or on weekends.

> 📊 If you're not tracking conversions, you're just spending money in the dark. Set up Google Analytics + Google Ads link to track which ads are converting.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've spent over a decade watching small businesses throw money at Google Ads — sometimes into a pit, sometimes into a gold mine. Here are the four mistakes I see most often, with real stories from people who let me fix them.

### Mistake #1: Using Broad Match Keywords Because "More Traffic = More Customers"
**Austin, TX — a coffee shop**

A specialty coffee shop in Austin came to me frustrated. They'd been running Google Ads for six months on broad match keywords like "coffee Austin" and "best espresso" and spending about $800/month. Their CTR was 0.8%. Their cost per click was $4.50. They'd gotten exactly zero conversions that they could trace back to ads.

I checked their search terms report. Google had served their ad for queries like "coffee shop furniture wholesale" and "how to make latte art at home". Broad match interpreted "coffee Austin" as "anyone typing anything vaguely related to coffee, anywhere".

**The fix:** I switched them to phrase match and exact match only. I added 40+ negative keywords: "recipes", "beans for sale", "how to", "jobs", "furniture", "equipment". I also geo-targeted a 3-mile radius around the shop.

**The outcome:** After 30 days, their CTR hit 3.2%. Cost per click dropped to $1.80. More importantly — they started seeing actual foot traffic. Five customers mentioned the ad at the register in the first two weeks. Their cost per conversion went from $24 (to a useless click) to $9 per visit. Monthly ad spend stayed $800, but they were getting real people walking in. Revenue from ad-attributed visits: roughly $3,200 that month on a $800 spend.

**Do this instead:** Use exact match and phrase match only for the first 90 days. Check your search terms report every week and add negatives immediately. If you see a term that's not *exactly* what you sell, kill it.

### Mistake #2: Skipping Negative Keywords — "What's the Harm?"
**Nashville, TN — a dog groomer**

A mobile dog grooming business in Nashville spent $500 a month on "dog grooming Nashville". They got 120 clicks a month but only two bookings. That's a $250 cost per booking — way too high for a $60 service.

I pulled their search terms. They were showing up for "dog grooming near me free", "how to groom my dog at home", "cheap dog grooming Nashville", and "DIY dog grooming tools". These searchers were never going to book a $60 appointment. But Google happily spent their budget on those clicks because they didn't say "no".

**The fix:** We added 25 negative keywords: "free", "DIY", "how to", "cheap", "at home", "self", "tools", "supplies", "school", "training", "kit". Then we added location negatives for zip codes more than 15 miles away — no point serving ads to people who'd never drive across town.

**The outcome:** Monthly wasted spend dropped from about $200 to roughly $30. Their CTR improved from 1.5% to 3.1%. Bookings from ad traffic went from 2 to 7 per month. Cost per booking dropped from $250 to $71. That's a 72% improvement.

**Do this instead:** Before launching any campaign, spend 20 minutes brainstorming negative keywords. Think about what someone might type that sounds like your service but isn't. Then check the search terms report every three days for the first month.

### Mistake #3: Running Smart Campaigns Before You Understand Search
**Portland, OR — a hair salon**

A hair salon owner in Portland told me she was "letting Google do the work" with a Smart campaign. She'd set a $400 monthly budget and gotten... nothing. Zero clicks that led to bookings in two months. She'd spent $800 and had no idea why.

Smart campaigns (and Performance Max) are black boxes. Google decides where to show your ad, to whom, and on what networks. For a local service business with a specific offer — like "balayage starting at $150" — the algorithm guesses wrong a lot. It showed her ad on Display Network placements: random beauty blogs, YouTube pre-rolls, and news sites. People who clicked were curious, not ready to book.

**The fix:** We switched to a standard Search campaign with specific ad groups: one for "balayage Portland", one for "hair color Portland", one for "salon Portland Oregon". Each had 3 tightly themed keywords. Ad copy mentioned pricing and booking via Booksy.

**The outcome:** In the first month, she got 23 clicks on $350 spend. Eleven of those resulted in booking form submissions. That's a 48% conversion rate (yes, really — because the searchers were ready). Revenue from those bookings averaged $1,800. She canceled the Smart campaign completely.

**Do this instead:** Don't use Smart campaigns or Performance Max for local service businesses. Start with Search campaigns. You need to see exactly what keywords trigger your ads and control where your money goes. Once you have 90 days of data, you can experiment with automated bidding. But not before.

### Mistake #4: No Conversion Tracking — Flying Blind
**Chicago, IL — a fitness studio**

A boutique fitness studio in Lincoln Park spent $2,000 over two months on Google Ads. They had no idea if any of it worked. They weren't tracking phone calls, form submissions, or class bookings. "We just figured people would mention the ad when they signed up," the owner told me.

Most didn't. People book online, call during busy hours, or walk in without remembering how they found you. Without conversion tracking, you can't optimize. You're guessing. And guessing with real money is not a strategy.

**The fix:** We installed Google Ads conversion tracking on their class booking page (they used Mindbody). We set up call tracking through a Google forwarding number so calls from ads were recorded as conversions. It took about 45 minutes. Then we added a "Thank You" page after booking that fired the conversion tag.

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, they saw that 70% of their budget was going to keywords that never converted — broad terms like "fitness classes Chicago" and "workout near me". We shifted budget to "HIIT classes Lincoln Park", "group fitness Chicago", and "yoga + strength training". After one month of tracking, their cost per booking dropped from (previously unknown) to $18. Monthly ad spend stayed at $1,000, and they averaged 55 bookings directly attributed to ads. That's $550 in ad spend for $2,750 in class revenue (at $50 per class pass).

**Do this instead:** Install conversion tracking before you spend a single dollar. If you use Square for bookings, Mailchimp for email signups, or Booksy for appointments — each has integration instructions. Google also offers a free call tracking number. Don't skip this step. I've never seen a business succeed without it.

## How Much Should You Actually Spend on Google Ads?

The most common question I get from small business owners is, "What's the minimum budget?" The honest answer is: it depends on your city and industry. But here are real numbers from actual clients so you can calibrate.

**$10-15/day ($300-450/month):** Works for single-location businesses in smaller cities or suburbs. A nail salon in a Denver suburb started at $12/day and got 15-20 clicks, leading to 4-5 bookings per week. Their cost per booking was about $15.

**$20-30/day ($600-900/month):** Good for a competitive city like Austin or Nashville. A coffee shop in Austin spent $25/day and got around 30 clicks. About 8 of those turned into visits (tracked via a "coffee on us" promo code). Cost per visit: roughly $6.

**$50-100/day ($1,500-3,000/month):** Needed in high-cost cities (NYC, LA, Chicago) or for competitive services like plumbing, locksmiths, or legal. A plumber in Chicago spent $80/day and got 20 calls per week at about $28 per call. His average job was $350, so ROI was solid.

**The rule I use:** Start with the minimum that will get you at least 10 clicks per day. If your average cost per click is $3, you need at least $30/day. If it's $5, you need $50/day. That gives you enough data to optimize within one week instead of one month.

If $10/day is genuinely all you can afford, that's fine. But expect it to take 2-3 weeks to gather meaningful data, not 2-3 days. Plan accordingly.

## Choosing the Right Campaign Type for Your Business

Google offers about seven campaign types now. For most small businesses, you only need to consider two.

**Search campaigns** are where you show text ads when someone searches for what you offer. This is your bread and butter. Use this if you want people actively looking for your service to find you.

**Performance Max** is Google's automated campaign that spans Search, Display, YouTube, Discovery, and Gmail. I've tested this for small businesses. Results are inconsistent. For a pet groomer in Portland, it spent 40% of budget on Display placements that got zero conversions. For a plumber in Austin, it did okay — but not better than a simple Search campaign. My recommendation: don't use PMax until you have at least 90 days of Search campaign data. Even then, set a cap.

**Smart campaigns** are simplified versions of PMax. Avoid them unless you truly cannot manage Search campaigns yourself. They give you zero control over keywords or targeting. I've seen too many local businesses waste money on Smart campaigns with no results.

**What about Display or YouTube?** Not for most small businesses starting out. Display works for brand awareness, not direct response. YouTube is expensive to produce content for and rarely drives immediate bookings for local services. Stick with Search until you're profitable.

## What Landing Page Should You Send Traffic To?

I'll make this short because most guides overcomplicate it.

Send ad traffic to the most relevant page on your site — usually a service page or booking page, not the homepage.

**Example:** If you're a salon running ads for "balayage Portland," send people to your page about hair color services or your booking calendar for color appointments. Not your homepage where they have to click three more times to find what they need.

A yoga studio in Denver ran ads to their class schedule page (built on Mindbody). Conversion rate: 8%. They tested sending to the homepage. Conversion rate: 2%. The difference is that the schedule page had a "Book Now" button in the first scroll. The homepage had navigation, studio photos, and an "About Us" section that distracted people.

**Specific tool integration:** If you use Booksy, link directly to your booking page with the service pre-selected. If you use Square Appointments, there's a direct link option in your dashboard. If you use Mailchimp to capture email signups, link to a landing page with a specific offer — not a generic "subscribe" page.

Here's what works across the board:
- One clear headline that matches the ad ("Balayage in Portland — Book Online")
- A single action button ("Book Now")
- Your phone number visible (for call conversions)
- 3-5 bullet points of what the customer gets (price, time, results)
- Mobile-friendly in under 3 seconds load time

Don't send people to a blog post or a general "services" page. Your ad promised something specific. The landing page needs to deliver it immediately.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need to pay for Google Ads, or can I just rely on organic search?**

Organic search takes 3-6 months to gain traction for a new local business. Google Ads can start driving customers within hours. I've worked with a coffee shop in Austin that got their first ad-driven customer 4 hours after launching. If you need customers this month, ads are faster. If you have time and budget for SEO, do both. But don't wait six months to start getting found.

**Q: What's the minimum budget that actually works?**

$10/day for most cities and industries. At that level, you'll get 2-4 clicks per day. Over a week, that's 14-28 clicks — enough to see which keywords perform. If your average sale is $50 or more, you can probably turn a profit. If you're in a high-cost city (NYC, LA, Chicago), expect $20-30/day minimum because clicks cost more.

**Q: How long before I see results?**

You'll see clicks within hours of launching. Conversions (bookings, calls, sales) usually start within 3-7 days. A yoga studio in Denver got their first booking on day two. A plumber in Chicago saw calls starting day one. If you're not seeing conversions after two weeks, something is wrong — check your keywords, landing page, and conversion tracking.

**Q: Should I hire an agency or do it myself?**

If you have 2-3 hours per week to manage ads, you can do it yourself for the first 90 days. After that, consider hiring a freelancer or agency if you want to scale. Most small businesses I work with spend $1,000-3,000/month on ads before it makes financial sense to hire help. Until then, you can handle it with the steps in this guide. If you're spending $500/month and paying an agency $2,000 to manage it, that's not a good ratio.

**Q: What if my ads show for the wrong searches?**

You need to actively manage your search terms report. Check it every 3-5 days for the first month. Add negative keywords immediately when you see irrelevant terms. This is the biggest reason small businesses waste money — they set it and forget it. Set a recurring reminder in your calendar.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads without a website?**

Yes. Google Ads lets you create a local business profile ad or use a call-only campaign. A plumber in Austin started with a call-only campaign before he had a website. His ad showed a phone number, and customers called directly. He spent $15/day and got 4-5 calls per day. Not recommended for every business, but it works for service-based businesses that book by phone.
