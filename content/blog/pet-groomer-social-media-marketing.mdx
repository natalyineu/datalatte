## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Posting Randomly and Hoping for the Best

I worked with a groomer in Austin, Texas — let's call her shop "Paws & Relax." She had a solid client base from walk-ins, but she wanted to grow. So she threw up a Facebook page, posted a photo whenever she remembered (about twice a month), and waited. After six months, she had 47 followers — most of them her cousins.

The problem wasn't effort. She was working 50 hours a week on dogs. The problem was that social media algorithms punish erratic posting. Facebook and Instagram interpret a sporadic schedule as "this account isn't active" and stop showing your content to anyone. Her posts were reaching three or four people.

**The fix wasn't complicated.** We set up a schedule: three posts per week, every week, using a free tool like Later or Buffer. She pre-shot photos on Monday mornings (takes 15 minutes) and scheduled them in batches. We also added two Stories per day — a quick clip of a dog getting brushed or a before-and-after. Nothing fancy.

**The outcome:** Within eight weeks, her reach jumped from 47 followers to 340. More importantly, she started getting DMs from people asking about appointments. She tracked the numbers: those DMs turned into 22 new clients over three months, generating roughly $3,800 in additional revenue. That's from 15 minutes of photo-taking and a scheduling tool that costs nothing.

### Mistake 2: Treating Social Media Like a Billboard

A groomer in Nashville — "The Dapper Dog" — had a different problem. He posted consistently, and his photos were beautiful. Every post was a perfectly lit, professionally framed shot of a freshly groomed poodle. Looked like a magazine spread.

But nobody was engaging. No comments, no shares. He'd get maybe three likes.

**Here's what was actually happening:** People scroll past perfect photos the same way they ignore billboards on a highway. There's no reason to stop. His posts were advertising, not content. They didn't start conversations, they didn't tell stories, and they didn't make anyone feel anything.

**The fix:** We changed his content mix. Instead of 100% polished before-and-after shots, we introduced three other types of posts:
- **Behind-the-scenes videos** — a 30-second clip of a golden retriever being patient through a bath, set to music
- **Educational content** — "Why we don't shave double-coated breeds in summer (and what to do instead)"
- **Customer stories** — "This is Max. His owner noticed a lump on his neck during our groom. We recommended a vet visit. Turns out it was early-stage cancer. Max is fine now. Always check your dog."

Within a month, his engagement rate went from 1.2% to 4.8%. People started tagging their friends. He got 17 new client inquiries from Instagram alone in that first month. Estimated revenue from those inquiries: around $2,400.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Local Search on Social Platforms

A groomer in Portland, Oregon — "Paws of Glory" — had great content but was invisible to anyone not already following her. She never used location tags. Never put her address in her bio. Never uploaded her services list to her Facebook Shop.

So when someone in Portland searched "dog groomer near me" on Instagram or Facebook, she didn't show up. Instead, a competitor who had filled out every field in their business profile — address, hours, services, photos of the storefront — appeared first.

**The fix took one hour:** We filled out the complete Google Business Profile, added location tags to every post, and turned on the "get directions" button on Instagram. We also added her services as product listings on Facebook (cost: $0).

**The outcome:** In three months, her Instagram profile visits from location-based searches increased by 180%. She started getting calls from people who said "I found you on Instagram when I searched for groomers near me." Those calls converted to 34 new client bookings, worth approximately $4,600 in total revenue.

### Mistake 4: Trying to Be on Every Platform (And Doing None Well)

A groomer in Denver told me she was on Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, Pinterest, Nextdoor, and Yelp — and "none of it works." I checked. She had posted one TikTok in three months, her Instagram was inconsistent, and her Yelp page used the same five photos from 2019.

**The uncomfortable truth:** You don't need to be everywhere. You need to be good on one or two platforms where your actual customers are. For pet groomers in the US, that's typically Instagram and Google Business Profile. If your customer base skews older, add Facebook. If you're in a tight-knit neighborhood, add Nextdoor. TikTok can wait — most dog owners booking grooming appointments are busy parents in their 30s and 40s, not teenagers.

**The fix:** We shut down everything except Instagram and Google Business Profile. She committed to posting three times a week on Instagram, and once a week on Google Business (uploading a post via the Google Business app takes two minutes).

**The outcome:** Within two months, her Instagram growth actually accelerated because she wasn't spreading herself thin. She gained 400 followers organically. Google Business Profile drove 15 direct calls in one month. Total revenue from those two channels alone: $2,800.

## What to Actually Post: A Simple 4-Category Framework

Most social media advice for small businesses is too vague. "Post engaging content" means nothing. Here's a structure I've used with actual pet grooming clients that works because it answers the question: *what do I post on Tuesday morning when I have nothing prepared?*

### Category 1: The Before & After with a Story (30% of posts)

Yes, before-and-after photos work. But only if you add a sentence that makes the photo mean something. "Before and after" tells me nothing. "This is Bella. Her owner said she's been scratching for weeks. We found matting behind her ears that was pulling on her skin. Two hours and a lot of patience later, she left feeling like a new dog." That post gets shared because it's about Bella, not about your grooming skills.

**Real example:** A groomer in Chicago started adding two-sentence stories to her before-and-after posts. Her share rate tripled. One post about a terrified rescue dog who calmed down after 20 minutes of treats and gentle brushing got picked up by a local pet adoption account. That single post drove 12 new client bookings over the next month, worth roughly $1,800.

### Category 2: Educational Content (30% of posts)

Pet owners are anxious. They're worried they're brushing wrong, bathing wrong, or feeding wrong. Answer those questions, and you become the expert they trust.

Topics that work:
- "How often should you actually brush your dog?" (answer: depends on breed, but most people don't do it enough)
- "Signs your dog's nails are too long (and what happens if you ignore it)"
- "Why your dog hates the groomer (and what you can do to help)"

These posts don't have to be long. A 15-second video with a text overlay works. One groomer in Austin made a short video explaining how to tell if a dog's ears need cleaning. It got 12,000 views on Instagram. She gained 200 new followers that week, and her next month's bookings were up 35%.

**Dollar impact:** Educational content builds trust over time. I've seen it reduce price resistance — when clients already know you're knowledgeable, they don't haggle over a $75 groom.

### Category 3: Behind-the-Scenes (20% of posts)

Not the glamorous stuff. The real stuff. Show dogs being goofy. Show your shop before opening. Show a mess on the floor. People love seeing the process.

**Specific example:** A groomer in Portland filmed a 45-second time-lapse of a standard poodle groom — from matted disaster to perfect poof. She posted it on Instagram Reels. It got 45,000 views. The comments were full of "Where are you located?" and "I need to bring my doodle to you." She traced 28 new client bookings directly to that reel over the next six weeks. Total value: approximately $4,200.

### Category 4: Customer Content (20% of posts)

Repost your happy customers' photos and stories. This does two things: it gives you content you didn't have to create, and it makes those customers feel special (which means they'll keep coming back).

Set up a simple process: when a client picks up their dog, ask if you can take a photo. Say "I'd love to share this on Instagram — can I tag you?" Most people say yes. Put the photos in a folder on your phone. Post one a week.

**Real outcome:** A groomer in Denver started doing this. Within three months, 14 of her clients had shared her post to their own Stories, effectively advertising her to their entire friend group. She estimated that this word-of-mouth-on-steroids led to 8 new clients in that period, worth about $1,200.

## How to Actually Turn Followers Into Bookings

Here's a question I hear from every small business owner I work with: "I have followers. People like my posts. But they don't book appointments."

**The issue is that you're giving them no reason to stop scrolling and take action.**

### Step 1: Make Your Bio Do the Work

Your Instagram bio is prime real estate. Most groomers waste it on "Dog lover. Pet stylist. Based in [City]." That's useless. Here's a better version:

"Pet grooming in Austin, TX.
✂️ Specializing in anxious and senior dogs
📅 Book online at [link]
🚗 Free pickup within 5 miles
⬇️ Tap to book your first groom"

**The difference:** This bio tells someone exactly what you do, who you serve, and how to book — all in under 150 characters.

### Step 2: Use the Right Call-to-Action in Your Posts

"Like and share" doesn't work. "Tag a friend who needs this" works slightly better. But the best CTA for a pet groomer is specific and low-friction:

"DM us 'BRUSH' and we'll send you a guide on how to brush your dog between grooms."

That guide costs you 10 minutes to write. But now you have a DM conversation started. From there, you can say "by the way, we have openings next Tuesday if you need a full groom." A groomer in Nashville used this exact strategy. In one month, she sent 47 DMs with the brush guide. Fifteen of those converted to bookings. That's $2,400 in revenue from a 10-minute guide.

### Step 3: Use Booking Tools That Integrate With Social Media

Don't make people dig for your booking link. Tools like Booksy, Square Appointments, and Vagaro let you put a direct booking button on your Instagram and Facebook profiles.

**Real numbers:** A groomer in Austin had a website booking page that required three clicks to confirm an appointment. She switched to Booksy, which has a one-click Instagram integration. Her booking completion rate went from 12% to 38%. That meant every 100 clicks turned into 38 actual appointments instead of 12. Over a month, that difference was worth roughly $1,600 in revenue.

### Step 4: Use Google Business Profile Posts (They Work)

Most pet groomers ignore Google Business Profile posts. That's a mistake. When someone searches "dog groomer near me," Google shows the top three results. If one of those results has recent posts with photos, it stands out.

Post once a week: a photo of a recent groom, a quick promo (mention a discount code that expires in a week), or a seasonal tip ("summer is coming — double-coated dogs need extra attention").

**The dollar impact:** A groomer in Portland started posting to Google Business Profile weekly. Within a month, her profile views increased by 65%. She got 12 phone calls directly from people seeing her post in search results. Those calls led to 10 new client bookings, worth about $1,700.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much time do I actually need to spend on social media per week?**

Three hours total. One hour to shoot content (Monday morning, take 10–15 photos and 3 short videos). One hour to schedule posts for the week using a free tool. One hour to engage — reply to comments, DMs, and leave thoughtful comments on local pet accounts. If you're spending more than three hours, you're overcomplicating it. If you're spending less, you're not consistent enough to see results.

**Q: Do I really need to be on Instagram, or can I just use Facebook?**

Depends on your customers. If your client base is mostly 45+ and lives in a suburban neighborhood, Facebook is still strong. If your customers are 25–45 and in a city or urban area, Instagram is non-negotiable. But here's the thing: you can cross-post the exact same content to both platforms using a tool like Later. It takes two extra minutes per post. So the answer is: do both, but prioritize the one where your actual clients hang out.

**Q: I got a bad review on Google/Yelp. What should I do?**

Reply publicly within 48 hours. Keep it short and professional. Apologize if you messed up. If you didn't, explain what happened without being defensive. Do not argue. Do not blame the customer. A single negative review buried under 50 positive ones won't hurt you. But ignoring a negative review makes it look like you don't care. I've seen a groomer in Chicago turn a bad review into a positive outcome by offering a free re-groom and then posting a follow-up. The customer updated their review to 4 stars, and the responses showed future clients that you take complaints seriously.

**Q: Should I pay for ads or just post organically?**

Start with organic for 90 days. If you're posting consistently (three times a week) and using the four-category framework I laid out above, and you're still not getting traction, then consider a small ad budget. Start at $200/month on one platform — Instagram or Facebook. Target people within a 10-mile radius of your shop, age 25–55, who have shown interest in "dog grooming" or "pet care." Track the results after one month. If one new client from ads costs you less than $30, it's worth scaling. If it costs more than $50, stick with organic for another month.

**Q: I don't like being on camera. Can I still do social media?**

Yes. You never have to show your face. Shoot hands-only videos of grooming. Use photo slideshows with text overlays. Repost customer content. Post educational graphics. I worked with a groomer in Denver who refused to be on camera but had an Instagram account with 2,000 followers and a full booking calendar. She used photos of dogs and text-based carousel posts. It's doable. Just don't let perfectionism stop you from posting.

**Q: How do I track whether social media is actually bringing in money?**

Use a system. Every time a new client books, ask "how did you find us?" Write it down in a spreadsheet or use the notes app on your phone. Track three numbers: new clients per month, which platform they came from, and the average revenue per new client. Within three months, you'll know exactly which channel is driving revenue and which one is a time-suck. A groomer in Portland did this and discovered that 60% of her new clients came from Instagram, not Yelp or Google. She stopped paying for Yelp ads and put that $400/month into Instagram content instead. Her revenue increased by $3,200 in the next quarter.
