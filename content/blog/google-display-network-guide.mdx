With Google Display Network, you can create targeted ads that appear on websites, mobile apps, and YouTube videos. This means you can reach customers who are likely to be interested in your business, increasing the chances of converting them into sales.

Here's how to get started:

### Step 1: Create a Google Ads account

If you don't already have a Google Ads account, create one today. This will give you access to all of Google's advertising tools, including Google Display Network.

### Step 2: Choose your targeting options

With Google Display Network, you can target your ads based on factors like location, language, and audience interests. You can also use Google's targeting options to exclude certain websites or demographics.

### Step 3: Create your ad creative

Your ad creative should include a clear headline, a compelling image or video, and a strong call-to-action. Make sure your ad is visually appealing and easy to understand.

### Step 4: Set your budget and bidding strategy

Decide how much you want to spend on your ad campaign and set your bidding strategy. You can choose from cost-per-click (CPC) or cost-per-thousand impressions (CPM) bidding.

### Step 5: Launch your campaign

Once you've set up your campaign, launch it and start tracking your results. Use Google's analytics tools to see how your ads are performing and make adjustments as needed.

Google Display Network offers a range of benefits for small businesses like yours. Here are a few:

* **Increased brand awareness**: With Google Display Network, you can reach customers across the web, increasing your brand awareness and establishing yourself as a leader in your industry.

* **Measurable results**: With Google Display Network, you can track your ad performance and see exactly how much you're spending and what you're getting in return.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Everyone in a 50-Mile Radius

A coffee shop in Austin, Texas came to me after burning $1,200 in three weeks on Google Display ads. The owner, Sarah, wanted to reach "everyone near downtown." Her ads showed up on a dog breeding blog, a heavy machinery parts website, and an article about rare tropical diseases. She got zero in-store redemptions of her "free latte with purchase" coupon.

**What went wrong:** Google's default targeting is aggressive. If you set a broad radius and no audience exclusions, the algorithm will spend your money wherever inventory is cheap. That means your ad for a $5 coffee might run on a construction equipment forum at 2 a.m.

**The fix:** I had Sarah rebuild her campaign with three specific audiences: "Coffee Lovers," "Local News Readers (Austin-specific)," and "Small Business Owners." We added negative placements — anything related to cars, real estate, and medical topics got excluded. We also set a dayparting schedule: 6 AM to 10 AM and 3 PM to 5 PM, Monday through Friday.

**The outcome:** Her next $500 spent generated 42 coupon redemptions tracked through a unique QR code. That's roughly $3,800 in incremental revenue assuming one latte plus a pastry per redemption. Cost per redemption dropped from infinity (zero redemptions) to $11.90.

### Mistake #2: Using a Single Static Image for Three Months

A hair salon in Nashville had a graphic designer cousin make one nice-looking ad. They ran it for four months straight. By month two, they were wondering why their cost-per-click had doubled and click-through rate dropped below 0.05%.

**What went wrong:** Ad fatigue is real. Google's system keeps showing the same ad to the same people if you don't refresh it. Your audience literally stops seeing it — not in a "they're ignoring it" way, but in a neurological sense. The brain filters out visual noise it's seen before.

**The fix:** I gave them a schedule: one ad for "first-time client discount," one for "color services," one for "bridal packages." Rotate every two weeks. Use Google's responsive display ads (which automatically test combinations of headlines, descriptions, and images) as a backup. They also tested video ads — a 15-second clip of a stylist explaining a balayage technique.

**The outcome:** After the first rotation, CTR jumped from 0.03% to 0.21%. Cost-per-click dropped from $1.80 to $0.45. Bookings from display ads hit 14 appointments in the first month after the change, worth about $2,100 in service revenue. The salon now spends $400/month on display and tracks it through a dedicated phone number.

### Mistake #3: No Conversion Tracking, Then Blaming Google

A dog groomer in Portland, Oregon spent $800 on GDN ads over two months. She told me "display ads don't work." I asked what data she had. She showed me Google Ads click data — 340 clicks. I asked how many of those clicked actually booked an appointment. She shrugged.

**What went wrong:** She had zero conversion tracking. Google was reporting "clicks" but those could be accidental taps from someone scrolling on their phone, or a competitor checking pricing, or someone who clicked and immediately closed the tab. Without tracking what happens after the click, you're flying blind.

**The fix:** We set up Google Ads conversion tracking in about 20 minutes. For her booking page (she used Booksy), we added a Google tag that fires when someone completes a booking form. We also set up a call tracking number so phone bookings from ads could be counted. Then we created a custom conversion for "15+ minute session duration" on her website to identify serious browsers.

**The outcome:** Of those 340 clicks, only 12 resulted in completed bookings. But those 12 bookings were worth $960 in revenue (average appointment: $80). Her actual cost per acquisition was $66.67, not the $2.35 per click she was proud of. Once she saw the real numbers, she stopped complaining about "bad ads" and started optimizing for actual conversions. She now spends $300/month and gets a consistent 6–8 bookings, which at her average ticket is $480–$640 in revenue — roughly 1.6x to 2.1x return on ad spend.

## How to Set a Budget That Actually Works for Your Type of Business

Most guides tell you to "start with $500 and test." That's not wrong, but it's not helpful either. The right number depends on what you sell, how much it costs, and how competitive your local market is.

### The Math for a Low-Ticket Service

Say you run a fitness studio in Chicago offering $30 drop-in classes. Your average customer visits three times a month, so lifetime value is around $90. You need to keep cost per acquisition under $30 to make sense.

At a 1% click-through rate (reasonable for a well-targeted display campaign) and a 5% conversion rate from click to sale, you need 2,000 impressions to get one customer. If your CPM (cost per thousand impressions) is $8 in Chicago, that's $16 per customer. That's fine — $16 cost per acquisition on a $90 lifetime value gives you room.

But if your cost per click is $1.50 and you need 20 clicks to get one booking? That's $30 per customer. Now you're breaking even on the first visit, hoping they come back.

**The fix:** Start with $300/month for a low-ticket business. Run for two weeks. If you're getting customers for under 50% of your ticket price, scale to $500. If you're above 80%, fix your targeting or creative before spending more.

### The Math for a High-Ticket Service

A pet groomer in Denver charges $85 for a full groom. Lifetime value is higher — most customers come back every 4–6 weeks for years. A $50 cost per acquisition is fine.

At the same $8 CPM and 1% CTR, you need 2,000 impressions to get one click, and maybe 10 clicks to get one booking. That's $16 in ad spend for 2,000 impressions, then $15 in click costs (10 clicks at $1.50). Total: $31 per customer. That's excellent — you're paying 36% of your service price.

**The fix:** Start with $500/month. You can afford more aggressive targeting, like layering on affinity audiences ("Pet Owners," "Dog Lovers") plus in-market segments for pet products. Use a landing page with a clear booking button — not your general website homepage.

### The Real Tool for Budget Decisions

Use Google Ads' budget planner tool inside your campaign settings. It will show you estimated impressions, clicks, and conversions based on your bid strategy. But take it with a grain of salt — Google tends to overestimate. I'd cut their predictions by 30%.

Also: set a daily budget, not a lifetime budget. I've seen clients hit their monthly budget in three days because they didn't cap daily spend. If your monthly budget is $500, set daily max at $16. If the algorithm underperforms one day, it can spend more the next, but it won't blow your whole month in a weekend.

## Why Your Ad Creatives Are Probably Killing Your ROI

I've audited dozens of small business display campaigns. Almost every one of them had a logo, a generic stock photo, and text that said something like "Best [Service] in [City]." That approach works in 2012. It doesn't work now.

### The Attention Span Problem

You have roughly three seconds before someone scrolls past your ad. In those three seconds, they need to:
- Understand what you offer
- See a reason to care
- Know what to do next

Most small business ads fail on step one. I saw a salon in Denver run an ad with a photo of scissors and the text "Book Now." That tells me nothing. Scissors could be for haircuts, tailoring, or crafting.

**Better approach:** Show the result, not the process. A coffee shop should show a latte being poured with steam rising. A fitness studio should show a person smiling after a workout, not an empty room with weights. A pet groomer should show a fluffy dog before and after — that's visual proof of value.

### The Copy Test That Worked

I tested two versions of a display ad for a Nashville barbershop. Version A said "Best Fades in Nashville — Book Today." Version B said "Tired of barbers who rush through your cut? We spend 45 minutes on every head. First cut $20 off."

Version B's click-through rate was 0.34% vs Version A's 0.09%. The specific pain point ("rushing") plus a concrete offer ($20 off) plus a time commitment (45 minutes) outperformed generic promises every time.

### How to Make Ads Without Hiring a Designer

Use Canva's display ad templates — they have pre-sized options for Google Display ads (300x250, 728x90, 160x600, etc.). Use one of your own photos. Add a headline in a bold font. Add one line of supporting text. Add a button that says "Book Now" or "Get Offer." That's it.

But here's the trick I learned from a GroupM creative director: use the same image across multiple ad sizes, but change the headline for each audience segment. One ad for "New Customers: $20 Off First Visit" and another for "Loyalty Program: Every 10th Service Free." Run both simultaneously and see which gets more conversions.

### The Video Option

If you have 15 seconds, record a phone video. Show your space, explain one benefit, and say "click to book." Upload it to YouTube, link your Google Ads account, and set it as a video discovery ad. I've seen CTRs of 0.75–1.2% on these for local businesses, which trounces standard display.

A Chicago coffee shop tested a 10-second Instagram Reel-style video of a barista pouring latte art. Their cost per website visit dropped from $0.89 to $0.31. That's a 65% reduction from static ads.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much do Google Display ads actually cost for a small business?**

Plan on $300–$600 per month for a local campaign in a mid-sized US city. In expensive markets like NYC or San Francisco, expect $500–$1,000. Your cost-per-click will range from $0.30 to $2.00 depending on targeting and competition. You can start smaller — $150/month — but you'll get limited data to optimize from. I'd rather see someone test $300 for two weeks than $150 for a month, because you hit statistical significance faster.

**Q: Can I target people who have already visited my website?**

Yes, with remarketing. Set up a Google tag on your site, build an audience of "site visitors in the last 30 days," and show them specific ads. For a pet groomer, someone who visited your booking page but didn't book might respond to an ad saying "First groom 20% off — book today." Remarketing costs less than cold targeting and converts at 2–3x the rate. Just cap frequency at 3–5 impressions per week so you don't annoy people.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from display ads?**

If you have conversion tracking set up correctly, you should see data within 3–7 days. But "results" might mean clicks and impressions, not necessarily sales. I tell clients to give it two weeks before judging performance. The first week is Google's learning phase — the algorithm figures out who clicks. The second week is when you start seeing actual conversions. If you're not seeing any conversions after three weeks with at least 500 clicks, your landing page or offer is probably the issue, not the ad platform.

**Q: Do people actually click on display ads, or is it a scam?**

Yes, people click on display ads — when they're relevant. The average click-through rate across all display ads is 0.07%, which sounds tiny. But for a well-targeted local campaign, I consistently see 0.15% to 0.35%. That means 15–35 people out of 10,000 impressions click. For a coffee shop offering a free drink, that's 15–35 potential new customers. The problem isn't that display ads don't work — it's that most small businesses target too broadly or use bad creatives.

**Q: Should I use Google Display Network or just stick with Google Search ads?**

Both, but start with search if your budget is under $300/month. Search captures people actively looking for what you offer. Display is for people who don't know they need you yet. A pet groomer: search captures "dog groomer near me" — high intent, low volume. Display catches "pet supplies" or "labrador training tips" readers — lower intent, but larger volume. If you have to choose one, pick search. If you can afford both, allocate 60% to search and 40% to display until you know what works.

**Q: How do I stop my ads from showing on bad websites?**

Use placement exclusions. In your Google Ads account, go to your display campaign, then "Placements," then "Exclusions." Add categories like "Mature Content," "Religion," "Politics," "User-Generated Content." Also exclude specific websites you don't want to appear on (like competitor sites or irrelevant blogs). One client in Portland found her ad for a bridal salon running on a divorce lawyer's website. That's a 15-minute fix that saves you from wasting money on the wrong audience.
