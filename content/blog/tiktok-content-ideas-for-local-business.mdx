## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Posting Random Content Without a Strategy

**The story:** A coffee shop owner in Austin, TX, started posting TikTok videos three times a week. One video showed their espresso machine. Another was a sunrise shot of the storefront. Another was the owner's cat sitting on a bag of beans. After two months, they had 47 followers and zero foot traffic traced back to TikTok. They were spending about $200/month in labor time filming and editing.

**What went wrong:** The content had no hook, no consistent format, and no call to action. A video of a cat might get likes, but it doesn't tell anyone why they should drive across town for a latte.

**The fix:** We rebuilt their content calendar around one format: "The 30-Second Taste Test." Every video showed a customer taking the first sip of a drink, saying one word to describe it, and the price tag appeared on screen. That was it. Same structure, different drinks, different customers.

**The outcome:** After six weeks, they had 2,300 followers. The "first sip" videos got an average of 8,000 views. More importantly, they started running a simple offer — "Show this video for $1 off" — and tracked 86 redemptions in the first month. That's roughly $430 in incremental revenue from a format that took 15 minutes to film.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Local Hashtags and Location Tags

**The story:** A pet groomer in Portland, OR, was using hashtags like #dogsofinternational #petgroominglife and #goldenretriever. Their videos were well-shot. The dogs looked great. But the reach was almost entirely outside Portland. They were getting comments from people in Brazil and the Philippines, not from the neighborhood where they actually do business.

**What went wrong:** TikTok's algorithm prioritizes content that performs well with people who match the creator's audience. If your audience is global, TikTok shows your videos globally. But this business only serves customers within a 10-mile radius.

**The fix:** We switched to hyperlocal hashtags: #portlanddogs, #pdxpets, #albertapets (their neighborhood), and tagged the location of the shop in every video. We also added text overlays like "Portland groomer, 5 years, same location" in the first two seconds.

**The outcome:** Views dropped by 60% initially, but engagement from local users went up 4x. Within three months, the shop was getting 8–10 new clients per month who said they found them on TikTok. Average grooming ticket: $75. That's $600–$750 in monthly new revenue. Prior to this, they were getting zero.

### Mistake #3: Treating TikTok Like a Commercial

**The story:** A fitness studio in Nashville spent $500 on a professional video ad for TikTok. It had slow-motion shots, a voiceover with a British accent, and ended with the logo fading in over a black screen. It got 212 views and 3 likes. The owner told me they "felt like it was a good investment because it looked expensive."

**What went wrong:** TikTok rewards content that looks like it was made by a person, not an agency. The platform actively penalizes polished, ad-like content because users scroll past it. That video looked like a car commercial. Nobody watches car commercials on purpose.

**The fix:** The owner filmed herself on her iPhone the next morning, out of breath at 6:30 AM, saying, "I've been doing this for 12 years and I still hate burpees. But here's why I do them anyway." Raw. Sweaty. One take. She posted it with no editing.

**The outcome:** 14,000 views in 48 hours. 47 new class sign-ups via the link in bio over the next two weeks. Total cost: $0. The lesson: spend $500 on a real camera if you want, but spend zero on making it look polished. Authenticity beats production value every time on TikTok.

## How to Set Up a TikTok Ad Campaign That Actually Drives Walk-Ins (Without Wasting $500)

Let me be direct: most small businesses should not run TikTok ads until they have 10–15 organic posts that get at least 500 views each. If you skip that step, the ads platform will have no data on who actually engages with your content. You'll be paying to show your video to people who don't care.

Once you have the foundation, here's the campaign structure I've used with actual local businesses:

**Target radius:** 5 miles from your address. Don't let TikTok's defaults expand this. I've seen a coffee shop in Chicago accidentally target all of Cook County and spend $300 showing ads to people 40 minutes away.

**Budget:** Start at $15/day. Run for 7 days. That's $105 total. If you don't see at least 5–10 website clicks or leads by day 4, pause it. Do not increase the budget. The targeting is wrong.

**Objective:** Use "Website Conversions" not "Video Views." I know the view cost looks cheaper. But a view of someone scrolling past doesn't pay rent. Set up the TikTok pixel on your website and track "View Content" as your primary event.

**Creative:** Use your best-performing organic post. Not a new ad. Not something you paid a designer for. The exact same video that already got views. I've tested this at three different clients and the organic post that got 20,000 views almost always outperforms the polished version in paid campaigns.

**The tool you need:** Connect TikTok Ads Manager to your Square or Shopify account. This lets you track actual in-store purchases back to TikTok users. Without this, you're guessing. I've seen a salon in Denver run TikTok ads for six weeks and think they got $3,800 in revenue. When we connected the pixel properly, it turned out to be $1,200. The rest was just random foot traffic they attributed to TikTok because it felt right.

## Tracking TikTok to Foot Traffic: The Setup That Actually Works

Most local business owners I talk to want one thing: more people walking through the door. But TikTok's reporting tools aren't built for brick-and-mortar tracking. Here's the system I've used with businesses in five different US cities.

**Step 1: Unique QR codes.** Set up a QR code in Mailchimp or Canva that links to a specific landing page. Put it on a physical sign in your window. In every TikTok video, show the sign for 2 seconds and say, "Scan the code in our window for a free [small item]." When someone scans it, you know they came from TikTok.

The coffee shop in Austin used this for a free cookie with purchase. They tracked 34 redemptions in two weeks. Average spend per customer who redeemed: $8.50. Cost of cookies: $0.75 each. Net gain: $263.50. Not life-changing, but proof the channel works.

**Step 2: Use Google Business Profile.** Post your TikTok videos to Google Business Profile as well. This is free. I've seen a hair salon in Denver get 4x more clicks on their Google profile after cross-posting TikTok content there. Google ranks businesses with fresh content higher in local search.

**Step 3: Tie it to your booking system.** If you use Booksy or Square Appointments, add a custom field in the booking form that asks "How did you hear about us?" with TikTok as an option. After 90 days, pull that data. A pet groomer in Nashville did this and found that 18% of new clients in Q3 2023 listed TikTok as their source. That's almost one in five new customers. Before tracking, they had zero idea.

**Step 4: The unglamorous spreadsheet.** I hate tracking things manually, but it works. Keep a Google Sheet at the register. Write down every customer who mentions TikTok. At the end of the month, calculate total revenue from those customers. It's not sophisticated. It's better than data that doesn't exist.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a 50-year-old business owner. Isn't TikTok for teenagers?**

No. The fastest-growing demographic on TikTok is 35–54. In the US, 38% of users are over 30. Your customers are on the app. The question is whether your content shows up where they scroll. I've worked with a 62-year-old salon owner in Denver who posts videos of her trimming bangs. She has 22,000 followers and gets 4–5 new clients a week from those videos. Age of the business owner doesn't matter. The content does.

**Q: How do I get customers to give me permission to film them?**

Ask directly. After they pay, say, "I loved your reaction to that latte. Do you mind if I post a quick video of you taking a sip? I'll tag your account." Most people say yes. If they say no, thank them and move on. Do not film without asking. I've seen a coffee shop lose a regular customer because they posted someone without permission. It's not worth the risk.

**Q: Do I need a ring light, microphone, and expensive camera?**

No. Use your phone. Film in natural light or under your existing shop lights. The pet groomer in Portland films with a phone mounted on a cheap tripod from Amazon ($18). That's it. The video that got them 18% of new clients was shot in a slightly dim room with a dog shaking water everywhere. TikTok's algorithm doesn't care about your production quality. It cares about whether people watch the first three seconds.

**Q: Can I just repost my Instagram Reels to TikTok?**

You can, but you shouldn't. Instagram Reels often have text overlays and a different aspect ratio. TikTok users can tell when content is recycled. The algorithm also deprioritizes content that has visible watermarks from other platforms. Film separate content for each platform. It takes 10 extra minutes per video. A fitness studio in Nashville tested this — their Instagram Reels reposted to TikTok averaged 200 views. Original TikTok content averaged 3,000.

**Q: How much time will this realistically take per week?**

Two hours. Film three videos in one batch on a slow morning (Monday works for most businesses). Use one camera angle. No script. Just talk to the camera or show what you're doing. Edit in the TikTok app. Schedule posts for Tuesday, Thursday, and Saturday. Total time: 2 hours, including cleanup. A coffee shop owner in Austin told me they spent 8 hours a week on TikTok until we showed them this system. They cut it to 2 hours and got better results.

**Q: What if I don't want to be on camera?**

Show your hands. Show your product. Show your space. The pet groomer in Portland filmed only the dogs for two months before appearing on camera. Their most-viewed video is a Golden Retriever getting a blow-dry. No human face in it. 45,000 views. You do not need to be the face of the brand if you don't want to.
