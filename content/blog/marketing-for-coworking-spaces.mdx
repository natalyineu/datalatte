* **Conversion rate**: The percentage of people who convert into members.
* **Cost per acquisition**: The cost of acquiring a new member.
* **Return on investment**: The revenue generated by each member compared to the cost of acquiring them.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched coworking spaces hemorrhage money on the wrong marketing for years. Here are the four mistakes I see most often — and what actually works instead.

### Mistake #1: Chasing Tourists Instead of Locals

**The story:** A coworking space in Austin, TX — let's call it "Capital Desks" — spent $2,800/month on Instagram ads targeting "digital nomads" and "remote workers" nationwide. They got 400+ tour requests in three months. Almost zero conversions. Why? Tourists wanted a day pass, not a $500/month membership. The owner told me, "I thought more eyeballs meant more members. I was wrong."

**What went wrong:** They optimized for vanity metrics — impressions and tour requests — not for committed members. Tourists don't sign leases. They don't refer other people. They clog your Wi-Fi for a week and leave.

**The fix:** I told them to kill the national ads. Instead, run a $500/month Google Ads campaign targeting three zip codes within a 3-mile radius. Keywords: "coworking space Austin," "private office Austin downtown," "meeting room rental." Pair that with a referral program: existing members get one week free for every new member they bring in.

**The outcome:** Within 60 days, occupancy went from 42% to 68%. Monthly membership revenue jumped from $18,000 to $29,000. The $500 ad spend generated $11,000 in new recurring revenue. They stopped wasting $2,300/month on tourists who never intended to stay.

### Mistake #2: Treating Your Website Like a Brochure

**The story:** A space in Portland, OR — "Rose City Workspace" — had a beautiful website. Professional photos, drone footage of the building, a full amenities page. They were getting 1,200 unique visitors per month. Almost no one booked a tour. The owner was baffled. "Everyone says our space looks amazing," she said.

**What went wrong:** Their "Book a Tour" button was buried at the bottom of the homepage, after three paragraphs of mission statement and a video that autoplayed. Visitors had to scroll past "Our Story," "Our Values," and a photo of the team at a ping-pong tournament. Nobody has time for that. They were losing people who wanted to sign up but couldn't find the button.

**The fix:** I moved the booking CTA to the top-right corner of every page. Changed the button text from "Book a Tour" to "See Pricing & Availability." Added a live chat widget (free with Tidio) that popped up after 15 seconds: "Need a desk today? We have 4 open spots." Simplified the homepage to three sections: pricing, photos, and a testimonial from a local accountant who signed a 12-month lease.

**The outcome:** Tour requests went from 12 per month to 47 per month in six weeks. The live chat converted 22% of chats into tours. They didn't change anything about the space itself — just made it easier for people to say yes. Monthly revenue increased by $8,400 from new memberships.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Local Search (And Relying on Word of Mouth)

**The story:** A coworking space in Denver, CO — "Mile High Desks" — had been open for 18 months. They had 35 members, mostly from referrals. The owner told me, "We're a community space. We don't do ads. People find us through friends." Meanwhile, a WeWork opened six blocks away and took 80% of their walk-in traffic.

**What went wrong:** Referrals are great. But they're slow, unpredictable, and they cap out. Mile High Desks had zero Google My Business reviews, no local directory listings, and their website didn't even mention "Denver" in the title tag. When someone Googled "coworking space Denver," they showed up on page four. The WeWork showed up on page one with 200 reviews and a "Book Now" button.

**The fix:** I spent a weekend fixing their local SEO. Claimed their Google My Business listing, added 15 photos, and asked their 35 members to leave reviews. Set up listings on Yelp, Bing Places, and Apple Maps. Updated the website title tag to: "Coworking Space Denver | Private Offices & Hot Desks | Mile High Desks." Started a simple Google Ads campaign targeting "coworking Denver" and "private office Denver" — $600/month budget.

**The outcome:** Within 90 days, they had 28 Google reviews (4.7 stars). Their website traffic from local search went from 150 visitors/month to 1,100. The Google Ads campaign brought in 22 tour requests at $27 per lead. They signed 14 new members in three months. Revenue increased by $7,800/month. The WeWork down the street? Still there. But Mile High Desks stopped losing walk-ins to them.

### Mistake #4: Offering Discounts Instead of Value

**The story:** A space in Nashville, TN — "Music City Workspace" — was struggling with 55% occupancy. The owner's solution: slash prices. "First month free!" "50% off your first three months!" "Bring a friend, get two weeks free!" They got a flood of sign-ups — and a flood of churn. Members stayed for the discount, then left when the price went up. The owner was burning cash on acquisition and retention at the same time.

**What went wrong:** Discounts attract price-sensitive people who will leave for the next deal. They don't build loyalty. They don't attract the people who will actually commit to a 6-month or 12-month lease. Music City Workspace was essentially paying people to churn.

**The fix:** I told them to kill all discounts. Instead, add value that costs you almost nothing but feels expensive to the member. Free printing (costs you $0.03/page, feels like $50/month value). A weekly catered lunch (costs you $15/person, feels like $200/month value). A dedicated parking spot for members who sign a 6-month lease (costs you nothing, feels like $100/month value). Raise the base price by 15% and bundle the extras.

**The outcome:** Occupancy dropped to 48% for six weeks as the discount-seekers left. Then it climbed to 72% over the next four months — with higher-quality members who stayed longer. Average membership length went from 4.2 months to 9.8 months. Monthly revenue per member increased by $85. Total monthly revenue went from $22,000 to $34,000. ## How to Run a Local Ad Campaign That Actually Fills Desks

Most coworking spaces throw money at Facebook ads and hope. Here's the specific system I've used for four different spaces — it works because it targets people who are already looking.

### Step 1: Set Up Google Ads for "High Intent" Keywords

Skip the brand awareness campaigns. You want people typing "coworking near me" or "private office [your city]" into Google. These people have a credit card in hand.

**Budget:** Start with $500/month for a single city. Increase to $1,000/month once you see positive ROI.

**Keywords to target:**
- "coworking space [city]" (high volume, moderate competition)
- "private office [city]" (lower volume, higher intent)
- "meeting room rental [city]" (event-based, good for upsells)
- "[city] office space for rent" (captures people comparing coworking vs. traditional leases)
- "coworking near [landmark]" (e.g., "coworking near downtown Austin" or "coworking near Union Station Denver")

**Negative keywords (add these immediately):**
- "free" (you don't want freeloaders)
- "day pass" (unless you sell day passes as a separate product)
- "virtual office" (different product, different customer)
- "we work" (you're not paying to send people to your competitor)

**Ad copy that works (test these):**
- Headline: "Coworking in [City] — From $[price]/month"
- Description: "Private offices, hot desks, and meeting rooms. Tour today and get your first week free."
- Headline: "Need a Private Office in [City]? We Have 5 Spots Left"
- Description: "Fully furnished, all utilities included. Month-to-month leases available. Book a tour in 60 seconds."

### Step 2: Track Everything With a Simple Spreadsheet

Most small business owners don't track their ad spend properly. They look at "impressions" and "clicks" and feel good. You need to track what matters.

**What to track weekly:**
- Ad spend ($)
- Clicks (#)
- Cost per click ($)
- Tour requests (#)
- Cost per tour request ($)
- New members (#)
- Cost per new member ($)
- Revenue from new members ($)
- ROI (revenue / ad spend)

**Example from a Chicago coworking space I worked with:**
- Ad spend: $600/month
- Tour requests: 18
- Cost per tour: $33
- New members: 7
- Cost per member: $86
- Revenue from new members (first month): $4,200
- ROI: 7x

If your cost per new member goes above $150, pause the campaign and adjust your targeting or ad copy. If it's below $100, double the budget.

### Step 3: Use Retargeting to Catch the People Who Didn't Book

Here's a stat that will annoy you: 97% of people who visit your website will leave without booking a tour. Most of them are interested — they're just busy, or they got distracted, or they wanted to check one more space before deciding.

**The fix:** Install the Facebook Pixel and Google Ads remarketing tag on your website. Then run a retargeting campaign with a simple offer: "Still looking? Tour our space this week and get your first month at 50% off."

**Budget:** $200/month on top of your main campaign.

**What to show them:**
- Day 1-3: A photo of your best corner office with the text "Your desk is waiting."
- Day 4-7: A testimonial video from a member saying "I doubled my revenue after moving here."
- Day 8-14: A limited-time offer: "Book a tour by Friday and get 50% off your first month."

**Real result:** A space in San Diego added retargeting and saw tour requests from website visitors jump from 3% to 8%. They signed 5 additional members in the first month — $3,000 in new recurring revenue from a $200 ad spend.

## How to Get Local Businesses to Rent Your Private Offices

Your private offices are your highest-margin product. A hot desk might bring in $300/month. A private office can bring in $1,200/month. But you need to sell to a different audience: small business owners who are tired of working from home or from coffee shops.

### Who to Target (And Where to Find Them)

**Target #1: Solo professionals who outgrew their home office**
- Realtors, accountants, lawyers, financial advisors, therapists
- They need a professional address for client meetings
- They're tired of Zoom calls interrupted by their kids/dogs/roommates
- **Where to find them:** LinkedIn (search by profession + location), local Chamber of Commerce directories, Google Maps (look for home-based businesses in your area)

**Target #2: Small teams (2-5 people) who want to escape WeWork**
- They're paying $2,000/month for a WeWork office and hate the noise
- They want their own door, their own Wi-Fi, their own coffee machine
- **Where to find them:** Google Reviews of WeWork locations (look for 1-star reviews complaining about noise or price), local business Facebook groups

**Target #3: Remote employees who need separation from home**
- People who work for out-of-state companies but live in your city
- Their employer pays for coworking memberships (many do)
- **Where to find them:** Local "remote workers" meetup groups, Nextdoor, local subreddits

### The Outreach Strategy That Works

**Step 1: Send a handwritten note (yes, really)**

I tested this with a space in Kansas City. We bought a list of 200 home-based businesses from the local Chamber of Commerce. We sent each one a handwritten postcard: "Need a quiet place to work? We're two blocks away. First week is on us." Cost: $0.75 per postcard including postage. Response rate: 12%. That's 24 tour requests from a $150 investment.

**Step 2: Offer a "try before you buy" day pass**

Most private office renters are nervous about committing to a lease. They want to test the Wi-Fi, the noise level, the parking situation. Offer a free day pass — no strings attached. Give them a tour, let them work for a day, and follow up the next day. Conversion rate from free day pass to monthly lease: 35-40%.

**Step 3: Partner with local businesses that serve the same audience**

A coworking space in Nashville partnered with a local coffee roaster. The roaster gave a free bag of coffee to anyone who signed a private office lease. The coworking space promoted the roaster on their social media. Both businesses got new customers. Cost to the coworking space: $0. The coffee roaster spent $8 per bag on wholesale. They both won.

### Pricing Your Private Offices (Without Leaving Money on the Table)

Most coworking spaces underprice their private offices because they're scared of being "too expensive." Here's the truth: a private office is cheaper than a traditional commercial lease. Your competition isn't other coworking spaces — it's a $2,000/month office in a strip mall.

**Pricing formula I use:**
- Take the average rent for a commercial office of the same size in your area
- Add 15% for included utilities, internet, and cleaning
- Subtract 10% for the lack of a long-term lease commitment
- That's your base price

**Example for a 150 sq ft office in Denver:**
- Commercial office: $1,800/month (all-in)
- Plus 15% for utilities/internet/cleaning: $2,070
- Minus 10% for month-to-month flexibility: $1,863
- Your price: $1,850/month

**What to include to justify the price:**
- Dedicated high-speed internet (mention the speed: "500 Mbps dedicated line")
- Weekly cleaning service
- Access to meeting rooms (2 hours/month included)
- Mail handling and package receiving
- Coffee, tea, and filtered water

**What NOT to include (these eat your margin):**
- Free printing beyond 100 pages/month (charge $0.10/page after that)
- 24/7 access without a security deposit (charge a $200 refundable deposit)
- Parking (if you have limited spots, charge $50/month or make it a perk for 12-month leases)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it take to see results from local marketing?**

If you're starting from zero — no Google My Business listing, no ads, no reviews — expect 4-6 weeks before you see a meaningful increase in tour requests. The first two weeks are setup and testing. Weeks 3-4 are optimization. By week 6, you should see a clear trend. If you don't, you're targeting the wrong keywords or your pricing is off.

**Q: Should I offer day passes or monthly memberships only?**

Offer both, but don't market day passes aggressively. Day passes are a low-margin product that attracts tourists and people who won't commit. Use them as a conversion tool: give a free day pass to someone considering a monthly membership. Don't run ads for day passes unless you're in a tourist-heavy area and can charge $30+/day.

**Q: My space is in a small town, not a big city. Does this still work?**

Yes, but adjust your targeting. In a small town, Google Ads might be too expensive because the volume is low. Focus on local Facebook groups, the Chamber of Commerce, and direct mail. I worked with a space in Bozeman, MT — population 55,000 — and they filled 80% of their offices by sending postcards to every real estate agent and therapist within 10 miles. Cost: $300. Result: 12 new members in 60 days.

**Q: How do I compete with WeWork or Regus?**

You don't compete on price. You compete on personality and flexibility. WeWork has a 2-page contract and a corporate vibe. You can offer month-to-month leases, a key fob that works 24/7, and the owner's cell phone number when something breaks. Emphasize that in your marketing: "No call center. No corporate bureaucracy. Just a desk and good Wi-Fi."

**Q: Do I really need a website, or can I just use Facebook?**

You need a website. Facebook is where people browse. Your website is where people decide. I've seen spaces with zero social media presence fill up because their website had clear pricing, good photos, and a "Book a Tour" button that worked. I've also seen spaces with 10,000 Facebook followers and 40% occupancy because their website was a mess. The website is your salesperson. Make it work.

**Q: What's the biggest mistake you see coworking spaces make with their marketing?**

Thinking that "community" will fill the space. Community is what keeps people. Marketing is what gets them in the door. I've seen too many owners spend $5,000 on a ping-pong table and $0 on Google Ads. The ping-pong table is nice. The Google Ads bring in the people who will use it. Prioritize acquisition before amenities.
