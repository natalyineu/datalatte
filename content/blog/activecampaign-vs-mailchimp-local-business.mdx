## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched local business owners burn money on email marketing for over a decade. Not because they're bad at their jobs — they're great at roasting coffee or cutting hair. They just got bad advice from people who've never managed a real P&L.

Here are the four mistakes I see most often. Each one comes with a story from an actual business owner I've worked with or consulted for.

### Mistake #1: Buying the Cheapest Plan and Never Touching the Settings

**The story:** A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas — let's call her Maria — signed up for Mailchimp's free plan because she had 1,200 subscribers and the price looked right. She imported her entire Square transaction history into one list, hit "send" on a weekly newsletter, and called it done.

Six months in, her open rate was 18%. Her click rate was 1.2%. She was spending $200/month on Google Ads to bring people in the door, but her email list — full of people who'd actually given her money — was doing almost nothing.

**What went wrong:** Maria never cleaned her list. Mailchimp's free plan doesn't include automated list hygiene. Inactive subscribers — people who hadn't opened an email in six months — were dragging down her deliverability. Worse, she was sending the same message to everyone: the early-morning regular who bought a latte every Tuesday, the tourist who visited once and lives in Ohio, and the guy who unsubscribed but ended up on the list through a glitch.

**The fix:** I had Maria switch to ActiveCampaign's Lite plan ($15/month for 500 contacts, scaling up as she cleaned her list). We ran a re-engagement sequence: three emails over two weeks asking inactive subscribers if they still wanted to hear from her. The ones who didn't respond got removed. Her list dropped from 1,200 to 870.

Then we built two segments: weekday regulars (purchase frequency >3 times per month) and weekend visitors (purchase frequency <1 time per month). Weekday regulars got a loyalty offer — buy 10 drinks, get one free. Weekend visitors got a brunch special.

**The outcome:** Within 60 days, her open rate went from 18% to 41%. Her click rate hit 6.8%. She added $2,400 in monthly revenue from email alone — on a $15/month tool. The Google Ads budget got cut in half.

### Mistake #2: Automating Everything Without Understanding Timing

**The story:** A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon — let's call him James — had been using Booksy for booking and Mailchimp for email. He set up an automation: new client books an appointment → send a thank-you email. Appointment happens → send a review request. One week later → send a "book again" offer.

His conversion rate from first visit to second visit was 22%. Industry average for salons is about 35%.

**What went wrong:** James's automation was sending the "book again" email exactly seven days after the appointment. But his typical client — a woman in her early 30s with a busy job — doesn't think about her next haircut for three weeks. She gets the email at day seven, thinks "not yet," and by the time she's ready to book, she's forgotten about it. The automation had no logic for restyling cycles, no reminder sequence, and no way to handle no-shows.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the automation in ActiveCampaign (Mailchimp's three-step limit wouldn't have cut it). Here's what the sequence looked like:

- Day 1: Thank-you + "we saved your stylist notes"
- Day 21: "Your roots are probably showing" (with a photo of her last color) + booking link
- Day 28: "Still thinking about it?" + testimonial from someone who booked late
- Day 35: "Last call — we have openings this week" + $10 off

If she booked at any point, the sequence stopped. If she didn't, we flagged her as "at risk" and sent a different offer at day 45.

**The outcome:** James's second-visit conversion rate went from 22% to 44%. His average client lifetime value jumped from $380 to $620. The automation took about three hours to set up and has been running for nine months without touching it.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Purchase History in Segmentation

**The story:** A pet groomer in Nashville — Sarah — had 2,800 email subscribers and was sending a monthly newsletter to everyone on her list. Same content for the golden retriever owner, the cat owner (yes, she grooms cats), and the person who brought their dog once three years ago.

She was spending $79/month on Mailchimp's Standard plan. Her revenue per email was $0.42. For context, well-segmented local businesses typically see $2–$4 per email.

**What went wrong:** Sarah had purchase data in her Square account — breed, service type, frequency, total spend. She wasn't using any of it. Her monthly email was generic: "Come see us for spring grooming!" — which means nothing to someone who just had their dog groomed two days ago.

**The fix:** I helped Sarah migrate to ActiveCampaign and connect it to Square. We created segments based on actual behavior:

- **High-value clients** (spent >$400/year): Monthly email with priority booking + 10% off add-on services
- **Seasonal clients** (grooming 2–3 times per year): Reminder emails tied to breed-specific grooming needs (e.g., "Your poodle needs a trim every 6–8 weeks")
- **Cat owners**: Separate content entirely — cat grooming tips, cat-only appointment slots
- **Lapsed clients** (no visit in 6+ months): Win-back sequence with a $25 discount (cost her about $18 in product cost, brought back 34% of lapsed clients)

**The outcome:** Revenue per email went from $0.42 to $3.80. Her open rate hit 52%. She generated $5,400 in additional revenue in the first three months. The migration took two afternoons. Her Mailchimp bill was replaced by an ActiveCampaign bill that was actually $5 less per month because she cleaned her list.

### Mistake #4: Not Tracking Attribution Properly

**The story:** A fitness studio owner in Denver — Mike — was running a promotion: "Bring a friend for free" — driven entirely through email. He sent it to his list of 1,500 members. Forty-seven people brought a friend. Eight of those friends signed up for memberships.

Mike's cost: $0 (he was on Mailchimp's free tier). Revenue from new memberships: $4,200. He thought it was a home run.

**What went wrong:** Mike had no idea why people actually came in. He didn't ask new members how they heard about the studio. He didn't use UTM parameters, track link clicks, or set up any kind of attribution. Three months later, when he wanted to run the same promotion again, he had no data on which segment responded best — did the offer work better with morning class attendees or evening class attendees? Was it stronger with members who'd been around for a year or new members? He couldn't answer any of those questions.

**The fix:** We set up three things:
1. ActiveCampaign automation with UTM tracking on every link
2. A "how did you hear about us?" field in his booking software (he was using Mindbody)
3. A landing page specifically for the promo (not just an email with a link to his main site)

After the second run, he had data: the offer worked best with members who attended at least 8 classes per month and had been members for 3–6 months. Those members brought friends who converted at 2.5x the rate of members who attended infrequently.

**The outcome:** The second promotion cost him $35/month (ActiveCampaign Lite plan + landing page fee). It generated $8,200 in new memberships. He now runs the promotion quarterly, targeting specific segments, and tracks every dollar back to the email that sent it.

## How to Actually Set Up Email Automation for a Local Business (Without Getting a Degree in Tech)

Most guides make this sound harder than it is. Here's what I tell my clients. I'll use ActiveCampaign because it has the features you need without the learning curve of HubSpot or Klaviyo.

### Step 1: Connect Your POS System First

If you're using Square, Shopify, or Lightspeed, connect it to ActiveCampaign before you do anything else. This imports purchase data, which is your most valuable asset.

I worked with a salon in Austin that skipped this step. They spent three weeks building segments manually based on a spreadsheet. Then they connected Square and realized half their data was wrong — people who'd bought products were tagged as "service only" and vice versa. They had to rebuild everything.

**Do this instead:** Connect your POS on day one. Wait 24 hours for the data to sync. Then start segmenting.

### Step 2: Build Three Automations — Not Twelve

You don't need a "welcome sequence" and a "birthday sequence" and a "holiday sequence" and a "re-engagement sequence" and a "post-purchase upsell sequence." You need three:

1. **New subscriber welcome** (3 emails over 5 days): introduce yourself, share your best content, offer a first-time discount
2. **Post-purchase follow-up** (2–3 emails over 2 weeks): thank-you, review request, next-time offer
3. **Win-back for lapsed customers** (3 emails over 10 days): "we miss you," "here's what's new," "here's a discount"

Set these up first. They'll cover 80% of your email revenue. Everything else is bonus.

### Step 3: Set Up a Simple Scoring System

ActiveCampaign lets you assign points to contacts based on behavior. Here's the only score you need for a local business:

- +5 points for opening an email
- +10 points for clicking a link
- +20 points for visiting your website (from a tracked link)
- +50 points for making a purchase (from your POS integration)

Set your threshold at 100 points. Anyone above 100 is "engaged." Anyone below is "at risk" after 60 days.

When someone drops below engaged, move them to your win-back sequence. When they stop opening altogether for 90 days, remove them from your list.

This simple system will keep your list clean and your deliverability high. It takes about 20 minutes to set up.

### Step 4: Actually Test Your Subject Lines

I'm not talking about the A/B test button. I'm talking about sending two different subject lines to two small segments and seeing which one wins before you send to your whole list.

ActiveCampaign's A/B testing lets you do this automatically. Send version A to 15% of your list, version B to 15%, and the winner goes to the remaining 70%. Mailchimp also has this feature, but only on paid plans starting at $20/month.

Here's a subject line test that worked for a Denver fitness studio:

- Version A: "Your free class is waiting"
- Version B: "Your first class is on us"

Version B had a 38% higher open rate. Same offer, different phrasing. The difference was $1,100 in additional class bookings over the next month.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I switch from Mailchimp to ActiveCampaign without losing my data?**

Yes, but don't just export and import. Export your active subscribers (last 90 days of engagement) separately from your inactive ones. Only import the active ones into ActiveCampaign. For the inactive ones, run a re-engagement campaign in Mailchimp first, give them 30 days, then import whoever responds. I helped a salon in Chicago do this. They imported 800 active subscribers, ran the re-engagement campaign on the remaining 400, got 110 to re-engage, and permanently unsubscribed the rest. Their deliverability improved 22% within two weeks.

**Q: Does ActiveCampaign work with Square, and is it easy to set up?**

Yes, it works with Square. You'll need ActiveCampaign's Plus plan or higher ($49/month for 1,000 contacts) because Square integration isn't available on the Lite plan. The setup takes about 45 minutes if you know your way around settings. If you don't, budget two hours and have ActiveCampaign's support chat open — they're actually helpful, unlike most chatbot support I've dealt with.

**Q: What if I only have 200 subscribers? Is ActiveCampaign still worth it?**

Maybe not. For 200 subscribers, the Lite plan costs $15/month and Mailchimp's Free plan costs $0. A $15 difference matters when you're starting out. But here's the catch: Mailchimp's free plan has zero automation, zero segmentation, and zero contact scoring. You're basically sending a newsletter, not running an email program. If you have time to manually manage your list, stick with free for the first 3–6 months. If you want to set up automations that run while you sleep, spend the $15. A Portland coffee shop started with 200 subscribers and ActiveCampaign and grew to 2,800 in eight months. The automation they built on day one still runs today.

**Q: Does ActiveCampaign send emails to my "Promotions" tab on Gmail?**

Sometimes, but the Promotions tab isn't the enemy. Gmail's Promotions tab has higher engagement than the Primary tab for commercial email because people check it when they're ready to buy. The problem isn't the tab — it's your subject line and content. If you're writing "June Newsletter" as your subject line, you deserve the Promotions tab. Write something specific: "Your next coffee's on us" lands just fine.

**Q: How do I know if my email marketing is actually working?**

Don't look at open rates. Look at revenue per email. Here's the formula: take the total revenue you can attribute to email in a month, divide by the number of emails you sent. For a local business, anything above $1.50 per email is solid. Above $3.50 is excellent. I had a pet groomer in Denver hitting $5.20 per email — they were sending very targeted offers to specific dog breeds.

**Q: Can ActiveCampaign replace my booking software (like Booksy or Mindbody)?**

No. ActiveCampaign is a CRM and email marketing tool, not a booking system. Keep your booking software for scheduling. Connect it to ActiveCampaign so you can send automated reminders, follow-ups, and re-engagement emails based on appointment data. Don't try to make it do everything. I've seen businesses try to use ActiveCampaign's CRM features to manage appointments — it doesn't work well and you'll waste hours fighting the system.
