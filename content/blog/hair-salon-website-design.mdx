When it comes to designing a website for your hair salon, there are several key elements to consider. Here are a few tips to get you started:

### 1. Simplify Your Navigation

A cluttered website can be overwhelming for visitors. Make sure your navigation is clear and easy to understand, with clear labels and minimal options. This will help visitors quickly find what they're looking for and reduce bounce rates.

### 2. Showcase Your Services

Your website should clearly showcase the services you offer, including photos, descriptions, and pricing. This will help visitors understand what you do and make informed decisions about whether to book an appointment.

### 3. Display Reviews and Testimonials

Reviews and testimonials from satisfied clients can go a long way in building trust and credibility with potential clients. Make sure to display them prominently on your website, along with photos and names.

### 4. Make it Easy to Book an Appointment

Online booking is a game-changer for hair salons, allowing clients to book appointments 24/7. Make sure your website includes a clear call-to-action to book an appointment, along with a simple and easy-to-use booking system.

### 5. Keep it Mobile-Friendly

More and more people are using their mobile devices to browse the internet, so make sure your website is optimized for mobile. A mobile-friendly website will ensure that your visitors have a seamless experience, regardless of the device they're using.

### 6. Use High-Quality Images

High-quality images can help showcase your services and make your website more visually appealing. Make sure to use high-resolution images that are optimized for web use.

### 7. Keep it Up-to-Date

Your website should be regularly updated with fresh content, including new services, promotions, and events. This will help keep visitors engaged and coming back for more.

### 8. Use Analytics to Track Performance

Analytics can help you understand how visitors are interacting with your website, including which pages they're visiting and what actions they're taking. This will help you make data-driven decisions to improve your website and increase conversions.

### 9. Optimize for Local SEO

If you're a local business, make sure to optimize your website for local SEO. This includes including your business in online directories, using location-specific keywords, and creating content that's relevant to your local audience.

### 10. Make it Secure

Make sure your website is secure by using an SSL certificate and keeping your software up-to-date. This will help protect your visitors' data and prevent security breaches.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Hiding Your Phone Number and Booking Button

**The story:** A two-chair salon in Austin, Texas — let's call it "The Cut Club" — spent $3,200 on a Squarespace site that looked gorgeous. Full-screen hero video of hair being blow-dried, moody lighting, the whole thing. Problem: there was no phone number above the fold. The "Book Now" button was hidden in a hamburger menu on mobile. They assumed clients would scroll or click around.

**What actually happened:** In the first month, they got three online booking requests. The owner, Maria, was fielding frustrated calls from people who said they almost gave up. She had no way to track mobile visits, but her Google Business Profile calls jumped 60% — people were googling "hair salon Austin" and clicking the phone link instead of dealing with the website.

**The fix:** Moved the phone number to the top header on every page. Added a sticky "Book Now" button that follows you down the page on mobile. Made the "Services" page have one-click call buttons next to each service.

**The outcome:** Within six weeks, phone calls from the website increased from zero to about 45 per week. Online bookings went from 3 to 28 per month. That translated to roughly $2,100 in additional monthly revenue (average ticket $75). The site redesign cost $0 — just rearranged existing blocks.

**The lesson:** If you make a client think even once, they're gone. Your phone number and booking path need to be so obvious that someone who's been up since 5 a.m. with a toddler can find it in under two seconds.

### Mistake #2: Using Stock Photos (and Losing Every New Client at the Credibility Check)

**The story:** A salon in Nashville, Tennessee — "Luxe Locks" — had a well-laid-out website, but every single image was from a stock library. The "Our Work" gallery showed models with perfect skin and generic highlights. The owner, Derek, thought it made the site look "aspirational." 

**What actually happened:** A friend of mine went there based on the website, paid $200 for a balayage, and left crying. The result looked nothing like the photos. She posted a one-star review saying "false advertising." Three more similar reviews popped up over the next two months. Their average rating dropped from 4.7 to 3.9. New client acquisition slowed to a crawl.

**The fix:** Derek hired a local photographer (Amber from Nashville Headshots, $750 for a half-day shoot). They staged six real clients — different hair types, skin tones, ages — and shot before/after shots in natural light. He also asked five regulars to let him take quick phone photos after their cuts and added those to a "Real Clients" section.

**The outcome:** Within three months, the "before/after" page became the second most-visited page. The bounce rate on the services page dropped from 68% to 41%. New client bookings increased by 44%. Estimated revenue gain: $3,800 per month. The $750 photo investment paid for itself in six days.

**The lesson:** Stock photos signal "I don't have enough real work to show" — even if you're amazing. Real photos, even if imperfect, build trust. Your salon's actual clients are your best marketing asset.

### Mistake #3: No Online Booking — Relying Solely on Phone Calls

**The story:** A larger salon in Denver, Colorado — "Mountain Mane" — with six stylists was still using a paper appointment book in 2023. The owner, Jenna, insisted that "people like to call." Her website had an email address and a phone number. That was it.

**What actually happened:** I ran a free audit for her through a small business program. We pulled her phone logs. Out of 340 incoming calls in a week, 47 went to voicemail (busy signal or after-hours). Of those 47, only 12 called back. That's 35 missed booking opportunities per week. At an average ticket of $85, that's nearly $3,000 in lost revenue every seven days. And that's not counting the people who didn't even bother calling because they couldn't find the number easily.

**The fix:** Integrated Booksy (free tier, then $29/month for the premium features). Added a "Book Now" widget that shows real-time availability by stylist. Also set up automated text reminders to reduce no-shows.

**The outcome:** Online bookings accounted for 62% of all appointments within two months. Voicemail abandonment dropped to near zero (people just book online at 2 a.m.). No-show rate fell from 14% to 6%. The monthly revenue increase from recovered bookings alone: $4,100. Jenna's biggest surprise was that 70% of new clients under age 35 booked online; she had assumed they'd prefer phone.

**The lesson:** Phone calls are a leaky bucket. Online booking isn't just convenient — it's the only way to capture the impulse booker who lands on your site at 11 p.m.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Google Business Profile (You're Letting Competitors Steal Your Lunch)

**The story:** A salon in Chicago, Illinois — "Windy City Cuts" — had a beautiful website but had never claimed its Google Business Profile (GBP). When you searched "hair salon Chicago" on Google Maps, the top result was a Supercuts three blocks away. Windy City Cuts showed up on page two or three.

**What actually happened:** The owner, Carlos, was spending $600/month on Google Ads driving traffic to his website. But his organic local search traffic was essentially zero. I checked BrightLocal data: 76% of people who search for a local service visit a business within 24 hours. His GBP listing had no photos, no services listed, no posts, and an unverified phone number. Meanwhile, the Supercuts listing had 42 photos, 150 reviews, and Q&A answers.

**The fix:** Claimed and verified the GBP (took one phone call with Google). Added 15 high-quality photos of the interior, stylists, and recent work. Listed all services with pricing (yes, pricing — it increases click-through rates by 30% according to Google). Started asking every client to leave a review (sent a follow-up text via Mailchimp with a direct link). Posted weekly updates with specials and new stylist introductions.

**The outcome:** Within four weeks, Windy City Cuts appeared in the "local pack" for 8 key search terms. Organic traffic to the website from Google Maps increased by 180%. Carlos reduced his Google Ads spend to $300/month because organic was now doing the heavy lifting. Net monthly savings: $300 in ad spend, plus an estimated $2,200 in new client revenue directly attributed to GBP visibility. Total: $2,500/month improvement.

**The lesson:** Your website is useless if no one can find it. Google Business Profile is the front door of your digital storefront. Claim it, fill it out completely, and treat it like a second website.

## The Mobile Reality: 70% of Your Traffic Is on a Phone (And Your Site Probably Looks Like Trash)

Here's a number that still shocks me: According to a 2023 Statista survey, 68% of local service searches happen on mobile devices. For hair salons specifically, I've seen it hit 75% during weekday evenings when people are scrolling after work. If your site isn't designed for a 5.5-inch screen held in one hand, you're actively pushing away three out of four potential clients.

I worked with a salon in Portland, Oregon — "Rainier Roots" — that had a desktop-first site built in 2019. On Chrome's mobile view, the text was tiny, buttons overlapped, and the booking form required zooming in. Their mobile bounce rate was 82%. I showed the owner a heatmap from Hotjar: visitors were tapping frantically on the "Book Now" button that was hidden behind the stylist photo carousel. They were literally fighting the site.

**The fix wasn't a rebuild.** We switched to a responsive template (they were on WordPress with Divi — we just adjusted breakpoints). We made the "Book Now" button a fixed bottom bar on mobile. Simplified the menu to five items. Increased font size to at least 16px (Apple recommends 17px minimum). Added click-to-call buttons next to the phone number.

**The results:** Mobile bounce rate dropped from 82% to 44% in three weeks. Mobile bookings increased by 90%. Total site conversions (booking or call) went from 1.2% to 3.8%. That salon now attributes $5,400/month in new revenue to mobile improvements alone.

**What you should do immediately:**
- Open your website on your own phone. Try to book an appointment. Is it painful? Fix it.
- Use Google's Mobile-Friendly Test tool (free). If it says "page is not mobile-friendly," drop everything.
- Install a tool like Lucky Orange or Clarity to record mobile sessions. Watch three recordings. You'll see exactly where people struggle.
- Keep your mobile menu to five items max: Home, Services, Gallery, Book Now, Contact.
- Never, ever use pop-ups on mobile that cover more than 30% of the screen. Google will penalize you, and your visitors will hate you.

Tools I actually use:  
- **Google Mobile-Friendly Test** (free)  
- **Lucky Orange** (heatmaps start at $10/month)  
- **Hotjar** (free tier captures 35 sessions per day)  
- **Squarespace** and **Wix** both have strong mobile preview modes

## Reviews and Social Proof: The Only Thing That Converts Lookie-Loos Into Paying Clients

I've seen a salon website that checks every box — fast loading, clear navigation, gorgeous photos — and still converts at 1%. The missing piece? Social proof. When I ask salon owners why they don't put reviews on their site, the most common answer is "I have reviews on Yelp and Google, isn't that enough?"

No. It is not enough.

**Why embedding reviews matters:** A BrightLocal study from 2024 showed that 72% of consumers trust a business more after seeing positive reviews on the business's own website. The same study found that having at least five reviews on your site increases the likelihood of a booking by 45%. People want to see that you're good *before* they click away to Yelp.

**Real example:** A salon in NYC called "SoHo Shears" was getting one review per month on Google. They had a "Testimonials" page that was blank (just a heading with "coming soon"). The owner didn't think anyone would read it. We installed a simple plugin (WP Customer Reviews, free) that lets clients submit reviews directly on the site. We also used a service called EmbedSocial to pull Google and Yelp reviews into a live feed on the homepage.

We added a pop-up after booking confirmation: "How was your visit? Leave a review and get 10% off your next appointment." Within six weeks, they had 34 reviews on the site. The conversion rate on the homepage went from 2.1% to 4.8%. The "Reviews" page became the third most-viewed page.

**What to do:**  
- Use a tool to aggregate reviews from Google, Yelp, and Facebook into one widget. **EmbedSocial** ($9/month) works. **Taggbox** also works.  
- Ask for reviews immediately after a service. Send an automated text via **Mailchimp** or **Twilio** with a direct link to your Google review page.  
- Feature your best 3-5 reviews prominently on the homepage, especially ones that mention specific stylists or services.  
- Include before/after photos with the client's name (with permission). This is gold.

**Cost to implement:** $0 to $15/month. Potential lift: 20-50% in conversion rate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a website if I have an Instagram account with 5,000 followers?**

Yes, you do. Instagram is not searchable the way Google is. Someone searching "hair salon Austin TX" won't find your Instagram unless they already follow you. A website gives you control over your brand and allows you to capture bookings without algorithm interference. Plus, many people still trust a website more than a social media profile for making a purchase decision. I've seen salons with 10K Instagram followers get 80% of their new clients from Google search — not Instagram. Keep Instagram for engagement, but make your website the official hub.

**Q: How much should I spend on a salon website?**

It depends on your goals. A DIY site on Squarespace or Wix with a template: $200-$300/year plus your time. A professionally designed site: $1,500-$5,000 one-time, plus hosting ($20/month). What matters is not how much you spend but whether the site drives calls and bookings. I've seen $500 DIY sites outperform $5,000 agency sites because the DIY owner focused on speed, mobile optimization, and clear calls to action. Start with a template, spend $200 on a photographer, and invest in a booking system. That's usually enough.

**Q: Can I just use a booking link from Booksy or Vagaro and skip the website?**

You can, but you're leaving money on the table. A standalone booking page gives you no opportunity to showcase your work, build trust, or explain why someone should choose you over the 12 other salons in your area. A simple one-page website with your portfolio, reviews, contact info, and a Booksy embed costs almost nothing and will dramatically increase your conversion rate. Don't send people directly to a booking tool — they'll comparison shop while they're there.

**Q: How long until I see results from a new website?**

If you launch a site that's mobile-friendly, has clear booking buttons, shows real photos, and you've claimed your Google Business Profile, you should see an increase in calls and bookings within two to four weeks. Organic SEO takes longer — three to six months for noticeable traffic from search engines. But if you're running any ads (Google or social), you can see ROI within days. One salon in San Diego got their first booking from a new site within four hours of going live because they had a "new client special" prominently displayed.

**Q: Do I need a blog for my hair salon website?**

No. A blog can help with SEO if you consistently write about hair topics people search for (e.g., "best balayage for brunettes" or "how to maintain curly hair between appointments"). But if you hate writing, don't force it. A better use of time: add a "Tips" section that's essentially short Q&As from your stylists. Or do a monthly video and post the transcript. I'd rather see you invest time in getting reviews, updating your gallery, and optimizing your GBP than writing blog posts nobody reads.

**Q: Should I list my prices on the website?**

Yes. 100% yes. I know some salon owners worry that listing prices will scare people off or invite comparison shopping. The truth is that not listing prices scares away more people. According to a 2022 survey by Salon Today, 62% of clients want to see pricing before booking. Transparency builds trust. One salon I worked with in Seattle listed all service prices and saw a 15% increase in booking conversion — people were relieved to know the cost upfront. If you're worried about competitor undercutting, focus on the value you provide, not the price.

**Q: What if I have no good photos? Can I start without them?**

You can, but you shouldn't launch without at least six real photos. Use your own phone if you have to. Get a friend or a client with a good haircut, stand in natural light near a window, and take before/after headshots. Blur the background if needed. Photos taken on an iPhone 12 or newer look fine. The worst thing you can do is use generic stock images. Nothing kills credibility faster.
