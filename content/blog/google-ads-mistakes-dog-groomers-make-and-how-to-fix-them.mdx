## Mistake #1: Using Vague Location Targeting

If your Google Ads are showing up for searches 50 miles away, you’re wasting money. 62% of local searches convert to a visit within a day, but only if the business is *truly* in that area.

**Example:** A groomer in Austin set her location radius to 20 miles. Result? 40% of clicks were from people in San Antonio (35 miles away). No one drives that far for a dog trim.

**Fix:**
- Set your location radius to **3-5 miles** if you offer in-person services
- Use **"Address Extension"** to show your exact location in ads
- Exclude areas with low conversion rates via the "Location Exclusions" tool

Pro tip: Pair this with Google Maps ads for local visibility.

## Mistake #4: Missing Long-Tail Keywords

Competing on "dog grooming" is like fighting in a crowded room. But there’s gold in long-tail keywords like:
- "emergency dog grooming near me"
- "grooming for poodles in [City]"
- "affordable dog bath and trim"

**Fix:**
- Use **Google Keyword Planner** to find 10-15 long-tail keywords/month
- Add these to **Negative Keywords** list (e.g., exclude "cat grooming")
- Create keyword-specific ad groups (e.g., one for "senior dog grooming," another for "puppy first trim")

Pro tip: Bid 20% less on long-tail keywords—they have higher intent and lower competition.

## Mistake #5: Not Using Ad Extensions

You’re leaving value on the table if you’re not using ad extensions. One groomer I worked with added **Sitelink Extensions** to their Google Ads, which increased their ad space by 50%—and lowered their cost-per-click by 18%.

**Must-have extensions for dog groomers:**
- **Call Extensions** (for phone bookings)
- **Location Extensions** (to show your exact address)
- **App Extensions** (if you have a booking app)
- **Promotion Extensions** (e.g., "10% Off First Visit!")

Every extra line of text in your ad improves ad rank—and makes your listing look more trustworthy.

## Mistake #6: Overpaying with Manual Bidding

If you’re manually adjusting bids every day, you’re likely overpaying. The average cost-per-click for pet services is $1.50-$2.00. But many groomers are paying 50% more because they’re using outdated bidding strategies.

**Fix:**
- Switch to **Target CPA Bidding** (Google automatically optimizes for your cost-per-booking)
- Set a realistic **Target ROAS** (Revenue per Acquisition) based on your average booking value
- Use **Bid Adjustments** for devices and times (e.g., increase bids by 30% on mobile, since 45% of pet parents book via phone)

Case study: One client switched to Target CPA and reduced cost-per-booking from $3.75 to $2.10—*without losing volume*.

## Mistake #7: Not A/B Testing Everything

You wouldn’t do a haircut without seeing the result, so why run Google Ads blindly? Most groomers test *nothing*—which is why their ads stagnate.

**Test these elements weekly:**
- Headlines (try 3-5 variations per campaign)
- Call-to-action texts ("Book Now" vs "Get a Free Quote")
- Landing pages (track which pages convert best)

One studio A/B tested "Book Your Pet’s Summer Cut" vs "Get Your Dog Ready for Summer" and saw a 3x difference in bookings.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake: Bidding on the Wrong Keywords

A groomer in Denver, "The Happy Hound," was spending $1,200 a month on Google Ads and getting maybe two bookings a week. I looked at their search terms report and nearly choked on my espresso.

Seventy percent of their clicks were coming from people searching "dog grooming near me" — which sounds great until you realize those searchers click five different listings and go with the cheapest option. They were also paying for clicks on "DIY dog grooming," "how to groom my dog at home," and, I swear, "dog grooming cost." People searching for "cost" are not ready to book. They're price-checking.

**What they should have done:**
- Added "DIY," "how to," "cost," "price," and "near me" as negative keywords immediately
- Focused on intent-driven keywords like "goldendoodle grooming Denver," "puppy's first groom," "de-shedding treatment," and "nervous dog groomer"
- Used phrase match instead of broad match to maintain control

**The fix:** We rebuilt their keyword list around service-specific terms and set a strict negative keyword list. Budget stayed at $1,200.

**The outcome:** In 45 days, their cost per booking dropped from $47 to $14. Monthly bookings from ads went from 25 to 72. That's $3,800 in additional revenue per month from the same budget.

### Mistake: Sending Everyone to the Homepage

A mobile groomer in Chicago was sending every ad click to her homepage. Homepage is where you learn about who she is. Not where you book. Not where you see availability. Not where you pick the "goldendoodle full groom" package.

Her homepage had a cute photo of her van and a paragraph about how she started grooming in college. Inspiring? Sure. Converting? Not even close.

**What actually happened:** People clicked the ad, landed on the homepage, scrolled for three seconds, didn't see a big "Book Now" button, and bounced. Her bounce rate was 83%. She was paying $3.50 per click for people to look at a photo of a van.

**The fix:** I had her create three specific landing pages — one for "full grooming," one for "puppy's first groom," and one for "mobile de-shedding." Each page had a single header, three bullet points of what the service includes, the price, and a calendar booking widget from Booksy. No navigation. No blog links. No photo gallery.

**The outcome:** Bounce rate dropped to 38%. Cost per booking fell by 61% ($22 down from $56). Within 60 days, she was averaging 38 booked appointments per month from ads — up from 14.

### Mistake: Ignoring Mobile Users Entirely

A groomer in Portland was running ads that looked fine on her desktop computer. But 72% of dog owner searches happen on mobile phones. And her ad copy? "Click here to view our full service menu." On a phone. To view a PDF.

She had a "call" button hidden in her website footer. Not in the header. Not in the ad extension. Buried.

**The reality:** People searching for dog grooming on a phone at 8 a.m. on a Saturday are trying to book right now. They do not want to download a PDF. They do not want to scroll to your footer. They want to call you or book a time slot in under 30 seconds.

**The fix:**
- Added Call extensions to every ad group so the phone number appeared directly on the search result
- Wrote ad copy that said "Same-Day Appointments Available — Call Now" for mobile-preferred ads
- Simplified the mobile booking flow to three taps: service → time → confirm

**The outcome:** Call volume from ads increased by 130%. Total booking value from mobile traffic jumped from $1,500/month to $4,200/month. She also stopped paying for clicks from people who landed on that PDF and immediately left.

### Mistake: Not Tracking What Actually Happens After the Click

A groomer in Austin was looking at the Google Ads dashboard like it was the final truth. "I got 200 clicks this week!" She was thrilled. She was also getting maybe three phone calls.

Here's what was happening: Google counted the click. Google reported "conversions" anytime someone stayed on her site for more than 10 seconds. But those "conversions" weren't real. People were reading her pricing page and leaving. No call. No booking form. No booking.

She thought she was getting a 15% conversion rate. She was actually getting a 1.8% booking rate. She had no idea.

**The fix:** We set up call tracking through CallRail (about $30/month). Every ad click that led to a phone call was tracked. We also connected her Square Appointments booking data to Google Ads so we could see exactly which keywords produced actual paid appointments.

**The outcome:** In the first month, we discovered that 40% of her "conversions" were people bouncing after 12 seconds. We redirected that budget to keywords that were actually driving calls and bookings. Her real cost per booked appointment dropped from over $80 to $28. She stopped celebrating fake metrics and started celebrating real revenue.

## Why Your Google Ads Campaign Needs a Landing Page (Not a Website)

You have a website. It has five pages. There's a blog. There's a photo of your shop. There's a "About Me" section. There's a contact form hidden somewhere on the "Contact" page.

None of that belongs in front of someone who just searched "doodle grooming near me."

Here's what happens when you send paid traffic to your homepage: the visitor lands, sees a general "we groom all breeds" message, looks for a price, doesn't see one immediately, wonders if you're too expensive, and clicks back to the search results to try the next ad. You just paid $4 for someone to look at your logo.

A landing page is not a website. It's a single page with one job: get the person to book or call. No navigation bar. No blog posts. No "our story" section. Just:

- A headline that matches exactly what they searched for
- Three bullet points of what they get
- The price
- A big button or phone number

I tested this across 12 grooming clients over two years. The ones who used dedicated landing pages had an average cost per booking of $18. The ones who sent traffic to their homepage averaged $47. That's not a small difference. That's the difference between a profitable campaign and a tax write-off.

If you're using Google Ads and not building separate landing pages for at least your top three services, you are lighting money on fire. Square has a simple landing page builder for $0 extra if you're already using their payment system. Mailchimp also offers basic landing pages on their free tier. You do not need a web developer. You need a single page with a button.

## Ad Scheduling: The Most Overlooked Budget Saver

Most groomers run their ads 24/7 because they assume someone might search at 2 a.m. Someone might. But that search is not going to turn into a booking.

Here's actual data from a groomer in Nashville who was running ads around the clock: between 10 p.m. and 6 a.m., she spent $220 over three weeks. Zero bookings. Zero phone calls. Zero form submissions. $220 for nothing.

The reason is obvious if you think about it: people searching for dog grooming at 3 a.m. are either procrastinating, browsing out of boredom, or they need a groomer so urgently that they'll call whoever opens first. They are not loyal customers. They're not booking in advance. They're not converting.

**What actually works:**

Look at your Google Ads "Hours of the day" report. For most grooming businesses, 80% of conversions happen between 7 a.m. and 11 a.m., with a second smaller spike between 2 p.m. and 4 p.m. That's when people are making plans for their day, dropping off their dog on the way to work, or booking for the weekend.

Set your ad schedule to run only during those peak hours. Use a bid adjustment of +25% for the 7-11 a.m. window. Reduce bids by 50% outside of those hours if you must keep ads running.

The Nashville groomer narrowed her schedule to 7 a.m. to 12 p.m., Tuesday through Saturday. Same monthly budget. Her bookings from ads went from 18 per month to 31 per month. The only thing that changed was timing. She stopped paying for 2 a.m. window shoppers.

## Retargeting: The Obvious Play Most Groomers Skip

Someone visits your site. They look at your "goldendoodle grooming" page. They leave without booking. You assume they went somewhere else.

Maybe they did. But maybe they just got distracted. Maybe their kid started crying. Maybe they decided to check prices at two other places first.

Here's what happens next in most small businesses: nothing. You never see that person again. They book with your competitor because PetSmart ran a $10 off coupon.

Retargeting solves this. It's cheap. It works. And almost no one in the grooming space is doing it.

**Here's the setup:**

- Install the Google Ads remarketing tag on your site (30 seconds, Google gives you the code)
- Create a list of people who visited your pricing or services page but did not book
- Show them a simple ad for 7 days that says: "Still looking for a groomer? First groom 15% off — book now."
- Budget for retargeting: $3-$5 per day. No more.

A groomer in Charlotte tried this. She spent $90 total on retargeting over 30 days. That $90 brought in 14 additional bookings. Average booking value: $68. That's $952 in revenue from a $90 spend. A 10.5x return.

The ad itself was not fancy. It was a photo of a freshly groomed doodle with text overlay. No video. No animation. No "leverage" or "synergy." Just a photo and a discount.

You can run retargeting ads directly from Google Ads without using any other platform. If you're already running search ads, the retargeting campaign is a one-time setup. Most small business owners skip this because they don't know it exists or they assume it's complicated. It's neither. It's $90 for almost a thousand dollars in revenue.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I budget for Google Ads as a dog grooming business?**

Start with $500 per month for a single service in one location. Do not start with $3,000. Do not start with $200. $500 is enough to get statistically meaningful data within 30 days. I've seen solo groomers get 15-20 bookings per month on a $500 budget when the keywords and landing pages are right. If you're in a competitive city like New York or LA, you might need $800-$1,000 to get the same volume. Anything under $300 and you're not giving the system enough data to optimize.

**Q: Should I bid on my own business name?**

Only if you're already ranking organically on the first page for your business name. If a search for "Happy Paws Grooming Austin" already shows your Yelp page, your Google Business profile, and your website in the top three results, you don't need to pay for a click. In 2024, I audited a groomer who was spending $180/month bidding on their own name. They were already ranking #1 organically. That $180 was completely wasted. Turn it off and add your business name as a negative keyword.

**Q: What if a big competitor like PetSmart is outbidding me?**

Do not compete on "dog grooming near me." You will lose. PetSmart's cost per acquisition is lower than yours because they can afford to lose money on ads and make it up on retail sales. You need to compete on specific services they don't highlight: "nervous dog grooming," "puppy introduction groom," "breed-specific cuts," "mobile grooming for anxious dogs." Your advantage is that you can adjust your pricing and service to one dog at a time. They cannot. Lean into that.

**Q: How long should I run a campaign before deciding it's not working?**

Thirty days with consistent daily spending. Do not change your keywords, ad copy, or budget every three days. Let the algorithm learn. After 30 days, look at cost per booking, not cost per click. If your cost per booking is below your average service price, scale the budget. If it's above, look at your keywords and landing page first before blaming Google.

**Q: Should I use Yelp Ads or Google Ads for a grooming business?**

Google Ads first. Yelp Ads can work, but Yelp's click quality for service businesses is notoriously poor. I've had multiple clients report that 30-40% of Yelp ad clicks came from competitors or people who clicked accidentally while scrolling. Google Ads gives you more control over keywords, location, and scheduling. Add Yelp as a secondary channel only after Google Ads is profitable.

**Q: What's the one thing I should stop doing right now in my Google Ads account?**

Stop using broad match keywords unless you check your search terms report every single day. Broad match gives Google permission to show your ad for anything remotely related to "dog grooming." That includes "can I bathe my dog with human shampoo," "how to cut my dog's nails at home," and "dog adoption near me." None of those will book a grooming appointment. Switch to phrase match or exact match. You'll get fewer clicks but more appointments.
