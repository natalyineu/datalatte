### Creating a Podcast for Your Local Business

Creating a podcast can seem daunting, but it doesn't have to be. With the right equipment and a clear plan, you can start producing high-quality content in no time. Here are some tips to get you started:

* Invest in a good microphone: A good microphone will make all the difference in the quality of your audio.
* Keep it simple: Don't try to create a 2-hour podcast episode. Keep your episodes short and sweet.
* Be consistent: Consistency is key when it comes to podcasting. Try to release new episodes on a regular basis.

### Using Podcasts for Lead Generation

Once you have a podcast up and running, it's time to start thinking about lead generation. Here are some tips to get you started:

* Make sure to include a clear call-to-action in each episode: Whether it's a link to your website or a phone number, make sure listeners know how to get in touch with you.
* Use a lead magnet: A lead magnet is something of value that you can offer in exchange for a listener's contact information.
* Follow up with listeners: Make sure to follow up with listeners after each episode to build relationships and encourage them to take action.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Talking to Nobody in Particular

**The story:** A coffee shop owner in Austin, TX — let's call it "Cultivar Coffee" — decided to start a podcast in 2023. The owner, Mark, bought a $400 Shure SM7B microphone, set up in his back office, and started recording 45-minute episodes about coffee bean origins and roasting techniques.

The problem? He was trying to be the next "Coffee with Chris" — a national coffee show with 50,000 listeners. He talked about Ethiopian single-origin beans and the chemistry of pour-over extraction. His actual customers were busy parents and downtown office workers who just wanted a decent latte and a place to sit for 20 minutes.

After three months of weekly episodes, Mark had 47 total downloads across 12 episodes. He told me he was "building long-term brand awareness."

**What went wrong:** He was creating content for coffee nerds, not for his actual customers. His episodes didn't answer any question his regulars were asking. Nobody at the counter ever said, "Hey, I wonder about the altitude variance in Yirgacheffe farms."

**The fix:** We reworked the podcast into "Cultivar Mornings" — 15-minute episodes focused on exactly four things: local events happening that week, a drink recommendation for the weather, a two-minute interview with a regular customer, and one local business shout-out. No roasting curves. No extraction ratios.

**The outcome:** Within 60 days, downloads went from 47 total to 320 per episode. More importantly, Mark started running a "mention this episode" promotion — 10% off if you said the password from Tuesday's show. That generated $1,800 in incremental revenue over the first month. His cost was a $20 sign next to the register.

**The lesson:** Your podcast audience for a local business is already in your building or on your block. Talk to them, not to the internet.

### Mistake #2: Treating Audio Like It's a Radio Ad

**The story:** A pet grooming salon in Nashville — "Paws & Claws" — launched a podcast that was essentially a 20-minute commercial. The owner, Denise, would spend the first five minutes talking about nail trimming techniques, then pivot to a hard sell for their "Premium Gold Package" at $89. Every episode ended with "Call us now!"

Denise spent $2,000 on a producer through Fiverr who added intro music, sound effects, and a "buy now" button in the show notes. After eight episodes, she had exactly zero inquiries from the podcast. Zero.

**What went wrong:** The podcast sounded like a radio infomercial from 1995. Nobody wants to listen to a sales pitch while they're driving to work or folding laundry. Denise was so worried about ROI that she forgot to give any value first.

**The fix:** We killed the hard sell entirely. Replaced it with genuinely useful content: "How to brush your golden retriever so you don't get fur all over your couch." "What to do when your dog hates baths." "The three grooming mistakes that cause matting." Each episode mentioned the salon exactly once: "This tip works great with our deshedding service — we did 40 of them last week."

**The outcome:** Over the next three months, Denise's podcast became the top local pet grooming show in Nashville. She got 28 direct bookings from people who said "I heard you on the podcast." Average ticket value: $65. Total revenue from podcast listeners: $1,820. The show cost her $200/month in hosting and basic editing.

**The lesson:** Give away your expertise for free. Listeners will pay you back by showing up with their dog.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Your Existing Marketing Channels

**The story:** A fitness studio in Denver — "Mile High Barre" — started a podcast and promoted it exclusively on Instagram. They posted clips, Reels, and podcast art. After six episodes, they had 150 followers on their podcast account and about 80 listens per episode.

The owner, Rachel, was frustrated. She had 4,200 email subscribers from a giveaway she ran two years ago, and she was sending exactly zero emails about the podcast.

**What went wrong:** Rachel thought the podcast would "grow organically" if she just posted about it on social media. She ignored her biggest asset: the 4,200 people who already said "yes, I want to hear from you."

**The fix:** We sent one email to the list. Subject line: "I started a podcast. No, it's not weird." Body: Three bullet points about what the show covers, a link to the first episode, and a PS: "If you listen and reply to this email with one word you learned, I'll send you a free class pass." Open rate: 34%. Click rate: 12%. That email alone generated 504 new downloads in 48 hours.

**The outcome:** Rachel added a podcast mention to every email she sent — just a one-line P.S. She also started printing QR codes on the checkout counter that linked to the latest episode. Downloads per episode went from 80 to 650 within two months. She booked 14 new members from the podcast in Q4, worth about $5,600 in recurring monthly revenue.

**The lesson:** You already have a distribution channel. Your email list, your point-of-sale system, your Yelp page, your Google Business Profile — those are all places to put your podcast. Don't start from zero.

## Where to Find Guests (And How to Avoid Wasting Time)

The fastest way to grow a local business podcast is to interview other local business owners. Not national experts. Not thought leaders from LinkedIn. Other people in your city who have customers that overlap with yours.

A hair salon owner in Portland, OR — "Violet Door Salon" — figured this out by accident. She was booking guests by cold-DMing local influencers with 50,000 followers. Most said no. The ones who said yes wanted $500 in exchange for their time.

She pivoted and started interviewing the owners of a nearby plant shop, a bakery, and a small clothing boutique. No money exchanged hands. She promoted their businesses on her show. They promoted her on theirs. After three months, the plant shop sent her 14 new clients. The bakery ran a "mention Violet Door" discount that brought in 22 new appointments. Total cost of guest acquisition: $0.

The trick is to find businesses that serve the same customer but don't compete with you. If you're a fitness studio, interview a massage therapist, a nutritionist, a healthy meal prep service. If you're a coffee shop, interview a bookshop, a record store, a co-working space. You're creating a local recommendation network that runs on audio.

One practical way to find these guests: search your city name plus "small business" on Instagram or Google Maps. Look for places with 4.5+ stars and an owner who actually responds to reviews. Those are the people who will show up, promote the episode, and actually send business your way.

## Turning Listeners Into Customers Without Being Gross

This is where most small business owners get uncomfortable. They worry that promoting their own business will sound desperate or salesy. So they do the opposite — they never mention their product or service at all. And then they wonder why the podcast doesn't make money.

A Chicago bakery called "Sally's Sourdough" handled this well. Every episode included exactly one specific offer: a discount code that changed weekly. "Use code CRUMBLE for 15% off our weekend brioche loaf. Code expires Sunday at 5 PM."

That's it. No "please support our small business" speeches. No guilt trips about local economy. Just a clear, time-limited offer that was easy to remember. Sally tracked the codes through Square. Over six months, the podcast generated $3,800 in direct sales from those codes. Not huge, but it covered her hosting costs and her editor's fee with $2,400 left over.

Another approach: use the podcast to sell appointments, not products. A dog trainer in Denver offered "mention the podcast" and got a free 15-minute phone consultation. That turned into paid packages at $400 each. He booked nine of those in a quarter from podcast listeners.

The key is to make the call-to-action feel like a side benefit of the episode, not the main event. If your episode is about "how to keep your dog calm during fireworks," the last 30 seconds can say "and if you want personalized help, mention this episode and get a free consult." That's natural. It's not a bait-and-switch.

## Repurposing Your Podcast for the Channels You Already Use

One episode of audio can become ten pieces of content. If you're not repurposing, you're wasting time and money.

Take a pet groomer in NYC — "Brooklyn Paws." Each 20-minute episode got turned into:
- One 60-second clip for Instagram Reels (the best tip from the episode)
- Three quote cards for Instagram Stories (text on a photo)
- One blog post on their website (transcription with a few photos)
- One email to their list (three bullet points plus a link)
- One Google Business Profile update (a short post saying "new episode about [topic]")

The clips on Reels drove the most new listeners. One clip — "the mistake that's causing your dog's fur to mat" — got 12,000 views and brought in 200 new podcast downloads. That clip took about 15 minutes to produce using Descript. The owner was already recording on a $100 microphone she bought on Amazon.

She stopped running a $1,200/month Instagram ad campaign because the podcast clips were outperforming the ads on engagement and click-through rate. Not every clip went viral, but the ones that did paid for the entire podcast setup for a year.

Most podcast hosting platforms (Buzzsprout, Captivate, Transistor) offer automatic transcription for about $20/month extra. Take that transcript, run it through ChatGPT with a prompt like "turn this into a 300-word blog post for a local business website," and you have a blog post in five minutes. Do that for each episode and you build up your site's content for SEO without writing anything from scratch.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How many episodes do I need to record before I see any results?**

You need 10 to 12 episodes before you can decide if it's working. Not three. Not five. The first few episodes will be bad — your audio will be uneven, you'll ramble, you'll say "um" too much. That's normal. By episode 8, you'll find your rhythm. If after 12 episodes you have under 100 total downloads and zero customer mentions, something is wrong with your topic or your distribution, not the format.

**Q: Do I need to spend money on equipment?**

No. A $50 microphone from Amazon (the FIFINE USB mic works fine) and a quiet room is enough. Do not buy a $300 mixer or a $400 microphone until you have 20 episodes under your belt and a clear reason to upgrade. The content matters more than the sound quality. Local customers will forgive bad audio if you're giving them useful information. They won't forgive boring content.

**Q: How do I get people to actually listen?**

You don't. You get them to find it in their feed. The majority of podcast listening happens on apps like Apple Podcasts and Spotify. Make sure you're listed there (your hosting platform will do this automatically). Then promote every episode in your email list, on your Instagram, on your Google Business Profile, and on a sign in your physical location. If you're not promoting to people who already know you, nobody new will find it.

**Q: What if I don't have time to edit audio?**

Outsource it. A basic edit for a 20-minute episode costs $30 to $60 on sites like Podcastle or through a freelance editor on Upwork. That includes removing pauses, coughs, and long silences. If you can't afford that, record episodes that are 10 to 12 minutes long and edit them yourself in Audacity (free) or GarageBand (free on Mac). Long episodes with bad edits hurt your reputation more than short episodes with decent edits.

**Q: Can I just talk about my own business the whole time?**

You can. You shouldn't. If every episode is about your services and how great you are, listeners will unsubscribe after two episodes. Aim for 80% value (tips, insights, stories) and 20% promotion. Your podcast should feel like a helpful neighbor, not a salesperson at the door.

**Q: Does podcasting help with Google rankings?**

Yes, indirectly. Each episode creates a show notes page on your website with text, keywords, and links. Over 30 episodes, that's 30 new pages of content your Google Business Profile can point to. Several local businesses I've worked with saw their "near me" search rankings improve after they consistently published podcast episodes with local keywords. But don't start a podcast just for SEO. Start it because you have useful things to say to your customers.
