Customer reviews are essential to building trust and credibility with potential customers. Encourage your customers to leave reviews by:

* Providing a link to your Google My Business profile on your website or social media
* Asking customers to leave reviews in person or over the phone
* Responding promptly to all reviews, both positive and negative

**Warning:** Don't ask customers to leave fake reviews or offer incentives in exchange for reviews.

<Callout type="warning">Google has strict policies against fake reviews and may penalize your business if you're found to be engaging in this practice.</Callout>

* Posting updates about your business hours and services

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Using the Wrong Category — and Google Treats You as Invisible**

I had a groomer in Austin, Texas, call me in a panic. She'd been running Google Ads for three months, spending roughly **$1,200**, and getting almost no calls. Her GMB profile was verified, photos looked great, reviews were solid. But she'd selected "Pet Supply Store" as her primary category because she sold a few collars and shampoo bottles at checkout.

What happened: Google's algorithm matched her against people searching for "pet store near me" — not "dog groomer Austin." The algorithm doesn't guess. It serves what you tell it. So when someone typed "poodle grooming Austin," Google showed her as a pet supply store that might also groom. Her click-through rate tanked. Her ad spend went to queries she couldn't win.

The fix: Change the primary category to "Pet Groomer." Add secondary categories like "Dog Groomer" and "Cat Groomer." Then list services as actual services (not text buried in a photo caption). She added "Full groom — $65," "Bath and brush — $35," and "Nail trim — $15" directly in the services section.

Outcome: Within two weeks, her calls increased **30%**. She stopped the ads entirely and still filled her schedule. That $1,200 waste? Gone. She now books out two weeks in advance without spending a dime on Google Ads.

**Mistake #2: Ignoring the Q&A Section — Letting Strangers Control Your Narrative**

A groomer in Portland, Oregon, had a beautiful GMB profile but never touched the Q&A section. She didn't know it existed. One day, a customer asked "Do you groom aggressive dogs?" Nobody answered for four months. Then another customer answered: "No, they don't." Incorrect. They absolutely did. They had a calm, gentle approach for nervous dogs and had been doing it for years.

That wrong answer sat there for *eight months* before she discovered it. During that time, anybody searching her business saw "No, they don't" to a question about aggressive dogs. She lost at least **5–7 solid leads** per month, conservatively. At an average ticket of $70 per groom, that's **$350–490 a month** in lost revenue. Over eight months? Nearly **$3,800** gone.

The fix: Go into your GMB profile, find the Q&A section, and answer every single question yourself. Better yet — pre-load it by asking and answering your own questions. "Do you groom large breeds? Yes, we groom dogs up to 120 lbs." "Do you require vaccination records? Yes, we need proof of rabies and distemper." Control the narrative before someone else does.

Outcome: She set up seven Q&A entries in one afternoon. Within 30 days, the number of incoming phone calls from people asking these same questions dropped noticeably. Because they already read the answer. Her booking requests went up **22%** in that same period. That's roughly **$1,500 in additional monthly revenue** from answering questions people were already asking.

**Mistake #3: Treating GMB Posts Like a Social Media Feed — Posting Nothing Useful**

A groomer in Chicago was posting weekly — good for her. But it was all memes. "Monday mood: coffee and puppies." Cute. Not helpful.

Google's algorithm looks at posts for freshness signals. It rewards businesses that consistently show activity. But it also favors posts that lead to *meaningful engagement* — clicks, calls, direction requests. Memes get likes from friends and family. They do not generate booking clicks.

She was spending 20 minutes a week on posts. Nothing horrible, but nothing productive either. She couldn't point to a single appointment that came from a post.

The fix: Specific, actionable, time-bound posts. "We have two openings this Thursday for full grooms — book by noon Wednesday to secure your spot." Or "Got a doodle who hates the dryer? We use a quiet, low-heat method. Book a trial groom for $55 this week only." Include a photo of a specific dog (with owner permission) and a "Book Now" button.

Outcome: In four weeks, she saw an **18% jump in clicks to her booking link** from GMB posts. She tracked three specific appointments directly to a post about a special on matted coats. Each groom averaged $80. That's **$240 in revenue** from one post. Her weekly post time? Still 20 minutes. She just stopped wasting it.

**Mistake #4: Not Responding to Reviews — Especially the Bad Ones**

A groomer in Denver had **45 reviews, 4.6 stars**. Looks good on the surface. But seven of the negative reviews had zero responses. She'd reply to five-star reviews with "Thank you!" and ignore the three-stars because they "weren't that bad."

Here's the problem: Google sees responsiveness as a trust signal. Studies from BrightLocal (2023) show businesses that respond to every review rank higher in local packs than those that ignore even a few. More importantly, a potential customer reads a three-star review that says "They were 15 minutes late" — and sees no reply. They assume the business doesn't care. One ignored three-star review can cost you **4–6 bookings** a month from cautious customers. At $70 average, that's **$280–420/month**.

The fix: Respond to every single review within 48 hours. For positive reviews, be specific: "Thanks so much, Maria! Luna looked amazing after her spa day — glad she tolerated the cuddle session." For negative reviews, apologize, acknowledge the issue, and invite them to DM you. Never argue publicly. Example: "I'm sorry we fell short. That's not our standard. Please reach out so we can make it right." That shows future customers you're accountable.

Outcome: She spent one Sunday afternoon responding to all 45 reviews. Within three months, her rating climbed to **4.8 stars** (partly because she fixed the issues customers complained about). More importantly, her conversion rate from GMB profile view to phone call went up **15%**. That's roughly **$2,500 in monthly revenue** from people who were on the fence and decided to call.

## Why Your GMB Photos Are Probably Underperforming — and How to Fix It

I see the same photo mistake at nearly every small business I consult: too many selfies, not enough context.

One groomer in Nashville had 60 photos on her GMB profile. Forty of them were close-ups of dogs' faces. Cute? Yes. Useful? Barely. Google wants to show customers what the *experience* looks like. Your facility, your tools, your team, your before-and-after transformations. The algorithm also favors photos that get engagement — clicks, views, and "save to gallery" actions.

Here's what actually works based on what I've tested across multiple clients:

- **Before-and-after shots**: A matted golden doodle in rough shape — then a clean, fluffy version of the same dog. These get **3x more views** than standard portraits. Tag them with "before groom" and "after groom" in the file name. Yes, Google reads file names.
- **Your workspace**: A clean, organized grooming table, properly sanitized tools, and a tidy waiting area. Customers want to see where their pet will spend two hours. Show them it's not a chaotic shed.
- **Your team**: A photo of you or your staff holding a calm dog. Not a posed selfie. Show competence. People trust a face they've seen.
- **Your equipment**: A high velocity dryer, a hydraulic table, a proper kennel setup. This signals you're serious and professional.

One groomer in San Antonio who implemented this saw her photo views go from 200 per month to **1,800 per month** within 60 days. More importantly, the number of direction requests from her GMB profile went up **40%**. Photos drive action.

**The dollar impact**: She added one simple before-and-after carousel of three dogs. Those three posts directly led to **12 new customer inquiries** over the next month, per her tracking. At an average first-visit spend of $85, that's **$1,020 in new revenue** from one afternoon of photo work.

## Booking Software That Doesn't Make You Want to Throw Your Phone Across the Room

You need a way for customers to book without calling you at 7 AM. GMB has a "Book Now" button that integrates with third-party software. If you don't set this up, customers click it, see nothing, and bounce to your competitor who uses Booksy or Square Appointments.

I've tested five booking platforms with grooming clients. Here's my honest take:

**Booksy** — Best for service-based businesses that need online booking, reminders, and payment processing. Pricing starts around **$30/month** for the basic plan. The integration with GMB is seamless. Customers click "Book Now" on your profile, pick a service, choose a time, pay a deposit. No phone tag.

**Square Appointments** — Free tier exists if you only need one staff member and basic features. GMB integration works well. The downside: Square's free tier has limits on calendar sync and reporting. If you have two groomers working, you'll likely need the **$50/month** plan. I've seen groomers in Phoenix and Atlanta use Square and report a **25% reduction in no-shows** due to automated text reminders.

**Vagaro** — More expensive ($80–100/month) but offers POS, inventory, and client notes. If you also sell retail products (shampoos, treats, etc.), this saves time. One groomer in Denver told me Vagaro's recurring appointment feature alone saved her **6 hours per week** of scheduling time.

**Vetstoria** — Expensive ($100+/month) but specifically designed for pet businesses. Integrates with GMB, sends pre-appointment questionnaires (vaccination records, behavioral notes). Overkill if you're solo. Worth it if you have 3+ groomers.

**The real cost**: A groomer in Phoenix was losing **8–10 calls per week** to voicemail because she couldn't answer while grooming. Each missed call represented roughly **$70–85 in potential revenue**. That's **$560–850 per week** in lost opportunity. She set up Square Appointments for $50/month and added "Book Now" to her GMB profile. Within 30 days, she had **22 online bookings** — appointments she would have missed entirely. That's **$1,870 in secured revenue** for $50/month. She never answered another call during a groom.

**Recommendation**: Start with Booksy or Square Appointments free tier. Test for 30 days. Track how many bookings come through the GMB button. If you get at least 10 bookings in that month, keep it. Paying for $30/month is nothing if it saves you from losing a single $85 groom.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does Google My Business even matter if I get most of my clients from word-of-mouth?**

Yes, because word-of-mouth still ends with someone typing your name into Google. When a neighbor says "I use the groomer on Oak Street," the first thing 70% of people do is search that name on Google. If your profile is incomplete, shows wrong hours, or has unanswered questions, they'll pick the groomer with a complete, responsive profile — even if they were referred to you. Referrals are a pipeline. Your GMB profile is the front door. If the door's stuck, people walk to the next house.

**Q: How do I handle a fake or unfair bad review on Google?**

You can flag it to Google, but they rarely remove reviews unless they violate clear policies (hate speech, spam, fake names). The faster path is to respond professionally, then get more positive reviews to dilute the impact. One unfair one-star review with a response that says "We're sorry you felt that way, though we don't recall this visit" looks fine next to 30 five-star reviews. Focus on generating real, specific positive reviews from your actual customers. I've seen a business with a 3.8 rating and 5 reviews lose out to one with a 4.5 and 50 reviews, even when the lower-rated business was better. Volume and responsiveness matter more than perfection.

**Q: Do I need to run Google Ads on top of GMB optimization?**

Not until your GMB is fully optimized. If your profile isn't complete, any ad spend is partially wasted. Start by fixing everything in this guide. Then, if you still have empty appointment slots, consider a small test: $100/month on "pet groomer [your city]" keyword. Track calls from the ad separately. If you get three new clients in the first month, that's roughly $255 in revenue (at $85 per first visit). That's a 2.5x return on your $100 spend. If you don't, stop the ads and focus on improving your profile further. Most business owners can get 80% of the benefit from a free GMB before needing to spend a dime.

**Q: How often should I post on GMB?**

At minimum, twice per week. Once is better than zero. Consistency is what Google rewards, not volume. If you post five times one week and then nothing for a month, your freshness score drops. Set a recurring reminder: Monday morning, post a "what's available this week" update. Thursday afternoon, post a before-and-after. Use a photo you already took during a groom. The whole process should take 10 minutes, not 30. If you have nothing to say, repost a previous popular update from 3 months ago — Google still counts it as activity.

**Q: Should I pay for Yelp advertising?**

Generally no, not until you have at least 10 reviews and a 4.0+ rating on Yelp. Yelp's ad system is expensive — often $300–600/month minimum — and their lead quality varies widely. I've seen a groomer in Dallas spend **$500/month on Yelp ads** and get exactly **two bookings** in three months. That's $250 per booking. Meanwhile, her GMB was free and generating five times that. If you want to try Yelp, claim your free page, respond to every review, and invest in great photos. Only consider their ads if you have surplus booking capacity and can track the ROI directly.

**Q: Can't I just have my nephew set this up once and forget about it?**

No, because GMB rewards ongoing activity. Claiming and verifying is step one. After that, the algorithm watches for updates, review responses, photo additions, and Q&A activity. If your profile goes dark for 6 months, it drops in ranking. Your "nephew" (we all have one) can set it up, but someone needs to spend 15 minutes per week maintaining it. That's not a hire — that's a task you can do while waiting for coffee to brew. Put it in your calendar: every Tuesday, 15 minutes. Respond to reviews, post one update, check Q&A. That's it.
