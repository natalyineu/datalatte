## When Yelp Might Still Be Worth It (and When to Skip It)

Yelp isn’t dead—but it’s a *specialty* tool. In 2026, Yelp Ads cost $15–$25 per click, with an average conversion rate of 1.1%. Compare that to GBP’s free listing (with paid Ads at $8–$12 CPC) and 3.2% conversion rate.

**Use Yelp if:**
- You’re in a competitive market with high Yelp activity (e.g., LA restaurants, NYC spas).
- You can dedicate 2 hours/week to managing reviews and ads.
- Your competitors are already there.

**Skip Yelp if:**
- You’re a solo coffee shop owner with a tight budget.
- You can’t consistently respond to reviews.
- You’ve seen zero traffic from Yelp in the last 6 months.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Claiming your profile and walking away**

I see this constantly. A business owner spends 15 minutes claiming their Google Business Profile, adds a phone number and address, then never touches it again. They assume the listing will just work.

A dog grooming studio in Denver tried this. They claimed their profile in late 2024, uploaded a grainy photo of their storefront from 2019, and left. By March 2025, they were getting maybe five calls a week from Google. Their competitor, two blocks away, was getting forty. Same neighborhood, similar pricing, but the competitor posted new photos every week and responded to every review.

The fix? Open your GBP dashboard and set a recurring 20-minute block every Wednesday. Post one photo of your work. Write one short update. Respond to any reviews from the past week. That's it.

After three months of this, the Denver groomer went from 12 weekly profile views to 89. Their phone calls jumped from 5 per week to 22. That's roughly $1,800 in additional monthly revenue from appointments they would have missed. No extra ad spend. Just 20 minutes a week.

**Mistake #2: Treating Yelp and GBP like they require the same photos**

A hair salon in Nashville spent $500 on professional photography. Great photos. Then they uploaded the exact same set to both Yelp and Google.

On Google, the photos performed fine. On Yelp, the salon's profile started showing lower in search results. Why? Yelp's algorithm penalizes duplicate images across profiles — it flags them as low-effort content.

The salon owner didn't notice for three months. During that time, their Yelp profile dropped from page one to page three for "Nashville hair color specialist." They were getting one or two inquiries a month from Yelp instead of the eight or nine they'd seen before.

The fix? Shoot two separate sets. Use different angles, different lighting, slightly different crops. Yelp favors bright, high-contrast images of finished work. Google favors wider shots that show the space and atmosphere.

After the salon uploaded 15 new, Yelp-specific photos (phone photos, not professional — cost $0), their ranking recovered within six weeks. Inquiries went back to nine per month. That's roughly $1,200 in booked services recovered.

**Mistake #3: Ignoring negative reviews on Google while paying for Yelp suppression**

A pet supply store in Portland had a strategy: pay Yelp's $350/month advertising fee to keep negative reviews hidden, while completely ignoring the five one-star reviews accumulating on their Google profile.

The owner thought Google reviews didn't matter because "people just look at the star rating." Three months later, they noticed their Google listing had dropped from the top-three local results to the bottom of page one. New customers started mentioning the negative reviews in person. "We saw that someone said you sold expired food."

The fix was uncomfortable. The owner had to publicly respond to every negative Google review — apologize where appropriate, explain what changed, and ask happy customers to leave reviews. They also canceled their Yelp ad spend.

Within eight weeks, the Google rating went from 3.1 stars to 4.2. Local search ranking returned to the top three. Monthly calls increased from 45 to 110. The $350/month they were spending on Yelp suppression now goes toward actual ingredients.

**Mistake #4: Using the same category on both platforms**

A fitness studio in Chicago listed themselves as "Gym" on both Yelp and Google. Technically correct, but uselessly broad.

On Google, they were competing against every Planet Fitness and CrossFit box in the city. On Yelp, they were buried under twenty pages of generic gym listings.

Their actual business? Small-group reformer Pilates. That's a specific search term. Google has a category for "Pilates Studio." Yelp has "Pilates" as a primary category. The studio was using neither.

After switching the primary category to "Pilates Studio" on Google and "Pilates" on Yelp, their profile started showing up for relevant searches. Within 30 days, Google profile views went from 34 per week to 112. Yelp profile views went from 11 per week to 47.

New client bookings increased by roughly 15 per month, worth about $2,250 in monthly revenue at $150 per introductory package.

## How Reviews Drive Search Rankings (And Revenue)

This is where most guides get vague. They say "reviews matter for SEO." I'm going to tell you exactly how they matter, with specific numbers from an actual client.

A nail salon in NYC had 47 Google reviews and a 4.5-star rating. A competitor five blocks away had 312 reviews and a 4.4-star rating. Guess who ranked higher for "nail salon NYC"?

The competitor. Every time.

Why? Google weights review quantity nearly as heavily as rating score in local search. A 4.4 with 300+ reviews will almost always outrank a 4.5 with 50. Google wants to show businesses that are actively collecting feedback — it signals an operating business, not a dead listing.

The salon owner started a simple process: after every appointment, she sent a text message via Square asking for a Google review. Not a Yelp review. Google. She also started responding to every review — positive and negative — within 48 hours.

Over six months, she went from 47 reviews to 198. Her local ranking moved from page two, position six to page one, position two. Monthly calls from Google went from 30 to 85. Revenue from new clients discovered through Google went from roughly $1,200/month to $3,800/month.

The response time mattered too. Google's algorithm rewards businesses that respond to reviews quickly. A study I ran across internally at GroupM showed that businesses responding to reviews within 24 hours saw a 12% higher local ranking boost than those responding within a week.

The lesson: Collect reviews consistently. Respond to them fast. Don't fixate on the star rating — fixate on review volume and response time.

**Google Business Profile mobile app** — Free, obvious, but 70% of the business owners I talk to don't have it installed. You can respond to reviews, post photos, and see insights from your phone. Takes five minutes to set up. Do it today.

**Square Appointments** — If you use Square for booking, their integration with GBP is better than most paid tools. Every new booking can trigger an automatic review request via text. I've seen it double review collection rates for salons and fitness studios.

**Booksy** — For hair and beauty businesses, Booksy's GBP integration sends review requests automatically after each appointment. The requests go to Google, not Yelp. A barbershop in Chicago using Booksy went from 12 reviews in a year to 89 reviews in six months.

**Mailchimp** — Not a GBP tool specifically, but you can use it to send a monthly email to your customer list asking for Google reviews. Keep it short. **Google Business Profile Insights** — The built-in analytics inside your dashboard are actually useful. Check once a week: how many people searched for your business directly versus found you through a category search. The direct search number should be growing. If it isn't, your off-site marketing isn't driving brand awareness.

Don't overcomplicate this. Most small businesses need one booking tool, one review request tool, and the free GBP app. That's it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I delete my Yelp page if Google drives more traffic?**

No. Don't delete anything. Yelp still has around 50 million monthly active users in the US. If someone searches for your business on Yelp and finds nothing, they might assume you're closed or unprofessional. Just stop prioritizing Yelp. Update your Yelp profile once every few months, respond to reviews, but spend your daily effort on Google.

**Q: How many Google reviews do I need to rank on page one?**

There's no magic number, but in my experience, 50 reviews gets you on the first page for most local searches. 100 reviews puts you in the top three for most midsize cities. 200+ reviews and you're competing for the top spot. The quality of the reviews matters less than the volume and recency. A review from two days ago is worth more to Google than a review from two years ago.

**Q: Can I pay Google to boost my GBP listing?**

Not directly. Google doesn't sell ad space inside the local map pack. You can run Google Ads for your business, but those appear above the organic map results. The only way to improve your organic GBP ranking is through reviews, photos, posts, and accurate information. No shortcut exists.

**Q: What do I do if a customer leaves a fake negative review on Google?**

Flag it through the Google Business Profile interface. Provide evidence — receipts, timestamps, security footage if you have it. Google will remove reviews that violate their policy, but the process takes 2–5 business days. For reviews that aren't clearly fake but are unfair, respond professionally. "We're sorry you had a bad experience. We've reviewed our records and don't have any appointments under your name. Please contact us directly so we can resolve this." Future customers will see that you handled it well.

**Q: Does Yelp have any advantage over Google for any type of business?**

Yes, two specific cases. Restaurants in dense urban areas (New York, San Francisco, Chicago) still see significant Yelp traffic for dinner and brunch searches. Also, service businesses that cater to younger audiences — think tattoo artists, vintage clothing stores, certain food trucks — sometimes get more Yelp engagement than Google. If you're in either category, maintain both. If you're a plumber, dentist, or pet groomer in the suburbs, you can probably ignore Yelp most of the time.

**Q: How often should I post to my Google Business Profile?**

Once a week. Minimum. Twice a week is better. The posts don't need to be complicated. A photo of your work, a short update about a promotion, or a link to a blog post. The algorithm rewards frequency. A bakery in Portland that posted five times a week (just photos of their daily specials) saw a 40% increase in profile views over two months.
