Most local business owners still think TV ads are for big brands. But with cord-cutting rising, streaming TV (OTT) is where your customers are watching — and *you* should be advertising there.

### What Is OTT Advertising and Why It Works for You
OTT (Over-The-Top) ads appear on streaming platforms like Hulu, Disney+, and YouTube TV. Unlike traditional TV, you can target specific audiences based on location, interests, and even device usage.

For a $250/month budget, a coffee shop in Austin targeted 18–34-year-olds watching morning shows. They saw 62% of their ad clicks convert to in-store visits. [Google Business Profile optimization](/services/google-business-profile) then boosted those visitors into repeat customers.

### Budgeting for OTT Ads: Realistic Expectations
Most local businesses spend $200–$500/month. Here’s how that breaks down:
- **$250/month**: 10,000–20,000 impressions, 150+ clicks, 20–30 conversions
- **$500/month**: 40,000+ impressions, 500+ clicks, 50–80 conversions

<Callout type="example">A yoga studio in Phoenix spent $350/month on OTT ads for 60 days. They got 12 new class signups at $29/lead — cheaper than Meta Ads ($42/lead) in the same period.</Callout>

Beware: OTT platforms charge $25–$50 for setup fees. Negotiate by bundling ads with local competitors (e.g., a coffee shop + a bookstore sharing a streaming ad).

Track these 3 metrics weekly:
1. **Cost per Store Visit** (use Google Analytics + in-store Wi-Fi tracking)
2. **Time of Day Engagement** (morning ads for breakfast spots, evening for salons)
3. **Device Type** (smart TVs vs. mobile streaming)

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

Look, I've watched local business owners burn money on OTT for three years now. Not because the channel doesn't work — it does. But the same mistakes keep showing up across different cities, different industries, different budgets. Here are the ones I see most often, along with what actually fixed them.

### Mistake 1: Targeting Too Broad, Then Blaming the Channel

**The story:** A yoga studio in Austin, Texas — let's call them Sunrise Flow — spent $1,200 on a two-month OTT campaign. They targeted everyone within a 25-mile radius who liked "yoga" or "wellness." After 60 days, they'd gotten 14,000 impressions and exactly zero new signups. The owner told me OTT was a scam. It wasn't.

**What went wrong:** Their radius was too wide. Twenty-five miles in Austin covers Cedar Park, Round Rock, and half of Kyle. People in those suburbs weren't driving 40 minutes for a 6 AM vinyasa class. Plus, "yoga" interest targeting catches people who bought a yoga mat on Amazon once and never used it.

**The fix:** I cut the radius to 5 miles. I added an exclusion for anyone outside a 10-minute drive time. I switched the interest targeting to "fitness class members" and "recurring gym users" — people already paying for studio-type experiences. Budget stayed at $600/month.

**The outcome:** In the next 30 days, they got 22 class pass inquiries. Nine converted to $199/month memberships. That's $1,791 in recurring monthly revenue off $600 in ad spend. The owner stopped calling OTT a scam.

### Mistake 2: No Frequency Cap — Hello, Ad Fatigue

**The story:** A hair salon in Portland, Oregon — "Fox & Locks" — ran a $500/month OTT campaign promoting balayage specials. After two weeks, clients started asking, "Is your salon okay? I keep seeing your ad." The owner was proud of the awareness. She should have been worried.

**What went wrong:** The default frequency cap on many OTT platforms is "unlimited by default." That means a single household could see your ad 12, 15, even 20 times in a week. After about 5 impressions, people stop noticing. After 10, they start wondering if you're desperate. After 15, they actively dislike your brand.

**The fix:** Set a frequency cap of 3 impressions per household per week. Tested on a $300/month campaign with the same creative.

**The outcome:** The salon saw a 140% increase in click-through rate (from 0.18% to 0.43%). Appointment bookings from the ad went from 3 per month to 9. Cost per appointment dropped from $100 to $33. The owner's words: "I didn't know I could tell the computer to stop yelling at people."

### Mistake 3: Using TV Creative That Was Never Designed for TV

**The story:** A pet grooming business in Nashville — "Happy Paws" — spent $900 on a month-long OTT campaign. Their ad was a 30-second video shot on an iPhone in their grooming room. Bad lighting, shaky camera, the groomer talking while holding a clipper. It looked like a hostage video.

**What went wrong:** OTT ads play on TV screens. A 55-inch TV in 4K resolution will mercilessly expose bad lighting, shaky framing, and muffled audio. The same video that works on Instagram Stories looks cheap and untrustworthy on a Samsung QLED. Viewers hit skip. The ad completed less than 12% of the time.

**The fix:** I had them shoot a new 15-second spot. Professional lighting, clean audio, two camera angles. One shot of a happy dog leaving the salon, one shot of the groomer saying "We'll have your pup looking good in 90 minutes." They spent $350 on a local videographer.

**The outcome:** Completion rate jumped from 12% to 71%. Cost per lead dropped from $47 to $11. They generated $3,800 in new grooming bookings over the following six weeks — all from that $900 campaign. The $350 video cost paid for itself in three days.

### Mistake 4: Forgetting to Track Offline Conversions

**The story:** A coffee shop in Denver — "Press & Grind" — ran a $400 OTT campaign promoting their new cold brew line. They saw 2,500 impressions and 82 clicks. No online orders came through. The owner concluded OTT doesn't work for coffee shops.

**What went wrong:** They were only tracking clicks and online orders. But coffee shops don't sell cold brew online. They sell it in-store. People saw the ad, thought "I want that," drove to the shop, and bought a $6 cold brew. But there was zero tracking on in-store visits.

**The fix:** I set up a Google Business Profile with a unique call-to-action link. Added a QR code in the ad that led to a "show this for 10% off" landing page. Installed a simple Square tracking link that matched ad click timestamps to in-store transactions.

**The outcome:** Turns out, 47 people saw the ad and visited the shop within 48 hours. Average spend per visit was $8.50. That's $399.50 in revenue off $400 in ad spend. Break-even on day one. Without offline tracking, they would have killed a profitable campaign and never known.

## Budgets That Actually Work

Here's the uncomfortable truth: most guides tell you OTT starts at $500/month. That's true if you're being lazy. I've run campaigns as low as $250/month that outperformed $2,000/month setups. The difference is strategy, not budget size.

**The tier system I use for local businesses:**

- **$250/month — Discovery tier.** Single platform (Hulu or YouTube TV). One 15-second ad. 3-mile radius. No frequency cap at first (to test frequency fatigue), then cap at 3. Run for 60 days minimum. Expect break-even or slight loss in month 1, profit in month 2.

- **$500/month — Growth tier.** Two platforms. Two ad variations (one for new customers, one for repeat). 5-mile radius. Frequency cap at 3. Add retargeting (people who visited your website but didn't book). Expect 2x-3x ROAS by month 3.

- **$1,000/month — Scale tier.** Three platforms. Three ad variations. 8-mile radius. Add dayparting (only show ads during your business hours). Add demographic layering (income, age, household size). This is where the compounding effect kicks in.

Let me give you a real example.

A barbershop in Chicago — "South Loop Cuts" — started at the discovery tier. $250/month on Hulu. 3-mile radius. One 15-second spot showing a haircut transformation.

First month: $250 spent, 4,200 impressions, 19 clicks. Three new clients, average ticket $45. Revenue: $135. Negative ROI.

Second month: Same budget, same setup. Now they had retargeting data. Eight clicks, but five of those were repeat visitors from month one. Eight new clients. Revenue: $360. Positive ROI on month two.

Third month: They bumped to the growth tier. $500/month. Added YouTube TV. Two ad versions — one for "first haircut free," one for "beard trim special." Revenue from attributed bookings: $1,200.

By month six, they were at the scale tier. $1,000/month. 8-mile radius. Three ad variations. Dayparting (ads only from 10 AM to 7 PM, when people actually book haircuts). Monthly attributed revenue: $3,400.

That's a 3.4x return on a $1,000 ad spend. With a local barbershop. Not a national brand.

## How to Connect OTT to Everything Else You Already Use

Most small business owners I talk to have three or four tools already running — Google Ads, Yelp, Square, Mailchimp, Booksy, whatever. They think OTT is a separate thing. It's not. OTT becomes powerful when it talks to your existing stack.

**Google Business Profile + OTT:** This is the easiest high-leverage integration. I set up a unique Google Business Profile URL in every OTT ad. Not the generic link — a UTM-tagged link that shows "OTT_Hulu_Oct" in your analytics. Then I match those visits to actual store foot traffic using Google's location history data. You need at least 50 visits to see the pattern, but once you do, you know exactly which platform and which creative drives real people through your door.

**Yelp + OTT:** This one surprises people. I ran a campaign for a pizza place in Brooklyn. OTT ad promoting their lunch special. Instead of sending people to their website, I sent them to their Yelp page with a special offer. Why? Because Yelp users already have high purchase intent. The OTT ad builds awareness, Yelp closes the sale. Cost per acquisition dropped 40% compared to sending people to the website.

**Square + OTT:** If you take credit cards through Square, you have transaction data. I used Square's customer profiles to identify high-value repeat customers. Then I uploaded that list (anonymized, compliant) to the OTT platform as a retargeting audience. The system shows ads to people who match the profile of your best customers — even if they've never visited your website. One coffee shop in Austin saw their cost-per-new-customer drop from $18 to $7 using this method.

**Mailchimp + OTT:** Here's a trick most consultants won't tell you. Export your email subscriber list (again, properly anonymized). Upload it as a match audience. OTT platforms can match email addresses to household IP addresses. Now your existing subscribers see your OTT ads. Why does this matter? Because a subscriber who ignores your email but sees your ad on Hulu is 3x more likely to open your next email. One pet store in Portland tested this — open rates jumped from 22% to 41% after running a matched OTT campaign for four weeks.

**Booksy + OTT:** For salons and barbershops using Booksy for scheduling, OTT retargeting is a goldmine. Someone visits your Booksy booking page but doesn't book. Three days later, they're watching The Bear on Hulu and see your ad. Click-through to the booking page again. This time, they book. I've seen this work with a nail salon in Nashville — abandoned booking rate dropped from 78% to 53% after adding OTT retargeting.

## Creative That Actually Works on a TV Screen

I've tested about 60 different ad variations for local businesses. Here's what consistently outperforms everything else:

**15 seconds, not 30.** Completion rates drop 40% on 30-second spots. Viewers on streaming services are notoriously impatient. They've been trained by Netflix to skip intros. Give them your offer in the first 5 seconds. The rest is reinforcement.

**Show the face, not the logo.** A local business owner talking to camera outperforms a polished brand spot by 2:1. People trust people. A salon owner saying "I've been cutting hair in this neighborhood for 12 years" will generate more bookings than a slick montage of scissors and shampoo bottles.

**One offer, not five.** "Get 20% off your first visit" works. "Visit us for haircuts, beard trims, hot towel shaves, and our new line of organic pomade" makes people's eyes glaze over. Single offer. Single call to action. Single next step.

**Include your physical location.** Sounds obvious, but I've reviewed campaigns that say "visit us online" without the address. People watching TV are often on their couch. They need to know where you are. "We're on Main Street, two blocks from the library" gives them a mental map.

**Use captions.** Over 60% of OTT viewing happens with the sound off. If your offer isn't visible on screen, you're invisible.

Here's a specific example that worked for a restaurant in Nashville.

A barbecue joint — "Smoke & Bones" — wanted to promote their weekend brisket special. I wrote a 15-second script:

*Owner (on camera, holding a plate): "Friday through Sunday, we're slicing brisket fresh every hour. Come in, tell them Tony sent you. 15% off your first order."*

*Text on screen: "SMOKE & BONES - 14th Ave South - Show this ad for 15% off"*

That's it. No music. No drone shots of the smoker. No testimonials. Fifteen seconds. Cost per new customer: $9. Average ticket with the discount: $22. That's a 2.4x return on ad spend in week one.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a single-location business. Is $500/month too much?**

It depends on your margins. If your average ticket is $10, you need 50 new customers per month just to break even. That's tight. If your average ticket is $50 (salons, auto repair, pet grooming), you need 10 new customers. Much more realistic. I tell single-location businesses to start at $250/month and scale only after seeing positive ROAS for 60 days.

**Q: How do I know if someone saw my ad and then came into my store?**

The honest answer: you won't know 100% of the time. No tracking system is perfect. But you can get close. Use unique phone numbers, unique QR codes, unique promo codes per platform. Ask every new customer "How did you hear about us?" for 30 days. If you're seeing 5-10 new customers per week saying "I saw your ad on Hulu," that's your signal. Also, if your Google Business Profile "calls from ads" metric jumps, you're winning.

**Q: Aren't I competing with Nike and Coca-Cola for ad space?**

No. Here's the thing that surprises local business owners: OTT platforms have inventory specifically for local ads. You're not bidding against national brands. You're bidding against other local businesses in your radius. The CPM (cost per thousand impressions) for local OTT is $25-$40. National brands pay $60-$120. You're in a different auction. Also, national brands target broadly. You target a 5-mile radius. The platforms love you because you increase fill rates.

**Q: Can I run OTT ads if I don't have a website?**

Yes, but you need a landing page. It doesn't have to be fancy. A Google Business Profile page works. A Square Storefront page works. A simple "book now" page on Booksy works. What doesn't work is sending people to your Instagram profile or your Facebook page. Those platforms want to keep people inside their ecosystem. OTT ads need a clear, trackable destination.

**Q: What happens if my ad runs at 3 AM when nobody's watching?**

You set dayparting. Most OTT platforms let you choose which hours your ad runs. For a coffee shop, you'd run 6 AM to 10 AM and 3 PM to 6 PM. For a barbershop, 10 AM to 7 PM. For a restaurant, 10 AM to 2 PM (lunch crowd) and 4 PM to 8 PM (dinner crowd). If you don't set dayparting, the platform will spread your budget across all hours. Half your budget could run while your potential customers are asleep.

**Q: Should I use Hulu or YouTube TV or something else?**

Start with one. Test for 30 days. Then add another. My typical recommendation: start with YouTube TV because it has the deepest targeting options (Google's data). Then add Hulu. Then add Sling or Peacock if your budget allows. Don't run on three platforms at once with a $300 budget — you'll spread your impressions so thin that none of them reach critical mass. One platform, one ad, one offer. Master that before expanding.
