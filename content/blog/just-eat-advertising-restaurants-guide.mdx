## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Running Promoted Placements Without a Daily Cap

**The story:** A BBQ joint in Austin, Texas—let's call it "Smoke Ring"—had a decent lunch crowd but wanted to dominate dinner delivery. The owner read about Promoted Placements and turned them on with the default settings. He didn't set a daily maximum bid cap. Just Eat's algorithm happily charged him $4.80 per click for the first week. He spent $1,200 before he checked his account. Most of those clicks came between 10 PM and 2 AM, when his kitchen was closed. He paid $1,200 for people to see a "closed" sign.

**What went wrong:** Just Eat's Promoted Placement system, like Uber Eats and DoorDash, uses an auction model. If you don't set a maximum cost-per-click (CPC) cap, the platform will charge whatever the market bears in that moment. Late at night, with fewer restaurants bidding, your CPC can spike because the algorithm tries to "win" every auction. You're paying peak prices for off-peak traffic.

**The fix:** Set a hard CPC cap at $1.50 for the first 30 days. Then adjust based on data. Also set a daily budget of $25–$50. This limits your downside while you figure out when your actual customers order. Run Promoted Placements only during your operating hours. Just Eat lets you schedule ad delivery. Use it.

**The outcome:** After resetting with a $1.25 CPC cap and a $35 daily budget limited to 11 AM–9 PM, Smoke Ring spent $490 over two weeks. They generated 47 orders from those clicks. Average order value was $28. That's $1,316 in revenue from $490 in ad spend—a 2.7x return. Not spectacular, but profitable. And they weren't paying for 2 AM window-shoppers.

### Mistake 2: Running a 50% Off Offer With No Minimum

**The story:** A taco spot in Portland, Oregon—"Cartel Tacos"—wanted to break into the top 10 of Just Eat's rankings. They offered 50% off all menu items, no minimum order. The offer ran for two weeks. They got 183 orders. Average order value dropped to $9.40—because people ordered a single taco and a water. After Just Eat's commission (roughly 30% on the discounted subtotal), Cartel Tacos netted $1.80 per order. They lost money on every single delivery. The owner told me, "I thought volume would make up for it."

**What went wrong:** Deep discounts on Just Eat attract the wrong customers. You get deal-seekers who order the cheapest possible combination and never come back. The platform's algorithm does rank offers higher when they're aggressive—but it also trains your customer base to expect 50% off. When you stop the promo, your orders drop off a cliff. I've seen this pattern at three different restaurants. It's a death spiral.

**The fix:** Run a "bundle" offer instead of a percentage discount. "Buy one burrito, get a second for $5." Or "Free chips and queso with orders over $25." This raises the average order value instead of crushing it. The minimum order threshold prevents the $9.40 disaster. For new restaurants on Just Eat, I recommend a 20% discount with a $20 minimum for the first 30 days. Then shift to a loyalty offer (free item after 5 orders) to retain the customers who actually like your food.

**The outcome:** Cartel Tacos switched to a "Free nachos with any entree over $15" offer. Average order value climbed to $22.40. The cost of the nachos was about $1.80 in food cost. They spent 90 days running this offer. Revenue from Just Eat went from losing $330 in that two-week disaster to generating $2,100 in profit over three months. Not a home run, but they stopped bleeding money.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Your Menu Pricing for Delivery

**The story:** A pizza place in Chicago—"Southside Slice"—had a $14 pepperoni pizza on their in-house menu. They listed it for the same $14 on Just Eat. After Just Eat's 31% commission and the cost of the pizza ($4.20 in ingredients), they made $5.46 per order before labor and overhead. That's a 39% margin on the pizza itself. The owner thought that was fine until he realized delivery orders were cannibalizing his higher-margin in-house business. His dining room traffic dropped 22% in 6 months. People were ordering delivery instead of coming in, and his per-order profit was lower.

**What went wrong:** Most restaurant owners don't account for the fact that delivery customers don't buy drinks, appetizers, or dessert at the same rate as in-house diners. A $14 pizza in-house often comes with a $3 soda and maybe a $6 appetizer. That $14 pizza on Just Eat is $14—period. The platform's commission eats into a higher percentage of the total transaction because there's no add-on revenue. Your menu needs to be priced higher on delivery platforms to account for this.

**The fix:** Raise your Just Eat prices by 15–20% across the board. Then run a "Free delivery" offer or a "10% off first order" to offset the sticker shock. Customers are used to delivery platforms having different pricing. They don't compare your Just Eat menu to your in-house menu line by line. They compare your Just Eat price to the pizza place next door on Just Eat. Keep your in-house pricing lower and make that the value proposition for dining in.

**The outcome:** Southside Slice raised their Just Eat prices by 18%. The $14 pizza became $16.50. They added a "Free large soda with any pizza" offer. Their in-house menu stayed at $14. Within 60 days, in-house traffic recovered to previous levels. The delivery orders stayed roughly the same volume, but per-order profit went from $5.46 to $8.70. That's a 59% improvement. The owner told me, "I should have done this years ago."

## How Promoted Placements Interact With Your Other Marketing Channels

Here's something most guides skip: Promoted Placements on Just Eat don't exist in a vacuum. They interact with your Google Business Profile, your Yelp reviews, your Instagram ads, and your email list. If you're not coordinating these, you're wasting money.

**The Google Business Profile connection:** When someone searches "[your city] pizza delivery" on Google, they see a map pack with three restaurants. Those rankings are influenced by review volume, response rate, and listing completeness. I worked with a restaurant in Denver that spent $800/month on Just Eat Promoted Placements but had a Google Business Profile with zero photos and one review from 2019. A competitor with a fully optimized profile and 45 reviews was ranking in the top 3 for "Denver Thai food" without spending a dime on Promoted Placements. The fix: Claim and optimize your Google Business Profile before you spend on Just Eat ads. Add photos of your food, respond to every review, and make sure your hours are correct. This costs nothing but time and pays for itself in organic discovery.

**The Yelp factor:** Yelp users are notoriously skeptical of promoted content. If a customer clicks your Promoted Placement on Just Eat but sees a 3.5-star rating with unanswered negative reviews, they'll bounce. A Nashville hot chicken spot found that their conversion rate on Promoted Placements was 8% when their Just Eat rating was 4.2 stars or above, but dropped to 3% when it fell below 4.0. The fix: Actively manage your Just Eat reviews. Respond to every review within 48 hours. If a customer had a bad experience, offer a solution publicly, then move the conversation to DMs. A 0.3-star improvement in your rating can double your conversion rate on paid placements.

**The email list shortcut:** If you have a customer email list (from Square, Toast, or Mailchimp), use it to amplify your Just Eat presence. Send a weekly email with a direct link to your Just Eat page. This drives external traffic to your Just Eat listing, which the algorithm interprets as demand. Higher external traffic signals to Just Eat that your restaurant is popular, which can improve your organic ranking and lower your Promoted Placement CPC over time. A coffee shop in Austin used this tactic: they sent a "Order delivery this week and get free delivery" email to their list of 1,200 subscribers. That week, their Just Eat orders increased 34% without increasing their Promoted Placement spend. The algorithm responded by dropping their CPC from $1.80 to $1.10 over the next 10 days.

**The Instagram retargeting angle:** Use Instagram ads to retarget people who visited your Just Eat page but didn't order. You can't do this natively from Just Eat, but you can set up a tracking link with UTM parameters. Create a custom audience in Facebook Ads of people who clicked your Just Eat link but didn't convert within 24 hours. Serve them an Instagram ad showing your most photogenic dish with a "Get it delivered" call-to-action. I advised a Portland ramen shop to try this. They spent $200 on Instagram retargeting over 30 days and tracked $1,800 in attributed Just Eat orders. That's a 9x return on ad spend from a channel most restaurants ignore completely.

## When (and When Not) to Run Offers vs. Promoted Placements

You have two levers on Just Eat: paid visibility (Promoted Placements) and paid conversion (Offers). Most restaurants either run both badly or choose one arbitrarily. Here's the decision framework I use with clients.

**Run Promoted Placements when:** You're a new restaurant on the platform and nobody knows you exist. Promoted Placements are the fastest way to get seen. Also run them if you have a high-margin menu (average order value over $25) and you can afford to pay $1-$3 per click knowing that 5-10% of clicks convert to orders. The math works when your breakeven cost per order is at least 3x your Promoted Placement cost per order.

**Run Offers when:** You have a loyal customer base but they're ordering from competitors because of price. Offers are also effective during slow periods (Monday-Tuesday lunch, Sunday dinner). A 20% off offer during your slowest day can fill a production gap without cannibalizing your peak hours.

**Run both together when:** You're launching a new menu item or entering a new delivery zone. For a 30-day window, combine Promoted Placements (visibility) with a 15% off offer (conversion incentive). This is expensive but works as a launch strategy. I did this with a New York sandwich shop expanding into a new Brooklyn neighborhood. They spent $900 on Promoted Placements and lost $300 on the offers (discounts eaten into margin) over 30 days. But they acquired 140 new customers. Of those, 38 reordered at full price within 60 days. The lifetime value of those 38 customers was $4,200. The upfront loss was worth it.

**Don't run either when:** Your restaurant has fewer than 20 reviews on Just Eat and your rating is below 4.0. Fix those first. Otherwise you're paying to drive people to a listing that will repel them. Also, don't run any paid promotion if your average prep time is over 45 minutes. Just Eat customers are impatient. A 50-minute wait will generate refund requests and bad reviews. Fix your kitchen workflow before you buy traffic.

**The seasonal play:** Run stronger offers during slow seasons (January-February for most restaurants) and rely more on Promoted Placements during high-demand periods (Mother's Day, Super Bowl Sunday, Valentine's Day). During peak demand, customers are already searching. They don't need a discount to order—they need to find you. Promoted Placements capture that existing intent. During slow periods, customers need a reason to order at all. Offers provide that reason.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn't Just Eat just stealing my margin with their commission? Should I even be on the platform?**

That depends on whether Just Eat orders are incremental to your existing business or cannibalizing your higher-margin in-house sales. If your dining room is at 80% capacity and your phone is ringing, you don't need Just Eat. But most small restaurants have excess kitchen capacity, especially between 2 PM and 5 PM. Even at a 30% commission, a $20 order that uses idle kitchen time still contributes $14 to your overhead. That's better than $0. The math works if you price your menu correctly for delivery and don't let Just Eat orders crowd out your in-house diners. Raise your delivery prices by 15-20% and treat Just Eat as a capacity-filler, not your primary revenue stream.

**Q: Does Promoted Placement actually work for a small restaurant with a $500 monthly budget?**

Yes, but only if you target correctly. With $500/month, you can afford roughly 300-400 clicks at $1.25-$1.50 CPC. At a 7% conversion rate (which is average for a well-optimized listing), that's 21-28 orders. If your average order value is $22, that's $462-$616 in revenue from your ad spend. Break even at best. The real value is that those orders generate reviews and improve your organic ranking. After 90 days of consistent Promoted Placements, your organic visibility will improve, and you can reduce your paid spend. Don't expect Promoted Placements to be profitable in month one. Expect them to be an investment in ranking that pays off in month three and beyond.

**Q: Can I target specific neighborhoods or delivery zones on Just Eat?**

Yes, but the targeting options are less granular than Google Ads or Uber Eats. Just Eat lets you set a delivery radius and you can choose to promote your listing within specific areas. The system uses your restaurant's location and delivery range as the base. If you're in Austin and want to target only downtown, you can set your delivery radius to 3 miles and turn on Promoted Placements. The algorithm will prioritize users within that radius. For more precise targeting, you need to combine Just Eat with Google Ads—target people searching "[your neighborhood] food delivery" and link to your Just Eat page. That gives you better geographic control than Just Eat's native tools.

**Q: What's the minimum I should budget for a 30-day test of Promoted Placements?**

$600 total. That's $20 per day for 30 days. Set a $1.00 CPC cap and a $20 daily budget. Run it for a full month so you see at least two full weeks of data (because week 1 is usually the algorithm learning phase with higher CPCs). Track your orders, average order value, and commission costs. If your cost per order is under $15 and your average order value is over $20, you're in reasonable territory. If your cost per order hits $25+, stop and fix your listing first (reviews, photos, menu pricing) before trying again.

**Q: How do I know if a customer found me through Promoted Placement or organic search?**

Just Eat doesn't give you clean attribution data. You have to back into it. Method one: Turn off Promoted Placements for one week and compare order volume. The difference is your paid traffic. Method two: Use unique offer codes. Run a "10% off" offer that you only promote through Promoted Placements. Track how many times that unique code is used. Method three: Use Google Analytics with UTM parameters on your Just Eat link. This won't catch every click, but it gives you directional data. None of these methods are perfect, but combined, they give you enough to make decisions. Don't let perfect be the enemy of good here.

**Q: Should I run Promoted Placements on Uber Eats AND Just Eat at the same time?**

Only if you have the kitchen capacity and the budget to do both well. A common mistake is splitting a $1,000 budget across three platforms, getting 300 clicks on each, and seeing no meaningful results on any of them. You're better off concentrating $1,000 on one platform for 90 days, building organic ranking, then adding a second platform with new budget. I typically recommend starting with the platform where you have the highest rating and review count. If your Just Eat rating is 4.5 and your Uber Eats rating is 3.8, start on Just Eat. Fix Uber Eats later.
