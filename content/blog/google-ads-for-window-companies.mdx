## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Running Ads for “Windows” Without Any Modifiers

A window and door company in Austin, Texas came to me after spending $2,400 in six weeks on Google Ads. They had one campaign, one ad group, and one keyword: “windows.” Their cost per click averaged $14, and they got exactly zero leads. Zilch. The problem is obvious to anyone who’s run ads for more than a week: people searching for “windows” are looking for Microsoft Windows, new house windows, cleaning services, or just browsing. They are not looking to hire an installer.

The fix was brutal but simple. I pulled their search terms report and saw 67% of clicks came from irrelevant queries—computer software, window cleaning, and “how to install windows myself.” I built a separate campaign targeting only high-intent keywords with modifiers: “window installation Austin,” “energy efficient windows cost,” “replace old windows near me,” and “double hung window installers.” I added 140 negative keywords including “free,” “DIY,” “cleaning,” “repair,” and “how to.” Their budget stayed the same ($400/month), but their CPC dropped to $8 after two weeks of data accumulation. In the first month after the fix, they got six phone calls, three form fills, and closed two jobs worth $3,800 total. Cost per lead went from infinity to about $66.

The outcome: They kept running the same campaign for the next four months with consistent lead flow. Most guides skip this part—if you don’t clean up your search terms weekly, you’re paying for people who want a $5 can of Windex, not a $12,000 window replacement.

### Mistake 2: Using a Single Location Radius That’s Too Wide

A door installer in Portland, Oregon set their location targeting to “within 50 miles of Portland.” Sounded reasonable—they were willing to drive. But 50 miles in Portland traffic means you can technically reach Salem, but you’re losing two hours of labor time each way. Their ads were showing to people in Vancouver, Washington and Hood River, Oregon, who would click, call, then ghost when they heard the travel fee.

They spent $1,800 over three months. I looked at their lead list. Twelve leads came from outside their actual service area, which they defined as “within 30 minutes of our shop.” That’s about 15 miles in suburban Portland. They had paid $620 on clicks from people they couldn’t realistically serve at a profitable rate.

I changed their targeting to a 15-mile radius around their physical address, plus a few specific suburbs by name where they already had jobs. I also added location exclusions—no ads showing in areas where they didn’t want to drive. Within two weeks, their click-through rate dropped slightly (fewer impressions), but their conversion rate went up from 1.2% to 3.8%. Their cost per lead fell from $85 to $41. One of their best months after the change: $480 in ad spend generated $2,900 in closed jobs.

The uncomfortable truth is that wider targeting doesn’t mean more business. It means more clicks from people who will never hire you.

### Mistake 3: Running One Ad for Every Service

A company in Nashville called me after they’d burned $3,200 in three months. They had one ad that said “Window and Door Installation” with a generic landing page listing everything they did. They offered vinyl windows, fiberglass doors, custom wood frames, storm doors, sliding patio doors, and bay window installations. The ad matched to any search vaguely related. Their quality score was 4/10. Google was charging them $18 per click for “patio door installation Nashville” because the ad text mentioned doors but the landing page talked about windows first.

I built separate ad groups for each major service line: “vinyl window replacement,” “fiberglass entry doors,” “sliding patio doors,” “bay window installers.” Each had its own ad copy with specific benefits: “Vinyl windows starting at $X installed,” “Fiberglass doors that never warp,” “Sliding patio doors with lifetime warranty.” Each ad group sent to a dedicated landing page—just one page per service, no navigation, a single form and a phone number. Their quality scores went to 8/10 across the board. CPCs dropped to $7–$11 depending on the term.

In the first month after restructuring, they spent $850 and got 14 leads. They closed six jobs worth $7,400 total. The owner told me, “I can’t believe I was paying extra for ads that didn’t even talk about what people were looking for.” That’s the thing—Google rewards relevance. If you make them guess what you do, they’ll charge you more and send you tire kickers.

### Mistake 4: Not Tracking Phone Calls

A window company in Denver had Google Ads running for eight months. They had no call tracking set up. They were counting form fills as leads, but forms gave them maybe one or two submissions per month. The owner assumed the ads weren’t working and was about to turn them off. I asked if they ever got phone calls they couldn’t explain. He said, “We get calls all day, but I don’t know where they came from.”

I added call tracking using a tool called CallRail—costs about $30/month for basic tracking. Within two weeks, they had data showing that 40% of their inbound calls came from Google Ads, and those calls converted to booked consultations at a 22% rate. They had been ignoring 19 calls per month that were directly from their ad spend. Their cost per phone call lead was $28, compared to $64 for form fills. They had been spending $700/month for eight months thinking they were getting zero results, when actually they were getting around seven to eight phone leads per month that they never counted.

We optimized the ad schedule to run during business hours when they could answer the phone. We added a call-only campaign for mobile users. Their monthly ad spend stayed the same, but their tracked leads jumped from 3 (form fills) to 14 (form fills plus tracked calls). Their monthly revenue from Google Ads went from $1,200 to $3,400. The fix was literally just plugging in a tracking number. Most guides skip this because it’s not glamorous—but if you can’t measure phone calls, you are flying blind on half your business.

## How to Structure Your Campaigns for Maximum Efficiency

Most window and door companies set up one campaign, throw in all their keywords, and hope. That works about as well as using a sledgehammer to hang a picture. Here is the structure I’ve used for thirty-plus clients in this industry, including a company in Chicago that went from $4,200/month ad spend to $2,800 with the same lead volume.

**Use three separate campaigns:**

1. **Brand Campaign**: Your company name, variations, and any common misspellings. Budget: $100–$150/month. This prevents competitors from bidding on your name and catches people who already know you. One client in NYC saw a 14% increase in direct calls just from claiming their own brand terms.

2. **Service Campaign**: High-intent keywords for specific services. “Window installation Chicago,” “door replacement services,” “bay window installers.” Budget: $300–$600/month depending on your market. Target a 20–30% impression share to start. Do not chase 100% share—you’ll overpay.

3. **Problem Campaign**: Keywords that describe the problem your service solves. “Drafty windows,” “high energy bills windows,” “old door replacement,” “swollen door frame.” Budget: $200–$400/month. These often have lower CPCs because fewer advertisers bid on problem-focused terms. A company in Houston ran a problem campaign for “energy bill too high windows” and got a 5.1% conversion rate—nearly double their service campaign.

Each campaign should have four to six ad groups. Each ad group should have at least two responsive search ads with different headlines. Test your ad copy monthly. Swap out the bottom performer. Over six months, you can double your click-through rates just by iterating.

The Chicago company I mentioned: they had been running a single campaign with sixty keywords. I broke it into three campaigns. Their average CPC dropped from $13 to $9. Their cost per lead fell from $82 to $54. Their monthly ad spend decreased by $1,400 while maintaining the same number of leads. They used the savings to start a remarketing campaign for people who visited their site but didn’t call.

## Using Local Service Ads and Yelp to Complement Google Search

Google Ads search is your workhorse, but it should not be your only tool. Two additional channels I’ve seen work well for window and door companies are Google Local Service Ads (LSAs) and Yelp Ads.

**Google Local Service Ads**: These are the “Google Guaranteed” listings that appear above search ads. You pay per lead instead of per click. A client in Austin tested LSAs alongside their search campaign. Their LSA cost per lead was $47, compared to $62 for search. The trade-off: LSAs give you less control over targeting and ad copy. But if you have good reviews and a solid Google Business Profile, LSAs can be a cheaper source of leads. The same Austin company got 8 leads from LSAs in one month, closed 4, and paid $376 in lead fees. They booked $5,200 in revenue from those four jobs. Set them up through your Google Business Profile dashboard. They take about 20 minutes to configure.

**Yelp Ads**: Yelp gets a bad reputation from many small business owners, and sometimes deservedly so. But for home services, Yelp drives calls—especially for older demographics. A window company in Denver ran a $400/month Yelp ad for three months. Their cost per lead was $55, which was slightly higher than Google. But the leads that came from Yelp closed at a 38% rate, compared to 22% for Google Ads leads. The owner speculated that Yelp users who clicked ads were further along in their research and more likely to book. The Yelp campaign generated $3,600 in closed jobs over three months on $1,200 spend. Not terrible.

The catch with Yelp: you need five reviews with a 4-star average or higher before ads become cost-effective. If you don’t have those reviews, skip Yelp until you do. Use Google Ads and LSAs first.

**Call tracking is non-negotiable for both channels.** I use CallRail because it integrates with Google Ads and Yelp. One client in Portland discovered that 30% of their Yelp calls came from people searching on a desktop and calling a mobile number. Without tracking, they would have credited those calls to their website traffic, not Yelp.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I’m already spending $500/month on Google Ads and getting nothing. Do I need to spend more?**

Probably not. Most window and door companies I’ve worked with get results at $500–$800/month when their campaigns are structured correctly. The problem is usually not the budget—it’s the targeting, ads, or landing pages. I’ve seen companies spend $1,500/month and get zero leads because they were bidding on “windows and doors” without modifiers. Fix your campaign structure first. If you’re still getting nothing after two weeks of clean data, then consider increasing your budget to capture more impression share on your best keywords.

**Q: How long before I see results from Google Ads?**

If you set it up correctly on Monday, you might see clicks on Tuesday. Phone calls could start within 48 hours. But judging performance on a few days of data is a mistake. Give it two full weeks before making any major changes. Some keywords take time to accumulate conversion data. For a Denver window company I worked with, their cost per lead started at $120 in week one, dropped to $68 in week two, and settled at $44 by week four. If they had panicked and changed everything after week one, they would have wasted their own time.

**Q: Does Google Ads work for small towns or only big cities?**

It works fine if you adjust your expectations. A window company in a town of 15,000 people in rural Wisconsin spent $250/month and got two to four leads per month. That might not sound like much, but they closed nearly every lead because they were the only installer in the area running ads. Their cost per lead was $62 on average, and each closed job averaged $4,200. The challenge is volume: you might not get enough clicks to optimize your campaigns quickly. If your town has fewer than 10,000 households, consider running ads on a 30-mile radius and targeting multiple small towns nearby to build up data.

**Q: Should I use broad match or exact match keywords?**

Start with phrase match and exact match. Broad match can work, but it needs negative keywords and close monitoring. A company in Nashville burned $400 in one week on broad match because Google matched “window installation” to “window cleaning equipment.” If you’re not checking your search terms report every week, avoid broad match entirely. After three months of data, you can test broad match for one ad group with a separate budget and tight negatives.

**Q: Do I need a landing page or can I just send people to my website homepage?**

Send them to a specific landing page for the service they searched. If someone searches for “patio door installation,” do not send them to a homepage that talks about windows first. You will lose them. I tested this with a client in Chicago: sending traffic to the homepage versus a dedicated landing page for the same keyword. The dedicated page converted at 3.2%. The homepage converted at 0.9%. That’s the difference between profitable ads and wasted money. A simple landing page with your phone number, a form, three bullet points about why you’re better, and a photo of a finished job is enough. Use a free tool like Carrd or a basic page on your website. Don’t overthink it.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads myself or do I need to hire someone?**

You can run them yourself if you have the time to learn and the discipline to check performance weekly. But most small business owners I’ve worked with are better off spending their time installing windows and doors. The learning curve for Google Ads is shallow enough to get started, but deep enough that mistakes can cost you hundreds of dollars per month before you realize what’s happening. If you have a $500/month budget and no experience, expect to waste $300–$400 learning what works. If that sounds like a bad deal, pay someone with experience to set it up and hand it over to you. My agency does this, but plenty of freelancers on Upwork or local marketing consultants can do it for a flat fee of $500–$1,000.
