## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting "Wedding Venue" Too Broadly

**The story:** A venue in Austin, TX called "Hillside Estate" spent $1,800 in their first month on Google Ads. They targeted keywords like "wedding venue," "event space," and "wedding reception." Sounds reasonable, right? Here's what actually happened: they got 347 clicks, exactly zero bookings, and the owner called me asking if Google Ads was a scam.

**What went wrong:** Their ads were showing to people searching for "cheap wedding venues under $1,000" (Hillside started at $8,500), "event spaces for corporate parties" (completely different audience), and "wedding reception halls near me" (which pulled in people who wanted a banquet hall experience, not an estate).

**The fix:** I killed 80% of their keywords and rebuilt the campaign around intent-based match types. We used exact match for "luxury Austin wedding venue," "wedding estate Austin," and "outdoor ceremony venue Austin." Added negative keywords like "cheap," "budget," "corporate," "birthday," and "conference." Set a location radius of 45 miles — not 100.

**The outcome:** Second month: $1,200 spend, 42 clicks. Three tour requests. One booking worth $12,000. Conversion rate went from 0% to 7.1%. The owner said, and I quote, "I was ready to fire Google. Now I'm firing my old agency."

### Mistake 2: Sending Everyone to a Generic Homepage

**The story:** A venue in Nashville called "The Cotton Mill Loft" ran ads for their rustic barn weddings, but every ad — "urban loft wedding," "barn ceremony," "reception hall" — landed on the same homepage. The homepage featured photos of a modern art gallery event they hosted the previous month. Couples looking for a barn saw exposed brick and industrial lighting and bounced within 12 seconds.

**The fix:** We built three landing pages. One for barn weddings with hay bales and string lights. One for urban loft weddings with city views and cocktail bars. One for elopement packages with the price listed upfront ($3,200 all-in). Each ad group pointed to its specific page. Added a calendar widget so couples could see availability without calling.

**The outcome:** Cost per lead dropped from $87 to $31. Page load time improved by 1.2 seconds because we stripped the landing pages down to essentials. They booked 11 weddings that quarter from Google Ads alone. Total ad spend: $4,600. Total revenue from those bookings: roughly $62,000.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Negative Keywords Entirely

**The story:** A venue in Portland, OR — "The Fernwood Garden" — had a manager who set up their first campaign and thought "the more keywords, the better." They added 400 keywords including "DIY wedding," "backyard wedding," "elopement ideas," and "micro wedding." Their ad budget of $900/month evaporated in 11 days. The owner told me, "I got calls from people who wanted to have their ceremony in their aunt's backyard and just needed a photographer recommendation."

**The fix:** I exported their search terms report and found 183 irrelevant queries. Added negative keywords: "DIY," "backyard," "micro," "budget," "cheap," "small," "intimate" (unless they specifically wanted intimate weddings, which they didn't). Also added competitor names as negatives so they weren't paying for clicks from people searching for their rivals.

**The outcome:** Saved $380/month in wasted clicks immediately. Their cost per click dropped from $4.20 to $2.15 because Google stopped showing their ad to people who weren't going to convert. The manager sent me a screenshot of the new metrics with a message: "I want to cry. Happy tears."

### Mistake 4: No Tracking Beyond the Click

**The story:** A venue in Denver called "Rocky Mountain Pines" spent $3,200 over three months and couldn't tell me which campaigns produced their bookings. They were using a phone number on their website and asking "where did you hear about us?" during tours. The answer was always "Google" or "online" — useless data. They assumed the ads weren't working and were about to cancel everything.

**What went wrong:** No call tracking, no form tracking, no conversion goals. They didn't know if the $3,200 produced one booking or ten. I've seen this mistake kill campaigns at three different clients. If you can't measure, you can't optimize. If you can't optimize, you're burning cash.

**The fix:** Implemented Google Ads conversion tracking with a thank-you page for form submissions. Added call tracking through Google's call forwarding feature (free with Ads). Set up a simple Google Sheet to log which campaign generated each tour request.

**The outcome:** They discovered their "Weekend Open House" campaign generated 60% of their bookings but only consumed 25% of their budget. The "Luxury Wedding Package" campaign spent 40% of the budget and produced exactly one booking. They shifted $500/month from luxury to open house campaigns and saw a 32% increase in tour requests within six weeks.

## How to Structure Your Campaign For Maximum Bookings

I've set up campaigns for wedding venues in Chicago, Austin, and small-town Wisconsin. The structure that consistently works looks like this:

**Three Campaigns, Three Budgets**

Campaign 1: "Wedding Venue — High Intent" (60% of budget). Keywords: "book [city] wedding venue," "[venue name] reviews," "wedding venue tour [city]." These are people ready to sign a contract. Bid aggressively for top positions.

Campaign 2: "Wedding Venue — Research Phase" (30% of budget). Keywords: "best wedding venues in [city]," "affordable wedding venues [city]," "wedding venue checklist." These people are comparing options. Your ad should say something like "See Our Pricing & Availability — Virtual Tours Available."

Campaign 3: "Competitor Names" (10% of budget). Target people searching for your competitors. Use ads that say "Compare [competitor] to [your venue] — See Why Couples Choose Us." This is legal, it works, and most venues don't do it.

**Budget allocation for a small venue:** Start at $800-$1,200/month. Not $200. At $200, you'll get maybe 40 clicks and learn nothing. At $1,000, you'll get enough data to make decisions within two weeks. I ordered a second coffee I did not need while typing that last sentence. No regrets.

**Use Performance Max campaigns carefully.** I put "carefully" in italics because Google will try to sell you on PMax as a magical solution. It's not. PMax works well for venues with strong brand recognition and lots of high-quality images. For a new venue with no reviews and six photos? Stick to Search campaigns until you have proof of concept. I've seen PMax burn through $2,000 in a week for a venue in Savannah, GA and produce two calls — neither of which was a couple. One was a florist trying to sell them services.

## The Right Metrics: Ignore Vanity Numbers

Most marketing guides will tell you to track impressions and click-through rates. Those are vanity metrics. Here's what actually matters for wedding venues:

**Cost Per Qualified Lead (CPQL):** This is your cost per email or phone call from someone who doesn't sound like they're reading a script. I track this in a spreadsheet with columns for "sounds serious," "maybe," and "definitely not." The "definitely not" column usually includes people asking if they can hold the reception at 3 PM on a Tuesday.

**Lead-to-Tour Rate:** What percentage of leads actually schedule a visit? If this number drops below 20%, your landing page or phone script needs work. One venue in Madison, WI had a lead-to-tour rate of 8%. Turned out their receptionist was saying "we start at $12,000" in a tone that implied "please hang up." We rewrote the script. Rate went to 24%.

**Tour-to-Booking Rate:** This reveals how your venue performs in person vs. online. If your website is amazing but your venue is average, you'll see a gap. Fix the venue, not the ads. A venue in Phoenix had a 40% tour-to-booking rate during fall (perfect weather) and 12% during summer (112 degrees, no air conditioning in their ceremony space). They installed misters and fans. Summer bookings went up.

**Cost Per Booking:** This is the only number that actually matters. If you spend $1,000 on ads and get one booking worth $15,000, your cost per booking is $1,000. That's excellent. If your cost per booking exceeds 10% of your average booking value, something is broken.

## Offline Conversion Tracking: The Missing Piece

Here's something most guides skip: wedding venue bookings don't happen online. A couple sees your ad, visits your website, calls you, tours the venue, goes home, thinks about it, maybe calls their parents, and then books two weeks later. Google Ads doesn't know any of this happened unless you tell it.

**Import offline conversions into Google Ads.** Here's how:

1. After a tour, import that data back into Google Ads using a simple spreadsheet with the GCLID (Google Click ID) captured during the call or form submission. I use a free tool from Google called Offline Conversion Import — it takes about 30 minutes to set up.

2. Assign a conversion value. If your average booking is $12,000, assign $12,000 as the conversion value when a couple signs the contract. Google's algorithm will start optimizing for $12,000 bookings instead of $12 clicks.

3. Do this consistently. I know a venue in Charleston, SC that imported offline conversions six months after starting ads. Their campaign immediately improved because Google finally understood what a "good" click looked like. Their cost per booking dropped from $1,400 to $600 within three weeks.

Without offline conversion tracking, Google is flying blind. You're paying for clicks when you should be paying for contracts. I had a third coffee while writing this section. I have a problem.

## Retargeting Couples Who Almost Booked

Not every couple will book on the first visit. In my experience, about 30% of couples who tour a venue will book it — the rest need time. Or they're comparing you to another venue. Or their parents are paying and need to be convinced.

**Facebook Retargeting works better than Google Display for wedding venues.** Here's why: couples spend a lot of time on Instagram and Facebook researching wedding ideas. Google Display ads show up on random websites. A couple reading a news article doesn't want to see a wedding ad. A couple scrolling Instagram looking at wedding cakes? That's your audience.

**Set up a Facebook Pixel on your website.** Create a custom audience of people who visited your pricing page but didn't book a tour. Show them an ad with a testimonial from a real couple who got married at your venue. Include the bride and groom's names with their permission — generic stock photos of models pretending to be engaged won't cut it.

**Use Google Display for one specific thing:** remarketing to people who watched your virtual tour. Virtual tours are expensive to produce but worth it. A venue in Seattle set up a 360-degree virtual tour, then retargeted viewers with a "Schedule Your In-Person Tour" offer. Their conversion rate was 18% — nearly triple their standard display remarketing.

**Budget for retargeting:** $200-$300/month. That's enough to keep your venue in front of couples for 30-60 days after their first visit. If you're spending $1,000 on search campaigns and $0 on retargeting, you're leaving money on the table.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why should I use Google Ads instead of just posting on Facebook or Instagram for free?**

Because organic reach on Facebook is currently around 2-5% for business pages. That means if you have 1,000 followers, maybe 30-50 people see your post. Google Ads puts you in front of people actively searching for wedding venues right now. I've had clients spend $500 and get 15 tour requests. You can't get 15 tour requests from a Facebook post unless you already have 50,000 followers and a viral video.

**Q: How much should I budget for Google Ads as a small venue?**

Start with $800-$1,200 per month. If your average booking is $10,000+, you only need one booking every four months from Google Ads to break even. Most venues I work with see a booking within the first 30 days. If you're in a small town with only 50 weddings per year, you can start at $500/month. If you're in a competitive market like Denver or Austin, expect to spend $1,500-$2,000/month to get consistent results.

**Q: I tried Google Ads before and wasted money. What would be different this time?**

Most failed campaigns have one of three problems: too many broad keywords, no negative keywords, or no conversion tracking. If you didn't do all three correctly, you weren't really running Google Ads — you were donating money to Google. I've taken over campaigns from three different agencies where the client said "Google Ads doesn't work." In every case, the agency had set up garbage campaigns. We rebuilt them properly. All three clients now spend more every month.

**Q: Do I need a website specifically for Google Ads?**

Yes, kind of. You don't need a brand new website, but you need a landing page that's different from your homepage. Your homepage talks about your venue's history and philosophy. Your Google Ads landing page needs to answer one question: "Can I get married here, and how much will it cost?" Include photos of actual weddings (not empty rooms), pricing starting points (even if it's a range), availability calendar, and a clear call-to-action button. That's it. No lengthy about us section. No blog posts.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads myself, or do I need a professional?**

You can run them yourself if you have the time to learn and the patience to test. Google's own certification courses are free and take about 10 hours to complete. If your time is worth $100/hour and running Google Ads takes you 20 hours per month, you're effectively spending $2,000/month on your own labor. At that point, hire someone. I've told twelve that they needed help.

**Q: What about Yelp ads? Should I do those instead?**

I'll give you a direct answer: Yelp ads work best for businesses with lots of existing reviews. If you have 50+ positive Yelp reviews, Yelp ads can work for wedding venues. If you have fewer than 20 reviews, your money is better spent on Google Ads. Yelp's algorithm heavily favors businesses with high review counts and recent activity. One venue in San Diego spent $400 on Yelp ads and got zero leads because they only had 12 reviews. Their competitor had 200 reviews and got 8 leads from the same spend.
