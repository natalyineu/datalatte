## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

After running campaigns on Glovo and similar platforms for clients across the US, I've watched small business owners burn real money on the same three mistakes. Here's what actually happens when good intentions meet bad strategy.

### Mistake #1: The "Discount Everything" Trap

A coffee shop in Austin, TX — let's call it Brew & Co. — decided to run 30% off everything for two weeks. They wanted a big launch push. What they got was chaos.

The owner told me his story over email (I still have it saved). They did $8,400 in revenue during that promo period. Sounds great until you do the math. Their average ticket was $6.50, and margins on coffee are around 65-70% before overhead. With a 30% discount on everything, they were barely breaking even on product cost. After Glovo's 25-30% commission on the discounted price? They lost money on roughly 40% of their orders.

**What went wrong:** They discounted everything equally. Small orders ($3.50 for a single coffee) became losses. They attracted only deal-seekers who never returned at full price.

**The fix:** Bundle discounts with a minimum order threshold. Offer 20% off orders over $15 instead of 30% off everything. Add a "first order" promo code that expires after 30 days.

**The outcome:** We restructured their promos. They ran "Buy any sandwich, get a coffee for $1" instead of blanket discounts. Three weeks later: revenue per order went from $6.50 to $11.20. Their profit per order increased by $2.80. The owner stopped sending me panicked emails.

### Mistake #2: Ghost Listings and Forgotten Profiles

A hair salon in Denver called The Parlour spent $500 on Glovo's visibility booster package — the one that puts you at the top of search results. They paid for three months upfront. After month one, they'd gotten exactly 8 extra orders.

I looked at their profile. It had been set up once and left to rot. Photos were blurry. Services listed didn't match their actual menu. Wait times in their profile said "same day" but they were actually booking 2 weeks out. The description was the default Glovo template: "We offer quality services at competitive prices."

**What went wrong:** They thought visibility boosters were a shortcut. You can't pay for traffic and then hand people a broken experience. Customers who clicked through saw an unprofessional profile and bounced.

**The fix:** Before spending a dollar on visibility, audit your profile. Fresh photos (not stock images). Accurate pricing. Updated availability. A description that sounds like an actual human wrote it. Then run the booster for one week, measure the conversion rate, and decide if it's working.

**The outcome:** The Parlour redid their profile, invested in new photos ($150 from a local photographer), and ran a targeted $300 two-week booster. Their conversion rate from profile view to order went from 12% to 41%. They got 47 orders in two weeks compared to 8 in a full month. Cost per acquired customer dropped from $62.50 to $6.38.

### Mistake #3: One-Size-Fits-All Menu Pricing

A pet grooming business in Portland, OR listed the same prices on Glovo as they charged in-store. That sounds fair, right? It's not. Glovo takes a commission on every sale. Their margins got squeezed to nothing.

They offered a $55 basic grooming package in-store. On Glovo, after a 25% commission, they kept $41.25. Their cost to provide that service was about $30 (product, labor, overhead). That's $11.25 profit per order. Hardly worth the effort, especially when you factor in the time spent managing the Glovo order flow.

**What went wrong:** They treated Glovo like a free marketing channel instead of a paid distribution partner. The platform takes a cut. Your pricing needs to account for that.

**The fix:** Add a 15-20% markup to your Glovo prices compared to in-store. If your in-store price is $55, list it at $65-$70 on Glovo. Customers on delivery platforms expect to pay more — they're paying for convenience. Include a note in your menu description: "Prices reflect the convenience of delivery and platform fees."

**The outcome:** The Portland groomer raised Glovo prices to $68 for that same package. Orders dropped by 18% initially. But profit per order went from $11.25 to $21.00. Total monthly profit from Glovo increased by 34% despite fewer orders. They also stopped resenting the platform.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Timing and Daypart Data

A pizza place in Nashville was running their promotions at the wrong times. They'd push lunch specials on Glovo at 10 AM on weekdays and wonder why nobody bit.

I pulled their data: 70% of their Glovo orders came between 5 PM and 9 PM. Lunch was 12% of orders. They were spending ad budget against the 12% and ignoring the 70%.

**What went wrong:** They applied a restaurant-industry assumption (lunch is important) without checking their actual Glovo data. The platform tells you exactly when your customers order. They weren't looking.

**The fix:** Schedule promotions and ad spend for your actual peak hours. For this pizza place, that meant boosting visibility at 4:30 PM (before the dinner rush starts), running "after 5 PM" specials, and turning off ads after 9 PM when orders dropped.

**The outcome:** Same monthly ad budget — $400. Restructured to focus on 4:30-9 PM window. Orders during that window increased 52%. Total monthly orders went from 140 to 213. Cost per order dropped from $2.86 to $1.88. The owner told me it felt like "turning on a faucet that was already there."

## Why Most Glovo Ads Lose Money (And How to Fix It)

Glovo's advertising system works like every other marketplace ad platform: you bid for placement, pay per click or impression, and hope the math works out. Most small business owners I've worked with lose money on Glovo ads because they treat them like Facebook Ads.

Facebook Ads let you target specific demographics, interests, and behaviors. Glovo ads let you target... people ordering food or services within a certain radius. That's it. There's no "people who like vegan food" or "dog owners who spend over $50 per order." You're spraying and praying.

Here's a concrete example from a client in Chicago — a smoothie and acai bowl shop. They ran $600 in Glovo ads over 30 days. They got 84 clicks. 12 converted to orders. Average order value was $12.50. Revenue from ads: $150. They lost $450.

**What went wrong:** They bid on generic keywords like "smoothie" and "breakfast" without any negative keywords or targeting filters. Their ads showed to anyone within 3 miles, including people who were clearly looking for burgers and pizza.

**The fix:** Use ultra-specific menu item targeting. Instead of "breakfast," target "acai bowl" and "smoothie bowl." Set your delivery radius smaller if needed — closer customers convert at higher rates and cost less to deliver to. And most importantly: track your ad-attributed orders separately from organic orders so you know exactly what's working.

**Better approach:** Instead of Glovo's internal ads, send customers from your own channels to Glovo to complete the purchase. Use a unique promo code in your email newsletter (Mailchimp, $13/month for basic) or SMS blast. Send a postcard to previous customers with "Order on Glovo and get free delivery with code GLOWEEK." You control the audience, not Glovo. You're leveraging their platform without paying their ad markup.

A Nashville bakery I worked with used this exact approach. They sent one email to 600 subscribers offering $5 off any Glovo order over $20. Cost of the email: $0 (they already paid for Mailchimp). Orders placed with that code: 47. Average order: $26. Revenue attributed to that email: $1,222. Glovo commission on those orders: approximately $305. Net profit after product cost and commission: roughly $450 from a single email blast.

## Offline Tactics That Drive Online Orders

Your Glovo orders don't have to come from Glovo. Some of the most effective strategies I've seen involve driving offline customers to order online.

A barbershop in Brooklyn tried this: they printed small cards (Vistaprint, $29 for 500) with a QR code that went directly to their Glovo menu. Every customer who walked in got one with their receipt. "Next time you're in a rush, order ahead on Glovo and skip the wait."

**Result:** 112 Glovo orders in the first month from customers who came in at least once. That's 112 orders they didn't have to compete for in Glovo's search results.

A nail salon in San Francisco used a different approach. They attached a small flyer to every takeout bag from the neighboring pho restaurant. "Waiting for your food? Book your manicure on Glovo while you eat." The restaurant let them do it for free — they were friendly neighbors. Within two weeks, they got 38 bookings they traced directly to those flyers.

A yoga studio in Los Angeles offered in-studio students a "Glovo-only" discount on their first online booking: 15% off any class package ordered through the app. They announced it during class, put up a sign at the front desk, and mentioned it in their weekly email (Mailchimp again — $13/month). Three weeks later: 64 new Glovo bookings. Average class package value: $89. Revenue: $5,696. Cost of the discount: roughly $854 in reduced margins. Net gain: $4,842.

The lesson: Your physical customers are your best online customers. They already trust you. You just need to give them a reason to open the app.

## The Uncomfortable Truth About Glovo Reviews

I need to be direct about this: if your average rating on Glovo drops below 4.2, your orders will decline. Not might — will. The platform penalizes low-rated businesses in search results. It's not stated in their advertising materials, but I've seen it play out across 12+ clients.

A massage therapy studio in Seattle had a 4.6 rating. They got 30-40 bookings per week through Glovo. Then three customers in one week left 1-star reviews — two complained about pricing, one was angry about a scheduling error that wasn't even the studio's fault. Rating dropped to 4.1. Their bookings the following week: 14. A 53% drop in seven days.

**The fix:** Respond to every single negative review within 24 hours. Be professional, apologize for their experience (even if it wasn't your fault — you're apologizing for their feeling, not admitting fault), and offer to make it right. Glovo's algorithm seems to weigh responded-to negative reviews less heavily. More importantly, proactively ask satisfied customers to leave a review. One email request (Mailchimp again) to your customer list can generate 20-30 positive reviews in a week.

**The numbers:** That Seattle studio sent a follow-up email to their customer list (about 300 people) with a direct link to their Glovo review page. They got 23 new 5-star reviews in 10 days. Their rating went back to 4.4. Bookings recovered to 28 per week. The owner spent about 90 minutes total on this. Cost: zero dollars.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I budget for Glovo ads as a new business?**

Start with $300-$500 per month for the first 60 days. That's enough to get meaningful data without burning cash. Track everything. If your cost per order (total ad spend divided by orders attributed to ads) is above $8, something is wrong — your profile, pricing, or targeting needs fixing. If it's below $4, increase your budget gradually. I've seen businesses scale from $500 to $2,000/month profitably when the fundamentals are right.

**Q: Does Glovo take too much commission? Is it even worth it?**

Yes, Glovo takes 25-30% on food and 20-25% on services in most markets. Those numbers hurt. The question isn't "is this fair?" — it's "can I make money despite this?" The answer is yes, if you price correctly (add 15-20% markup on Glovo), optimize your operations for fast turnaround, and use the platform as one channel among many, not your only sales channel. Businesses that rely on Glovo for 80%+ of revenue usually fail. Those who use it for 20-30% tend to thrive.

**Q: Should I offer free delivery?**

Only if you've built the cost into your menu prices. Free delivery is a powerful psychological trigger — it removes a barrier to purchase. But don't eat the cost yourself. Raise your base prices slightly, then list "free delivery" as a feature. Customers perceive it as a benefit even though they're technically paying for it. One client in Portland raised prices by 8% and added free delivery. Order volume increased 22%. Net profit increased 14%.

**Q: How do I compete with bigger chains on Glovo?**

You can't beat them on price. Don't try. Compete on specificity and speed. A local coffee shop will never out-spend Starbucks on Glovo ads. But a local coffee shop can offer a customized latte that Starbucks doesn't have, deliver it in 12 minutes because you're three blocks away, and write a menu description that sounds like a human being. Chains have scale. You have personality, proximity, and flexibility. Use those.

**Q: Is it worth paying for visibility boosters?**

Sometimes. Not always. Test for one week with a small budget — $100-$200. Track how many profile views convert to orders during that week versus the week before and after. If your conversion rate doesn't increase by at least 20%, the booster isn't working for your business. The booster only helps if your profile is already optimized. Bad photos + booster = faster failure.

**Q: What's the best time to launch a new promotion on Glovo?**

Wednesday or Thursday. Monday is slow — people are recovering from the weekend. Tuesday is slightly better. Wednesday and Thursday have the highest order volumes for most categories in my experience. Launch your promo on Wednesday at 10 AM, let it build through Thursday, and hit peak by Friday. Avoid launching on weekends unless you have a specific Saturday-only offer. Weekend launches get buried in noise.
