## Understanding Your Website's Current State
To start optimizing your website for conversions, you need to understand its current state. Take a close look at your website's analytics to see where your traffic is coming from, what pages are most popular, and where visitors are dropping off.
You can use tools like Google Analytics to get a better understanding of your website's performance.
For example, if you notice that most of your traffic is coming from social media, you may want to focus on optimizing your social media presence.

## Setting Up Your Website for CRO
Once you have a good understanding of your website's current state, it's time to start setting it up for CRO. This includes making sure your website is mobile-friendly, has a clear and concise message, and is easy to navigate.
You should also make sure that your website has a clear call-to-action (CTA) on every page, such as "Sign up for a free trial" or "Book a class now".
<Callout type="tip">Make sure your CTA is prominent and above the fold, so visitors can't miss it.</Callout>

## Optimizing Your Website's Content
Your website's content is crucial for attracting and retaining customers. Make sure your content is high-quality, engaging, and optimized for local search.
You should include keywords like "fitness studio near me" or "yoga classes in [your city]" to help your website rank higher in search results.
For example, if you're a fitness studio in New York City, you could include keywords like "NYC fitness studio" or " Manhattan yoga classes".

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve looked at more fitness studio websites than I care to count, and the same problems show up every time. Here are the ones that cost real money.

### Mistake #1: Hiding Your Schedule Behind a “Contact Us” Form

**The story:** A fitness studio in Austin, Texas — let’s call it Austin Heights Fitness — was running $2,000/month in Google Ads. They had a clean website with good photos. But to see the class schedule, you had to fill out a “Request a Schedule” form. The owner told me, “We want to qualify leads first.”

I checked their Google Analytics. The form page had a 73% bounce rate. People landed there, saw they had to give their email and phone number just to see what time the 6 AM spin class was, and left.

**The fix:** We moved the schedule to the homepage. A simple table. Days across the top, class times down the side. No login required. We kept the form for the newsletter, but put it *after* the schedule.

**The outcome:** In 30 days, their form submissions dropped by 60% (because they weren’t a gate anymore). But class bookings from the website went up 34%. They were getting $3,800 more in monthly revenue from the same ad spend. The owner admitted, “I was scared people would just take the schedule and never buy. Turns out, they book when you show them what you have.”

### Mistake #2: Stock Photos That Scream “We Don’t Actually Have Members”

**The story:** A CrossFit gym in Portland, Oregon had a website full of images of impossibly fit models doing rope climbs in perfect lighting. No sweat. No red faces. No one over 30. The owner told me he paid $50/month for a stock photo subscription because “I don’t have time to take photos.”

The problem? Their bounce rate from Google Ads was 68%. People who clicked were expecting a real gym and found a catalog.

**The fix:** I made him take 47 photos with an iPhone over two days. Before class, after class, during warm-ups. Real people. Bad hair. Sweaty tanks. We swapped out every stock image.

**The outcome:** Bounce rate dropped to 41%. More importantly, their trial pass requests went up 52% in six weeks. The owner said, “I got three calls from people who said ‘I joined because your gym looks like it’s for real people, not Instagram.’” That’s $1,600/month in new memberships they attribute directly to the photo change.

### Mistake #3: Making Your Pricing a State Secret

**The story:** A yoga studio in Denver had a beautiful website. Great photos, good class descriptions. But to see pricing, you had to click through three pages and then enter your email. The owner’s reasoning: “We want to build a list first.”

I ran a simple test. I went through the process myself. It took five clicks and two form fields to see that a monthly unlimited pass was $149. On mobile, the “View Pricing” button was below the fold.

**The fix:** We created a single pricing page. No email required. Monthly, drop-in, intro offer. Right there.

**The outcome:** Page views for pricing went up 400% (because people could actually find it). But here’s the part that surprised the owner: email list growth actually went *up* by 22%. Why? Because people who saw the pricing and were interested were more likely to sign up for the newsletter when it was presented as a bonus, not a barrier. Revenue from new members increased $2,100 in the first month.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Mobile Users Entirely

**The story:** A barre studio in Nashville had a desktop-only website. Their WordPress theme was “responsive,” but that apparently meant “shrinks everything to the size of a postage stamp.” On an iPhone, you had to pinch-zoom to see the schedule. The “Book Now” button was 8 pixels wide.

The owner told me, “Most of our bookings come through Instagram anyway.” I checked. 63% of their website traffic was mobile. They were losing the people who didn’t have Instagram or got tired of fat-fingering tiny buttons.

**The fix:** We switched to a mobile-first theme. Large buttons, single-column layout, schedule that scrolls vertically. We also added a “Tap to Call” button on the header.

**The outcome:** Mobile bookings went up 47% in two months. The phone started ringing more because people could actually tap to call. They added $1,400 in monthly revenue without spending a dollar more on ads.

## Optimizing Your Google Business Profile (The Free Traffic You’re Leaving on the Table)

Your website is important. But for most fitness studios, the first thing a potential customer sees isn’t your homepage — it’s your Google Business Profile.

I’ve worked with small businesses who spent $500/month on ads while their GBP was actively driving customers away. Here’s what I saw at a CrossFit affiliate in Chicago that was getting 230 profile views a week but only 5 website clicks.

**The problem:** Their profile had:
- Photos from 2019 (the gym had since painted and added new equipment)
- No Q&A section (so unanswered questions from 2022 were still visible)
- No posts for 8 months (so Google showed them less frequently in local search)
- The wrong category (they were listed as “Gym” instead of “CrossFit Gym”)
- No booking button

**The fix took two hours:**
1. I uploaded 15 new photos showing current classes, current members, current equipment
2. I answered every unanswered question in the Q&A section (there were 11)
3. I wrote and published three posts a week for six weeks: one about a member success story, one about a new class, one about an upcoming event
4. I changed the category to “CrossFit Gym” (specific categories rank better)
5. I added a “Book a Free Trial” button using the built-in scheduling feature

**The outcome:** In 10 weeks, profile views went from 230/week to 580/week. Website clicks went from 5 to 38. They started getting calls from people who found them through Google Maps search for “CrossFit Chicago.” That’s $2,700 in new memberships directly attributed to the GBP changes.

If you haven’t touched your Google Business Profile in 90 days, you’re losing money. I’ll say it slower: If you haven’t touched your Google Business Profile in 90 days, you’re losing money.

Go right now and check:
- When was the last photo uploaded?
- Have you answered all questions?
- Are you posting at least once a week?
- Is your booking link working?

## Email Sequences That Actually Convert (Not Just “Welcome to Our Newsletter”)

Most fitness studios treat email like a megaphone — blast the same message to everyone and hope something sticks. That’s not how it works when you’re a small studio with a local customer base.

I worked with a Pilates studio in Seattle that had 1,200 email subscribers but was getting $200/month in revenue from email. That’s $0.17 per subscriber per month. Bad.

**What they were doing wrong:**
- One email a month with “Check out our new class schedule”
- No segmentation
- No automation
- No offers specific to email subscribers

**What we changed:**
We set up three automated sequences in Mailchimp (cost: free at their list size):

1. **New subscriber sequence** (5 emails over 14 days)
   - Email 1: “Here’s our class schedule and pricing” (with a direct booking link)
   - Email 2: “Meet Sarah, our instructor who gets asked about hip mobility the most”
   - Email 3: “What to expect on your first visit” (reduces anxiety = increases bookings)
   - Email 4: “Special offer just for email subscribers: first class free”
   - Email 5: “Still thinking? Here’s what our members say”

2. **Lapsed member sequence** (3 emails over 10 days)
   - Email 1: “We miss you. Here’s what’s new in the studio”
   - Email 2: “Come back for 50% off your first month back”
   - Email 3: “Your spot is still here” (personalized, mentions the class they used to take)

3. **Referral sequence** (2 emails)
   - Email 1: “Bring a friend to class, you both get 50% off”
   - Email 2: “Your friend just signed up — here’s your referral credit”

**The outcome:** In 90 days, email revenue went from $200/month to $1,100/month. The new subscriber sequence alone generated 34 new trial pass sign-ups. The lapsed member sequence brought back 12 former members who had stopped attending.

The best part? This took about four hours to set up and costs nothing in software. Mailchimp does it all. Most of the content was stuff the owner already had — they just weren’t using it in sequence.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My studio is full. Why would I care about website conversions?**

Because “full” doesn’t last. Membership churn in fitness is real. If you lose 10 members a month and only replace 8, you’re shrinking. A good website keeps the pipeline flowing even when you’re not thinking about it. Also, the members you have might upgrade from 2x/week to unlimited if your website makes it easy. That’s money you’re leaving on the floor.

**Q: Can’t I just rely on Instagram for new members?**

You can, but Instagram is a rental property. You don’t own your followers. If the algorithm changes (it will), your reach drops. A website you control is an asset. Plus, the people who are ready to buy often search Google, not Instagram. They want to see your schedule and pricing without scrolling through Reels. I’ve seen studios with mediocre Instagram accounts get 60% of their new members from Google searches.

**Q: How long does it take for CRO changes to show results?**

It depends on your traffic volume. If you’re getting 100 visitors a month, you’ll need to wait 2-3 months to see a trend. If you’re getting 1,000 visitors a month, you can see meaningful data in 2-4 weeks. The changes I described in this article (pricing visible, mobile-friendly, GBP optimization) showed results within 30 days for the studios I worked with. But don’t change everything at once or you won’t know what worked.

**Q: Do I need to hire a developer for this?**

No. Most of what I’ve described can be done through your website theme settings, Google Business Profile dashboard, and Mailchimp. If you’re on Squarespace, Wix, or WordPress with a page builder, you can do it yourself. The only time you might need help is if your theme doesn’t support mobile responsiveness or you need custom booking integration. But for 80% of studios, the changes are administrative, not technical.

**Q: Should I put my prices on my website? Won’t my competitors see them?**

Your competitors already know your prices. They can walk in and ask. Or they can check your Instagram and see the rates in the comments. Hiding prices doesn’t protect you — it hurts you. Studies show that 70% of consumers won’t contact a business that doesn’t show pricing. You’re losing the people who are ready to book and keeping only the ones who are willing to jump through hoops. That’s not a higher-quality lead. That’s a tired customer.

**Q: What’s the one thing I should do first?**

Make your class schedule and pricing visible on mobile without requiring a form submission. That’s the single highest-ROI change I’ve seen. It’s free, takes 30 minutes, and immediately removes friction from your booking process. Do it today. Then fix your Google Business Profile. Then set up your email sequences. That’s your order of operations.
