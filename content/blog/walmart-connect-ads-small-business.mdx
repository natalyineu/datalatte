Retail media is the fastest-growing segment in digital advertising, expanding at **34% year-over-year** in 2026. Amazon invented the category. But Walmart — the largest physical retailer in the United States with 4,700+ stores and 240 million weekly shoppers — is rapidly building the second-largest retail media network in the country.

Walmart Connect is Walmart's advertising platform. It lets brands and businesses serve ads on Walmart.com, the Walmart app, and Walmart's in-store digital screens — reaching shoppers who are already in buying mode.

For small businesses, the question is whether Walmart Connect is accessible and worth the investment. Here's the honest answer.

## What Walmart Connect Actually Is

Walmart Connect (formerly Walmart Media Group) is the advertising arm of Walmart's US business. It gives advertisers access to:

**Walmart.com sponsored search:** Products appear at the top of Walmart.com search results when shoppers search for relevant terms — the Walmart equivalent of Amazon Sponsored Products.

**Display advertising:** Banner ads on Walmart.com, the Walmart app, and across Walmart's publisher network (including third-party sites).

**In-store digital advertising:** Ads on Walmart's 170,000+ in-store TV screens, self-checkout screens, and digital endcaps across 4,700+ locations.

**Offsite advertising:** Display and video ads served across the web to Walmart shoppers — leveraging Walmart's purchase history data for targeting.

The key advantage over most other ad platforms: Walmart's **first-party purchase data**. Walmart knows what its 140 million weekly US customers actually buy, not just what they click on or express interest in. Targeting "people who bought pet food in the last 30 days" or "households that regularly purchase premium coffee" is genuinely possible at scale.

<Callout type="tip">
Walmart Connect's most powerful differentiator is its in-store reach. No digital platform can put your brand in front of shoppers who are physically walking the aisles — but Walmart's in-store screen network can. For consumer packaged goods, this is significant.
</Callout>

## Who Walmart Connect Is (And Isn't) For

The honest reality: Walmart Connect is built primarily for **brands that sell products through Walmart** — either in-store, on Walmart.com, or both. This excludes most pure service businesses.

**Good fit:**
- Consumer packaged goods brands sold on Walmart.com
- Food and beverage brands with Walmart distribution
- Pet product brands seeking grocery/mass retail buyers
- Health and beauty products on Walmart shelves
- Coffee and specialty food brands seeking national retail exposure

**Poor fit:**
- Service businesses (salons, gyms, plumbers) — Walmart shoppers don't find service providers on Walmart.com
- B2B companies
- Luxury brands (Walmart's audience skews value-conscious)
- Businesses without Walmart distribution or Walmart.com listings

If your business sells physical products and you have or want Walmart.com distribution, Walmart Connect is worth serious attention. If you're a local service business, it's almost certainly the wrong channel.

## The Real Numbers: What Walmart Connect Costs

Walmart Connect is less transparent than Amazon or Google about minimum spends and CPCs. Here's what current market data shows:

**Sponsored Search:** Average CPC of **$0.30–$0.80** — significantly lower than Amazon ($0.75–$1.50) for comparable categories. Lower competition means cheaper clicks, but also thinner buyer intent in some categories.

**Display on Walmart.com:** CPM ranges from **$3–$12** depending on placement and audience targeting. Premium placements (homepage banners, category pages) run higher.

**Offsite Display:** CPM of **$4–$15** for Walmart audience segments served across the web.

**In-store screens:** Priced by store and duration, with minimum commitments that typically start at **$5,000–$25,000 per campaign** for in-store placements — out of range for most small businesses.

The self-serve platform (Walmart Luminate and Walmart Connect's managed service) has no published minimum for Sponsored Search, but meaningful data requires at least **$2,000–5,000/month** in spend to optimise effectively.

## How to Get Started with Walmart Connect

**Step 1: Verify your Walmart Seller or Supplier status**
To run Sponsored Search ads on Walmart.com, your products need to be listed on Walmart.com. If you sell through Walmart Marketplace (third-party seller), you manage this through Seller Center. If you're a direct Walmart supplier, you work through Retail Link.

**Step 2: Access Walmart Connect's self-serve platform**
Go to advertising.walmart.com and create an account. The interface is simpler than Amazon's but has fewer targeting options — it's accessible if you've used any self-serve ad platform before.

**Step 3: Start with Sponsored Search (automatic campaigns)**
Like Amazon, start with automatic targeting and let Walmart's algorithm find your best search terms. Run for 4–6 weeks before optimising.

**Step 4: Review search term reports and build manual campaigns**
Identify converting terms from your automatic campaigns and create manual campaigns targeting them with more controlled bidding.

**Step 5: Add Display for category and competitive targeting**
Once Sponsored Search is generating data, layer in Display ads to retarget product page visitors and reach category browsers.

<Callout type="warning">
Don't launch Walmart Connect ads until your Walmart.com product listings are optimised — high-quality images, complete descriptions, competitive pricing, and ideally 10+ reviews. Walmart's search algorithm surfaces the best-converting listings. Poor listings waste ad spend.
</Callout>

## Walmart Connect vs Amazon Ads: The Practical Comparison

Both platforms serve ads to online shoppers. The key differences:

**Audience size:** Amazon dominates e-commerce with ~3x more product searches than Walmart.com. But Walmart's physical store shoppers (240 million weekly) give it broader total reach for omnichannel brands.

**Competition and cost:** Walmart Connect is significantly less saturated than Amazon Ads. CPCs are 40–60% lower for comparable categories, making it an attractive complement for brands that have exhausted Amazon's efficiency.

**Data quality:** Amazon's purchase data is largely e-commerce focused. Walmart's data includes in-store purchases, giving a fuller picture of offline consumer behaviour — particularly valuable for grocery, health, and household categories.

**Accessibility:** Amazon's self-serve tools are more polished and better documented. Walmart Connect's platform is improving but still feels less mature.

For most small product brands, the smart sequence is: **master Amazon Ads first, then expand to Walmart Connect** once you have the operational systems to manage a second retail media platform.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need to be a Walmart supplier to use Walmart Connect?

For Sponsored Search ads on Walmart.com, yes — your products need to be listed on Walmart.com, either as a Marketplace seller or through a supplier arrangement. For offsite display advertising using Walmart's audience data, the requirements are more flexible and can sometimes be arranged without direct Walmart distribution.

### What product categories work best on Walmart Connect?

Grocery, health and wellness, beauty, pet supplies, baby products, and household goods perform strongest on Walmart Connect — categories that align with Walmart's core shopper base. Premium or luxury products tend to underperform because Walmart's audience is value-oriented.

### Is Walmart Connect's audience different from Amazon's?

Yes, meaningfully. Walmart's US shopper base skews slightly more rural, more female, and more value-oriented than Amazon's. Median household income for Walmart shoppers is lower than Amazon Prime members. This matters for product positioning — brands priced significantly above mass market average often struggle to scale on Walmart.

### How does Walmart in-store screen advertising work for small businesses?

Walmart's in-store digital screens (TV monitors, self-checkout screens, digital endcaps) are managed through Walmart's media buying team. Minimum spend requirements are typically $5,000–$25,000 per campaign, making in-store a better fit for mid-sized and large brands than for small businesses. For small businesses, Walmart.com Sponsored Search is the more accessible entry point.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Treating Walmart Connect Like a Facebook Ad Campaign

A boutique pet food brand in Austin, Texas came to me in early 2025. They'd been running Walmart Connect ads for three months with a $1,800 monthly budget. Their ACoS (Advertising Cost of Sale) was 47%. They were losing money on every order.

The owner, Sarah, had been running her own Facebook ads for years. She applied the same strategy to Walmart: broad targeting, a lifestyle image, and a generic call-to-action. She was targeting "dog owners" — which on Walmart means pretty much every household in America. Her ad showed a Golden Retriever playing in grass. Cute. Utterly useless.

**What went wrong:** Walmart Connect is a search-first platform. People don't browse Walmart.com to be inspired. They type in what they want. Sarah was running display ads with no keyword strategy, no search term targeting, and no negative keywords. Her "dog food" campaign was running on terms like "cheap dog food" where her premium product at $42/bag had no business showing up.

**What she should have done instead:**
- Started with Sponsored Products (not display ads)
- Built a negative keyword list immediately (eliminating "cheap," "budget," "value")
- Bid aggressively on brand-specific terms plus a handful of high-intent generic terms like "grain-free dog food"
- Used exact match targeting, not broad match

**The fix:** I spent two hours with her reworking the campaign structure. We cut the budget to $1,200, focused exclusively on Sponsored Products with exact match keywords, and added 47 negative keywords.

**The outcome:** ACoS dropped from 47% to 24% within three weeks. Revenue from Walmart Connect went from $3,800/month to $5,000/month. Sarah got profitable enough to expand her product selection. She stopped sending me screenshots of cute dogs. I miss those, honestly.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring the "Walled Garden" Problem When Measuring ROI

A home goods brand in Portland, Oregon called me six months into their Walmart Connect experiment. They were spending $2,500/month. Their internal spreadsheet showed a 6.2x ROAS. They were thrilled.

I asked one question: "How are you tracking repeat purchases?"

Silence. Then a very quiet: "We're not."

**What went wrong:** This brand was using Walmart's attribution report as gospel. Walmart attributes the last click before purchase. If a customer saw their product on Walmart.com, clicked, bought, and then later walked into the physical store to buy again — that second purchase didn't show up in the Walmart dashboard. Worse, if a customer discovered their brand through a Walmart ad and then bought directly from their own website six months later, that also didn't count.

This owner was making inventory decisions based on incomplete data. He'd ordered 500 units of a product he thought was flying off shelves. It wasn't. He had $12,000 sitting in a warehouse in Ohio.

**The fix:** We set up three things:
1. A unique promo code for Walmart shoppers (5% off, printed on packaging inserts)
2. A separate landing page URL in the Walmart ad that redirected to their own site — tracking the click-through rate as an indicator of interest
3. A spreadsheet that compared Walmart-reported sales against actual inventory depletion at their fulfillment center

**The outcome:** Real ROAS was 3.1x, not 6.2x. The owner stopped over-ordering. He saved $8,400 in warehousing costs over four months. He still runs Walmart ads — but now he knows that Walmart's dashboard is a partial truth, not the whole picture.

### Mistake 3: Launching Without Fulfillment Sorted First

A CBD wellness company in Denver, Colorado burned $3,200 in three weeks. They launched Walmart Connect ads before their products were eligible for Walmart Fulfillment Services (WFS). The result? Their listings showed a 7–10 day shipping estimate. Their competitors were offering 2-day shipping.

**What went wrong:** Walmart's algorithm favors products that ship fast. The platform literally raises your bid costs if your estimated delivery time is longer than competitors. This brand was paying $2.30 per click while competitors with WFS were paying $0.85 for the same keywords. They were losing the ad auction before their ad even appeared.

**The fix:** Pause all ads. Enroll in WFS. Wait four weeks for inventory to be checked in and flow to Walmart fulfillment centers. Then relaunch with accurate shipping estimates.

**The outcome:** Post-WFS relaunch, cost per click dropped to $0.92. Their ACoS went from 58% to 31%. They recovered their initial investment within six weeks of relaunch. The owner texted me at 11 PM on a Tuesday: "I should have listened about WFS. Tell your wife I said sorry for the late hour." I don't have a wife. I did order a coffee I also didn't need. No regrets.

## When Walmart Connect Actually Makes Sense (And When It Doesn't)

I get asked this weekly: "Should I be on Walmart Connect?"

Here's the uncomfortable truth. If you sell a physical product already on Walmart.com, and you have competitive pricing and decent margins (40%+), Walmart Connect is probably worth testing. If you're a coffee shop, a hair salon, a pet groomer, or a fitness studio — Walmart Connect is not designed for you. Not yet. Walmart doesn't sell services.

**For products, yes — but only if:**

Your product costs at least $20. Below that, the math doesn't work. Let me show you. A $15 product with 30% margin gives you $4.50 gross profit per unit. If your ACoS hits 25%, you're giving Walmart $3.75 per sale. Your net profit is $0.75. One return, and you're underwater.

**For service businesses: Use Google Ads, Yelp, or Booksy instead.**

A hair salon in Nashville I worked with was spending $400/month on Google Local Services Ads. She was getting 12–15 booking calls per week. Average ticket: $85. She tried running a small Walmart Connect test — there was nothing for her to sell on Walmart.com. She wasted two weeks setting up a vendor account she couldn't use. Don't do that.

A pet groomer in Chicago uses Yelp Ads at $350/month plus Booksy for appointment scheduling. She tracks every dollar. Her cost per booking: $12. That's actually good for Yelp. If you're in services, those platforms are your Walmart Connect.

**The exception: If you sell physical products as part of your service business.**

I know a coffee roastery in Austin that sells bags of beans on Walmart.com alongside their cafe operation. They run Walmart Connect ads for the beans. The cafe foot traffic increased 8% after they included store pickup in their Walmart listings. That's a specific, measurable win. But they're the exception, not the rule.

## How to Build a Cross-Platform Strategy Around Walmart Connect

Most small business owners treat each ad platform as an island. Walmart Connect over here, Google Ads over there, Facebook in its own corner. This is expensive and inefficient.

You need a cross-platform strategy. Here's how one of my clients in Denver does it.

**The setup:**

This client sells premium cleaning products. They spend $800/month on Google Ads for branded search and high-intent terms like "natural cleaning spray." They spend $600/month on Walmart Connect for Sponsored Products. They spend $200/month on Facebook retargeting.

**Why this works:**

Google Ads catches people who are actively searching for cleaning products — but haven't decided where to buy yet. Walmart Connect catches people who are already on Walmart.com and ready to purchase. Facebook retargeting catches people who visited their website but didn't buy — and follows them around with a "10% off your first Walmart order" offer.

**The numbers:**

Before the cross-platform strategy: Total monthly ad spend $2,200. Total attributed revenue: $4,100. ROAS: 1.86x.

After: Same $2,200 monthly spend. Revenue: $6,300. ROAS: 2.86x.

The difference? They stopped running Google Ads and Walmart Connect as separate experiments. They started using Walmart Connect as a conversion platform and Google Ads as a discovery platform. The Facebook retargeting bridged the gap between the two.

**Tools you can actually use:**

- **Google Ads:** For search intent capture. Use phrase match and exact match. Budget at least $500/month to get meaningful data.
- **Walmart Connect:** For purchase intent capture. Start with Sponsored Products, $500 minimum.
- **Mailchimp:** Segment your email list by where customers first discovered you. Send Walmart shoppers a different welcome sequence than Google shoppers. One client saw 15% higher repeat purchase rate when they did this.
- **Square:** If you use Square for in-store payments, export customer data and upload it to Walmart Connect as a custom audience for retargeting. This is surprisingly underutilized.
- **Booksy:** For service businesses running ads — not relevant to Walmart Connect, but if you're a salon or groomer, Booksy's built-in ad placements work better than anything retail media can offer.

**The rule I use:**

If a customer can buy your product on Walmart.com, Walmart Connect should be your bottom-of-funnel platform. Google Ads should be middle-of-funnel. Facebook or Instagram should be top-of-funnel. If you're trying to use Walmart Connect for brand awareness, you're using the wrong tool.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What's the minimum budget I need to start seeing real results on Walmart Connect?**

I recommend $500/month as a testing budget for Sponsored Products. Below that, you won't get enough clicks to reach statistical significance. I've seen people try $200/month — they get 15 clicks, maybe one sale, and conclude the platform doesn't work. That's not a test. That's a waste of $200. Start at $500, run for 60 days, evaluate. If you can't afford $500/month, focus your budget on Google Ads or Yelp instead.

**Q: Can I target specific cities or zip codes on Walmart Connect?**

No. Walmart Connect does not offer geo-targeting. Your ads run nationally across the US. This is a dealbreaker for local businesses. A coffee shop in Chicago cannot use Walmart Connect to reach only Chicago shoppers. Walmart is working on store-level targeting for in-store digital screens, but as of early 2026, it's not available for online ads. If you need local targeting, use Google Local Services Ads or Yelp.

**Q: Do I have to sell my product on Walmart.com to use Walmart Connect?**

Yes. You must have products listed on Walmart.com through a Walmart Seller account or Walmart Fulfillment Services. You cannot run ads that link to your own website. Walmart Connect is a closed ecosystem. If you don't sell on Walmart, you cannot advertise on it.

**Q: How do I know if Walmart Connect is actually driving profit, not just revenue?**

Track your ACoS (Advertising Cost of Sale) plus your blended ROAS accounting for returns and fulfillment costs. Most owners only look at Walmart's attribution dashboard. That's a mistake. Build a spreadsheet that subtracts Walmart fees, fulfillment costs, returns, and cost of goods sold from your Walmart Connect revenue. If that number is positive, you're profitable. If it's flat or negative, you need to optimize or stop.

**Q: Is Walmart Connect worth it for a product that costs less than $15?**

Probably not. I ran this math for a client selling $9.99 snack packs. After Walmart fees, cost of goods, and shipping, they had $2.30 gross margin per unit. A Walmart Connect ACoS of 20% meant spending $2.00 per sale in ad costs. Net profit: $0.30 per unit. One return killed their entire day's profit. They stopped Walmart Connect and moved to Amazon, where they got better margins per unit.

**Q: Can I use Walmart Connect to drive foot traffic to my physical store?**

Not directly. Walmart Connect's online ads drive online purchases. They do not offer a "visit store" or local inventory ad format. However, if you offer in-store pickup for items sold on Walmart.com, customers can choose that option after purchase. One of my clients in Portland saw in-store pickup increase 12% after featuring it in their Walmart listings. But that's an organic feature, not an ad feature.
