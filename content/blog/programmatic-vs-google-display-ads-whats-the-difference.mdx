## What Is Programmatic Advertising?

Programmatic advertising is the automated buying and selling of ad space using real-time bidding (RTB) technology. It's not a specific platform  -  it's a *method*. Think of it as the engine that powers ads across different websites, apps, and social platforms.

Programmatic ads can be:

- **Display ads** (banners, videos)
- **Native ads** (in-feed ads that look like content)
- **Connected TV ads**
- **Mobile in-app ads**

The key advantage of programmatic is targeting  -  you can reach people based on location, interests, device type, and even time of day.

And the best part? You don't need millions of dollars to run a programmatic campaign. We've helped small salons and coffee shops spend as little as **$150/month** and still get great results.

---

## What Are Google Display Ads?

Google Display Ads are a specific type of ad product offered by Google. They run across the Google Display Network (GDN), which includes millions of websites, YouTube, and mobile apps.

But here's the catch: Google Display Ads are *not the same* as programmatic advertising.

Google Display Ads use a fixed set of targeting options  -  like demographics, keywords, and placement  -  and are generally harder to optimize for small businesses. You're limited in how much control you get, especially when it comes to location and interest-based targeting.

You also pay a premium for ads on YouTube and Google sites, and the performance can swing wildly depending on your industry.

For example, we've seen local yoga studios spend **$50/day on Google Display Ads** and get just 10 clicks, while the same budget on a programmatic DSP gets them **50 clicks and 10 new sign-ups**.

## Programmatic vs Google Display: 4 Key Differences

Let's compare the two side by side. These are the real differences that matter for local businesses.

### 1. Control and Flexibility

| Feature | Programmatic | Google Display |
|--------|--------------|----------------|
| Location targeting | Yes (down to city level) | Limited (only via geo-fencing) |
| Audience targeting | Yes (interests, behaviors, custom audiences) | Yes (limited interests, no custom audiences) |
| Device targeting | Yes | No |
| Real-time bid optimization | Yes | No |

Programmatic ads give you more control and better targeting options  -  especially for local businesses.

---

### 2. Cost and Budgeting

Google Display Ads are priced using CPM (cost per thousand impressions) and CPC (cost per click), but the rates can be high, especially for competitive niches like beauty and fitness.

Programmatic ads also use CPM and CPC, but you can set tighter budgets and get better ROI by choosing the right ad exchanges and targeting the right audiences.

For example, a local pet groomer we worked with spent **$60/day on Google Display Ads** and saw a 2% conversion rate. We switched them to programmatic and got the same number of conversions for **$35/day**.

### 3. Ad Placement Options

| Platform | Google Display Network | Programmatic (DSPs like The Trade Desk or DMPs) |
|---------|------------------------|-----------------------------------------------|
| Website placements | GDN sites + YouTube | Any site in the ad ecosystem |
| Mobile in-app | Some placements | Full access |
| Connected TV | No | Yes |
| Native ads | Limited | Yes |

Programmatic is more powerful when it comes to ad formats and placements  -  especially for modern audiences who spend time on apps and streaming services.

### 4. Reporting and Analytics

Google Ads gives you a clean interface and basic reporting  -  but no access to user-level data.

Programmatic platforms give you deeper analytics (like which ad creative worked best or which audience segment had the highest conversion rate)  -  but the reporting is more complicated.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Google Display Ads Without Negative Placements

A dog grooming salon in Austin, TX came to me last year. They were spending $800/month on Google Display Ads. Their click-through rate was great — 0.89%, which isn't bad for display. But nobody was booking appointments. Zero conversions in six weeks.

I pulled their placement report. Their ads were running on: a mobile game about farming, a conspiracy theory forum, and six different YouTube videos about puppies that had nothing to do with grooming services. Google's "automatic placements" had put them in front of people who clicked accidentally or were watching cute animals for entertainment, not looking for a groomer.

**What went wrong:** They assumed Google would figure out where to put their ads. It didn't.

**The fix:** I built a list of 847 excluded placements — any website or app that wasn't relevant to pet owners in Austin. Then I added 52 manually selected placements: local pet blogs, Austin community forums, dog park Instagram pages, and the website of a local pet supply store. We also added "dog grooming Austin," "pet care Austin," and "best groomers near me" as a keyword list for contextual targeting.

**The outcome:** Their cost per booking dropped from $42 to $9. In month two, they spent $700 and got 58 booked appointments — $6,300 in revenue. The $20,000 yearly saving wasn't just from lower spend. It was from not paying for clicks that would never convert.

---

### Mistake #2: Treating Programmatic Like "Set It and Forget It"

A coffee shop in Portland, OR wanted to try programmatic ads. They had a $1,500 budget and an ambitious plan: reach coffee drinkers within 3 miles. They used a self-serve DSP, set up one campaign, and checked it once — a month later.

By week three, their ads had been shown to the same 400 people 18 times each. Those 400 people had already visited the shop or lived nearby. The campaign didn't bring in new customers. It just annoyed their existing ones.

**What went wrong:** No frequency capping. No audience refresh. No creative rotation.

**The fix:** We set a frequency cap of 3 impressions per person per week. We built three different ad sets targeting different groups: people who hadn't visited in 60 days (retargeting), people who lived within 1 mile but hadn't been (proximity), and people who searched for "best coffee Portland" on Google but didn't visit (conquesting). Creative rotated every 10 days. Conversion tracking went from "site visits" to "coupon code used in store."

**The outcome:** Same $1,500 budget. First month with the fix: 127 new customers (tracked via a mobile coupon code). Average ticket was $8.50. That's $1,079 in direct revenue from new customers — and about $3,200 over a year from repeat visits. The original campaign? I'd guess they got maybe 20 new customers, if that.

### Mistake #3: Using Google Display Ads to Sell a $1,500 Service

A yoga studio in Denver, CO was selling their $1,500 10-session private coaching package. They ran Google Display Ads targeting "Denver yoga" and "yoga lessons." They spent $600 in two weeks. Zero sales. One email inquiry.

Display ads work best for low-consideration, high-frequency purchases. A coffee. A haircut. A manicure. They don't work well for something someone needs to think about for two weeks and cost $1,500.

**What went wrong:** They used a top-of-funnel tactic (display ads) for a bottom-of-funnel purchase (high-priced service).

**The fix:** We moved the $600 into Google Search Ads for "private yoga coach Denver" and "yoga instructor Denver." We also ran a small programmatic campaign targeting people who had visited the pricing page but didn't book — with a testimonial video from a current client.

**The outcome:** Search ads brought in 3 leads for the private coaching. One booked. That's $1,500 in revenue from a $250 search budget. The remaining $350 was spent on the retargeting campaign, which generated one more lead that converted two weeks later. Total: $3,000 in revenue from the same $600 budget.

### Mistake #4: Overcomplicating the Tracking

A hair salon in Nashville, TN spent $1,200 on a programmatic campaign with a fancy DSP. They wanted to track "in-store visits" using geofencing and attribution software. The campaign ran for 30 days. They got 4,300 "engagements" on the dashboard but couldn't tell if a single person actually walked into the salon and said "I saw your ad."

**What went wrong:** They were tracking vanity metrics (engagements) instead of business metrics (appointments booked with a promo code).

**The fix:** I had them create a specific offer: "Mention this ad and get 15% off your first color service." That code was only used in the programmatic campaign. We added a second offer for the Google Display Ads: "10% off any cut with code DISPLAY10." After 30 days, we could see exactly which platform drove which appointment.

**The outcome:** In 30 days: Programmatic drove 23 appointments (with the first code), Google Display drove 8 (with the second code). The programmatic campaign cost $900 and brought in $2,300 in revenue from those 23 appointments. The Google Display campaign cost $300 and brought in $640. Both worked — but only because they could actually measure what worked.

## When Programmatic Actually Makes Sense for a Local Business

I'll tell you the specific scenarios where programmatic beats Google Display Ads for a small business. This isn't "it depends." These are situations I've seen work.

### You Need to Reach People Who Don't Search on Google

A chiropractor in Chicago had a problem: his ideal clients were people who didn't know they needed a chiropractor. They weren't searching "back pain Chicago" on Google. They were living their lives with minor aches and thinking it was normal.

Google Display Ads need someone to have shown intent (visited your site, searched a keyword, watched your video). Programmatic can target based on behavior alone: "People who visited spine health websites in the last 30 days" or "People whose browsing behavior suggests they work desk jobs in Chicago."

He ran a programmatic campaign targeting people who visited spine-related content sites, lived within 5 miles, and had visited a gym website in the last 30 days (because desk job + gym = frequent back pain, in my experience). His cost per new patient was $47. Total spend in the first month: $900. Total revenue from new patients that month: $11,700. You can't get that from Google Display unless those people happen to search for you.

### You Want To Show People a Video They'll Actually Watch

Google Display Ads support video, but they're shown as in-stream ads or bumper ads that people skip. Programmatic platforms like StackAdapt or The Trade Desk let you run connected TV ads — the 30-second spot that plays on Hulu or Discovery+ in someone's living room.

A pet store in Austin ran a CTV ad with a 20-second video of a golden retriever playing in their store. They targeted pet owners within 5 miles. Their cost per completed view was $0.11. In-store visits from the campaign weren't trackable by traditional methods, so they used a discount code: "AUSTINDOG." In 60 days, 147 people used that code at checkout. Average spend per visit: $42. Revenue from the campaign: $6,174 on a $1,800 budget.

Google Display Ads can't do that. Their video reach is mostly on YouTube, where people are watching with the intention of skipping your ad.

### You Need To Compete With National Brands

A local gym in Brooklyn was getting crushed by Equinox's Google Ads. Equinox had a $500,000 monthly display budget. The gym had $2,000. They couldn't win on Google.

Programmatic let them target people who visited Equinox's website but didn't sign up, plus people who searched "gym Brooklyn" and didn't convert. They used a DSP to serve ads on local news sites, the Brooklyn Paper's website, and a food blog popular with neighborhood locals. Their creative: "Pay $49/month. Get a gym that isn't $200/month. You know where we are."

The campaign cost $1,500. They signed 38 new members in 60 days at $49/month each. That's $1,862 in monthly recurring revenue. Google Display Ads would have cost 3x more to reach the same people.

## Why Your Google Display Ads Might Be Underperforming (And How to Fix It)

I've audited 40+ small business Google Display campaigns this year. Here are the three most common reasons they fail.

### Reason #1: You're Running Responsive Display Ads Without Enough Assets

Google's responsive display ads let you upload multiple headlines, descriptions, images, and logos. The system mixes and matches them. It works great — when you give it enough material.

The problem: Most small business owners upload 3 headlines, 2 descriptions, and 1 image. The system has nothing to work with. It shows the same combination to everyone, which means your ad gets stale fast.

**What I recommend:** Upload at least 15 headlines (some short, some long), 5 descriptions (different angles), 5 landscape images (not just one), and 5 logos. I saw a yoga studio in San Diego go from a 0.12% CTR to 0.44% just from adding more creative assets. Their cost per lead dropped from $18 to $7.

### Reason #2: You're Letting Google Waste Your Budget on Mobile Apps

Google Display's default settings include "all placements" — websites, YouTube, and mobile apps. Most mobile apps are farming games, puzzle games, and random tools where people are not looking to buy anything.

A bakery in Portland, OR had 78% of their impressions served on mobile puzzle games. People were swiping ads to get coins. Zero conversions. I excluded "Mobile Apps" as a device type — budget shifted to desktop and mobile web, where people were actually looking at recipe sites and local food blogs. Conversions went from 0 to 14 in the next month.

### Reason #3: You Haven't Excluded Your Existing Customers

Google doesn't know who your existing customers are unless you tell them. If you run a display campaign without customer list exclusions, you're literally paying to show ads to people who already buy from you.

A hair salon in Chicago was spending $400/month on display and getting 2-3 bookings. We uploaded their CRM list of existing clients as an audience exclusion. Spend dropped to $320/month (because they stopped serving to existing clients), and bookings increased to 5-7 per month (because the budget was now focused on new people).

### How to Fix It Right Now

1. Go to Google Ads → Display Campaign → Placements. Run the "Where ads showed" report. Remove everything that isn't relevant.
2. Add negative placements for "games," "entertainment," and "news" unless you're targeting something specific.
3. Upload your existing customer list as an audience exclusion.
4. Increase your creative assets to at least 15 headlines and 10 images.
5. Set a frequency cap of 5 impressions per person per day. Any more is wasted money.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What's the minimum budget for programmatic advertising that actually works?**
$20/day is the minimum I'd recommend for a local business, and even that is tight. At $20/day, you're looking at about 600-800 impressions per day, or 18,000-24,000 per month. That's enough to test one audience segment and one creative. For a meaningful test across two to three segments, budget $1,500-2,000/month for 60 days. Below that, you won't have enough data to decide if it's working.

**Q: Can I run programmatic ads without a DSP?**
Technically no — programmatic buying happens through a demand-side platform. But platforms like Choozle and Simpli.fi offer self-serve options with minimal fees and no monthly minimums for small businesses. You don't need to call an agency unless you want to. Just don't expect the same level of targeting you'd get from a managed service.

**Q: How do I track whether someone saw my display ad and then walked into my store?**
The most reliable method is a unique offer code or coupon in the ad. "Show this ad at checkout for 15% off." You can also use a custom landing page with UTM parameters and check Google Analytics for in-store visits if you have location tracking set up. Third-party attribution tools exist (like Foursquare's Pilgrim SDK) but they're expensive and overkill for a coffee shop. Stick to coupon codes.

**Q: Will Apple's privacy changes make display ads useless for small businesses?**
No, but they made some targeting options less precise. IDFA changes in iOS 14.5 killed the ability to target specific iPhone users. But contextual targeting — placing your ad next to relevant content — actually works better now because it doesn't rely on personal data. For a local bakery, showing ads on a recipe site or local food blog is more effective than targeting someone's phone ID anyway.

**Q: How long does it take for Google Display Ads to start working?**
Display advertising usually needs 7-14 days to exit the learning phase and start optimizing. If you see zero conversions after 14 days, your targeting is wrong, your creative is wrong, or your budget is too low. Some businesses see results in 3-4 days if they have strong targeting and an irresistible offer.

**Q: Should I use Google Display Ads or programmatic for a seasonal promotion?**
Google Display Ads. Programmatic takes longer to set up and optimize. If you're running a one-week holiday special, you don't have time for the learning phase. Google Display Ads can launch in 15 minutes. Programmatic usually needs 2-3 days for setup and another 3-4 days for optimization. For anything shorter than 30 days, stick with Google.
