Here's how to get started:

### 1. Identify Your Target Audience

As a plumber, your target audience is likely homeowners in your local area who need emergency repairs or scheduled maintenance. Use tools like Google Trends and Google Keyword Planner to identify relevant keywords and phrases your customers are searching for, such as "emergency plumber near me" or "plumbing services in [your city]."

### 2. Set Up Your Google Ads Campaigns

Create separate campaigns for emergency services and scheduled maintenance to target different customer needs. Use location targeting to reach customers in your local area, and set up ad extensions to showcase your business hours, address, and phone number.

### 3. Create Compelling Ad Copy

Write ad copy that speaks directly to your target audience's pain points and needs. Use clear, concise language to explain your services and benefits, and include calls-to-action to drive customers to your website or phone number.

### 4. Optimize for Conversions

Use conversion tracking to measure the success of your campaigns and make data-driven decisions. Set up conversion goals for phone calls, website form submissions, and in-store visits to track the effectiveness of your ads.

### 5. Monitor and Optimize Your Ads

Regularly monitor your ad performance and make adjustments as needed. Use tools like Google Ads Editor to modify ad copy, targeting, and bidding strategies to improve your ROI.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've managed Google Ads for dozens of service businesses across the US. Plumbing accounts are some of the trickiest, because the difference between a profitable campaign and a money pit often comes down to three or four specific decisions. Here are the mistakes I see most often — and what actually works instead.

### Mistake 1: Running One Campaign for Everything

**The story:** A plumbing company in Austin, TX — let's call them Atlas Plumbing — came to me after spending $3,200 in six weeks with exactly two booked jobs. They had one campaign with keywords like "water heater repair," "emergency plumber Austin," and "drain cleaning" all mixed together. Their ad copy said "24/7 service" and "schedule your appointment" in the same headline. The problem was obvious: someone searching for a burst pipe at 2 AM and someone searching for a slow drain on a Tuesday afternoon want completely different things. By bundling them, Atlas was showing emergency messaging to people looking for routine work, and routine messaging to people in crisis. Neither group felt understood.

**The fix:** We split their account into two campaigns. Emergency got its own budget, its own ad copy ("Pipe burst? We're there in 30 minutes"), and its own landing page optimized for immediate action — phone number front and center, no form fields. Scheduled maintenance got separate messaging ("Book your annual inspection, $89"), a call-to-action for appointment booking, and a landing page with photos of their work and testimonials. We also set different geographic radiuses: emergency targeted a 15-mile radius, scheduled work targeted 25 miles to capture people willing to drive for a good deal.

**The outcome:** In the next 60 days, their emergency campaign produced 27 calls and 19 booked jobs. The scheduled maintenance campaign added 12 booked appointments. Total ad spend: $2,100. Revenue: approximately $8,400 from emergency work and $2,100 from maintenance (at roughly $180 per emergency call and $175 per inspection). That's a 5x return on ad spend, up from effectively zero in the previous six weeks. The owner told me, "I can't believe I was trying to sell the same thing to someone with a flooded basement and someone who just wanted their toilet to stop running."

### Mistake 2: Not Using Negative Keywords

**The story:** A plumbing business in Nashville, TN — let's call them Music City Plumbing — was spending $1,200 per month on Google Ads and getting a lot of clicks. Their conversion rate was 1.8%, which felt low but they assumed that was normal. When I looked at their search terms report, I found that 43% of their clicks were coming from people searching for things like "how to fix a running toilet," "DIY plumbing repair," "plumbing apprentice jobs," and "plumbing supply store." These people were not hiring a plumber. They were looking for information, employment, or parts. Music City Plumbing was paying $2.50–$4.00 per click for traffic that would never convert.

**The fix:** I built a negative keyword list with over 200 terms across four categories: DIY-related (fix, how to, tutorial, repair guide), job-seeker related (hiring, plumber jobs, apprenticeship, salary), supply-related (parts, store, wholesale, supplier), and irrelevant modifiers (free, cheap, discount, coupon). We also added "plumber" as a negative keyword in their branded campaigns to avoid cannibalization. More importantly, I set up a regular cadence — every two weeks, pull the search terms report, add new negatives. This is the kind of maintenance most agencies skip because it's tedious, but it's the difference between profitable and wasteful.

**The outcome:** After two weeks of negatives fully ramping up, their click-through rate held steady but their conversion rate jumped from 1.8% to 4.2%. Their cost per lead dropped from $62 to $24. They went from spending $1,200 per month to get about 19 leads to spending $1,100 per month to get 45 leads. The owner said, "I was basically paying Google to send me people who were trying to fix their own sink. That's insane." It is insane. It's also extremely common.

### Mistake 3: Targeting Too Wide a Radius

**The story:** A plumbing company in Portland, OR — let's call them Rose City Repairs — set their targeting to 50 miles around their office. Their reasoning was logical: "We serve a big area, and we want everyone to know about us." The result was that they were showing up for searches in Salem, Oregon City, and even parts of Washington state. The problem wasn't just wasted spend. Someone in Salem searching for "plumber Portland" is likely willing to drive to Portland for a work trip but not for a clogged drain. Rose City was paying for clicks from people who were never going to hire them, and simultaneously lowering their Quality Score because those visitors were bouncing immediately.

**The fix:** I pulled their Google Ads location report and saw that 70% of their business in the last six months came from a 12-mile radius. We tightened targeting to 15 miles for emergency campaigns and 25 miles for scheduled maintenance. For the emergency campaign, we also set a "presence" targeting option — meaning the searcher had to be physically located in that radius at the time of the search, not just interested in the area. This filters out tourists planning ahead, people researching for a relative, and anyone else who isn't in immediate need.

**The outcome:** Their ad spend dropped from $1,800 per month to $1,400 per month. But more importantly, their cost per lead dropped from $55 to $31, and their conversion rate went from 2.9% to 5.1%. They also reduced their call wait time because they were prioritizing leads within their actual service area. The owner told me, "I thought I was just being efficient by covering more ground. Turns out I was paying for people I could never help." The fix took 20 minutes.

## How to Structure Your Bidding Strategy for Plumbing

Most small businesses treat bidding as a set-it-and-forget-it task. For plumbers, that's a mistake. Your willingness to pay for a click should change depending on the time of day, the day of week, and the type of service requested.

**For emergency searches, bid aggressively during high-traffic windows.** Analyze your call data. In most markets, emergency plumbing calls spike between 6 PM and 10 PM on weekdays, and between 10 AM and 4 PM on weekends. That's when people realize a small leak is becoming a flood, or when they finally have time to deal with a toilet that's been running for three days. Use Google Ads' ad scheduling to increase your bids by 20–30% during those windows. Conversely, late-night hours (midnight to 6 AM) have extremely low search volume for most areas, and the few clicks you get are likely from price shoppers or people who aren't in immediate crisis. Lower your bids there.

**For scheduled maintenance, bid consistently but low.** Routine searches like "drain cleaning cost" or "water heater inspection" are the ones where customers are comparing multiple options. They're less urgent and more price-sensitive. Set your bid to be competitive but not maxed out — you want to be in position 2–3, not position 1, for these queries. Position 1 often gets more clicks but also more wasted clicks from people just browsing. Position 2–3 gets better conversion rates because those searchers are further along in the decision process.

**Use target CPA for emergency campaigns and maximize clicks for maintenance.** For emergency, a target CPA of $40–$60 per call is usually reasonable, depending on your average ticket price. Google's automated bidding can optimize for that specific cost, which means you won't overpay for casual searchers. For maintenance, maximize clicks works better because the volume is lower and you want to maintain visibility without overspending. Just set a daily budget cap and monitor.

A plumbing company in Denver that implemented this structure saw their cost per emergency call drop from $78 to $43 in three weeks, and their maintenance appointments increase by 22% without raising spend. The key was treating the two intents as completely different products.

## The Surprising Role of Yelp and Reviews in Your Google Ads Performance

Here's something most guides skip: your Google Ads performance is directly influenced by your Yelp rating and Google Business profile reviews. Not indirectly — directly. Google uses your overall online reputation as a signal for ad quality. A plumbing company with a 4.7-star rating and 100+ reviews will consistently get lower cost-per-click than a competitor with a 3.5-star rating and 12 reviews, even if their ads are identical.

**Why this matters for plumbers:** A friend of mine runs a plumbing company in Chicago. He opened in 2018 and had decent Google Ads performance by year two — cost per lead around $45, conversion rate around 3.5%. Then he got hit with a string of negative reviews from three different customers who were unhappy about pricing. His Google rating dropped from 4.6 to 4.1. Within two weeks, his cost per lead jumped to $68, and his conversion rate dropped to 2.8%. Not because his ads changed — because Google's algorithm decided he was less trustworthy.

**What to do about it:**
- **Claim your Yelp and Google Business profiles.** This sounds obvious, but I've worked with three plumbing businesses in the last year that hadn't claimed their Yelp listing. They were losing potential customers because negative reviews went unanswered.
- **Respond to every review.** Positive reviews get a simple thank-you that mentions a specific detail ("Glad we could help with that water heater in Lincoln Park!"). Negative reviews get a direct, unhurried response that acknowledges the issue and offers a way to make it right. Don't get defensive. I've seen a single thoughtful response increase a company's rating by 0.2 stars over a month.
- **Include review snippets in your ad copy.** If you have a 4.8-star rating, use Google Ads' seller ratings extension. It shows your rating directly in the ad. That little three-word addition — "4.8 stars from 200 reviews" — can increase click-through rates by 10–15% for service businesses.
- **Track your review velocity.** The number of reviews you're getting matters as much as the rating. Google wants to see that you're actively accumulating reviews, not just sitting on a static number. Aim for 3–5 new reviews per week. Tools like Broadly or Podium can automate the request process via text message after a service call. A plumbing company in Austin that implemented Podium went from 45 reviews to 230 reviews in 90 days, and their cost per lead dropped by 18% during that same period.

**One more thing about Yelp:** Don't trust their advertising sales reps when they tell you that you need to buy Yelp ads to rank higher in organic search. That's false. Yelp does not give algorithmic preference to advertisers. However, having a complete Yelp profile — lots of photos, detailed service descriptions, hours, and answered reviews — does help you rank higher in Yelp's own search results, which is where a significant portion of your potential customers will research you before clicking your Google ad.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads per month as a plumber?**
Start with $1,000–$1,500 per month if you're in a mid-sized city, $2,000–$3,000 if you're in a major metro like NYC or LA. That's enough to generate 20–40 leads per month with a properly set up campaign. Any less and you won't have enough data to optimize. That said, don't set a budget and walk away. The first month is about learning — testing keywords, ad copy, and landing pages. You might spend $2,000 and get $4,000 in revenue, or you might spend $2,000 and get $800. If the latter happens, it means your account structure or targeting is broken, not that Google Ads doesn't work.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads myself, or should I hire someone?**
You can run it yourself, and many plumbers do. But understand that "running it" means more than setting up a campaign and letting it run. You need to check your search terms report weekly, add negative keywords, test ad copy, adjust bids based on time of day, and monitor your landing page performance. If you have the time and patience, you can probably manage it yourself after two to three months of learning. If you're already working 60 hours a week on service calls, hire someone who charges a flat monthly fee (not a percentage of spend, which incentivizes them to spend more). A good agency or freelancer will save you money.

**Q: What about bad reviews — do they kill my Google Ads performance?**
They don't kill it, but they do raise your costs. A 3.5-star rating vs. a 4.7-star rating can mean paying 30–50% more per click for the same keywords. The fix isn't to try to remove negative reviews (which is almost impossible). It's to respond to them professionally and to flood your profile with positive reviews so that the negative ones get drowned out. If 98% of your 200 reviews are positive, one negative review doesn't matter. If 10 of your 30 reviews are negative, that's a problem.

**Q: Should I only target emergency keywords, or should I also target routine work?**
Both. But you should treat them as separate campaigns with separate budgets and separate ad copy. Emergency keywords have higher search intent and higher conversion rates, but also higher cost-per-click. Routine keywords have lower cost-per-click and lower conversion rates, but higher lifetime value because those customers often become repeat clients. A plumbing company I worked with in Nashville split their budget 60/40 — 60% emergency, 40% routine — and saw a better ROI than when they ran everything together. Test your own mix and adjust based on which keywords actually produce booked jobs.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from Google Ads for plumbing?**
You'll see impressions and clicks within hours of launching your campaign. But meaningful results — calls that turn into booked jobs — usually take two to three weeks. The first week is for Google's algorithm to learn your campaign structure and bidding. The second week is for data collection and initial optimization. By week three, you should have enough data to start making informed decisions. If you're not seeing at least a 3:1 return on ad spend by the end of month two, something is wrong with your setup.

**Q: What if I'm in a small town — does Google Ads still work?**
Yes, but the strategy changes. In a small town with low search volume, you need to bid on broader keywords that combine your service with your town name and the 2–3 nearest cities. You also need to rely more on Google Business profile optimization than on paid ads. A plumber in a town of 8,000 people might get only 50–100 searches per month for relevant keywords. With that volume, you're better off spending $300 per month on Google Ads as a supplement and investing the rest of your marketing budget into local Facebook groups, Nextdoor, and direct mail. Don't ignore ads, but don't expect them to drive the majority of your business the way they can in a city of 500,000.
