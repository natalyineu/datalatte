## Understanding Your HVAC Business Goals

To create an effective marketing strategy, you need to understand your business goals. What do you want to achieve? Do you want to increase emergency calls, boost routine maintenance bookings, or expand your service area? Knowing your goals will help you focus on the right marketing tactics.

## Local SEO for HVAC Companies

Local SEO is critical for HVAC companies. You want to appear in search results when people in your area search for HVAC services. Here are some local SEO tips:

* Claim and optimize your Google My Business listing
* Use location-specific keywords on your website
* Build high-quality backlinks from local directories and websites
* Respond promptly to online reviews

[local SEO services](/services/local-seo) can help you improve your search engine rankings and attract more customers.

## Google Ads for HVAC Companies

Google Ads can help you reach customers who are actively searching for HVAC services. Here are some Google Ads tips:

* Target specific keywords like "HVAC repair" or "air conditioning installation"
* Use location targeting to reach customers in your area
* Set up conversion tracking to measure your ad performance
* Optimize your ad copy and landing pages for better results

[Google Ads management](/services/google-ads) can help you create and manage effective ad campaigns.

## Customer Retention Strategies

Retaining existing customers is just as important as attracting new ones. Here are some customer retention strategies:

* Offer loyalty programs or discounts for repeat customers
* Provide excellent customer service and follow-up calls
* Send regular newsletters or promotional emails

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Letting Your Google Business Profile Collect Dust

**The story from Nashville, TN**

A seven-truck HVAC company in Nashville came to me frustrated. They were getting maybe two calls a week from their Google Business Profile. Their Google My Business listing was set up in 2019 and hadn't been touched since. They had six reviews total — three of them five-star, two one-star from 2020, and one where someone complained about a technician showing up two hours late.

The owner told me, "We're good at the work. We just don't get the calls."

I checked their profile. The business hours still said "Monday-Friday 8am-5pm" even though their actual schedule included Saturday emergency service. The one-star reviews had no response. The photos were four blurry pictures from a flip phone. And they'd never posted a single update.

**The fix**

We spent one afternoon fixing this:
- Updated hours to include Saturdays and "24/7 emergency service available"
- Responded to every single review — the one-stars got a genuine apology and an offer to make it right (two of them actually updated their ratings after the response)
- Added 25 photos: clean shots of their trucks, a before-and-after of a furnace install, their crew in uniform, and the office
- Started posting once a week — simple stuff like "It's 95 degrees today. Our trucks are dispatched. Call for same-day service." or "Check your air filter. Here's how."
- Added a booking button linked to their scheduling software

**The outcome**

Within 60 days, their profile was getting 800+ views per week instead of 200. Their phone calls from the profile went from 8 per month to 42. They booked $3,800 in new service calls in the first month after the changes. Cost: about four hours of my time and zero ad spend.

This is the single cheapest fix most HVAC companies ignore. Your Google Business Profile is not a "set it and forget it" thing. It's a live storefront. Treat it like one.

### Mistake #2: Running Google Ads Without Negative Keywords

**The story from Austin, TX**

An HVAC company in Austin was spending $1,200 per month on Google Ads and getting almost no booked jobs. The owner showed me his dashboard: 4,000 impressions, 120 clicks, but only 2 form fills and 1 phone call. He was paying about $10 per click. A classic money incinerator.

I looked at his search terms report. You know what his ads were showing for? "How to fix AC yourself," "HVAC salary," "HVAC training near me," "What does an HVAC technician do," and — I am not making this up — "AC repair costs Reddit."

People researching their own repairs or looking for careers were clicking his ads. He was paying $10 a pop for people who would never hire him.

**The fix**

We built a negative keyword list. It took about 30 minutes. We added:
- "DIY," "how to," "instructions," "tutorial," "guide," "manual"
- "Salary," "training," "certification," "school," "jobs," "career"
- "Cost," "price," "Reddit," "forum," "review"
- "Parts only," "freon only," "used"
- "Apartment," "property management" (he didn't serve those)

Then we tightened his location targeting from a 30-mile radius to a 15-mile radius with a tighter budget around his actual service area.

**The outcome**

His cost per click dropped from $10.23 to $4.87. But more importantly, his cost per lead dropped from $200 to $22. In the next 30 days, he spent $1,000 and got 45 calls. He booked 18 service calls worth $5,400 in revenue. He went from losing money on Google Ads to making a 5.4x return.

Most HVAC companies skip the search terms report. They throw money at broad keywords and wonder why nothing works. The uncomfortable truth is that Google will happily show your ads to anyone unless you specifically tell them not to. That's your job.

### Mistake #3: Chasing One-Time Emergency Calls Instead of Building Recurring Revenue

**The story from Portland, OR**

A single-truck HVAC business in Portland had been running for six years. Owner worked 80-hour weeks during summer, spent winter worrying about bills. His marketing was entirely reactive — Google Ads for "emergency AC repair" and "furnace not working."

I asked him what percentage of his customers were on a maintenance plan. He said, "Maybe ten percent." I asked him what he did after a service call to get them back. He said, "I hand them a business card."

Most HVAC companies compete for the same emergency calls. You fight 15 other companies for the person whose AC died at 3pm on a July Tuesday. That's an expensive, exhausting way to run a business. You're one bad summer away from cash flow problems.

**The fix**

We did three things:
1. Built a simple email follow-up sequence in Mailchimp: day 1 thank you with a maintenance plan offer ($199/year for two visits), day 7 check-in, day 30 "here's how to change your filter" with another maintenance plan pitch, month 6 "It's almost summer — here's our pre-season tune-up special"
2. Added a maintenance plan signup to every invoice. Each customer got asked: "Want priority scheduling and a 15% discount on repairs? $199 a year."
3. Called every single customer who'd had a major repair in the last 18 months and offered a free system check if they signed up for the plan

**The outcome**

In 90 days, he added 63 maintenance plan customers at $199 each. That's $12,537 in predictable revenue before he turned a wrench on a single emergency call. His summer emergency calls dropped by 30% because his maintenance customers weren't breaking down as often. And when they did need service, they called him first — no ad spend required.

That $12,537 covered his Google Ads budget for 10 months. He stopped panicking every October.

## Seasonal Ad Targeting: Where to Spend and Where to Save

HVAC marketing has a predictable rhythm. The problem is most small business owners treat every month the same. They shouldn't.

Here's what I've seen work across a dozen HVAC clients in different climates.

**Winter (November-February)**

In Minneapolis, a client told me he used to pause all ads in December. Wrong move. People whose furnaces fail on Christmas Eve will pay a premium. We shifted his spend to focus on "emergency furnace repair" and "no heat" keywords with a higher bid for overnight and weekend searches. His December ad spend was only $600, but he booked $4,200 in emergency calls when other companies were closed. The $600 was fully covered by two service calls.

What we paused: maintenance ads. Nobody's thinking about spring tune-ups in January. Don't waste money.

**Spring (March-May)**

This is where you capture the planners. People who schedule their AC check before summer. In Denver, we ran ads for "spring AC tune-up" and "furnace to AC switch" from March 15 to May 15. Budget was $800/month. It generated 34 booked maintenance appointments at $179 each. That's $6,086 in revenue. Those same customers then called first when their AC actually failed in July — no extra ad spend.

**Summer (June-August)**

High competition, high cost per click. In Phoenix, we saw CPCs hit $18 for "emergency AC repair" in July. Most small businesses can't compete with the big nationals at those numbers. So we got smarter.

We ran ads for specific neighborhoods. Instead of targeting all of Phoenix, we targeted zip codes where we knew the homes were older (older systems fail more) and had higher income (they can pay for service). We also ran ads only from 10am-6pm because emergency calls at midnight were mostly from rentals with property managers who'd call whoever answered first.

Summer budget was $1,500/month. We capped it. When the budget ran out, ads stopped. We didn't chase the $18 clicks. That discipline saved one client $2,800 in wasted spend over July and August.

**Fall (September-October)**

Most HVAC companies are recovering from summer. They cut ad spend. That's a mistake. This is the best time to run "furnace check before winter" ads. Low competition means lower CPCs. In Chicago, we spent $500/month in September and October and got 52 maintenance appointments. At $199 each, that's $10,348 in revenue. The ads themselves cost $1,000 total.

**The tool that makes this manageable**

Use Google Ads' ad scheduling and location targeting. Set your budget by daypart. I've had clients run ads only from 8am-8pm on weekdays and 9am-5pm on Saturdays. That alone saves 20-30% of budget that would go to unqualified late-night clicks.

And for the love of god, use the Google Ads mobile app. I had a client in Atlanta who saw his AC unit fail on a Saturday in July. He checked his ad dashboard and realized his budget was running out by 2pm. He increased the daily budget from his phone while sitting in his own 90-degree living room. That day generated 12 calls and $3,200 in booked work.

## Review Generation: The Most Profitable Thing You're Not Doing

Yelp, Google, and Facebook reviews are the single highest-ROI marketing investment for HVAC companies. I've seen this across dozens of clients. And yet most small business owners treat reviews like something that just happens to them.

**The data from actual clients**

A client in Charlotte, NC went from 12 Google reviews to 87 in 90 days. Their call volume increased by 40%. Their average job value went up because people who read reviews before calling are more price-tolerant — they've already decided you're the reliable option.

How? They automated the review request process. After every completed job, the customer got a text message with a link to leave a review. The text went out 2 hours after the technician left. Not 24 hours later. Not never. Two hours.

**The specific process that works**

I've tested every approach. Here's what consistently produces the most reviews:

1. Send the review request immediately after service. The customer is either thrilled the AC is working or pissed they waited. Capture the thrilled ones fast.
2. Use a tool like Podium, Birdeye, or even just a simple text. Don't email. Email open rates for service follow-ups are terrible. Text open rates are 90%+.
3. Ask specifically for a Google review. Yelp might hide positive reviews. Google reviews show up in local search results. That's where you need them.
4. Make it easy. The text should have a single link. Not "click here for our website, then find the review section, then leave a review." One click.
5. Respond to every review within 48 hours. Good or bad. The bad ones are actually more important. I've seen a genuine response to a negative review convert more customers than five positive reviews.

**What to do with your reviews**

Don't just let them sit on Google. Use them.

Pull your best reviews and put them on your website. Create a "what our customers say" section. Add a review quote to every service page. I had a client in Raleigh who added a three-sentence review testimonial to his "AC installation" page and his conversion rate on that page went from 2.1% to 5.8%.

Put reviews in your email signatures. Put them on your invoices. Put them on postcards you mail to neighborhoods where you did work. Reviews are social proof that costs you nothing except a few seconds of your technician's time to send a text.

**The uncomfortable truth about negative reviews**

You will get bad reviews. It's inevitable. A customer will be unreasonable. A technician will have a bad day. A part will take too long to arrive.

The worst thing you can do is ignore them. The second worst thing is to argue publicly.

I had a client in Houston who got a one-star review from a woman whose AC wasn't fixed on the first visit (it needed a part that took three days to arrive). She was angry. He responded: "I'm sorry we couldn't resolve this faster. The part arrived Tuesday and we had a technician at your house by 8am Wednesday. We've refunded the diagnostic fee. Please call me directly if there's anything else I can do."

That response got 27 "helpful" clicks. Her review stayed at one star, but anyone reading the exchange could see this was a reasonable company handling a tough situation. He booked two jobs from people who saw that exchange.

Never delete reviews. Never get into a public argument. Apologize, fix the problem offline, and move on.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads each month for my HVAC company?**

Start with $500-$800 per month in a small-to-midsize market. Track calls and booked jobs, not clicks. If you can't get a cost per booked job under $50 in 60 days, something is wrong with your targeting, your landing page, or your phone script. I've seen clients do well at $300/month in smaller markets and need $2,000/month in competitive cities like Phoenix or Miami. The number doesn't matter as much as the return. If you spend $1,000 and get $4,000 in booked work, increase it. If you spend $1,000 and get $200, stop.

**Q: Is it worth paying for Yelp ads?**

In most cases, no. Yelp's algorithm tends to hide businesses that don't advertise. I've tested this with clients in six different cities. Yelp ads produced a cost per lead of $80-$120 for HVAC services. Google Ads produced cost per lead of $15-$35 with the same budget. Focus on Google and your own referral network. If a customer asks about your Yelp page, just have a profile that's filled out completely with photos and a few organic reviews. Don't pay Yelp for ads.

**Q: How long does it take for local SEO to start working?**

Three to six months if you're starting from scratch. Faster if you already have some reviews and a basic website. I had a client in Columbus, OH who went from page four to page one for "furnace repair Columbus" in four months. He did three things: optimized his Google Business Profile, got 25 new reviews, and added location pages to his website. He spent $0 on ads for those four months and his calls from organic search went from 5 per month to 22 per month. Local SEO is slow but it compounds. Every review you get today helps your ranking six months from now.

**Q: Should I offer maintenance plans? Won't I lose money on the tune-ups?**

The tune-ups cost you maybe $40-$60 in labor and parts. You're charging $179-$199. That's a 300% margin. But the real money isn't in the tune-up. It's in the repair work that maintenance customers call you for first, before calling your competitors. My clients see 2x-3x more repair revenue from maintenance customers than from one-time emergency callers. Plus, maintenance customers have a 75% retention rate year-over-year. Emergency callers have a 15% retention rate. The math is obvious.

**Q: How do I compete with the big national HVAC companies like One Hour Heating & Air?**

You don't outspend them. You out-service them. Most customers choose an HVAC company based on speed, communication, and trust. Big companies often have dispatchers who don't know the technicians, technicians who are paid by the job (so they rush), and call centers that put you on hold. You can offer: a real person who answers the phone, the same technician every time, a text message when the tech is on the way, and a follow-up call to make sure the work was done right. Those things cost you nothing and beat $50,000 ad budgets all day.

**Q: I tried Google Ads and wasted $1,000 in two weeks with zero calls. Should I give up?**

No, but you should fix your setup. The most common reasons for wasted ad spend are: showing ads outside your service area, bidding on broad keywords instead of specific services, not using negative keywords, and sending traffic to a bad website or a voicemail. I've taken over accounts that were spending $15 per click with no conversions and fixed them in a weekend. Get a professional audit or use Google's free support. The platform works. The problem is always the setup.
