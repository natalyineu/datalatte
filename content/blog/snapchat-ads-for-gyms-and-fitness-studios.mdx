## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting Everyone in a 50-Mile Radius

A CrossFit box in Austin, Texas came to me after running Snapchat ads for six weeks. They'd spent $1,200 and gotten three leads. Three. The owner was ready to declare Snapchat dead.

I pulled their campaign data. They'd targeted "Austin, Texas" — which Snapchat interprets as the entire metro area, plus suburbs, plus exurbs, plus random towns an hour away. Their ad was showing to a 19-year-old in Round Rock who drove a Honda Civic and had zero interest in lifting weights. She was seeing a barbell video and swiping away in 0.4 seconds.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the campaign with a 3-mile radius around their actual location. Then we layered on interest targeting: "fitness," "crossfit," "weightlifting," "running," "yoga." We excluded anyone who'd been to a competing gym listed on Google Maps in the last 90 days — available through Snapchat's Audience Match tool.

**The outcome:** Same $400/month budget. 29 leads in week one. Cost per lead dropped from $400 to $13. They sold twelve intro memberships in the first month — roughly $3,800 in revenue from a campaign they were about to kill.

Target your actual service area. If you're in a dense city like Austin or Chicago, start with 1–2 miles. You can expand later if you have capacity.

### Mistake 2: Using Static Photos of Your Equipment

A boutique fitness studio in Nashville — think neon lights, reformer machines, $28 classes — ran a Snapchat ad with a photo of their empty studio floor. Clean floors, nice lighting, nobody in it.

They spent $800 over four weeks. Zero conversions. Not a single swipe-up.

**The problem:** Nobody cares about your clean floors. They care about whether they'll look good, feel welcome, and not embarrass themselves. An empty studio signals "we have no customers," not "we have space for you."

**The fix:** We shot three 15-second vertical videos on an iPhone in one afternoon. First clip: a client walking in and smiling at the front desk. Second clip: a class in motion — sweaty people, laughing, a coach adjusting someone's form. Third clip: the same client leaving, saying "I actually feel amazing." We added captions for people watching without sound (90% of Snapchat views are muted). Then we ran a first-class-free offer: "Sweat with us Tuesday — your first session is on us. Tap to claim."

**The outcome:** 47 people claimed the free class. Eleven became recurring members in the next 30 days. Studio revenue from that campaign: roughly $2,100 in membership and class-pack purchases. From a $500 ad spend.

Always show real people having a good time. Stock photos of fit models? People can smell them from a mile away. Use your actual clients (with permission) and your actual space.

### Mistake 3: No Retargeting — Just Cold Traffic and Hope

A climbing gym in Portland, Oregon ran Snapchat ads for their annual membership sale. They got decent click-through rates — around 1.8% — but nobody actually bought. The owner told me "Snapchat people just aren't serious."

I checked their pixel. They had it installed but had never built a retargeting audience. Every dollar was going to cold traffic. People would tap the ad, browse the membership page, then get distracted by a notification, a dog, or the existential dread of committing to a gym.

**The fix:** I set up two retargeting audiences. First: people who tapped the ad but didn't land on the checkout page. Show them a different video — a quick tour of the bouldering area, someone sending a V5. Second: people who reached the checkout page but didn't complete the purchase. Show them a limited-time offer: "Your 3-day pass expires tonight — finish your signup and get your first month for $49."

We also created a "lookalike" audience based on people who'd converted on other channels (email signups from their website, people who'd checked in via their Square POS system). Snapchat allows you to upload customer email lists and build lookalikes.

**The outcome:** The retargeting campaign cost $350 over two weeks. It drove 22 membership sales. That's roughly $4,800 in annual recurring revenue from a retargeting spend of $350. The cold traffic? It got better too, because the pixel had more conversion data to learn from.

If you don't retarget, you're leaving 70% of potential conversions on the table. This isn't a "nice to have." It's where the math starts working.

### Mistake 4: No Clear Call to Action or Offer

A yoga studio in Denver ran a Snapchat ad that said "Come visit us!" with a photo of a peaceful mountain view. Pretty picture. No urgency. No mechanism. Just vibes.

They spent $900. Exactly two people showed up over six weeks. One of them asked if they were a juice bar.

**The fix:** We gave people a reason to act immediately. "Show this ad at the front desk for 50% off your first class. This week only." We made the offer exclusive to Snapchat — not on Instagram, not on their website. That made the ad feel like a secret. We also added a "Swipe Up to Book" link that went directly to a Booksy booking page so people could reserve a spot without calling.

**The outcome:** 38 people showed up with the Snapchat offer in the first two weeks. 14 bought class packs afterward. Total campaign cost: $400. Revenue from class packs: $1,960. The studio now runs this as a recurring monthly campaign.

Your Snapchat ad needs one job: get someone to do one thing. Free class. Discounted session. Free water bottle if they tour the gym. Don't ask them to "learn more" or "visit our website." Ask them to book, claim, or show up. Measurably.

## How to Measure What Actually Matters (Not Vanity Metrics)

Most gym owners I talk to think a successful ad campaign means lots of views and comments. It does not. Let me tell you what I've seen happen when people chase the wrong numbers.

A boxing gym in Chicago was thrilled their Snapchat ad had 12,000 views. They showed me the screenshot. "Look, we're going viral!" I asked how many memberships they'd sold. "Well, we haven't tracked that yet." They'd spent $1,500 to feel popular.

Here's what you should actually track:

**Cost per lead (CPL):** How much do you spend to get someone to fill out a form, claim an offer, or book a trial? If you're spending more than $15 per lead for a fitness studio in a medium-cost city, your targeting or creative needs work. In expensive markets like NYC or LA, $25–30 might be acceptable, but that's the ceiling.

**Cost per conversion (CPA):** This is the real number. How much do you spend to turn an ad viewer into a paying member? For a gym with $89/month memberships and a 6-month average retention, a CPA under $50 is excellent. Under $100 is fine. Above $150? Something's broken.

**Return on ad spend (ROAS):** Calculate this conservatively. If you spend $1,000 and get five members who each pay $89/month, that's $445/month recurring revenue. In 3 months, you've made $1,335. Your ROAS is 1.33x. In 6 months, it's 2.67x. Snapchat rarely gives you instant 5x ROAS on day one. If someone promises that, they're selling something. But 2–3x over 90 days is achievable for most local gyms.

**Store visits:** Snapchat offers a "Store Visits" metric that uses location data to estimate how many people came to your gym after seeing your ad. It's an estimate, not a precise count, but it's useful for trend tracking. Pair it with a manual check-in code or a promo code to verify.

**What not to care about:** Impressions, views, swipe-ups, comments. These are ego metrics. They don't pay your rent. I've seen campaigns with a 45% swipe-up rate and zero conversions, because the offer was weak and the landing page was slow. Focus on dollars in the bank.

To track this properly, install the Snapchat Pixel on your website, use UTM parameters for any links (you can see these in Google Analytics), and create unique promo codes for each campaign. If someone books through Booksy or Mindbody, ask how they heard about you. A simple dropdown menu in your booking flow will answer this better than any pixel.

## How to Combine Snapchat Ads with Google Ads and Local SEO (Without Burning Budget)

You don't have to choose between Snapchat and Google. But you do have to use them differently.

A spin studio in Denver ran Snapchat ads to get people in the door and Google Ads to catch people already searching. Here's what their setup looked like:

**Snapchat:** $400/month. Targeted 18–34 year olds within 2 miles of their location. Offer: "First class free — tap to book." Creative: vertical videos of actual classes with loud music and sweaty people. The goal was warm reach — people who didn't know they wanted a spin class until a video of someone crushing a hill climb popped up while they were procrastinating in bed.

**Google Ads:** $500/month. Three ad groups: "spin class Denver," "cycling studio near me," and "best spin class in Denver." Each ad went to a dedicated landing page with a clear booking button. The goal was capture — people who already knew they wanted a class and were comparing options.

**Local SEO:** Free, but required work. They optimized their Google Business Profile with recent photos, class schedules, and responses to every review — positive or negative. They also added location-specific pages to their website: "Spin Classes in Cherry Creek," "Cycling in RiNo," etc. This cost time, not money.

**The results over 90 days:**
- Snapchat: 64 trial bookings, 23 converted to memberships
- Google Ads: 41 bookings from search, 19 converted to memberships
- SEO: 27 direct bookings from Google Maps and organic search

Total ad spend: $2,700. Total new member revenue (first 3 months): roughly $9,100. Plus the recurring revenue from those members going forward.

**The key insight:** Snapchat fills the top of your funnel. Google captures the middle. SEO handles the long tail. They are not competing channels. They are a funnel.

If you're a small business with a limited budget, start with one channel (Snapchat or Google) and get it profitable before adding the other. But plan for both from day one. Your Snapchat ads should use consistent language and branding with your Google Ads, so when someone sees your Google result after seeing your Snapchat ad, it feels familiar, not random.

Also worth noting: Yelp ads can work well for gyms, but they're expensive and the ROI is uneven. I've seen studios in NYC pay $3–5 per click on Yelp with conversion rates under 2%. I'd put that money into Snapchat or Google first. Test Yelp after you have consistent results elsewhere.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn't Snapchat just for teenagers? Will people over 25 actually see my ads?**

The demographic has shifted. Snapchat's largest user group in the US is now 18–34, with significant growth in the 25–34 bracket. In 2023, over 60% of new Snapchat users in the US were over 25. Your 28-year-old lawyer who wants to get back in shape? She's on Snapchat. Your 32-year-old dentist who needs to de-stress? Also on Snapchat. The "it's for kids" stereotype is about five years out of date.

**Q: How do I track if someone actually walks into my gym from a Snapchat ad?**

Three methods. First: Snapchat's Store Visits metric gives you an estimate based on location data. Second: create a unique promo code for Snapchat (e.g., "SNAP24") that people have to mention when they check in. Third: use a dedicated booking link in the ad that goes to a specific landing page with a form. Combine all three for the most accurate picture. No single method is perfect, together they give you a reliable trend line.

**Q: What's the minimum budget I need to make this work?**

I've seen effective campaigns start at $300/month in smaller markets and $600/month in expensive cities like NYC or LA. Below $200/month, Snapchat's algorithm doesn't have enough data to optimize properly. You'll get impressions but few conversions. Start at $400/month, test for two weeks, then scale what works. Anything under $100/month is a waste of money — you'll get five impressions and a bill.

**Q: I tried Snapchat ads three years ago and got nothing. What changed?**

Three things. First, Snapchat's targeting has improved dramatically — you can now upload customer email lists, create lookalikes, and use location data more precisely. Second, the user base has aged up, so the audience is more likely to have disposable income. Third, the ad formats have matured — Collection Ads, Story Ads, and Dynamic Ads work much better than the clunky old geofilters. If you haven't tried it in the last 18 months, you haven't really tried it.

**Q: Do I need a professional video crew to make Snapchat ads?**

No. I've run campaigns using iPhone footage that outperformed professionally produced spots. The key is authenticity — real clients, real classes, real reactions. That said, you need good lighting and clear audio. Shoot near a window during daytime. Use a tripod or prop your phone against a water bottle. Keep videos under 15 seconds. Add captions because nobody unmutes. You can edit in Canva or CapCut for free. If you have $500 to invest, spend it on a local videographer for a half-day shoot, not on a fancy agency.

**Q: Should I run Snapchat ads year-round or only for promotions?**

Consistent small campaigns outperform burst campaigns. A studio in Portland ran $200/month for 12 months and generated steady leads every month. Another studio in Austin spent $2,000 in January (New Year resolutions) and nothing the rest of the year. They got a spike in January and dead months from February onward. The consistent spender got more total members at a lower cost per acquisition. Run something every month, even if it's only $200. Increase spend during January and September (peak gym season) but don't stop entirely.
