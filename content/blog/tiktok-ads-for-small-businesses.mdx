**Setting Up Your TikTok Ad Campaign**

Before you start creating ads, you need to set up your TikTok ad campaign. This includes defining your target audience, setting a budget, and choosing your ad objectives. Here are a few tips to keep in mind:

* **Know your audience**: Who are your ideal customers? What are their interests and pain points? Make sure you have a clear understanding of who you're trying to reach.
* **Set a realistic budget**: TikTok ads can be cost-effective, but they can also add up quickly. Make sure you have a budget in mind and stick to it.
* **Choose the right ad objective**: Are you trying to drive sales, increase brand awareness, or drive website traffic? Choose an ad objective that aligns with your business goals.

**Creating Effective TikTok Ads**

Now that you have your campaign set up, it's time to create some ads. Here are a few tips for creating effective TikTok ads:

* **Keep it short and sweet**: TikTok users are bombarded with ads every day. Make sure your ad is short, engaging, and to the point.
* **Use eye-catching visuals**: TikTok is a visually-driven platform. Make sure your ad has eye-catching visuals that grab users' attention.
* **Focus on benefits**: Don't just tell users about your product or service. Show them how it will benefit them.

<Callout type="tip">Use TikTok's built-in features, such as Duet and Reaction, to make your ads more interactive and engaging.</Callout>

**Targeting on TikTok**

One of the most powerful features of TikTok ads is targeting. With TikTok, you can target users based on their interests, behaviors, and demographics. Here are a few targeting options to consider:

* **Interest targeting**: Target users who have shown an interest in your product or service.
* **Behavioral targeting**: Target users who have shown a specific behavior, such as purchasing a similar product.
* **Demographic targeting**: Target users based on their age, location, and other demographics.

<Callout type="warning">Be careful not to over-target. Too many targeting options can lead to wasted ad spend and decreased effectiveness.</Callout>

**Measuring the Success of Your TikTok Ad Campaign**

Finally, it's time to measure the success of your TikTok ad campaign. Here are a few key metrics to track:

* **Click-through rate (CTR)**: The percentage of users who click on your ad.
* **Conversion rate**: The percentage of users who complete a desired action, such as making a purchase.
* **Return on ad spend (ROAS)**: The revenue generated by your ad campaign compared to the cost of that campaign.

<Callout type="example">Let's say you run a coffee shop and you want to increase sales of your signature latte. You create a TikTok ad campaign targeting users who have shown an interest in coffee and have a high likelihood of making a purchase. After running the campaign for a few weeks, you see a 20% increase in sales of your signature latte. Your ROAS is 300%, meaning you're generating $3 in revenue for every $1 you spend on ads.</Callout>

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting “Women 25–45” and Hoping for the Best

A coffee roaster in Portland, Oregon came to me last year. They had spent $1,800 on TikTok ads over six weeks targeting “women 25–45 interested in coffee.” That’s like telling a fisherman “catch something in the ocean.” Their cost per click was $1.42, and they got exactly two in-store redemptions from a coupon code.

Here’s what went wrong: they assumed “coffee” was specific enough. TikTok’s algorithm doesn’t know if that interest means single-origin Ethiopian pour-overs, oat milk lattes with caramel drizzle, or the gas station 7-Eleven special. Their ad showed a perfectly lit shot of espresso being pulled. The people who saw it wanted pumpkin spice. The people who wanted espresso never saw it.

The fix: we narrowed to people who follow coffee trade accounts, local food bloggers, and accounts that post about latte art. We also excluded anyone who followed Starbucks. Sounds harsh, but Starbucks customers are not your customers if you’re selling $6 pour-overs.

We reset with $500/month budget. Audience: 20-mile radius, interests in local food festivals, follow accounts like Portland Food Map, aged 28–50. We also ran a separate ad set for “morning routine” content — hoodies, rain, a warm mug. That one overperformed by 400%.

Outcome after 30 days: $1,200 in attributed sales, cost per acquisition dropped from $900 to $21. They still call me to complain about Portland weather. I tell them their ads are fine.

### Mistake 2: Using a Stock Video for the First Three Seconds

A fitness studio in Austin, Texas wanted to promote their “new member” offer: $49 for the first month. They paid someone on Fiverr $80 to make a polished ad. It opened with a slow-motion shot of weights clanking. Generic background music. No person’s face for the first four seconds.

They spent $1,200 in two weeks. Got 14 link clicks. Zero sign-ups.

TikTok users can smell stock footage from a mile away. It triggers the same instinct as a telemarketer calling during dinner. The platform rewards raw, imperfect, real content. I once saw a bakery in Chicago get 50,000 views from a 15-second video of a croissant being dropped on the floor. The owner said “oops” and kept filming.

The fix: we filmed the owner on her iPhone at 6 AM before the first class. She’s out of breath, wearing a sweaty tank top. She says: “I’m about to teach a 6:30 spin class. If you sign up today, I’ll personally buy you a smoothie after. The smoothie costs more than the membership. That’s how serious I am.” No script. One take. One minute and twelve seconds.

We spent $400 on that ad. It generated 38 new memberships in 10 days at $49 each — $1,862 in direct revenue, plus five more from people who saw the ad and walked in without clicking. The smoothies cost her $114. She told me she’d “pay for smoothies forever.”

### Mistake 3: No Retargeting — Expecting One Ad to Close the Sale

A pet grooming salon in Nashville ran a single ad for “$15 off your first groom.” Budget: $900. Impressions: 48,000. Clicks: 300. Bookings: 4.

The owner was furious. “TikTok doesn’t work,” she said.

TikTok worked fine. The problem is that people see a grooming ad while they’re waiting for their coffee and think “I’ll book that later.” Then they don’t. You need to catch them again.

The fix: I set up a simple funnel. First ad: a 20-second clip of a golden doodle getting a bow tie and looking absurdly pleased. Call to action: “Book now and get $15 off.” Then we uploaded the list of people who clicked (not just viewed) into a Custom Audience and ran a second ad just to them. The second ad was even simpler: text overlay that said “You almost booked. Here’s your reminder.” Same golden doodle, but now he’s wearing two bow ties.

We added a third layer: we took the email addresses from the four original bookings and uploaded them to a Facebook Custom Audience — cross-platform retargeting because not everyone lives on TikTok. We also sent them a Mailchimp sequence: “Your dog looked great. Here’s a video of the groom. Also, your next visit is 20% off if you book within 14 days.”

Outcome: from that original $900, we drove 32 new clients over six weeks. Average lifetime value of a pet grooming client in Nashville is about $400/year per dog. That’s $12,800 in potential revenue from a single campaign. The owner stopped being furious and started asking about cat grooming.

## How to Build a TikTok Ad Funnel That Actually Works (Not Just “Get More Views”)

Most small business owners treat TikTok ads like a billboard. You pay money, people see it, they call you. That’s not how it works anymore. TikTok users scroll past an average of 200 videos per day. Your one ad is competing with someone’s nephew doing a backflip into a pool.

Here’s the funnel structure I used for a hair salon in Denver that wanted to fill their Tuesday and Wednesday slots — historically their dead days.

**Top of funnel (awareness):** $200/month. Target people within 15 miles who follow Denver lifestyle accounts, local food bloggers, and “self care” hashtags. The ad is a 15-second before-and-after of a balayage transformation. No call to action besides “Follow for more.” Cost per thousand impressions: $8.40. We got 23,000 impressions and 180 new profile visits. The goal here is not sales — it’s building a tiny pool of people who know you exist.

**Middle of funnel (consideration):** $300/month. Retarget anyone who watched more than 50% of the awareness ad, plus anyone who visited your profile. The ad is a booking incentive: “Tuesday and Wednesday appointments get 20% off any color service.” This ad uses a static image of their price menu with a handwritten circle around the discount. No fancy editing. Cost per click: $0.38. We got 47 clicks and 12 online bookings in two weeks.

**Bottom of funnel (conversion):** $100/month. This goes to a Custom Audience of people who clicked the booking link but didn’t complete the form. The ad is a direct video from the salon owner: “Hey, I saw you were looking at booking. I have a 2 PM slot open tomorrow. First-time clients also get a free deep conditioning treatment. Just use the link.” This one ad generated 5 additional bookings at an average ticket of $185 each — $925 in revenue on a $100 ad spend.

Total ad spend: $600. Total attributed revenue over four weeks: $3,780. The salon owner now runs this funnel every month. She told me Tuesdays are her highest-revenue day. I asked if she wants to open a second location. She said she’s “not crazy.”

## Integrations That Make TikTok Ads Actually Profitable (Tools You Already Use)

Here’s the part most guides skip: TikTok ads alone won’t save you. You need to connect them to the tools you already pay for. I’ve watched too many small businesses spend $1,000 on ads and then manually type customer names into a spreadsheet. Stop that.

### Square and TikTok Pixel

If you take payments through Square — and most coffee shops, bakeries, and boutique retailers do — install the TikTok Pixel on your Square Online store. This is a 15-minute job. It lets you track which ad views turned into actual purchases. A bakery in Chicago used this to discover that their TikTok ads cost $22 per first-time customer but those customers spent an average of $78 in the first 90 days. That’s a 3.5x return. Without the pixel, they would have thought the ads were losing money.

### Booksy or Mindbody + TikTok

Service businesses like salons, barbers, and spas usually use Booksy or Mindbody for booking. Neither integrates directly with TikTok, but here’s the workaround: use a custom URL parameter like `?source=tiktok` on your booking link, then check the source in your booking dashboard. A barbershop in Brooklyn did this and found that 22% of their new clients in Q4 2023 came from TikTok ads. They shifted $400/month from Google Ads to TikTok and saw a 15% increase in total new clients without increasing total ad spend.

### Yelp and TikTok Together

This one is counterintuitive but effective. A Mexican restaurant in San Antonio ran TikTok ads promoting their taco Tuesday special. They included a link to their Yelp page instead of their website. Why? Because Yelp shows your rating, reviews, and photos in one place. The ads cost $350/month. Yelp attributed 44 “calls to business” and 16 “direction requests” in 30 days. The owner said Tuesday revenue went from $1,200 to $1,900 on average. He didn’t need a website. He needed people to see a video of a fish taco and then immediately see that 200 people gave it 4.5 stars.

### Mailchimp and Email Capture

TikTok doesn’t let you capture emails directly in most ad formats. But you can run an ad that offers a discount code in exchange for an email sign-up on your site. A flower shop in Portland did this with a $200 TikTok ad promoting “free delivery on your first order.” They collected 86 emails in one week. Then they sent a three-email Mailchimp sequence: confirmation, one week later with 15% off, two weeks later with a Mother’s Day reminder. They attributed $1,400 in sales directly to that sequence. The TikTok ad was the front door. The email was the hallway that led to the register.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is TikTok ads just for Gen Z? My customers are mostly 35–55.**

No. I ran a campaign for a women’s boutique in Nashville targeting women 35–55. We hit a cost per purchase of $18. TikTok’s user base in the US is now majority 25+ and growing fastest among 35–54 year olds. The content just needs to match the demographic. Don’t use Gen Z slang. Don’t use dancing. Use real customers, real products, real settings. Think QVC energy, not TikTok dance challenge energy.

**Q: How much should I actually spend to see if TikTok ads work for my business?**

Start with $300 on a single ad set. Run it for 7 days minimum. If you get less than 5,000 impressions in 7 days, your budget is too low for your targeting radius or your audience is too narrow. If you get impressions but zero clicks, your hook is bad. If you get clicks but no conversions, your landing page or offer is bad. Do not spend more than $500 until you fix the first funnel. I had a client in Austin spend $2,000 on ads before fixing their website checkout flow. They could have saved $1,800 by running a $200 test first.

**Q: Do I need to make the videos myself or can I hire someone?**

Make them yourself. I mean it. The polished, produced ads underperform 80% of the time for local businesses. Your phone camera is fine. Film in natural light. Speak like a human. The bakery in Chicago I mentioned earlier shot their best-performing ad on an iPhone X with a cracked screen. They got 14,000 views and 52 orders. The owner was wearing an apron with a flour handprint on it. That ad cost $0 to produce and $150 to boost.

**Q: What about return on ad spend? What’s realistic?**

For a local service business, a 3x ROAS in the first 60 days is realistic. Anything above 5x is excellent. For retail or e-commerce, 2x is acceptable at the start. But don’t just look at direct ROAS. A customer who finds you through TikTok and spends $40 today might spend $400 over the next year. If you only measure the first sale, you’ll miss the real picture. Use your point-of-sale system to track repeat purchases. A dog groomer in Portland found that TikTok-originated customers returned 2.3 times more often than Google Ads customers. That changes the math entirely.

**Q: Can I just boost an organic post instead of running real ads?**

Sometimes yes, but usually no. Boosting a post gives you very limited targeting options. You can’t retarget people who didn’t convert. You can’t create lookalike audiences. You can’t split-test. For a $50 boost on a post that is already performing well organically, it’s fine. But if you want consistent, predictable customer acquisition, use the Ads Manager. The control you get is worth the extra 10 minutes of setup.

**Q: How do I measure offline sales from TikTok ads?**

You can’t perfectly. But you can get close. Use a unique promo code in your ad (like TIKTOK20). Ask customers at checkout how they heard about you and log it in your POS system. If you use Square or Clover, create a custom discount code tied to TikTok. Track redemptions. It’s not perfect, but it’s better than guessing. A pizzeria in Denver used code TIKTOKFREE for a free drink with any pizza. They redeemed 63 codes in one month. That’s 63 customers who explicitly came from TikTok. Multiply by average ticket and you have a rough revenue number.
