## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Using Broad Match Keywords Because You Think You'll Catch More Fish**

I had a coffee shop owner in Austin named Mike who came to me frustrated. He'd spent $1,200 in three weeks on Google Ads and gotten exactly zero sales he could point to. When I pulled up his account, his keyword list was one line: "coffee shop Austin." Broad match. Google had served his ad for phrases like "coffee shop Austin Powers costume" and "best coffee in Seattle." He paid for 87 clicks from people who would never walk into his shop on South Congress.

**The fix:** Switch to phrase match and exact match keywords. Add a negative keyword list before you launch a single campaign. For Mike, that meant keywords like "coffee shop near me" (phrase match), "Austin coffee shop open now" (exact match), and negatives like "costume," "Seattle," "Starbucks," and "jobs."

**The outcome:** Mike's weekly spend dropped from $400 to $180. His cost per click went from $4.60 to $2.10. In the first month after the switch, he tracked 22 in-store redemptions of a "Google Ad Special" coupon he promoted in his ad copy. Average ticket at his shop is $12. That's $264 in direct tracked revenue on $720 in ad spend. Not a home run, but he was in the positive for the first time. The second month, we optimized further, and he saw $3,800 in attributable sales on $900 in spend. He fired his nephew who had "set up" the account. No regrets.

**Mistake #2: Targeting the Entire City When You're a Neighborhood Shop**

A pet groomer in Portland named Sarah told me she "wanted to reach everyone in Portland." She set her location targeting to the entire metro area. Problem: she's on Southeast Division Street and most of her clients come from a three-mile radius. She was spending $50 a day showing ads to people in Beaverton and Gresham — locations that would never drive 40 minutes for a $65 nail trim.

**The fix:** Set a radius of five miles around her shop. Turn on "presence" targeting instead of "interest" targeting. That means Google only shows her ad to people who are physically in that area, not people who searched about Portland from another state.

**The outcome:** Sarah's click-through rate doubled because her ad was actually relevant to the people seeing it. Cost per click dropped from $4.50 to $1.80. Her daily spend went from $50 to $25. Appointment bookings from Google Ads increased 40% in the first two weeks. She told me she "felt stupid" for not doing this sooner. I told her most business owners make this mistake. The ones who fix it are the ones who actually see results.

**Mistake #3: Sending All Clicks to Your Homepage and Wondering Why Nobody Buys**

A hair salon owner in Nashville named Jordan was spending $600 a month on Google Ads. She sent every click to her homepage. Her bounce rate was 85%. Her conversion rate was under 2%. She was about to give up on Google Ads entirely.

**The fix:** Build a dedicated landing page for each ad group. For Jordan, that meant one page for "hair color services" and another for "balayage specialist Nashville." Each page had a single call to action — book an appointment through Booksy, which she already used for scheduling. The page needed a phone number, a clear price range, and photos of her actual work. Not stock photos. Not a generic "About Us" paragraph.

**The outcome:** Jordan's conversion rate went from 2% to 14% in three weeks. Her cost per booking dropped from $28 to $8. She spent $1,200 over two months on ads and booked $4,200 in appointments directly from those ads. The landing page cost her $400 to build with a local freelance designer. She made that back in the first week. She now has eight landing pages for different services.

**Mistake #4: Not Tracking Offline Conversions and Assuming the Ads Don't Work**

A boutique clothing store owner in Denver named Emily was ready to shut down her Google Ads account. She told me she'd spent $2,000 over two months and had "zero online sales." She was an e-commerce-only business in her mind. Except she also had a physical store on Larimer Street. I asked her how many people came in and mentioned seeing her ad. She said, "I don't ask."

**The fix:** Set up Google Ads' store visits measurement (if your account meets the data threshold). More practical: add a call tracking number to your ad extensions. Create a unique promo code for Google Ads traffic ("GOOGLE10" for 10% off). Train your staff to ask every customer, "How did you hear about us?" and log it in Square or whatever POS system you use.

**The outcome:** Emily started using a call tracking number. Within two weeks, she saw that her ads were generating 22 calls per week. She added a promo code and tracked 14 in-store redemptions in the first month. Average basket with the promo code was $65. That's $910 in tracked revenue from one month of ads, on a $1,000 monthly spend. Not break-even yet, but she also discovered that customers who came in from Google Ads had a 30% higher repeat purchase rate than her walk-in traffic. She adjusted her ad copy to emphasize the promo code and started tracking customer acquisition cost properly. Three months later, her ROAS was 3.2x.

## How to Use Local Inventory Ads (Even If You're Not a Big Box Store)

Most small retailers don't realize they can use Google's Local Inventory Ads. You don't need a massive e-commerce operation. You need a real-time inventory feed, which most POS systems can generate.

Here's how it works: someone in your area searches for a specific product — "Men's leather wallet brown" — and your ad shows that exact product, with your store name, price, and "in stock" status. They click and see directions to your store. No checkout process. No shipping. They walk in and buy.

A hardware store in Minneapolis called Anderson Hardware implemented this. They had 600 SKUs in their feed from their Square inventory system. Their monthly ad spend was $800. Within six weeks, they saw a 22% increase in foot traffic for people searching for specific tools or materials. A customer searching for "pipe wrench 14 inch" saw Anderson's ad with the exact price ($24.99) and drove across town because Home Depot was showing a different brand at $32.

The setup cost them about three hours of work with their Square integration and a Google Merchant Center account. The incremental revenue they tracked through in-store promo codes was $2,400 in the first month.

Most guides skip this part — don't. If you carry inventory that people search for by name, Local Inventory Ads are the most efficient dollar you'll spend. You're not paying for brand awareness. You're paying to show a person the exact thing they already decided to buy.

## Setting a Budget That Actually Works for a Local Retail Store

I've seen budgets of $300 a month work beautifully and budgets of $3,000 a month burn with nothing to show. The difference isn't the amount. It's the structure.

Here's a rule I've used across three agencies and dozens of local retail clients: start with a minimum of $300 per month for at least one full sales cycle. If your average customer buys once every two weeks (coffee shop, pet groomer), you need four weeks of data. If they buy once a quarter (hair color appointment, boutique clothing), you need three months.

Break your budget into two campaigns:

**Campaign 1 (70% of budget): Branded and high-intent keywords.** Your business name plus service. "Blowout bar Nashville" or "Austin coffee shop cold brew." These convert at 8-15% if your landing page is good. They're cheap. They protect you from competitors bidding on your name.

**Campaign 2 (30% of budget): Non-branded but local intent.** "Best coffee in Austin" or "pet groomer Portland Southeast." These are more expensive but expand your reach. They convert at 2-5%.

A fitness studio in Denver used this split on a $500 monthly budget. After six weeks, her branded campaign was driving 12 leads per week at $3.50 per lead. Her non-branded campaign was driving 8 leads per week at $7 per lead. She shifted 10% more budget to the non-branded campaign once she had proof it worked. Her total monthly spend stayed at $500. Her leads went from 80 per month to 96.

I tested this with a client in Chicago — a bakery that was spending $1,200 a month on a single campaign with 40 keywords in one ad group. We split the budget, cleaned up the keywords, and cut the spend to $800. Her revenue from Google Ads went up 35%. She called it "the best $400 I ever saved."

## Using Ad Extensions to Stand Out Without Spending More

Ad extensions cost nothing extra. You only pay when someone clicks. And they make your ad take up more space on the search results page. I've seen CTR increases of 15-25% just from adding three extensions that most businesses ignore.

**Location extension.** If you have a physical store, this is non-negotiable. It shows your address, phone number, and a map pin. A pizza place in Portland added this and saw a 20% increase in calls directly from their ad. They were already paying for clicks. The extension didn't cost extra. They just had to link their Google Business Profile.

**Call extension.** Put your phone number in the ad. I had a plumber in Austin who was getting zero leads from Google Ads. I added a call extension. His first week: 14 phone calls. He booked 9 of them. His ad spend was $450 that week. He made $2,800 from those 9 jobs. The call extension took 30 seconds to set up.

**Structured snippet extension.** List your products or services. For a boutique in Nashville: "Dresses, Jeans, Accessories, Sale Items." A hardware store: "Power Tools, Plumbing, Electrical, Paint." This tells the customer exactly what you carry before they click. The Minneapolis hardware store I mentioned earlier added structured snippets and saw a 12% increase in CTR on their best-selling product categories.

**Promotion extension.** Show a deal directly in the ad. "20% off first visit" or "Free consultation this month." A hair salon in Chicago ran a promotion extension for "New client special: $25 haircut" and saw a 40% increase in bookings. They tracked it with a specific offer code in Booksy. The cost per booking went from $15 to $9.

Most small businesses skip extensions because they don't know they exist or think they're complicated. They're not. Set them up once, and they run automatically. I've never had a client who added them and regretted it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I tried Google Ads once and it didn't work. Why would this time be different?**

You probably set it up yourself or handed it to someone who didn't know local retail. I see the same mistakes across clients: broad match keywords, no negative keywords, no location targeting, no call tracking, homepage as the landing page. The fix isn't spending more money. It's fixing those five things. I've had clients go from zero sales to positive ROAS in two weeks just by switching to phrase match and adding a location extension.

**Q: I only have $300 a month. Is that enough?**

Yes, but only if you're surgical with it. Don't run display ads. Don't run YouTube ads. Don't use broad match. Spend it all on branded and high-intent keywords in a five-mile radius. Track everything. If you see a keyword costing you money with zero conversions after two weeks, pause it. I've seen $300 a month generate $1,200 in tracked revenue for a coffee shop. I've also seen $300 a month burn in two days on irrelevant clicks. The difference is setup and monitoring.

**Q: How do I compete with big chains like Home Depot or PetSmart?**

You don't compete on their terms. You compete on neighborhood relevance. Big chains bid on generic terms like "dog food" at $5 a click. You bid on "dog food Portland Southeast" at $1.50 a click. You use location extensions to show you're three blocks away. You use structured snippets to list specific brands you carry that they don't. A pet store in Austin did this and went from $2,000 a month on ad spend to $600 a month with better ROAS. The big chains aren't optimized for your neighborhood. That's your advantage.

**Q: How do I know if my ads actually drove someone into my store?**

Three ways. One: set up call tracking with a unique number in your ad. Two: create a promo code that only appears in your Google Ads (like "GOOGLE20"). Three: train your staff to ask every customer how they found you and log it. I had a boutique in Denver that used all three. They found that 35% of new customers came from Google Ads, but only 12% had been admitting it when asked casually. The tracking paid for itself.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads myself or do I need an agency?**

You can, but be prepared to invest time learning. I've watched small business owners waste $2,000 in two months because they didn't know about negative keywords or landing page optimization. If your time is worth more than $50 an hour and you'd rather spend it serving customers, hire someone who specializes in local retail. If you enjoy the learning curve and have the patience, start with a $300 budget and read Google's official guides. But don't set it and forget it. That's how $300 becomes $3,000 wasted.

**Q: How long until I see results?**

For a well-set-up campaign, you'll see data within a few days and actionable trends within 4-6 weeks. Google's learning phase takes about two weeks. During that time, cost per click can be unpredictable. Don't make major changes in the first 10 days. After three weeks, look at what's working and double down. A pet groomer in Portland saw her first booking within 48 hours of launching her corrected campaign. A hair salon in Nashville took six weeks to hit profitable ROAS because her services have a longer purchase cycle. If you're not profitable after 8 weeks, something is fundamentally wrong with your setup.
