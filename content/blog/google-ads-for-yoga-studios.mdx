## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Ads for “Yoga” and Expecting Class Sign-Ups

I had a client in Austin, Texas — a Vinyasa studio near South Congress. They were spending $1,800/month on Google Ads. Their conversion tracking showed “37 conversions” in the last 30 days. They were thrilled. Until I looked at what those conversions actually were. Turns out, they were running broad match keywords for “yoga,” “yoga classes,” and “yoga near me.” That sounds logical, right?

Here's what actually happened. Their ads were showing up for people searching “yoga pants,” “yoga mats near me,” and “yoga teacher training certification.” One guy clicked an ad because he thought it was a store selling blocks and straps. Another clicked because she wanted to find a beginner-friendly online course in Arizona. The studio is in Austin. None of these people were going to walk through the door.

Over three months, this studio wasted roughly $3,600 on clicks that had zero chance of converting into in-person class attendees. The fix was uncomfortable but simple: I added about 200 negative keywords. I paused the broad match campaigns entirely and switched to phrase match and exact match for location-specific terms. Then I created separate campaigns for different class types — “hot yoga Austin” in one ad group, “restorative yoga Austin” in another.

The outcome? Their cost per lead dropped from $24 to $8. They went from 37 “conversions” (mostly irrelevant clicks) to 12 actual class sign-ups in the first month after the change. They lowered their monthly spend to $1,200 and started filling 6 PM classes that had been running at 40% capacity.

### Mistake #2: Using the Same Landing Page for Every Single Ad

A studio in Portland, Oregon called me after their first month of Google Ads. They'd spent $950 and gotten zero bookings. Zero. When I looked at their ad-to-landing page flow, it was painful. They had five different ads running — one for prenatal yoga, one for power flow, one for beginner classes, one for hot yoga, and one general brand ad. Every single one of them linked to their homepage.

The homepage had a hero image of someone doing a headstand on a beach, a general “about us” paragraph, and a navigation bar with nine drop-down menus. If someone searched “beginner yoga Portland” and their ad said “Start Your Yoga Journey Today,” they landed on that homepage and had to hunt for the beginner schedule.

I'll be direct: that doesn't work. People click an ad because they expect the next page to deliver exactly what the ad promised. When it doesn't, they leave in under five seconds. That studio had a 94% bounce rate.

The fix cost nothing but time. I built five separate landing pages using Carrd (free tier). Each page matched one ad group. The “beginner yoga” landing page had a headline that said “Beginner Yoga in Portland — Next Session Starts Tuesday.” It showed the schedule, a photo of the actual teacher, and a single button: “Reserve Your Spot.” No navigation. No drop-downs.

Within two weeks, that campaign generated 9 booked intro classes from $320 in ad spend. That's a cost per acquisition of about $35. The studio owner told me that one of those intro students upgraded to a monthly membership worth $120. The ROI math works if you actually give people what they clicked for.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Location Settings (And Paying for It)

This one kills me because it's so easy to fix. A studio in Denver ran ads that targeted a 50-mile radius around their address. That sounds fine until you realize that a 50-mile radius in Denver covers Boulder, Aurora, Lakewood, and parts of the mountains. People in those areas are not driving 45 minutes to take a 60-minute yoga class on a Tuesday evening.

This studio was spending $700/month. After I pulled their search terms report, I found that 40% of their clicks came from people whose location was outside a 10-mile radius. One click came from someone in Colorado Springs — 70 miles away. That person clicked the ad, saw the address, probably laughed, and closed the tab. The studio paid for that click.

I tightened the radius to 8 miles. I also added location bid adjustments (+20% for people physically in the neighborhood during class times). I excluded zip codes that were more than 15 minutes away by car.

The result? Their ad spend stayed at $700, but their click-through rate went up because the ads were showing to people who could actually attend. Class bookings increased from 14 per month to 23 within six weeks. They were no longer subsidizing clicks from people who had no intention of driving across Denver for a Downward Dog.

### Mistake #4: Running Ads Without Call Tracking (You're Flying Blind)

A lot of yoga studio owners think a “conversion” is someone filling out a contact form. That's great. But in my experience working with fitness businesses, 40-60% of actual bookings happen over the phone. If you're not tracking phone calls, you're underestimating your results by a massive margin.

I worked with a studio in Nashville that told me their Google Ads campaign was “broken” because they were getting 1-2 form submissions a week. I asked if their phone was ringing. The owner said, “Oh yeah, the phone rings constantly during business hours.” I asked how many of those calls came from ads. She didn't know.

We set up call tracking using CallRail (there are cheaper options like CallTrackingMetrics, but CallRail works well for small businesses). Within 10 days, we found that 18 calls came directly from Google Ads. Of those calls, 12 booked a class. That's more than six times the number of conversions they were tracking.

If you are not tracking phone calls, you cannot optimize your ads. You cannot know which keywords drive phone leads versus form leads. You cannot calculate your actual cost per acquisition. This isn't optional. It's a $30/month investment that will save you from making decisions based on incomplete data.

## The Exact Keywords That Work (And Which Ones to Avoid)

Let me save you some time and money. Here are the keyword categories I have tested across 12+ fitness and studio clients in the last three years. These are not guesses. These are actual results.

### Keywords That Convert (Use These)

**“Yoga studio [city]” and “yoga classes [city]”** — These are the workhorses. Someone searching this is probably within a few days of making a decision. They know they want in-person classes. They just need to find the right studio. If your ad says “Yoga Studio in Austin — First Class Free,” they will click.

**“Beginner yoga [city]”** — High intent. These people are nervous. They might have never been to a studio before. They want reassurance. Your ad should mention that your studio is welcoming to beginners. Your landing page should show a photo of a real beginner, not an advanced practitioner in a handstand.

**“Hot yoga [city]” or “Vinyasa [city]” or “Restorative yoga [city]”** — If your studio offers specific class types, create separate ad groups for each one. Someone searching “hot yoga Chicago” does not want to see a generic ad for “yoga classes.” They want the hot room. Give it to them.

**“Yoga near me”** — This works, but only if your location settings are tight. Bid slightly higher on this keyword because it signals immediate intent. Someone searching “yoga near me” at 5:30 PM is probably looking for a class tonight.

**“Yoga for [specific need]”** — Examples: “yoga for back pain,” “yoga for stress,” “yoga for athletes.” These searches are lower volume but much higher conversion rates. A studio in NYC that offered “yoga for runners” got a 12% conversion rate on that keyword over three months. Their average across all keywords was 3%.

### Keywords to Avoid (Or Bid Very Low On)

**“Yoga” alone** — Too broad. You will pay for clicks from people looking for teacher training, retreats in Costa Rica, online classes in India, and yoga mats on Amazon. Do not do this.

**“Free yoga”** — Unless you offer free classes permanently (most studios do not), this keyword attracts people who will never pay. They will come for a free class and then disappear. I've seen studios waste $400+ on this keyword in a single month with zero paying customers.

**“Yoga teacher training”** — If you do not offer teacher training, add this as a negative keyword immediately. People searching this are not looking for a drop-in class. They want a 200-hour certification. That is a different business model.

**“Online yoga” or “yoga app”** — If you run a physical studio, these keywords will drain your budget. The person on the other end wants to practice in their living room. They are not your customer.

### A Note on Keyword Match Types

I have one rule for small businesses with limited budgets: use phrase match and exact match only. Broad match is a budget incinerator. Google's algorithm will interpret “yoga studio Austin” as “stretching classes Denver” if it thinks it can get a click. It doesn't care about your budget. It cares about spending it.

For a typical yoga studio with a $500-$1,500 monthly budget, start with 15-20 exact match keywords and 20-30 phrase match keywords. That's enough to generate data. After 30 days, look at your search terms report and add negatives. Then expand slowly.

## How to Structure Your Campaign for Maximum ROI

Most yoga studio owners I talk to set up one campaign, throw 50 keywords into one ad group, write two headlines, and hope for the best. That is the equivalent of putting every class you offer on the same flyer and stapling it to a telephone pole.

Here is the structure I use with clients. It works because it forces you to match your ads to the specific person searching.

### Campaign Level: Separate by Class Type or Goal

Run two to three campaigns max on a small budget:

1. **Brand Campaign** — Just your studio name, maybe a misspelling. Budget: $50-100/month. This ensures that when someone searches your studio name, they see your ad instead of a competitor running a branded campaign against you. Yes, that happens.

2. **Service Campaign** — This is your main campaign. Under this, create separate ad groups for: beginner classes, hot classes, Vinyasa, prenatal, and any specialty offering. Each ad group gets its own keywords, its own ads, and its own landing page.

3. **Offer Campaign** (optional) — If you run a new student special ($20 for two weeks or first class free), run this as a separate campaign with its own budget. That way you can measure exactly how much it costs to acquire a new student through that offer.

### Ad Group Level: Match the Search Intent

If someone searches “beginner yoga Austin,” your ad group for “beginner yoga” should have an ad that says “New to Yoga? Start Here.” The headline should include the phrase “beginner yoga” if possible. The description should mention that the class is for people who have never done yoga before. The landing page should show a schedule for beginner classes only.

Do not put “hot yoga” and “beginner yoga” in the same ad group. The search intent is different. The ad copy needs to be different. The landing page needs to be different. If you combine them, your click-through rate drops and your quality score suffers, which means you pay more per click.

### Ad Scheduling: Spend Money When People Actually Book

Most yoga studio ads run 24/7. That's a mistake. If your last class ends at 8 PM and nobody is booking at 2 AM, why are your ads running at 2 AM? You're paying for clicks at 3:14 AM from people who are browsing and will forget about your studio by morning.

Set your ad schedule to run from two hours before your first class until two hours after your last class. That's usually 6 AM to 9 PM for most studios. Then add a bid adjustment of +20% during your peak booking times — typically 12 PM to 2 PM (lunch break browsing) and 5 PM to 7 PM (right before evening classes).

I tested this with a studio in Chicago. They had been spending $1,200/month with a 24/7 schedule. After restricting to 6 AM - 9 PM and adding the lunch and evening bid adjustments, their monthly spend dropped to $900. Their bookings actually increased by 18% because they were showing up at the exact moment people were deciding what to do that evening.

### Budget Allocation: The 80/20 Rule

If you have a $1,000 monthly budget, allocate $800 to your service campaign and $100 to your brand campaign. Keep $100 in reserve for testing. If a new keyword shows promise in the first two weeks, you can shift that reserve into it without blowing your entire budget.

Do not spread $1,000 across five campaigns with five different targets. You will dilute your data. You won't know what's working. Instead, put your money where the intent is highest — people searching for specific classes in your specific neighborhood.

## Tracking the Right Metrics: Leads, Not Likes

I'll keep this short because I've already mentioned it, but it deserves its own section. Most yoga studio owners track the wrong things. They check “impressions” and “clicks” and feel good because the “reach” is high.

Impressions don't fill classes. Clicks don't fill classes. Form submissions and phone calls fill classes. That's it.

### What to Actually Track

**Cost per lead (CPL)** — How much did you pay for each person who booked a class? If you spent $500 and got 10 bookings, your CPL is $50. Compare that to the lifetime value of a student. If a student pays $120/month and stays for six months on average, that's $720 in revenue for a $50 acquisition cost. That math works.

**Conversion rate** — What percentage of people who click your ad actually book? A good benchmark for yoga studios is 4-7%. If you're below 3%, something is wrong — your landing page, your ad targeting, or your offer.

**Phone call duration** — If you track calls, look at calls that lasted longer than 60 seconds. Those are the calls that likely led to a booking. Five-second calls are wrong numbers or people asking for the studio's address while standing in front of it.

### The Tools to Use

- **Google Tag Manager (free)** — Set up a tag that fires when someone clicks your “Book Now” button. Do not rely on Google Ads' auto-tagging alone. It misses things.
- **CallRail or CallTrackingMetrics ($30-50/month)** — Assign a unique phone number to your Google Ads campaign. Every call from an ad gets recorded and logged. Listen to the first 10 calls. You'll learn more in 30 minutes than you will from a month of spreadsheet analysis.
- **Square Appointments or Booksy** — If you use a booking system that integrates with Google Ads, you can import offline conversions. This tells Google “this person clicked an ad three days ago and now they've booked.” Google uses that signal to find more people like them.
- **Google Analytics 4 (free)** — Set up events for “booking started” and “booking completed.” Link it to your Google Ads account. This isn't optional. I have never seen a profitable studio campaign run without conversion tracking that goes deeper than “page view.”

### A Warning About Vanity Metrics

I once had a client in Los Angeles who was proud that their ad got 12,000 impressions in one week. Twelve thousand. They thought that meant success. I asked how many bookings they got. Three. They paid $800 for three bookings.

Impressions and clicks are inputs. Bookings and revenue are outputs. If you are only looking at inputs, you are lying to yourself about how well your campaign is running.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I run Google Ads with a $500/month budget or is that just a waste?**
No, $500 is not a waste, but you have to be surgical about it. Do not run broad match. Do not target a 50-mile radius. Stick to 10-15 exact match keywords with your studio name and specific class types. Set a daily budget of about $16. Focus on one ad group for one class type — probably beginner classes, because those are the highest intent for new students. I've seen studios in midsize US cities generate 8-12 bookings per month on $500. That pays for itself immediately if your average student spends $80-120/month.

**Q: How long does it take for Google Ads to start working for a yoga studio?**
If your tracking is set up correctly, you should see your first lead within 24-48 hours. That lead might be a phone call or a form submission. But profitable results — meaning cost per lead is low enough to justify the spend — typically take 2-4 weeks. You need that much time to collect data, add negative keywords, and adjust your bids. Anyone who promises “instant results” in the first week is selling something that doesn't exist.

**Q: Should I run Google Ads or Facebook Ads for my studio?**
You should probably run both, but start with Google Ads. Here's why: Google Ads captures people who are actively searching for what you offer. They have intent. Facebook interrupts people's scrolling with an ad they didn't ask for. Facebook is useful for retargeting and building brand awareness. But if you have $500, put it on Google first. Once you have Google working, take 20% of that budget and test Facebook retargeting to people who visited your site but didn't book.

**Q: What if someone clicks my ad but books through a friend's recommendation or walks in off the street? How do I track that?**
You can't track every single attribution perfectly, and that's okay. But you can get close. Use a dedicated phone number for your ads. Use UTM parameters on all your ad links. Ask every new student, “How did you hear about us?” during the check-in process. It's low-tech, but it works. I've had studio owners tell me that 35% of their new students mentioned Google Ads when asked at the front desk — even though the ads weren't the last touchpoint.

**Q: I'm worried about competitors clicking my ads to drain my budget. Is that a real problem?**
It's less common than people think, but it does happen. Google has a system called “invalid clicks” that refunds you if it detects malicious clicking. Enable the “click fraud protection” setting in your account. Also, set a frequency cap if you're worried. But honestly? I've managed hundreds of campaigns and only seen clear competitor click fraud twice. Most “suspicious” click patterns turn out to be people clicking multiple times because they're comparing options. Don't lose sleep over this.

**Q: Do I need a separate landing page for each ad group, or can I just send everyone to my schedule page?**
You can send everyone to your schedule page, but you will get worse results. I have tested this across multiple clients. A dedicated landing page that matches the ad copy converts 40-80% better than a generic schedule page. If you don't want to build multiple pages, at least create a single landing page that is stripped down — no navigation, one clear call to action, and the schedule for the specific class type you're advertising. Use Carrd, Unbounce, or even a simple Google Site. It takes 30 minutes per page and the ROI is immediate.
