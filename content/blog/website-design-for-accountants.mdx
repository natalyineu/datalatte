## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve audited dozens of accounting firm websites over the years. The same patterns keep showing up — and they’re costing you qualified leads. Here are the three I see most often, along with what actually works.

### Mistake #1: The “Professional” Design That Screams “Expensive” (And Scares Off Small Business Owners)

**The story:** A CPA firm in Austin, Texas, spent $8,000 on a custom website. It was sleek. Dark navy with gold accents. Stock photos of people in suits shaking hands. The homepage headline: “Comprehensive Financial Advisory Services for High-Net-Worth Individuals.”

Their actual clients were food truck owners, yoga studios, and plumbers looking for bookkeeping and tax prep. The site was generating maybe one inquiry per month through the contact form — despite the firm spending $1,200/month on Google Ads driving traffic there.

**What went wrong:** The design signaled “we’re expensive and we don’t understand your business.” Small business owners in Austin — hair salon owners, electricians, coffee shops — don’t see themselves in photos of executives. They don’t know what “comprehensive financial advisory services” means. They want to know: *Can you do my quarterly taxes? Will you answer my emails? How much does it cost?*

**The fix:** We rewrote the homepage. Headline became: “Bookkeeping & Tax Prep for Austin Small Businesses — Starting at $350/Month.” We swapped the stock photos for screenshots of real client work (with permission) and added a short video of the firm’s owner explaining what a typical engagement looks like. We also put a clear pricing table on the services page — no “contact us for a quote” gatekeeping.

**The outcome:** In 60 days, the contact form submissions went from 1–2 per month to 12–15. The firm’s cost per lead dropped from $1,200 to roughly $280. They actually raised their rates by 10% the following year because they were now attracting clients who understood the value.

### Mistake #2: The “Trust” Section That Actually Undermines Trust

**The story:** A two-partner accounting firm in Nashville had been in business for 17 years. Their website had a testimonials page with five glowing quotes — all from clients they’d worked with for over a decade. Words like “integrity,” “trustworthy,” and “family-oriented” appeared in every single one.

Meanwhile, their Google Business Profile had 34 reviews — average 4.7 stars — but the website didn’t link to it. They also had a “Partners” section with headshots and bios that read like resumes: “Jane earned her CPA in 2004 and specializes in tax compliance for partnerships and S-corporations.”

**What went wrong:** The testimonials were too generic and too old. They sounded like they could have been written by the firm itself (they weren’t, but they read that way). The bios were credential-focused but said nothing about what it’s actually like to work with these people. A potential client — say, a pet groomer in East Nashville — reads that and thinks, “These people work with big corporations, not me.”

**The fix:** We moved the Google reviews feed directly onto the homepage — live, unfiltered, with names (where permitted). We rewrote the bios to answer the question every small business client actually has: *What happens when I send you a panicked email on April 10th?* Jane’s bio now says: “If you text me at 9 PM during tax season, I’ll text you back before I go to sleep. Yes, I’ve done that. No, I don’t recommend making it a habit.”

**The outcome:** The firm started getting calls from business owners who specifically said, “I liked that you actually sound human.” Organic contact form submissions increased 40% over three months. No extra ad spend.

### Mistake #3: Making People Work Too Hard to Pay You

**The story:** A solo accountant in Portland, Oregon, had a clean, modern website. Good typography. Nice color palette. The “Contact” button was prominent. But the only way to get started was to fill out a form with 14 fields — including “Tax Entity Type (C-Corp, S-Corp, LLC, Partnership, Sole Proprietor, Trust)” and “Preferred Tax Year End.”

That form was the single bottleneck. I looked at their analytics. People landed on the page, scrolled, clicked “Contact,” saw the form, and left. Average time on contact page: 11 seconds.

**What went wrong:** The form asked for information most small business owners don’t know off the top of their head. They don’t want to look up their entity type at 9 PM on a Tuesday. They just want to know if you can do their taxes and how much it costs. The form was designed for the accountant’s convenience, not the client’s.

**The fix:** We replaced the 14-field form with a single-field “What’s your email address? We’ll send you a simple questionnaire” — plus a clearly labeled phone number. We also added a scheduling tool (Calendly, free tier) for a 15-minute “Is this a fit?” call. No forms required.

**The outcome:** Form completions went from about 3 per week to 14. Of those 14, roughly 8 would schedule a call. The accountant’s close rate on those calls was about 60%. That’s roughly 20 new clients per quarter from a website that was already getting traffic — just failing at the final step.

## What Actual Small Business Owners Care About (Not What You Think)

I’ve had this conversation at least a dozen times with accounting firm owners:

*Me: “Who’s your ideal client?”*
*Them: “Small businesses.”*
*Me: “Okay, what’s their biggest concern about hiring an accountant?”*
*Them: “Probably taxes. Or financial planning.”*

That’s usually wrong.

I ran a small experiment a few years ago. I pulled Yelp reviews — not for accountants, but for the businesses that hire them. Coffee shops, hair salons, pet groomers, fitness studios. I looked for mentions of “accountant,” “taxes,” “bookkeeping,” or any related term.

The number one concern wasn’t tax expertise. It was *communication*. Specifically: “My accountant doesn’t explain things to me in plain English.” Number two was “I’m not sure what I’m paying for” — confusion about pricing and scope.

So when you design your website, here’s what that actually means:

**Your “Services” page needs to answer three questions, in this order:**  
- What will you do for me?  
- How much does it cost?  
- How long does it take?

Don’t lead with “Our comprehensive tax planning solutions maximize shareholder value.” Lead with “We file your business and personal taxes. You get a checklist. We do the work. You get your return in 2–3 weeks. Price: $500–$1,200 depending on complexity.”

Use a simple table. A hair salon owner in Denver doesn’t care about SALT deduction nuances — she cares that you won’t call her at noon on a Saturday.

**Your “About” page should build trust, not credentials:**  
A five-paragraph bio with your certifications and education history doesn’t build trust with a small business owner. What builds trust is:  
- A short video of you talking (bad lighting is fine, don’t overproduce it)  
- A sentence like “I’ve worked with 12 coffee shops in Austin and none of them have been audited”  
- A photo of you with your actual office (not a stock photo of a conference room)

I’ve seen a single 90-second Loom video increase conversion rates by 30% on a CPA’s website. Not because it was polished — because it was real.

## How to Turn Your Website Into a Lead Gen Machine (Without Spending More on Ads)

Most accountants I work with are spending money on Google Ads or Yelp ads. They’re tracking clicks, maybe even conversions. But the website itself is leaking leads.

I’ll give you the three highest-leverage changes I’ve made for clients — all of them with measurable results.

### Add a “How We Work” page that pre-sells your process

Small business owners are afraid of hidden fees, scope creep, and being ignored after they sign. A “How We Work” page that spells out exactly what happens — “Step 1: You send us your receipts and P&L. Step 2: We review and ask clarifying questions within 48 hours. Step 3: You get a completed return with a plain-English explanation” — removes that fear.

One firm in Chicago added this page and saw their average call length drop from 22 minutes to 9 minutes. Why? Because people already knew what they were signing up for. Fewer questions, higher close rate.

### Put your phone number in the header — big, clickable on mobile

This sounds obvious. I can’t tell you how many accounting websites have the phone number in a footer in 8pt gray font. If a pet groomer in Kansas City needs to talk to someone about their quarterly estimated taxes, they’re not filling out a form. They’re calling.

Make the number visible, make it big, make it tappable on mobile. One client increased phone calls by 60% just by moving the number from the footer to the top right corner of every page.

### Use Mailchimp or similar to follow up with people who didn’t convert

Your website analytics will show you a bunch of people who visited the Services page, maybe clicked Contact, then left. Those people are interested but not ready to commit.

Set up a simple email sequence:  
- Day 1: “Hey, I noticed you were looking at our tax prep services. Here’s what a typical engagement looks like (link to ‘How We Work’ page).”  
- Day 4: “Three common tax mistakes small businesses make (and how we prevent them).”  
- Day 7: “Here’s what our clients say about working with us (link to Google reviews page).”

No hard sell. Just useful information. I’ve seen a CPA firm get 5–8 new clients per quarter from this alone — people who visited, didn’t convert, then got the email and called three weeks later.

## How to Get Found on Google (Without Spending a Fortune)

If you’re in a mid-size US city — think Columbus, Cincinnati, Nashville, Austin, Portland — the local SEO landscape for accountants is actually pretty wide open. Most of your competitors have bad websites and worse Google Business Profiles.

Here’s a cheap strategy that works:

**Claim and optimize your Google Business Profile.** Fill out every field. Add photos of your office (yes, the messy desk is fine — it’s real). Upload your services list. Respond to every review — good or bad — within 48 hours. Use a tool like BrightLocal ($29/month) to track your local rankings.

**Get listed in local directories.** Many small business owners still search “accountant near me” on Yelp or Yellow Pages. Make sure your NAP (name, address, phone) is consistent across every directory. Use Moz Local or Yext to manage this — about $100/year.

**Get listed on Booksy or similar if you work with beauty/wellness businesses.** I’m serious. A lot of hair salon owners use Booksy for scheduling. If you can get listed as a “recommended accountant” in their local directory (some platforms have business services directories), you’ll get in front of the exact audience you want.

**One specific tactic that worked for a client in Denver:** They created a page on their site called “Tax Preparation for Denver Hair Salons.” Not a generic services page — a page specifically about tax issues for hair salons (tips, booth rent vs. employee, product deductions, etc.). That page now ranks #2 in Google for “tax preparation Denver hair salons” and generates about 4 inquiries per month from exactly the right people.

Cost to create that page: zero (they wrote it themselves in about two hours). Value: roughly $12,000/year in new client revenue.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I already get clients from referrals. Do I really need a website?**

Yes, because referrals aren’t a pipeline — they’re a trickle. When someone is referred to you, the first thing they do is Google your name. If your website looks like it was built in 2009, they’ll question whether you’re still in business. A good website doesn’t just attract new clients — it confirms to referred prospects that you’re legitimate. I’ve seen firms lose referrals because the website was so bad the prospect assumed the firm wasn’t taking new clients.

**Q: How much should I expect to spend on a decent website?**

You can get a functional, good-looking website for $2,000–$5,000 if you work with a solo designer or a small agency. Don’t pay $15,000 for a custom build unless you’re a multi-location firm. The most expensive part isn’t the design — it’s the copy and strategy. Invest in getting the messaging right, not in fancy animations. A $3,000 site with great copy will outperform a $15,000 site with fluff every time.

**Q: Should I put my prices on the website? I’m worried about scaring people away.**

Test it. I’ve run this test for three different accounting firms. Every single time, putting pricing on the website increased the quality of leads and decreased the time spent on sales calls. People who are scared off by a $500/month price were never going to be good clients. People who see the price and still contact you are ready to buy. You might lose 20% of visitors, but the ones you keep close at a much higher rate.

**Q: My website gets traffic but no one fills out the contact form. What am I doing wrong?**

Three most likely culprits: (1) Your form asks for too much information — reduce it to name, email, and a sentence. (2) Your call-to-action is unclear — “Contact Us” is weak; try “Book a Free 15-Minute Call” or “See If We’re a Fit.” (3) You haven’t answered the visitor’s main question on the page — they’re not ready to contact you because they still don’t understand what you do or how much it costs. Fix those three things before you spend another dollar on ads.

**Q: Do I need a blog to get traffic?**

No, but you need something that shows you understand the business owner’s world. A single page like “Tax Tips for [Your City] Coffee Shops” will do more for you than 50 generic blog posts about tax law changes. Small business owners don’t search for “Section 179 depreciation rules” — they search for “how to write off my coffee shop equipment.” Write for the questions they actually ask.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from a redesign?**

If you fix your messaging and your conversion path, you’ll see a difference within 30 days. Not a huge one — but you’ll see more form fills, more calls, or more booked calls. The real compounding happens over 3–6 months as Google starts trusting your site more and your local rankings improve. I’ve seen firms triple their lead volume within a year of a proper redesign — without increasing ad spend.
