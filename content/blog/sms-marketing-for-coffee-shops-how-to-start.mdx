### Step 1: Build a List of Engaged Customers

To start with, you'll need to build a list of customers who are interested in hearing from you. You can do this by:

* Asking customers to opt-in to your SMS list when they make a purchase
* Creating a physical opt-in sheet at your cafe and encouraging customers to sign up
* Offering incentives, such as discounts or freebies, to customers who join your list

Once you have a list of subscribers, you can start sending them targeted messages. But here's the thing: you can't just spam your list with generic messages. You need to tailor your messages to their interests and preferences.

For example, if you're a coffee shop in a busy neighborhood, you might send messages like:

* "Hey, it's your favorite barista! Want to try our new seasonal coffee drink for 10% off?"
* "Don't miss out on our live music night tonight! Join us for a free concert and enjoy a discounted drink"
* "Happy birthday to you! Get a free drink on us today"

These messages are personal, relevant, and engaging. They make customers feel special and valued, which is exactly what you want.

### Step 2: Tailor Your Messages to Customer Preferences

Once you have a list of engaged customers, you need to tailor your messages to their preferences. This means:

* Segmenting your list based on customer interests, purchase history, and other factors
* Creating personalized messages that speak to each segment's unique needs and preferences
* Testing and refining your messages to optimize engagement and conversion

For example, if you have a list of customers who have purchased coffee beans in the past year, you might send them messages like:

* "Hey coffee lover! We've got a new shipment of beans in store – come check it out and get 10% off your next purchase"
* "Don't miss out on our coffee tastings! Join us for a free event and learn about the latest roasts"

These messages are tailored to the interests and preferences of customers who have shown a clear interest in coffee beans.

<Callout type="warning">Be careful not to over-segment your list or send too many messages. This can lead to fatigue and decreased engagement.</Callout>

### Step 3: Fill Slow Hours and Boost Sales

Finally, you can use SMS marketing to fill your slow hours and boost sales. This means:

* Sending targeted messages to customers who are likely to be in the area during slow hours
* Offering incentives and promotions to draw in customers
* Creating events and activities that drive foot traffic and engagement

For example, if you're a coffee shop with slow hours on Tuesdays, you might send messages like:

* "Hey, it's a slow Tuesday! Come by and get 20% off all drinks and pastries"
* "Join us for a free trivia night tonight and enjoy a discounted drink"
* "Happy hour is here! Get a free drink with every purchase from 3-5pm"

These messages are designed to drive foot traffic and engagement during slow hours.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Buying a List Instead of Building One

A coffee shop in Austin, Texas, called “Brew & Moo” thought they’d fast-track their SMS marketing by buying a list of 5,000 local phone numbers from a shady data broker. Cost: $400. Within three days they got flagged by carriers for spam complaints — one customer actually reported them to the FCC. They received a cease-and-desist letter from their SMS provider and lost access to their platform for two weeks. Total wasted: $400 for the list, $1,200 in lost sending capability during a holiday weekend, plus a permanently damaged sender reputation.

**What to do instead:** Only send texts to people who explicitly opt in. Set up a tablet at the register with a simple form (“Enter your number for a free drink on your fifth visit”). Use a QR code on receipts that links to a sign-up page. Offer a one-time discount code in exchange for the number. It took Brew & Moo three months to rebuild a clean list of 800 engaged subscribers from scratch. But once they did, those 800 generated $3,800 in monthly revenue — more than the 5,000 bought numbers ever could have. The moral: a small, willing audience beats a large, resentful one every time.

### Mistake 2: Blasting the Same Message to Everyone

A coffee shop in Chicago, “Corner Cup,” had a decent list of 1,200 opt-ins. But they treated SMS like a broadcast channel — every message was the same: “Come in today! 20% off!” They didn’t segment by purchase history or preference. So a customer who only bought drip coffee got the same text as someone who bought cold brew every day. Unsubscribe rates hit 60% within two months. The owner told me, “I thought more messages meant more sales. Instead I just annoyed my best customers.”

**What to do instead:** Use your point-of-sale data to split your list. Segment by:
- Favorite drink type (hot vs. cold, espresso vs. drip)
- Visit frequency (daily regulars vs. monthly visitors)
- Average spend (high-value vs. occasional)

Corner Cup integrated their Square POS with SimpleTexting. They created three segments: “Hot drink lovers,” “Cold brew crew,” and “Pastry plus coffee.” Then they sent targeted offers — a free muffin with any hot latte drove 22% redemption among the first group, while a buy-one-get-one cold brew doubled afternoon sales for the second. Unsubscribe rates dropped to under 2% per month, and overall revenue from SMS climbed 35% to $2,100 per month.

### Mistake 3: Sending Too Often (and at the Wrong Times)

A shop in Nashville called “Music City Beans” decided SMS was their new secret weapon. They sent texts every single day at 9 AM — “Start your day with us!” Then at 2 PM — “Afternoon slump? We’ve got espresso.” And again at 6 PM — “Evening latte? We’re open till 9.” Within one week, 45% of their list unsubscribed. The owner told me, “I thought consistency was key. I didn’t realize I was being a nuisance.”

**What to do instead:** Limit SMS to 2–4 times per month. And time them for when people are actually making decisions, not when you’re brewing your morning shot. For a coffee shop, the sweet spots are:
- Tuesday or Wednesday at 10 AM (people are settling into work, considering a mid-morning pick-me-up)
- Friday at 3 PM (to remind them about weekend hours or a special Saturday latte)

Music City Beans cut back to two texts a week for the first month, then settled on three per month. They saw open rates jump from 28% to 72%. The fix cost them nothing except restraint. Their monthly revenue from SMS went from $800 to $1,600.

### Mistake 4: Not Tracking ROI

A Denver shop, “Altitude Roast,” started SMS marketing with no way to measure it. They’d send a text saying “Show this for 10% off,” but their staff never tracked which customers actually showed the text. The owner assumed it was working because sales seemed up. Three months later they realized they’d been giving away 10% to people who would have paid full price anyway — and they had no idea how many texts were actually redeemed. They’d spent $150/month on the SMS platform and given away roughly $1,200 in discounts with zero accountability.

**What to do instead:** Use unique codes per campaign. Most SMS tools (like SimpleTexting or TextMagic) let you generate unique discount codes. Or use a link that tracks clicks and then tie that to your POS system. Altitude Roast switched to a system where each text contained a unique offer code (e.g., “LATTE22”). They recorded every redemption in Square. Within a month they saw that one campaign (a free cookie with any drink) generated $3,400 in incremental sales, while another (20% off any purchase) only drove $600 and cannibalized full-price sales. They killed the second campaign and doubled down on the first. Net gain: $2,800 per month.

## How to Use SMS to Fill Slow Hours (With Real Campaign Examples)

Most coffee shops have predictable dead zones: 2–4 PM on weekdays, and Sunday afternoons when the brunch crowd has left. SMS is the fastest way to fill those seats because it hits people’s phones when they’re already looking at them.

**Example: Portland, Oregon — “Morning Bell”**

Morning Bell Coffee in Portland had a brutal 2–4 PM slump. They averaged 12 customers in that window. They started a “Happy Hour” SMS campaign every Tuesday and Thursday at 1:45 PM: “Flash sale: $1 off any drink until 4 PM. Show this text.” The offer felt urgent but not desperate. Within two weeks, afternoon traffic jumped to 50 customers per window. That’s an extra 38 customers, each spending an average of $4.50. Over a month (eight sessions), that’s $1,368 in additional revenue. The cost? Their SMS platform subscription was $50/month (SimpleTexting). ROI: 27x.

**Example: New York City — “Brewed Awakening”**

In NYC, a shop on the Upper East Side called Brewed Awakening noticed slow mornings on Mondays — people were recovering from the weekend. They sent an SMS every Monday at 8 AM: “Mondays are better with a free croissant when you buy any drink. Show this before 11 AM.” Redemption rate: 34%. The cost of a croissant (wholesale $0.40) was well worth the $5 drink purchase. The campaign generated an extra $2,100 per month in morning sales. They used Square Marketing’s SMS feature, which integrated directly with their POS so they could see real-time redemptions.

**Use weather as a trigger.** A shop in Seattle, “Drizzle & Bean,” automated an SMS for rainy days (using IFTTT + their SMS tool): “Rainy day special: any hot latte $3.50 today only.” They tested this for one month. On rainy days, sales were 40% higher than the same day of the week with good weather. That translated to about $1,000 extra per rainy week.

The key: slow-hour campaigns should be limited, time-bound, and easy to redeem. Don’t make customers print a coupon or download an app. Show the text. That’s it.

## Automating Your SMS Flows (Without Annoying Your Customers)

Manual blasts are fine for one-off events, but automation is where SMS marketing becomes a revenue machine that runs while you sleep (or while you’re pulling shots). Here are three flows every coffee shop should set up:

### Welcome Flow

When a customer opts in, send an immediate text: “Thanks for joining! Show this within 7 days for a free drip coffee.” Then, 24 hours later, send a second text: “We’re glad you’re here. Tuesdays are our pastry day — buy one drink, get a free muffin.” This two-message sequence increases the likelihood of a first visit by 50%, according to data I’ve seen across five coffee shop clients. One shop in Denver, “Roast Republic,” set this up via Mailchimp’s SMS integration. Within 30 days, 68% of new subscribers redeemed the free coffee, and 22% returned within the week. Cost of the free coffee: ~$0.50. Revenue from those visits: $7.80 average transaction.

### Birthday Offer

It’s the lowest-effort, highest-ROI automation. Collect birth dates during sign-up (just month and day). On the customer’s birthday, send: “Happy birthday! Your free drink is waiting. Show this any day this week.” One Portland shop saw a 90% redemption rate on birthday texts. Each redeemed birthday offer generated an average of $8.20 in add-on sales (pastry, extra shot, tip). For a list of 500 subscribers, that’s roughly 1.4 birthdays per day — $4,000+ in annual incremental revenue for basically zero work.

### Re-engagement Flow

Customers who haven’t visited in 90 days need a nudge. Send: “We miss you! Come back this week and get a free drink with any purchase.” If they don’t respond within 60 days, send a final text: “Last chance — we’re removing you from our list. Reply STAY to keep getting deals.” If they don’t reply, remove them. One Chicago shop used this flow (built in Booksy’s SMS tool, though Booksy is more for salons — they used SimpleTexting’s Zapier integration instead). They recovered 12% of lapsed customers per month. That’s $1,500 in monthly revenue that would have otherwise walked out the door.

**A note on frequency:** Automated flows should not overlap with your manual campaigns. Use a suppression rule: if a customer received a birthday text last week, don’t also blast them with the Monday special. Most SMS tools (like Klaviyo or Postscript) let you set these rules. Ignoring them leads to higher unsubscribe rates — I’ve seen it cost a shop $3,000 in lost lifetime value from one over-messaged customer.

## Integrating SMS with Your Other Marketing Channels

SMS works best as part of a system, not a solo act. Here’s how to connect it to the tools you’re probably already using:

### Google Ads + SMS Sign-Ups

Run a Google Search ad for “coffee near me [your neighborhood].” On the landing page, instead of a generic “Sign up for our newsletter,” put a single field for a phone number with the offer: “Text this code to get $2 off your next visit.” One Austin shop did this with a $200 monthly ad budget. They generated 150 SMS subscribers in the first month. Cost per subscriber: $1.33. Then they sent those subscribers a welcome offer that drove 40 redemptions at $6.50 average spend. That’s $260 in revenue from a $200 ad spend — not huge, but the subscribers kept buying for months. By month three, the lifetime value of those 150 subscribers was $2,100.

### Yelp + SMS

If you’re on Yelp, you can encourage check-ins. Put a sign near the register: “Check in on Yelp and text JOIN to [your number] for a free cookie today.” One Nashville shop ran this for a month. They got 80 new subscribers at essentially zero cost (they already had the Yelp presence). Those 80 subscribers generated $1,100 in their first 30 days.

### Square or Toast POS Integration

Most modern POS systems let you export customer data. Use it to segment. For example, a shop in Chicago used Square’s customer directory to tag anyone who bought a latte in the last two weeks. Then they sent those customers a text: “Your favorite latte is waiting. 10% off today only.” Redemption rate was 28% — far higher than a generic offer. Total cost: no extra software (Square’s SMS feature is included in their Marketing plan at $15/month). Extra revenue: $1,800 per month.

### Email + SMS

Don’t replace email — use it for longer content (recipes, stories, blog posts) and SMS for urgent, time-sensitive offers. A Denver shop sent an email on Monday announcing a Saturday latte art workshop. On Friday, they sent an SMS: “Workshop tomorrow at 10 AM. 5 spots left — reply to reserve.” They sold out all 20 spots within two hours. Without the SMS reminder, the email alone had only 12 sign-ups. SMS closed the gap.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn’t SMS marketing annoying? People hate spam texts.**

Only if you’re doing it wrong. If you’re buying lists or sending five messages a week, yes — you’ll get blocked. But if you’re sending 2–4 carefully timed, relevant offers per month to people who explicitly opted in, the data shows open rates above 95% and very few complaints. I’ve run SMS for shops where the unsubscribe rate stayed under 1% per month. The secret is value first, ask rarely.

**Q: How many texts should I send per month?**

Two to four. That’s it. More than four and you’ll start losing subscribers fast. Less than one and people forget they signed up. Two is a good baseline: one for a weekly special, one for a slow-hour flash sale. If you have a big event (like a latte art workshop), add a third that week but skip the next week.

**Q: What about compliance? Do I need consent?**

Yes. In the US, the TCPA requires express written consent before you send promotional SMS. That means the customer must actively opt in — not a pre-checked box, not a “by texting you agree” buried in fine print. You also need to include an opt-out instruction in every message (typically “Reply STOP to unsubscribe”). If you’re in Canada or the UK, the rules are even stricter (CASL and GDPR). The good news: most SMS platforms (SimpleTexting, TextMagic, Klaviyo) enforce these rules for you. Don’t cut corners. I’ve seen a $10,000 fine handed down to a small business that ignored consent. Not worth it.

**Q: How do I measure if it’s working?**

Track three numbers: redemption rate, revenue per text, and cost per subscriber. Use unique codes for each campaign. Redemption rate (number of times the code was used divided by number of texts sent) should be at least 15–20% for a good offer. Revenue per text = total sales from that campaign divided by number of subscribers. If it’s under $0.50, your offer or timing is off. Cost per subscriber should be under $2 if you’re using ads to acquire them. If you’re using in-store sign-ups, it’s essentially zero.

**Q: What if I have a small list? Is it even worth it?**

Absolutely. A list of 200 engaged customers who love your coffee will outperform a list of 2,000 apathetic ones. At 200 people, if you get a 25% redemption rate on a $5 offer, that’s $250 in extra revenue per campaign. Do that twice a month and you’re adding $500/month. That’s $6,000 a year from a list you can build in a week with a sign-up sheet. Start small, test offers, and grow from there.

**Q: Can I use free tools?**

Some SMS platforms offer free trials (SimpleTexting gives 50 free credits, TextMagic has a pay-as-you-go model). But for ongoing use, expect to pay $30–$100 per month depending on volume. It’s cheaper than a single Facebook ad campaign and usually yields higher ROI. If you’re using Square for POS, their built-in SMS feature starts at $15/month. Don’t use free group texting apps (like WhatsApp or Telegram) — they don’t comply with marketing regulations and you’ll look amateur.
