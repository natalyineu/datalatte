**Choosing the Right Google Ads Strategy**

When it comes to Google Ads, there are several strategies you can use to attract renters to your storage facility. Here are a few options to consider:

* **Location-based targeting**: Target renters in specific zip codes or cities to attract local business.
* **Keyword targeting**: Target renters who are searching for specific keywords, such as "storage facilities near me" or "climate-controlled storage units".
* **Competitor targeting**: Target renters who are searching for storage facilities similar to yours.

**Example: Google Ads Campaign for Storage Facility**

Let's say you own a storage facility in a busy city with a high demand for storage units. You want to target renters who are searching for storage facilities in your area. Here's an example Google Ads campaign that you could use:

* **Ad group**: "Storage Facilities in [City]"
* **Target keywords**: "storage facilities near me", "climate-controlled storage units", "storage facilities in [City]"
* **Ad copy**: "Secure storage units in [City]. Get a quote today!"

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Bidding on Broad Keywords Like “Storage” Alone

**The story from Austin, TX**

I worked with a self-storage facility owner named Tom who had been running Google Ads for six months. He was spending $2,400 a month on campaigns targeting broad keywords like “storage,” “storage units,” and “cheap storage.” Tom’s logic: “People looking for storage will type those words, right?”

Wrong.

Here’s what actually happened. Tom’s ads were showing up for searches like “storage bins for organizing my closet,” “storage shed for backyard,” and “how to store winter tires.” He was paying $4 to $7 per click for people who had zero interest in renting a climate-controlled unit. Tom told me his phone wasn’t ringing, and his occupancy rate hadn’t budged. He was burning through his entire monthly budget on clicks that would never convert.

The uncomfortable truth is that Google’s broad match has gotten smarter over the years, but it still loves to spend your money on anything that remotely resembles your keyword. For a local storage facility, broad match is a leaky bucket.

**The fix**

I paused all of Tom’s broad match keywords and rebuilt the campaign with phrase match and exact match keywords only. Here’s what we used:

- “storage units Austin TX” (phrase match)
- “climate controlled storage Austin” (phrase match)
- “self storage near me 78701” (exact match)
- “cheap storage units Austin” (phrase match)

We also added a long list of negative keywords: “storage bins,” “storage shed,” “storage ideas,” “storage containers for sale,” “warehouse storage,” “moving boxes.” That alone stopped the bleeding.

**The outcome**

Within three weeks, Tom’s cost per lead dropped from $38 to $12. His phone calls went from maybe two a week to eight to ten a week. He signed four new tenants in the first month of the new campaign. That’s $3,200 in monthly revenue from those units alone, against a revised ad spend of $1,200 per month.

Tom’s response: “I can’t believe I let Google just take my money for six months.”

I can. I’ve seen this exact mistake kill campaigns at three different clients. Storage is a hyperlocal business. Your keywords need to reflect that.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Location Targeting Radius and Overlapping Markets

**The story from Nashville, TN**

A facility owner named Carla was running ads with a 50-mile radius around her property. She thought bigger was better. “More people will see my ad,” she told me. “That’s more potential renters.”

Here’s what actually happened. Carla’s facility was located near the Nashville airport. Her 50-mile radius covered parts of Murfreesboro, Franklin, Hendersonville, and even Lebanon. People in those cities have their own storage options. They don’t want to drive 40 minutes to store a box of Christmas decorations. But Carla was paying for those clicks anyway.

Worse, her campaign was competing with facilities in those other cities. Google’s algorithm was showing her ad to people in Lebanon who searched “storage units Lebanon TN,” and Carla was paying for that click. She was essentially funding her competitors’ free exposure while getting zero calls.

**The fix**

I condensed Carla’s targeting to a 10-mile radius around her facility. I also added location exclusions for areas that were closer to other storage facilities. We set up different ad groups for the key neighborhoods within that radius—East Nashville, Donelson, Hermitage—and wrote ad copy that referenced those specific neighborhoods. “Convenient storage for East Nashville residents” beats “Storage near the airport” every time.

**The outcome**

Carla’s monthly ad spend went from $1,800 to $1,100. Her cost per click actually went up slightly because the audience was more competitive locally. But her cost per lead dropped from $29 to $11. She went from spending money on clicks in Murfreesboro to actually renting units in her own backyard. Within two months, she filled 12 units that had been sitting empty. That’s $4,800 in new monthly recurring revenue.

Carla’s observation: “I was paying to advertise my competitors.”

Yes. Yes, you were.

### Mistake #3: Using a Generic Landing Page Instead of a Dedicated Storage Landing Page

**The story from Portland, OR**

A facility chain with three locations hired me after their agency had been running Google Ads for eight months with almost nothing to show for it. They were sending all traffic to their homepage. The homepage had a hero image of a storage unit, a phone number at the top, and links to four different pages about unit sizes, pricing, locations, and a blog post about “storage tips for college students.”

I clicked on the ad, landed on that homepage, and couldn’t find a way to rent a unit in less than three clicks. I had to scroll, click “units and pricing,” then click a location, then click “reserve now.” By the time I got there, I was annoyed. Most people don’t have that patience.

The uncomfortable truth is that a homepage is not a landing page. It’s a navigation hub. When someone clicks on a Google ad, they want one thing: a fast, clear path to renting a unit or getting a price. If you make them hunt for it, they bounce.

**The fix**

I built a dedicated landing page for each of the three locations. Each page had:

- A headline that matched the ad copy exactly (e.g., “Climate Controlled Storage in SE Portland – Starting at $89/mo”)
- A simple form with four fields: name, phone, email, unit size needed
- A phone number displayed prominently at the top and repeated at the bottom
- Three bullet points that addressed common objections: no admin fee for online reservations, month-to-month leases, 24/7 access
- A single call-to-action button: “Check Availability Now”

We also added trust signals: a Google star rating, a short testimonial, and a note that the facility was locally owned and operated. No blog posts, no news articles, no “about us” history lesson.

**The outcome**

The conversion rate jumped from 1.2% to 6.8% in the first week. The chain’s monthly leads went from 14 to 48. The campaign became profitable within the first month. They went from spending $2,600 per month with a negative ROI to spending $1,900 per month and generating $8,400 in new monthly revenue.

One of the owners said to me: “Why didn’t the other agency tell us to do this?”

Because they were charging a management fee based on ad spend, not on performance. They had no incentive to optimize the landing pages. Most guides skip this part—don’t.

### Mistake #4: Not Tracking Phone Calls as Conversions

**The story from Chicago, IL**

A family-run storage facility in the Wicker Park neighborhood was spending $1,500 per month on Google Ads. They had conversion tracking set up for form submissions, and they were getting about 10 per month. They thought things were fine.

But the owner, Maria, mentioned in passing that she was getting “maybe 30 to 40 phone calls a week” from the ads. I asked her how she knew those were from ads. She said “people say they saw us on Google.”

So she was getting three to four times as many leads from phone calls as from forms, and none of those were being counted as conversions. Google’s algorithm, which optimizes for conversions, was seeing only form submissions. It was allocating more budget to keywords and ad copy that drove form fills, even if those same keywords were driving phone calls at a lower cost.

**The fix**

I set up Google’s call tracking using call forwarding numbers. We ran a test for two weeks: half the traffic saw a form-first landing page, the other half saw a phone-number-first landing page with a smaller form below the fold. We tagged all incoming calls with the Google click ID to attribute them correctly.

**The outcome**

The phone-number-first page generated 18 calls and 4 form fills per week. The form-first page generated 6 calls and 8 form fills. Total leads for the phone-first page: 22. Total for the form-first: 14. The cost per lead for phone calls was $7. The cost per lead for form fills was $18.

Maria switched to the phone-number-first page permanently. Her cost per lead dropped to $7.50, and her monthly leads went from 40 to 85. She filled 22 units in the next two months. That’s $7,920 in new monthly revenue.

Maria’s takeaway: “I was optimizing for the wrong thing the whole time.”

She was right. If you’re not tracking phone calls as conversions in Google Ads, you’re flying blind.

## Why Your Google Business Profile Matters More Than Your Website

Most storage facility owners I talk to spend weeks agonizing over their website design. They argue about font sizes and hero images. Meanwhile, their Google Business Profile is collecting dust with an incorrect phone number and a photo of the parking lot from 2017.

Here’s a fact that might surprise you: when someone searches for “storage units near me,” Google shows a local pack with three listings. These three listings get roughly 40% of all clicks on that search results page. Your website gets the rest. If your business profile isn’t in that pack, you are invisible to nearly half of your potential renters.

**Ranking in the local pack depends on three things: proximity, relevance, and prominence.**

Proximity is where you’re located. You can’t change that. But you can control relevance by making sure your business categories are correct. Most storage facilities have their primary category set to “Storage Facility,” which is correct. But I’ve seen them set as “Warehouse,” “Moving Company,” or even “General Contractor.” One facility in Denver had their profile set to “Self Storage,” which isn’t even a standard Google category. That alone was killing their local rankings.

Prominence is about reviews and citations. This is where most storage facilities drop the ball.

**The review problem**

A facility in Denver had 23 reviews total. Their competitor had 187. Guess who showed up in the local pack more often?

I told the owner we needed to get at least 50 reviews in three months. He said “people don’t want to leave reviews for storage.” I asked him if he asked them. He said no.

We set up a simple system: when someone rented a unit, we sent them a text message two weeks later with a direct link to leave a Google review. We offered a $10 discount on their next month’s rent for leaving a review. Within 90 days, they had 64 new reviews. Their average rating stayed at 4.6 stars. They started appearing in the local pack for searches they had never ranked for before. Organic phone calls from Google Maps went up 40%.

**The citation problem**

Your business name, address, and phone number need to be consistent across every site: Yelp, Facebook, Yellow Pages, Nextdoor, storage directories, your own website. Google cross-references these. If your NAP data is inconsistent, Google gets confused and ranks you lower.

One client in Phoenix had his address listed as “1234 W Main St” on his website and “1234 West Main Street” on Yelp. That simple discrepancy was enough to drop his local rankings. We fixed it across 12 different directories. Within two weeks, his business profile moved from position 7 in the local pack to position 3.

**Actionable steps for your Google Business Profile:**

- Set your primary category to “Storage Facility.” Add secondary categories like “Boat Storage,” “RV Storage,” or “Climate Controlled Storage Facility” if relevant.
- Upload 20+ photos. Include the exterior, interior, security features, and a shot of a clean, empty unit. Update photos quarterly.
- Enable messaging. Many renters prefer to text rather than call.
- Post a Google Post every week. Offer a discount on first month’s rent. Announce a new security feature. Answer a common question. These posts show Google that your profile is active.
- Respond to every review, positive or negative. Keep it short and professional. Google weighs response rate in its ranking algorithm.

One final note: your Google Business Profile is free. It costs nothing to optimize. But most storage facility owners treat it as an afterthought. That’s a mistake that costs you renters every single day.

## The Retargeting Loop That Actually Works for Storage Rentals

Storage is not an impulse purchase. People don’t see a Google ad at 10 AM and rent a unit at 11 AM. They compare prices, check reviews, measure their stuff, and sometimes wait a week or two before deciding. If you’re not retargeting them, you’re leaving money on the table.

Most storage owners set up a basic retargeting campaign. Someone visits the website, they see a banner ad for the next two weeks, and then the campaign runs on autopilot. That’s not a strategy. That’s throwing darts in the dark.

Here’s what I’ve seen work across three different storage campaigns.

**Step 1: Segment your retargeting audiences**

Not all visitors are equal. Someone who landed on your “climate controlled 10×10” page is different from someone who landed on your homepage and left after two seconds. You need separate retargeting audiences for each.

We use Google Ads to create three retargeting lists:

- Visitors who viewed a unit-specific page (high intent)
- Visitors who started the reservation process but didn’t finish (very high intent)
- Visitors who spent more than 30 seconds on the site but didn’t take action (medium intent)

Each audience gets different ad copy.

**Step 2: Write ad copy that addresses the hesitation**

A storage facility in Phoenix was running a generic retargeting ad that said “Rent a Storage Unit Today.” It was getting a 0.2% click-through rate. I rewrote the ad copy based on the most common objections I heard from their actual phone calls:

For the high-intent audience who looked at a specific unit size: “Still deciding on the 10×10? Reserve it now and get your first month at $1. We’ll hold it for 48 hours.”

For the medium-intent audience: “Not sure how much space you need? Use our unit size calculator. Takes 2 minutes.”

For the abandoned reservation audience: “You were one step away. Complete your reservation and we’ll waive the admin fee.”

The new ads got a 1.8% click-through rate. That’s 9x higher than the generic version.

**Step 3: Set frequency caps**

One mistake I keep seeing: hitting the same person with the same ad 15 times in a week. That’s not effective marketing. That’s harassment.

Set your frequency cap to 3 impressions per day per person. Any more than that and you’re training people to ignore your ad. You’re also paying more for the same audience.

**Step 4: Use time-limited offers in retargeting**

Storage is a local business, and local businesses benefit from urgency. I’ve tested two approaches: open-ended offers (“first month half off”) and time-limited offers (“first month half off if you rent within 72 hours”). The time-limited offers consistently convert at 3x the rate.

You need to honor the offer. Don’t bait and switch. If you say “72 hours,” honor it. People talk.

**The tool stack for a proper retargeting campaign:**

- Google Ads for search and display retargeting
- Facebook Ads for social retargeting (yes, even though it’s storage)
- Mailchimp or Constant Contact for email retargeting to anyone who provided their email but didn’t rent

**The real numbers from a campaign in Austin, TX:**

We set up retargeting for a facility that was already running search ads. Monthly search ad spend: $1,400. Monthly retargeting ad spend: $400. The retargeting campaign generated an additional 11 leads per month at a cost of $36 per lead. Of those, 4 rented a unit. That’s $1,440 in new monthly revenue from $400 in additional spend. ROI: 3.6x.

Retargeting isn’t optional. It’s the difference between renting units to visitors and watching them go to your competitor because they saw your ad once and forgot your name.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads for my storage facility?**

Start with $1,000 to $1,500 per month if you have a single location in a medium-sized city. If you’re in a competitive market like Los Angeles or New York, expect to spend $2,500 to $3,500 per month to get meaningful results. The key isn’t how much you spend—it’s what you get for that spend. A well-optimized campaign at $1,200 per month will outperform a sloppy campaign at $3,000 per month. I’ve seen both.

**Q: How long until I see results from Google Ads?**

You’ll see clicks within hours of launching your campaign. You’ll see leads within the first week if your targeting and landing page are set up correctly. But real results—signed leases, filled units, measurable ROI—take 30 to 60 days. That’s how long the data collection and optimization cycle takes. Anyone who promises you “overnight results” is selling something. I’m not.

**Q: Should I bid on my own business name?**

Usually not. If your Google Business Profile is set up correctly, you’ll already show up when someone searches your business name. Bidding on your own name means paying for clicks you’d get for free. The one exception: if you have a common name like “Austin Storage” and there are competitors also using that name in their ads. In that case, a small bid of $0.50 to $1.00 per click on your own name can protect your brand traffic. Test it. If you’re getting those clicks for free, turn off the bid.

**Q: Do I need to advertise on Yelp, too?**

Yes, but only after your Google Ads are working. Yelp ads are more expensive per click and harder to optimize. I’ve seen storage facilities spend $500 per month on Yelp and get maybe three phone calls. That’s a 66% premium over Google for worse results. Start with Google. Once you’re consistently getting leads at a cost you can live with, test Yelp with a small budget. Don’t let Yelp’s sales team talk you into a $1,000 monthly commitment. They will try.

**Q: How do I compete with big storage chains like Public Storage or Extra Space Storage?**

You don’t outspend them. You out-local them. Large chains have national campaigns with massive budgets. They run on generic keywords and broad messaging. You can beat them by targeting specific neighborhoods, using local landmarks in your ad copy, and showing up in local search results. Write ads that say “Storage near the Target on Broadway” or “5 minutes from downtown Nashville.” Big chains can’t do that at scale. Also, your Google Business Profile reviews matter more for you than for them. Get 100 reviews with real names and local locations mentioned. That credibility can’t be bought.

**Q: Should I include pricing in my Google Ads?**

Yes, but test it first. Some markets are price-sensitive. If you’re in a lower-cost market like Tulsa, listing a starting price of $49 per month can drive clicks and calls. But if you’re in a high-cost market like San Francisco, listing $199 per month might scare people away before they understand that’s for a large climate-controlled unit. Test both approaches: one ad group with pricing, one without. Let the data decide. I’ve seen both work depending on the market.
