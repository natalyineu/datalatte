## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched pet groomers throw money at email marketing and get nothing back. Then they blame the tool. Then they blame their customers. Then they go back to printing flyers that end up in the recycling bin.

The tool isn't the problem. Here are the four mistakes I see most often, with real stories from businesses that made them.

### Mistake #1: Emailing Everyone the Same Thing

A groomer in Nashville, TN — let's call her shop Happy Paws — had 1,200 email subscribers. She sent the same monthly newsletter to every single one. A 15% off coupon for full grooming. Every time.

Her open rate hovered around 12%. Her click rate was under 2%. She was spending $79/month on Mailchimp's Standard plan and getting maybe 3–4 bookings from each send. At an average ticket of $65 per groom, that's $260 in revenue from a campaign that took her two hours to write. Before Mailchimp fees, before her time.

The problem: She was sending a full-grooming discount to customers who only came in for nail trims and ear cleaning. She was sending it to customers who'd just been in last week. She was sending it to people who'd ghosted her for six months.

The fix: We segmented her list into three groups — active clients (booked within 60 days), lapsed clients (90+ days since last visit), and service-specific (nail-only, full groom, specialty breeds). Active clients got a reminder that their next appointment was due, plus a seasonal add-on offer (deshedding treatment for spring, paw balm for winter). Lapsed clients got a "we miss you" email with a modest 10% off but no expiration date — because urgency doesn't work on people who already feel guilty about not coming in. Service-specific clients got offers that matched what they actually bought.

The outcome: After three months, her open rate hit 31%. Click rate hit 8%. That same $79/month plan was generating 18–22 bookings per send, worth roughly $1,200 in revenue. She cut her email frequency from 4x per month to 2x and got better results.

### Mistake #2: Trying to Sell Before You've Built Trust

A mobile grooming van operating in Portland, OR — Furry Road — sent their very first email to a list of 80 subscribers. The subject line: "50% Off First Groom — Book Now."

Zero opens. Zero clicks. Zero bookings.

I talked to the owner, and she'd bought the email list from a local business networking event. Every person on that list had checked a box saying "yes, I'd like to hear from local businesses." But none of them had ever used Furry Road's service. None of them knew who she was. None of them had any reason to trust her with their anxious rescue dog.

The fix: She deleted that entire list. Started over. Built a pop-up form on her website offering a free PDF guide called "5 Signs Your Dog Needs a Groom (Beyond the Obvious)." The form asked for name, email, and pet type. No phone number. No hard sell.

She sent three welcome emails spaced a week apart:
1. A short intro to her background and philosophy — not a sales pitch, just who she is.
2. A practical article about matting in long-haired breeds, with photos of before/after from her actual clients.
3. An offer: $15 off the first groom, but only after showing her work in the first two emails.

The outcome: That original 80-person list grew to 410 over four months. Open rate on the welcome sequence was 44%. Booking rate from that third email averaged 9%. She was spending $20/month on Mailchimp's Essentials plan and averaging $780/month in new client revenue from email alone. The list she bought originally? She still has it. She hasn't sent to it once.

### Mistake #3: Forgetting Time Zone Math

A groomer in Denver, CO — Alpine Grooming Co. — was sending her weekly reminder emails at 9:00 AM Eastern time. She lived in Denver. She scheduled everything from her phone, and Mailchimp defaults to your account timezone.

She was sending appointment reminders to Denver clients at 7:00 AM Mountain time. Her open rate on reminder emails was 18%. About half her clients would show up late or not at all. She thought it was a client problem.

It was a timezone problem. Her clients were checking email at 8:30 AM — after the school drop-off, after the first coffee, when they're actually looking at their calendar. Her emails were sitting at the bottom of their inbox for an hour and a half, buried by work emails, school alerts, and Amazon shipping confirmations.

The fix: She set Mailchimp to send at 9:30 AM Mountain time, and she started using Mailchimp's "send time optimization" feature (available on Standard plans) which uses historical data to predict when each subscriber is most likely to open. She also changed her reminder cadence — a 48-hour reminder instead of 24 hours, because Denver clients needed more lead time to adjust their schedules.

The outcome: Open rate on reminders jumped to 37%. No-show rate dropped from 12% to 4%. That's not just a convenience improvement — at $70 per groom slot, a 12% no-show rate on 30 weekly appointments means $252 in lost revenue per week. Cutting that to 4% saves over $3,500 in six months. For a scheduling change that took ten minutes.

### Mistake #4: Sending Without a Single, Clear Action

A groomer in Austin, TX — Bark & Board — had beautiful emails. Professional photos. Witty copy. Two thousand subscribers who clearly liked her because her open rate was 28%.

But she was getting maybe 5 bookings per send.

I looked at her most recent email. It had three different calls to action: "Book an appointment," "Shop our new pet bandanas," and "Follow us on Instagram." Plus a link to her blog about flea prevention.

She was asking people to do four different things. So nobody did anything.

This is called Hick's Law, and I'm not going to pretend I learned it in a marketing textbook. I learned it from watching GroupM spend $2 million on a campaign that buried the CTA under three secondary options, and watching the client's VP scream at us in a conference room. Some lessons stick.

The fix: Bark & Board started sending one email, one purpose. If the email was about booking an appointment, the only link was the booking button. The hero image was a dog getting brushed. The body text said exactly: "Lily's due for her next groom. Pick a time that works." One link. One decision.

The outcome: Booking rate tripled. Not doubled. Tripled. She was averaging 15–18 bookings per email send within six weeks. She also discovered that the "follow us on Instagram" traffic she was worried about? It actually went up, because people clicked the booking link, then voluntarily followed her from the confirmation page. She didn't have to ask.

## Using Automation to Book More Appointments (Without Losing the Personal Touch)

Most small business owners hear "automation" and picture cold, robotic emails that make their clients feel like account numbers. I get it. I've seen the alternative — a groomer in Chicago who sent individual text reminders to 200 clients every week by hand. She was proud of the personal touch. She was also burning six hours a week on something a $29/month Mailchimp plan could do in three minutes.

Here's the automation strategy that actually works for pet groomers, with the specific settings I've tested across multiple businesses.

### The Booking Recovery Sequence

About 40% of your new clients won't book a second appointment. Not because they didn't like you. Because life happens. The dog had a bad day. They forgot. They meant to call but got busy. Then three months pass, and now they're embarrassed to come back. They find a different groomer instead.

Mailchimp can handle this automatically. Set up a "post-visit" automation that triggers 28 days after a booking is marked complete. The sequence:

- **Day 28**: "Is Fluffy due for a touch-up?" — Short email. One photo from their last visit (you have to take these during the groom, and if you're not taking before/after photos, you're leaving money on the table). A direct booking link. No discount needed yet.
- **Day 42**: "We saved your groomer's notes" — Remind them that their preferred groomer has notes about their dog's coat, behavior, and preferences. This creates psychological ownership — they feel like they're losing something by not booking.
- **Day 60**: "It's been two months. Here's $15 off." — Now you add an incentive. But only after two non-discount touches. Because if you lead with a coupon, you train clients to wait for the coupon.

I set this up for a groomer in Charlotte, NC who was losing 55% of new clients after the first visit. Within 90 days, her second-booking rate went from 45% to 68%. That's not a vanity metric — that's recurring revenue from a segment she was previously abandoning.

### Seasonal Trigger Campaigns

Mailchimp's automation can also react to dates. Set up a campaign that triggers when a client hasn't booked in 90 days and the local weather crosses a temperature threshold. This sounds complex but it's not — Mailchimp integrates with weather APIs through Zapier, or you can just pair date-based triggers with manual seasonal sends.

The practical version: Set a tag for "winter_coat" and "summer_coat" based on breed. When October hits, send a sequence to clients with double-coated breeds (Huskies, Golden Retrievers, Shepherds) explaining why their shedding is about to get worse and how a deshedding groom can help. Use your own before/after photos. Be specific.

A groomer in Minneapolis used this exact approach. She sent three emails over three weeks starting October 1. Her October bookings were up 34% year-over-year. She attributed $2,100 in additional revenue to those three emails. Cost: maybe an hour to write them, then Mailchimp automation handled delivery.

### The Cancellation Recovery

Clients cancel. It happens. But most groomers just mark the cancellation and move on.

Set up an automation that fires when you change a booking status to "cancelled" in your booking system (Booksy, Square Appointments, Vagaro, whatever you use). The email should go out 24 hours later, and it should say something specific: "We're sorry Fluffy's appointment didn't work out. We've held her usual slot open for the next two weeks." Attach a link to your reschedule page. No discount. No pressure. Just a clear path to rebook.

A groomer in Phoenix told me this single automation recovered 22% of cancellations over six months. At an average $65 per groom, that's roughly $1,400 in revenue she would have just lost.

## Segmentation: Why Your One-Email-Fits-All Approach Is Costing You Money

I've sat through too many agency meetings where someone said "we need to personalize the experience" and then put everyone in one bucket and called it a day. Mailchimp offers 30+ segmentation options. You need exactly four.

### Segment 1: Breed-Specific Groups

Not all dogs groom the same. A Poodle needs grooming every 4–6 weeks. A short-haired Labrador can go 8–10 weeks. A matting-prone breed like a Doodle needs education about brushing between appointments.

Set up tags in Mailchimp for breed categories: "high_maintenance," "medium_maintenance," "low_maintenance." When you send reminders, adjust the frequency and tone.

Example: A groomer in Seattle had a Doodle client who was booking every 10 weeks. The dog kept coming in matted, requiring extra time and shaving down instead of a normal groom. The client was frustrated because her dog looked terrible. The groomer was frustrated because she was spending 90 minutes dematting a dog she could have groomed in 45.

The fix: A specific email sequence for Doodle owners about home brushing techniques, sent automatically two weeks after every groom. Plus a reminder that the optimal booking window is 6–8 weeks. No sales pitch — just education. Within three months, the average Doodle booking interval dropped from 9.2 weeks to 7.1 weeks. Matting incidents dropped by half. The groomer's revenue per Doodle client increased because she was booking more frequently and spending less time on dematting.

### Segment 2: New Client Onboarding

Your first email to a new client should not be a coupon. I've seen this kill relationships at three different businesses.

When someone books their first appointment, they trigger a "new client" tag. Send them an email before the appointment confirming what to expect: "Your dog will be with us for approximately 90 minutes. We'll call you 15 minutes before we're done. Please make sure Fluffy has had a bathroom break within the last hour."

Then after the appointment, send a follow-up with a photo of their dog fresh from the groom. Ask for a review. Do not ask for a referral yet. That comes at the third visit.

A groomer in Brooklyn used this sequence and increased her Google review rate from 3% to 18% of new clients. That's roughly 45 extra reviews over six months. In Brooklyn's dog-owner-heavy market, that many positive reviews directly translates to higher Google Maps ranking and more phone calls.

### Segment 3: Referral Promoters

Some clients will send you their friends unprompted. You need to identify them and reward them separately from clients who barely open your emails.

In Mailchimp, create a segment for clients who have clicked any "forward to a friend" link or who have sent you a direct referral. Tag them as "promoters." Send them a quarterly "thank you" offer — not a general coupon, but something exclusive. For pet groomers, this could be a free nail trim or a premium add-on like a teeth brushing treatment.

A groomer in Atlanta offered her top 50 promoters a "VIP appointment window" — first dibs on Saturday slots before they opened to the general public. She didn't even give a discount. The exclusivity was enough. Her referral rate from this segment went from 12% to 29% over the next quarter. She tracked $2,400 in new client revenue directly from those referrals.

### Segment 4: Event-Triggered Re-Engagement

People unsubscribe because they feel guilty for not booking. Not because they don't like you. I've seen the data — most unsubscribes from grooming businesses happen after 3+ months of non-booking, not after an annoying email.

Set up a re-engagement sequence that triggers at 90 days of inactivity. The sequence should be three emails over two weeks:

1. "We miss Fluffy" — just a photo and a note. No offer.
2. "What's changed?" — A genuine question. Link to a one-question survey asking if they've switched groomers, moved, or just been busy. You'll be surprised how many people respond, and the responses are genuinely useful.
3. "Come back. Seriously." — A modest offer, 10–15% off one groom. No expiration.

A groomer in San Diego ran this sequence on a list of 340 lapsed clients. She got 48 responses to the survey. 22 of them booked within two weeks of the third email. That's a 6.5% re-engagement rate on a segment she had completely written off. At $75 average ticket, that's $1,650 from people she was planning to delete from her list.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn't Mailchimp too expensive for a small grooming business? I only have 200 clients.**

Mailchimp's free plan covers up to 500 contacts and 1,000 sends per month. That's enough for one weekly newsletter to 200 clients, plus a couple automated sequences. The only catch is the free plan includes Mailchimp branding on your emails, which looks unprofessional. If that bothers you, the Essentials plan starts at $13/month for 500 contacts. At that price, one additional booking per month covers the cost. If you can't get one booking from email, the problem isn't Mailchimp's price.

**Q: I tried email marketing and got absolutely zero bookings. What am I doing wrong?**

Either you're emailing the wrong people, or you're emailing the right people with the wrong message. The most common failure I see: sending a coupon to a cold audience. Your first email to someone should never ask for a sale. Provide value first — a tip about shedding, a warning about ticks in your area, a photo of a dog your client will recognize. After two or three value-only touches, then you offer something. I have never seen this fail when done correctly. I have seen it fail hundreds of times when done wrong.

**Q: How often should I email my clients? I don't want to annoy them.**

Once a week is fine for active clients. Once every two weeks for lapsed clients. The clients who complain about frequency are almost always clients who haven't booked in months — they're already disengaged. Don't optimize for the 5% of your list who will unsubscribe. Optimize for the 95% who might book if you remind them. That said, don't email just to email. If you have nothing useful to say, skip a week. Your open rate will thank you.

**Q: Can Mailchimp connect to my booking system, or do I have to manually export lists?**

Mailchimp integrates directly with Square Appointments, Booksy, Vagaro, and most grooming-specific software. If your booking system isn't a direct integration, Zapier can usually bridge the gap for $20/month. The manual export approach works but it will break down the first time you get busy and skip a week. Automate the connection. Future you will appreciate it.

**Q: What's the one thing most groomers get wrong with email marketing?**

They don't ask for the booking. I've read emails from groomers that are three paragraphs of cute dog stories with a link buried at the bottom that says "click here to see our services." Your email should have exactly one job. If you want them to book, the main action should be booking. Not reading your blog. Not following you on Instagram. Not looking at your gallery. Booking. One link. One button. One decision.

**Q: I'm worried about spam laws. Do I need a lawyer to send email marketing?**

No. Canada's CASL is the strictest, and it essentially requires that you have consent and include an unsubscribe link. Mailchimp handles the unsubscribe part automatically. For consent, if someone gives you their email at checkout or through your website, that's fine. Don't buy lists. Don't scrape emails from Yelp. If you follow those two rules and include your physical business address in the footer (Mailchimp requires this anyway), you're fine. I am not a lawyer. But I've managed compliance for agencies running campaigns across 12 countries, and this is the part that actually matters.
