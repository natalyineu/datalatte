If you're not using Google Ads, you're missing out on a huge opportunity to reach potential customers. Here's what you need to know to get started:

### Setting Up Your Google Ads Account

To start using Google Ads, you'll need to set up a Google Ads account. This involves creating a new account, adding your business information, and setting up your payment method. It's a straightforward process that takes about 15-20 minutes to complete.

### Choosing the Right Keywords

Once you have your account set up, it's time to choose the right keywords for your ads. This is the most critical part of the process, as it determines who sees your ads and when. For florists, some key keywords include "Valentine's Day flowers," "wedding bouquets," and "same-day delivery." Use tools like Google Keyword Planner to find the most relevant and high-traffic keywords for your business.

### Crafting Compelling Ad Copy

Your ad copy is what will grab the attention of potential customers and entice them to click on your ad. Keep it short, clear, and compelling. Use a mix of keywords and creative language to make your ads stand out. For example: "Beautiful Valentine's Day flowers delivered same-day to NYC. Order now and get 10% off!"

### Targeting Your Audience

Google Ads allows you to target your audience based on location, language, and device. For florists, it's essential to target people in your local area, as they're more likely to order from a local business. You can also target people based on their interests, such as "wedding planning" or "Valentine's Day gifts."

### Budgeting for Your Campaigns

Budgeting is a critical aspect of Google Ads. You'll need to determine how much you're willing to spend on your campaigns each month. A good rule of thumb is to start with a small budget and gradually increase it as you see results. For florists, a budget of $500-$1000 per month is a good starting point.

### Monitoring and Optimizing Your Campaigns

Once your campaigns are up and running, it's essential to monitor and optimize them regularly. Use Google Ads' built-in tools to track your performance, identify areas for improvement, and make data-driven decisions. Some key metrics to track include click-through rate (CTR), conversion rate, and cost per acquisition (CPA).

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Running one generic campaign for Valentine’s Day and weddings together**

I watched a florist in Austin, Texas spend $2,400 in February last year. She had one campaign called “flower delivery” with broad match keywords: roses, bouquets, same-day delivery, wedding flowers, bridal party. Every search for “Valentine’s Day roses” and “June wedding flowers” hit the same ad. The problem? Valentine’s Day searchers want immediate delivery and impulse buys. Wedding searchers want inspiration, pricing guides, and lead times. Her ads couldn’t speak to either audience well. Her click-through rate sat at 1.2%, and she got three wedding inquiries that never converted because the landing page was a generic shop page with no wedding-specific content. She also spent $700 on clicks from people looking for cheap grocery-store roses—not her $80 deluxe arrangements.

**The fix:** Separate campaigns by intent. One campaign for Valentine’s Day with ad schedule starting January 20, end date February 14, budget $300/day peak. Another campaign for weddings year-round with budget $25/day, lead gen ads pointing to a dedicated wedding page with a form and gallery. Use exact match and phrase match keywords only—no broad match. For Valentine’s Day: “Valentine’s Day roses delivery Austin,” “same-day Valentine flowers Austin.” For weddings: “wedding florist Austin,” “bridal bouquet Austin,” “wedding centerpieces Austin.”

**Outcome:** After the fix, the same florist ran Valentine’s Day on a $2,000 budget (saved $400 from the original waste) and brought in $8,200 in sales tracked through promo codes. The wedding campaign ran $30/day and generated 12 qualified leads per month, 4 of which booked (average wedding order $1,500). Total monthly wedding revenue $6,000. Combined cost per conversion dropped from $85 to $22.

**Mistake #2: Not adding negative keywords early**

A florist in Nashville told me she was burning $500 a month on searches for “flower girl dresses,” “flower seeds,” and “artificial flowers.” She sells fresh cut arrangements and wedding services, not dresses or gardening supplies. Google’s broad match had expanded her reach to anyone typing “flowers.” She didn’t check the search terms report for three weeks. By then, $1,200 had evaporated.

**The fix:** Build a negative keyword list before launch. Common ones for florists: “artificial,” “fake,” “silk,” “seeds,” “plants,” “delivery from” (if you don’t ship nationally), “diy,” “wholesale,” “flower girl dress,” “flower pot,” “flower shop” (if you want specific name searches). Then check the search terms report every 48 hours for the first two weeks and add new negatives.

**Outcome:** That Nashville florist cut wasted spend to $80/month on irrelevant clicks. Her conversion rate on the remaining traffic went from 1.8% to 4.1% because every click had purchase intent. She saved $420/month, which she reallocated to Valentine’s Day ads and saw a 3x return.

**Mistake #3: Sending all clicks to a homepage**

I see this more than I should. A florist in Portland, Oregon ran an ad for “bespoke wedding bouquets Portland.” The ad copy was great—it mentioned custom designs, free consultation, and a portfolio. But the destination URL went to her homepage, which had a hero image of Valentine’s roses, a Mother’s Day banner, and a link to her online shop. The wedding tab was buried in the menu. Her bounce rate hit 78%. She had four wedding form submissions in four months from $1,800 in ad spend.

**The fix:** Create a dedicated landing page for each campaign. For weddings: a simple page with a lead capture form, 6–8 photos of past weddings, a short pricing summary ($1,500–$5,000 for full wedding flowers), and a testimonial from a couple. For Valentine’s Day: a page showing current bouquets with prices, same-day delivery cut-off time, and a checkout button. No distractions.

**Outcome:** The Portland florist built two landing pages using Unbounce (about $80/month). Her wedding campaign conversion rate went from 1.1% to 5.6%. She spent $400 less per month on the same number of clicks and got 8 leads per month instead of 1. One lead turned into a $4,200 wedding package. She paid for the landing page tool for years with that single order.

**Mistake #4: Ignoring mobile experience and call extension**

A Chicago florist was losing mobile traffic because her site wasn’t optimized for phones. Her Google Ads were set to “all devices,” and 68% of impressions came from mobile. But the checkout process required desktop-level form filling, and the phone number was in the footer. People tapped the ad, saw a tiny phone number, pinched to zoom, gave up, and called a competitor. She spent $2,300 in December and only $600 in tracked phone calls (call extension not set up, so she couldn’t attribute them).

**The fix:** Call extensions are free and should be on for every campaign. Set up call-only ads for high-intent searches like “flower delivery near me” or “same-day rose delivery Chicago.” Make sure the landing page has a sticky “call now” button at the top. Test mobile page load time—under 2 seconds. Google PageSpeed Insights is free.

**Outcome:** After adding call extensions and a call-only ad, her call volume tripled in three weeks. She could finally see that 40% of her ad spend was generating phone orders she hadn’t been counting. December revenue jumped to $4,100 from the same $2,300 budget. She also reduced mobile bounce rate from 72% to 44% by compressing images and enabling AMP on her bouquet pages.

## How to Budget for Valentine’s Day vs. Wedding Campaigns

Valentine’s Day and weddings are two different revenue streams with different cost structures. Treating them the same is how you end up spending $500 on clicks for people who want a $25 bouquet next Tuesday when your minimum order is $75.

**Valentine’s Day budgets:** You need front-loaded spend because customers search early—mid-January starts the “best Valentine’s Day flowers” queries, but the bulk of conversions happen February 10–14. In my experience running ads for a GroupM client in the floral space, the average cost-per-click (CPC) for “Valentine’s Day roses” doubles in the final three days. If you have a $2,000 monthly budget, don’t spend $500 evenly each week. Instead:

- Jan 20–31: $300 total (testing ad copy, collecting negative keywords)
- Feb 1–9: $700 (building retargeting audiences)
- Feb 10–12: $600 (high-intent, competitive)
- Feb 13–14: $400 (last-minute buyers, call-only ads)

Set a daily budget cap of $150 for Feb 10–14 and raise it to $200 if you see profitable conversion rates. Use ad scheduling to run ads only during local business hours for delivery—nobody needs a 2 AM click for roses.

**Wedding budgets:** This is a longer game. You’re not selling a five-day spike; you’re selling a high-ticket service with a lead cycle of 3–9 months. Budget $20–$50/day, consistent year-round, with a bump in January (engagement season) and late summer (fall wedding inquiries). My Chicago florist client runs $35/day, and she books 2–3 weddings a month from Google Ads alone. Average order: $2,800. Monthly ad spend: $1,050. Monthly wedding revenue from ads: $5,600–$8,400. That’s a 5–8x return.

Track cost per lead, not cost per sale. If a wedding lead costs $25 and you close 20% of leads, your cost per acquisition is $125. That’s a no-brainer against a $2,800 average order. But you need patience—don’t kill the campaign after two weeks of no conversions. Wedding customers research for months.

One more thing: Use a separate Google Ads conversion action for wedding forms vs. Valentine’s Day purchases. If you lump them together, you’ll think your overall account is profitable when the wedding campaign is dragging down Valentine’s Day performance. Split them in the “Conversions” section of Google Ads. Label one “Valentine’s Day - Purchase” and one “Wedding - Lead Form.” Then compare apples to apples.

## The Tools You Actually Need to Run Profitable Florist Ads

You don’t need a $500/month tool stack. I’ve managed accounts with nothing but Google Ads, Google Analytics 4, and a free call tracking number. But if you want to stop guessing and start seeing real numbers, here’s the cheap stack I recommend to small business owners.

**1. A call tracking service (CallRail or WhatConverts)**  
Cost: $45–$65/month for 50 tracked numbers. This is the single best investment you can make. Without it, you have no idea which ad keyword drove that phone order. Florists get 30–50% of sales over the phone—especially for weddings and custom arrangements. Google’s call extensions only record clicks, not call duration or outcome. A call tracking tool logs the number, the keyword, and even records the call so you can hear if the customer said “I found you on Google.” The Nashville florist I mentioned earlier added CallRail and discovered that 40% of her revenue came from calls she previously attributed to “word of mouth.”

**2. A landing page builder (Carrd or Unbounce)**  
Carrd is $19/year for a single page. Unbounce starts at $99/month if you want A/B testing. For a florist, you need at least one dedicated page for weddings and one for Valentine’s Day. Don’t use your homepage—Google Ads rewards relevance. A custom landing page with a clear call-to-action (schedule consultation, order now) will outperform a homepage by 2–3x. The Portland florist used Unbounce, but even a simple Carrd page with a Google form embed works. The tool doesn’t matter. The focus matters.

**3. Google Merchant Center (if you sell online)**  
If you ship bouquets or have a local delivery radius, set up a free Merchant Center account and run Shopping ads. They show product images, price, and star ratings directly in search results. A florist in Denver added Shopping ads for Valentine’s roses and saw a 12% higher click-through rate than text ads, with a 20% lower cost per conversion. The setup takes two hours—create a product feed spreadsheet, upload via Google Sheets, and submit. No need for a paid feed management tool.

**4. A retargeting pixel (Google Ads remarketing tag plus Facebook Pixel)**  
This is free. Put the code on your site. After someone visits your Valentine’s Day page but doesn’t buy, show them a display ad with a 10% off code for the next 14 days. I’ve seen retargeting recover 5–8% of lost sales for florists. The key: set a frequency cap of 3 ads per day. Nobody wants to see your roses 12 times in one day.

**5. Square or a POS that feeds data back to Google**  
If you use Square to process in-store orders, connect it to Google Ads via Square’s integration or a tool like Zapier. You can upload offline conversion data—someone saw your ad, called, then came in and paid with Square—and Google will use that to optimize bids. That’s advanced, but it’s free once you have Square. The Austin florist did this and saw her cost per acquisition drop by 18% in two months because Google learned which keywords actually led to a purchase, not just a click.

Avoid overcomplicating. I once worked with a coffee shop that had nine marketing tools running simultaneously—HubSpot, Mailchimp, Yext, Moz, SEMrush, two Facebook scheduling tools, three analytics platforms—and they couldn’t tell me their cost per customer. Three tools plus Google Ads is plenty for a florist.

## Conversion Tracking: The Step Most Florists Skip

I’ll be direct: if you don’t have conversion tracking set up properly, you’re flying blind and hoping for clouds to clear. I’ve audited fifteen florist accounts over the past two years. Eleven had no conversion tracking at all. Three had the Google Ads tag but never set up a “purchase” or “lead” conversion action. One had tracking but didn’t count phone calls. Every single one was overpaying for clicks they thought were working.

**What you need to track for a florist Google Ads account:**

- **Online purchases (e-commerce):** If you sell bouquets through WooCommerce or Shopify, install the Google Ads global site tag and the event snippet. Test it with Google Tag Assistant (free Chrome extension). If the “purchase” event fires after checkout, you’re good. If not, check that your thank-you page has the correct code.

- **Form submissions (wedding inquiries):** Set up a “lead” conversion on your wedding landing page form. Most site builders (Wix, Squarespace, WordPress) have a Google Ads integration or let you paste a Google tag. Make the conversion value zero unless you know the exact average order from forms. Better to track lead count and calculate value later.

- **Phone calls from ads:** Two ways. Google’s free call extensions will track call clicks (the number of times someone taps the phone icon), but it won’t tell you if the call lasted longer than 30 seconds or if it resulted in an order. Better: use a call tracking service (as mentioned above) and import those as offline conversions into Google Ads. It’s a bit technical, but your call tracking provider will have a guide. If you can’t do that, at least set up Google’s “Calls from Ads” conversion action with a minimum call duration of 30 seconds. That’s better than nothing.

- **In-store visits (Google’s store visits):** This only works if you have location extensions enabled and enough ad spend to trigger the machine learning. For most small florists, it won’t show accurate data. Don’t rely on it.

**The mistake I see most often:** Florists set up conversion tracking, see 50 “conversions” in a week, and celebrate. But those are often page-view conversions (people who landed on the shop page) or scroll-depth events. They aren’t actual sales. I had a client in Chicago who thought she was getting 200 conversions a month. Three months later she realized it was tracking every time someone scrolled past the hero image. Real sales? 34. Always verify that your conversion fires only on the specific action that means money—completed purchase or submitted lead form.

**Monthly check:** Open Google Ads >> Conversions >> choose your action >> click “Diagnostics.” It will tell you if the tag is firing correctly. Do this on the 1st of every month. Set a reminder. If your tag stops firing, you’ll waste money for weeks before noticing.

**One pro tip:** Enable “value-based bidding” once you have at least 30 conversions in 30 days. This tells Google to prioritize clicks that lead to higher-value orders (a $200 wedding consultation form vs. a $40 bouquet). I ran this for a florist in Seattle, and her return on ad spend increased 34% in six weeks because Google started bidding higher for searches that historically led to wedding orders.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads for the first month?**

Start with $300–$500 for a test month. Run two campaigns: one for local delivery orders (Valentine’s Day or everyday) at $15/day, and one for wedding leads at $10/day. Don’t go higher until you see at least 30 clicks per campaign and know your conversion rate. I’ve seen florists spend $2,000 in month one with no tracking—they might as well have burned the cash.

**Q: Will Google Ads work for a small florist in a small town like Boise?**

Yes, but differently. In small cities, competition is lower, so your cost-per-click might be $0.60 versus $2.00 in NYC. But search volume is lower—you won’t get 500 clicks a day. Adjust your budget to $10–$20/day and focus on “near me” keywords and location extensions. A florist in Boise with $400/month in ad spend can dominate her local market. The key is making sure your Google Business Profile is fully filled out (hours, photos, services) because Google will show that alongside your ads.

**Q: Should I advertise on Yelp instead of Google Ads?**

Yelp can work for florists if you have >20 reviews with 4+ stars, because Yelp highlights your star rating in search results. But Yelp’s ad platform is less transparent than Google’s. I’ve seen clients spend $300 on Yelp ads and get zero calls that they could attribute—Yelp’s analytics are murky. My recommendation: use Google Ads for active searches (people typing “flower delivery”), and use Yelp as a free profile listing. Only pay Yelp if you have the budget and can track calls with a separate phone number.

**Q: Do I need to hire an agency or can I manage this myself?**

You can manage it yourself if you have five hours a week, patience, and comfort with spreadsheets. The biggest risk isn’t complexity—it’s giving up after two weeks because you see no sales. Florists have seasonal cycles; Valentine’s Day ads may take three weeks to show returns. If you want to DIY, read Google’s “Google Ads for Small Business” guide (free, written by people who aren’t trying to sell you training). If that sounds like a chore, pay a freelancer $500–$1,000/month to manage it, but make sure you own the account and can see all data yourself.

**Q: What if someone searches for a competitor’s name?**

You can bid on competitor keywords like “Ftd flowers” or “ProFlowers.” It’s legal. The ad copy must be clear you are not that brand—say “Local alternative to FTD” or “Austin flower shop, not ProFlowers.” Be careful: competitor keywords often have high CPCs and low conversion rates because the searcher already made up their mind. Test with $5/day for one week. If you get two orders, keep it. Otherwise, drop it and use that budget on your own brand terms.

**Q: How do I compete against big floral networks (FTD, 1-800-Flowers) on Google Ads?**

You don’t beat them on price—they have bigger budgets and lower flower costs. You beat them on relevance and speed. Use keywords with local intent: “same-day delivery in [city],” “local florist near me,” “hand-delivered bouquets [city].” Mention “hand-arranged,” “local,” “family-owned” in your ads. If a customer in Nashville searches “Nashville florist,” the FTD ad might say “Order flowers online,” but your ad can say “Hand-tied bouquets, delivered in Nashville today.” That specificity wins clicks. Also optimize for mobile, because big floral networks’ mobile checkout is often slower. Every second of load time you save is an order you steal.

**Q: My Valentine’s Day campaign did great in February. Should I run the same ads in March?**

Only if you want to waste money. Valentine’s Day search volume drops 90% on February 15. Switch to wedding and everyday flower ads. March has St. Patrick’s Day, Mother’s Day prep (May), and spring weddings. Pause your Valentine’s Day campaign and repurpose the winning ad copy for a “fresh spring flowers” campaign. Don’t leave a Valentine’s-only campaign running—you’ll pay for clicks from people searching for last-minute apology flowers who will see “Valentine’s Day special” and click away.
