Create a series of automated emails to welcome new customers. Include:

**2. Loyalty Program: Reward Repeat Customers**

Develop a loyalty program to retain repeat customers. Offer points for repeat visits, referrals, or social media engagement.

Example: Pawsome Pets in Chicago rewards customers with 100 points for each visit and 20 points for each social media share. When they accumulate 500 points, they get a free bath.

## 6. Segmentation: Send the Right Message to the Right Pet Owner

Not every customer needs the same grooming service. A Great Dane owner has very different needs than a Persian cat owner. Segment your email list based on pet type, service history, or purchase behavior. For example, create a segment for “dogs over 50 lbs” and send them a targeted email about your large-breed bath package. Or send cat owners a reminder that you offer low-stress grooming for felines.

Example: Happy Tails Pet Spa in Austin segmented their list by pet age. They sent “Senior Pet Care” emails to owners of dogs over 8 years old, featuring arthritis-friendly grooming techniques and a 15% discount on nail trims. The campaign saw a 42% click-through rate — double their average. By brewing the right message for each customer, you’ll see stronger engagement and more bookings.

<Callout type="tip">Start with two segments: “new customers” and “repeat customers.” Then add more as your list grows. Most email platforms make segmentation easy.</Callout>

## 7. Referral Programs: Turn Happy Customers into Marketing Ambassadors

Word-of-mouth is the strongest lead generator for pet groomers. But you can supercharge it with a referral email campaign. Encourage current clients to refer friends by offering a reward — like a free nail trim or 20% off a full groom — for each new customer who books.

Example: Bubbly Paws in Denver launched a “Refer a Friend” email series. They sent a dedicated email to existing customers with a unique referral link. For every friend who redeemed a first-visit discount, both the existing customer and the new customer got $10 off their next groom. Within 90 days, referrals brought in 68 new clients — a 23% increase in their customer base. The cost per acquisition was just $7.50, a fraction of paid ads.

To implement, include a referral call-to-action in your welcome series, loyalty program emails, and monthly newsletters. Offer a simple, trackable link to make it easy for customers to share.

## 8. Seasonal Campaigns: Capitalize on Pet Holidays and Grooming Needs

Pet owners love celebrating their furry family members. Use seasonal events to create urgency and excitement. Send emails for National Dog Day (August 26), National Cat Day (October 29), or even “Spring Shedding Season” in March. Offer a limited-time package — like a “Summer Cut and Cool Down” with a free bandana — and use countdown timers in your emails to drive action.

Example: Grooming Works in Manchester, UK, ran a “Valentine’s Paw-Ty” campaign offering a pink-themed groom and a heart-shaped treat. They segmented their list by pet type and sent the offer 10 days before Valentine’s Day. The campaign generated 52 bookings in one week — a 40% increase over their typical February week. The email had a 31% open rate and 12% conversion rate.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched pet groomers throw money at email marketing and get nothing back. Then I watched them fix one thing and triple their revenue. The difference isn’t complicated. It’s usually one of these four mistakes.

### Mistake 1: Buying an Email List

A groomer in Austin — let’s call her business “Happy Paws” — spent $1,200 on a list of 8,000 email addresses she bought from a third-party broker. She imported them into Mailchimp and sent a blast offering 20% off first visits. 

Here’s what actually happened: 72% of those emails bounced. Of the remaining 28% that landed, only 4 people clicked. Zero booked. Mailchimp flagged her account for high spam complaints and suspended her sending privileges for 72 hours. She lost a week of legitimate campaigns and had to rebuild sender reputation from scratch.

The fix: Build your list yourself. Every single email should come from someone who chose to hear from you. Use your point-of-sale system — Square, Booksy, or Vagaro — to capture emails at checkout. Put a simple sign-up form on your website. Offer something specific: “Get 15% off your next groom when you join our email list.”

Happy Paws switched to an opt-in model in April. By October, she had 1,200 engaged subscribers. Her open rate sits at 38% now (it was 2% with the bought list). That small list generates $4,200 in recurring revenue per month from repeat bookings and add-on services she promotes via email.

### Mistake 2: Sending Only When You Need Something

A groomer in Portland, Oregon, sent emails once a month: always a promotion. “10% off this week,” “Buy one get one free nail trim,” “Last chance for summer haircuts.” She wondered why her unsubscribe rate kept climbing and her open rate dropped to 12%.

I looked at her data. She had never sent a single email that didn’t ask for something. Not one.

The fix: Send value before you ask for money. Share a video on how to brush a golden retriever’s coat between visits. Write a short tip about what to do if your dog hates the blow dryer. Send a seasonal reminder about flea and tick prevention (with a link to book a medicated bath, sure, but lead with the help).

She changed her approach: three non-promotional emails for every promotional one. Open rate climbed to 26% over six weeks. Repeat bookings increased 22% in the next quarter. That shift didn’t cost her a dollar — just time. About $2,800 in extra monthly revenue.

### Mistake 3: Forgetting Mobile Design

A groomer in Denver sent beautifully designed emails with multi-column layouts, small fonts, and images that looked great on desktop. Problem: 67% of her subscribers opened on a phone. The text was unreadable. The buttons were too small to tap. The booking link was buried at the bottom of a five-paragraph email.

She told me she was getting 30–40 bookings a month from email. That sounded low for a list of 4,500.

We redesigned one email template in Mailchimp: single column, large text, one clear button at the top that said “Book Your Groom” and another near the bottom. That was it. No sidebars. No small print.

First month after the redesign: 92 bookings from email. That’s $1,900 in additional service revenue from one design change. She spent an hour making it.

### Mistake 4: No Segmentation

A groomer in Nashville sent every email to every subscriber. New clients, long-time regulars, people who hadn’t visited in two years — all got the same message. Her engagement metrics were flat.

The fix: Split your list into basic groups. New clients (visited within last 30 days) get a thank-you and a reminder to book their next appointment. Regulars (visited 3+ times) get loyalty rewards. Lapsed clients (no visit in 6+ months) get a win-back offer: “We miss Max — here’s $15 off his next groom.”

This groomer set up three simple segments in Mailchimp. It took 20 minutes. Her email revenue jumped from $1,200/month to $2,400/month within two months. Same list. Same frequency. Just better targeting.

## How to Integrate Email with Google Ads for Faster Growth

Email works best when it’s not the only channel. I tell pet groomers to treat Google Ads as the front door and email as the hallway that leads to repeat business. Used together, they outperform either channel alone.

Here’s a setup that’s working for a groomer in Charlotte, North Carolina. She runs Google Local Service Ads for “pet grooming [city name].” When someone clicks through and books, that client goes into her email list automatically via a Zapier connection between Google Ads and Mailchimp. No manual entry. No delay.

Within 24 hours of the first groom, the client gets an automated welcome sequence. Not a generic “thanks for your business” — specific to their first visit. “Bandit looked great after his blueberry facial. Here’s a tip on keeping his coat shiny between visits.” That client is now in a “new client” segment. Two weeks later, they get a reminder to rebook. Three weeks after that, a special offer for their second groom.

Cost breakdown: She spends $500/month on Google Local Service Ads. That generates about 25 new clients per month. Her email automation costs $40/month for Mailchimp. Of those 25 new clients, 18 rebook within 60 days because the email sequence keeps her top of mind. Before the email follow-up, only 8 of those 25 would rebook. That’s 10 additional rebookings per month at an average of $65 per groom. That’s $650 in monthly revenue directly attributable to email — against $540 in total channel costs. She’s profitable on the ads-plus-email combination, not just the ads alone.

You can do this with Booksy integration too — many grooming software tools connect directly to email platforms. If your POS system exports customer data, you can automate this. I’d rather see a groomer spend $500 on Google Ads with an email follow-up than $1,000 on ads alone. The email does the retention work for cheap.

## Use Yelp Reviews to Fuel Your Email Content

Yelp reviews are free content for your email list. Most groomers ignore this. Stop.

A groomer in San Diego with 187 Yelp reviews uses them as email material. She picks the most specific positive review each week and highlights it in her newsletter. “Last week, Sarah brought in her Bernedoodle, Bailey. Here’s what she said about our deshedding treatment.” Then she adds a short tip related to whatever the review mentioned — in this case, how often to deshed a double-coated breed.

Why this works: Social proof is the most persuasive thing you can put in an email. A customer saying something is better than you saying it. Plus, featuring clients makes them feel seen. That groomer in San Diego saw her Yelp review count increase 40% in six months because people wanted to be featured in the email. Every new review cycles back into the content loop.

She also sends a monthly “Client of the Month” email with a photo (with permission) and a short Q&A about the pet. That email consistently gets the highest click-through rate — 14% — and generates booking inquiries. Cost: zero. Takes 15 minutes to write.

If you have a Yelp Business Page, pull your reviews once a week and repurpose them. If you don’t have many reviews yet, email your best clients directly and ask. “We’d love to feature your pup in our next newsletter. Would you be open to leaving a short review on Yelp?” Most will say yes. Then you close the loop: featured client, new review, email content, repeat.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I have 200 email subscribers. Is that too small to bother with?**

No. 200 engaged subscribers who open your emails are worth more than 10,000 bought ones. A groomer in Boise started with 147 subscribers. She sent a monthly grooming tip email with a booking link. Within three months, 22 of those subscribers rebooked directly from email. At $60 average per groom, that’s $1,320 in revenue from 147 people. Grow the list and scale the result.

**Q: How often should I send emails to my pet grooming clients?**

Twice a month minimum. Once a week is better if you have useful content. Beyond that and you risk annoying people. A groomer in Tucson sends every Tuesday morning: one week a grooming tip, the next week a promotion. Her unsubscribe rate is below 0.2%. Consistency matters more than frequency.

**Q: What if I’m not a writer and I don’t have time to write emails?**

You don’t need to write essays. Send a photo of a dog before and after a groom with two sentences about what you did. Write bullet points of seasonal tips. Record a 60-second voice memo and embed it. The groomer in Tucson spends 10 minutes on each email. She uses Mailchimp’s templates and copies from her own notes. Good enough is better than not sending.

**Q: Can’t I just use Instagram instead of email?**

You can, but you shouldn’t rely only on Instagram. You don’t own that audience. If the algorithm changes or your account gets suspended, you lose everyone. Email lists are assets you control. Plus, email drives 3x more bookings per dollar spent than social media for service businesses in my experience. Use both. Prioritize email.

**Q: How do I get more people to sign up for my email list?**

Put a sign-up form everywhere: on your booking confirmation page, at checkout on Square, on your website footer, on a tablet in your waiting room. Offer a specific incentive — 15% off next groom or a free nail trim on their next visit. A groomer in Richmond added a simple “Join our list for 10% off” card to every receipt. She gained 80 subscribers in the first month.

**Q: What’s the best email tool for a pet grooming business?**

Mailchimp is fine for most small groomers. Free up to 500 contacts. Automations are straightforward. If you use Booksy or Square for booking, check if they have built-in email features — many do. Booksy has email marketing built into its platform for about $30/month. That’s cheaper than separate tools and integrates directly with your client data.
