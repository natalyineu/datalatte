**Why HTTPS Matters for Small Businesses**

HTTPS (Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure) is the secure version of HTTP. It encrypts data between your website and your customers' browsers, ensuring their sensitive information remains protected. This is especially crucial for small businesses handling sensitive data, such as:

* Online payments and transactions
* User credentials and login information
* Personal data and contact information

**The Benefits of HTTPS**

HTTPS offers numerous benefits for small businesses, including:

* Improved search engine rankings and visibility
* Increased customer trust and loyalty
* Enhanced security and protection against cyber threats
* Better protection against phishing and man-in-the-middle attacks

**Getting HTTPS for Free**

You don't need to break the bank to get HTTPS for your small business website. Here are a few options:

* **Let's Encrypt**: A free, automated SSL certificate provider that issues certificates in minutes.
* **Cloudflare**: A content delivery network (CDN) that offers free SSL certificates and other security features.
* **Google's Site Kit**: A free plugin that offers SSL certificates and other security features for WordPress websites.

**Setting Up HTTPS**

Setting up HTTPS is a straightforward process. Here are the general steps:

1. **Obtain an SSL certificate**: Choose a provider and follow their instructions to obtain an SSL certificate.
2. **Install the certificate**: Install the certificate on your website's server.
3. **Update your website**: Update your website's configuration to use the new HTTPS protocol.
4. **Test your website**: Test your website to ensure it's working correctly over HTTPS.

<Callout type="tip">Tip: Make sure to update all internal links to use HTTPS to avoid any issues with search engine rankings.</Callout>

**Common HTTPS Mistakes to Avoid**

While setting up HTTPS is relatively straightforward, there are some common mistakes to avoid:

* **Not updating internal links**: Failing to update internal links can lead to search engine ranking drops and other issues.
* **Not testing your website**: Failing to test your website after setting up HTTPS can lead to issues with security and functionality.
* **Not using a trusted SSL certificate**: Using a non-trusted SSL certificate can lead to security issues and decreased customer trust.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Installing HTTPS but Keeping Mixed Content

I had a client in Austin, Texas — a boutique fitness studio called Core & Sole. They ran a membership booking page through Square. The owner, Megan, was proud she'd finally gotten an SSL certificate. Her browser showed the little padlock icon. She thought she was done.

She wasn't.

Every single image on her site was still loading over HTTP. So were the embedded Google Fonts and a Mailchimp sign-up form. Browsers don't call this "fine." They call it "mixed content." They show a gray padlock with a yellow triangle or outright say "Not Secure" in the address bar.

Megan's bounce rate from Google Ads (she was spending $1,200/month) was 68%. Her conversion rate was 1.1%.

When I ran her site through a free mixed-content checker (whythehost.com has a decent one), we found 23 insecure elements. I spent 45 minutes updating internal links and forcing HTTPS on her WordPress site using a plugin called Really Simple SSL (free, works fine). Then I updated the image URLs in her Mailchimp templates because those were still pulling from the old HTTP source.

Outcome: Bounce rate dropped to 42% over the next three weeks. Her Google Ads conversion rate hit 2.8%. That $1,200/month ad spend started producing 2.5x the leads. She was paying $43 per lead before the fix. After? $18.

The fix took less than an hour. The cost? Zero dollars.

### Mistake #2: Letting the SSL Certificate Expire (and Not Noticing for Weeks)

A pet grooming salon in Nashville — Paws & Claws by Claire — had HTTPS set up correctly in 2022. Then the certificate expired in February 2024. Claire didn't notice for three weeks.

Here's what happened: Customers who typed her URL directly saw a full-screen "Your connection is not private" error. Most of them left. Her Yelp reviews started mentioning "I tried to book online but the site gave a scary warning." She lost an estimated 18 bookings in that window. At $75 per groom, that's $1,350 in direct lost revenue. Plus the customers who never came back.

She also ran a Google Business Profile campaign — a local service ad that cost about $400/month. Google had been sending traffic to her HTTPS version. Once the certificate expired, those clicks hit the error page. The ad platform reported "no issues" because the URL didn't change. The problem was invisible unless you actually clicked the link.

Claire's fix: I set up auto-renewal on her SSL through her hosting provider (SiteGround, though most hosts offer this). I also added expiration monitoring via an UptimeRobot free plan — it sends a Slack alert 30 days before expiry. Total setup time: 12 minutes.

The uncomfortable truth: if you manage your own SSL, you will forget to renew it. Everyone does. Automate it or pay someone $50/year to handle it.

### Mistake #3: Only Securing the Homepage

A coffee roaster in Portland, Oregon — Red Bird Roasters — had HTTPS on their homepage and product pages. But their checkout page for wholesale orders ran on a subdomain (shop.redbirdroasters.com) that was still HTTP.

The owner, Tom, was spending $3,800/month on Google Ads driving traffic to wholesale product pages. His cart abandonment rate was 79%. He thought it was the prices.

When I tested the checkout flow, Chrome showed a "Not Secure" badge right above the credit card field. No one is typing their card number into that page. No one.

The fix: I generated a free wildcard SSL certificate through Let's Encrypt (covered the main domain + all subdomains) and installed it on the subdomain server. Total time: 30 minutes. Cost: zero.

Within two weeks, the cart abandonment rate went to 61%. That 18-point drop translated to roughly $680/month in recovered wholesale orders. Tom had been blaming his pricing strategy. The problem was a missing $0 certificate.

### Mistake #4: Assuming HTTPS Handles Everything

A hair salon in Chicago — Studio Cuts — had HTTPS. They were proud of it. They still got hit with a phishing attack.

Here's what happened: The salon had a contact form that emailed submissions to their booking manager. The form itself was secure. But the email it sent wasn't encrypted in transit unless the salon's email provider used TLS (transport layer security). Their provider (an old GoDaddy email plan) did not.

A fake booking request came through with a malicious link. The manager clicked it. That's not an HTTPS problem — that's a "your security awareness is the weak link" problem.

HTTPS protects data between the customer's browser and your server. It does not protect what happens after the data reaches your inbox. It does not protect you from phishing. It does not protect you from an employee using "Password123" on the admin panel.

The fix I suggested: Enable SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records on the salon's domain (most hosts have guides for this). Move email to a provider that uses TLS by default — Google Workspace ($6/month per user) or FastMail. And run a 30-minute phishing awareness session with the team. Not a training module from HR. A real conversation.

Most small business owners skip this part. Don't.

## The Real Cost of HTTPS for Booking, Payments, and Email Links

If you use any third-party booking or payment tools, you need to check something specific.

**Square Online:** Square handles its own SSL on the checkout page. But if you have a custom domain pointing to your Square store, you need to enable SSL on that domain through Square's settings. It's free. Most people don't know it's there.

**Mailchimp:** If you send email campaigns with links back to your site, those links need to point to the HTTPS version. Mailchimp does not automatically update your link URLs. If your old emails pointed to HTTP, people are still clicking through to an unsecured page. I found 14 old campaign links for a Nashville pet groomer that were still pointing to HTTP. Each one was a potential trust failure. We updated the domain-level redirect in Mailchimp's template settings and set up a 301 redirect from HTTP to HTTPS on the server. Problem solved in 20 minutes.

**Booksy (for salons and barbers):** Booksy generates booking links that live on their subdomain (booksy.com). That subdomain is already HTTPS. But if you embed a Booksy booking button on your site, that button is fine from a security perspective. The issue is whether the page hosting that button is HTTPS. If your site page is HTTP, the browser still warns the user. I've seen this at three different barbershops in Austin.

**Yelp:** Yelp does not require your website to be HTTPS to list your business. But if someone clicks through from Yelp to your HTTP site, they see the warning. They blame Yelp. Then they blame you. Then they go to a competitor. Yelp's own data shows that click-through rates drop significantly when the destination site shows a security warning. I don't have the exact Yelp internal number, but I've seen a 12% drop in Yelp-originated bookings for a client who let their HTTPS lapse.

## Why Your Yelp and Google Business Profile Might Already Be Penalizing You (Even If You Have HTTPS)

This is the one that surprises people.

You can have HTTPS installed correctly on your main website. But if your Yelp business page or Google Business Profile links to an HTTP version of your site (because you typed the wrong URL when you set up the profile), you are losing traffic.

I checked a client in Nashville — a hair salon — and their Google Business Profile listed "http://salonname.com" instead of "https://salonname.com". Every time someone clicked that link from Google Maps, they hit an HTTP page. Google Business Profile doesn't check for this. It just takes whatever URL you gave it.

The fix: Log into your Google Business Profile, click "Info," and update the website URL to the HTTPS version. Do the same on Yelp. Do the same on Facebook, Instagram, and any other directory. Most small business owners set this up once and never touch it again. That's how old HTTP URLs persist for years.

I changed that Nashville salon's URL. They said the phone started ringing more within a week. I'm not claiming it was the only factor, but removing a roadblock never hurts.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: "I'm a coffee shop. I don't handle credit cards on my site. Do I really need HTTPS?"**

Short answer: yes. Even if you don't take payments, you still collect data. Contact form submissions. Email signups. Newsletter subscriptions. That's all personal data. Plus, Google penalizes HTTP sites in search results — that means fewer people find you when they search "coffee shop near me." The "Not Secure" warning in the browser makes people leave before they even read your menu. You need HTTPS for trust, not just payments.

**Q: "My web developer said I need to pay $100/year for an SSL certificate. Is that real?"**

No, unless you need a specific Extended Validation (EV) certificate (you don't). Let's Encrypt is free and works fine for 99% of small businesses. Your hosting provider might have a free option built in. If they're charging you $100, they're either on an old reseller plan or they're selling you something you don't need. Ask them to use Let's Encrypt or switch to a host that offers it free.

**Q: "I moved to HTTPS and my site broke — the images don't load and the layout is weird. What did I do wrong?"**

You've got mixed content. Your page is loading over HTTPS, but it's trying to load images, scripts, or fonts over HTTP. Browsers block these for security reasons. Use a free tool like Why No Padlock (whynopadlock.com) to find the broken elements. Fix the URLs in your site's database — a plugin like Better Search Replace can do this in minutes if you're on WordPress. If you didn't backup before the move, you should have. But it's fixable.

**Q: "Does HTTPS slow down my site?"**

Negligibly. The initial SSL handshake adds maybe 50-200 milliseconds on the first visit. That's less than the blink of an eye. Modern protocols (TLS 1.3, HTTP/2) actually make HTTPS faster than HTTP for repeat visitors because they allow multiplexing — loading multiple files simultaneously. The security benefit far outweighs any tiny speed difference. Anyone telling you HTTPS slows down your site is repeating outdated advice from 2015.

**Q: "I have a simple one-page site that just has my address and phone number. No forms, no orders. Still need HTTPS?"**

Technically, you don't need encryption for static content. But Google still ranks HTTPS over HTTP. If someone searches your business name, the competitor with HTTPS will rank higher just for having that signal. Plus, 75% of consumers avoid "Not Secure" sites — they'll think you're out of business or sketchy. A one-pager is the easiest site to move to HTTPS. There's zero reason not to. You can do it in 10 minutes.

**Q: "I use Wix/Squarespace/Shopify — isn't HTTPS already handled?"**

Yes, those platforms include free SSL on all custom domains. But you need to make sure it's turned on. In Wix, go to Settings > Domains and verify SSL is enabled. In Squarespace, it's automatic. In Shopify, it's automatic. The mistake people make is using a third-party domain (like yourname.com) that they bought elsewhere and didn't connect properly — the SSL might work on your Shopify store but not on the main domain redirect. Test by typing "https://yourname.com" into a browser. If you get a warning, fix the domain connection.
