## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a one-person shop. Do I really need a CRM?**

Honestly, not at first. If you have fewer than 100 active customers and you can remember what they like, a notebook or a Google Sheet works fine. The moment you hit 100 customers, you start forgetting things. That's when you lose business. A CRM isn't about tracking data for fun — it's about not accidentally ignoring the person who spent $600 last year. Start with the free tier of Square or Mailchimp. If you outgrow it in a year, great. That's a profitable problem.

**Q: Won't AI-powered CRM be too complicated for me?**

The AI features in small business CRMs are mostly invisible. You don't need to train a model or write code. The CRM shows you a list of "at-risk" customers or suggests what time to send emails. That's it. If a tool requires you to set up complex automation rules from day one, it's not designed for a small business owner. Pick one that runs on autopilot after a 15-minute setup.

**Q: How much should I expect to spend per month?**

Between free and $50/month, depending on how many customers you have. At 200 customers, Square's free tier might work. At 500, you'll probably need the $29/month plan. At 1,000, bump it to $59. Do not spend $200/month on a CRM until you have multiple employees and at least 2,000 active customers. I've seen too many owners sign up for HubSpot's $450/month plan because they thought "Enterprise" meant more sales. It means more headaches.

**Q: What if my customers don't want to give me their email or phone number?**

Then you can't use a CRM effectively, and you're losing money. Here's what I tell clients: make the ask part of the transaction. "I need your email to send you the receipt" works for 90% of people. For the remaining 10%, offer a loyalty card or a discount on their next visit in exchange for an email. The ones who still refuse? Accept it. Some people value privacy over convenience. That's fine. But don't design your system around the 10% who opt out.

**Q: Does AI-powered CRM actually help with Google Ads or Yelp?**

Indirectly, yes. If you link your CRM to Google Ads, it can track which ad customers came from and whether they returned. That lets you turn off ads that bring in one-time visitors and double down on ads that bring in loyal customers. I had a client in New York who was running 12 Google ad campaigns. After connecting their CRM and looking at repeat purchase data, only 2 campaigns were profitable. They cut 10 campaigns, saved $2,800 per month, and their revenue stayed flat. That's the kind of thing a CRM with good reporting can tell you. Without it, you're guessing.

**Q: How long will it take to see results?**

If you already have decent customer data? Two to three months. If you're starting from scratch and entering data manually? Four to six months. Most people quit in month two because they don't see immediate ROI. That's normal. A CRM is a compound interest investment — slow at first, then it accelerates. The business owners who stick with it are the ones who see 20-30% revenue growth from existing customers by month six. The ones who quit go back to notebooks and wondering why their marketing doesn't work.
