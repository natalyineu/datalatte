## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting Broad Keywords and Wasting Budget on Looky-Loos

A pediatric dentist in Austin, Texas came to me after three months of running his own Google Ads. He was spending $2,400 per month and had booked exactly four new patients. His cost per acquisition was $600. His average patient value was about $200.

The problem? He was bidding on keywords like "kids dentist," "children's dental," and "pediatric dentistry near me."

Sounds right, doesn't it? Here's what happened: His ads were showing up for parents who were just doing research. People looking up what a pediatric dentist even does. Moms comparing options with no intention of booking. Parents whose kids had specific conditions that this dentist didn't even treat.

**The fix:** We built a negative keyword list that was longer than the positive one. We excluded terms like "vs" (as in "pediatric dentist vs general dentist"), "what does," "salary," "cost of," "education," and every single dental school query we could find. Then we added modifier keywords that signal intent: "book appointment," "new patient special," "first visit," "emergency appointment," and the names of nearby neighborhoods.

**The outcome:** Monthly spend dropped to $1,200. New patient appointments went from 4 to 18 per month. Cost per acquisition fell from $600 to $67. The dentist called me after two weeks and said his front desk was annoyed because the phone wouldn't stop ringing. I consider that a win.

### Mistake 2: Sending All Clicks to the Homepage

A pediatric dentist in Nashville had a beautiful website. Gorgeous photos, lots of information about fluoride treatments, a blog post about thumb-sucking. He was paying $3,500 per month on Google Ads and getting clicks. His conversion rate was 2.1%.

I looked at his ad-to-landing-page flow. The ad said: "New Patient Special – Exam, X-Ray & Cleaning for $99. Book Now." The click went to his homepage. The homepage had a navigation bar with seven options, a rotating hero image, a smiling child, and a button that said "Learn More."

That button did not lead to the booking page. It led to a page about his team.

**The fix:** We built a dedicated landing page. No navigation bar. No links to blog posts. No "Meet the Team" section. Just the offer, a photo of the kid-friendly waiting room, a bullet list of what's included in the $99 special, and one button that said "Book My Child's Visit." Below that, a phone number. That's it. The entire page was 350 words. I timed a client to see how long it took them to read it. Thirty seconds.

**The outcome:** Conversion rate went from 2.1% to 11.4%. His monthly spend stayed the same at $3,500, but his new patient flow went from 9 to 38 per month. The $99 special cost him about $20 out of pocket per patient because most scheduled follow-up treatments. His ROI on that campaign hit 8x inside sixty days.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Phone Call Tracking

A pediatric dentist in Denver was proud of his Google Ads setup. He had conversion tracking installed. He knew exactly how many people filled out his contact form. He told me he was getting about 15 leads per month from a $1,800 ad spend.

I asked him how many calls he was getting. He didn't know. He thought maybe a few.

We added a call tracking number from a tool (I use CallRail because it's cheap and works, but there are other options). What we found: He was getting 47 calls per month that he was not counting. His form submissions were only 24% of his total leads. The calls were converting at a higher rate because parents who call are ready to book, not just browsing.

**The fix:** We made sure calls were counted as conversions in Google Ads. We also set up call extensions and call-only ads for mobile. We wrote ad copy specifically for the caller: "Need to see a dentist today? Call us now. Same-day appointments available for new patients."

**The outcome:** His reported lead volume went from 15 to 62 per month. Google's algorithm started optimizing for calls instead of just clicks, which changed the kind of traffic he was getting. His cost per lead dropped from $120 to $29. He told me at month two that his receptionist asked for a raise because she was busier handling the phone. He gave it to her.

### Mistake 4: Setting a Budget and Walking Away

A pediatric dentist in Portland, Oregon had been running ads for six months. At first, they worked. Then performance plateaued. Then it dropped. She kept paying the same $2,500 per month and assumed Google would figure it out.

Google does not figure it out. Google will happily spend your entire budget on keywords that used to work last month but stopped working yesterday.

**The fix:** We checked her search terms report. She was showing up for "free dental clinic for kids" and "Medicaid dentist near me." Neither applied to her practice. We also found that her best-performing ad was a responsive search ad that Google had stopped favoring. We paused it, built three new ads, and set up a weekly schedule to review the search terms report and add negative keywords.

**The outcome:** Cost per acquisition dropped from $150 to $48. Her monthly spend actually went down to $2,000 because we stopped wasting money on irrelevant clicks. She booked more patients for less money. She said it felt like magic. It's not magic. It's maintenance.

## Writing Ads That Don't Sound Like Every Other Dentist Ad

I have read approximately 9,000 Google Ads headlines for pediatric dentists. They all say some version of: "Gentle Pediatric Dentist" or "Caring Dentist for Kids" or "Trusted Pediatric Dentistry."

These words are not working. They are too generic. Parents scrolling through search results see five ads that all say the same thing. They click the one that sounds most specific.

**Here is what actually works:**

Be specific about a problem. Parents searching for a pediatric dentist are often worried. Their child has a toothache, or they had a bad experience at a general dentist, or they're scared of the cost. Address the worry directly.

Examples of headlines that outperformed generic ones in real campaigns:

- "Child Scared of the Dentist? We Handle That Every Day. Call Now."
- "First Visit for $99 – Includes Exam, X-Ray & Cleaning. No Hidden Fees."
- "Same-Day Appointment Available for Kids with Tooth Pain. Call (555) 000-0000."
- "We Accept Delta Dental, Cigna, MetLife – No Surprise Billing."
- "Our Waiting Room Has iPads. Your Kid Will Want to Come Back."

I tested the "iPads" headline against a "gentle, caring, trusted" version for a client in Chicago. The iPad headline had a 73% higher click-through rate. Why? Because it's a specific, believable detail. Parents know their kids love iPads. It's not a promise of kindness they've heard a thousand times.

**Use sitelink extensions for specific offers.** Below your ad, Google lets you add links to specific pages on your website. Use this. Don't link to your homepage. Link to:

- "New Patient Offer – $99"
- "Meet Our Dentists"
- "Insurance Accepted"
- "Request Appointment"

I had a client in Phoenix add these four sitelinks. His click-through rate on the ad itself didn't change much, but the number of people who clicked through to the appointment page increased by 40%. The sitelinks gave hesitant parents a reason to click.

**Callout extensions matter too.** These are the short text snippets that appear below your ad. Use them to list things that differentiate you:

- "Board Certified Pediatric Dentist"
- "Open Saturdays"
- "Sedation Options Available"
- "Ages 0–17 Welcome"
- "Near [Neighborhood Name]"

The "Near [Neighborhood Name]" callout is one I use constantly. If your practice is in a specific area of a city, name it. Parents search for dentists near their home or school. Give them a reason to believe you're close.

## What Your Landing Page Needs (Your Ad Is Only Half the Battle)

I mentioned this in the mistakes section, but it deserves its own space because most dentists get this wrong and it costs them thousands.

**Your landing page is not your website.**

Your website serves everyone — existing patients, people looking for directions, job applicants. Your landing page serves one person: the parent who just clicked your ad and is trying to decide whether to call or bounce.

A good pediatric dentist landing page needs exactly five things. No more.

**1. A headline that matches the ad.** If your ad says "New Patient Special – $99," your landing page headline should say the exact same thing. Do not change the language. Do not get creative. I had a client in San Diego whose ad said "First Visit for $99" and whose landing page said "Welcome to Our Practice." That disconnect alone cost him 60% of his potential conversions. We changed the headline to match. Conversions tripled.

**2. A photo of your actual waiting room.** Not a stock photo of happy children. Not a stock photo of a smiling dentist. A real photo of your waiting room, taken during operating hours, with the toys and the fish tank and the kid-sized chairs visible. Parents can tell the difference. Stock photos tell them you don't have enough real photos to use. Real photos tell them, "This is a real place where real kids go."

**3. A clear, single action.** One button. "Book My Child's Appointment." Or "Call Now to Schedule." Do not also have a button that says "Learn More" or "View Services" or "Meet the Team." Every additional option is a distraction. Give them one thing to click.

**4. A phone number visible at all times.** Some people want to call. Especially parents who are stressed about their kid's dental issue. Make the phone number appear in the header, the footer, and in a sticky bar at the top of the page. I use a tool called CallRail to track which calls come from the landing page, and I always see a spike in calls when the number is prominently displayed.

**5. Social proof that feels real.** Not "trusted by hundreds of families." Real testimonials with the parent's first name and city. "Dr. Smith was amazing with my 4-year-old. He was scared going in and left smiling. — Jessica, Austin." A photo of the kid if the parent agreed to it. A Google review score displayed. I had a client in Denver show a 4.8-star Google rating on his landing page. Conversions went up by 22%. Parents trust other parents more than they trust dentists.

**Bonus: Use a scheduling tool that works.** I recommend Booksy for medical practices. It lets parents see available slots and book online without a phone call. Some people prefer to book at 10 PM after the kids are asleep. Give them that option.

A pediatric dentist in Seattle added online booking through Booksy to his landing page. His conversion rate went from 8% to 14% in two weeks. The appointments booked at night accounted for 30% of his new patient volume.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it take for Google Ads to actually start working for my practice?**

If you set it up correctly — targeted keywords, solid landing page, proper conversion tracking — you will see impressions and clicks within hours of launching. The first new patient call could come the same day. But I'm going to be honest with you: the first two weeks are usually ugly. Your cost per click will be high because Google doesn't know who to show your ads to yet. It's testing. If you don't touch anything for 14 days, the algorithm starts learning. By week three, your cost per click should drop. By week six, you should see consistent leads. Most dentists quit at week two because they see a $50 cost per click and panic. Don't panic. It gets better.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads myself, or do I need to hire someone?**

You can run them yourself. Google makes the interface approachable. I have seen dentists set up a campaign in thirty minutes and get results. But I have also seen dentists waste $1,500 in three days because they didn't add negative keywords and their ads showed up for "how to become a pediatric dentist" queries. The difference is time. If you have an hour per week to review search terms, adjust bids, test new ad copy, and check your landing page performance, you can do it yourself. If you're going to set it and forget it, hire someone. The money you spend on an agency or a freelancer will be less than the money you lose to bad clicks.

**Q: What budget should I start with?**

I start most pediatric dentists at $1,500 to $2,500 per month for a single location in a mid-sized city. That gives you enough data to make decisions without bleeding cash. If you're in a competitive market like Los Angeles or New York, you might need $3,000 to $5,000. If you're in a smaller town, $800 might be enough. The key is not the budget size. The key is that your budget should be enough to get at least 50 clicks per week. Below that, you don't have enough data for Google to optimize. I have a client in Austin who started at $1,000 per month and got 3 leads in the first month. We moved to $2,000 and got 15 leads. More budget doesn't always mean more leads, but below a certain threshold, you're paying for nothing.

**Q: Will Google Ads still work if I don't have a website?**

You need a landing page at minimum. It can be a single page hosted on a subdomain. You cannot run Google Ads without a place to send people. However, you can run call-only ads that show your phone number and skip the website entirely. These work well for pediatric dentists because many parents prefer to call. You still need something — a Google Business Profile with accurate hours, a phone number that gets answered during business hours, and ideally a voicemail that says "if this is a dental emergency, call (555) 000-0000." If you don't have any of that, fix that first. Ads will only amplify your problems.

**Q: How do I know if my ads are actually working? What numbers should I track?**

Stop looking at clicks. Clicks are vanity. Track three numbers: cost per lead (how much you spend to get a phone call or form submission), cost per new patient (how much you spend to get someone who actually shows up and pays), and conversion rate (what percentage of people who click your ad actually become a lead). For pediatric dentists, a good cost per lead is under $40. A good cost per new patient is under $100 if you structure your offer to cover the cost. A good conversion rate from click to lead is above 5%. If your numbers are worse than that, something is off. Either your keywords are too broad, your landing page is confusing, or your ad is lying about what you offer.

**Q: What about Yelp ads? Should I do those instead?**

Yelp ads can work for pediatric dentists, but the volume is lower. Google gets about 5.6 billion searches per day. Yelp gets about 178 million unique visitors per month. Google is where parents type "pediatric dentist near me" at 2 AM when their kid is crying. Yelp is where they go to read reviews after they've narrowed it down to two options. If you have a limited budget, spend it on Google first. Once you're getting consistent results there, Yelp ads can be a complement, not a replacement. I've seen dentists spend $500 per month on Yelp and get zero calls. I've also seen them spend $500 and get ten. It depends entirely on your existing Yelp reviews and your category ranking. It's a gamble I don't recommend as your first move.
