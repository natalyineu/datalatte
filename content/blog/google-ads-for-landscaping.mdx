## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting Too Broad — And Paying for Clicks You’ll Never Convert

**The story:** A landscaping company in Austin, TX called “GreenScape Pros” came to me after burning through $1,500 a month on Google Ads with nothing to show for it. The owner, Mike, was running a single campaign targeting “landscaping” on broad match across the entire state of Texas. His ad was showing to someone in El Paso looking for drought-tolerant plants — 600 miles from his service area in Austin. He was also showing up for “landscaping ideas” and “landscaping software.” Every click cost him $4–6, and none of those people wanted to hire a crew.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the campaign from scratch. First, I set a 15-mile radius around Austin and excluded everything outside it. Second, we switched from broad match to phrase match and exact match keywords like “lawn mowing service Austin,” “landscape design Austin TX,” and “patio installation Austin.” Third, I added a long list of negative keywords: “DIY,” “how to,” “ideas,” “software,” “supply,” “school,” and all city names in Texas except Austin. Finally, we set location bid adjustments — 0% for the outer ring, +20% for downtown and the high-end suburbs where the work actually was.

**The outcome:** Cost per click dropped from $5.20 to $2.80. More importantly, cost per lead went from $87 to $38. Mike started getting actual phone calls from people who said, “I saw your ad and I’m two blocks away.” Within three months, his monthly ad spend stayed the same ($1,500), but he went from 2–3 leads a month to 12–15. He closed an average of 5 new clients per month, worth roughly $3,800 in new revenue. He cut the wasted clicks by 60%.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Negative Keywords — A Shortcut to Wasted Budget

**The story:** A Nashville lawn care company called Music City Mowing was running a campaign for “lawn care service Nashville.” Sounds fine. Except they were paying for clicks from people searching for “lawn care service Nashville how to” and “lawn mower repair Nashville.” Their ad copy said “get a free quote,” but the searcher wanted to fix their own mower. The owner, Carla, was spending about $800 a month and getting maybe one call a week. She thought Google Ads just didn’t work for lawn care.

**The fix:** I ran a search term report and found 47 search terms that had nothing to do with hiring a service. “Lawn mower blade sharpening” — $4.30 per click, zero conversions. “How to fertilize lawn” — $3.90, zero. “DIY lawn care” — $5.10, zero. We added every one of those as negative keywords. We also added broad negative keyword themes: “how to,” “repair,” “DIY,” “do it yourself,” “homemade,” “reviews,” “cost calculator,” “price,” and “near me” (because people searching “lawn care near me” are often comparing multiple companies and won’t convert — we actually tested this and removed it later). We also excluded phrases that signal research intent like “best lawn care” or “lawn care companies” (we found those attracted tire-kickers).

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, Carla’s click-through rate went from 1.8% to 4.2%. Cost per click dropped from $4.50 to $2.90 because Google’s algorithm had less junk to sort through. Her conversion rate jumped from 2% to 7%. She went from spending $800/month with one call to spending $600/month with six calls (she reduced her budget because she didn’t need to waste money on irrelevant traffic). She added three new recurring clients that season, each paying $250/month for weekly mowing. That’s an extra $9,000 in annual revenue from the same ad spend.

### Mistake 3: No Call Tracking — Flying Blind on Phone Leads

**The story:** A landscaping company in Portland, OR — Pacific Northwest Landscapes — was spending $2,000 a month on Google Ads. The owner, Dave, told me he was getting “a lot of calls” but couldn’t tell if they were from his ads, his Yelp page, or just people walking by his crew. He was also running a billboard. He had no idea what was working. He asked me to look at his account, and the first thing I noticed: no call tracking installed. Google Ads was reporting 0 conversions because he never set up conversion tracking for phone calls. He was flying blind.

**The fix:** I set up Google Ads call extensions with a tracking number through CallRail. I also added a separate landing page with a form and a phone number that was unique to that campaign. I installed Google Ads conversion tracking for both calls over 60 seconds and form submissions. Then I linked his Google Ads account to his Google Business Profile so we could see which calls came from the ad vs. the map listing. Within a week, we had real data.

**The outcome:** Dave was shocked to see that 70% of his calls were actually coming from his Google Ads, not the billboard or Yelp. But the bigger shock was that only 1 in every 4 of those calls was a qualified lead — the rest were people asking for prices on small jobs he didn’t want ($50 tree trimming, asking about mulch delivery). Armed with the data, we adjusted his ad copy to filter out low-value inquiries. We added negative keywords for “mulch” and “small tree.” We also added a qualification question on his landing page form: “Describe the project size (under $500, $500–2,000, over $2,000).” He started getting fewer calls but higher-quality ones. His close rate went from 20% to 55%. He reduced his monthly spend to $1,500 and still booked 30% more revenue — an increase of about $4,200 per month in new contracts.

## How to Write Ads That Make Someone Pick Up the Phone (Instead of Scrolling Past)

You’ve got the keywords right, you’ve killed the waste, now you need ads that actually get clicked — and more importantly, get calls. Here’s the uncomfortable truth: most landscaping Google Ads are boring. They say “Professional Landscaping Services — Call Now.” That’s not an ad, it’s a signpost. Nobody signs up for a signpost.

I tested this with a client in Chicago — a company called Windy City Landscapes. They had three ads running with different headlines. The control ad: “Chicago Landscaping | Free Estimates | Call Today.” It had a 1.9% CTR. The first variant: “Your Yard, Done Right | 5-Star Rating | Book Your Free Quote.” CTR: 2.3%. Better, but not great. The winner was: “Tired of Overgrown Lawns? | We Cut & Haul — $59 First Mow | Call to Claim.” That ad had a 5.1% CTR. Why? Specificity. A price point. A pain point. A call to action that felt urgent and tangible.

You don’t have to give away your margin. But you do need to stand out. Here are three rules I’ve used across 20+ landscaping clients:

1. **Include a dollar amount or a concrete promise.** “Free Estimates” is table stakes. “Save $50 on Your First Service” is compelling. “Lawn Mowing Starting at $39” tells them exactly what to expect. In Austin, I ran an ad that said “Front Yard Makeover — $299” for a specific promotion. Cost per lead dropped to $18.

2. **Lead with a pain point, not your company name.** Your business name goes in the headline, but it shouldn’t be the first thing they see. Try “Overgrown Weeds? We’ll Fix It in 24 Hours” or “Spring Cleanup — We Haul Everything Away.” I ran a test in Denver: “Denver’s Best Landscaper” vs. “Mulch Delivered & Spread — Starting $199.” The second ad had 3x the conversion rate.

3. **Use call-only ads when you can.** For landscaping, most leads come by phone. Google’s call-only ads (mobile only, click to call) skip the website and go straight to your phone. I set this up for a company in Nashville — they went from a 2% call-in rate to a 12% call-in rate. The ad said: “Nashville Lawn Care | Same-Day Service | (615) XXX-XXXX.” That’s it. Mobile users tap, phone dials. No landing page friction. Just a call.

If you’re running display ads (which I usually don’t recommend for small landscaping budgets), use before-and-after photos. One client in Portland had a display ad showing a sad yellow lawn next to a vibrant green one. CTR was 0.8% — low but the cost per conversion was $12 because the leads were highly qualified. Display works best when you’re retargeting people who visited your site, not cold audiences.

## Geo-Targeting: Why “Austin” Isn’t Specific Enough

Most landscaping businesses serve a radius of 10–20 miles. But Google Ads lets you go way deeper than a city name. I worked with a company in Denver that was targeting all of Denver county. Their ads were showing in Aurora, which is a 25-minute drive from their shop. They got calls from people in Aurora, but they refused to travel there for jobs under $2,000. They wasted about $300/month on clicks from people they would never service.

**The fix:** Use location targeting with a radius around your physical address. But don’t stop there. Use location exclusions to carve out areas you don’t serve. For example, if you serve only north of the river in Nashville, exclude south Nashville. If you only do high-end residential, exclude areas with homes under a certain value (you can’t directly target by home value, but you can use zip codes or demographic targeting). I also like to use “presence” targeting — only show ads to people who are physically in your area, not people who are searching for your service while on vacation in Florida.

One more trick: Use bid adjustments by location. If you get more profitable jobs from a specific neighborhood (say, the historic district in Portland), increase your bid by 20–30% for that area. Conversely, if an area gives you mostly small jobs or no-shows, lower the bid to 50% or even 0%. I had a client in Austin who adjusted bids for the “South Congress” area — it was his sweet spot for large re-landscaping projects. He increased his bid by 25% and got 40% more leads from that area, while cutting spend in less profitable zones.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads for my landscaping business?**

Start with $500–1,000 a month if you’re a solo operator or small crew. That’s enough to test a few keywords and get 10–20 leads. If you have a team of 3+ crews, $1,500–3,000 is more realistic. I’ve seen clients in medium-sized markets (Nashville, Portland) generate $4,000–8,000 in new revenue from $1,500 in ad spend — but only if the campaign is set up correctly. The average cost per click for landscaping keywords in the US is $4–7. Do the math: $1,500 ÷ $5 = 300 clicks. If your conversion rate is 5%, that’s 15 leads. If you close 30%, that’s 4–5 new clients. That pays for itself.

**Q: How long until I see results?**

You’ll see clicks within 24 hours. But real results — calls that turn into paying customers — usually take 2–4 weeks. The first week is data collection. The second week you start optimizing. By week four you should see a trend. I’ve had accounts that were profitable in 10 days (when the keywords were tight and the landing page was strong) and others that took two months to break even (because we had to kill bad keywords). Don’t judge after one week.

**Q: Should I use broad match, phrase match, or exact match?**

Use phrase match and exact match for the first 90 days. Broad match is a money pit for small businesses. I’ve seen broad match campaigns waste 40% of the budget on irrelevant searches like “landscaping salary” or “landscaping near me free.” Once you have a history of conversions and a solid negative keyword list, you can try broad match with smart bidding — but only if you have at least 30 conversions in the past 30 days. Most landscaping businesses don’t have that volume, so stick with phrase and exact.

**Q: Can I run ads only on weekdays?**

Yes, and you probably should. Landscaping calls rarely come in at 2 a.m. on a Saturday. I set most landscaping campaigns to run Monday–Friday, 8 a.m. to 6 p.m. (local time). You can also add Saturday 9 a.m. to 1 p.m. if you do weekend consultations. But watch your data — if you’re getting calls on Sunday from people who want a Monday estimate, don’t cut Sunday entirely. Use ad schedule bid adjustments to lower bids during low-quality hours instead of turning ads off completely.

**Q: How do I handle competitor clicks?**

First, add your competitors’ names as negative keywords. Someone searching “Better Lawn vs GreenScape” is comparing — they may click just to see your prices. Second, use IP exclusions if you know your competitor’s office IP. But in practice, competitor clicks are overblown. Google’s invalid click detection catches most of them. I’ve managed accounts with $100,000+ monthly spend and never seen more than 1–2% invalid clicks. If you’re truly worried, use a click fraud detection service like ClickCease. It costs about $20/month and can block repeat offenders.

**Q: Do I need a website or can I use a phone number only?**

You can use call-only ads (no website) if your goal is phone calls. That works well for simple services like lawn mowing or leaf removal. But if you offer multiple services (design, installation, maintenance, snow removal), a landing page is better. You can direct people to a page that lists services, shows photos, and has a clear “Get a Free Estimate” button. That page should load in under 2 seconds. I’ve seen landing pages that take 5 seconds to load destroy conversion rates — drop from 8% to 2% just from load time alone.
