## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Setting Up Google Ads Once and Walking Away

A bakery in Austin, Texas hired me after they'd spent $1,800 over three months with exactly zero online orders to show for it. They'd handed their Google Ads to a nephew who "knew computers." He set up one campaign, picked "coffee" as the keyword, and left it running.

Here's what actually happened: Their ads showed up for people searching "coffee near me" at 2 AM, when they were closed. Their budget blew through in the first week of each month. And their landing page was a static menu from 2019 that didn't even list current hours.

The fix took one afternoon. I helped them restructure into two campaigns: one for "best latte Austin" and "breakfast tacos + coffee" (high intent, local modifiers), and one brand campaign for people searching their actual business name. We added schedule-based bidding so ads only ran 6 AM–2 PM. We set a $30 daily cap. And we updated their website to show real-time inventory and a prominent "Order Online" button.

The outcome: Their cost per click dropped from $4.80 to $1.20. Within 60 days, they'd generated $3,200 in online orders from a $1,100 ad spend. That's a 190% return they'd been literally throwing away.

Most small business owners treat Google Ads like a sink: turn it on, hope water comes out. You have to check the pipes weekly.

### Mistake 2: Automating Social Media Until You Sound Like a Robot

An independent coffee roaster in Portland, Oregon signed up for a $99/month AI content generator. They fed it their menu and told it to "post daily." Within two weeks, they were publishing content like "Are you craving a warm beverage this Tuesday? ☕️ #coffeelife" — the same generic sludge every other account was posting.

Their engagement dropped 40% in a month. Their regulars started commenting "did a bot write this?" and "where's Jake? He usually does these posts."

The uncomfortable truth is that AI-generated generic content is worse than no content. It actively erodes trust because customers can smell it. What this owner should have done was use AI as an assistant, not the author. Use it to batch-write captions based on *real* things happening in the shop: the new single-origin Ethiopian they just got, the barista who won a latte art competition, the 10-year regular who proposed at table 7.

I had them reset: delete the automated queue, write three real posts per week (photos they took on their phone, stories they actually lived), and use AI only to repurpose those posts into different formats. One video of their roaster explaining the fermentation process became a blog post, a newsletter snippet, and an Instagram caption.

Within 90 days, their engagement was back to baseline, and they'd picked up 200 new followers who actually lived in Portland — not bots from the AI's generic audience targeting.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Your Own Customer Data for Fancy AI Tools

A pet grooming salon in Denver spent $400/month on an AI "predictive analytics" platform that claimed to tell them which customers would churn. The tool kept flagging their most loyal clients — people who'd been coming for years — because they hadn't visited in exactly 28 days. Meanwhile, the actual churning customers (people who'd moved, switched jobs, or found a cheaper spot) went completely undetected.

The problem? They'd connected the tool to their Booking page but not their Square POS system. So it had no idea those "inactive" clients had prepaid packages for next month. And it couldn't see the 12-year regular who always booked by phone.

I told them to cancel the $400 subscription and spend that money on something boring: cleaning up their client list in Square. They had 1,200 contacts with no tags, no notes, no last visit dates. We spent two hours with a barista and a spreadsheet tagging clients by service type, visit frequency, and lifetime spend.

Then we set up a free Mailchimp automation: clients who hadn't booked in 8 weeks got a "We miss your pup — here's $5 off a nail trim" email. Clients who'd spent over $500 got an early access link to holiday booking slots.

Within 60 days, they recovered 34 clients who'd silently drifted away, generating $2,400 in new bookings. The tool had cost $1,200 total and produced exactly zero recoveries.

Don't buy an AI marketing tool until you've looked at the data you already own. Most small businesses are sitting on a goldmine in their POS system and doing nothing with it.

### Mistake 4: Setting Up Yelp Ads Without Understanding Local Search

A hair salon in Chicago spent $900 on Yelp ads over three months and got 12 calls — none of which booked. Their owner showed me the Yelp dashboard: 2,400 impressions, 85 clicks, 12 calls, 0 bookings.

Here's why: Their Yelp page listed "hair salon" as the category and their "specialties" section was blank. When someone clicked the ad and landed on their page, they saw generic photos and a "Message Us" button that went to an email nobody checked. The owner was literally losing money on ads that sent strangers to a dead-end page.

The fix required zero new tools. We:
- Changed their Yelp categories to "hair salon, balayage specialist, curly hair salon"
- Added a "services" section with average prices ($85–$250 range, transparently listed)
- Enabled online booking through Booksy (free integration)
- Responded to every existing review (52 of them) within 24 hours

We paused the ad spend for two weeks while this was set up. Then we relaunched with a $300/month budget — one-third of what they'd been spending.

Outcome: 18 calls in month one. 9 bookings. $2,100 in revenue from an $300 spend. A 600% improvement.

Most small businesses run ads to a storefront that looks abandoned. Fix the storefront first. Then turn on the lights.

## How to Actually Set Up AI-Powered Email That Doesn't Annoy People

Most guides tell you to "segment your audience and send relevant content." That's like telling someone to "bake a cake and make it tasty." Let me be specific.

I helped a fitness studio in Nashville set up an email sequence that does actual work while they sleep. Here's exactly what we did:

**Step 1: Integrate Square with Mailchimp**

This took 12 minutes. Square has a native Mailchimp integration. Every customer who swipes a card or books a class gets added to a Mailchimp list with tags: class type, visit frequency, total spend. No manual entry.

**Step 2: Build Three Automated Emails, Not Fifty**

Most small business owners overcomplicate this. You need three sequences:

1. Welcome sequence for new clients: "Thanks for coming to your first class. Here's a free 7-day pass for a friend." This fires automatically 24 hours after their first visit. I've seen this alone generate 15–20% referral lifts.

2. Re-engagement sequence for lapsed clients: If someone hasn't booked in 45 days (based on their Square data), they get an email saying "We noticed you haven't been in — here's a 20% off punch card. Valid for 30 days." The Nashville studio recovered 28 clients in 90 days with this one email. At $85 average class price, that's $2,380.

3. VIP sequence for high-value clients: Anyone who's spent over $500 sends an automated email offering early access to new class schedules, private workshop invitations, and a birthday month credit. No discount — just access. High-value clients don't need discounts. They need to feel valued.

**Step 3: Use AI to Write Email Drafts, But Edit Them Personally**

I used ChatGPT to generate subject lines like "You haven't been to yoga in 45 days" and body copy. But then I sat down and replaced 60% of the text with actual things the owner says. "We missed you at the 6 AM flow" became "Julie asked about you at yesterday's 6 AM. She says hi and hopes everything's okay." That email got a 34% open rate and a 12% click rate.

**Step 4: Track One Metric**

Don't obsess over open rates. They're vanity. Track bookings from email. Mailchimp's tag system can attribute which email led to a booking. The Nashville studio's welcome series gets a 4.2% booking conversion rate. Their lapsed series gets 2.8%. That's real revenue.

## Using AI to Optimize Your Google Business Profile (The Thing Everyone Ignores)

Your Google Business Profile is the most underused marketing tool you own. Most small businesses set it up once and forget it. AI can change that.

**What to Actually Do:**

A coffee shop in NYC (Upper East Side) was getting 400 Google profile views per month but only 20 direction requests. That's a 5% conversion rate. Their competitors were getting 12–15%.

I looked at their profile. They had three photos (all from 2021). Their description said "best coffee in NYC" — which is useless because every coffee shop says that. Their Q&A section had an unanswered question from 11 months ago: "Do you have oat milk?"

We used a free tool called GrowMyBusiness (Google's own AI) to analyze what nearby shops were doing differently. Here's what we found and fixed:

- Competitors were posting to Google Business Profile 3–4 times per week. This shop had posted zero times in 6 months.
- Competitors listed specific roast profiles and origin stories. This shop just said "coffee."
- Competitors responded to every review. This shop responded to 2 out of 47.

We set up a weekly routine: Monday morning, take one photo of the daily special. Write a 50-word caption. Post. Wednesday, share a 30-second video of the barista pulling a shot. Friday, answer all new reviews and questions from the week.

To automate the scheduling, I used Local Falcon's scheduling feature (free tier) to queue posts. Then I used the Google My Business API to pull weekly insights into a simple spreadsheet.

**The Outcome After 8 Weeks:**

- Profile views: 400 → 650 (62% increase)
- Direction requests: 20 → 72 (260% increase)
- Phone calls from profile: 12 → 31
- Answered the oat milk question: Yes, 24 visitors clicked that Q&A in the following month

Cost of all this: $0. Time investment: 30 minutes per week.

If you're not posting to your Google Business Profile weekly, you're leaving money on the table. The algorithm favors active profiles. Period.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why should I bother with AI if my shop is already busy?**

Because busy doesn't mean profitable. I've worked with busy shops losing money because they're running on volume with no margin. AI can help you shift from "we serve a lot of people" to "we serve the right people who spend more." A busy shop with $0 in AI-optimized email is a shop leaving 15–20% of its potential revenue behind. That's real money you're walking past every day.

**Q: Will AI replace the human touch in my business?**

No. AI can write an email, but it can't know that a regular's dog just died or that someone got a promotion. The shops that win use AI to handle the 80% of marketing that's repeatable and boring — scheduling posts, sorting data, writing draft emails — so they have time to do the 20% that genuinely matters. The shop owner who sends a handwritten card after a customer's marathon gets remembered. AI didn't write that card.

**Q: How much does this actually cost?**

It depends on how deep you go. A Mailchimp free tier handles 500 contacts. Google Business Profile is free. Square's basic plan is free. So you can start for $0. If you want Google Ads, set a daily budget you can afford to lose — I typically recommend $10–20/day for a first test. That's $300–600/month. If it doesn't generate at least 2x that in revenue in 90 days, kill it. Don't let these tools drain your account.

**Q: I'm not technical. Can I still do this?**

Yes. I've set up automated email sequences with shop owners who'd never touched Mailchimp. The key is to start small: pick one thing (your Google Business Profile), do that for two weeks, see what happens. You don't need to learn coding or AI prompt engineering. You just need to know what buttons to click. And most tools have customer support lines where a real person will walk you through it.

**Q: What's the biggest waste of money I should avoid?**

Buying a $200/month AI tool before you've optimized your free Google Business Profile. I see this constantly. Someone subscribes to an AI content generator, an AI analytics platform, and an AI chatbot — and they haven't posted to Google in eight months. The fancy tools won't fix a broken foundation. Get the basics right first. Then add the AI layer.

**Q: How do I know if it's working?**

Pick one metric and track it. For email, track bookings from email. For Google Ads, track cost per booking. For social media, track website clicks (not likes). If you can't point to a direct revenue number from any tool, you're not using it right. I recommend a simple spreadsheet: tool name, monthly cost, revenue generated, profit. If the profit isn't positive for 90 days, pause or kill it.
