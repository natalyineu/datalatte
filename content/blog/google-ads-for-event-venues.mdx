### Step 1: Set Up Your Google Ads Account

To start using Google Ads for your event venue, you'll need to set up a Google Ads account. This is a simple process that involves creating a new account and setting up your business information.

<Callout type="tip">Make sure to set up your Google Business Profile and link it to your Google Ads account. This will help you track your ads and bookings in one place.</Callout>

### Step 2: Choose Your Keywords

Next, you'll need to choose the keywords that potential customers will use to search for your venue. These might include things like "event venues near me" or "wedding venues in [city]". You can use Google Ads' keyword planner tool to find relevant keywords and estimate their search volume.

<Callout type="warning">Make sure to choose keywords that are relevant to your venue and target audience. This will help you avoid wasting money on ads that aren't converting.</Callout>

### Step 3: Create Your Ad Copy

Once you have your keywords, it's time to create your ad copy. This should include a compelling headline, a clear description of your venue, and a strong call-to-action. Make sure to include your venue's name, location, and contact information.

<Callout type="example">Here's an example of a Google Ads ad for an event venue: "**Beautiful Views at The Venue** Book your wedding or event at The Venue, located in the heart of [city]. Our stunning views and expert staff will make your special day unforgettable. Contact us today to schedule a tour!"</Callout>

### Step 4: Set Your Budget and Bidding Strategy

Next, you'll need to set your budget and bidding strategy. This will determine how much you're willing to spend on ads and how they're displayed to potential customers. You can choose from a variety of bidding strategies, including cost-per-click (CPC) and cost-per-conversion (CPA).

<Callout type="coffee">At DataLatte, we recommend starting with a CPC bidding strategy and adjusting as needed based on your results.</Callout>

### Step 5: Monitor and Optimize Your Ads

Finally, you'll need to monitor and optimize your ads regularly. This means tracking your ad performance, adjusting your targeting and ad copy as needed, and making sure your budget is allocated effectively.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting "Event Venue" When You're a Wedding Barn in Austin

I watched a client — let's call her Jenna — run ads for her restored barn venue outside Austin, Texas. She did what most guides tell you: picked "event venue Austin" as a keyword and let Google run with it.

Two weeks in, she had spent $1,800. She got three leads. One asked if they could host a bachelor party for 80 people. Another wanted to film a music video. The third never replied after she sent pricing.

The problem was obvious: broad match keywords and zero negative keywords. Google interpreted "event venue" as "anywhere people gather." Jenna was paying for clicks from people looking for nightclubs, conference centers, and frat party spots. None of those people were brides.

**The fix:** Switch to phrase match targeting specific intent. Keywords like "wedding venue Austin Texas," "barn wedding venue near me," and "Austin wedding reception venue." Then add negative keywords: "club," "nightclub," "conference," "corporate event," "bachelor party," "DIY."

**The outcome:** Jenna's spend dropped to $600 per month. She started getting 4-5 serious inquiries per week. Her cost per qualified lead went from $600 to $120. Within three months, she booked 11 weddings from those ads — about $38,000 in total revenue. She told me she almost gave up on Google Ads entirely before we made that one change. One set of negative keywords. That's it.

### Mistake #2: Sending Everyone to Your Homepage

A warehouse-turned-event-space in Nashville was spending $1,200 a month on Google Ads. Their ad copy was decent. Their keywords were fine. But every click went to their homepage — a beautiful, full-screen video slider with navigation menus, five different sections, and a blog about "best Nashville event trends."

You already know where this is going.

Visitors landed, scrolled, got distracted by the blog post about catering trends, and left. The client's booking form was buried under three clicks and a "Services" dropdown. Their conversion rate was 0.8%. That means for every 100 people who clicked their ad, fewer than one filled out the booking form.

**The fix:** Build a dedicated landing page — no navigation, no blog posts, no distractions. One headline. Three bullet points (capacity, catering options, sound system). A gallery of real events that happened in the space. A short form: name, event type, guest count, desired date. That's it.

I used Google Sites for a client once. Took me two hours. Cost zero dollars. It out-converted their $5,000 website.

**The outcome:** Conversion rate went from 0.8% to 6.2%. Cost per booking dropped from $340 to $92. The Nashville client went from two bookings a month (via ads) to nine. Monthly ad spend stayed at $1,200. Monthly revenue from ads went from $3,400 to $11,200. All because we removed the option to get lost.

### Mistake #3: Running Ads When You're Fully Booked

A venue owner in Portland, Oregon, called me frustrated. She was spending $900 a month on Google Ads. She was also booked solid for the next eight months. Every time someone submitted a booking inquiry, she had to email back: "Sorry, we're unavailable."

She was paying Google to send people to a "no" experience.

**The fix:** Pause your ads when you're within 60 days of full capacity. Resume them when you have open dates. If you have one weekend date available in September, write ad copy specifically for that date: "September 14th is open. 150 guests. Inquire now."

Google Ads doesn't penalize you for pausing campaigns. There's no algorithm score that drops because you stopped spending for two months. The idea that you need to run ads year-round is something agencies say to keep your retainer. If your calendar is full, your ads should be off.

**The outcome:** The Portland venue saved $5,400 over six months. She redirected that money into a small retargeting campaign that filled the first three months of the following year — at half the cost because she only ran ads for specific open dates. Her cost per booking actually went down because she stopped wasting money on people who couldn't book her anyway.

## Which Campaign Type Actually Works for Event Venues?

Most guides will tell you to use "a mix of campaigns" — Search, Display, YouTube, Performance Max. That advice sounds strategic. It's also wrong for most event venues.

Here's what actually works, in order of priority:

### Search Campaigns — Your Core Investment

Put 70% of your budget here. Search is where someone types "event venue Chicago" and sees your ad. It's intent-based. They already want what you're selling.

Use phrase match keywords with location modifiers: "small wedding venue Denver," "corporate event space Austin," "birthday party venue Nashville." Add negative keywords aggressively. I've seen clients waste 30% of their budget on clicks from people searching "free event venue" — those people are never booking.

Set up call extensions. For event venues, about 40% of conversions come from phone calls, not form fills. Make sure Google Ads is tracking those calls. I used a client's call tracking data to prove that their most valuable leads came from 11am-1pm on weekdays — when they had lunch breaks. So we bid higher during that window.

### Performance Max — Use With Caution

Performance Max sounds great in theory: Google uses AI to place your ads across Search, Display, YouTube, and Gmail. In practice, I've seen it send event venue ads to people watching cat videos on their phone at 2am.

If you use Performance Max, narrow the audience. Exclude people outside your target radius. Exclude mobile app placements. Set a low budget — $300 to test — and check the search terms report daily for the first week. I had a client in Denver whose Performance Max campaign spent $700 in three days on people searching "funny dog videos." That's real. I have the screenshots.

### Display Campaigns — Only for Retargeting

Never use Display for cold audiences. Nobody is browsing blogs about cheese and thinking "I should book a venue right now." Display is for people who already visited your site. Set up a remarketing list of visitors who viewed your booking page but didn't convert. Then show them an ad with a specific date availability or a limited-time offer ($200 off for bookings made this month).

I had a venue in Chicago test this: they spent $400 on a Display retargeting campaign and got 3 bookings. Cost per booking: $133. Their regular Search campaign cost per booking at the time was $240. Retargeting works because those people already know you exist.

### YouTube — Only If You Have Video Content

If you don't have a 60-second video walkthrough of your venue, skip YouTube. If you do have one, run a short campaign targeting people who searched for venues in your area. Keep the ad under 30 seconds. Show the space. Show real people at events. End with a clear call to action: "Check availability at yourcityvenue.com."

## Retargeting: The Booking You Left on the Table

Here's a number that surprised me: at one agency, we tracked that 97% of visitors to event venue sites leave without booking. That's not a typo. Ninety-seven percent leave. Most of them never come back.

Most venue owners accept this as normal. It's not. It's a leak in your funnel.

### How to Build a Retargeting Campaign

First, set up a Google Ads remarketing tag on your website. Most booking platforms (Squarespace, Wix, WordPress with a plugin) let you add this in under five minutes.

Next, create an audience of people who visited your booking or pricing page but didn't submit a form. Give it a 30-day window. Someone who looked at pricing last week is still deciding between you and two other venues. Your ad can be the nudge.

Then write specific ad copy. Not "Book your event today" — that's too generic. Instead: "Still comparing venues? We'll waive the setup fee for bookings made this month." Or "Your 80-guest wedding. This October. Yes, that date is still available."

### The Results

A venue in Denver — a converted loft space that hosted birthday parties and small receptions — ran a retargeting campaign on a $500 monthly budget. They targeted people who had visited their site in the last 14 days but hadn't booked.

In the first month, they got 4 bookings directly from those retargeting ads. Average booking value: $2,800. Total revenue from retargeting: $11,200. Cost: $500.

The owner told me, "I didn't even know this was possible. I thought Google Ads was just for new customers."

She was right about one thing: most guides only talk about acquiring new customers. Retargeting is where the math actually makes sense for event venues. Your cost per click is lower. Your conversion rate is higher. And you're not fighting against the "I'll think about it" silence.

### Tools That Make It Easier

If you're using Mailchimp or Square to manage inquiries, you can integrate Google Ads remarketing with those platforms. Mailchimp lets you upload email lists of past inquirers who never booked, then target them with ads. Square has a similar feature for appointment-based businesses.

I also recommend using a simple CRM — even a spreadsheet — to track where each booking comes from. When a client books, ask: "How did you hear about us?" If they say "I saw an ad after visiting your site," that's retargeting working. Attribute it, track it, invest more.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I actually spend on Google Ads as a small venue?**

Start at $1,000 per month for a mid-sized venue in a US city. I've seen venues in smaller markets (think Tulsa, Spokane, Knoxville) do well with $600. I've also seen venues in NYC spend $3,000 and get lost because they didn't narrow their location targeting. Test $1,000 for 60 days. If you're getting at least 3-4 qualified leads per week, increase it. If you're getting nothing, your keywords or landing page are wrong — don't fix that by spending more.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads without a website?**

Technically yes — Google Ads can link to your Google Business Profile. Practically, don't. A Google Business Profile page limits what you can show. You need a landing page with clear availability, pricing, and a booking form. I've used Squarespace to build a single-page site for a venue in under a day. Cost: $16 per month. Worth it.

**Q: What about advertising on Yelp?**

Yelp works for venues — but differently. People on Yelp are browsing, not necessarily booking. Google Ads captures people actively searching. I usually recommend a 70/30 split: 70% Google Ads, 30% Yelp, then adjust based on which platform actually sends booked clients. But track everything. Yelp will happily take your money without clear reporting.

**Q: Should I run ads if I only have one or two events per month?**

Run ads only 4-6 weeks before your open dates. Pause the campaign when you're booked. I helped a small venue in Portland run ads only during February and August to fill their spring and fall dates. They spent $2,000 total across both campaigns and booked 8 events — $16,000 in revenue. The rest of the year they paid zero ad spend. Google Ads doesn't penalize you for pausing.

**Q: How do I know if an ad actually led to a booking?**

Set up conversion tracking in Google Ads. Track form submissions, phone calls, and booking page views. Use Google Analytics 4 to see the full journey. But honestly — I still recommend asking every client "How did you find us?" in your booking confirmation email. I've caught several tracking errors that way. The data lies sometimes. People tell the truth.

**Q: I tried Google Ads before and wasted money. What's different about your advice?**

What you probably did: picked broad keywords, sent traffic to your homepage, and let Google optimize for "conversions" that were just people clicking. What I'm suggesting: phrase match keywords with negative keywords, a dedicated landing page, conversion tracking, retargeting, and pausing ads when you're full. If you do those five things, your wasted spend drops by at least 60%. I've seen it happen at three different venues.
