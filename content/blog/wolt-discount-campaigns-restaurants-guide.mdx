## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running a Blanket 20% Off for Everyone, All the Time

**The story:** A BBQ joint in Austin, Texas — let's call it Smoky's. Owner Marcus had been running a 20% off promo on Wolt for six months straight. His thinking: "Everyone loves a deal, and I need to stay ahead of the new taqueria down the street." What actually happened: his average order value dropped from $38 to $28. Regular customers who used to order $45 worth of brisket and sides started buying the bare minimum. His margins went from 62% to 44%. On a $28 order, he was making about $12.32 instead of the $23.56 he used to make. Over 300 Wolt orders a month, that's a loss of $3,372 in gross profit.

**The fix:** I told Marcus to kill the blanket discount. Instead, run a **minimum purchase threshold** promo: 15% off orders over $40. Same perceived value, completely different economics. He also added a "first-time customer only" 20% off, which Wolt allows you to target. New customers got the full discount; existing customers got a smaller loyalty-based reward.

**The outcome:** His average order value climbed back to $42. Margins went to 58%. Monthly Wolt revenue went from $8,400 to $12,600. Net profit increased by $2,100 per month. Marcus told me, "I was basically paying people to order less. That was stupid."

**Why this happens:** Most restaurant owners treat Wolt promos like a light switch — on or off. They don't segment. Wolt's back end lets you set minimum order amounts, time windows, and customer type restrictions. Use them.

### Mistake #2: Setting the Discount Too High to Compete on the Front Page

**The story:** A pizza place in Nashville, Tennessee. Owner Jenna saw competitors offering 30% off and panicked. She matched it. Here's what she missed: Wolt's algorithm doesn't just rank by discount percentage. It ranks by a combination of discount depth, order frequency, and customer rating. Jenna's pizza place had a 4.8-star rating and consistent volume. She didn't need 30% off to show up on page one.

Her actual problem: she was only offering discounts during peak dinner hours (6–8 PM), when demand was already high. She was giving away margin when customers would have ordered anyway. On a $20 pizza, she was losing $6 per order. She ran 200 of those in two weeks. That's $1,200 in unnecessary loss.

**The fix:** I had her switch to a **tiered promo structure**: 10% off orders under $25, 20% off orders over $40, and completely remove discounts during 5–8 PM. Instead, run a "late-night recovery" promo from 9–11 PM, when orders were slow. Drop the discount to 15% for that window.

**The outcome:** Total promo spend dropped from $1,200 to $480 per two-week period. Order volume during slow hours increased by 35%. Average order size went from $18 to $26. Net margin on Wolt orders moved from 12% to 31%. Jenna's exact words: "I was literally paying people to eat dinner at the worst possible time."

**The lesson:** Discount during low-demand windows, not peak hours. You're not competing with a deeper discount — you're competing for placement in a specific time slot. Wolt shows you hourly order data. Use it.

### Mistake #3: Treating All Customers Like They're New

**The story:** A coffee shop in Portland, Oregon — three locations, decent Wolt presence. Owner Sarah ran a "buy one, get one 50% off" for three weeks straight. She thought she was building loyalty. What she was actually doing: giving a discount to customers who had already ordered 8 times in the past month. Those regulars would have ordered anyway. She was burning $4.50 per transaction on people who didn't need convincing.

I looked at her Wolt analytics. 40% of her promo redemptions came from customers in the top 20% of order frequency. She was spending $1,800 a month rewarding her most loyal customers for behavior they were already doing.

**The fix:** Segment by order history. New customers (0–2 orders) get 20% off. Medium-frequency customers (3–8 orders) get free delivery. High-frequency customers (9+ orders) get a "secret menu" item added for free — costs her $0.80 in ingredients, feels exclusive, and doesn't train them to expect discounts.

**The outcome:** Promo cost dropped from $1,800 to $600 per month. New customer conversion rate went from 22% to 41%. Repeat order rate from new customers at 60 days: 34%, up from 19%. Sarah said, "I was literally handing cash to people who already loved me. That's not marketing, that's charity."

**Why this works:** Most Wolt promos are a blunt instrument. The platform gives you targeting tools — you just have to use them. If you're not segmenting by order count, you're leaving money on the table.

### Mistake #4: Not Tracking Anything Beyond Order Volume

**The story:** A sushi spot in Denver, Colorado. Owner Tom ran a 25% off promo for two weeks. Orders jumped from 150/week to 310/week. He was thrilled. Until he looked at the actual numbers: average order value dropped from $32 to $21. Total revenue went from $4,800 to $6,510 — a 35% increase. But his food cost percentage went from 28% to 34%, and he had to hire an extra cook for $800/week. Net profit on Wolt actually *decreased* by $240.

**The fix:** Before running any promo, I set up a simple tracking sheet in Google Sheets. Columns: promo type, start date, end date, total orders, average order value, food cost %, labor cost, net profit. Tom ran his next promo at 15% off with a $25 minimum. Orders increased to 220/week. Average order value stayed at $30. Net profit increased by $520.

**The outcome:** Tom's total Wolt profit for the three months after the fix was $6,240 higher than the three months before. He now runs promos only when he can track profit, not just orders. His exact words: "I was celebrating a loss. That's embarrassing."

**The rule:** Orders are a vanity metric. Profit is the real number. If you're not tracking average order value, food cost, and labor impact, you're flying blind. I've seen this exact mistake kill margins at three different clients. Don't be the fourth.

## How to Budget a Wolt Promo Without Guessing

I've seen owners set promo budgets by picking a random number. "$500 a month." "Maybe $200." "I don't know, whatever feels right." That's not a strategy, that's a prayer.

Here's the framework I've used for 10+ years in media buying, now adapted for Wolt. It works for coffee shops, hair salons, and pet groomers. Yes, even non-food businesses — Wolt isn't just for food in every market.

**Step 1: Set a maximum cost per acquired customer.** This is the most important number you'll never hear a generic marketing article mention. Take your average profit per order. For a coffee shop, that might be $4.50. For a hair salon, $35. For a pet groomer, $28. Now decide: how much of that profit are you willing to spend to acquire a new customer? I recommend 30–50% for the first order. For a coffee shop at $4.50 profit, that's $1.35–$2.25 per new customer.

**Step 2: Calculate your promo cost per order.** If you're offering 20% off a $12 order, you're spending $2.40 per order. For a new customer, that's above your $2.25 max. So either reduce the discount to 15% ($1.80) or increase the minimum order to $15 (20% off = $3.00, still too high — so drop to 10% off at $15 = $1.50). Math isn't optional here.

**Step 3: Set a hard cap.** Total promo budget = (target new customers per month) x (cost per acquired customer). If you want 100 new customers and your cost per customer is $2.00, your budget is $200. Not $500. Not $1,000. $200. Anything above that, you're subsidizing existing customers.

**Tools I use for this:** Google Sheets for the math. Square or Toast for tracking actual profit per order. Booksy for service-based businesses that want to run Wolt-adjacent promos. Mailchimp for follow-up emails to promo customers (more on that later).

**Real example:** A dog groomer in Chicago ran a 20% off first visit on Wolt. Average first visit revenue: $65. Profit: $28. Cost per acquired customer at 20% off: $13. That's 46% of profit — on the high end but acceptable. They capped the promo at $500 total spend. After 38 new customers, they hit the cap. Those 38 customers generated $2,464 in profit on first visits. Lifetime value from those customers: estimated $6,840 over 12 months. The $500 promo spend returned 13.7x.

**The uncomfortable truth:** Most small business owners don't know their profit per order. They know their revenue. They know their costs vaguely. But they haven't done the subtraction. If you don't know profit per order, stop reading this article and go calculate it. I'll wait.

## Using Customer Data to Turn Promo Users Into Regulars

Here's the part most guides skip: what happens *after* someone orders through your Wolt promo. Because if you're not retaining them, you're just renting customers.

I've seen this pattern at every agency I worked at: a promo drives a spike, then two weeks later, orders drop back to baseline. The promo didn't grow the business. It just shifted when people ordered.

**The fix is boring but effective:** capture the data and follow up.

Wolt gives you some customer data — names, order history, sometimes phone numbers. But it's limited. You want to get customers into your own system. Here's how I've done it for clients:

**For restaurants:** Include a card in the delivery bag. "Thanks for your order! Text 'COFFEE' to [number] for $3 off your next direct order." Use a tool like Mailchimp or Klaviyo to set up the automated text response. Cost: $0.05 per text. Conversion rate: 8–12%. Average lifetime value of a captured customer: $47.

**For service businesses (hair salons, pet groomers, fitness studios):** Include a QR code on the receipt or confirmation page. Leads to a Google Form or Booksy booking page. Offer a small incentive: $5 off next appointment. Then email the list weekly with relevant content — new services, availability updates, before-and-after photos.

**Real example:** A hair salon in NYC ran a 20% off first visit on Wolt. They included a card with every delivery asking customers to book directly for 10% off next visit. 22% of promo customers booked through the QR code. Six months later, 41% of those had become regular clients booking every 4–6 weeks. Average spend per visit: $85. The salon spent $1,200 on the Wolt promo. Those customers generated $14,280 in revenue over six months.

**The tool stack:** Mailchimp for email (free up to 500 contacts). Google Voice for text (free). Square or Booksy for bookings. Canva for the card design (free template). Total cost: $0. You just need 30 minutes to set it up.

**What NOT to do:** Don't send a generic "thanks for your order" email with no offer. Don't wait two weeks to follow up. Don't ask for a review before you've delivered value. Sequence matters: day 1 — thank you + small incentive. Day 7 — reminder of the offer. Day 14 — "we miss you" with a time-sensitive discount. After that, monthly check-ins.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Won't running discounts make customers expect them and never pay full price?**

Yes, if you run discounts the same way every time. If you always offer 20% off, customers will wait for 20% off. The fix: vary the type and depth. Run a 15% off for two weeks, then free delivery for a week, then a buy-one-get-one on a slow day. Keep customers guessing. Also, reserve your best offers for first-time customers only. Existing customers get smaller, more targeted rewards. I've seen this work at a Chicago pizza chain that went from 60% of orders on promo to 22% over three months — without losing volume.

**Q: How do I know if a Wolt promo is actually profitable?**

Track three numbers before and during the promo: total revenue, average order value, and food cost (or service cost). If average order value drops and cost percentage goes up, you're losing money, even if orders increase. Also track labor — did you need an extra employee? That's a cost. Calculate net profit, not just gross revenue. If you can't do this math quickly, use Square or Toast's reporting features. They both show cost of goods sold per item.

**Q: What's better — percentage off or free delivery?**

Depends on your average order value. Free delivery works best for higher-ticket items ($30+). Why? Because the delivery fee is usually $3–$5, which feels like a smaller discount than 20% off. For lower-priced items (coffee, pastries, quick services), percentage off feels bigger. Test both for two weeks each and compare net profit, not just order volume. A Denver coffee shop I worked with ran both: free delivery increased orders by 15%, but 15% off increased orders by 28% and average order value by 9%.

**Q: Should I set a minimum order amount for promos?**

Yes. Always. Unless you're trying to clear inventory or drive traffic during a dead hour. Minimum order amounts protect your margin by ensuring the customer spends enough to offset the discount. For a $12 average order, set a $15 minimum. For $20 average, set $25. You'll lose some small orders but gain profitability on the ones you keep. A Nashville bar I worked with saw a 12% drop in promo redemptions after adding a $20 minimum, but net profit per promo order increased by 34%.

**Q: How often should I run a promo on Wolt?**

Not constantly. I recommend a 2-week promo every 6–8 weeks. That keeps the platform's algorithm happy (Wolt rewards active promos with better placement) without training customers to expect discounts. Off-weeks, focus on your direct ordering system or in-person traffic. A Portland sushi spot ran promos every 4 weeks and saw customer acquisition costs rise by 40% — customers were waiting for the next promo. They switched to every 8 weeks and acquisition costs dropped back to normal.

**Q: Can I run the same promo on Wolt and my own website simultaneously?**

Technically yes, but don't. You'll cannibalize your direct orders, which have no commission fee. If you offer 20% off on Wolt and 20% off on your site, customers will choose the easier path (Wolt) and you'll pay the 30% commission on every order. Instead, offer a better deal on your own site: 20% off direct orders, 10% off on Wolt. Or use Wolt for acquisition and your site for retention. A pet groomer in Austin tried running the same promo on both — her Wolt commission ate 28% of revenue. She switched to a 10% difference and within a month, 35% of new customers had moved to direct booking.
