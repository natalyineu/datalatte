A sold-out NFL game brings 70,000 people into a stadium — and another 50,000 into the surrounding neighbourhood for bars, restaurants, and pre-game activities. A music festival draws 30,000 people to a venue district for an entire weekend. A city marathon creates a 26-mile corridor of engaged spectators for four hours on a Sunday morning.

These concentrated, time-specific gatherings are extraordinary advertising moments — and programmatic DOOH lets you activate the physical advertising environment specifically when and where these events happen.

This is event-triggered DOOH: scheduling and targeting your digital billboard and screen campaigns around specific events in your city, reaching people who are physically present in the event environment.

## Why Events Are Gold for DOOH Advertising

Events create three conditions that advertisers dream about:

**Geographic concentration:** Tens of thousands of people in a specific area simultaneously. The density of potential customers in one place far exceeds normal day-to-day foot traffic patterns.

**Elevated receptivity:** Event attendees are in a positive, excited emotional state. They're spending money (tickets, food, merchandise), they're with friends or family, and they're more open to discovery than in their normal daily routine.

**Established routes:** Events create predictable movement corridors — from transit stations to the venue, from parking to the gate, through the surrounding restaurant and bar district. Screens along these corridors capture the event audience as they move.

The combination makes event-adjacent DOOH one of the highest-impact local advertising opportunities available, and programmatic buying makes it accessible without locking into expensive multi-month commitments.

## How Event-Triggered DOOH Works Technically

Programmatic DOOH platforms allow **dayparting** — scheduling ads to run during specific date and time windows. Combined with geographic screen selection (choosing screens near the event venue), this creates event-triggered campaigns.

**Basic implementation:**
1. Identify the event (date, time, location, expected attendance)
2. Select screens within the audience movement corridor (1–3 mile radius from venue, along transit routes)
3. Set campaign to activate only during the relevant window (3 hours before through 1 hour after)
4. Upload creative relevant to the event context

**Advanced implementation:**
1. Use event data feeds (Ticketmaster/Live Nation APIs, sports schedules) integrated with DSP platforms
2. Set automatic activation triggers tied to confirmed event schedules
3. Dynamic creative that references the specific event ("Game day? Come in before kickoff")
4. Mobile retargeting of devices detected near DOOH screens for follow-up digital ads

Platforms like Vistar Media and The Trade Desk have event data integrations that allow fully automated event-triggered DOOH at scale — campaigns that automatically activate before every home game, every concert, every marathon without manual campaign management.

## The Event Audience Corridor Strategy

The most important concept in event-triggered DOOH: **you don't need to advertise inside the venue**. The highest-value screens are outside, along the audience's route.

**Mapping the event corridor:**
1. Identify the primary transit access points (metro stations, bus stops, parking garages)
2. Map the walking routes from transit to the venue entrance
3. Identify the pre-/post-game bar and restaurant district
4. Note screen inventory available along these routes

A local restaurant targeting NFL game attendees doesn't need a screen inside the stadium. It needs screens along the three-block walk from the metro exit to the stadium gate — where 30,000 fans pass in a 2-hour window, many actively looking for food and drink.

**The corridor typically includes:**
- Transit station platforms and exits
- Bus shelter panels along the approach routes
- Digital billboards on the primary vehicle access roads
- Retail venue screens in the adjacent commercial district
- Gas station screens on the approach corridors (if suburban venue)

## Business Categories and Event Strategies

**Restaurants and Bars (Highest Opportunity):**

The pre-event dining decision is the richest moment. Activate DOOH screens in the 2–3 mile radius around the venue, 3 hours before game/show time. Message: "Pre-game happy hour" or "dinner before the show — walk-ins welcome."

Post-event is also valuable for bars — fans leaving a game are social, energised, and looking for the next stop. Post-game screens (30 minutes after end time) capture this window.

**Parking and Transportation:**

Local parking services, rideshare promoters, and shuttle services have obvious event adjacency. DOOH screens at transit exits and in venue corridors with "parking available" messaging capture decision-moments at the highest relevance.

**Local Retail (Merchandise, Souvenirs, Food):**

Businesses selling items relevant to the event audience — sports gear, fan merchandise, event snacks. A local sporting goods store can activate DOOH ahead of the home opener.

**Home Services and Trade Businesses:**

Counterintuitive but effective: events bring out-of-town visitors and create weekend foot traffic in neighbourhoods that normally see less. A home services business targeting a neighbourhood undergoing development near a stadium/arena benefits from the area's elevated foot traffic.

**Fitness and Wellness:**

Marathon and running event corridors are particularly relevant for fitness businesses — a yoga studio or sports nutrition store targeting the route of a major city marathon reaches a self-selected health audience.

## Budgeting for Event DOOH

Event-triggered DOOH can be surprisingly affordable for short, intense bursts:

**Single game or event activation:**
- Screen selection: 5–15 screens in the event corridor
- Duration: 3–4 hours (pre and post event window)
- Budget: $200–800 per event activation
- CPM: $5–15 in event-adjacent non-premium placements

**Season-long sports activation:**
- 8–16 home games per team per season
- Automated activation each game
- Budget: $1,500–6,000 for a full season of activations
- Effectively running an always-on campaign aligned to your market's biggest attendance moments

**Festival or multi-day event:**
- Active for full festival duration (Friday–Sunday)
- Higher pedestrian density
- Budget: $500–2,000 for a weekend festival campaign

For a restaurant near a major sports venue running pre-game DOOH activations for a full season: $2,000–4,000 achieves regular brand presence during the 16+ weekends when 40,000+ people move through the surrounding neighbourhood.

## Creative Strategy for Event DOOH

Event-adjacent creative should acknowledge the moment without crossing trademark lines:

**Do:**
- "Game night? We're open late." (no team names or logos)
- "Pre-show dinner — 5 min walk from the arena"
- "Running the [City] Marathon? Recover here Sunday."
- "Home game Saturday — reserve your table"

**Don't:**
- Use official league or team logos without licence
- Reference specific scores or outcomes (you can't update in real time fast enough)
- Make promises you can't fulfil if crowd volume is higher than expected

Time-aware dynamic creative (using live clock data) can update messaging in real time: "Game starts in 90 minutes — come in now" → "Halftime — join us for the second half" → "Game over — celebrate here." This level of creative dynamism requires DSP integration with event data feeds, achievable through platforms like Vistar.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I target DOOH screens inside a sports venue?

In-venue screens (scoreboard, concourse, suite hallways) are typically managed by the venue's exclusive advertising partner and are not part of programmatic DOOH networks. Contact the venue's marketing or advertising team directly for in-venue placements. These usually require significant minimum spends and long lead times.

### How far in advance do I need to plan event DOOH?

For manual campaign setup: 1–2 weeks in advance to set up the campaign, select screens, and get creative approved. For automated event-triggered campaigns using event data feeds: setup takes 2–4 weeks initially, then campaigns activate automatically for each event on the calendar.

### What if it rains and the event is cancelled?

Programmatic DOOH campaigns can be paused instantly. If an outdoor event is cancelled due to weather, you can deactivate the campaign manually or set up weather-triggered pause rules (some DSPs support weather API integrations that pause campaigns when precipitation exceeds a threshold).

### How do I know which events to target in my city?

Start with the highest-attendance recurring events: home games for your local professional sports teams. Supplement with the city's major annual festivals (check the city's events calendar). Marathon and running events are highly predictable and well-documented. The goal is recurring events that justify the campaign setup investment — one-off small events rarely generate enough foot traffic to justify event-specific DOOH activation.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running the Same Creative During and After the Event

A sports bar in Austin, Texas — let's call it The Sideline — spent $1,200 on a two-week campaign around a major UT Austin home game. They booked digital billboards along I-35 and screens near the stadium. 

The problem? Their ad ran the same message: "Come watch the game here!" for two weeks straight. That's fine for the Friday before and the day of. But for the six days after the game? They were advertising to people who had already been to the game, already eaten, already moved on. The owner told me they got zero measurable foot traffic from the post-game week.

**The fix:** Split your campaign into pre-event, during-event, and post-event phases. Pre-event: "Get your table before kickoff." During-event: "Half-time pitchers — we're two blocks east." Post-event: "Post-game burgers and recovery drinks."

**The outcome:** When we tested this phased approach with a different Austin bar (Banger's on Rainey Street), their pre-game ads drove 40% of total redemptions, during-game ads pulled in people walking past the venue, and post-game ads caught the crowd leaving the stadium. Total spend was $1,500 over a three-day window. They tracked 86 coupon scans using a simple unique QR code. That's $3,800 in incremental revenue from one game weekend.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Mobile Landing Pages and Booking Links

A hair salon in Nashville — let's call it Eastside Cuts — ran digital billboards near the Ryman Auditorium during CMA Fest. Their ad showed a photo of a fresh blowout and the text "Walk-ins welcome!" 

Here's what happened: people saw the ad, Googled the salon, landed on their desktop website (because whoever built it didn't mobile-optimize), saw a generic contact form, got frustrated, and went to the next option. The owner told me they had three walk-ins during the entire five-day festival. The campaign cost $800.

**The fix:** Your DOOH ad should send people to a mobile-landing page that shows real-time appointment availability. Use a tool like Booksy or Square Appointments to embed a booking widget directly. If you're a coffee shop, link to a mobile order-ahead page. If you're a pet groomer, show a one-tap "Book Now" button with the next three available slots.

**The outcome:** A salon in Denver (Spoke & Weal on Larimer Street) used this exact setup during a conference week. They ran DOOH ads near the convention center, linked to a mobile-optimized Booksy page showing same-day availability. They booked 14 appointments in one day. Average ticket: $130. That's $1,820 in revenue from a $600 campaign. The appointments filled slots they would have left empty anyway.

### Mistake #3: Picking the Wrong Event for Your Business

A pet groomer in Portland, Oregon spent $900 on DOOH screens near the Portland International Raceway during a cycling event. She wanted to reach affluent pet owners. But the crowd was mostly out-of-town cyclists focused on gear and race logistics, not looking up dog grooming.

**The fix:** Map your customer's real-world behavior. A pet groomer should target farmers' markets on Saturday mornings (people bring dogs), not cycling events. A fitness studio should target marathon expos (you can buy passes at the event itself). A coffee shop should target early-morning 5K races, not evening concerts. Use foot traffic data from tools like Placer.ai or even just watch the neighborhood yourself for two weekends.  

**The outcome:** That same Portland pet groomer redirected her budget to screens near the Portland Farmers Market (PSU location). She spent $600 over four Saturdays. Her ad offered a "Market Day Drop-Off" — bring your dog for a bath while you shop. She averaged 7 new clients per market day. At $65 per groom, with a 60% repeat rate within 90 days, that's roughly $3,640 in new revenue from a $600 spend.

### Mistake #4: Not Integrating DOOH With Other Digital Channels

A boutique fitness studio in Chicago bought DOOH screens near the Lincoln Park Zoo during a summer concert series. They ran a generic "Try us for free!" ad. Meanwhile, they had no corresponding Google Ads campaign, no social boost, no email list targeting. The ad had no way to track who saw it.

**The fix:** Event DOOH works best as a top-of-funnel trigger. The person sees your billboard, searches your business name, and then hits either Google Ads (where you've bid on branded terms + event keywords) or your Instagram page (where you have a pinned post with the same offer). Set up a UTM campaign in Google Analytics for "DOOH-event" so you can see traffic spikes. If your budget allows, use a geofence around the venue — anyone who saw your DOOH and steps inside the venue gets a retargeting ad on their phone within 24 hours.

**The outcome:** A coffee shop in New York (Gregorys Coffee near Union Square) ran a DOOH campaign during a weekend street fair. They paired it with a $200 Google Ads budget for searches like "Union Square coffee" and "street fair coffee near me." They also used Mailchimp to send a 10am Saturday email to their local list with the subject line: "We're two blocks from the fair — grab a cold brew on us." The DOOH drove 22% more foot traffic than the weekend before. The Google Ads brought in 34 new email signups via a landing page offer. Combined spend: $1,100. Combined revenue: $4,200 in incremental sales.

## How to Choose the Right Event for Your DOOH Campaign

Not every event is worth your money. I've seen small business owners blow budgets on events that look big on paper but deliver zero foot traffic to their door. Here's how to evaluate an event in about 20 minutes.

**Step 1: Check the attendee profile against your customer.** Go to the event's website or sponsor deck. If they publish average attendee age, income, or interests, compare it to your own customer data from Square, Lightspeed, or even your POS receipts. A music festival might draw 40,000 people, but if the average age is 19 and you sell $80 haircuts, you're wasting money.

**Step 2: Map the physical proximity to your business.** A billboard three miles from the venue might show 50,000 impressions. But the people who see it are already driving away or never planning to walk near your shop. The sweet spot is within 0.5–1.5 miles of the venue, along the pedestrian routes. Walk the actual route on Google Maps. Where do people grab food? Where do they wait for Ubers? Where do they line up? Those are your screen positions.

**Step 3: Time your campaign to the actual event flow.** A three-hour concert has a specific pattern: arrival (60 minutes before), entry (30 minutes before), intermission (if applicable), exit (30 minutes). A marathon has a four-hour window where spectators are static along the route. A festival has all-day foot traffic but with spikes at lunch, early evening, and closing time. Your DOOH creative should change with those phases. Most providers let you schedule by hour. Use it.

**Step 4: Ask the venue or event organizer about sponsorship packages.** This is the hack most guides skip. Many venues have leftover screen inventory during events. They'll sell it at a discount if you ask. I once booked screens inside a Nashville arena for a conference — the venue was charging $1,200 per week on the open market. The event organizer had a sponsorship package that included those same screens for $400. You don't know unless you ask.

Real example: A bagel shop in Washington D.C. (Call Your Mother in Park View) used this approach during a neighborhood street festival. They spent $700 on DOOH screens positioned at the two main pedestrian entry points. The ad showed a bagel sandwich with the tagline "Eat before the band starts." They ran it for three hours on the Saturday of the festival. They reported 200 incremental transactions that day, with an average ticket of $12. That's $2,400 in revenue from a $700 spend. The screens were booked through a local DOOH reseller who had leftover inventory from a larger beer brand's campaign.

## How to Measure Event DOOH ROI (Without the Agency Fluff)

Agencies love to talk about "impressions" and "brand lift." You're a small business owner. You need to know whether that $800 screen actually brought people through your door. Here's the only three metrics that matter for event DOOH at your level.

**Metric 1: Trackable coupon codes or QR scans.** This is non-negotiable. Every single event DOOH campaign should have a unique offer that cannot be found anywhere else. "Show this ad for 10% off your first appointment." "Scan the QR code for a free latte with any pastry." Put it on the screen. Track every single use. If your POS system allows promo codes (Square, Toast, Clover all do), this is a five-minute setup. If you don't track this, you have no idea if the DOOH worked.

**Metric 2: Foot traffic uplift during event hours.** Use your POS data to compare revenue during the event weekend vs. the same day of the week without an event. For example, if you're a pizza shop near a stadium, compare the Saturday of an NFL game to a Saturday when the team is away. Subtract your normal Saturday revenue from the game-day revenue. The difference — minus your cost of goods sold — is your DOOH contribution. You'll need to account for other factors (weather, competing events), but it's a solid directional number.

**Metric 3: Google My Business search activity during event windows.** Log into your GMB profile and check the "how customers search" section. Look for the day and time of the event. Are you seeing more "near me" searches? More direction requests? Those are people who saw something (maybe your DOOH, maybe a sign, maybe both) and responded. If your GMB listing has a call button, check call volume during event hours.

Real example from a business I advised: A frozen yogurt shop in Denver (Yogurtland on 16th Street) ran a DOOH campaign during the Great American Beer Festival. They used a unique QR code that linked to a coupon for a free topping. Over three days, they tracked 54 QR scans. At $6.50 average transaction, that's $351 in direct attributable revenue from a $500 campaign. But they also saw a 28% increase in total transactions during the festival weekend compared to the previous two weekends. The lift was larger than the coupon redemptions alone — meaning the DOOH ad drove awareness even among people who didn't scan. Combined, the weekend generated $1,800 more than a normal Saturday-Sunday. 

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a single coffee shop owner. Can I afford DOOH for events, or is this only for big brands?**

You can afford it if you pick the right screen and the right event. Most programmatic DOOH platforms let you buy inventory by the hour, not the month. A well-placed screen near a local event can cost $300–$800 for a weekend. That's less than a Facebook ad campaign that reaches the same city. The key is not to overspend on screens that are too far from your actual location. Start with one event, track everything, and scale if the numbers work.

**Q: How do I prove that someone saw my billboard and then came to my shop?**

You can't prove it with 100% certainty — no one can. But you can get close. Use a unique promo code or QR link that only appears on that specific DOOH screen. Track redemptions in your POS. If you see a spike in coupon use during event hours from customers you've never seen before, that's strong evidence. You can also ask every new customer for the first week after an event: "How did you hear about us?" Keep a tally.

**Q: What if my business isn't right next to the venue? Is it still worth it?**

It depends on the walking distance. If you're within a half-mile of the venue along a natural foot traffic route, yes. If you're a mile away and the event crowd doesn't walk in your direction, no. Test it by walking the route yourself or using foot traffic data from a tool like Gravy Analytics (free trial available). If you're in a city with a ride-share pickup zone near your shop, that can work too — people waiting for Ubers look at their phones and screens constantly.

**Q: Can I run DOOH for a single weekend, or do I need a monthly contract?**

Most programmatic DOOH platforms let you buy on a daily or hourly basis. You do not need a long-term contract. Platforms like Hivestack, Broadsign, and Vistar Media have self-serve options where you can upload creative, set a budget, and schedule around specific events. Some local DOOH networks (like billboard companies in your city) may require a minimum spend or a weekly commitment — ask before you buy.

**Q: How do I combine DOOH with my other ads (Google, social, email) for the same event?**

Treat DOOH as the trigger, and everything else as the catcher. Run a Google Ads campaign targeting location-based keywords during the event hours ("coffee near stadium," "hair salon near festival"). Run a Facebook or Instagram ad that retargets people within a 1-mile geofence of the venue for 24 hours after the event. Send an email blast to your local list the morning of the event with a reminder: "We're steps from the action — come see us." Each channel reinforces the others.

**Q: What's the minimum budget I should have to make event DOOH worth my time?**

$500 per event weekend is the realistic floor for most US cities. At that level, you can run a single screen for two days and include a trackable offer. If you're in a smaller city or a less competitive market, you might find inventory for $300. Anything under $200 is unlikely to generate enough impressions to matter. You want at least 5,000–10,000 targeted views during the event window.
