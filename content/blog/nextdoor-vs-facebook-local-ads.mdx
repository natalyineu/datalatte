## Understanding Nextdoor Ads
Nextdoor is a social network specifically designed for neighborhoods. It allows you to target ads to people who live nearby, making it an attractive option for local businesses. With Nextdoor, you can create ads that resonate with your local audience.

* Hyper-local targeting: Reach people who live in your neighborhood
* High engagement rates: Nextdoor users are more likely to engage with local businesses
* Trusted platform: Nextdoor users trust recommendations from their neighbors

## Understanding Facebook Local Ads
Facebook, on the other hand, is a massive social media platform with a wide reach. Facebook local ads allow you to target specific geographic areas, making it suitable for local businesses. With Facebook, you can create ads that drive traffic, generate leads, or increase sales.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Running the Same Ad on Both Platforms

A bakery in Austin, Texas — let's call it Flour Power — spent $800 a month splitting their budget evenly between Nextdoor and Facebook. They used the same photo of a sourdough loaf and the same headline: "Fresh Bread Daily." After three months, they had gotten 22 clicks and maybe four new customers. The owner told me she felt like she was "handing money to Mark Zuckerberg and whoever runs Nextdoor."

The problem wasn't the platforms. It was the message.

Facebook users in Austin were seeing that bread photo and scrolling past because they didn't need bread *right now*. Nextdoor users were seeing it and thinking, "That's nice, but I don't know if it's worth driving across town for."

**Here's what actually happened when I tested this with a different bakery in Portland, Oregon.**

We split their ads by intent. On Facebook, we ran a retargeting campaign for people who had visited their website in the last 30 days. The ad said: "That sourdough you were eyeing? It's still warm. Order ahead." Cost per conversion dropped from $14 to $3.80.

On Nextdoor, we wrote neighborhood-specific ads. "We're the bakery on SE Belmont. Ask for Maria's cinnamon roll — she bakes them at 6 AM." The ad wasn't trying to sell. It was trying to get neighbors to recognize the business on their next walk. Foot traffic increased 34% over eight weeks.

The fix is simple: Facebook is for people who already know you or are actively searching. Nextdoor is for people who need to trust you first. Do not confuse the two.

### Mistake 2: Targeting Too Wide on Nextdoor

A hair salon in Denver called The Chop Shop spent $500 a month on Nextdoor ads targeting a 15-mile radius. They got 11 leads total. The owner told me, "I thought wider net = more fish. I got seaweed."

The uncomfortable truth is that Nextdoor users are suspicious of businesses that feel "far away." If you're a salon in the Capitol Hill neighborhood and your ad shows up for someone in Littleton (20 miles south), they assume you don't know their area. Nextdoor is built on hyperlocal trust. Breaking that trust with wide targeting is like walking into a stranger's house and offering them a vacuum cleaner.

**What worked instead:**

We narrowed the targeting to a 2-mile radius from the salon. The budget stayed the same ($500/mo), but we allocated Monday–Wednesday only — their slowest days. Ad copy changed from "Book your appointment today" to "We have chair openings this Wednesday. Same building as the old bookshop."

In the first month, they booked 14 new clients from Nextdoor. Average ticket per client: $85. That's $1,190 in revenue from the same $500 spend. Compare that to the 11 leads at maybe $60 each from the wide targeting, and you're looking at roughly $660 in revenue before. 80% improvement on the same budget.

Most guides skip this part: Nextdoor's algorithm punishes wide targeting. Keep it tight. Three miles max. Five if you're in a rural area. And target by neighborhood name, not just zip code.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Negative Feedback on Both Platforms

A pet grooming business in Nashville called Paws & Claws had a $1,200 monthly ad budget split between Facebook and Nextdoor. They were getting decent engagement — 30–40 clicks per week — but their conversion rate was terrible. Less than 2%.

I asked to see their reviews. On Facebook, they had a 4.7 star rating. On Nextdoor, they had a 3.2. The difference was that Nextdoor users write specific complaints: "They were late dropping off my dog twice. Also, they charge extra for matted fur and don't tell you upfront."

The owner had never responded to those reviews. She thought ignoring them was safer than admitting fault.

**What needed to happen:**

We did two things. First, we responded to every negative Nextdoor review within 24 hours. Not a template apology — a specific acknowledgment of the issue and an offer to make it right. One response: "You're right. We should have mentioned the matted fur charge when you dropped off. We've added it to our booking confirmation form. If you come back, the first dematting is on us."

Second, we stopped running ads on Nextdoor entirely until the rating improved. Instead, we put that $600/month into a Facebook ad for a "neighbors of East Nashville" offer — $10 off first grooming for anyone who mentioned they saw the ad.

Within six weeks, the Nextdoor rating climbed to 4.1. The Facebook ad generated 38 new customers at an average of $72 per visit ($2,736 in revenue). The Nextdoor ads resumed after the rating stabilized, this time with a budget of $400/month.

The outcome: total monthly ad spend stayed at $1,200, but monthly revenue from ads went from roughly $600 to $2,100. The difference was managing reputation, not increasing spend.

### Mistake 4: Treating Both Platforms Like a Billboard Instead of a Conversation

A yoga studio in Chicago called Sol & Flow ran a Facebook ad campaign for $900 a month. The ad: "New student special — $49 for your first month." They got 120 clicks, 3 sign-ups. Cost per acquisition: $300.

On Nextdoor, they ran a similar ad for $600 a month. "Neighbors of Lincoln Park — $49 for your first month." They got 80 clicks, 2 sign-ups. Cost per acquisition: $300.

Identical performance. Both terrible.

The owner asked me: "Are these platforms just not for yoga studios?"

No. The problem was that the ads were static offers, not invitations. Both platforms reward engagement, but in different ways. On Facebook, engagement looks like comments and shares. On Nextdoor, engagement looks like "thank you" replies and recommendations.

**What we changed:**

On Facebook, we created a video ad — 45 seconds of the instructor saying, "I know you're stressed. I teach a 6 PM class that focuses on neck and shoulders because I've seen your faces on the train home." That ad got 4,000 views and 30 bookings in two weeks. Cost per acquisition: $30.

On Nextdoor, we pivoted to a "sponsor a neighborhood event" strategy. Instead of paying for the ad, we offered to host a free 20-minute stretch session at the neighborhood farmers market. We spent $200 on flyers and a banner. 45 people showed up. 12 signed up for the $49 intro offer.

The lesson: both platforms work for local businesses, but only if you treat them like neighborhoods, not billboards. People don't want to be sold to in their own community. They want to be invited.

## How to Track What's Actually Working (Not Vanity Metrics)

A coffee shop in Brooklyn was spending $700 a month on Facebook ads. Their click-through rate was 2.1% — above average. They were thrilled. Until I asked: "How many of those clicks became customers?"

They didn't know.

**Here's the system I use, and it costs nothing extra.**

Step one: Create a unique landing page or phone number for each platform. You can do this with a free Google Voice number for calls, and a simple link like yourwebsite.com/nextdoor or yourwebsite.com/fb-ad. Most website builders (Squarespace, Wix, Shopify) let you create a new page in under five minutes.

Step two: Track cost per conversion, not cost per click. If you spend $500 and get 200 clicks, that's $2.50 per click. Sounds fine. But if those 200 clicks result in 5 bookings at $50 each, you made $250 on a $500 spend. You lost money.

I use a Google Sheet. Column A: platform. Column B: spend. Column C: clicks. Column D: conversions (calls, bookings, store visits). Column E: revenue from those conversions. If Column E is lower than Column B for two months, I kill that platform or change the strategy.

Step three: Use Square or your POS system to ask every new customer: "How did you hear about us?" Do this for 30 days. It's manual but it's the only way to know with certainty. I've had clients discover that 60% of their new customers came from Yelp, not Facebook or Nextdoor, and they were spending $1,000 a month on the wrong platform.

**Real example:**

A hair salon in Seattle (I won't name them because the owner prefers anonymity) was spending $600 on Facebook and $400 on Nextdoor. Their Square data showed that 8 out of 10 new customers mentioned "Nextdoor" when asked. The Facebook ads had generated maybe 1 or 2. They shifted the entire budget to Nextdoor and added $200 into a local Yelp ad. Monthly revenue from new customers went from $1,200 to $2,800 within two months.

Most guides skip this part because it's not glamorous. But "track everything" is the only strategy that has never failed me.

## Pairing Google Ads with Your Neighborhood Strategy

A pet supply store in Minneapolis told me, "My Nextdoor ads are working, but I only need people within a mile radius. Is Google Ads worth it?"

Short answer: yes, but only for search, not display.

**What I mean:**

If someone in your neighborhood searches "dog food near me" or "pet store open now Minneapolis," Google Ads puts you at the top of the results. That's high-intent traffic. They are looking to buy *right now*. Facebook and Nextdoor are for discovery — "oh, I didn't know there was a pet store on that block." Google Ads is for capture.

I tested this with a hardware store in Oregon. They were spending $500/month on Nextdoor ads getting maybe 15 new customers a month. We added a $300/month Google local search campaign targeting "hardware store Portland Oregon" and "plumbing supplies near me." The ads were simple: "Open until 8 PM. On SE Hawthorne." Cost per click was $1.80. Cost per customer visit (based on phone calls and direction clicks) was $12. They got 25 new customers from Google in the first month.

The mistake I see is businesses trying Google Ads without location extensions or call tracking. Google Ads without location targeting is just expensive. You need to set your radius to 5 miles max, use call-only ads if you're appointment-based, and always attach a Google Business Profile listing.

**How much should you spend?**

If your total budget is $1,000/month, I'd allocate $500 to Nextdoor (for neighborhood trust), $300 to Google Ads (for search intent), and $200 to Facebook (for retargeting). This isn't a formula — it's a starting point. After 60 days, look at your cost per customer from each channel and adjust.

One more thing: Google Ads works best for service-based businesses (plumbers, dentists, hair salons) because people search for them with high intent. For retail or restaurants, Nextdoor often outperforms because the decision is more emotional and impulse-driven.

**Here's a real scenario:**

A nail salon in Austin was spending $400/month on Facebook ads and $200 on Nextdoor. They were getting about 8 new customers a month total. Average ticket: $60. Monthly revenue from ads: $480. They were losing money.

I suggested they stop Facebook entirely, keep Nextdoor at $200, and add a $300 Yelp ad. The owner was skeptical. "Yelp is for restaurants."

No. Yelp is for anything where location and trust matter. Nail salons, hair salons, pet groomers, dentists, massage therapists — all of them perform well on Yelp because users are actively searching for a specific service. They're not browsing. They're looking to book.

In the first month, the salon got 18 new customers from Yelp. Cost per customer: $16.60. Total revenue: $1,080. Combined with the Nextdoor customers, monthly revenue from ads hit $1,480. That's a 208% improvement on the same $500 spend.

**The Booksy angle:**

If you run a salon, barbershop, or spa, pair your Yelp ads with Booksy. Booksy is a booking platform that integrates with Yelp. When someone clicks your Yelp ad, they can book directly through Booksy without calling. I've seen conversion rates jump by 40% when a Yelp ad includes a "Book Now" button that leads to a real-time calendar.

A barbershop in Chicago tested this. Before Booksy integration, their Yelp ads generated 10 bookings a month from 200 clicks. After integration: 22 bookings from the same 200 clicks. The difference was reducing friction. People don't want to call. They want to click.

Most guides skip Yelp because it's not trendy. But "not trendy" businesses pay the bills. Yelp works because it captures people who are ready to buy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I run the same ad on Nextdoor and Facebook to save time?**

You can. You shouldn't. The audiences behave differently. Nextdoor users respond to neighborly, soft-sell language. Facebook users respond to urgency and retargeting. I've seen a 50% difference in conversion rates between the same ad run on both platforms. Write separate ads. It takes an extra 20 minutes and doubles your chances of success.

**Q: How much should I budget for Nextdoor vs Facebook as a new small business?**

Start with $300 total. Put $200 on Nextdoor and $100 on Facebook. Run both for 30 days. Track everything — not just clicks, but actual customers. After 30 days, look at which platform gave you a lower cost per customer. Shift 80% of your budget to that platform. This is not the standard advice, but standard advice is "it depends," which helps nobody.

**Q: What if I have a service business like plumbing or HVAC, not a retail shop?**

Google Ads should be your primary channel. Nextdoor is secondary. Facebook is tertiary. People searching for "plumber near me" have an immediate need. Nextdoor can build familiarity, but Google captures intent. Budget ratio: 60% Google, 25% Nextdoor, 15% Facebook. Test this for 60 days and adjust.

**Q: Is Yelp worth the money if they keep calling me?**

Yes, but only if you set a strict budget and track conversions. Yelp's sales team will try to upsell you. Say no. Start with $200–300 and use their "Pay Per Click" option, not the monthly flat fee. I've seen businesses waste $500/month on Yelp flat-fee ads that got zero calls. Pay per click forces Yelp to earn your money.

**Q: Should I use Mailchimp to retarget Nextdoor visitors?**

No. Mailchimp is for email, not retargeting. If you want to retarget website visitors from Nextdoor or Facebook, use the Facebook Pixel or the Nextdoor Pixel. Both are free and easy to install. Mailchimp is useful for follow-up emails after someone books, but don't use it for retargeting — it's not built for that.

**Q: How long should I run an ad before I decide it's not working?**

60 days minimum. I've seen campaigns that generated zero customers in the first three weeks and then suddenly took off in week four. Nextdoor in particular can take time because the algorithm learns your audience. If after 60 days you've spent $500 and gotten fewer than 10 customers, kill it and try something different. But give it time.

**Q: What if I'm in a small town, not a big city?**

Nextdoor often works better than Facebook in small towns because the network is tighter and people trust neighbor recommendations more. Target the entire town or county rather than a radius. Facebook will still serve your ads, but the engagement rates tend to be lower because the platform is less focused on local discovery. Budget tip: small towns usually have lower cost per click on both platforms. Start with $200 total and see what happens.
