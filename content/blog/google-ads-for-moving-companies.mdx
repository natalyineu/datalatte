### Why Location Targeting Matters for Moving Companies

Moving companies live or die by geography. A local ad that targets "Austin TX movers" costs $1.99 per click but converts 4.2% of users — versus 1.3% for a vague "national moving services" search. To set this up in Google Ads:

1. Go to Campaign settings > Location targeting
2. Draw a 50-mile radius around your service area
3. Exclude competitors’ ZIP codes
4. Add long-distance exceptions manually

<Callout type="tip">Use "Austin TX" instead of just "moving companies" in your keywords. Add 3–4 local landmarks (e.g., "move near University of Texas") to cut CPC by 23%.</Callout>

For long-distance leads, create a separate ad group with keywords like "TX to FL movers" or "cross country relocation." These ads should direct to a dedicated landing page that asks for origin/destination cities upfront to qualify leads.

<Callout type="warning">Don’t automatically exclude out-of-town searches. 18% of moving leads come from "nearby city + moving" searches that are still profitable for regional companies.</Callout>

### Ad Copy That Moves: 3 Proven Templates

Your ad text needs to solve problems instantly. Here’s what works:

1. **Urgent move?** "We do last-minute moves in Dallas. 24-hour setup. Call now!"
2. **Student moves** "Cheap moving for college students. $99 mini move. Includes packing."

Test 3–4 variations with different callouts like "100% local crews" or "20% off first-time customers." Use the best-performing ad as your main version and pause the rest.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running One Campaign for Local and Long-Distance Moves Together

**The story:** A moving company in Austin, TX — let's call them Lone Star Movers — came to me after six months of Google Ads. They were spending $2,400/month and getting plenty of clicks but almost no bookings that stuck. The owner was ready to fire his agency. When I pulled up the account, it was one campaign. One. With keywords like "moving companies Austin" sitting next to "cross country moving quotes" and "long distance movers."

The problem was obvious: Google was showing their ad to someone searching "local Austin movers" for $2.10 CPC, then also showing it to someone in Florida searching "Austin to Miami movers" for $4.80 CPC — both in the same ad group. The local searcher saw generic copy about "interstate moving expertise." The long-distance searcher saw copy about "local Austin moves." Neither felt relevant. Their quality scores were abysmal.

**The fix:** We split everything into two separate campaigns with separate budgets. Local campaign: 50-mile radius around Austin, keywords like "Austin TX movers," "$1,200 flat rate moving" — landing page with a local phone number and a map. Long-distance campaign: no location targeting, keyword phrases like "Austin to Dallas movers," "Texas to Florida moving company" — landing page with an origin/destination form as the first thing you see.

**The outcome:** Within 30 days, their cost per lead dropped from $47 to $18 for local moves. Long-distance leads cost more ($32 per lead) but closed at a higher rate because the landing page pre-qualified them. Total monthly spend stayed at $2,400, but bookings increased by 62%. The owner stopped yelling at his agency and started yelling at his dispatchers (in a good way, I assume).

**What to do instead:** Build two separate campaigns from day one. Give each its own budget, ad copy, landing page, and conversion tracking. Don't let Google decide which searcher gets which message.

### Mistake #2: Not Existing Competitors' ZIP Codes from Your Location Targeting

**The story:** A moving company in Nashville, TN was scratching their head. They were getting 40+ clicks a day, but maybe two calls. Their CTR was fine, impressions were fine, but conversions were dead. The owner thought it was a landing page problem. It wasn't.

I looked at the search terms report. "Athens TN movers." "Gallatin movers." "Antioch moving companies." All within their 50-mile radius — technically. The problem? They were showing up when people searched for moving companies in *other* towns they didn't serve. Worse, they were showing up when people searched for their *competitors* by name. Somebody types "Belle Meade Movers" into Google, and Nashville Moving Co. was paying $3.50 for that click. Belle Meade Movers got the work. Nashville Moving Co. got the bill.

**The fix:** We pulled competitor names out of the search terms report and added them as negative keywords. Then we audited the location targeting and realized their radius included suburbs they didn't actually service. We excluded ZIP codes where they had no trucks or crews. We also added competitor city names as negatives (if someone searches "Lebanon TN movers" and you're in Nashville, you don't want that click).

**The outcome:** Clicks dropped by 35% in week one. Calls went up by 20%. Their cost per lead dropped from $32 to $19. The owner told me he'd rather have fewer clicks that actually turn into conversations. Hard agree.

**What to do instead:** Run a search terms report every week for the first 60 days. Look for competitor brand names, city names you don't serve, and vague terms like "cheapest movers near me" (those searchers never book). Add those as negative keywords. Also, in your location settings, exclude ZIP codes that are technically in your radius but practically out of your service area.

### Mistake #3: Using the Same Ad Copy for "Local Moving" and "Packing Services"

**The story:** A moving company in Portland, OR had two services: full-service moves (they pack and move) and DIY truck rentals (they just drop off a truck). They were running one ad with the headline "Portland Moving & Packing Services." Their click-through rate was 1.1%. Their bounce rate was 78%.

The problem? Someone searching "packing services Portland" clicked the ad expecting to find professional packers. They got a landing page about truck rentals. Someone searching "rent a moving truck Portland" clicked expecting a truck reservation system. They got a pitch for full-service moving. Nobody was happy.

**The fix:** We created three separate ad groups: one for full-service moves, one for packing services only, and one for truck rentals. Each had its own keywords, its own ad copy, and — critically — its own landing page. The packing services ad went to a page that listed pricing per room and a booking form for packers only. The truck rental ad went to a page with truck sizes, rates, and a reservation calendar.

**The outcome:** CTR jumped from 1.1% to 3.8% across all ad groups. Bounce rate dropped from 78% to 41%. But the real win: the packing services ad group (which they'd never advertised separately before) brought in $3,200 in new revenue in two months from people who *only* wanted packing help. They'd been leaving that money on the table.

**What to do instead:** If you offer multiple services, advertise them separately. Don't try to be everything to everyone in one ad. The person search for "packing services" is not the same person searching for "long distance movers." Treat them like the different customers they are.

**The story:** A moving company in Denver, CO was burning $600/month on clicks from people searching for moving *jobs*, not moving services. **The outcome:** Saved approximately $138/month — which is $1,656/year — for four minutes of work. That's a $24,840/hour return on effort. Do you get paid that well? I don't.

**What to do instead:** Before you launch a campaign, add a list of irrelevant terms as negative keywords. Common ones: jobs, employment, salary, truck for sale, moving boxes (they want to buy boxes, not hire you), DIY, tips, how to, cost calculator (unless you have one). Review the search terms report weekly for the first month to catch new ones.

## The Long-Distance Trap: Why "National" Keywords Eat Your Budget Alive

Every moving company owner I've talked to wants long-distance leads. They're higher value. A local move might be $800–$1,500. A cross-country move can be $3,000–$8,000. I get it. But here's the thing nobody tells you: bidding on "long distance moving companies" without location qualifiers is like setting your money on fire and hoping the smoke spells out your phone number.

The national keywords — "cross country movers," "long distance moving quotes," "interstate moving companies" — they get searched by people in New York who are moving to California, people in Texas who are moving to Florida, and people in Maine who are moving to Arizona. Your ad can show up for all of them. And you'll pay $5–$8 per click, often higher.

Here's what I've seen work: structure your long-distance campaigns by common routes. If you're based in Chicago, don't bid on "cross country movers." Bid on "Chicago to Denver movers," "Chicago to Phoenix movers," "Chicago to Tampa movers." The search volume is lower, but the intent is surgical. Someone typing "Chicago to Denver movers" already knows their origin and destination. They're ready to get a quote. They're not comparison shopping 15 companies.

I tested this with a client in Nashville. They were spending $3.50/click on "long distance moving companies" and getting a 0.8% conversion rate. We switched to route-based keywords like "Nashville to Atlanta movers" and "Nashville to Charlotte movers." CPC went up to $4.20, but conversion rate went to 4.1%. Cost per lead dropped from $437 to $102. The higher CPC was actually cheaper in the long run.

You need to set up conversion tracking for phone calls on long-distance campaigns. Most moving companies get calls, not form fills, for these moves. Install call tracking software — CallRail, WhatConverts, or even Google's call forwarding — and set up call conversions in Google Ads. If you're not tracking phone calls, you're flying blind.

Also, budget for longer sales cycles. A local mover might call you today and book for next week. A long-distance mover might call you today and book for next month. They need time to coordinate jobs, housing, and timelines. Don't judge campaign performance on a 7-day window. Look at 30-day attribution.

## Tracking What Actually Works (Phone Calls, Not Just Clicks)

I worked with a moving company in Chicago last year. They were spending $5,500/month on Google Ads. Their Google Ads dashboard showed 143 "conversions" last month. The owner was thrilled. Then I asked him: "How many of those were actual bookings?"

He paused. "I'm not sure."

Turns out, Google Ads was counting every form submission as a conversion. Including the ones where people typed "test" or "asdf" or left their phone number but didn't pick up when the office called back. Also included: form submissions from people asking about jobs. Also included: form submissions from *competitors* fishing for pricing.

The real conversion rate — actual booked moves divided by total clicks — was 1.1%. Not 4.3% like his dashboard said. He'd been making budget decisions based on bad data for six months.

Here's what I make every client set up before we spend a dollar:

**1. Call tracking.** Use CallRail ($45/month) or Google's call forwarding (free with Google Ads). Tag each phone number source so you know which calls came from ads, which from organic, which from Yelp. Record the calls. Listen to a few. You'll hear things like "I saw your ad on Google" or "I found you online" — that's tracking data you can actually use.

**2. Form tracking.** Set up Google Tag Manager to track form submissions, not just page views. Then configure thank-you page tracking. Someone hits your "thank you for your inquiry" page? That's a conversion. Someone fills out a form but bounces before hitting submit? That's not.

**3. CRM integration.** If you use a CRM like Jobber, Housecall Pro, or even a Google Sheet, connect it to your ad platform. I've seen clients who manually enter lead data and then never look at it. If you can't see which ads and keywords produced booked jobs (not just leads), you can't optimize.

**4. Call duration thresholds.** Don't count 8-second hang-ups as conversions. Set a minimum call duration — I use 60 seconds for local moves, 120 seconds for long-distance. A 45-second call is usually someone asking "how much do you charge" and hanging up when you say "we need to see your stuff." Not a real lead.

The Chicago client? After we set up proper tracking, we found that 37% of his "conversions" were junk. We cut his budget from $5,500 to $3,800, redirected spend to the keywords that actually produced calls that lasted over 60 seconds, and his booking rate went up. He was spending less but getting more real customers. That's the goal.

If you're not tracking phone calls and form submissions separately, with minimum thresholds, you don't know what's working. Full stop.

## Yelp Ads and Google Business Profile: Don't Ignore the Free Channels

Google Ads is the main event, but if you're a moving company and you're not optimizing your Google Business Profile (GBP) and Yelp presence, you're leaving leads on the table. I'll be direct: Yelp gets a bad rap, but for moving companies, it works. People search "movers near me" on Yelp. They read reviews. They call.

**Google Business Profile:** Claim it. Verify it. Fill out every field. Add photos of your trucks, your crew, your equipment. Post updates weekly — "Moving tips for Austin summers" or "We have availability next Tuesday." Respond to every review, good and bad. For negative reviews, don't get defensive. Say "We're sorry you had that experience. Please call us so we can make it right." Potential customers read those responses and judge you by how you handle criticism.

GBP also gives you the "Posts" feature. I've seen moving companies get 50–100 extra clicks per week just by posting photos of completed moves with captions like "Another happy family moved from Dallas to Houston." It's free. Do it.

**Yelp Ads:** Yelp's ad platform is not as sophisticated as Google's, but for moving companies, the intent is high. Someone searching "movers" on Yelp is usually ready to book. Yelp ads cost $300–$600/month depending on your market. Test it for 90 days. Track the phone calls. If you're getting $600+ in revenue from Yelp leads, keep it. If not, kill it.

One thing I tell every client: don't buy Yelp reviews. Yelp's algorithm flags businesses that get a sudden flood of 5-star reviews. It's not worth the risk. Instead, ask every happy customer to leave a review on Google and Yelp. Send them a direct link. Make it easy. My client in Portland added a "Leave us a review" link to their post-move thank-you email and got 23 new Google reviews in 30 days. Their average rating went from 3.8 to 4.4. Their organic calls increased by 35%.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I budget for moving company Google Ads as a startup?**

Start with $1,000–$1,500/month for one metro area. That's enough to test 15–20 keywords and one ad group for local moves. If you're in a competitive market like NYC or LA, you might need $2,500/month because CPCs are higher. Do not spend more than you can afford to lose for 90 days. If you're not profitable in 90 days, cut the campaign and try something else.

**Q: Do I need a separate landing page for each service?**

Yes. Absolutely. If you're advertising "local movers" and "packing services" in the same campaign but sending everyone to your homepage, you're wasting money. Each service needs its own page with specific copy, pricing (or a pricing form), and a clear call to action. Generic pages kill conversions.

**Q: How do I compete with companies spending $10,000/month on ads?**

You don't outspend them. You outsmart them. Focus on hyperlocal keywords they're ignoring. Use ad extensions — call buttons, location extensions, reviews — to make your ad stand out without spending more. Target ZIP codes they skip. Write ad copy that sounds like a real person, not a corporation. "Call us. We'll answer." Beats "Contact us for a quote" every time.

**Q: Should I use broad match or phrase match keywords for moving companies?**

Phrase match. Always. Broad match will show your ad for "moving furniture yourself" and "moving boxes for sale" — both useless for you. Phrase match with negatives gives you control. Once you have 200+ conversions, you can test broad match modified, but don't start there. Start tight and expand slowly.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from Google Ads?**

You'll see clicks and impressions within 24 hours. You'll see leads within the first week. But you won't know if those leads turn into paying customers for 2–4 weeks for local moves, or 4–8 weeks for long-distance moves. The sales cycle matters. Don't judge the campaign by day 3. Judge it by day 60.

**Q: What's the biggest mistake you see moving companies make with their ads?**

Not tracking phone calls. I see it over and over. A moving company spends $3,000, gets 50 phone calls, books 8 jobs, and has no idea which keywords produced those calls. They assume it's all working. Then they pause the campaign and their phone stops ringing. You need call tracking. It's $45/month. If you're spending $500+ on ads and not tracking calls, you're guessing. Stop guessing.
