## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve managed Google Ads for roofing contractors in three different agencies before starting DataLatte. I’ve seen the same handful of mistakes kill campaigns over and over. Here are the ones that cost real money.

### Mistake #1: Bidding On “Storm Damage” Without a Safety Net

**The story from Austin, TX**

A roofer in Austin — let’s call his business Texas Peak Roofing (not his real name, but he’ll recognize himself) — came to me in March 2022. He’d spent $2,400 in the previous 90 days on Google Ads. He’d gotten 11 calls. Two of them were actual leads. One was a wrong number. The other eight were people asking if he could patch a single shingle for free because “the storm last week should be covered by insurance, right?”

He was bidding aggressively on “storm damage repair Austin” and “hail damage roofer.” Every time a spring storm rolled through, his costs-per-click would spike to $18. Then he’d get five calls from people who wanted him to file insurance claims on their behalf for free. He wasn’t charging for estimates. He was basically working as unpaid customer service for the insurance industry.

**What went wrong**

He was competing against the same 20 roofers for the same storm-chaser keywords during peak demand. His Quality Score was 4/10 because his landing page was a generic homepage with no mention of storm work. He had no negative keywords excluding “free estimate” or “insurance claim help.” Every click cost him $18, and 80% of the calls were unqualified.

**The fix**

We rebuilt his campaign with three ad groups:
1. **Emergency storm response** — only active during active weather alerts, bid capped at $12/click
2. **Year-round repair and maintenance** — separate budget, lower bids ($5–7/click), targeting “roof repair Austin” and “leak detection”
3. **Branded and service-area terms** — his company name plus specific neighborhoods

We added negative keywords: free, insurance, claim, adjuster, patch, DIY, how to, estimate (for his paid version — he offered a free estimate separately).

**The outcome**

Within 60 days, he was spending $1,100/month instead of $2,400. His cost-per-lead dropped from $218 to $47. His phone started ringing with people who wanted to book a repair, not a tutorial on how to file a claim. He made $3,800 in revenue from those first two months of the new campaign. The old campaign was burning money on people who were never going to hire him.

If you’re a roofer in any US city and you’re bidding on storm keywords without a landing page that says “We handle storm damage claims from start to finish,” you’re paying for clicks from people who will call three roofers and pick the cheapest. Don’t do that.

### Mistake #2: Targeting Everyone Within 50 Miles

**The story from Denver, CO**

I worked with a Denver roofer who set up his own Google Ads. He targeted a 50-mile radius around the city center. He was getting clicks. He was getting calls. But the calls were from people in Boulder, Colorado Springs, and even a guy in Castle Rock who wanted a quote for a barn roof.

The problem: his crew only worked within the Denver metro area. He couldn’t service Boulder (it’s 45 minutes without traffic). He couldn’t do Colorado Springs (over an hour). He wasted $1,200 in three weeks on clicks from people he couldn’t actually work for.

He didn’t know that Google Ads location targeting isn’t a hard line by default. Google shows your ads to “people interested in your location” — which includes people who live 100 miles away but happened to search for “roofer Denver” while sitting at home in Fort Collins. Google was happy to take his money. The people in Fort Collins were happy to click. Nobody was happy except Google.

**What went wrong**

He set location targeting to “people in or regularly in your targeted location” instead of “people in your targeted location.” Google defaults to the broader option. He also didn’t exclude ZIP codes where he didn’t have crew capacity.

**The fix**

We narrowed to a 15-mile radius around his physical address. We changed location targeting to “presence” only — meaning people must physically be in that area when they search. We excluded ZIP codes where he couldn’t service within 48 hours. We added location bid adjustments: +15% for neighborhoods where he’d already done good work and had referrals.

**The outcome**

His click volume dropped by 40%, but his lead quality went up 200%. His cost-per-valid-lead went from $94 to $38. He stopped getting calls from people in Boulder. His crew started working more efficiently because they weren’t driving across three counties for a single inspection. Within 30 days, he’d recovered the $1,200 he wasted and was making $2,100 in booked jobs from the new campaign.

**The uncomfortable truth** : Google Ads wants to show your ads to as many people as possible. That is not the same thing as showing them to people who can actually hire you. If you’re not manually tightening your location targeting, you’re burning money.

### Mistake #3: Using Broad Match Keywords and Wondering Why You’re Getting Crap Leads

**The story from Nashville, TN**

I talked to a roofer in Nashville who was running a campaign with exactly one keyword: “roofing services.” Broad match. He was getting 300+ clicks a month and maybe two jobs. He said Google Ads didn’t work for roofers.

I looked at his search terms report. He was showing up for:
- “roofing services for churches”
- “how much does a metal roof cost DIY”
- “roofer salary Nashville”
- “flat roof repair mobile home”
- “cheap roofing near me”

He was paying $7–12 per click for people searching for content, DIY advice, and job salary data. He was also paying for “cheap roofing” searches — which meant he was competing against the lowest-end roofers in town. His average click cost was $9.50, and his conversion rate (actual booked inspection) was 0.4%.

**What went wrong**

Broad match on a single keyword with no negative keywords, no phrase match, no exact match. He was trusting Google to figure out what “roofing services” meant to a homeowner in Nashville. Google figured it meant “anyone who types the word roofing and lives within 500 miles.”

**The fix**

We built a keyword structure with:
- **Exact match**: [roof repair Nashville], [roof replacement Nashville], [roof inspection Nashville]
- **Phrase match**: “roof repair near me”, “emergency roof repair”, “residential roofing contractor”
- **Negative keywords**: DIY, how to, cost, price, cheap, free, job, salary, church, commercial, flat roof, mobile home, metal roof, installation

**The outcome**

Click volume dropped from 300/month to roughly 80/month. But every click was from someone who actually wanted to hire a residential roofer in Nashville. His cost-per-lead went from $312 to $89. His conversion rate from click to booked job went from 0.4% to 3.8%. Within 90 days, he was getting consistent work from the campaign instead of wondering why his phone wasn’t ringing.

If you’re using broad match without a search terms report review every 7 days, you are paying for traffic Google thinks you want, not traffic that actually hires you.

## How to Track What Actually Matters (Calls, Not Clicks)

Most roofing business owners I talk to look at clicks and impressions. They see “1,200 clicks! The campaign is working!” Then they realize they booked three jobs and want to know where the money went.

Here’s what I do with every roofer client now. It’s not complicated. It’s just not what Google’s default dashboard shows you.

**Use Google call tracking, but don’t trust it alone.**

Google Ads has a call reporting feature that tracks calls from your ad extensions. But it only tracks calls that come directly from the ad — not calls from your website, not calls people make after leaving a voicemail. If a homeowner sees your ad, clicks through, reads your site, then calls you from a saved bookmark an hour later — Google doesn’t count that.

I recommend pairing Google Ads with a dedicated call tracking service like CallRail or WhatConverts. You put a unique phone number on your Google Ads landing page. Any call to that number gets recorded, transcribed, and tagged with the keyword and campaign that drove it. CallRail costs about $45/month for the basic plan. A roofer spending $1,500/month on ads can justify $45 to know exactly which keywords are producing real conversations.

**Connect your CRM or booking system.**

If you’re using Square for invoices, or Jobber or Housecall Pro for scheduling, connect it to your Google Ads account. You can import offline conversions. That means when a call turns into a booked job and you enter the invoice in your CRM, Google learns that “roof repair Nashville” actually produced $2,400 in revenue. Then its algorithm starts bidding more aggressively on that keyword and less on “storm damage claim” because it knows which one pays the bills.

I had a roofer in Portland, OR, who did this. He was spending $800/month on a campaign that was getting him calls. But he didn’t know which calls turned into jobs until he connected his Jobber account. Once he did, he saw that 70% of his revenue came from “roof inspection” and “leak repair” keywords, not “storm damage.” He shifted 60% of his budget to those keywords. His revenue per month from Google Ads went from $2,100 to $4,300.

**Track your close rate by source.**

Here’s a number most roofers don’t know: the percentage of calls or form fills from Google Ads that actually turn into a booked and paid job. If you’re tracking this, you can calculate your true cost per acquisition.

Example: You spend $1,500/month. You get 40 calls. 20 turn into estimates. 10 turn into jobs. Average job value is $2,800. That means you spent $1,500 to generate $28,000 in revenue — a 18.7x return. That’s a damn good campaign.

But if only 2 of those 40 calls turn into jobs, you spent $1,500 for $5,600 in work — a 3.7x return, which isn’t great after your materials and labor costs.

You can’t fix what you don’t measure. Google doesn’t tell you close rates. You have to ask your team or yourself every week: “Out of the 10 calls we got from ads this week, how many became paying customers?” Write it down. It takes three minutes.

## How to Win Year-Round Work With Retargeting and Maintenance Campaigns

Storm season is a spike. If you’re only advertising when people have emergencies, you’re leaving money on the table for nine months of the year.

**Retargeting is cheap and it works.**

Roofing has a long consideration cycle. A homeowner looks at your site on a Tuesday. They’re not ready to book. They want to think about it, compare prices, ask their neighbor. If you don’t retarget them, they’ll forget your name by Friday.

Google Ads retargeting costs about $0.20–$0.50 per click. It serves ads to people who already visited your site. If they looked at your “roof replacement” page, show them an ad that says “Free roof inspection — schedule online in 30 seconds.” If they looked at “storm damage,” show them “We handle insurance claims directly.”

I ran a retargeting campaign for a roofer in Chicago. He had 1,400 unique visitors to his site in March (pre–storm season). We spent $350 on retargeting for 90 days. Those 1,400 visitors saw his ads for three months. He got 52 return visits — and 12 of those turned into booked inspections. Seven of those became jobs. Total revenue from retargeting alone: $19,600. Cost: $350.

Most small business owners skip retargeting because they don’t want to “annoy” people. I promise you: someone who looked at your roofing services page and then saw your reminder ad two weeks later is not annoyed. They’re relieved you made it easy to find you again.

**Maintenance campaigns fill the slow months.**

The roofer who only advertises during storm season is the roofer who spends two months stressed and ten months broke.

Here’s a campaign structure I’ve used for clients in Minneapolis, St. Louis, and Charlotte:

Create a separate campaign for “roof maintenance” and “roof inspection.” Target keywords like:
- “roof inspection near me”
- “annual roof checkup”
- “roof maintenance plan”
- “gutter cleaning and roof check”

Bid lower on these — maybe $4–6/click instead of $10–12 for emergency terms. Budget $200–300/month. Offer a seasonal maintenance package: $149 for a full inspection, photo report, and small fixes (resealing a vent, replacing a loose shingle).

The maintenance campaign does two things:
1. It keeps cash flow consistent during non-storm months.
2. It makes you the first call when a real problem happens — because the homeowner already knows you, already paid you, and already has your number saved.

A roofer in Minneapolis ran a maintenance campaign from May through September (their quiet season). He spent $1,200 total across five months. He got 43 inspections booked. 18 of those turned into minor repair jobs ($200–600 each). Four turned into full roof replacements ($8,000–12,000 each). The maintenance campaign alone generated $52,000 in revenue from $1,200 in ad spend.

**Yelp Ads can work for roofers — if you set them up right.**

Yelp Ads are controversial. Some small business owners hate them. I’ve seen roofers waste money on Yelp because they didn’t understand how the bidding worked. But if you’re a roofer in a city where Yelp is dominant for home services (Portland, Austin, San Francisco, Boston), it can be worth testing.

The key: Yelp charges per click, but the click includes phone calls from your Yelp page. If someone calls you from Yelp, that’s a click. You pay for it. So you need to track those calls separately and understand your cost per lead.

I tested Yelp Ads for a roofer in San Diego. We set a $500/month budget. We used a tracking number to separate Yelp calls from Google calls. First month: $500 spent, 27 clicks, 4 actual calls. Two calls were wrong numbers. One was a guy asking if they do commercial flat roofs (they didn’t). One was a legitimate lead for a residential tile roof repair — which turned into a $3,200 job.

That one lead made the campaign worthwhile, but only barely. We optimized: added negative keywords (commercial, flat roof, metal), changed photos to show residential work only, and adjusted bid to $4/click max. Second month: $500 spent, 23 clicks, 7 calls. Two wrong numbers, one commercial inquiry, four legitimate residential leads. Two turned into jobs: $1,800 and $2,400.

Yelp isn’t for every roofer. But if you’re in a competitive metro and your Google Ads campaign is already optimized, testing Yelp with a small budget and strict tracking is fine. Just don’t set it and forget it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I just set up Google Ads myself and save the management fee?**

You can, and many roofers do. Then they call me six months later wondering why they spent $3,000 and got 12 calls. Google Ads is not hard to set up. It’s hard to set up well. The difference between a self-managed campaign and a managed one is usually $200–500/month in management fees versus $1,000+ wasted on bad keywords, wrong locations, and unqualified clicks. I’ve seen self-managed campaigns with 8% wasted spend on search terms that had nothing to do with roofing. That’s money you don’t get back.

If you want to do it yourself, you can. But read the search terms report every week. Add negative keywords constantly. Check your location targeting. Don’t trust broad match. And realize that Google’s “smart” bidding works best when you have conversion data — which you won’t have for the first 30–60 days.

**Q: How much should I budget for Google Ads as a roofer?**

I recommend $800–$1,500/month for a single location roofer in a metro area. If you’re in a competitive market like Atlanta or Denver, start at $1,200. If you’re in a smaller market like Boise or Knoxville, start at $600. The budget should cover at least 80–100 clicks per month to get enough data to optimize. If you spend $300/month and get 20 clicks, you won’t have statistically meaningful data for 90 days. Spend enough to learn fast, then scale.

**Q: What if I’m in a small town and there aren’t many searches?**

Focus on your service area and add proximity-based targeting. If you’re in a town of 5,000 people but you’re willing to drive 30 minutes, target that full radius. Also consider Google Local Services Ads — they charge per lead instead of per click, and they show up above traditional Google Ads. They’re less flexible but can be cheaper for small service areas. And don’t ignore the next town over. Many small town roofers only target their own ZIP code. If you target the next three towns, you might double your search volume.

**Q: I tried Google Ads before and got junk leads. Why would it work now?**

Because you probably set it up wrong the first time. I don’t say that to be rude — it’s true. Most roofers who try Google Ads once use broad match, skip negative keywords, target 50 miles, and have a generic homepage as a landing page. Then they get calls from people who want free estimates or DIY advice, not people who want to pay for a repair. A properly built campaign with phrase match keywords, exclusions, a dedicated landing page, and call tracking will produce different results. The problem isn’t Google Ads. It’s how you used it.

**Q: Should I run ads year-round or only during storm season?**

Run ads year-round, but adjust the budget. During storm season (typically spring and fall in most US regions), increase your budget by 50–80% and prioritize emergency terms. During slow months (summer in the south, winter in the north), reduce your budget to $300–500/month and focus on maintenance and inspection keywords. The roofer who pauses ads entirely for six months loses all the momentum and data Google has built. You’ll spend more to restart than you would to keep a small campaign running.

**Q: How do I compete with bigger roofing companies that have higher budgets?**

You don’t try to outspend them. You out-niche them. Bid on neighborhood-specific keywords: “roof repair in West Nashville” instead of “roof repair Nashville.” Target service-area terms that bigger companies ignore: “flat roof repair” if you do it, “metal roof specialist,” “historic home roofer.” You can also compete on landing page quality — a simple page with your photo, your certifications, and a clear phone number beats a generic corporate page every time. A prospect does not care about the big company’s 50 years of history if they can’t find your phone number in 10 seconds.
