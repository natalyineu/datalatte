## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Only Posting “Perfect” Grooms

I saw this at **Paws & Co. in Austin, TX**. The owner, Rachel, had 3,200 followers and was posting three times a week — all pristine after-shots. Groomed poodles, immaculate schnauzers, a golden retriever that looked like a cloud. Beautiful photos. She was getting maybe 25 likes and one comment per post.

Here’s what was actually happening: her ideal clients — the ones with anxious dogs, matted rescue mutts, or senior pets — didn’t see themselves in that feed. They thought, “My dog would never look like that. She only takes perfect dogs.”

**The fix:** Rachel started posting one “ugly duckling” transformation per week. A shih tzu with mats so bad the owners couldn’t see its eyes. A Great Pyrenees that hadn’t been brushed in six months. She showed the matted fur, the dog’s discomfort, then the shaved-down result with a note: “This is what happens when grooming gets postponed. No judgment — we’re here to help.”

**Outcome:** Within six weeks, her DM inquiries went from 3/week to 14/week. She raised her prices by $15 across the board. Her revenue increased by roughly $1,200/month. The “ugly” posts got shared 8x more than the “pretty” ones.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Google My Business Completely

**Barks & Bubbles in Nashville, TN** had a beautiful Instagram. 5,000 followers. Great content. But the owner, Marcus, told me he was “getting no new clients” and had to run Facebook ads just to stay afloat.

I asked him to pull up his Google My Business listing. It had a default photo of the front door (blurry), two reviews from 2019, and the business hours listed wrong. He was paying $400/month on Instagram ads to send people to a Google listing that told them he closed at 5pm on Saturdays (he actually closed at 3pm).

**The fix:** We updated the GMB profile in one afternoon. Added 15 photos of actual grooms, answered every Q&A, and set up a Google Posts rotation — one before/after per week with a “Book Now” button. We also ran a two-week push asking his top 20 clients to leave reviews (no incentive, just a direct text).

**Outcome:** Organic Google search calls went from 4/week to 22/week. He stopped running Facebook ads entirely within 30 days. Saved $1,200 in ad spend over three months, and those lost calls turned into about $3,800 in new grooming revenue.

### Mistake #3: Posting Too Salesy, Too Often

**The Groom Room in Portland, OR** was posting a “deal” every single day. “30% off first groom!” “$10 off nail trims!” “Refer a friend and save $5!” The owner, Jenna, had 6,800 followers but her engagement rate was 0.4%. She was burning through her audience’s goodwill like cheap coffee.

**The problem:** People don’t follow pet groomers for discounts. They follow for the dogs. Every time she led with a deal, she trained her audience to ignore her until the next coupon came. When she stopped offering discounts for a month during the experiment, her DMs actually went *up* — because people finally saw her as a groomer, not a Groupon.

**The fix:** We cut the sales posts to one per week, max. Replaced them with: a “meet the groomer” series, a “question of the week” (best way to brush a doodle?), and a “client story” post each Friday. The Friday posts were just photos of a dog with a two-sentence story about their personality.

**Outcome:** Over 8 weeks, her engagement rate went from 0.4% to 4.1%. Post reach tripled. She got 17 referral DMs in one month — people tagging their friends saying “this is our groomer, she’s the best.” She didn’t run a single discount that entire month.

## When to Actually Run Paid Ads (And When to Skip Them)

Most pet groomers who try Instagram ads waste money. I’ve seen it at three different clients. Here’s the pattern: $500/month on “boost post” with zero targeting strategy. Results: a few likes, no bookings, they quit after two months and declare “Instagram ads don’t work.”

The truth is, **Instagram ads work — but only for the right content and the right audience.**

### The Only Two Ad Types You Should Run

**1. Local Awareness** — Target a 10-mile radius around your shop. Use a video of a real groom (not stock footage). Run it for 7 days at $10/day. Budget: $70 total. Track it with a unique booking link or a promo code. If you get 3+ bookings, scale it. If you get 0, kill it and change the creative.

**Example:** A groomer in **Denver, CO** ran this for two weeks. She targeted pet owners who followed three local dog parks and a local vet. Her cost per booking was $8.50. She booked 12 new clients. Revenue: roughly $840. Ad spend: $140. Net: $700.

**2. Retargeting** — People who visited your Instagram profile but didn’t book. Run a simple carousel: “Still looking for a groomer?” with three photos of happy dogs. Target only people in your city who have engaged with your profile in the last 30 days. Budget: $5/day.

**Outcome for a groomer in **Chicago**: She retargeted 340 profile visitors over two weeks. 19 people clicked “Book Now.” 7 became new clients. The campaign cost $70 total. Her average groom is $65. That’s $455 in new revenue from a $70 spend.

### When to Absolutely *Not* Run Ads

- If you have fewer than 3 Google reviews. Fix that first.
- If your last post was more than 10 days ago. Ads sending people to a dead profile is a waste.
- If you can’t answer the phone during business hours. I watched a groomer in **Phoenix** spend $600 on ads while she had “do not disturb” on. She missed 6 calls. She blamed the ad platform.

## The Review Funnel: Turning One Groom Into Five Referrals

Most groomers ask for a review once and move on. That’s like fishing with one cast and giving up.

Here’s a system that works, tested at **Pawsitively Clean in Raleigh, NC**. The owner, Sarah, had 47 total Google reviews after three years in business. Six months later, she had 214. Her appointments went from 18/week to 34/week.

### The System (Free Tools Only)

**Step 1: Square for checkout.** Every client gets an emailed receipt automatically. Sarah set up the Square receipt to include a one-sentence request: “Loved your groom? Leave us a review here → [link to Google].” That alone added 6-8 reviews per month.

**Step 2: Mailchimp automated email.** Three days after the groom, a short email goes out: “We hope Fluffy is still feeling fresh! If you’re happy, would you mind sharing a photo and a sentence? We read every one.” 15% of recipients responded. She posts these as user-generated content (with permission) on Instagram.

**Step 3: The referral card.** Printed on 4x6 card stock (50 cards cost $12 on Vistaprint). Hand to every client at pickup: “Give this to a friend and you both get $10 off your next groom.” Track the unique code. Sarah saw 38 referrals in the first 90 days. At an average groom value of $55, that’s $2,090 in new revenue. The $10 discount cost her $380. Net gain: $1,710.

**Why this works better than Instagram alone:** a Google review from a local client is worth about 8x more than an Instagram like. A referral from a friend is worth about 20x more. Instagram gets people to *notice* you. This system gets people to *book* you.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need Instagram for my pet grooming business? Or can I just use Google and word of mouth?**

You can survive without it, but you’re leaving money on the table. Instagram is the best free tool for showing proof of work — and pet grooming is a visual business. That said, if you hate making content, you don’t need to go viral. Post one before/after per week. That’s it. Consistency beats volume. I’d rather see a groomer with 1,000 followers who posts every Tuesday than one with 10,000 who posts once a month.

**Q: How often should I actually post? I keep hearing “every day” and I can’t do that.**

You don’t need to post every day. Three times a week is enough to grow. Twice a week is enough to maintain. Once a week is enough to stay visible. The algorithm rewards consistency, not frequency. Pick a schedule you can keep for six months without hating your life.

**Q: Should I use TikTok instead?**

Not instead. In addition, maybe. But here’s the reality: the average TikTok user in the US spends 95 minutes a day on the app and rarely books a service directly from it. Instagram’s booking flow is better. TikTok is great for brand awareness. If you have 20 minutes a week to make a short video, post it to both. But don’t let TikTok distract you from the things that actually drive bookings — Google reviews, a proper booking system, and a clean Instagram grid.

**Q: How do I get more reviews without feeling like I’m begging?**

Stop asking in person. People feel awkward saying no to your face. Send a text or email link after the appointment. Use Square, Booksy, or Mailchimp to automate it. The key is timing — send the request within 2 hours of pickup, while the dog still smells good and the owner is happy. Also, don’t ask for a “review” — ask for a “photo of your dog looking fresh.” Reviews come naturally when you have happy clients showing off their pets.

**Q: I tried Instagram and got zero results for three months. What did I do wrong?**

You probably posted without a strategy. Posting a photo and hoping for the best is like handing out flyers in a parking lot and expecting people to walk into your shop. You need to: (1) use location tags, (2) engage with local accounts (vet clinics, dog parks, pet stores), (3) use stories to show real-time work, (4) include a clear call to action in every post — not just “like this photo” but “DM to book your spot.” If you did all that for three months and still got nothing, your photos might not be good enough. Invest $50 in a ring light and learn basic photo editing. Grooming is visual. Bad photos = no engagement.

**Q: What’s the one thing I should do this week that will actually make a difference?**

Fix your Google My Business listing. Update the hours. Add 10 photos. Reply to every review (even the bad ones — professionally). Then ask three happy clients for a review using a direct text message. This takes 45 minutes total and will generate more new clients than 20 Instagram posts.
