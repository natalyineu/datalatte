### Rule #1: Get permission before sending SMS
Before you can even think about sending SMS, you need to have a list of subscribers who have explicitly opted-in to receive messages from you. This is non-negotiable.

### Rule #2: Keep it short and sweet
Your SMS should be concise, clear, and to the point. Aim for under 160 characters and avoid using jargon or overly technical terms.

**Tip:** Keep your SMS focused on a single call-to-action (CTA) to avoid confusing recipients.

### Rule #3: Use clear and compelling subject lines
Your subject line is the first thing recipients will see, so make it count. Use action verbs, questions, or statements that create curiosity.

**Warning:** Avoid using spammy keywords or phrases that might trigger a recipient's spam filter.

### Rule #4: Personalize your SMS
Use the recipient's name, location, or other relevant details to make your SMS feel more personal and engaging.

### Rule #5: Segment your list
Segmenting your list allows you to target specific groups with tailored content. This can help increase engagement and conversion rates.

**Example:** If you're a coffee shop, you might segment your list by location (e.g., downtown vs. uptown) or by loyalty program status.

### Rule #6: Use timing to your advantage
Send SMS at the right time to maximize engagement. Avoid sending during peak hours or when recipients are likely to be busy.

**Tip:** Use analytics to determine the best times for your specific audience.

### Rule #7: Monitor and adjust
Keep an eye on your open rates, response rates, and conversion rates to see what's working and what's not. Adjust your strategy accordingly.

**Warning:** Don't be afraid to try new things and take calculated risks.

**Example:** You might test two different subject lines to see which one gets more opens.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched small business owners burn through their SMS budget in ways that make me order that second coffee. Here are the four most expensive mistakes I see regularly — and exactly what to do about them.

**Mistake #1: Treating SMS Like a Blast Email**

A gym owner in Denver was proud of his 2,000-subscriber list. He sent daily motivational texts every morning. "Just a little push to get them moving," he said. Within two weeks, 30% of his list unsubscribed. Open rates dropped to 40%. People were actually complaining — calling his phone number, leaving voicemails asking to be removed.

The fix: I told him to cut frequency to three texts per week, and every single one had to offer something tangible. A free guest pass. A discounted smoothie after class. Early access to a new HIIT session. No more "think positive" quotes.

The outcome: Unsubscribes dropped to 2% the first month. Class attendance from SMS tracking codes went up 40%. That translated to roughly $1,200 per month in incremental revenue. His problem wasn't that people didn't want to hear from him. It's that they didn't want to hear *nothing* from him every single day.

**Mistake #2: Sending at the Wrong Time By Default**

A coffee shop in Portland sent their lunch specials at 5:30 AM. The owner set it up once and never thought about it again. By 11 AM when people were deciding where to eat, that text was buried under 40 other notifications. Open rate was 18% — worse than their email list.

The fix: We moved the send time to 10:45 AM. That's when people are starting to get hungry and haven't made lunch decisions yet. We also added a simple urgency element: "First 20 orders get a free cookie."

The outcome: Open rate jumped to 82%. Coupon redemption hit 22%. The shop averaged $800 in extra revenue per week from those texts — money they'd been leaving on the table because they were sending at a time that worked for *them*, not for their customers.

**Mistake #3: Buying a List (Yes, People Still Do This)**

A hair salon owner in Austin bought a list of 5,000 phone numbers from a data broker for $300. "Everyone does it," she told me. Two weeks later, she had three complaints filed with the FCC. Her SMS provider suspended her account. She also got hit with a cease-and-desist from her local business bureau.

The fix: We started from zero. I helped her set up a double opt-in through her Square POS system — customers checking out could text a simple keyword ("SALON") to get 10% off their next visit. She collected 400 real opt-ins in six weeks.

The outcome: First campaign to that 400-person list generated $3,800 in booked appointments. More importantly, she wasn't facing legal fines that could have hit $10,000 per violation. The $300 she saved on the data list cost her about three months of business risk. Not worth it.

**Mistake #4: No Clear Call-to-Action**

A boutique in Chicago sent texts like "New arrivals just hit the floor!" with no link, no keyword, no offer. They expected people to just show up. Response was basically zero. The owner told me SMS "just doesn't work for retail."

The fix: Every text got one specific action. "Reply YES for 20% off your next purchase." "Show this text at checkout for a free tote bag." "Limited to the first 50 responses." I also added a two-hour expiration window.

The outcome: Response rate hit 15% within the first week. Average order value from SMS-driven purchases was $68 — 30% higher than walk-in traffic. Monthly revenue from SMS went from nothing to $2,100. The boutique owner stopped saying SMS doesn't work and started asking why she didn't do it sooner.

## When to Send (and When to Stay Silent)

Timing isn't just about the hour of day. It's about understanding what your customer is doing when your text arrives.

Based on campaigns I've run across dozens of small businesses, here's what actually works:

**For appointment-based businesses** (hair salons, pet groomers, massage studios): Send confirmation texts 48 hours before, then a reminder 2 hours before. A pet groomer in Nashville using Booksy integrated her SMS this way. No-shows dropped from 18% to 3% in eight weeks. That's about $600 per month in recovered revenue per grooming station.

**For retail and restaurants**: Best open rates hit between 10 AM and 12 PM on weekdays. Saturdays from 9 AM to 11 AM also work well. Avoid Sunday mornings entirely — open rates drop below 30%. A deli in Portland tested this: Monday lunch texts got 89% open rates. Sunday brunch texts got 22%. They stopped sending Sunday texts and reallocated that slot to Tuesday breakfast specials.

**For fitness studios**: Early morning texts (before 6 AM) get opened because people are checking their phones before class. But don't send promotional content then. Send motivational content. A yoga studio in Denver sends "Your 7 AM mat is waiting" at 6:15 AM with a link to book. That one text drives 65% of their daily bookings.

**When to stay silent**: Between 8 PM and 7 AM. Also, don't send during major holidays unless it's specifically about holiday hours or a time-sensitive offer. Your customers aren't ignoring you — they're with their families. Show some respect and they'll respect your messages back.

## Segmentation That Actually Works Without Overcomplicating It

Most small business owners skip segmentation because it sounds like something agencies do for Fortune 500 clients. It's not. You can segment with a spreadsheet and a Mailchimp account.

**What to actually segment by:**

**Purchase frequency**. A coffee shop in Austin had 400 SMS subscribers. I divided them into three groups: visited within 30 days, visited 31-90 days ago, and visited 90+ days ago. We sent different offers to each. The recent customers got a "come back this week" text. The lapsed customers got a "we miss you, here's a free drink" text. Revenue from the lapsed segment alone hit $1,400 in the first two weeks. That was money they'd written off.

**Service type**. A pet groomer in Portland separated dog owners from cat owners. Dog owners got nail-trim reminders and grooming package offers. Cat owners got quieter appointment slots and calming product recommendations. Response rate nearly doubled. Average spend per visit went from $65 to $89.

**Booking channel**. A hair salon in Chicago tracked whether customers booked through Yelp, Google, or direct call. The Yelp bookers got an SMS with a "skip the platform fee" offer for their next direct booking. Within three months, 28% of Yelp bookers switched to direct booking, saving the salon about $400 per month in Yelp fees.

You don't need fancy software for this. Most POS systems (Square, Booksy, Mindbody) let you export customer data with purchase history. Spend 30 minutes tagging people in your SMS platform. The ROI is immediate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Won't people be annoyed by texts from a business?**

Not if you're useful. The average person checks their phone 96 times a day. They're not annoyed by texts — they're annoyed by irrelevant texts. Give them something worth opening: a discount, an appointment reminder, exclusive access. Keep it under three messages per week. If you're useful, they'll look forward to hearing from you.

**Q: How do I collect phone numbers without being pushy?**

Make the value obvious. A bakery in Denver put a sign at the register: "Text BAKERY to 555-1234. Get a free muffin next visit." Simple, low-friction, and they got 80 sign-ups its first week. You can also collect numbers at checkout through Square or Toast. Offer a discount or free item in exchange. If you're not offering anything, you're asking for a favor.

**Q: What laws do I need to worry about?**

In the US, you must have explicit written consent and include an opt-out instruction in every message. That's TCPA compliance. In Canada, CASL applies. In the UK and Australia, similar consent rules exist. The fine for violating TCPA is up to $1,500 per unsolicited text. If you bought a list or scraped numbers, you're risking thousands. Just use double opt-in and you're covered. It's not complicated — it's just non-negotiable.

**Q: How much does SMS marketing actually cost?**

Depends on your volume and provider. Most platforms charge between $0.01 and $0.03 per message. If you send 1,000 texts per month, that's $10-30. Some platforms have monthly minimums around $20-50. Compare that to a $500 Google Ads budget that might generate 10 leads. SMS cost per conversion is consistently lower for small businesses focused on repeat customers.

**Q: Can I send pictures or links in texts?**

MMS (picture messages) cost more — about $0.05-0.10 per message — but they outperform plain text for visual businesses. A cupcake shop in Portland sends a photo of their flavor of the week via MMS. Response rate is 3x higher than their plain text offers. For links, use a URL shortener like Bitly so the message stays clean and under 160 characters.

**Q: What happens when someone texts STOP?**

The platform automatically unsubscribes them. It's immediate and permanent. Don't try to re-add them unless they explicitly opt back in. Some business owners freak out about unsubscribes. Don't. It's a cleanup of people who weren't going to buy anyway. A clean list of 500 engaged subscribers is worth more than a dirty list of 5,000 people who ignore you.
