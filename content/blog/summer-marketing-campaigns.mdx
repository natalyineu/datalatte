### 1. Leverage Seasonal Promotions Before Summer Begins

A coffee shop in Austin saw 40% more midweek orders by bundling iced coffees + discounted pastries ($8 vs $13). Use **email & SMS marketing** to send these offers directly to your existing clients.

- Create urgency (e.g., "3-day flash sale")
- Bundle summer products/services (e.g., spa packages for salons)

### 6. Partner with Complementary Local Businesses for Cross-Promotions

Summer is the perfect time to team up with nearby businesses that share your audience but aren’t direct competitors. A coffee shop can partner with a yoga studio: buy a smoothie, get 10% off a class. A pet groomer can cross-promote with a dog-friendly café.

**Steps to copy:**
- Identify 2–3 non-competing businesses within a 5-block radius
- Create a simple "Summer Pass" card: visit each partner for a stamp, redeem for a freebie
- Split the cost of joint social ads or printed flyers

A hair salon in London partnered with a local nail bar and saw a 22% increase in combined bookings during July. Each business spent only $40 on shared Instagram ads targeting the same zip code.

### 8. Optimize Your Website for Mobile and Speed

Summer browsers are on their phones—waiting for a hair dryer or chilling at a park bench. If your site takes more than 3 seconds to load, you lose 53% of visitors.

**Quick wins:**
- Compress images (use free tools like TinyPNG)
- Enable "click-to-call" buttons on every service page
- Add a prominent "Book Now" button that stays visible while scrolling

A fitness studio in Sydney updated their mobile booking flow and cut abandoned bookings by 18%. Their summer "heatwave" landing page loaded in 1.8 seconds, driving a 12% conversion lift.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Running the Same Offer You Ran Last Summer

A dog grooming salon in Nashville ran a "Summer Shedding Special — $10 off any bath and brush" for three consecutive summers. The owner couldn't understand why response dropped 60% year-over-year. By year three, she was spending $400/month on Facebook ads for an offer that brought in exactly two bookings — both from existing clients who would have booked anyway.

**What went wrong:** Her customers had seen the same creative, same price point, same call-to-action so many times that their brains auto-filtered it. Worse, she was training them to never book at full price. When she tried to run a full-price campaign later that summer, no one bit.

**The fix:** She switched to a "New Customer Summer Passport" — $25 for a wash, brush, nail trim, and a bandana. Existing clients got a different offer: "Refer a new dog and get a free nail trim kit ($18 value)." She promoted the passport on Yelp and Google Ads ($600 total spend), and the referral program through her existing text list.

**The outcome:** 44 new customer passports sold in six weeks. Referral program brought in 12 new dogs. Total revenue from these two campaigns: $3,840. Her ad spend dropped to $600 because she stopped throwing money at people who already knew the $10-off routine.

### Mistake 2: Not Updating Your Hours or Services for Summer

A hair salon in Portland OR lost an estimated $3,200 last July because their Google Business Profile still showed "closed Sunday and Monday" — but they'd added Monday hours for summer specifically. More painfully, their online booking system still listed "Winter Hydration Treatment" as the top service, which no one was searching for in 85-degree weather.

**What went wrong:** A client drove 20 minutes on a Monday, found the door locked, and left a one-star Google review saying "Updated your hours, maybe?". She also posted in a local Facebook group with 12,000 members, warning others to check before visiting. The salon owner didn't check his GBP for two weeks. By then, three other negative reviews had piled on.

**The fix:** First, update Google Business Profile hours immediately — not "sometime this week." Second, rename the summer service to "Cooling Scalp Treatment + Blowout: $55" and set it as the primary service in Booksy. Third, respond to the negative review publicly (apologize, state the new hours, invite her back for a complimentary service). Fourth, ask three happy clients from that week to leave fresh reviews.

**The outcome:** The negative review stayed, but the owner's response showed accountability. Three new reviews (all 5-star) pushed the average back up. The "Cooling Scalp Treatment" service booked 18 appointments in August alone, generating $990 of incremental revenue against $0 in new ad spend — just renaming and reordering what they already offered.

### Mistake 3: Advertising to the Same Audience Year-Round

A yoga studio in Chicago spent $1,200 in June on Instagram ads targeting their usual "women 25–45, interested in wellness." They got 28 clicks and zero memberships. The owner told me she felt like she was screaming into a void. She was.

**What went wrong:** Her core audience was already buying — they showed up, they paid, they sometimes brought friends. But summer for a fitness studio means your regulars are traveling, working from a lake house, or skipping class to sit in a park with a rosé. Advertising to them is useless. They already know you exist. They just don't want to come.

**The fix:** Build a separate audience: people within 2 miles of your studio who do NOT follow you or visit your site. No interest targeting. No "wellness" keywords. Just geography and exclusion lists. Then run an offer they can't ignore: "First month unlimited for $49 (normally $159) — no commitment, cancel anytime. 30 spots only." Promote it on Google Ads with keywords like "yoga near me" and "summer yoga classes Chicago."

**The outcome:** $800 ad spend over three weeks. 22 new members. 11 converted to full-price after the first month. Total first-month revenue: $1,078 from the offer + $1,749 from renewals = $2,827. She could have spent $400 less and still won.

## Why Your Existing Customers Are Your Best Summer Bet

Here's the uncomfortable truth: most local businesses spend June through August chasing new customers while ignoring the people who already pay them. This is backwards.

Your regulars are worth 5–10x more than a first-time buyer. They trust you. They know the menu, the vibe, the pricing. Getting them to visit one extra time per month has higher ROI than convincing a stranger to walk through your door once.

**But they're also distracted.** They have vacations, barbecues, kids out of school, and a general desire to sit in air conditioning. The trick isn't to scream louder. It's to give them a reason to choose you over the hammock in their backyard.

**What actually works:**

Create a "Summer Loyalty Sprint" — not a generic punch card, but a time-bound challenge with a real prize. A coffee shop in Denver ran this: "Come 8 times in June and get a free pound of our cold brew beans ($18 value) plus a branded Yeti-style cup." They tracked it through Square's loyalty module. 73 customers completed the challenge. Those 73 customers accounted for 292 transactions within the month — that's 4 visits each, minimum. Many bought food or merchandise on top of their drink. The total incremental revenue from the sprint: $4,380. The cost of prizes: $986 (wholesale). Net gain: $3,394.

The key details:
- **Limited time.** 30 days. No extensions. Creates urgency.
- **Specific goal.** Not "earn points." Come 8 times. Simple math.
- **Visible tracking.** Square sends a push notification after each visit showing progress. Customers can see "You're 3 of 8" on their phone.
- **Relevant prize.** Cold brew beans and a cup for summer. Not a winter scarf in July.

You can run this with Mailchimp for a 100-person list, Square for a free loyalty setup, and a spreadsheet for tracking. Total cost: your time and the prizes.

**Why most businesses skip this:** It feels like work to set up. You have to promote it, track it, fulfill prizes. But the businesses I've seen pull it off never skip summer again — because the data proves it works.

## The One Google Ads Setup That Actually Works for Summer

I've managed Google Ads for everything from a $50,000/month Dentsu client to a $500/month coffee shop in Austin. Most small business owners set up their ads wrong for summer. Here's what to do instead.

**The mistake:** Broad match keywords and "maximize clicks" bidding. Google will happily spend your budget on people searching for "coffee near me" in San Diego when you're in Chicago. It happens constantly.

**The fix:**

1. **Go phrase match or exact match only.** For a pet groomer in Austin: "dog grooming Austin TX" (exact) and "dog grooming near me" (phrase with location bid adjustment). That's it. Two keywords. Test with $20/day. Scale the winner.

2. **Add negative keywords aggressively.** Block: "free", "DIY", "cheap", "self-service", "grooming school", "jobs", "career". You'd be shocked how many $5 clicks go to someone watching a YouTube video about how to groom their own dog.

3. **Use location targeting with radius.** Set 5 miles for a coffee shop or 10 miles for a salon. No exceptions. Then use location bid adjustments: add +25% for people physically in your area (the ones who can drive to you in 10 minutes), and —50% for people searching for your city but located outside it.

4. **Schedule for human hours.** If you close at 6pm, your ads should stop at 5:30pm. No one is going to drive to your shop at 9pm for a haircut tomorrow. Don't waste the budget.

**Real example:** A fitness studio in Denver used this exact setup. $800/month budget. Two ad groups: "yoga classes Denver" and "hot yoga Denver." Phrase match only. Negative keyword list of 40 terms. Location radius of 4 miles. They got 142 clicks in July, 14 class pass signups, and 3 full memberships. Cost per lead: $29. Revenue from new members: $2,400. That's $1,600 net against their ad spend. Not bad for a setup that took 45 minutes.

**What most guides skip:** The bid strategy. Do NOT use "maximize conversions" when you have fewer than 15 conversions in the last 30 days. Google doesn't have enough data to optimize. Use "maximize clicks" with a daily budget cap — then manually adjust bids based on what you see after two weeks. It's more work, but it keeps your money from disappearing into Google's black box.

## How to Turn Yelp from a Frustration into a Revenue Channel

I worked with a hair salon in NYC that was spending $500/month on Yelp ads and getting zero bookings they could track. The owner was ready to cancel. When I looked at her Yelp Business account, I saw the problem: she had no call tracking number, no "Request a Call" button, and her photos were from 2019 (same filter, same sad lighting).

**What most small businesses get wrong about Yelp:**
- They rely on Yelp's automatic lead form, which sends users to a generic page
- They don't separate Yelp from their regular phone number, so they can't attribute calls
- They upload dark, low-resolution photos that make their business look closed

**The fix for summer specifically:**

**Step 1:** Set up a unique phone number in Yelp's ad dashboard. It costs $0 extra and takes 3 minutes. Now you know exactly how many calls come from Yelp vs your website vs Google.

**Step 2:** Upload new summer photos. Good lighting, open sign visible, a picture of your best summer product/drink/service with a clear price. If you're a coffee shop: an iced drink with condensation on the glass, a pastry next to it, a chalkboard in the background with your WiFi password. No stock photos.

**Step 3:** Enable "Request a Quote" or "Request a Call" buttons — whichever your business type allows. This converts the "just browsing" user into a lead without them having to dial a number.

**Step 4:** Run a Yelp Check-in Offer. You pay per check-in, not per impression. For a salon: "Check in and get 15% off any color service." Cost: about $2–3 per check-in. If someone checks in, they're in your store, with their phone out, seeing your offer. The conversion rate on check-in offers hovers around 40%, according to Yelp's own data (which I've seen match what my clients actually get).

**Real numbers:** That same NYC salon switched from Yelp ads to Yelp check-in offers for summer. She set the offer for June through August. Cost per check-in: $2.50. She got 64 check-ins over three months. Total cost: $160 (vs $1,500 she would have spent on ads). Of those 64, 27 redeemed the offer and spent an average of $85 per visit (color service + tip). Direct revenue: $2,295. And 12 of those 27 booked a second appointment within 60 days.

**Is Yelp worth it for everyone?** No. If you're a plumber or an accountant it's probably not your channel. But for coffee shops, salons, pet services, and fitness studios — Yelp's summer traffic bump is real. You just need to use it correctly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a small business with just me and one part-time employee. Do I really have time for all this?**

No, you don't. Pick one tactic. The highest ROI for your time is updating Google Business Profile hours and adding summer photos. That's 20 minutes. Then run one email or text blast to your existing list with a summer bundle offer. That's 30 minutes. Everything else can wait. Don't try to do six things at once. You'll burn out and none of them will work.

**Q: Why should I spend money marketing when I'm already losing revenue? That feels backwards.**

Because the revenue loss compounds. If you do nothing, July is slow. August is worse. By September your regulars have formed new habits — the new coffee shop they tried, the new yoga studio their friend dragged them to. You're not just losing summer revenue. You're losing the momentum that carries into fall. Spending $500 to protect $3,000 in recurring revenue is a smart bet, not a risky one.

**Q: I tried Google Ads and it didn't work. Why would it work now?**

Most small businesses set up Google Ads once, spend $300, get three clicks, and give up. That's not a test. That's an accident. The problem is almost always one of three things: wrong keywords (too broad), wrong location (targeting 50 miles when you should target 5), or wrong landing page (sending people to your homepage instead of a specific offer page). Before you write off Google Ads, run the exact setup I described above for exactly two weeks. If it still doesn't work, kill it. But at least you'll know why.

**Q: Do I really need to offer discounts? I don't want to train customers to only buy on sale.**

Fair point. But there's a difference between "always on sale" and "summer-specific offer." The latter has an expiration date. Run a June-only bundle, then stop it. Your customers won't expect iced coffee discounts in November. They'll just appreciate that you made summer easier. If you're really worried, test a free upgrade instead of a discount. "Buy any sandwich, get a free iced tea" costs you 40 cents in syrup and ice. That's cheaper than a 20% discount and feels more like a treat than a fire sale.

**Q: What's the cheapest tool I need to set up the email/SMS campaign?**

Mailchimp's free plan handles up to 500 contacts and 1,000 sends per month. That's enough for most local businesses. For SMS, use Textedly or SimpleTexting — both start around $25/month for a few hundred texts. You don't need Klaviyo or ActiveCampaign for a single summer campaign. Keep it simple. If you already have a Square or Booksy account, check if they include email or text messaging in your subscription. Many do. You're probably paying for it already.
