## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Dumping Money Into Facebook Ads Without a Retargeting Plan

I worked with a coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas—let's call him Mike. He ran a spot near the University of Texas campus. Good coffee, good pastries, decent foot traffic. But he wanted to grow the afternoon crowd. So he did what every small business owner does: threw $800 at a Facebook ad campaign that reached 45,000 people in two weeks.

Here's what actually happened: 45,000 impressions, 1,200 link clicks, and exactly 14 people showed up with the ad coupon. Mike spent $57 per new customer. For a $4 latte, that math doesn't work.

The fix was boring but effective. We paused the broad campaign and instead ran a tiny retargeting ad for $200/month. We put a Facebook pixel on his website—which took about 15 minutes—and showed ads only to people who had already visited his site or engaged with his Instagram. The offer was simple: "Come back before 3 PM and get a free pastry with any drink."

Outcome: That $200 ad brought in 86 redemptions over 30 days. Average ticket with a pastry add-on? $9.50. So $817 in revenue from $200 in ad spend. Mike's mistake wasn't running ads. It was running ads without a retargeting funnel. Most small business owners treat Facebook like a billboard. It's not. It's a follow-up tool.

### Mistake 2: Starving Your Google Business Profile Then Wondering Why Nobody Shows Up

A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon—she'd been in business for eight years—came to me frustrated. "I post on Instagram every day. Why am I not getting new clients?" She had 2,300 Instagram followers and maybe three new clients a month from it. I asked to see her Google Business Profile.

It hadn't been touched since 2019. No new photos. No responses to reviews. The description still said "specializing in balayage and cuts"—but she'd added eyelash extensions and brow services three years prior. Google didn't know. So when people in Portland searched "eyelash extensions near me," her salon didn't show up.

We spent 90 minutes together. Updated the description. Added 12 new photos showing the lash work. Responded to every review—even the one-star from someone who was angry about parking. (She responded politely, didn't argue, just acknowledged the feedback.) Then we started posting a photo every Tuesday to the Google Business Profile directly—not Instagram, not Facebook. Just a fresh photo on Google.

Within 60 days, her search impressions for "hair salon Portland" plus "eyelash extensions Portland" went up 140%. She booked $3,200 in new lash appointments that month. The cost? Zero dollars. The only investment was her time to upload photos and write short replies. Most guides skip this part—don't. A stale Google profile is like having a storefront with a sign that hasn't been cleaned since 2019.

### Mistake 3: Paying for Unnecessary Listings and Directory Spam

A pet groomer in Nashville told me she was spending $180/month on "local SEO packages" from a company she found on Fiverr. They claimed to build "200+ citations." She'd been paying for eight months. Total spent: $1,440. When I checked her Google Business Profile, she ranked on page 3 for "pet grooming Nashville." Not great.

I looked at what that $180 bought. The company had submitted her business name and address to 200 directory websites—most of which were spammy, irrelevant, or completely broken. One directory was for "Australian business listings." She doesn't live in Australia. Google ignores those links. Worse, some of the citations had her phone number formatted wrong, which confuses Google's algorithm.

We canceled the service. Instead, I had her spend one hour cleaning up her NAP (name, address, phone) across the five directories that actually matter in a local market: Yelp, Nextdoor, Facebook, Yellow Pages (yes, it still exists), and the local Nashville chamber of commerce website. Then we made sure every listing matched exactly—same spelling of "Pet Palace" (not "Pet Palace Nashville"), same phone number format, same zip code.

Within three weeks, she moved from page 3 to position 4 on page 1 for her main keyword. No additional cost. The $1,440 she saved went into buying a better clipper set and a small Google Ads test. The lesson: 200 bad citations are worse than five good ones. Paying someone to spam directories is paying to hurt your rankings.

## ## Reputation Management: The One Thing Chains Do That You're Probably Ignoring

Big chains spend real money on reputation management software—tools like Birdeye, Widewail, or even simple review-gating platforms. They know that a single one-star review on the first page of your Google results can cost you dozens of customers a month. I've seen the data.

Here's the uncomfortable truth for local businesses: most of you aren't actively managing reviews. You're hoping people leave good ones and praying they don't leave bad ones. That's not a strategy. It's a prayer.

Let me give you a specific example. A fitness studio in Denver—small, nine classes a week, 140 members—was getting about one review per month. Most were from members who really loved the place, but the studio's Google rating sat at 4.2 stars. Why? Because four bad reviews from two years ago were sitting there weighing down the average. The owner hadn't responded to any of them.

We implemented a three-step process:

**Step 1:** We responded to every single existing review—good and bad. For the bad ones, we didn't argue. We said "Thanks for the feedback, we're sorry your experience wasn't great. We've changed our check-in process since then and would love to have you back." That alone signals to Google that the business is active.

**Step 2:** We set up a simple automated text after class. Using a tool called Podium (about $300/month, but they have a free trial), we sent each member a text 30 minutes after class ended. The text said: "How was class today? Reply with a number 1-10." If they replied 7 or above, the next text said: "Great! Would you mind leaving a quick Google review? Here's a link." If they replied 6 or below, they got a different text: "Sorry to hear that. Tell us what went wrong and we'll make it right."

**Step 3:** We kept this running for 90 days.

The outcome: Review volume went from 1 per month to 14 per month. The average rating climbed from 4.2 to 4.7. New member sign-ups from Google searches increased by 40% in that quarter. The owner estimated that increase was worth roughly $1,600 in additional monthly revenue from Google alone.

The tool cost $300/month. The revenue gain was $1,600/month. Most small business owners are leaving that on the table because they think "review management" sounds like something only big companies do. It's not. It's a 30-minute setup and a recurring text message.

If Podium is too pricey, there's a workaround: use Mailchimp's automation (free plan up to 500 contacts) to send a review request email 24 hours after a service. The open rate on transactional emails like "How was your appointment?" tends to be 40-50%, way higher than a newsletter. I've seen it work at a barbershop in Chicago that went from 23 reviews total to 180 in six months. No spam. Just a simple "Hey, mind leaving a review?" email sent at the right time.

## ## Hyperlocal Ads: Where to Spend $500 That Actually Moves the Needle

I'm going to say something that might annoy Google Ads reps: most small business owners should not be spending money on Google Ads for general keywords. You know why? Because "coffee shop" in Seattle costs $4.50 per click. That click might become a customer, or it might be someone who's already three blocks away and just looking for the address. Congratulations, you paid $4.50 for a directional query.

Here's what actually works for a $500 monthly ad budget.

### Google Local Services Ads (Not Google Ads)

If you're a service-based business—plumber, electrician, pet groomer, hair stylist, even a fitness trainer—Google Local Services Ads are probably the best deal in digital advertising right now. They show up above regular Google Ads in search results. You pay per qualified lead, not per click. And Google guarantees the leads are from people actively looking for your service.

A locksmith in Austin I worked with spent $400/month on Local Services Ads. He got 23 leads in the first month. He closed 12 of them. Average job value: $185. That's $2,220 in revenue from $400 in ad spend. The setup took two hours: background check, license verification, and setting his service area. You can't optimize for "brand awareness" with Local Services Ads—you can only optimize for people who need your service right now. Which is exactly what a locksmith wants.

### Geo-Fencing Your Competitors (For Real)

This sounds fancier than it is. A yoga studio in Nashville had a problem: a new CorePower Yoga opened half a mile away. The studio was losing drop-in clients. We took $100 of their $500 budget and used a simple geo-fencing tool called Simpli.fi (minimum spend is low, around $50). We drew a digital fence around the CorePower location—about 200 feet radius.

Any phone that crossed that fence got served a mobile banner ad for the local studio: "Drop-in for $12. First class free with this ad." That $100 reached about 1,400 phones over two weeks. Sixteen people clicked through. Nine showed up for a class. The studio made $108 from drop-in fees but, more importantly, sold three class packs worth $180 total. The $100 geo-fence test paid for itself and brought in customers who were actively at a competitor's location. Would I recommend this for everyone? No. But if you have a direct competitor within walking distance, it's worth a test.

### The $100 Newsletter Sponsorship

Most local businesses overlook newsletter sponsorships. Every mid-sized US city has at least two or three popular local newsletters. In Portland, the "Portland Food and Drink" newsletter has 35,000 subscribers. A sponsorship slot costs around $150-200 per edition. For a bakery or coffee shop, that's a better deal than Facebook ads because the audience is already local and engaged.

A bagel shop in Chicago spent $175 on a three-line sponsorship in a local food newsletter. They offered a coupon: "Show this email, get a free shmear with any bagel purchase." The owner told me 47 people redeemed the offer over the following week. Average ticket for those customers was $11.50 (bagel, coffee, and a side). That's $540 in revenue from a $175 spend. And unlike a Facebook ad that disappears after 72 hours, that newsletter lives in subscribers' inboxes for days.

**How to find these:** Search "[your city] newsletter" or "[your city] Substack." Email the editor directly—don't use a booking form. These aren't programmatic ad networks. You're buying a recommendation from a human who knows the local audience. Most editors will negotiate on price if you offer to trade product or services.

## ## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a website if I have a Google Business Profile and a Facebook page?**

Yes. A Google Business Profile is a listing. Facebook is a social platform that algorithmically decides who sees your posts. A website is the only asset you fully own. If Google changes something tomorrow or Facebook deprioritizes business pages again (they've done this multiple times), your website still exists. It doesn't have to be expensive. A one-page site with your hours, location, menu/services, and a booking link costs about $200-300 on Squarespace or Wix. That's less than what most small businesses spend on Facebook ads in a month. And it lets you collect emails, track visitors, and retarget people. Your Google profile can't do that.

**Q: Is Yelp worth paying for?**

Rarely. I've looked at Yelp's paid advertising product for dozens of clients. The ROI is almost always worse than Google Ads or even decent Facebook targeting. The one exception is if you're in a category where Yelp dominates search volume—like restaurants and bars in dense urban areas. But even then, I'd start with the free Yelp page. Claim it, put up good photos, and respond to every review. Don't pay Yelp to remove competitor ads from your page. That's a racket. If you're getting good organic reviews on Yelp (which happens naturally if you're a good business), the free version is enough.

**Q: How long should I run a campaign before deciding it's not working?**

Two weeks minimum for ads, 90 days for SEO. If you're running Google Ads and haven't seen a single conversion in two weeks, something is wrong—your targeting, your landing page, or your offer. You can fix that. If you're doing local SEO (optimizing your profile, getting reviews, building citations), expect to wait 60-90 days before you see meaningful movement in rankings. Google doesn't trust new signals immediately. Anyone who promises you page one rankings in two weeks is selling snake oil.

**Q: Can I use ChatGPT to write my Google Business Profile description and website copy?**

You can, but be careful. ChatGPT writes generic fluff by default. If you copy-paste "We are a family-owned business dedicated to providing exceptional service to our valued customers," you sound like every other business. Instead, feed ChatGPT specific details: "We opened in 2017. Our owner is a former chef who switched to baking. We use a 70-year-old sourdough starter. Our bestseller is the jalapeño cheddar loaf." Then ask it to rewrite that into a Google Bio. The output will be better because the input is specific. But still edit it. Your voice matters more than AI fluency.

**Q: Is it worth running Google Ads if my budget is only $300/month?**

Yes, but only if you're surgical about it. Don't run broad "coffee shop" keywords. Run "buy coffee beans Austin" or "espresso machine repair Chicago" if you offer that. Use phrase match and exact match keywords. Exclude search terms that include "free," "cheap," or "jobs." Set a location radius of 5-10 miles. And send people to a specific landing page—not your homepage. The landing page should have one clear action: book an appointment, call you, or order online. At $300/month, you can afford about 50-100 clicks depending on your industry. Make every click count. If you're in a saturated market where clicks cost $8-10 each, $300 won't go far. In that case, put that money into Local Services Ads or a newsletter sponsorship instead.

**Q: I'm a salon owner. Should I use Booksy, Vagaro, or just keep taking bookings by phone?**

Stop taking bookings by phone if you want to grow. I mean it. A phone booking system scales like garbage. You can't be on the phone when you're cutting hair. Booksy or Vagaro (both cost around $30-80/month) let clients book online, send automatic reminders, and handle cancellations. A salon in Denver I worked with reduced no-shows from 22% to 8% just by enabling automated text reminders through Vagaro. That saved her roughly $600/month in lost appointment revenue. The tool cost $45/month. The math is obvious. The only reason not to do it is if you genuinely prefer the friction of phone bookings—and I'd question whether that friction is costing you customers who'd rather book online at midnight.
