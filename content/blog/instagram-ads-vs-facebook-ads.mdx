As a small business owner, you're constantly looking for ways to reach new customers and grow your revenue. Social media advertising can be a game-changer, but with so many options, it's hard to know where to start. In this article, we'll dive into the world of Instagram and Facebook ads to help you decide which platform is best for your business.

**Pros and Cons of Facebook Ads**

Facebook Ads are a popular choice for small business owners because they offer a wide range of targeting options, including age, location, interests, and behaviors. However, they can also be expensive, especially if you're targeting a specific geographic area.

* Pros: Wide range of targeting options, high ROI for local businesses, easy to create and manage ads
* Cons: Can be expensive, especially for targeted ads, may not be as visually appealing as Instagram ads

**Pros and Cons of Instagram Ads**

Instagram Ads are a great choice for businesses that want to showcase their products or services in a more visually appealing way. They offer a range of ad formats, including photo, video, and carousel ads. However, they can also be more expensive than Facebook ads, especially if you're targeting a specific audience.

**Which Platform Is Right for Your Business?**

The answer ultimately depends on your business goals and target audience. If you're a local business with a physical location, Facebook Ads may be a better choice due to their high ROI and wide range of targeting options. However, if you're an e-commerce business or want to showcase your products or services in a more visually appealing way, Instagram Ads may be the way to go.

<Callout type="tip">Consider running a split test to see which platform performs better for your business.</Callout>

**Example:**

Let's say you're a pet groomer in Los Angeles and you want to reach dog owners in the area. You create a Facebook ad targeting dog owners in Los Angeles, with a budget of $500 per month. After a month, you see a 20% increase in bookings and a 15% increase in revenue. You decide to switch to Instagram Ads, targeting dog owners in Los Angeles with a budget of $500 per month. After a month, you see a 30% increase in bookings and a 20% increase in revenue. In this case, Instagram Ads performed better for your business.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running the Exact Same Campaign on Both Platforms

**The story:** A coffee shop in Austin, Texas called Brew & Bean. They had a solid local following on Instagram — 4,200 engaged followers, people tagging them in photos of their latte art. Their owner, Marcus, decided to run Meta ads. He created one campaign, set it to "Traffic" with his website as the destination, and turned on Advantage+ placements. Meta auto-allocated 72% of his budget to Facebook and 28% to Instagram.

After 30 days and $1,400 spent, here's what happened:

- Facebook delivered 1,100 visitors to the site at $0.91 per click
- Instagram delivered 340 visitors at $2.15 per click
- Neither platform produced a single in-store visit tracked via their Square integration

**What went wrong:** Marcus ran the same creative — a photo of a coffee cup with text overlay — on both platforms. His Instagram audience wanted aesthetic shots of the cafe interior and staff. His Facebook audience (average age 45+) was looking for event announcements and hours. The creative message was wrong for both. More critically, sending Instagram users to a website when they're in an app built for quick actions was a bad user experience.

**The fix:** We split them into separate campaigns with platform-specific creative.

- Instagram campaign: High-res video of baristas pouring latte art, shot on an iPhone. Vertical format. Call-to-action button set to "Send Message" for catering inquiries.
- Facebook campaign: Static image of the shop's new brunch menu with pricing visible. Call-to-action set to "Get Directions" with the address pre-loaded.

**The outcome after 30 more days:**

- Instagram: $680 spent, 48 direct messages, 22 booked catering orders, $3,200 in revenue
- Facebook: $520 spent, 94 click-to-directions, 38 tracked walk-ins via Square loyalty data

Combined ROI: 4.2x on ad spend. Marcus stopped serving the same ad to both platforms and started looking at each platform for what it actually does well. He also stopped ordering "Traffic" as his objective, which leads to the next mistake.

### Mistake #2: Choosing the Wrong Campaign Objective

**The story:** A hair salon in Portland, Oregon called Fox & Ivy. Owner Jenna was spending $900/month on Instagram ads. She set them to "Engagement" because she wanted more likes and comments to make her profile look active. She got them — 2,500+ engagements per month. Her phone barely rang.

**What went wrong:** Engagement campaigns optimize for exactly that: people who tap, like, or comment. These are often people scrolling mindlessly who will never book. Instagram's algorithm found Jenna the cheapest possible taps — bot farms and casual scrollers in India and the Philippines — because the budget went to whatever geographic region delivered the lowest cost per engagement. She was paying $0.36 per engagement but getting zero appointments.

**The fix:** We switched to a "Conversions" campaign with the Meta Pixel tracking a "Complete Booking" event from her Booksy integration. Target radius: 10 miles from the shop. Daily budget: $30. Audience: women 22–55 who follow at least two Portland beauty or lifestyle pages.

**The outcome:**

- First 30 days: $900 spent, 48 booked appointments, $9,600 in services rendered (average ticket $200)
- Cost per booking: $18.75

Jenna's cost per engagement dropped from "who cares" to "actually profitable." She told me she wished someone had told her earlier that engagement is a vanity metric when you run a salon. I told her most guides skip that part — they're too busy recommending you "build brand awareness" without asking if you can pay rent with brand awareness.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Placement Controls on Instagram

**The story:** A pet groomer in Nashville, Tennessee called Bark + Scrub. Owner Michelle had been running Facebook ads for two years with decent results — about 12 new clients per month at $25 per acquisition. She wanted to test Instagram because her younger clients kept asking if she was on there. She duplicated her existing Facebook campaign, switched the placement to Instagram, and let Advantage+ handle the rest.

After $800 over three weeks, she'd gotten 4 new clients at $200 each. She was about to give up on Instagram entirely.

**What went wrong:** Michelle's campaign was running on Instagram Feed, Instagram Explore, Instagram Reels, Instagram Stories, and the Audience Network. Meta's algorithm was spending 63% of her budget on the Audience Network — which means her ads were showing on random third-party apps while people were playing mobile games or reading news articles. Those placements have conversion rates under 0.5% for local services.

**The fix:** I had her turn off everything except Instagram Feed and Instagram Stories. I also changed the creative from a static photo of a groomed dog to a 15-second Reel showing the before/after transformation of a particularly tangled Goldendoodle. We added the caption "Tag a friend who needs to see this transformation" with the location set to Nashville.

**The outcome:**

- Budget: $600/month (she saved $200 from the wasted Audience Network spend)
- Results: 34 new clients in 30 days, $7,480 in grooming revenue
- Cost per client: $17.64 — better than her Facebook campaigns

The Audience Network is not your friend for local business ads. Meta pushes it because it's cheap inventory they can sell. You pay less per click and get zero value. Turn it off before you start any campaign.

### Mistake #4: Not Retargeting Anyone Who Interacted

**The story:** A fitness studio in Denver, Colorado called Elevate Cycle. Owner Dave ran a "New Member Special" campaign — first class free — and spent $2,100 in one month. He got 340 people to click the "Sign Up" button, 89 people actually booked a free class, and 12 converted to memberships. He considered that a success and moved on to a new campaign.

**What went wrong:** Dave didn't retarget the 228 people who clicked but never booked, or the 77 people who booked but never showed up. Those people had explicitly raised their hands — they were interested enough to click a "Sign Up" button. They just needed a second nudge.

**The fix:** I set up a retargeting campaign targeting everyone who clicked "Sign Up" in the last 30 days but didn't complete a booking. The creative: a testimonial video from a current member saying "I almost didn't show up for my first class either. Here's why I'm glad I did." Budget: $15/day. We also set up a separate campaign for people who booked but no-showed with an offer: "Missed your first class? Try again — your free ride is still waiting."

**The outcome:**

- Retargeting campaign: $450 spent, 48 additional bookings, 19 converted to memberships ($5,700 in recurring monthly revenue at $300/mo average membership)
- No-show retargeting: $180 spent, 22 rebookings, 8 memberships ($2,400/mo recurring)

Dave's mistake cost him about $8,100 in potential revenue from people who were already interested. Retargeting is not optional for local businesses. If you're not doing it, you're leaving money on the table — specifically, exactly the money people were about to give you.

## When Each Platform Actually Works Best

This is the part most articles skip because "it depends" is easier than giving you a real answer. I've run enough campaigns to give you a hard rule of thumb.

**Use Instagram first if:**
- Your business is visually distinctive (coffee shops, bakeries, salons, tattoo studios, boutiques)
- Your customer is under 40
- Your product or service looks better in motion (fitness classes, hair styling, pet grooming before/afters)
- People make buying decisions based on aesthetics or social proof

**Use Facebook first if:**
- Your target customer is over 40 (which describes most homeowners, car owners, and people with disposable income for services)
- Your offer is informational or event-based (workshops, classes, open houses)
- You need detailed targeting (homeowners in specific neighborhoods, people who've recently moved, parents of toddlers)
- Your conversion happens on a website, not a direct message

**Use both if:**
- You have at least $800/month total budget ($400 per platform minimum)
- You can create different creative for each platform
- You're willing to check separate dashboards and optimize separately

Here's a specific example of when Facebook outperformed Instagram dramatically: A dentist in Chicago running a "new patient special" targeting homeowners in the Lincoln Park area, ages 35–65. Facebook delivered a cost per appointment of $22. Instagram delivered $47 on the same offer. The dentist's audience was older, decision-oriented, and not scrolling for visual inspiration — they were looking for a solution to a problem. Facebook's more utilitarian interface and detailed interest targeting matched that intent better.

On the flip side: A bakery in Portland running a "custom cakes" campaign. Instagram delivered cost per inquiry of $8. Facebook delivered $31. The bakery's customers wanted to see photos of cakes before inquiring. Instagram showed them exactly that, in the format optimized for it.

You don't have to guess which one works. Run a split test with $100 per platform for two weeks. The data will tell you. But if you only have $500 to spend, put it all on the platform where your customer actually hangs out — and be honest about which one that is, not the one you personally prefer.

## Budget Strategy: Where $500, $1,000, and $2,500 Per Month Actually Go

Let's talk real numbers. I see small business budgets in three tiers. Here's what I've seen work across dozens of campaigns.

### $500/month Budget

Put $350 on Facebook and $150 on Instagram. Run one campaign on each. Keep the targeting tight — 5-mile radius, specific interests relevant to your business, age range based on your actual customer data (not guessing). Do not test multiple audiences. Do not run Advantage+ placements. You can't afford the waste.

**Expected results for a hair salon:** 12–18 new clients per month, average $180 ticket, $2,160–$3,240 in revenue. That's a 4x–6x return.

### $1,000/month Budget

Split $500 on each platform. Run two ad sets per platform — one for cold targeting (people who don't know you), one for retargeting (people who visited your website or interacted with your profile). Creative matters more here because you're paying for attention.

**Expected results for a fitness studio:** 25–35 new member sign-ups at a $29 first-month trial, plus 8–12 full membership conversions within 60 days. Total revenue impact: $4,500–$7,200.

### $2,500/month Budget

Now you can get interesting. $1,000 on Facebook for cold traffic, $500 on Facebook for retargeting. $600 on Instagram for cold traffic, $400 on Instagram for retargeting. You can also test lookalike audiences based on your 100 best customers. At this level, you need to be checking performance every 72 hours and pausing ad sets that haven't delivered at least 1 new client in 7 days.

**Expected results for a pet groomer:** 50–70 new clients, average $85 per visit, repeat visit rate of 60% within 6 weeks. Revenue from ads alone: $4,250–$5,950 in first visits, plus $12,000–$18,000 in return visits over 3 months.

If these numbers feel high, it's because most small business owners underinvest in ads and then claim ads don't work. They work when you spend enough to give the algorithm real data. $200/month on Meta is a test, not a strategy. $500 is the minimum for a single platform. Anything less belongs on Google Ads or your local newspaper's website.

## How to Track What Actually Works (Without a Data Scientist)

Here's the uncomfortable truth: most small business owners don't know if their ads are working because they're not tracking properly. They look at Meta's dashboard, see 500 "link clicks," and assume that's good. It's not good if those 500 people landed on your website and left in 3 seconds.

**The tools I actually use with clients:**

- **Square or Toast integration:** Set up a unique promo code in your ads ("INSTA10" for 10% off). Run it for 30 days. Count how many times it's used. That's your tracked conversions. Everything else is guesswork.

- **Google Analytics (free):** Set up a "New Patient" or "First Visit" goal in GA4. Connect it to your Meta ads via UTM parameters. You'll see exactly which platform, ad, and audience drove the booking.

- **Booksy or Vagaro:** Both platforms have Meta conversion tracking that reports back when someone books. This is the easiest setup for salons, barbers, and wellness businesses.

- **CallRail or WhatConverts:** If you're getting phone calls from ads — and many local businesses do — these tools tell you which ad led to the call and record the conversation. One client discovered 34% of their calls were spam from a bad audience. They saved $400/month by fixing it.

- **Google Business Profile (free):** If you're running local campaigns, track "Direction Requests" in your Google Business Profile. Cross-reference timing with your ad schedule. If your ads are running but direction requests aren't increasing, your targeting is wrong.

**The one metric to ignore:** "Reach." Reach tells you how many people saw your ad. It doesn't tell you if anyone cared. A local coffee shop in Nashville bragged about reaching 45,000 people in one month. Their revenue that month was flat. Reach is ego. Conversions are rent money.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I just boost a post instead of running ads through Ads Manager?**

No. Boosting a post is the simplest way to waste money. You get no control over placements, no detailed targeting, no split testing, and no conversion tracking. Boosting sends your budget into Meta's cheapest inventory — often the Audience Network — with no way to turn it off. I've seen businesses spend $500 on a boosted post and get two clicks. Same budget in Ads Manager would have gotten them 30+ leads. Use Ads Manager. It takes an extra 15 minutes to learn. That 15 minutes will save you hundreds.

**Q: I already advertise on Yelp and Google. Do I need Meta too?**

Depends on your business type. If you're a plumber or electrician — people search for you when they have an immediate problem — Google Ads and Yelp are probably enough. If you're a coffee shop, salon, gym, or boutique — businesses people discover, not search for — Meta is where you get found. I had a client who was spending $2,500/month on Google Ads and getting solid results. We added a $750/month Instagram campaign focused on brand awareness and retargeting. Total revenue increased 40% because we were capturing people before they searched, then showing up again when they did.

**Q: Is Facebook dying for small businesses?**

No, but it's changing. Facebook's organic reach for business pages is essentially zero — you have to pay to be seen. The audience skews older (35+), which is actually great for businesses selling home services, healthcare, automotive, or financial planning. I've run Facebook campaigns for a Denver-based HVAC company that delivered $12 in revenue for every $1 spent. Facebook is not dying. It's just no longer free. Neither is Instagram. Neither is anything worth having.

**Q: How much should I spend to see if Meta ads work for my business?**

Minimum $500 total over 30 days on one platform. Do not spend $100 and declare "ads don't work." Meta's algorithm needs data to learn. With $100, you're buying about 1,000 impressions on a good day. That's not enough for any meaningful result. With $500 and proper targeting, a local coffee shop should see 15–20 new customer visits minimum. If you don't, the issue is your offer, your creative, or your targeting — not the platform.

**Q: Should I advertise on Instagram if my customers are mostly women over 50?**

Probably not as your primary platform. Women over 50 spend more time on Facebook and are more likely to take action there. I ran a campaign for a Denver med spa targeting women 45–65. Instagram cost per lead was $38. Facebook cost per lead was $12. We moved 80% of the budget to Facebook and doubled the results. Instagram is not required just because "everyone is on it." Your specific customer may not be.

**Q: Is it worth hiring someone to manage my ads, or can I do it myself?**

You can do it yourself if you're willing to spend 4–6 hours per week learning, testing, and optimizing. Most small business owners don't have that time because they're actually running their business. If your budget is under $1,000/month, do it yourself — the profit margin isn't there to pay a manager. If you're spending $2,000+/month, a good freelancer or agency should pay for themselves by improving your ROI by 30–50%. Just don't hire the agency that promises "millions of impressions" or "viral growth." Hire the one that talks about cost per acquisition and conversion rates.
