## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1 – Treating Your Google Business Profile Like a Parking Space

I met a groomer in Austin, Texas — let’s call her Sarah. She’d been running her shop for three years, had solid word-of-mouth, but new customer calls had flatlined. She blamed the economy. I blamed her Google Business Profile.

Sarah’s GBP listed her hours as “9–5 Mon–Fri.” She actually opened at 7:30 AM on Saturdays (her busiest day) and closed at noon on Wednesdays. The phone number on the profile was her personal cell, which she didn’t answer during grooms. She had one photo — a blurry shot of her storefront from 2019.

The result? Google showed her profile to people searching “dog groomer near me” but when they clicked, they saw wrong hours and a dead phone. They went to the competitor two blocks away who had photos of happy goldendoodles and a “book online” button.

**The fix:** We spent two hours cleaning up her GBP. Changed hours, added 15 recent photos (including before/after shots of grooms), wrote a description that mentioned her specialty (poodle cuts), and turned on booking via Booksy. We also posted a “customer appreciation” update twice a week.

**The outcome:** Within six weeks, Sarah’s GBP impressions jumped from 1,200/month to 4,800. Call requests increased 40%. She tracked $2,300 in additional monthly revenue directly from new customers who found her on Google Maps. Cost of the fix: $0 in software — just her time and my sarcastic commentary.

**What to do instead:** Claim and verify your GBP if you haven’t. Then audit it weekly for the first month. Use Ahrefs’ Local SEO audit to check for NAP consistency — it’ll flag mismatches you didn’t even know existed.

### Mistake #2 – Optimizing for “Dog Grooming” When Your Customers Type “Puppy’s First Groom Near Me”

A groomer in Nashville told me he was “ranking #1 for dog grooming” in his area. Great. Except nobody in Nashville searches for “dog grooming” without adding “Nashville” or their neighborhood. And the people who do search “dog grooming” are usually comparison shoppers looking for the cheapest price — not the loyal clients you want.

He had one page on his site. It said “Dog Grooming Services” with a list of prices. That was it. No pages for different breeds, no mention of “puppy first groom,” no “senior dog spa package.” His Ahrefs keyword data showed that “nashville mobile dog grooming” had 780 monthly searches locally — he didn’t rank for it at all. Neither did “goldendoodle grooming nashville” (490 searches) or “cat grooming nashville” (320 searches).

**The fix:** I helped him create five separate service pages — one for “Puppy & First Time Groom,” one for “Breed-Specific Grooming,” one for “Senior & Special Needs Pets,” one for “Mobile Grooming,” and one for “Cat Grooming.” Each page targeted a specific keyword with local intent. We used Ahrefs’ Keyword Explorer to filter for “Nashville” + service terms and found 23 keywords he wasn’t targeting.

**The outcome:** Organic traffic went from 89 visits/month to 342 within eight weeks. His “puppy first groom” page alone generated 15 bookings in its first month — at $90 per groom, that’s $1,350 in new revenue. The mobile grooming page brought in another $1,200. Total lift: roughly $2,550/month. The cost was his time to write the pages (I edited them) plus $99/month for Ahrefs Lite.

**What to do instead:** Don’t guess what people search. Use Ahrefs’ Keyword Explorer, set your location to your city, and look for keywords with “near me” intent or your city name. Create a dedicated page for each service. If you offer nail trims separately, make a page for that too.

### Mistake #3 – Ignoring Local Citations Until Google Decides You Don’t Exist

A groomer in Denver had been in business for seven years. Her website looked fine. Her GBP was OK. But she told me “Google just doesn’t show me for anything.” I ran a NAP consistency scan using a free tool (BrightLocal offers a basic version). The results were ugly.

Her business name appeared as “Denver Paw Spa” on the website, “Denver Paw Spa LLC” on Yelp, “Denver Paw Spa & Boutique” on Facebook, and “Paw Spa Denver” on a local directory. Her phone number on Yelp was her old number. Her address was listed as “Suite 102” on one site and “Ste 102” on another. Google’s algorithm saw all these mismatches and decided her business was either untrustworthy or had moved — so it suppressed her rankings.

**The fix:** We spent a weekend systematically correcting every citation: Yelp, Facebook, Nextdoor, Yellow Pages, three local pet directories, the chamber of commerce listing, and a few grooming-specific directories like PetGroomer.com. We also added her to Google’s “Local Inventory Ads” (free to set up) and submitted her business to the local pet rescue league’s website (which gave a backlink).

**The outcome:** Three weeks later, her average Google Maps ranking for “denver dog groomer” jumped from position 11 to position 3. Calls increased from 12/week to 28/week. She conservatively estimated $1,800/month in extra revenue from new clients. The cost was about 10 hours of grunt work and $0 in software.

**What to do instead:** Use Ahrefs’ Site Explorer to see which directories are already linking to you. Then use a tool like Moz Local or BrightLocal (both have free trials) to find citation inconsistencies. Fix every single mismatch. Consistency is dull but it works. I’ve seen it kill campaigns at three different clients when ignored.

### Mistake #4 – Building Zero Local Backlinks (Because You Think They Don’t Matter for Local SEO)

A groomer in Portland, Oregon, had a beautiful website, great reviews, and a solid GBP. But she couldn’t break into the top 3 for “portland dog grooming.” I checked her backlink profile in Ahrefs. She had exactly four backlinks — all from spammy “seo directory” sites that were essentially link farms.

Local SEO is not just about your GBP. Google uses backlinks from locally relevant sites as a trust signal. A link from the Portland Business Journal, the local SPCA, or a popular Portland pet blog tells Google “this business matters in this area.” Without them, you’re a voice in the wind.

**The fix:** I helped her identify local organizations she could partner with. She started a “spay and neuter discount day” at the local Humane Society — she donated 10% of her grooming fees on certain days. In return, the Humane Society wrote a blog post about her shop with a link. She then reached out to two local pet blogs (Portland Pet Living and PNW Pet Magazine) and offered them a free groom in exchange for a feature. Both wrote articles with links.

We also got her a listing on the Portland Chamber of Commerce website (cost: $250/year membership) which included a backlink. And she started writing one monthly “pet care tips” post on her own blog, which she promoted on Nextdoor — people in the neighborhood started linking to it.

**The outcome:** After three months, her domain authority (Ahrefs’ DR) went from 8 to 18. She jumped from position 6 to position 2 for “portland dog grooming.” New client inquiries rose 35%. She attributed $3,200 in monthly revenue directly to those links. Cost: $250 chamber membership, a few hours of outreach, and a free groom.

**What to do instead:** Use Ahrefs’ Site Explorer to see who links to your competitors. Filter by geo (e.g., “portland”) — almost always you’ll find local blogs, news sites, or community pages. Reach out to them with a genuine pitch: a free service, a story, or a partnership. Don’t ask for a link. Offer value first.

## How to Use Ahrefs’ Content Gap to Find Keywords Your Competitors Are Ignoring

Most pet groomers target the same five keywords: “dog grooming,” “cat grooming,” “pet groomer near me,” “dog haircut,” and maybe “mobile pet grooming.” That’s a battle you can’t win — 15 other groomers are fighting for the same terms, plus PetSmart, Petco, and half a dozen franchises.

I worked with a groomer in Chicago who was stuck. She was a decent shop with a loyal customer base, but online, she was invisible. I opened Ahrefs’ Content Gap tool, typed her domain into the first box, then typed three of her main competitors’ domains into the “vs” boxes. I hit “Show keywords.”

The results were embarrassing — for her competitors. They were all ranking for “doodle grooming chicago” (1,300 monthly searches locally) and “bernedoodle haircut chicago” (480 searches). She had zero content on those. Nor did she rank for “husky grooming chicago” (340 searches) or “poodle puppy first groom” (290 searches).

**The fix:** We created five new pages targeting those specific gaps. Each page was 800–1,200 words, included photos of the groom she’d done on those breeds, and mentioned Chicago neighborhoods. Then we optimized internal linking to point to those pages from her homepage.

**The outcome:** Within 45 days, she was ranking #1 for “doodle grooming chicago” and #2 for “bernedoodle chicago.” Those two keywords brought in an average of 12 new customers per month, at $105 per groom. That’s $1,260 in new monthly revenue from a 30-minute session in Ahrefs.

**How to do it yourself:**
1. Go to Ahrefs’ **Site Explorer** > **Content Gap**.
2. Enter your domain in the first box.
3. Enter 3–5 competitor domains (find them by searching “dog groomer [your city]” and picking the top results).
4. Set “Target country” to US and location to your city (if available).
5. Sort by volume. Look for keywords with 200+ searches that you don’t rank for but your competitors do.
6. Prioritize keywords that are highly specific — “goldendoodle grooming denver” beats “dog grooming” every time because the intent is crystal clear.

You don’t need to rank for everything. You need to rank for the things your customers actually type. Content Gap shows you exactly what those are.

## Why Reviews Matter More Than You Think (And How to Automate Asking for Them)

A groomer in Brooklyn told me, “I get five-star reviews from my regulars, but nobody new is coming in.” I checked her Google Business Profile — she had 23 reviews total. Her competitor two blocks away had 187.

Google’s local algorithm gives heavy weight to review quantity and recency. A groomer with 200 reviews and a 4.8 average will outrank a groomer with 30 reviews and a 5.0 average — even if the competitor’s content is better. People trust crowds.

**The fix:** I set up a simple review request workflow using **Booksy** (which she already used for scheduling) and **Widewail** (an automated review invitation tool — $150/month, but worth it). For groomers not using Booksy, you can use **Mailchimp** (free up to 500 contacts) or even a manual text message. The key is timing: send the request within one hour of the groom, when the dog still smells like lavender and the owner feels grateful.

The workflow: After every appointment, Booksy sends an automatic email asking for a review. If they don’t respond in 24 hours, they get a text message via Widewail. If they respond with 4 stars or below, Widewail routes it to the groomer’s personal email so she can resolve the issue privately — no public negative review.

**The outcome:** Within three months, her review count went from 23 to 142. Average rating stayed at 4.7. She now appears in the Google “local pack” for the first time. Estimated new customer revenue from those search results: $2,400/month. Cost: $150/month for Widewail, plus five minutes to set up the Booksy integration.

**What to do instead:** Don’t just ask for reviews randomly. Time them, automate them, and respond to every single one — even the negative ones. I’ve seen a well-worded response to a 3-star review bring in more customers than a fresh 5-star. Use Ahrefs’ Site Explorer to check how many review sites your competitors are using (Yelp, Google, Facebook, Nextdoor are the big four). Get on all of them.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need Ahrefs? I’m a small pet groomer with a limited budget.**

You don’t *need* Ahrefs — you can survive without it. But you’re flying blind. The $99/month Lite plan pays for itself if it helps you find one keyword that brings in one new client per month. I’ve seen it happen repeatedly. If $99 is too much, start with Google’s free tools (Search Console, GBP insights, and Keyword Planner) — but you’ll spend more time guessing. Most of the mistakes I described came from groomers who tried to do it without data. Ahrefs is a shortcut to answers. If you’re serious about growing, it’s the best $99 you’ll spend.

**Q: How long until I see results from local SEO?**

The honest answer: 4 to 12 weeks for meaningful movement, depending on your starting point. If your GBP is a mess (see mistake #1), you can fix that in a week and see changes in 2–3 weeks. If your backlink profile is empty, expect 12 weeks. For keyword ranking improvements, allow 6–8 weeks after publishing new pages. The groomer in Nashville saw results in 6 weeks. The one in Portland took 12 because we were building links from scratch. There’s no miracle pill — but you should see *something* within two months. If you don’t, something else is broken.

**Q: Should I pay for Yelp ads?**

Almost certainly not for a local groomer. Yelp’s ad platform is expensive and the clicks are often low-intent. I’ve seen shops spend $500/month on Yelp ads and get maybe $800 in revenue — a 60% ROI, but that same $500 spent on Google Ads with well-targeted local keywords would give you 3x the return. Yelp is useful for listing and reviews — claim your page, add photos, respond to reviews — but treat Yelp ads as a luxury, not a necessity. Test with $100 for two weeks, track calls, then decide.

**Q: I don’t have a website yet. Where should I start?**

Start with a Google Business Profile — it’s free and you can begin attracting customers immediately. Then build a simple website using Squarespace, Wix, or WordPress with a local SEO template. A one-page site with your services, prices, photos, and a booking link is enough. Don’t overthink design. Publish that within two weeks. Then optimize your GBP. Then create the service pages I described earlier. You can always upgrade later. The worst thing you can do is procrastinate on building a site because you’re afraid it won’t be perfect.

**Q: Can I do all this myself, or do I need to hire a local SEO specialist?**

You can do everything I described in this article yourself — if you have the time and patience. The work isn’t technically hard; it’s tedious. If you spend 5 hours a week on it for three months, you’ll see results. If you’d rather focus on grooming dogs (which makes you money), hire someone who specializes in local SEO for small businesses. But don’t hire a random agency that promises “game-changing” results — vet them. Ask if they’ve worked with a groomer before. I’ve seen more small businesses burn $2,000/month on bad SEO agencies than I can count.

**Q: How do I compete with PetSmart and other big chains?**

You don’t compete head-on with PetSmart for generic “dog grooming” — you’ll lose. You compete on specificity, personalization, and local trust. PetSmart can’t answer the phone at 8 PM and say “bring your old golden retriever in 30 minutes” — you can. They can’t know the name of every dog that walks in — you can. Use your Google Business Profile to highlight your specialization (poodles, seniors, mobile service). Target keywords like “doodle grooming [neighborhood]” where PetSmart doesn’t even show up. And get those local backlinks. A link from your local SPCA is worth more to a local search than a thousand links from pet product blogs. Lean into your location and your personality. Chains are bland. You’re not.
