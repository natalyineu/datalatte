## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: The Portfolio That Hides the Process

**The story:** A designer in Austin, TX — let's call her studio "Haven Interiors" — had a gorgeous website. Stunning photos of finished living rooms, kitchens, a bathroom that could make you weep. She'd spent $4,200 on the site plus a $600/month retainer for a photographer. Her monthly leads? Three to five inquiries, mostly from people asking for a single room quote. She was burning out on small projects and barely clearing $48,000/year in profit.

I asked to see her analytics. Her average time on page was 11 seconds. People looked at the hero image and left.

**What went wrong:** She showed only the "after." Potential clients didn't understand what she actually did. They saw a pretty room and thought, "I don't have that budget" or "I don't need that much work." Her portfolio was aspirational, not relatable.

**The fix:** We added a "How This Project Came Together" section underneath every portfolio piece. Showed the awful "before" photos. Listed the budget range ($18,000–$45,000 for that kitchen). Included a timeline (6 weeks from demolition to install). Added a paragraph about the client's biggest worry and how she solved it.

**The outcome:** Within 60 days, her average time on page jumped to 4:20. Inquiries went from 4/month to 14/month. More importantly, the average project value rose from $12,000 to $31,000 because clients self-qualified before contacting her. She raised her prices 22% the following quarter. Annual profit went from $48k to $94k. No extra ad spend.

### Mistake #2: Instagram as a Lead Gen Machine (It's Not)

**The story:** A designer in Portland, OR — "Pine & Pearl Design" — was spending $900/month on Instagram ads and another $500/month on a part-time social media manager. She posted beautifully curated content: color palettes, mood boards, process shots. Her engagement rate was solid at 4.8%. Her bookings? Flat at 2–3 per quarter for two years.

**What went wrong:** Her content was beautiful but useless for decision-making. She posted what designers like to see, not what clients need to hear. Nobody hires an interior designer because they liked a Reel about choosing between Farrow & Ball's "Pigeon" and "Dimpse" (yes, that was an actual post). They hire because they're stressed about a renovation budget, trust someone to manage contractors, or want to avoid a costly mistake.

**The fix:** We did two things. First, we replaced 70% of her content with client-focused educational posts: "Three Things Your Contractor Won't Tell You" (Reel, 47k views), "How to Budget for a Living Room Redesign Without Crying" (carousel, 12k saves), "The One Mistake That Adds $4,000 to Your Kitchen Reno" (static post with link to a free guide). Second, we moved her ad budget from Instagram to Google Search Ads targeting "[city] interior designer budget [low, mid, high]" and "[city] kitchen remodel designer."

**The outcome:** Instagram engagement actually dropped to 2.1%. Bookings went from 3 per quarter to 8 per quarter within three months. Her cost per lead on Google Ads was $18 versus $47 on Instagram. She dropped the social media manager (no hard feelings — the person was good at engagement, just not sales). She's now on track for $220k in revenue this year.

### Mistake #3: "I Don't Do Yelp"

**The story:** A designer in Nashville, TN — "Franklin Street Interiors" — told me she didn't need Yelp. "My clients are referrals." She had 2 reviews on Yelp, both from 2019, one was 3 stars from a client who complained about a shipping delay. She hadn't responded to it. Didn't even know she could.

**What went wrong:** Her ideal client (homeowners in the $750k–$1.2M range, doing whole-home renovations) was absolutely checking Yelp. Not for her specifically, but to vet designers they found on Google or Houzz. That single 3-star review from four years ago, with no response, killed at least one deal I know of — the homeowner told her point-blank, "I saw that review and wondered if you still use that supplier."

**The fix:** I told her to claim her Yelp page, respond to the 2019 review professionally ("We take shipping timelines seriously and now use a different vendor for special orders") and ask her last 10 happy clients to leave a review. Six did. She also started including a link to her Yelp page in her project close-out email with a note: "If you loved working together, a few sentences on Yelp helps other homeowners find us."

**The outcome:** Her Yelp rating went from 3.2 (2 reviews) to 4.8 (8 reviews). In the next six months, she was contacted by four clients who specifically mentioned Yelp. Three signed contracts worth $42k, $38k, and $67k. Total cost: about 45 minutes of her time and a little pride swallowing.

## How to Build a Referral System That Actually Works (Not Just "Ask Your Friends")

Most interior designers treat referrals like birthday wishes — they hope for them, feel grateful when they arrive, but never actually build a system to generate them on purpose. That's leaving money on the table.

Here's a specific system that works. I've seen it generate consistent referrals for a studio in Chicago that went from relying on word-of-mouth (inconsistent) to a repeatable process (predictable 3–5 referrals per month).

**Step 1: Time your ask.** Most designers ask for referrals at project completion. That's the worst time. The client is either exhausted from the process or distracted by their new space. Ask at the moment of highest emotional payoff — usually when they see the final reveal and say "Oh my god" for the first time. That's when they want everyone they know to experience the same feeling. Send a text that same evening: "I'm so glad you love the space. If you know anyone who's been putting off their renovation, I'd love to talk to them. I'll take great care of them."

**Step 2: Make it stupidly easy.** Don't ask them to write a paragraph. Send a template they can forward: "Hey [friend's name], we just finished working with [your name] on our [room]. It turned out better than we imagined. Here's her website if you're thinking about your own project: [link]." The client just copies, pastes, adds the friend's name. That's it.

**Step 3: Incentivize, but not with cash.** Offering $200 for a referral feels transactional. Instead, offer something that reinforces the relationship. For every referral that books a consultation, send the referring client a bottle of wine, a plant from a local nursery, or a $100 gift card to their favorite furniture store. Cost to you: $40–$80. Value of the lead: $5,000–$50,000.

**Step 4: Use your CRM.** If you're not using a simple CRM like Mailchimp, HoneyBook, or even a Google Sheet with reminders, you're losing referrals. Set a trigger: 30 days after project completion, send an email asking if they've had a chance to mention you to anyone. 90 days later, send another. Most referrals happen 3–6 months after a project, when the client's friends are finally ready to start their own renovation.

One designer in Denver added this system and saw referral revenue go from $0 (she never tracked it) to $38,000 in six months. She spent $320 on wine and gift cards.

## Google Ads for Interior Designers: How to Not Waste $1,200/Month

I've managed Google Ads for 12 different interior designers over the years. I've watched roughly $85,000 get lit on fire by people who set up campaigns without understanding how the platform actually works for service businesses. Let me save you the tuition.

**What doesn't work:** Broad match keywords like "interior designer" or "home design services." You'll spend $12–$18 per click on people who are "just looking." I had a client in NYC spend $1,200 in one month and get zero qualified leads. Google happily took her money. Every click was a person in the research phase, three to six months away from hiring anyone.

**What works:** Long-tail keywords with intent signals. These are phrases people type when they're ready to write a check:

- "[city] interior designer kitchen remodel budget [amount]"
- "[city] full house interior designer cost per square foot"
- "best interior designer in [neighborhood] for mid-century modern"
- "interior designer consultation fee [city]"

One designer in Denver targeted "Denver interior designer cost per room" and "Denver whole home interior designer pricing." Her cost per click was $9. Her conversion rate was 14%. She booked two $35,000 projects within 30 days of launching. Total ad spend: $1,400. Revenue attributed: $70,000.

**Set up negative keywords.** You need to tell Google what you don't want. Add negatives for: "cheap," "free," "student," "DIY," "tips," "ideas," "online," "virtual" (unless you do virtual design). One designer in Chicago was spending $400/month on clicks from people searching "interior design ideas for small bedrooms" — people who wanted Pinterest, not a professional. Twenty minutes of negative keyword work saved her $400/month.

**Use location targeting, but tighten it.** Don't target the entire metro area. Target specific zip codes where the average home value is $500k+. On Google Ads, you can layer income data onto your location targeting. I've seen designers reduce wasted spend by 40% just by excluding zip codes where the average household income is under $90k. That's not snobbery — that's math. You don't want leads from people who can't afford you.

**Track phone calls.** Interior design is a phone-call business, not a form-fill business. Most of my clients get 3x more calls than form submissions. Set up call tracking through Google Ads (it's free). If you're not tracking calls, you're flying blind. One designer in Austin thought her ads were "working okay." We added call tracking and discovered she was getting 12 calls per week from warm leads. She just had no way of knowing which came from ads. She doubled her ad spend the next month and got 21 calls.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a website? Can't I just use Instagram?**

Instagram is a storefront. A website is the store. If someone is considering spending $15,000–$150,000 on your services, they're going to Google your name. If they find an Instagram profile with no website, no portfolio page, no way to understand your process or pricing, they move on to the next designer. I've tested this: a designer in Nashville added a simple one-page site with her portfolio, process, and a contact form. Her conversion rate from Instagram DM to booked call went from 2% to 11%. That's the difference between one booked call per week and five.

**Q: How much should I spend on marketing per month?**

For a solo designer, start at $500–$1,000/month total. Split it: $300–$500 on Google Ads, $100–$200 on a CRM tool like Mailchimp or HoneyBook, and the rest on targeted content (photography or copywriting, not generic social media management). Track everything for 90 days. If you're spending $800/month and getting one $25,000 project every two months, your ROI is 15x. If you're getting nothing, kill the ads and try a different approach.

**Q: Should I offer free consultations?**

No. Free consultations attract people who aren't committed. They'll shop you against three other designers, waste your time, and usually go with the cheapest option. Charge $150–$350 for a 60-minute consultation. You'll get fewer inquiries, but the ones you get will be serious. I've seen designers double their close rate just by adding a consultation fee. Your time is worth something. Act like it.

**Q: How do I handle clients who ask for my portfolio before they'll book a consultation?**

Send them a PDF of 5–7 projects that are similar in scope and budget to what they're describing. If they're doing a $60k kitchen, don't send them photos of a $20k bathroom. If they want a whole-home remodel, don't show single-room projects. In the PDF, include the project budget, timeline, and a short quote from the client. That builds trust without giving away everything for free. If they say "that's not enough," they're probably not your client.

**Q: Yelp or Google Reviews — which matters more?**

Both, but Google Reviews matter more for SEO (they help you rank in local search) and Yelp matters more for social proof (people check it to vet you). Get at least 10 Google reviews and respond to every single one. Get 8–10 Yelp reviews, respond to any negative ones professionally, and move on. Don't pay for Yelp ads unless you're getting consistent organic reviews first — otherwise you're paying to send people to a page with 2 ratings.

**Q: What's the one thing I should stop doing immediately?**

Posting generic inspirational content on social media. "Here are 10 color trends for 2024." "How to style a bookshelf." That content gets likes from other designers and homeowners who aren't ready to hire. It doesn't get you clients. Instead, post content that solves a specific, painful problem your ideal client has right now: "My contractor said this wall isn't load-bearing but I'm not sure — here's how to check" or "The 3 biggest budget mistakes I see in kitchen renovations." That's what gets saved, shared, and messaged.
