## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've managed enough towing service accounts over the years to have a pretty solid list of what goes wrong. Most of these mistakes aren't unique to towing, but the consequences hit harder because your customer is actively panicking in a parking lot somewhere.

### Mistake 1: Bidding on a Service Radius That's Bigger Than Your Tow Truck Fleet

A client in Nashville came to me after blowing through $1,200 in three weeks with exactly zero tows booked. He was targeting the entire Nashville metro area plus a 50-mile radius. Sounded reasonable — more drivers, more potential breakdowns, right?

Here's what he didn't account for: his response time commitments. His website promised "30-minute arrival," and his ads said the same thing. But he only had two trucks for that entire radius. Anyone searching from Franklin or Murfreesboro was getting an ETA of 45–60 minutes, which meant they either called someone else or waited and left a 1-star review.

The fix was painful but necessary: we cut his target radius to 15 miles from his central garage, and we added location extensions so the ad showed his actual distance from the searcher. His budget dropped from $500/month to $350/month.

**Outcome:** $1,200 wasted becomes $1,800 in monthly revenue from tows he could actually reach. Cost per lead dropped from $38 to $12. He also stopped getting calls from people who were technically in his "area" but 45 minutes away.

**What to do instead:** Map your actual service radius based on your number of trucks and average traffic patterns. If you have one truck, don't pretend you cover the whole county. Be honest in your ad copy about availability, and use negative location targeting to exclude zip codes where your response time would kill the deal anyway.

### Mistake 2: Only Using Broad Match Keywords and Letting Google "Figure It Out"

I had a towing client in Austin who was convinced that the best strategy was "just let Google do the work." He set his keywords to broad match, no negatives, and wondered why he was getting calls from people asking if he could jump start a golf cart.

His ad was triggered for "towing services," which is fine. But broad match also served his ad for "towing my boat to the marina," "flatbed trailer rental," and "roadside assistance for motorcycles." None of those are towing a disabled vehicle — which is the only thing he did.

In one month, 60% of his ad spend went to non-relevant searches. That's real money. His budget was $800/month, so $480 was essentially lost. He did pick up some odd jobs, but the conversion rate on those was terrible because he didn't actually want to do them.

**Fix:** Switched to phrase match for core keywords like "towing service near me," "emergency towing," and "24 hour tow truck." Added about 50 negative keywords (jump start, lockout, winching, RV transport, boat towing, junk car removal, motorcycle pickup, etc.).

**Outcome:** Click-through rate went from 1.8% to 4.2%. Cost per conversion dropped from $28 to $11. He stopped getting annoyed calls at 2 AM from people who just needed a battery jump. Revenue from actual tows increased 35% over two months.

**What to do instead:** Start with phrase match for your core terms. Add exact match for your highest-converting phrases. Build a negative keyword list from day one — and keep adding to it as you see what people actually search.

### Mistake 3: Sending Towing Ad Clicks to Your Homepage

This one kills me every time. A shop owner in Portland was running Google Ads and directing all traffic to his homepage. His homepage was a general "we fix cars" page with nine menu items, a photo gallery, and a blog about winter tire maintenance. The phone number was buried in the footer.

Someone searching "towing near me" at 11 PM after their car died on I-5 does not want to scroll past three paragraphs about oil changes. They want to see a phone number, a clear call-to-action, and ideally a button that says "Call Now."

His bounce rate from ad traffic was 84%. Average time on page: 11 seconds. He was paying for clicks that literally did nothing.

**Fix:** Created a dedicated landing page — one page, two sections only: "Need a tow? Call (503) XXX-XXXX" and "We cover Portland metro, 24/7." Added a click-to-call button, a Google Maps embed showing his shop location, and a simple form for non-emergencies. No menu. No blog. No photos of his dog.

**Outcome:** Bounce rate dropped to 41%. Call-through rate increased 3x. He started tracking which ads actually led to calls instead of just guessing. His conversion rate went from less than 5% to about 18% within two weeks.

**What to do instead:** Build a separate landing page for your towing services. It should have one job: get someone to call you. Remove navigation, remove distractions, and make the phone number the biggest thing on the page. If you're running call-only ads (which I recommend for emergency services), make sure your call tracking is set up before you launch.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Time-Based Scheduling

A towing company in Chicago was running ads 24/7, but their staff only answered phones from 8 AM to 8 PM. After-hours calls went to voicemail that said, "We'll call you back during business hours."

If you're stuck on the side of Lake Shore Drive at 2 AM, a voicemail might as well be a middle finger. They burned through $400/month in nighttime ad spend for calls that never happened.

**Fix:** Set ad scheduling to run only during their phone coverage hours. Then we added a separate experiment: run a low-budget call-only campaign from 8 PM to midnight on weekends, staffed by an on-call driver willing to pick up. The driver got paid $20/hour to be on standby plus commission on any tow.

**Outcome:** Monthly ad spend dropped to $650 (from $1,000), but revenue increased because the calls that did come in actually got answered. The on-call driver experiment generated $1,200 in the first month alone. They eventually expanded to 24/7 coverage with a rotating schedule.

**What to do instead:** Be honest about when you answer the phone. If you don't offer 24/7 service, don't run ads at 3 AM. If you do offer it, make sure someone is actually available — not just an answering service that takes a message.

## Setting Up Your Campaign Structure for Maximum Efficiency

The way you structure your Google Ads account matters more for towing than for most businesses. Here's why: towing is an emergency service with a hyper-local radius, and your campaigns need to reflect that ruthlessly.

### Separate Campaigns for Different Service Types

Most towing companies I've worked with try to cram everything into one campaign. That makes it nearly impossible to see what's working.

Instead, set up at least two campaigns:

**Campaign 1: Emergency Towing (high-intent, immediate need)**
- Keywords: "towing near me," "emergency tow truck," "24 hour towing"
- Bid strategy: Maximize clicks with a bid cap, or Target CPA if you have enough conversion data
- Ad schedule: Only during hours you can actually dispatch
- Budget: 70% of your total

**Campaign 2: Planned Towing / Long-Distance**
- Keywords: "long distance towing," "flatbed towing quote," "transport my car"
- Bid strategy: Manual CPC or Target CPA
- Ad schedule: Broader — people plan these during the day
- Budget: 30% of your total

A client in Denver ran both campaigns with a combined budget of $900/month. The emergency campaign generated 75% of the calls but only 60% of the cost because the bids were more efficient. The planned towing campaign had a higher cost-per-click but a better conversion rate for higher-ticket jobs (long-distance hauling at $400–$800 per job vs. $150 for a local tow).

### Location Targeting: Get Specific

Google lets you target by city, zip code, or a radius around your garage. Do not just target your entire city — especially if your city is sprawled out like Austin, Houston, or Los Angeles.

Here's what actually worked for a towing client in Phoenix: we broke the metro area into four zones, each with its own campaign. Each zone had its own budget, ad copy, and tracking number. The client could see exactly which neighborhoods performed best.

South Phoenix had the highest call volume but the lowest average ticket ($90 per tow). North Scottsdale had 40% fewer calls but the average ticket was $220. Without the segmented campaigns, they wouldn't have known to allocate more budget to Scottsdale during evening hours (when people were stuck in the hills after sunset).

**Tool recommendation:** Use Google's location report in the Ads interface to see which zip codes drive the most conversions. Then adjust your bids by location. It takes 15 minutes once a month and can save you from wasting budget on areas you can't serve well.

### Call-Only Ads Are Non-Negotiable

For emergency towing, call-only ads outperform every other format. I ran a test for a client in San Antonio: call-only ads vs. standard text ads with a click-to-call button. The call-only ads had a 40% higher conversion rate and a 22% lower cost per lead.

The reason is simple: someone who needs a tow is on their phone, they're stressed, and they want to talk to a human. They don't want to click through to a website and then find the phone number. Call-only ads remove that friction completely.

One caveat: Google requires you to have a phone number that can receive calls. If you're using a call tracking service (and you should be), make sure it integrates properly. I've seen accounts get disapproved because the forwarding number wasn't set up correctly.

## Tracking What Actually Matters (And Ignoring Vanity Metrics)

Most small business owners I talk to are looking at the wrong numbers. They get excited about impressions and click-through rates, then wonder why their phone isn't ringing.

### What to Actually Track

**Cost per call** — This is the only metric that matters for emergency towing. If you're paying $15 per call and your average tow is $120, you're in good shape. If you're paying $50 per call, something is broken.

**Call duration** — A 30-second call is usually someone asking for a quote and hanging up. A 3-minute call is someone who actually needs a tow. Google Ads can track call duration if you set up call extensions properly. Filter out calls under 60 seconds when evaluating performance.

**Close rate** — This requires a small amount of manual tracking. Have your dispatcher ask, "How did you hear about us?" for each call. Track it in a spreadsheet or use a CRM like Square Appointments or even a simple Google Form. A client in Nashville found that 30% of his call-only ad leads were actually booking the tow. That meant his cost per booked job was about $35 — well worth the $500/month he was spending.

**Response time** — If your ad promises "30-minute arrival" but it takes you 45 minutes consistently, you're spending money to create bad reviews. Track your actual arrival times and adjust your ad copy (or your dispatch process) to match reality.

### What to Ignore

**Impressions** — I don't care if your ad showed up 10,000 times. I care if those impressions led to calls.

**Average position** — Google removed this metric from the interface for a reason. It was misleading. Focus on click-through rate and conversion rate instead.

**Quality Score** — Yes, it affects your cost per click. No, you should not obsess over it. If your ad is relevant, your landing page works, and you're getting calls, your Quality Score will be fine. I've seen accounts with Quality Scores of 6 that outperformed accounts with QS 9 because the conversion tracking was better.

### How to Set Up Tracking Without a Developer

You don't need to hire someone to set up call tracking. Here's the low-budget version:

1. Get a Google Voice number (free) for your towing ads.
2. Set up a free Google Ads account and use the website call conversion tracking option.
3. If you want more detail, use CallRail (starts at $25/month) or a simpler tool like Phone.com. Both integrate with Google Ads directly.

A client in Austin spent $30/month on a call tracking service and discovered that 40% of his call volume came from repeat customers — which meant he was essentially paying for his own reputation. He stopped running ads to customers in his existing service area and redirected budget to new neighborhoods. That one change saved him $150/month.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is Google Ads even worth it for a small towing company with one truck?**

Yes, if you do it right. I managed a campaign for a single-truck operator in Denver on a $300/month budget. He was targeting a 10-mile radius and running call-only ads. His cost per call averaged $11, and his average tow was $110. That's a 10x return on ad spend. The key is keeping your radius tight and only running ads during hours you can actually respond. If you're the only driver, you might need to pause ads when you're already on a job — you can do that with ad scheduling or by manually pausing campaigns.

**Q: How do I compete with big towing companies that have way more budget?**

You can't outspend them, so don't try. Focus on what they do poorly: response time and customer experience. Use ad copy that highlights your specific advantages: "I'm the driver who answers — not a call center." "Average arrival time 18 minutes." "Family-owned, no automated systems." Smaller companies can also use location targeting more aggressively. A national chain might target the whole city; you can target just the 5-mile radius around your garage and out-bid them at the hyper-local level.

**Q: Should I use Yelp ads instead of Google Ads?**

You should use both, but for different reasons. Google Ads will drive the majority of your emergency towing calls — when someone searches "towing near me," they're calling the first result. Yelp is better for reputation building and planned services (long-distance towing, specialty hauling). I've found that Yelp ads for towing have a higher cost per click ($8–12 vs. $4–6 on Google) but lower conversion rates because the intent isn't as high. Start with Google Ads. Once that's working, test Yelp with a small budget ($200/month) to see if it drives qualified calls.

**Q: What's a realistic monthly budget for towing Google Ads?**

For a single-location towing company in a mid-sized US city (Austin, Nashville, Portland), plan on $400–$800/month minimum. That will get you enough data to optimize within 4–6 weeks. In a larger market like Chicago or Los Angeles, expect $800–$1,500/month to be competitive. If you're in a small town with 50,000 people, $250–$400 might work fine. The biggest mistake I see is starting with $100/month and concluding "Google Ads doesn't work" — that's not enough budget to generate statistically meaningful data.

**Q: I tried Google Ads before and got no results. What did I do wrong?**

Probably one of three things: wrong keywords, bad location targeting, or no conversion tracking. Without tracking, you're flying blind. You might have gotten calls but didn't know they came from ads. You might have been showing up for searches like "towing tips" instead of "tow truck near me." Or you might have been targeting the entire state instead of your actual service area. I had a client in Charlotte who swore Google Ads "scammed him" — turns out his location targeting was set to "people interested in your location" instead of "people in your location." That one setting change doubled his call volume in a week.

**Q: How do I handle after-hours towing calls without paying for wasted clicks?**

Set up a call-only campaign with ad scheduling that matches your actual phone coverage. If you don't offer 24/7 service, don't run ads after 9 PM. If you do offer 24/7 but take calls home with you, make sure your call tracking number forwards to your cell. One option: use the "call extensions" setting to show a different phone number during off-hours — maybe an answering service that can book the tow for the next morning. Just be transparent in the ad: "Next-day towing — call now to schedule."
