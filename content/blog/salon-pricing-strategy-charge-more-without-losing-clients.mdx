### Step 1: Reassess Your Pricing Structure

Revisit your menu to determine which services are most profitable and which ones are struggling to break even. Consider removing or modifying services that aren't driving significant revenue.

<Callout type="tip">Use data to inform your pricing decisions. Analyze your sales reports to identify top-performing services and allocate resources accordingly.</Callout>

### Step 2: Introduce Tiered Pricing

Offer tiered pricing for services to cater to different customer segments. This approach allows you to charge more for premium services while still providing options for budget-conscious clients.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the ideal price increase for a salon to implement without losing clients?

A price increase of 10-15% is a good starting point for salons looking to boost revenue without alienating their loyal clients. This increase can be implemented by raising prices on individual services or by bundling services together. For example, a $50 haircut could be raised to $55 or $60.

### How can salons justify higher prices to their clients?

Salons can justify higher prices by highlighting the value they provide, such as the quality of their products, the expertise of their stylists, and the personalized service they offer. They can also emphasize the benefits of their services, such as the time-saving convenience of a quick trim or the confidence boost that comes with a new hairstyle. Additionally, salons can offer loyalty programs or rewards to retain existing clients.

### What are some common mistakes salons make when implementing a higher pricing strategy?

Common mistakes include raising prices too high, too quickly, which can lead to a loss of clients; not communicating the value of the services clearly; and not offering any incentives or rewards to loyal clients. Salons should also be aware of their competition and ensure that their prices are competitive.

### How can salons use data to support their pricing decisions?

Salons can use data to track their revenue, expenses, and client satisfaction to inform their pricing decisions. For example, they can use data to determine which services are most profitable and which services are most in demand. They can also use data to identify areas where they can cut costs and improve efficiency.

### What are some alternative pricing strategies that salons can consider?

Alternative pricing strategies include value-based pricing, where salons charge based on the value they provide to clients; tiered pricing, where salons offer different levels of service at different price points; and subscription-based pricing, where clients pay a recurring fee for regular services. Salons can also consider offering discounts or promotions to attract new clients or reward loyal clients.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: The Across-the-Board Price Hike

I watched a salon owner in Austin, Texas raise every service by $12 on the same Tuesday. She sent a single email the night before: "New prices start tomorrow." By Friday, she'd lost seven regulars — three of them clients she'd had since she opened the doors in 2018.

Here's what actually happened. The $12 increase on a $45 haircut is a 27% jump. On her $200 balayage, it's 6%. The haircut clients felt it. The color clients barely noticed. But she applied the same dollar amount to everything because "it felt fair." Fair isn't the goal. Profitable is.

The fix: tier your increases by service type and client lifetime value. Raise color services first — your clients who book $200+ visits are booking you for your skill, not your price. Raise haircuts later, by a smaller percentage, and give existing clients three weeks' notice. When that Austin salon owner re-priced carefully — 8% on color, 5% on cuts, 22% on add-ons like scalp treatments — she kept 95% of her clients and added $780/month in revenue. The clients who left were the ones booking the cheapest services anyway.

### Mistake 2: Forgetting Your Costs Changed Last Year

A pet groomer in Portland, Oregon kept the same prices from 2021 through early 2024. She told me she "didn't want to be the expensive place." I pulled her numbers. Her shampoo costs had gone up 40%. Her rent had increased $350/month. She was paying $4.20 more per bag of treats. She was literally losing money on every large-breed groom and didn't know it.

I've seen this at three different clients. You're not "being fair" by keeping prices steady — you're subsidizing your customers' haircuts with your own savings. The uncomfortable truth is that most small businesses should have raised prices by 15–20% just to maintain their 2021 margins.

The fix: square up (pun intended) with your actual numbers. Open your Square or Booksy dashboard. Pull your cost of goods for the last six months. If you're a salon, your color, toner, shampoo, foils, gloves, capes — the stuff you use per client. Add it up. Then add 30% for overhead allocation. If your $75 haircut costs you $58 in product, labor, and overhead, you're making $17. That's roughly 23% margin. For a service business, that's dangerous. Raise it to $92, you're at 37% margin. The groomer in Portland raised her large-breed grooms from $85 to $105. She lost exactly two clients in three months. Her net income went up $1,400/month.

### Mistake 3: Panic-Pricing Based on What the Salon Across the Street Charges

A fitness studio owner in Denver told me she couldn't raise her monthly membership because "CorePower is $10 cheaper." I asked how many of her clients actually went to CorePower. She didn't know. I asked if her studio had childcare, which CorePower didn't. Yes. I asked if her classes capped at 12 people, while CorePower packed 40 into a room. Yes. She was competing on price against a completely different product.

This is the mistake that kills campaigns at three different clients before they even start. You look at a competitor's menu and assume your clients are comparison shopping the way they compare laundry detergent. They're not. Your clients chose you because of something specific — your parking situation, your playlist, your cancellation policy, the way you greet them by name. That has a price.

The fix: audit your actual competitive set. Not "salons in my zip code." Salons within five blocks, with the same service level, same clientele age, same vibe. Write down your five real differentiators. If you can't name five, fix that before you touch pricing. That Denver studio raised memberships from $129 to $149 and kept 94% retention. The ones who left? Three people who moved out of state.

### Mistake 4: Raising Prices Without Changing Anything About the Experience

This one is personal. I once consulted for a nail salon in Chicago — beautiful space, great work, mid-range pricing. The owner raised all services by $8 and sent a printed card with new prices. No new towels. No upgraded lotion. No different music. No explanation. Six weeks later, her Yelp rating dropped from 4.7 to 3.9. The reviews said things like "still using the same old polish but paying more."

She fixed it by adding a 10-minute hand massage to every service, switching to a better brand of lotion (clients could smell the difference), and training her staff to greet every client by name. Then she raised prices again — another $5 — and framed it as "investing in your experience." Yelp rating went back up to 4.6. Revenue increased $2,100 in the first month.

## How to Use Booking Data to Find Your Hidden Pricing Opportunities

Most salon owners look at their pricing and see a list of services. I look at it and see a list of decisions made on different days, under different pressures, at different times of the year. Your menu probably doesn't reflect reality.

Open your Booksy or Square Appointments dashboard. Sort by revenue per hour, not total revenue. This is the number that matters. A $150 service that takes 90 minutes is $100/hour. A $75 service that takes 30 minutes is $150/hour. You might be making more money on the cheap service.

Here's what I found for a salon in Nashville. Their $55 blowout took 25 minutes. Revenue per hour: $132. Their $200 partial highlight took two hours. Revenue per hour: $100. They were booking highlight appointments three weeks out, turning away blowout clients who would walk in same-day. They had the demand wrong because they priced the time wrong.

The fix: raise your lower-revenue-per-hour services first. That Nashville salon raised their partial highlight from $200 to $235. They lost two clients out of 40. Revenue per hour went to $117.5. They also opened up more same-day availability for blowouts. Total monthly revenue increase: $1,800.

Another number to check: your no-show rate by service price. At a salon in San Diego, the $200+ services had a 3% no-show rate. The $35 brow wax had an 18% no-show rate. The clients booking cheap services were the ones wasting appointment slots. The owner moved brow waxes to a same-day booking only model and required a credit card for any appointment under $50. No-shows on inexpensive services dropped to 6%. That reclaimed 12 appointment slots per week, which she sold as premium services. Worth $1,200/month.

## The Bundle Strategy That Increased Average Ticket by 38%

I'm not a fan of the word "upsell" because most business owners do it wrong. They tack on a $15 add-on at checkout and hope. I've seen this fail at four different clients.

The better approach: bundle services into a single, time-blocked appointment that you price below what the services would cost separately — but above what the client currently spends per visit.

A salon in Atlanta tested this. They noticed their average ticket was $95. Most clients came in for one service. The owner created a "Full Refresh" package: haircut, gloss treatment, and blow dry. Booked in a 90-minute slot. Priced at $145 — separate services would be $165. She promoted it only to her email list of 800 people. In the first month, 47 people booked it. Average ticket for those 47 clients went from $95 to $132 — a 38% increase.

Why it worked: clients didn't feel upsold. They felt like they got a deal on a better experience. The salon made more money per hour. The client left happier because they got more done in one trip. Win.

You can do this with any service mix. Haircut + deep conditioning treatment. Manicure + paraffin wax. Brow wax + lash lift. The key is packaging services that make sense together, pricing them higher than your average ticket but lower than à la carte, and only offering the bundle to clients who already trust you.

Pro tip from my agency days: use Mailchimp or Klaviyo to send a one-time offer to clients who haven't booked in 60+ days. Say "We've been testing a new combination service and want your feedback — first 20 bookings get it at the introductory price." You get data, they get a deal, you protect your regular pricing for normal bookings.

## How to Use Paid Ads to Test a Price Increase Before You Commit

You don't have to guess how clients will react to a higher price. Run an ad.

Set up a Google Ads campaign for your highest-margin service. Target people searching for "salon near me" or "[service] + [your city]." Create two ad groups: one with your current price, one with your proposed higher price. Run both for two weeks. Here's what actually happens.

A salon in Brooklyn did this with their balayage service. Current price was $195. Proposed price was $215. The ad with the $195 price got more clicks. The ad with the $215 price got fewer clicks — but the conversion rate was 12% higher. The clients clicking the $215 ad knew the price upfront, wanted that service, and booked. The $195 ad attracted more lookers, not more bookers.

The owner raised her price to $215 for new clients only. Existing clients kept their old price for three months. After three months, existing clients moved to $205. New clients stayed at $215. After six months, everyone was at $215. Total client loss: 3 people. Revenue increase: $2,400/month.

If you don't want to run Google Ads, use Instagram or Facebook. Run a story poll: "We're thinking about adjusting our pricing for [service]. Which range feels fair to you?" Give three price ranges, all higher than your current one. You'll see what the market tolerates without committing to anything.

A salon in San Antonio used this method. They discovered their clients would accept a 12% increase on color services but only 5% on haircuts. They raised accordingly. No client complaints in three months.

## How to Handle the "I've Been Coming Here for Years" Conversation

One of the questions I get most from consulting clients is how to tell a long-time client that their favorite service now costs more. The answer is not "I'm sorry." The answer is "Thank you for being here."

Here's a script that works: "I want you to know that I value your business more than I can say. Because of that, I want to be transparent with you about something. The costs for the products and supplies I use have gone up, and I've made the decision to adjust my pricing so I can continue to give you the best possible experience. Starting next month, [service] will be [new price]. I'm offering you a four-week grace period at the current rate because I appreciate your loyalty. Thank you for understanding."

I've seen this exact script used by a salon owner in Phoenix. She had a client who'd been coming for eight years, every five weeks. The client's service was going from $85 to $98. The owner used the script, gave the grace period, and the client said "Thanks for telling me ahead of time. You're worth it." She didn't lose one long-term client.

Compare that to the salon owner in Seattle who sent a group text to all her clients: "Prices going up $10 starting Monday." She lost four long-term clients within a week. The difference is respect and transparency.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much can I raise prices before clients start leaving?**
Every market is different, but in my experience across 15+ clients, most service businesses can raise prices 10–15% without losing more than 5% of clients. The clients who leave are usually the ones booking the cheapest services with the lowest lifetime value. You'll make more money even after losing them. I've seen a salon in Chicago raise prices 18% and lose 3% of clients. Revenue went up $3,800/month.

**Q: Should I raise prices for existing clients or only new clients?**
Raise prices for new clients immediately. Give existing clients a 30–60 day grace period at the old rate. After that, move them to the new price. You communicate the change clearly and respectfully. The "new clients only" approach works in theory but creates a confusing two-tier system that irritates everyone. Just be direct.

**Q: What if my competitors are cheaper?**
Let them be cheaper. Your competition isn't the salon across the street — it's the salon that offers the same experience you do. If you're better, you charge more. If you're not better, work on that before you touch pricing. I've never seen a client succeed by racing to the bottom on price. I've seen many fail doing exactly that.

**Q: Should I offer discounts or coupons to offset the price increase?**
No. Discounts train clients to wait for the sale. You want clients who book because they value your work, not because they got a coupon. If you must offer something, make it a loyalty program — book five services, get one add-on free. That rewards repeat business without discounting your core services.

**Q: How do I know if my prices are too low right now?**
If you're fully booked and not making enough money, your prices are too low. If you have open appointment slots and your profit margins are thin, you have a marketing problem, not a pricing problem. If both are true, fix your operations first, then raise prices.

**Q: Will I lose clients if I raise prices during a recession?**
Clients care about value, not price. During economic downturns, they cut discretionary spending — but a haircut or blowout is often the last thing they cut because it's tied to confidence and looking professional for work. I raised prices for a salon in Miami during the 2020 slowdown. We lost exactly one client. The others stayed because they trusted us. If you deliver a great experience, your clients will pay for it even when money is tight.
