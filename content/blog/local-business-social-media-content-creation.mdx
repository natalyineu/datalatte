## Creating a Social Media Content Strategy
To create a social media content strategy that drives real results, you need to start by understanding your target audience. Who are your ideal customers? What are their pain points, interests, and hobbies? What kind of content are they already engaging with on social media? For example, if you own a coffee shop in a busy city like New York, your target audience might be young professionals who are looking for a quick caffeine fix on their way to work. You could create content that showcases your coffee shop's convenient location, fast service, and high-quality coffee.

## Types of Social Media Content
There are many different types of social media content you can create, including photos, videos, blog posts, and more. The key is to experiment and find what works best for your business. For example, if you own a hair salon, you could create before-and-after photos of your clients' hair transformations. If you own a pet grooming business, you could create videos of happy pets playing and getting groomed. <Callout type="tip">Remember to always use high-quality visuals and captions that are informative and engaging.</Callout>

To ensure that you're consistently creating and publishing high-quality social media content, you need to create a content calendar. ## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched small businesses burn money on social media in ways that make me reach for the second coffee. Here are the three most painful mistakes I've seen — with names changed, but numbers real.

### Mistake #1: Posting Seven Days a Week — Austin, TX Coffee Shop

A specialty coffee shop in Austin called me after six months of "going hard on Instagram." They were posting every single day. Reels every morning. Carousels at lunch. A Sunday quote graphic because someone told them that's what "engaging" looks like.

The owner, Sarah, was exhausted. She was spending 12 hours a week creating content. Her husband was filming latte art from fifteen different angles. They were paying a VA $600/month to schedule posts.

Their engagement rate: 1.2%.

Their foot traffic from social: impossible to measure because there was nothing to measure.

**The fix:** We cut posting to three times per week. Monday: one photo of a drink with a local ingredient (she used a Texas pecan syrup). Wednesday: a 15-second video showing the actual morning rush. Friday: a single user-generated photo from a customer who tagged them.

We redirected the $600/month to a $300 Google Ads campaign targeting "coffee near [street name]" within a 2-mile radius. The other $300 went to a Booksy booking system for their pastry pop-ups — they'd never tracked how many people reserved pastries in advance.

**The outcome:** After two months, their Instagram engagement rate hit 4.8%. The Google Ads drove 47 phone calls about catering orders in one month. They tracked $3,800 in revenue directly attributable to the Google campaign. Sarah got 9 hours of her week back.

**What I actually learned:** You don't need more content. You need less content that actually works. Most small business owners confuse "posting frequency" with "strategy." They're not the same thing.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Negative Reviews — Nashville Pet Groomer

A pet grooming business in Nashville came to me panicked. They had 87 Google reviews. Average rating: 3.2 stars. A competitor three blocks away had 4.7 stars on 200 reviews.

The owner, Marcus, was furious about three bad reviews. One mentioned a cut that took too long. Another claimed a dog came home with a matted spot. A third was a one-star rant from someone who'd never even visited (Marcus had the booking records to prove it).

His response to all three? Silence. He thought engaging with negative reviews would "make it worse."

**The real problem:** Those three negative reviews were pinned to the top of his profile by Google's algorithm because they had recent replies from the business — wait, no. He hadn't replied at all. Google was showing them because they had more recent activity (new likes, new responses from other users). The positive reviews were old.

**The fix:** I told Marcus to reply to every negative review within 48 hours. Not a corporate-sounding script. Real responses:

- "You're right, the wait was too long that Saturday. I've adjusted our scheduling. Here's my direct number if you want to come back."
- "I'm sorry about the matting. That's on me. We've updated our intake checklist so this doesn't happen again."

For the fake review: we flagged it to Google with booking timestamps attached. It was removed in six days.

Then we launched a simple campaign: every customer who checked out got a Square receipt text with a direct link to leave a review. Not a QR code on the counter. Not a sign. A text message.

**The outcome:** Two months later, Marcus had 172 reviews with a 4.4 average. The three resolved negative reviews stayed up — but they now showed a reply thread where he handled it professionally. Customers mentioned those replies in new reviews: "Saw how the owner handled that complaint. Brought my dog here. No regrets."

Estimated revenue lift: 22% increase in new customer bookings over 90 days. About $4,200/month. Cost: zero dollars.

**What I actually learned:** Negative reviews aren't the problem. Ignoring them is the problem. A replied-to complaint is better than a perfect score with crickets.

### Mistake #3: Trying to Be a Content Brand — Portland, OR Fitness Studio

A boutique fitness studio in Portland hired a content creator. They paid $2,500/month for "a full content calendar" — Reels, TikToks, influencer collabs. The content was gorgeous. Cinematic slow-motion of people lifting weights. Aerial drone shots of the studio parking lot (yes, really). A three-minute "day in the life" of an instructor that got 47 views.

The owner, Jenna, was spending $30,000/year on content that wasn't booking a single class.

**The fix:** I asked Jenna one question: "What does a person need to know before they walk through your door?"

She listed: class time, class type, instructor name, price, what to wear, where to park.

We killed the entire content package. Instead, we told her to post three things per week:

1. Tuesday morning: A 30-second video showing exactly what happens in a specific class — "This is 7am Hot HIIT. Here's the warmup. Here's the clock. Here's when we stretch." Filmed on an iPhone.
2. Thursday afternoon: A static image with the class schedule for next week. That's it. No quote. No "motivation."
3. Saturday morning: One current client talking to camera for 20 seconds about why they come.

Total weekly content creation time: 45 minutes.

We redirected the $2,500/month into a $500 Facebook ads budget targeting women 25–45 within 5 miles of the studio, interested in "yoga," "Pilates," or "fitness classes." The remaining $2,000 went into the business.

**The outcome:** In 60 days, Jenna's class attendance went from 60% capacity to 85% capacity. The Facebook ads drove 34 booked intro sessions at $29 each — $986 in direct revenue, plus 14 converted to monthly memberships at $150/month. The month-one recurring revenue from those conversions: $2,100.

Cost of the new content: $0. Time: 45 minutes/week. Results: measurable.

**What I actually learned:** Small businesses don't need a brand campaign. They need a "show up on Tuesday" campaign. Your customer's question isn't "who are you as a brand." It's "when does the 6pm class start, and do I need to bring a mat."

## How to Measure What Actually Works

Most social media advice tells you to track "engagement rate" and "reach." Those are vanity numbers. Here's what I actually track for local businesses.

### The Only Three Numbers That Matter

**Direct Attribution:** How many people clicked a link, called a phone number, or walked through your door because of a specific post. I use UTM parameters in every link. If you're posting an Instagram story with a link to book, it should have `?utm_source=instagram&utm_medium=story&utm_campaign=weekly_promo`. Mailchimp and Square both support this natively.

**Cost Per Acquired Customer (CPAC):** Take everything you spend on content creation (your time included — I value mine at $75/hour), add your ad spend, divide by the number of new customers you can prove came from social. If your CPAC is higher than your average ticket price, you're subsidizing the sale. That's fine for month one if retention is high. But if it's still high in month three, stop.

**Repeat Engagement Rate:** Not likes. The percentage of people who saw a post and then came back to your profile within 7 days. This tells you if your content is memorable enough to drive a second look. Instagram doesn't show this natively — I use a third-party tool called Later for this metric. It costs $25/month. Worth it.

### Real Example: Denver Hair Salon

A hair salon in Denver was posting beautiful photos of haircuts. High engagement. But bookings weren't moving. We checked their Instagram Insights:

- Reach per post: 2,800 average
- Website clicks per month: 47

We switched to posting one "booking reminder" reel per week — literally a phone screen recording of them opening the Booksy app, showing the next available slot, saying "Tuesday at 2pm is open." No music. No effects. 12 seconds.

Website clicks per month: 312. Direct bookings from that reel: 19.

CPAC on that single post: $0. Return: $2,375 in booked appointments (19 × $125 average ticket).

The beautiful haircut photos still got more likes. Nobody booked from them.

## Repurposing Content Without Burning Out

I have never met a small business owner who has "too much time for content." The fix isn't creating more. It's creating once and using the material across multiple channels.

### The One-Video Workflow

Film one 60-second video a week. Here's what you do with it:

- **Instagram Reel:** Post the full 60 seconds. Add captions (I use CapCut free version for this — takes 5 minutes).
- **Facebook Page:** Cross-post the same Reel. Facebook kills reach on links but boosts native video.
- **TikTok:** Post the same video. Yes, even if it has Instagram captions on it. Small businesses overthink this. The same video works across platforms for local content.
- **Email Newsletter:** Embed the video in your Mailchimp email. I've seen click rates go from 22% to 41% on emails with a video vs. a static image.
- **Google Business Profile:** Upload the video as a new post. Google loves fresh video content on local profiles.

One filming session. Five placements. Total time: 1 hour.

### Example: Chicago Bakery

A bakery in Chicago's Wicker Park neighborhood was posting to Instagram only. I showed them this workflow. They started filming one 45-second video per week — usually a close-up of them icing a cake with a voiceover explaining the flavor.

Results after 30 days:

- Instagram Reel views: 4,200
- Facebook video views: 1,800
- TikTok views: 900 (they had 47 followers — now 312)
- Email click-through: 38% (up from 19%)
- Google Business Profile views: up 240%

Cake orders from social: 22 in that month, up from 7 the month before. Additional revenue: $1,760.

## Why Most Local Social Media Advice Is Wrong

Most social media advice is written by people who sell social media services. They tell you to post daily, use trending audio, hop on trends, run giveaways, and "build community." That advice works if you're a DTC brand selling $40 candles.

It doesn't work if you're a pet groomer trying to fill a Tuesday slot.

### What Actually Works for Local Businesses

**Answer questions, don't entertain.** Your customer's brain is tired. They're looking for information, not inspiration. A post that says "We have availability this Thursday at 3pm" will outperform a motivational quote 10 times out of 10.

**Be specific about location.** "We're on Main Street, across from the blue awning" works better than any branding. I've had clients add their cross street to their Instagram bio and seen walk-in traffic increase within a week.

**Use the tools your customers already use.** If your clientele is 50+ years old, Facebook ads targeting local interests will outperform Instagram 4:1. If your customer books through Yelp, put your effort into Yelp's advertising platform, not TikTok. I worked with a dentist in Houston who was spending $800/month on Instagram ads. His patients found him through Google Maps and Yelp. We moved the budget there. New patient acquisition cost dropped from $87 to $34.

**Track everything through one system.** I use Square for most clients because it integrates with Mailchimp, Google Business Profile, and Instagram shopping. When a customer books a service through Square, I can see exactly which channel they came from. No guesswork. No "we think social media is working."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I only have 15 minutes a day for social media. Is it even worth it?**

Depends on your definition of "worth it." If you spend 15 minutes a day posting to three platforms without a strategy, you're wasting 15 minutes. If you spend 15 minutes filming one 30-second video and posting it to Instagram and Google Business Profile, and that video books you one appointment, it's worth it. Focus on one platform and one content type. Don't try to be everywhere.

**Q: Should I pay for a content agency or do it myself?**

If you have $2,000+/month and zero interest in touching social media, hire an agency — but vet them on local business results, not portfolio aesthetics. If you're willing to film three 30-second videos a week on your phone and post them yourself, do it yourself. I've seen better results from an iPhone video of a real customer than from a $5,000 professionally produced shoot. The person who owns the business has more credibility on camera than any actor.

**Q: What if I can't make videos? I hate being on camera.**

You don't have to be on camera. Film the product. Film your space. Film someone else's hands doing the work. A coffee shop owner I work with in Portland has never appeared on camera. She films the espresso machine, the pastries, the milk steaming. Voiceover explains the drink. Works fine. If you absolutely cannot do video, use a static image with a clear call to action. It won't perform as well, but it'll perform better than nothing.

**Q: Is it worth running paid ads with only 500 followers?**

Yes. Especially on Facebook and Instagram. The number of followers you have has zero correlation with ad performance. I've launched campaigns for accounts with 87 followers that drove $3,200 in sales. The algorithm doesn't care about your follower count. It cares about your targeting, your creative, and your offer.

**Q: How do I know if social media is actually bringing in customers?**

Set up a system. If you're a restaurant, ask every table "how did you hear about us?" and write it down for two weeks. If you're a service business, use a booking tool like Booksy or Square Appointments that asks "where did you find us?" as part of the booking flow. If you're running ads, use UTM links in every post. Measure for 30 days. If you can't track a single booking back to social, either your tracking is broken or your content isn't working. Figure out which one.

**Q: What should I do about my competitor who's posting 10 times a day?**

Ignore them. I've never seen a business win by out-posting a competitor. I've seen businesses win by having better offers, clearer information, and faster response times. Your competitor can post 10 times a day. If they take three days to respond to a DM and you respond within an hour, you win. If your website clearly lists your prices and hours and theirs is buried in a Linktree, you win. Frequency doesn't beat clarity.
