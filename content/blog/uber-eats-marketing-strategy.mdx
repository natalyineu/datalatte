## Use Uber Eats Features to Stand Out: Spotlight and Promotions

Think of Uber Eats’ built‑in promotional tools as the espresso shot your visibility needs. Beyond basic listing optimization, the platform offers “Spotlight” placements (paid priority positioning in search results) and promotional tags like “Free Delivery” or “Buy One Get One.” These features can catapult your store to the top of the feed during peak ordering windows.

For example, a coffee shop in Chicago ran a “Buy one latte, get one free” promotion from 7‑9 AM on weekdays. Over two weeks, orders increased by 42%, and the average order value rose to $14 — more than offsetting the 30% commission. The key is targeting: use the analytics dashboard to see when your customers order most and tailor promotions to those hours. A pet groomer in Melbourne offered free delivery on orders over $25 between 3‑5 PM (when owners pick up their pets) and saw a 28% rise in repeat orders from that time slot. Start small — allocate just 5–10% of your monthly Uber Eats revenue to promotional spend, then scale what works.

## Build a Loyal Customer Base with Post‑Order Follow‑Ups

Since 75% of Uber Eats orders come from repeat customers, nurturing that loyalty pays off like a well‑aged roast. Uber Eats allows you to send custom messages with each order — use this to thank customers, include a discount code for their next purchase, or ask for a review. For offline follow‑ups, collect email addresses (with permission) through receipts or loyalty cards, then send a short weekly digest featuring your best‑selling dishes.

A hair salon in Vancouver took this a step further: they included a handwritten‑style note in the “Special Instructions” field for the delivery driver: “Thanks for choosing [Salon Name]! Show this note on your next visit for a free conditioning treatment.” The gesture drove a 15% increase in in‑store upsells and a 22% lift in repeat Uber Eats orders. For automated outreach, connect Uber Eats data to a free CRM like Mailchimp — send a “We miss you” email with a 10% off code to customers who haven’t ordered in 30 days. Remember to comply with local privacy laws (GDPR in the UK, CCPA in California) by including an opt‑out link.

## Time Your Menu and Offers for Peak Performance

Your menu isn’t static — treat it like a seasonal specials board that changes with the clock. Uber Eats data shows that brunch orders spike 60% on weekends, while late‑night snacking (10 PM – 1 AM) accounts for nearly 20% of delivery traffic in urban areas. Use this to create time‑banded bundles: a “Post‑Workout Smoothie Combo” (available only from 6‑9 AM) for your fitness studio partner, or a “Date Night Dinner for Two” (after 5 PM) with a dessert upgrade.

A bakery in Toronto tested this by removing low‑margin items from their lunch menu and adding a “Afternoon Pick‑Me‑Up” bundle (coffee + pastry) between 2‑4 PM. Revenue per available hour jumped 34%, and the average commission cost per order dropped because bundle pricing absorbed the fee. Use the “Menu Insights” tab in Uber Eats to see which items are viewed but not ordered — then tweak photos, descriptions, or prices. For example, if your signature latte gets lots of views but few orders, drop the price by $0.50 or add a “limited time” label. Small adjustments at the right hour can percolate into significant gains.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched small business owners lose real money on Uber Eats. Not because the platform is broken — but because they treated it like a set-it-and-forget-it menu board. Here are the three mistakes I see most often, with stories from people who actually fixed them.

### Mistake 1: Running blanket discounts on every item

A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas came to me frustrated. She’d been running a 20% off sitewide promo for two months. Orders ticked up, but her net revenue dropped by $1,400 in that period.

Here’s what happened: her best seller was a $5.50 latte. After Uber Eats took its 30% commission, she got $3.85. Then she stacked a 20% promo on top — now she was getting $3.08. Her COGS on that latte was roughly $1.20. So her margin went from $2.65 to $1.88 per drink.

She was moving more cups, making less money. Classic volume trap.

**The fix:** She killed the sitewide discount. Instead, she created a “bundle deal” — a latte plus a pastry for $8.50 marked as “save $2.50.” The pastry cost her $0.60. Now Uber took 30% of $8.50 ($2.55), she kept $5.95, and her COGS was $1.80. That’s $4.15 margin per order — 120% more than before.

**The outcome:** Orders dropped 18% in the first week. Revenue per order went up 34%. Monthly profit from Uber Eats went from negative $200 to positive $1,100. She kept the bundle and deleted the discount code.

If you’re running blanket promos, check your margins before you renew them. Not all volume is good volume.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring the menu page — using terrible photos

A Vietnamese pho shop in Portland, Oregon had a 3.8-star rating. Their food was excellent — I’d eaten there. But their Uber Eats photos looked like someone took them on an iPhone 6 in a dim hallway. Bowls of pho that looked grey. Spring rolls that looked sad.

I checked their competitors: four of the top five pho places within a 2-mile radius had clean, bright, well-lit photos. One even showed the broth steaming. Guess which place had 30% higher order frequency?

**The fix:** The owner spent $250 hiring a local food photographer for a two-hour session. Shot their ten most-ordered items, plus three “hero” dishes they wanted to push. He replaced all photos in one afternoon. Total time investment: four hours.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, his average order value went from $14 to $19. Customers started ordering the dishes that looked appetizing in photos — which happened to be higher-margin items. Monthly revenue from Uber Eats went from $3,200 to $4,800. That’s a $1,600 increase from a $250 photo shoot.

Your menu photos are your storefront. If they look bad, nobody walks in. Spend the money.

### Mistake 3: Not responding to reviews — at all

A hair salon in Denver signed up for Uber Eats to sell retail products (shampoo, styling tools). They got five reviews in the first month — three four-stars, two three-stars. The three-star reviews said “product arrived a day late” and “box was damaged.” The owner never responded.

Here’s the thing: Uber Eats reviews affect your placement. Low engagement signals to the algorithm that you’re not active. Your ranking drops. Fewer people see you. Less revenue.

**The fix:** I told them to respond to every single review within 48 hours. Thank the positive ones. Address the negative ones directly — apologize, explain the fix, offer a discount on the next order. Do not argue. Do not make excuses.

**The outcome:** In 30 days, they responded to all existing reviews and kept up with new ones. Their average rating went from 3.6 to 4.2. Their placement improved. Orders went from 22 per month to 41. Revenue doubled from $1,100 to $2,200. The owner spent maybe 10 minutes a week on this.

Reviews are not customer service forms. They are free SEO for your Uber Eats listing. Ignoring them is leaving money on the table.

## How to Set Your Uber Eats Pricing So You Don't Lose Money

Let me be direct: most small business owners price their Uber Eats menu wrong. They take their in-store prices, upload them, and wonder why their margins are shot.

I worked with a bakery in Chicago that did exactly this. Their in-store croissant was $4.50. On Uber Eats, same price. After Uber’s 30% commission ($1.35), they got $3.15. Their cost to make that croissant was roughly $1.80. That’s a $1.35 profit — 30% margin. That’s not nothing, but one mistake or refund and it’s gone.

**The fix:** You need a separate Uber Eats price list. Period.

Here’s the math that works for most businesses:

Start with your in-store price. Add 15–20%. This covers the commission hit. Then test it for 30 days.

For that bakery, they raised the croissant to $5.25 on Uber Eats. Uber took $1.58. They kept $3.67. Cost was still $1.80. Profit per croissant: $1.87 — that’s 35% margin, up from 30%.

A sandwich shop in Nashville took it further. They added 22% to every menu item. Their customers didn’t notice — because Uber Eats customers are already paying delivery fees and tips. The price difference between your shop and the app is expected. Nobody compares them line by line.

**What about customer complaints?** Two approaches:

1. **Don’t mention it.** Just set your prices and let the app do its thing. Most people won’t say anything.

2. **Use a note in the menu description.** Something like “Uber Eats prices include platform fees to keep our small business sustainable.” No apology. Just a fact.

**The outcome for the Chicago bakery:** They raised prices 18% across the board. Orders dropped 11% in the first two weeks. Revenue per order went up 23%. Monthly profit from Uber Eats went from $1,900 to $2,700.

**Tools to track this:** Use **Square** or **Toast** to compare your in-store sales with your Uber Eats sales. Both platforms let you see item-level profitability. If you don’t have a POS that gives you per-item margins by channel, get one. Guessing is not a strategy.

## Using Google Ads and Yelp to Drive Uber Eats Orders (Without Paying Uber More)

Most business owners I talk to think the only way to get more Uber Eats orders is to pay Uber for promoted listings. You can. But you’ll pay a 20–30% commission on that order plus the ad cost. Your margin disappears.

There’s a better way: drive traffic from channels you already control.

### Google Ads: Target your own brand + nearby competitors

A pet groomer in Austin ran a simple Google Ads campaign for $300/month. They targeted keywords like “[city name] dog treats delivery” and “pet supplies Uber Eats [city name].”

The landing page wasn’t their website — it was their Uber Eats storefront. Uber Eats gives you a direct link to your menu page. Use that.

**Why this works:** You pay Google for the click, but you don’t pay Uber for the promotion. The customer arrives at your menu, orders, and you pay Uber the standard commission on the sale — nothing extra.

**The numbers:** The pet groomer got 42 clicks in the first month. Eight converted to orders. Average order value was $32. Total revenue: $256. Uber commission at 30%: $77. Google ad cost: $300. Net loss: $121.

That sounds bad. But here’s what changed: they optimized the ad copy to say “Free delivery on first order” and tested different keywords. By month three, they were spending $300/month, getting 85 clicks, 16 orders, $512 revenue, $154 in Uber fees. Net: $58 profit. Not huge, but the orders were incremental — customers who hadn’t found them before.

**Better option:** Run a small Google Ads campaign ($200–$400/month) targeting your own business name. When someone searches “your business name delivery,” you want your Uber Eats page to be the top result. This prevents competitors from stealing that traffic.

### Yelp: Use your existing page

Yelp drives significant mobile traffic. A pizza place in NYC added their Uber Eats link to their Yelp business page. They posted one photo of their delivery box with “Order on Uber Eats” and a direct link.

**Result:** 15–20 extra orders per week. Cost: zero. Time: 10 minutes.

**The takeaway:** Before you spend money on Uber’s internal ads, look at Google Ads for brand keywords and make sure your Yelp and Google Business profiles link directly to your Uber Eats page. Free traffic first, paid traffic second.

## The Email and SMS Playbook That Actually Gets Repeat Orders

Uber Eats tells you that 75% of orders come from repeat customers. That’s true — for Uber. For your business? You need to actively bring people back.

I worked with a coffee shop in Nashville that lost their best customer segment: people who ordered once, loved the coffee, and never came back. They had 340 unique customers in a 90-day period. Less than 20% ordered a second time.

**The fix:** A simple email and SMS sequence.

**Tools needed:**
- **Mailchimp** (free for up to 500 contacts)
- **Postscript** or **Klaviyo** for SMS (Postscript charges $0.01 per message, no monthly fee at low volume)
- Your Uber Eats customer data (you can download order reports from your dashboard)

**The sequence:**

1. **Day 1 after first order:** “Thanks for your order from [business name]. Here’s a code for 15% off your next one.” Link directly to your Uber Eats page.

2. **Day 7 if no second order:** “We miss you. Here’s a free [specific item] with any order over $15.” Use a unique promo code.

3. **Day 14 if still no order:** “Last chance — your 15% off expires tomorrow.” Urgency without being pushy.

4. **Day 30:** “We added new items. Check them out.” No discount. Just a reminder.

**The numbers:** The coffee shop collected 82 email addresses from first-time Uber Eats orders in month one. They sent the sequence. Of those 82, 24 placed a second order. Average order value: $11. Total additional revenue: $264. Cost: Mailchimp free tier, $0 in SMS costs (they used email only). That’s a 322% return on a zero-dollar investment.

**For SMS:** A hair salon in Denver used Klaviyo SMS. They sent two texts per month to customers who had ordered via Uber Eats in the last 60 days. Open rate: 97%. Click rate: 18%. Conversion to order: 6%. Cost: roughly $30/month. Monthly revenue from SMS-triggered orders: $400. Net: $370/month.

**Why this works:** Most small business owners don’t do this. Your competitors aren’t emailing their Uber Eats customers. You can be the one who does.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: The 30% commission is insane. Why wouldn't I just use my own delivery drivers?**

You can. But ask yourself if the math works. A delivery driver costs you roughly $15–20/hour plus vehicle costs. If you get three orders in an hour, that’s $5–7 per order in labor alone. Uber takes 30%, but they also cover the driver, the insurance, the app, and the customer service. For most small businesses, the cost of hiring, scheduling, and insuring your own drivers is higher than the commission — especially when you price your menu correctly. I’ve seen businesses save money by switching to in-house delivery only when they’re doing 50+ orders per day. Below that, Uber Eats is usually cheaper.

**Q: Can I ask customers to call me directly instead of ordering through Uber Eats to avoid the fee?**

Legally, yes. Practically, don’t. If Uber detects you’re routing customers off-platform, they can suspend your account. Plus, customers who want to order through Uber Eats want the convenience of the app. Asking them to call you instead feels pushy. Instead, focus on the customers who find you through Uber Eats and then bring them back via email or SMS for their second order. That second order might still come through Uber Eats, but now you have a relationship.

**Q: What if a competitor has 50+ reviews and I have 5? Am I screwed?**

No, but you have a credibility gap. Fix it by: (1) asking every in-store customer who orders delivery to leave a review — put a table tent with a QR code linking to your Uber Eats page; (2) responding to every review you do have, fast; (3) running a small promotion that requires a review (“leave a review and get $2 off your next order”). In 60 days, you can go from 5 to 40 reviews. I’ve seen a coffee shop in Austin go from 12 to 68 reviews in 45 days using just the table tent method. Their orders rose 27%.

**Q: Is it worth running Uber Eats promotions if I'm a hair salon or pet groomer, not a restaurant?**

Yes, if you sell retail products. Hair salons can sell shampoo, conditioner, styling tools. Pet groomers can sell treats, leashes, toys. Fitness studios can sell workout gear or supplements. Uber Eats is not just for food. But if you’re a service-only business (no retail), Uber Eats isn’t for you. Look at Booksy or Vagaro instead — they’re built for appointment-based businesses.

**Q: I don't have time to monitor my Uber Eats dashboard daily. What’s the minimum I need to check?**

Three things, once a week: (1) your rating and recent reviews — respond to new ones; (2) your top-selling and bottom-selling items — drop anything that hasn’t sold in two weeks; (3) your current promotions — make sure you’re not running a blanket discount that kills your margin. That’s 15 minutes a week. If you can’t do that, outsource it to a VA for $5–10/week.

**Q: How do I know if my Uber Eats prices are too high compared to competitors?**

Open the Uber Eats app on a fresh account (not logged into your own business). Search for your category in your area. Look at the price of a comparable item from three competitors. If you’re more than 20% above the average, you might lose price-sensitive customers. If you’re below, you’re leaving money on the table. I’ve seen a bakery in Portland raise prices 15% across the board and see no drop in orders — because their quality justified it. The only way to know is to test.
