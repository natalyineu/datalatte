### Setting Up Your Google Ads Campaign

Before you can start running Google Ads, you need to set up your campaign. This involves creating a budget, choosing your target audience, and selecting the types of ads you want to run. Here are some tips to get you started:

* Start with a small budget and gradually increase it as you see results
* Use the Google Ads platform to create targeted ads that reach your ideal customer
* Use Google's automated bidding strategies to optimize your ad spend for maximum ROI
* Use negative keywords to avoid showing your ads to irrelevant searches

### Choosing the Right Ad Format

When it comes to Google Ads, there are several ad formats to choose from. The most common formats include:

* Text ads: These are the standard Google Ads that appear at the top and bottom of search engine results pages (SERPs)
* Image ads: These ads use images to grab the attention of your target audience
* Video ads: These ads use video to tell a story and engage your target audience

<Callout type="tip">Use high-quality images and videos to make your ads stand out.</Callout>

### Creating Effective Ad Copy

Your ad copy is what sets you apart from your competitors. It's what convinces potential clients to click on your ad and visit your website. Here are some tips for creating effective ad copy:

* Use compelling headlines that grab the attention of your target audience
* Use clear and concise language that communicates the benefits of your spa services
* Use social proof such as customer testimonials and reviews to build credibility
* Use a clear call-to-action (CTA) that tells potential clients what action to take next

### Measuring Your Results

Measuring your Google Ads results is crucial to understanding the effectiveness of your campaign. Here are some key metrics to track:

* Click-through rate (CTR): This measures the number of clicks your ads receive compared to the number of impressions
* Conversion rate: This measures the number of conversions (such as bookings or sales) compared to the number of clicks
* Return on investment (ROI): This measures the revenue generated by your campaign compared to the cost of running it

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Bidding on generic terms like "massage" or "spa day"

I saw this happen at a spa in Nashville. They were spending about $1,200 a month on Google Ads, running one campaign with broad match keywords like "massage," "spa treatments," and "facial." On paper, they were getting impressions. In reality, their cost-per-click was absurdly high because Google was showing their ads to:

- People looking for massage therapy clinics (medical, not spa)
- Students researching career paths
- People searching for "massage chairs"
- That guy in Ohio who was never going to drive to Nashville for a hot stone massage

The owner told me she was getting "leads" — people filling out her contact form. But when I looked at the data, 80% of those leads were asking about prices or directions. They weren't actually booking. She was paying $45 per click to collect contact info from people who had no intention of spending $150 on a treatment.

**The fix:** Build a negative keyword list before you launch a single campaign. For a spa, that list should include:

- "massage envy" (competitor)
- "massage therapy" (medical)
- "massage near me" (too broad unless you're in a tiny market)
- "spa" + "gift certificate" (these people want to buy a card, not book)
- "hotel spa" (usually looking for the hotel's own spa, not yours)
- "free" (you know who I mean)

Also switch from broad match to phrase match. Bid on specific services you offer — "Swedish massage Nashville," "detox facial Nashville," "hot stone massage Nashville." These terms convert at 3-4x the rate of generic "massage" searches.

**The outcome:** This Nashville spa dropped their monthly spend from $1,200 to $900, added 47 negative keywords, and switched to phrase match. Their cost-per-booking went from $85 to $32. Bookings from ads increased 60% in six weeks.

### Mistake 2: Not using location targeting correctly

A spa in Austin ran their Google Ads targeting the entire Austin metro area. Sounds reasonable, right? Here's the problem: Austin is a 300-square-mile city. Someone searching "deep tissue massage Austin" from Cedar Park (25 minutes away) is less likely to drive across town than someone searching from the same zip code as the spa.

The spa's cost-per-click was reasonable — about $4.50 — but their conversion rate was 0.8%. They were getting clicks, then crickets. I asked the owner if she'd looked at where those clicks came from. She hadn't.

**The fix:** Set a radius of 5-10 miles around your spa location. If you're in a dense urban area like downtown Austin, go with 3-5 miles. Use "Target by location" in Google Ads and exclude zip codes that are clearly outside your realistic service area.

Here's the counterintuitive part: Your costs might go up slightly because you're competing for a smaller pool of searchers. But your conversion rate will increase dramatically. A 10-mile radius with a $5 CPC that converts at 5% is better than a 30-mile radius with a $4 CPC that converts at 0.8%. Do the math: $100 gets you 20 clicks in the broad campaign, zero bookings at 0.8%. Same $100 gets you 20 clicks in the tight campaign, one booking at 5%.

**The outcome:** This Austin spa set a 5-mile radius and excluded 12 zip codes on the periphery. CPC went up to $5.50, but conversion rate jumped to 4.2%. Cost-per-booking dropped from $562 to $131. She added 28 new clients in the first month.

### Mistake 3: Sending ad traffic to a generic homepage

A day spa in Portland had a nice website. Good photos, calming design, clear menu of services. But they sent all their Google Ads traffic to the homepage. Here's what happened when someone clicked an ad for "hot stone massage Portland":

They landed on the homepage, which shows 12 different services with no clear hierarchy. Maybe they scroll. Maybe they click "Services." Maybe they get distracted by a blog post about essential oils. Maybe they leave.

The conversion path for a high-intent search like "hot stone massage Portland" should be: click ad → land on specific service page → click "Book Now" button → pick time → done. That should take 45 seconds max.

This spa's conversion rate from Google Ads was 1.1%. They blamed the ads. I blamed the landing page.

**The fix:** Create a dedicated landing page for each ad group or service type. You don't need a fancy website redesign. Use a simple one-page layout:

- Headline matching the ad (e.g., "Hot Stone Massage — $129")
- 2-3 bullet points about what the treatment includes
- A prominent "Book Now" button
- A photo of the treatment room
- Your phone number

That's it. No testimonials. No blog links. No navigation menu to distract them. Just a straight shot from "I want this" to "when can I come in?"

**The outcome:** I built three landing pages for this Portland spa — one for massage services, one for facials, one for body treatments. Their conversion rate went from 1.1% to 4.8%. Same ad spend ($800/month). Bookings from ads doubled in two weeks.

### Mistake 4: Running ads 24/7 without scheduling

A med spa in Denver ran their Google Ads around the clock. They were open Monday through Friday, 9 AM to 7 PM, and Saturday 9 AM to 4 PM. But their ads were running at 2 AM on a Tuesday. And 11 PM on a Sunday. And showing up for people who were clearly searching from a bar at 1 AM on a Saturday.

The owner told me, "I want to catch people whenever they're thinking about booking." Fair instinct. But here's the problem: Someone searching for "Botox Denver" at 3 AM is either drunk, bored, or procrastinating. They're not booking a treatment that costs $400 at 3 AM. They're clicking your ad, maybe filling out a form, and then ignoring your follow-up email.

**The fix:** Use ad scheduling in Google Ads. Set your ads to run only during your business hours — maybe add an hour on either side for morning browsers and evening planners. If you're closed on Sundays, don't run ads on Sundays.

The exception: If you offer online booking and can process payments after hours, you can extend your schedule. But for most spas, appointments are made during business hours. Don't pay for clicks at 2 AM.

**The outcome:** This Denver med spa trimmed their ad schedule from 168 hours per week (24/7) to 54 hours per week (business hours). Their ad spend dropped from $1,600 to $1,050 per month. Their conversion rate actually *increased* because they weren't wasting clicks on low-intent off-hours searchers. Their cost-per-booking dropped from $110 to $68.

## Ad Scheduling: When Your Ads Should (and Shouldn't) Appear

Let's get specific about timing. I mentioned ad scheduling in the mistakes section, but this deserves its own treatment because it's one of the easiest ways to stop wasting money.

I worked with a spa in Chicago that was getting most of its bookings from ads between 10 AM and 2 PM on weekdays. That tracked: people at work, feeling stressed, daydreaming about a massage. They'd search, click, book for the weekend.

The owner was also getting decent traffic on Saturday mornings — people planning their Sunday self-care.

But Sundays were dead. And overnight clicks were essentially people looking up phone numbers for spas they'd visit "someday."

Here's how to find your own best hours:

Go to Google Ads → Campaigns → Audience → Days and Hours. Look at your conversion data by day and time. You're looking for patterns. Most service businesses — spas, salons, dental clinics — see peak booking activity between 10 AM and 3 PM on weekdays and Saturday mornings.

Set your ad schedule to:

- Monday-Friday: 8 AM to 7 PM
- Saturday: 9 AM to 3 PM
- Sunday: Off (or 10 AM to 2 PM if you get bookings)

You can also use bid adjustments to increase your bids during peak booking hours (say, 20% higher between 11 AM and 1 PM) and decrease them during slower hours (10% lower between 3 PM and 5 PM). This lets you stay competitive when it matters and save money when it doesn't.

A spa in Denver did this and their cost-per-booking dropped from $78 to $52 within a month. They spent more per click during peak hours (because they bid higher), but they got more than enough extra bookings to make up for it.

## Structuring Your Campaigns: How to Stop Throwing Money at the Wrong Keywords

Most spa owners I talk to set up one campaign, one ad group, and a list of 50 random keywords. That's like walking into a grocery store without a shopping list. You're going to spend money on things you don't need and miss the things you do.

Here's the structure that works for spas:

**Campaign 1: Massage Services**
- Ad group 1: Swedish massage
- Ad group 2: Deep tissue massage
- Ad group 3: Hot stone massage
- Ad group 4: Couple's massage

**Campaign 2: Facial Services**
- Ad group 1: Anti-aging facial
- Ad group 2: Acne facial
- Ad group 3: Hydrating facial
- Ad group 4: Chemical peel

**Campaign 3: Body Treatments**
- Ad group 1: Body wrap
- Ad group 2: Scrubs
- Ad group 3: Hydrotherapy

**Campaign 4: Gift Cards**
- Ad group 1: Spa gift card
- Ad group 2: Massage gift certificate

Each ad group gets its own ad copy and landing page. Why? Because "Swedish massage" and "deep tissue massage" attract different people with different search intent. Someone searching for Swedish massage wants relaxation. Someone searching for deep tissue wants pain relief. Your ad for "relaxing Swedish massage in Portland" won't resonate with someone looking for "deep tissue massage for lower back pain."

I set up this structure for a spa in Austin that was running one campaign with 15 keywords. They were spending $900/month and getting 12 bookings. After restructuring into four campaigns with 8 ad groups, they were spending the same $900/month and getting 34 bookings. Same budget. Smarter organization.

Why does this work? Google's Quality Score improves when your ad copy, keywords, and landing page are tightly aligned. Higher Quality Score = lower cost-per-click. One campaign with generic keywords gets a Quality Score of 4 or 5. A well-structured campaign with specific keywords gets a Quality Score of 7, 8, or even 9. That can cut your cost-per-click in half.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm in a small town with one or two other spas. Do I really need Google Ads?**

Maybe not. If you're the only spa within 20 miles, you might get enough traffic from a Google Business Profile, word of mouth, and local SEO. But test it small. Start with one campaign, a $300 monthly budget, and run it for 30 days. If you get 2-3 bookings from it, that's a $100 cost-per-booking. If your average treatment is $120, you're breaking even or close. Scale from there.

**Q: What's a realistic monthly budget for a spa just starting with Google Ads?**

$500 to $1,000 per month, depending on your city. In a mid-sized market like Portland or Austin, $500 can get you 10-15 clicks per day. In NYC or LA, expect to pay $5-8 per click, so $500 gets you 7-10 clicks per day. A good conversion rate for a well-optimized campaign is 3-5%, so figure 1 booking per 20-30 clicks. That's roughly 5-8 bookings per month from $500. That works if your average ticket is $100+.

**Q: How do I track whether a booking actually came from Google Ads?**

Set up conversion tracking in Google Ads. You need to install a small snippet of code on your "Thank You" or booking confirmation page. When someone completes a booking and hits that page, Google records it as a conversion. Without this, you're flying blind. If you use Booksy, Vagaro, or Square Appointments, they have built-in Google Ads integrations for tracking bookings.

**Q: I see "impressions" and "clicks" in my account. What matters most?**

Bookings. That's it. Ignore everything else. Impressions are vanity. Clicks are a means to an end. If you're getting clicks but no bookings, your ad or landing page is wrong. If you're getting impressions but no clicks, your ad copy or targeting is wrong. The only number that pays your bills is cost-per-booking. Don't let anyone sell you on "brand awareness" for a spa. You need people in the treatment room.

**Q: What's better — Google Ads or Facebook/Instagram Ads for a spa?**

For immediate bookings with clear intent, Google Ads wins. Someone searching "facial Austin" has already decided they want a facial. They just need to pick a place. Instagram is better for building awareness and attracting people who haven't decided yet — think photo-heavy posts of your treatment rooms, before/after shots, and seasonal offers. Ideally, run both. Start with Google Ads for direct response, then layer on Instagram for brand building. Most of my clients see a 3:1 return on Google Ads spend and a 2:1 return on Instagram.

**Q: Do I need a landing page, or can I just link to my website?**

You *can* link to your website. But a dedicated landing page converts 2-3x better than a homepage or services page. Use a simple tool like Carrd, Leadpages, or Instapage to create a one-page booking funnel. No navigation. No distractions. Just the offer, a photo, and a booking button.
