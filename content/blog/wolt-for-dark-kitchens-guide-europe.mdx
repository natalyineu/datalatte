## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating Your Wolt Menu Like a Diner Menu

I watched a BBQ spot in Austin, Texas burn through $1,200 in three weeks because the owner thought he could just throw his full restaurant menu onto Wolt and call it a day. Brisket platters. Loaded nachos. Fried pickles. Three kinds of mac and cheese. Thirty-two items.

Here’s what actually happened: his average prep time hit twenty-three minutes. Wolt’s algorithm flagged him as “slow.” His placement dropped to page three. Customers who did order received soggy fries and cold brisket. His rating went from 4.7 to 3.9 in two weeks. He lost $1,200 on ads for orders that either got refunded or generated one-star reviews.

**The fix:** I told him to cut the menu to eight items. Things that travel well. Things that could sit in a bag for ten minutes and still taste good. Pulled pork sandwiches. Collard greens. Cornbread. Banana pudding. He dropped the fried stuff and anything that required assembly.

**The outcome:** Prep time dropped to eleven minutes. Wolt promoted him to “quick” status. His rating climbed back to 4.6 over thirty days. Revenue hit $3,800 the following month on $400 in ads. He’s still running that shortened menu today. He told me he wished someone had hit him with a frying pan about this sooner.

If you’re selling on Wolt, your menu is not a love letter to your cooking. It’s a logistics document. If it takes longer to cook than it does for the customer to scroll past you, you lose.

### Mistake #2: Running Wolt Ads Without Fixing Your Photos First

A coffee shop in Nashville decided they wanted to break into the dinner delivery game. They had a solid breakfast business but wanted evening revenue. They put $500 into Wolt’s promoted listings. The problem? Their photos looked like someone took them with a flip phone in a parking lot at dusk. A grainy blur of a burger. A sandwich that could have been a shoe. A salad that looked sad.

They spent $500. They got four orders. Two of those orders were from friends. The owner called me frustrated, saying Wolt ads don’t work.

I asked her to pull up her competitors’ listings. Every single one had bright, well-lit photos. Half had lifestyle shots of people holding the food. The difference wasn’t the ad spend. It was the visual trust.

**The fix:** I had her hire a local food photographer for $350. One hour. Twenty photos. White background, natural light, no weird filters. She swapped out all her images on Wolt and Google Business Profile. Then she redid the $500 ad spend.

**The outcome:** Orders jumped to thirty-seven in the next two weeks. Average order value was $18. She made $666 in gross revenue from that $500. Not a home run, but she got her photos paid for and had a baseline to scale.

The uncomfortable truth is that Wolt customers decide whether to click your listing in about half a second. If your photo looks like it was shot in 2003, you are invisible. Fix your photos before you fix your budget.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Your Delivery Radius

A bakery in Chicago kept running out of dough by 2 p.m. and then getting spammed with Wolt orders they couldn’t fulfill. They had to cancel orders. Their rating tanked. Wolt penalized them with a lower placement.

The owner’s reasoning: “I want to reach as many people as possible.” So they set their delivery radius to cover the entire North Side. That’s a lot of Chicago. Problem is, their kitchen capacity was about eighty orders a day. They’d hit that by 1:30 p.m. and spend the rest of the afternoon canceling orders from people in Lincoln Park who wanted a 3 p.m. scone.

**The fix:** I told them to shrink the radius to a two‑mile zone around the kitchen. Cut the delivery area in half. That meant fewer potential customers, sure. But it also meant deliverable orders only. They adjusted their Wolt settings to cap daily orders at seventy-five.

**The outcome:** Cancellations dropped to zero. Their rating went from 3.8 to 4.9 in six weeks. Wolt started showing them as “top rated.” Orders actually increased because the algorithm favored them. Their weekly revenue stabilized around $4,200 instead of spiking and crashing. They weren’t leaving money on the table. They were finally picking it up.

If you’re a small kitchen, you don’t need the whole city. You need the people who can actually get your food while it’s hot.

### Mistake #4: Pricing Your Menu for a Fictional Reality

A sandwich shop in Portland decided their Wolt prices should match their in‑store prices. That sounds fair, right? It’s not. Because Wolt takes a 25–30% commission. So for every $12 sandwich they sold, they kept about $8.40. Their food cost was $4.50 per sandwich. After labor, packaging, and the Wolt fee, they were making less than $2 per order.

They ran $700 in Wolt ads in January. They made $2,100 in gross revenue. They lost money on every single order.

**The fix:** I had them raise Wolt menu prices by 20% across the board. Yes, customers pay more on the app. No, they don’t care. They’re ordering from their couch while watching Netflix. The $12 Reuben became $14.50. The $6 chips and dip became $7.50.

**The outcome:** Their margin per order went from $1.80 to $4.20. They turned their $700 ad spend into $3,100 in revenue. Net profit? $620. Not a fortune, but it beat losing money. They also stopped feeling like Wolt was a charity.

The rule: your Wolt prices need to cover Wolt’s cut plus an extra margin for the headache of delivery logistics. If you’re not charging more on the platform, you’re subsidizing your customers’ convenience out of your own pocket.

## How to Use Google Ads to Drive Delivery Orders (Without Burning Cash)

Most small business owners hear “Google Ads” and picture an endless pit of wasted money. I get it. I’ve seen $2,000 budgets disappear in three days with nothing to show for it. But if you’re running a dark kitchen on Wolt, Google Ads can actually work if you target the right search intent.

Here’s what I mean: people don’t search “food delivery near me” when they’re hungry. They search “best burger in Austin” or “Nashville BBQ delivery” or “Chicago deep dish pizza delivery Wolt.” They have a craving, not a category.

**Set up a local campaign targeting your exact neighborhood.** If your kitchen is in Denver’s RiNo district, bid on keywords like “RiNo dinner delivery,” “Denver craft burger delivery,” and “Wolt Denver RiNo.” Keep the radius tight — one to two miles. Your daily budget should be $15–$25, not $100.

**Use call‑only ads if you’re taking phone orders.** Some customers still want to order direct rather than through a third‑party app. Especially for larger orders. I had a BBQ spot in Austin run a $500/month Google Ads campaign targeting “brisket delivery Austin.” Direct phone orders added $2,100 in monthly revenue that went straight to their bottom line. No Wolt commission.

**Track everything with a simple spreadsheet.** Not a $200/month dashboard. A spreadsheet. Write down what you spent, what you got, and what the average order value was. If a keyword costs you $3 per click and generates a $20 order, that’s a 6.5x return before food cost. That’s good. If it costs $8 per click and generates a $15 order, kill it.

The biggest mistake I see is people setting up Google Ads with broad keywords like “food delivery” and hoping for the best. Don’t. You’re not Uber Eats. You’re one kitchen. Act like it.

## How to Use Yelp and Booksy to Build Pre‑Order Volume

Your Wolt listing is for hungry people who want food *right now*. But what about tomorrow? Or next week? That’s where pre‑ordering comes in.

**Yelp has a “Request a Quote” feature that nobody uses for food.** That’s your opportunity. If you’re a dark kitchen that does catering or large‑format meals, set up a Yelp Business page with clear photos of your packaging and a “Pre‑order for pickup” call to action. I’ve seen a pet grooming salon in Portland use Yelp to book appointments, and the same logic applies to food. If someone can book a groomer through Yelp, they can pre‑order a birthday platter.

**Booksy is technically for salons and barbershops, but it works for any scheduled service.** A dark kitchen in Nashville started using Booksy to take pre‑orders for weekend meal prep. Customers booked a 15‑minute pickup slot two days in advance. The kitchen knew exactly how much to cook. Waste dropped 40%. Average order value from pre‑bookings was $32 compared to $14 for Wolt impulse orders.

The strategy is simple: use Wolt for immediate hunger, use Yelp and Booksy for planned eating. Most small business owners only think in the moment. If you can capture both, you’re ahead of the other 40 kitchens in your zip code.

## Pricing Strategy: The Math That Actually Matters

I’m going to make this really simple. If you’re a small business owner running a dark kitchen, you need three numbers. Not six. Not a spreadsheet with seventeen tabs.

**Number one: your break‑even cost per order.** This is your food cost plus packaging plus labor plus the platform commission. If it costs you $11 to make and deliver a sandwich that you sell for $15, your break‑even is $11 and your margin is $4. That’s fine as long as you’re consistent.

**Number two: your minimum profitable ad spend per order.** If you spend $5 on ads to get one $15 order, and your margin is $4, you lost $1. That’s not fine. Your ad spend per order should never exceed 30% of your margin. If your margin is $4, spend no more than $1.20 to acquire that customer. Anything more and you’re subsidizing Wolt with your own cash.

**Number three: your repeat purchase rate.** If customers only order once, you’re playing a volume game that requires constant ad spend. If 30% of customers order again within 30 days, your acquisition cost drops dramatically. I worked with a kitchen in Denver that tracked this using a simple Mailchimp list. They sent a “we miss you” email with a $3 discount code to anyone who hadn’t ordered in 14 days. Their repeat rate went from 12% to 29%. They cut their ad spend by 40% because existing customers were coming back without paid ads.

If you don’t know these three numbers, stop running ads. Figure out the math first. Then turn on the spending.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Will Wolt let me run my own ads if I’m already paying their commission?**
Yes, and you should. Wolt’s promoted listings work inside their app, but they’re not the only game in town. You can run Google Ads, Facebook Ads, and even put up flyers in your delivery area. Wolt doesn’t own your customer. They own their app. Your customer is the person who wants your food. Find them however you can.

**Q: Can I run two different virtual brands out of the same kitchen?**
Yes, and plenty of kitchens do this. I know a kitchen in Chicago that runs a burger brand and a salad brand from the same space. Different names, different photos, different menus. They share the same fryer and the same lettuce. Wolt doesn’t care as long as each listing has its own identity. Just make sure your prep workflow can handle the volume without slowing down.

**Q: What do I do if I get a bad review on Wolt?**
Reply to it publicly. Apologize quickly, offer a refund or a replacement, and don’t argue. I’ve seen owners argue with customers in the comments and it never ends well. If the review is unfair, Wolt has a dispute process. Use it. But most bad reviews come from cold food or late delivery. Fix your packaging and your prep time. That solves 80% of the problems.

**Q: Should I be on Wolt, Uber Eats, and DoorDash at the same time?**
Not if you’re small. Pick one platform per meal period. If your breakfast crowd is on Wolt, be on Wolt. If your dinner crowd is on DoorDash, be on DoorDash. Trying to manage three tablets while cooking is a nightmare and leads to mistakes. You’ll cancel orders, you’ll get bad ratings, and you’ll hate your life. Start with one. Own it. Add a second only when your first is profitable and you have the staff.

**Q: How much should I budget for Wolt ads as a new kitchen?**
Start at $300 a month and run it for 30 days. Track everything. If you get $800 in revenue from that $300, good. If you get $200, bad. Don’t throw more money at a broken system. Fix your photos, fix your menu, fix your prep time, then increase the budget. I’ve seen kitchens double their revenue by fixing their basics before spending a dime on ads.

**Q: Do I need a website if I’m only on Wolt?**
No, but it helps. A simple one‑page site with your menu, your hours, and a direct ordering link (even if it redirects to Wolt) makes you look legitimate. People will Google your brand before they order. If they land on nothing, they go to the next option. Squarespace or Carrd. $20 a month. Do it.
