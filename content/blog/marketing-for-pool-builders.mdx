## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Building a Website That Looks Pretty but Never Gets Found

I worked with a pool builder in Austin, Texas last year. Let’s call him Mike. He had a gorgeous website — drone shots of infinity pools, a custom video walkthrough, the whole thing. He’d spent $8,000 on it. Problem: no one saw it. Mike had never claimed his Google Business Profile. When I searched “pool builder Austin,” his site appeared on page three. Page three is where websites go to die.

We claimed and verified his Google Business Profile in one afternoon. Added photos of completed projects, wrote a clear description with keywords like “custom pool installation Austin,” and asked his last ten happy customers to leave reviews. Within 90 days, his profile appeared in the top three local map results. His website traffic from local searches jumped 340%. More importantly, he booked three new pools worth $190,000 total — all directly from people who found him on Google Maps.

**The fix:** Claim your Google Business Profile. Don’t just fill out the basic fields. Add 50+ photos. Respond to every review. Use the posts feature to showcase recent projects. If you’ve ignored this step, you’re leaving money on the table.

### Mistake #2: Running Google Ads on Autopilot with No Negative Keywords

A pool builder in Nashville came to me frustrated. He’d spent $4,200 on Google Ads over three months and gotten exactly one lead — a guy who wanted a quote for cleaning his existing pool. The builder doesn’t do pool cleaning. That call cost him $4,200.

I looked at his campaign. He was bidding on broad match “pool builder” and “pool installation” without any negative keywords. So his ads were showing to people searching for pool cleaning, pool repair, pool covers, even “inflatable pool.” He was paying for clicks from people who would never hire him.

We rebuilt the campaign: exact match keywords for “pool builder Nashville,” “custom pool installation Nashville,” plus phrase match for “in-ground pool contractor.” Added negative keywords like “above ground,” “cleaning,” “repair,” “dollar,” “used.” Set up location targeting within a 25-mile radius. Weekly budget: $1,500, with ad scheduling to run during business hours only (no sense paying for midnight clicks).

After 60 days, his cost per lead dropped from $1,400 to $88. He booked five consultations, signed three contracts worth a combined $120,000. The fix didn’t take more time — it took *smarter* setup.

**The fix:** Use exact match keywords. Build a negative keyword list before you launch. Never run on broad match unless you have a very large budget and know exactly what you’re doing. Review search terms weekly in the first month.

### Mistake #3: Treating Every Lead the Same (and Following Up Too Late)

A pool builder in Portland, Oregon told me he was getting 20-30 inquiries a month from his website and Google Ads. He was converting maybe one or two. The rest just vanished. I asked him how fast he responded. “Usually within a day or two,” he said. He also admitted he didn’t have a system — he’d reply to emails manually, some calls went to voicemail, and he never texted.

I’ve seen this pattern at three different clients. Homeowners shopping for a pool are often talking to multiple builders. They want a response immediately. If you take 24 hours, you’ve lost.

We implemented a simple follow-up sequence using Mailchimp’s automation and a text service (Twilio). When someone submitted a form, they got an automated text within 60 seconds: “Thanks for your interest! We’ll call you within 15 minutes.” Then a team member called within that window. For after-hours inquiries, the text acknowledged the time and promised a call first thing in the morning.

Within 90 days, his lead response time went from 24 hours to 12 minutes. Conversion rate doubled from 8% to 16%. That added roughly $90,000 in annual revenue from the same number of leads. The tool cost him about $50/month.

**The fix:** Automate your lead response. Use a text message within 60 seconds. Call within 15 minutes. If you can’t do that, hire a virtual receptionist or use a service like Smith.ai. Speed kills — in a good way.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring the Power of Reviews (or, Worse, Buying Fake Ones)

A pool builder in Denver had a Yelp page with three reviews, all over a year old. One was a one-star complaint about a cracked tile. He never responded. When I searched “pool builder Denver,” his Yelp rating was 3.2 stars. He thought it wasn’t a big deal because he got enough word-of-mouth referrals. But word-of-mouth doesn’t show up in the local pack. Google uses reviews as a ranking signal.

We put together a simple system: after each completed project, we sent the homeowner a follow-up email with a direct link to leave a Google review. We also asked for permission to take photos for a future case study — that gave us content and another touchpoint. For the negative review, we wrote a genuine response: acknowledged the issue, explained the resolution (which had already happened), and invited the customer to update their review.

Six months later, he had 38 Google reviews, average 4.8 stars, and Yelp had climbed to 4.5. His inbound lead count from search and maps went from 5 per month to over 20. He told me his annual revenue from online leads alone jumped by $280,000. The only cost was a few hours of staff time setting up the review request email.

**The fix:** Ask every customer for a review at the right moment (right after the pool is finished and cleaned). Respond professionally to negative reviews — you can win back trust. Never, ever buy fake reviews. Google will penalize you, and homeowners who spot fake reviews will walk away.

## Google Ads for Pool Builders: How to Stop Wasting Money and Start Booking Projects

I’ve managed Google Ads for small businesses in 15 cities across the US. The pool builders who succeed on Google Ads don’t wing it. They follow a structure that most guides skip. Here’s what I’ve seen work, and what doesn’t.

### The Budget Trap

Most pool builders I talk to start with “I want to spend $500/month” or “I’ll try it for a month.” That’s not a strategy — it’s a donation to Google. A pool installation is a high-ticket sale, typically $40,000 to $70,000. If you’re spending $500/month, you’ll get maybe 10-15 clicks in a competitive city like Phoenix or Dallas. One click might cost $25-$40 for a high-intent keyword like “custom pool builder Phoenix.” You need at least 100 clicks a month to start seeing patterns. That means a budget of $2,500-$4,000 minimum in most metro areas.

**Real example:** A pool builder in Scottsdale, Arizona started with a $3,000 monthly budget on Google Ads. He used exact match keywords like “pool builder Scottsdale,” “saltwater pool installation Scottsdale,” and added negative keywords for “above ground pools” and “pool cleaning.” His cost per click was $32, his click-through rate was 6.2%. After two months, he had 240 clicks, 18 form fills, and 6 booked consultations. He signed 3 contracts worth $210,000 total. His ad spend was $9,000. That’s a 23x return in two months.

### Landing Page Matters More Than the Ad

Too many pool builders send Google Ads traffic to their home page. Bad idea. The home page is a brochure. The landing page should be a focused sales tool: one headline that matches the ad, a clear offer (“Get a free consultation and quote”), photos of recent projects, and a simple form. I helped a pool builder in Orlando, Florida change his landing page from a generic contact page to a dedicated page with a video walkthrough of a similar backyard transformation. His conversion rate went from 3% to 12%. That one change added about $60,000 in annual revenue from the same ad spend.

### Track Everything in Google Ads (Most People Don't)

Set up conversion tracking for phone calls and form submissions. Without it, you’re flying blind. I worked with a builder in Atlanta who thought his ads were failing because he got zero phone calls. Turns out, his call tracking was broken — calls were being routed to his personal cell phone but not logged. After fixing the tracking, he discovered he was getting 8-10 calls per week from his ads. He just didn’t know it. Fix your tracking before you spend another dollar.

### The Takeaway

Google Ads works for pool builders if you treat it like a precision tool, not a garden hose. Use exact match, build a dedicated landing page, track everything, and spend enough to gather real data. If you’re spending less than $2,000/month in a competitive market, consider starting with local SEO first — it’s free traffic, just slower.

## Why Your Pool Company Needs a Referral Program (And How to Build One That Actually Works)

Word-of-mouth is nice, but it’s not a system. A referral program is a system. I’ve seen pool builders leave hundreds of thousands of dollars on the table because they never asked for referrals in a structured way.

### The Problem with “Just Ask”

Most builders I meet say, “Yeah, I ask customers to refer me.” That usually means they mention it at the end of the project, maybe send a thank-you note. That’s not enough. The customer is excited about their new pool, but their neighbor might not be in the market for another six months. You need a mechanism that stays top of mind.

**Real example:** A pool builder in Charlotte, North Carolina launched a referral program. Every customer who completed a project received a nice letter with a $1,000 check — made out to them — as a referral fee. They could cash it immediately if they referred a friend who signed a contract. If the friend didn’t buy, the check was just a thank-you gift. (Yes, he included a check even without a referral, which surprised people.) He also offered the referred friend a $1,000 credit toward their pool installation.

Within the first year, he got 22 referrals that closed. Average project value: $55,000. That’s $1.21 million in revenue directly attributable to the program. His cost: $22,000 in referral fees (plus checks to non-referring customers, which he capped at $100 per project). Net gain: over $1 million.

### The Mechanics

You need three things:

1. **A clear offer:** “Refer a friend who signs a contract, and you get $1,000. Your friend gets $1,000 off.” Keep it simple. Avoid complex tiers.
2. **A way to track:** Use a simple CRM like Square or HubSpot to log referrals. Mailchimp can also handle automated referral links if you set up a landing page.
3. **Follow-up:** After 30 days, check in with the referred lead. After 90 days, send a reminder to the original customer. Most referrals happen in the first month after pool completion, but some take longer.

### Don’t Forget the Unhappy Customers

Your referral program should also include a “win-back” component. If a customer had a minor issue during construction, address it first. Then ask for the referral. I’ve seen builders lose referrals because they fixed the pool but never fixed the relationship. A personal call from the owner can turn a 3-star customer into a 5-star referral engine.

### What Tools to Use

- Square Appointments or Booksy to manage schedules and send automated appointment reminders (which also include referral asks)
- Mailchimp to send referral campaign emails
- A simple landing page with a referral form (Google Sites or Carrd is fine)
- Yelp and Google for reviews — reviews are a passive referral tool

A referral program isn’t a cost — it’s an investment with a measurable return. If you’re not running one, you’re leaving 10-20% of potential revenue on the table.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads as a pool builder?**

Start with $2,500-$4,000 per month in a medium-to-large metro area. That’s enough to get 100-150 clicks, test keywords, and see which ones convert. If your cost per lead is under $100 and you’re signing contracts worth $50,000+, your ROI is huge. If you can’t afford that budget, focus on local SEO first — it’s slower but free.

**Q: Do I need a website if I get leads from Facebook or Yelp?**

Yes. Every lead eventually visits your website to check you out. I’ve seen builders lose deals because their site looked like it was built in 2005. A clean, mobile-friendly website with project photos, testimonials, and a clear contact form is non-negotiable. It doesn’t have to be fancy — just functional. Squarespace or Wix with a template works fine for $200/year.

**Q: How do I get more reviews without being pushy?**

Automate it. After a project is completed, send a thank-you email with a direct link to your Google Business Profile review page. Follow up one week later. Offer a small incentive — a $50 gift card to a local restaurant — for leaving a review. (Check Google’s policy; gift cards are allowed for reviews as long as they’re not conditional on positive content.) The key is timing: ask when the customer is happiest, usually right after final walk-through.

**Q: Is Yelp worth it for pool builders?**

It depends on your city. In Austin, Portland, and Denver, Yelp drives serious leads if you have good reviews and a paid advertising budget. In smaller markets, Google is usually enough. Test Yelp with a small monthly budget — $300-$500 — and track cost per lead. If it’s under $150 per lead, it might be worth scaling. Just know that Yelp calls aggressively to sell ads. You can say no.

**Q: Should I use a CRM? Which one?**

Yes, use a CRM if you have more than 10 leads per month. Even a free one like HubSpot or a simple spreadsheet works better than sticky notes. I recommend Square Appointments for pool builders because it handles scheduling, invoicing, and lead management in one place. Cost is about $50/month. If you prefer something more robust, try Salesforce Essentials, but it’s overkill for most small builders.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from local SEO?**

Three to six months if you do it right. You need to claim your Google Business Profile, get consistent reviews, build local backlinks (from local chamber of commerce, community blogs, etc.), and publish regular content. I’ve seen builders in less competitive suburbs see first-page rankings in two months. In dense cities like Los Angeles or New York, it can take six to nine months. But once you rank, the traffic is free and consistent.
