## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Buying a Cheap Email Tool and Sending Blasts to Everyone

**The scene:** A BBQ joint in Austin, Texas. Owner Marcus had 4,200 email addresses collected over six years — from catering invoices, birthday sign-ups, a random QR code at the counter, and a giveaway he ran with a local radio station. He signed up for the cheapest tier of a generic email platform ($29/month) and started sending "Come get brisket!" emails twice a week to the entire list.

**What went wrong:** Within three weeks, his open rate dropped to 11%. His spam complaint rate hit 0.8% (anything over 0.1% gets you flagged by Gmail). Half his list hadn't visited in 18 months. He was paying to annoy people who weren't even in the same city anymore. The emails that did land in inboxes felt irrelevant — why would someone who only ever ordered a chopped beef sandwich on a Tuesday care about a Saturday rib tip special?

**The fix:** We cleaned the list by segmenting based on order history from his Square account. Anyone who hadn't visited in 90 days got a three-email re-engagement sequence ("We miss you — here's $5 off your next order"). Inactive after that? Removed. The remaining 2,100 addresses were split into four segments: lunch regulars (M-Th visits), weekend brunch crowd, catering clients, and late-night bar snack buyers. Each segment got a different offer and send time.

**The outcome:** Open rate went to 38%. Spam complaints dropped to 0.02%. Revenue from email marketing went from roughly $400/month to $1,850/month within two months. The tool switch and segmentation setup cost him about two hours of my time and $79/month for a proper platform. He later told me the re-engagement sequence alone brought back roughly 90 customers who'd ghosted for six months or more.

**What to do instead:** Don't buy an email tool until you've exported your customer data and figured out what you actually know about them. If your POS system can tell you what someone ordered and when they last came in, use that. Most restaurant email failures aren't creative problems — they're data hygiene problems.

### Mistake #2: Automating Social Posts With No Human Oversight

**The scene:** A pizza place in Denver. The owner, Jenna, had been posting manually for years but got busy after opening a second location. She bought a scheduling tool, loaded up three months of content in a weekend, and let it run. Photos of the daily specials, a weekly "behind the counter" shot, some national pizza day stuff.

**What went wrong:** On January 20th, a blizzard dumped two feet of snow on Denver. Roads were closed, delivery drivers couldn't get out, and the power flickered at both locations. The automated system posted a cheerful "Snow day? Come get a slice!" message at 11 AM with a photo of a pepperoni pizza. Customers showed up. The front-of-house staff had to turn people away because the dining room had no heat and the ovens were running at half capacity. The comments section filled with photos of snowed-in streets and complaints. One Yelp review the next day started with "They posted they were open but they weren't."

**The fix:** I set up a simple override protocol. Jenna kept the scheduling tool but added a 15-minute morning check-in: if the post was about weather, holidays, or a special event, it got flagged for manual approval. Everything else (recurring specials, menu photos, employee spotlights) could auto-publish. I also showed her how to create pre-written "we're closed" and "we're delayed" templates in Canva that could be published from her phone in under two minutes.

**The outcome:** No more weather-related disasters. More importantly, engagement actually went up on the auto-posted content because Jenna stopped overthinking it. The time she saved (about four hours a week) went to responding to comments and DMs within an hour instead of once a day. Revenue from Instagram-promoted specials held steady, but the sentiment shift was noticeable — no more "are you even open?" comments.

**What to do instead:** Automate the predictable stuff — recurring specials, blog post shares, menu updates. Flag anything time-sensitive, location-specific, or reactive. And for the love of god, have a "this post does not apply right now" kill switch. One of my clients in Chicago uses IFTTT to send herself a text message every morning with what's scheduled to post that day. Takes 30 seconds. Saves a lot of explaining.

### Mistake #3: Setting Up Loyalty Automation Without Testing the Experience

**The scene:** A bakery-café in Portland, Oregon. The owners, a husband-and-wife team, spent $2,500 on a loyalty app integration with their Toast POS. The concept was simple: scan a QR code at checkout, earn points, get a free pastry after ten visits. They promoted it on social media, put table tents everywhere, and waited for the sign-ups to roll in.

**What went wrong:** Their first customer attempted to sign up on a busy Saturday morning. The app required creating an account with name, email, phone number, and birthday, then verifying by text code, then linking the account to their Toast profile. The customer stood at the register for four minutes, holding up the line, while the cashier tried to help. The customer left without completing the sign-up. Over the next two weeks, only 23 people enrolled out of roughly 4,000 transactions. The owners assumed customers just "didn't want loyalty" and scrapped the whole thing.

**The fix:** I went in and actually ran through the sign-up flow myself. It was awful — five screens, two confirmation emails, and a confusing "points pending" status that never updated. I swapped them to a simpler system: a text message sign-up. Customer texts "PASTRY" to a short code, gets a link to a one-field form (phone number only), and earns points automatically when that number is used at checkout. No app download. No account creation. No scanning required.

**The outcome:** Sign-ups jumped from 23 to 340 in the first month. The average customer earned their free pastry in about 11 visits. Repeat visit frequency among enrolled customers went from 1.2 visits per month to 2.8 visits per month within three months. The free pastries cost roughly $1.20 each in food cost but drove an average check increase of $4.50 per visit among loyalty members — they'd come in for the free croissant but buy a latte and a sandwich.

**What to do instead:** Before you pay for any loyalty system, do two things. First, run through the entire customer experience yourself — from the customer's phone, not your admin dashboard. Time it. If it takes longer than 90 seconds to enroll, it's too long. Second, ask five actual customers (not your friends) to test it while you watch. Don't help them. Just watch where they get stuck. The problem is usually not the concept — it's the friction.

## Build a Review and Reputation Automation System That Actually Works

Most restaurant owners I talk to have the same relationship with Yelp and Google Reviews: they check them obsessively when they're bored, ignore them when they're busy, and feel vaguely resentful about the whole thing. That's not a system. That's a stress loop.

Here's what I've seen work for restaurants in Nashville, Chicago, and a taqueria in San Diego that I still think about sometimes.

**The goal is not to get more reviews.** The goal is to get reviews from customers you can actually serve again, while giving the unhappy ones a way to complain directly to you before they post publicly.

Start with your POS or booking system. If you use Square, Toast, or Clover, you already have a list of every paying customer with a receipt email. Most of these systems have a built-in review request feature, but they send it to everyone indiscriminately. That's how you get a one-star review from someone who ordered the wrong thing by accident last March.

Instead, set up a two-step filter. First, send a "how was your visit?" text message to every customer within 30 minutes of their transaction. Keep it short: "Thanks for coming in! Reply 1 if everything was great, 2 if something could have been better." The response rate on this is usually 15-20% for restaurants, which is decent.

Everyone who replies with "2" gets a text back from a real person (or a very carefully scripted autoresponder if you can't staff it) within 15 minutes: "Sorry about that. Can you tell me what went wrong? I'd like to make it right." Handle the complaint privately. Most people just want to be heard and offered a small gesture — a free drink on their next visit, a gift card code sent by text. If you resolve it in under an hour, roughly 70% of those people will not leave a negative public review. I've tested this across three different restaurant clients and the numbers hold.

Everyone who replies with "1" gets a review link two hours later. But not a generic "review us on Google" link. You need to direct them to the platform where you need reviews most. For most restaurants, that's Google (because it affects local search ranking) and then Yelp (despite everything, people still check it). Send a polite, specific request: "If you have a moment, a Google review helps us show up when neighbors search for [your type of food]. Here's the link."

**The numbers from a client in Nashville:** A hot chicken spot that was averaging 4 new Google reviews per month started this system. After three months, they were getting 28 per month. Their average rating actually went up (from 4.0 to 4.3) because the system captured the happy customers instead of only hearing from the angry ones. Estimated impact on new customer acquisition: roughly $1,200/month in additional revenue from people who found them because their star rating and review count improved in local search.

**Tools to use:** Most POS systems have this capability natively (Square Marketing, Toast Marketing). If yours doesn't, you can rig it with Zapier connecting your POS to a tool like BirdEye or Podium. Expect to pay $200-$400/month for a dedicated reputation management tool. For a single-location restaurant, the Square or Toast native option is usually sufficient and costs $0-50/month extra.

One warning: do not automate responses to bad reviews. I've seen restaurants use AI to reply to negative Yelp reviews and it reads exactly like what it is. A genuine, slightly awkward, human-sounding reply from the owner or manager will always outperform a polished generic response. Apologize, acknowledge the specific issue, offer a resolution, and move on. Do not argue with the reviewer. Do not post the same "thank you for your feedback" template on every response.

## Automate Your Google Ads to Stop Wasting Money on "Near Me" Searches

Google Ads for restaurants is a weird category. You can spend $500/month and get nothing, or spend $500/month and get $5,000 in revenue. The difference is almost never the budget — it's whether your automation is set up to kill losing keywords and scale winning ones without you checking it every day.

**The problem most restaurant owners have:** They set up a campaign, pick some keywords like "pizza delivery [city]" and "Italian restaurant near me," put in a budget, and hope. Two weeks later, they've spent $300 on clicks from people searching for "pizza recipes" or "best Italian restaurant in Chicago" (they're in Nashville). Google's default settings are designed to spend your money, not protect it.

**Here's the setup I use for restaurant clients that works consistently:**

First, use phrase match and exact match keywords only. Do not let Google use "broad match" unless you want to pay for someone in a different state searching for your cuisine type while researching a vacation. I watched a client in Denver spend $87 on clicks from someone in Phoenix searching for "Denver green chili" — technically relevant, completely useless since the person was 850 miles away.

Second, set a location radius of 5 miles for fast-casual and quick-service restaurants, 10 miles for full-service or destination spots. Exclude anything beyond that unless you have a compelling reason (catering, merchandise shipping). Apply a location bid adjustment of +20% for the 1-mile radius — people within walking distance are your highest-value audience.

Third, automate your bid strategy with a target CPA (cost per acquisition). If you know that a customer is worth $25 in profit per visit, and you're willing to spend $8 to acquire that visit, set your target CPA to $8. Google's automated bidding will then try to hit that number. It's not perfect, but it's dramatically better than manual bidding for most restaurant owners who don't have time to adjust bids daily.

Fourth, set up dayparting. If you're a breakfast spot, why are your ads running at 9 PM? If you close at 9 PM, turn ads off at 7 PM (people don't search for dinner and arrive within 2 minutes). I've seen restaurants save 30-40% of their ad budget just by running ads only during the hours they actually want customers to come in.

**Real example from a client in Austin:** A taco shop was spending $1,200/month on Google Ads with a 2.5x ROAS (return on ad spend). Not terrible, but not great. We implemented the above changes — narrowed keywords to exact match, tightened location radius, set target CPA to $7, added dayparting from 11 AM-2 PM and 5 PM-8 PM. First full month after changes: $780 spent, 4.8x ROAS. The campaign was actually driving more customers because the clicks were higher quality, even though total spend was lower. Revenue from ads went from $3,000/month to $3,744/month on $420 less ad spend.

The key is that none of this is set-it-and-forget-it. But with the right automated rules, you can check your ads for 15 minutes a week instead of two hours a day. Set up automated email alerts when your cost per click goes above a threshold, when your impression share drops below 10%, or when a keyword spends over $50 with zero conversions. Google Ads will send these natively. Use them.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Won't my customers hate automated emails and texts? I don't want to be spammy.**

Customers hate irrelevant emails. They don't hate timely, useful communication. The difference is whether you're sending "come eat here" to everyone or "your favorite dish is back for one week only" to people who've ordered that dish before. I've seen open rates of 50%+ on segmented restaurant emails. The bar for "spammy" is lower than you think — if you respect frequency and relevance, most customers welcome a reminder that you exist.

**Q: How much should I actually budget for marketing automation tools as a single-location restaurant?**

For a single location, expect $50-$150/month for email marketing (Mailchimp or Constant Contact with a decent subscriber count), $0-$50/month for social media scheduling (Buffer or Later), and $200-$400/month if you want a reputation management tool. Your POS system may already include some of this. Total should be under $500/month unless you're doing heavy Google Ads or have multiple locations. Most restaurants I work with spend between $200 and $400 per month and get solid results.

**Q: I barely know how to use the tools I already have. How am I supposed to set up automation?**

You're not supposed to set it up yourself if you don't have the time or patience. This is exactly where I come in — I set up the systems, write the email sequences, configure the triggers, and show you a 15-minute weekly check-in that covers everything. The tools themselves are not complicated once someone who knows what they're doing configures them. You don't need to become an automation expert. You need to hire one for a few hours and then run the system.

**Q: What if something goes wrong — an automated post goes live at the wrong time, or an email sends to the wrong segment?**

Something will go wrong at some point. It happens. The fix is not to avoid automation — it's to build in fail-safes. Use scheduled posts that you can pause, set up approval workflows for sensitive content, and check your dashboard once a day for the first two weeks after setup. After that, the error rate drops to near zero. I've been doing this for years and can count on one hand the number of automation failures that caused real problems. Most "disasters" are noticed by three people and forgotten in an hour.

**Q: Can I really measure if automation is driving more revenue, or is it just a nice-to-have?**

You can measure it, but you need to set up tracking before you start. Use unique coupon codes, UTM parameters on links, and ask customers how they heard about you. For email, the metric to watch is revenue per email sent, not open rate. For ads, it's cost per acquisition. For loyalty, it's repeat visit rate. If you can't measure the impact within 30 days, you either have the wrong setup or you're not looking at the right numbers. I don't recommend automation for restaurants that can't commit to a basic tracking system — it becomes expensive guesswork.

**Q: When's the right time to start — when I'm slow and have time, or when I'm busy and drowning?**

Start when you're busy and drowning. If you're slow, you don't have the data volume to know what's working, and you'll over-engineer a system that doesn't fit your actual business rhythm. The best time to set up automation is when you're overwhelmed, because the pain is specific and the solution gets immediately tested under real conditions. I've set up systems for restaurants doing $8,000 in weekly revenue and for ones doing $80,000. The ones that stuck were the ones where the owner said "I literally cannot keep doing this manually" and meant it.
