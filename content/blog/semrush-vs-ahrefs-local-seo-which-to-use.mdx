**Semrush vs Ahrefs: Key Differences**

Both Semrush and Ahrefs offer robust SEO toolsets, but they cater to different types of businesses and users. Here's a brief overview of each tool:

### Semrush: All-in-One SEO Solution

Semrush is a comprehensive SEO platform that offers a wide range of features, including keyword research, technical SEO audits, competitor analysis, and content optimization. Its user-friendly interface makes it accessible to businesses of all sizes, from solo entrepreneurs to large corporations.

### Ahrefs: Keyword Research and Link Building

Ahrefs is primarily a keyword research and link building tool, with a focus on providing actionable insights for content creation and backlink acquisition. Its advanced features, such as SERP analysis and content gap analysis, make it a favorite among SEO specialists and agencies.

**Which Tool is Right for Your Local Business?**

When choosing between Semrush and Ahrefs, consider your specific needs and goals. If you're a small business owner with limited resources, Semrush might be the better choice due to its user-friendly interface and all-in-one approach. However, if you're an SEO specialist or have a dedicated marketing team, Ahrefs might be more suitable due to its advanced features and robust link building capabilities.

Let's dive deeper into the features and pricing of each tool to help you make an informed decision.

* Project limit: 500 vs 500 (Guru), 1500 vs 1500 (Pro), 5000 vs 5000 (Business)

Ahrefs offers a range of pricing plans, from the $99/month Lite plan to the $999/month Agency plan. The main differences between plans include:

Ahrefs also offers a 7-day free trial, allowing you to test its features and pricing before committing to a plan.

**What About the Cost?**

However, Ahrefs' advanced features and robust link building capabilities might justify the higher cost for businesses with dedicated marketing teams.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Chasing High-Volume Keywords Without Local Intent

**The story:** A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas came to me proud of his new blog post titled “Best Coffee in the World.” He’d used Ahrefs to find a keyword with 12,000 monthly searches. He spent $800 on a freelance writer and another $200 promoting it on Facebook. Thirty days later: zero coffee sales from the post. Zero phone calls. His organic traffic jumped to 150 visits a day, but they were all people in India and Brazil.

**What went wrong:** Ahrefs shows global search volume by default. For a local coffee shop, “best coffee in Austin” gets 1,400 searches a month — but those are local customers with wallets. The owner never refined his keyword list to include city or neighborhood terms. He also assumed volume equals value.

**The fix:** Use Semrush’s local keyword tool (under “Keyword Magic Tool” > filter by location) or manually add geo-modifiers in Ahrefs by appending city names to your seed keywords. I made him redo the research: “best coffee Austin,” “coffee shop near me Austin,” “Austin local coffee.” Total volume was 3,200, but 80% of those searchers were within 10 miles.

**The outcome:** He rewrote the blog post targeting those local terms. Within six weeks, organic traffic grew 40%, and store visits (tracked via a Square promo code) increased 15%. Revenue from that single post rose to $2,800 in the first month. Total rewrite cost: $400. Net gain: $2,400.

### Mistake #2: Buying Backlinks from Random Directories

**The story:** A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon thought backlinks were the secret to beating her competitors. She paid a $500/month service that promised “high-quality local backlinks.” The service spammed 200 generic directories — think “Portland Hair Salons List” and “Oregon Business Index.” Google caught on within two months. Her local rankings dropped from page 1 to page 4. She lost 30% of her organic traffic.

**What went wrong:** She never used Ahrefs to check the quality of those backlinks. Most were from sites with spam scores above 60, zero traffic, and no relationship to Portland or hair. She didn’t know that Google’s local algorithm values relevance over quantity.

**The fix:** Use Ahrefs’ Site Explorer to audit your backlinks. Sort by “Domain Rating” and “Traffic.” Look for links from actual local businesses, chambers of commerce, local news sites, and industry associations. I showed her how to find competitor backlinks with Ahrefs’ “Competing Domains” report. She identified three local fashion blogs and two Portland food magazines that linked to a competitor but not to her. She reached out with a free haircut offer in exchange for a link and a review.

**The outcome:** She built 10 high-quality backlinks over three months. Rankings recovered and improved. Traffic returned to pre-penalty levels, then grew 30% beyond. Her booking rate via Booksy increased 25%. The $500/month waste stopped. She saved $6,000/year and gained an estimated $1,200/month in new clients.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Google Business Profile While Obsessing Over Site Audits

**The story:** A pet groomer in Denver, Colorado subscribed to Semrush’s Pro plan at $139/month. He ran weekly technical SEO audits — fixing broken links, optimizing meta descriptions, improving page speed. His website scored 95/100 on the Site Audit. But when I looked at his Google Business Profile, it had incorrect hours, no photos, and zero reviews from the last six months. He was not appearing in the local pack for “Denver pet grooming.”

**What went wrong:** He assumed website SEO was the only game. But for local businesses, Google Business Profile (GBP) is often the first thing customers see. Semrush has a dedicated GBP auditing feature (under “Local SEO > Listing Management”), but he never used it.

**The fix:** I walked him through Semrush’s GBP audit. It flagged missing categories, wrong phone number, and a description that didn’t include “pet grooming” or “Denver.” We updated everything. Then we used Semrush’s review monitoring to track new reviews and respond within 24 hours. He also added services like “nail trimming” and “de-shedding” to the GBP listing.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, his GBP started showing in the top 3 local results for “Denver pet grooming.” Website traffic from GBP grew 50%. Bookings via his Squarespace site jumped 25%. His monthly revenue increased by $1,200 — more than covering the Semrush subscription. He later told me he wished he’d spent the first two months working on GBP instead of fixing a broken link on a page nobody visits.

### Mistake #4: Thinking You Need Only One Tool (And Missing Half Your Data)

**The story:** A fitness studio owner in Nashville, Tennessee signed up for Semrush and cancelled his Ahrefs subscription to save $129/month. He used Semrush for keyword research, site audits, and content ideas. But when he wanted to analyze a competitor’s backlink profile, Semrush’s backlink data was less detailed. He missed that a rival had built a strong network of links from local health blogs. He also couldn’t see which keywords his competitor was ranking for in Ahrefs’ “Organic Keywords” export.

**What went wrong:** Each tool has strengths. Semrush excels at site audits, local SEO, and PPC data. Ahrefs has a larger backlink index and more granular keyword data for content-driven SEO. Using only one means you’re blind to certain strategies.

**The fix:** Instead of choosing, I helped him layer the tools. He kept Semrush for local SEO (listings, reviews, site audits) and PPC analysis. He reinstated Ahrefs at the $129/month Lite plan, but only used it for backlink research and content gap analysis. Total cost: $268/month. That might sound high, but he also cancelled two other unnecessary tools (a $79/month rank tracker and a $49/month backlink checker), saving $128. Net cost increase: $40/month.

**The outcome:** With both tools, he identified three local blogs where a competitor had backlinks. He secured links from two of them. His content gap analysis showed he was missing a “free workout guide” keyword that drove 800 monthly searches in Nashville. He created the guide, ranked #1, and generated 50 new email subscribers per week. His monthly studio revenue grew 15% ($1,800). The $40/month extra was a bargain.

## Beyond Keywords: How Semrush and Ahrefs Handle Local Listings and Reputation

Most small business owners think SEO tools are just for keywords. That’s like thinking a coffee machine only makes hot water. Semrush and Ahrefs serve very different menus when it comes to local listings and reputation management.

Semrush has a dedicated Local SEO toolkit. It includes listing management (pushing your business info to Google, Yelp, Bing, Apple Maps, and 60+ directories), review monitoring (track ratings across platforms), and a Google Business Profile audit that checks accuracy and completeness. I’ve used this with a Chicago bakery that had inconsistent hours on Yelp vs Google. Semrush flagged the discrepancy. A single correction led to a 20% increase in walk-ins because people stopped showing up at 9 AM when the bakery actually opened at 7 AM. That bakery’s owner, a skeptical guy named Mike, tracked the change via Square sales data. Revenue from 7–9 AM rose from $1,200 to $2,200 per week. He now calls Semrush his “hourly profit fixer.”

Ahrefs, in contrast, offers zero local listing management. No directory sync. No review monitoring. No GBP audit. If you’re a coffee shop or a hair salon, that’s a gap you’ll need to fill with another tool — Yelp’s Business account ($0–$300/month) or a service like Moz Local ($129/year). The problem is, now you’re juggling multiple dashboards.

But Ahrefs isn’t useless here. Use its “Site Explorer” to see how your GBP landing page is performing in organic search. Enter your GBP URL (usually a Google-hosted page) and look at organic traffic estimates. I once did this for a Denver pet groomer (yes, the same one from Mistake #3). Ahrefs showed his GBP page had zero organic traffic. That’s normal — Google doesn’t treat it like a regular website. But the insight helped him realize he needed to drive traffic to his actual website, not rely on GBP alone.

If you need to manage reviews and listings, Semrush wins. If you only care about backlinks and keyword data, Ahrefs plus a free Yelp account will do. For a small business with a $500/month marketing budget, I’d pick Semrush and skip the $129/year local listing tool. The built-in features cover more ground.

## Integrating PPC and Local SEO: Which Tool Helps You Spend Smarter?

Let’s talk about paid ads — because half the small business owners I meet waste money on Google Ads before fixing their organic local SEO. Semrush and Ahrefs treat PPC very differently.

Semrush has a full PPC toolkit. You can research competitor ads, find high-intent keywords with “Commercial Intent” scores, and even create ad copy suggestions. For a plumber in New York City, I used Semrush to find keywords like “emergency plumber NYC” and “$500 toilet repair.” The keyword analysis showed that “$500 toilet repair” had 300 searches per month with a high commercial intent (people ready to spend). Competition was low because other plumbers chased broad terms like “plumber NYC” (12,000 searches, but 90% informational). He set up a Google Ads campaign with a $500 monthly budget targeting that specific phrase. Conversion rate: 30%. Cost per click: $4.50. Revenue from those ads: $3,800 in one month.

Ahrefs does not have a PPC tool. At all. If you run Google Ads, you cannot use Ahrefs to analyze competitor ad strategies or estimate cost-per-click. You’d need to export keyword data and check Google Ads manually. That’s doable but inefficient.

However, Ahrefs offers something Semrush doesn’t: “Keyword Difficulty” scores that are more granular for organic content. If you’re a small business owner who prefers organic growth (because you hate paying per click), Ahrefs helps you find low-difficulty local keywords. For a hair salon in Austin, I used Ahrefs to find “Austin balayage” — difficulty 12, volume 450. She wrote a blog post, ranked #2, and now gets 30 weekly inquiries without ad spend. That saved her $600/month in Google Ads she was burning on generic terms.

My recommendation: If your local business relies on Google Ads (plumbers, locksmiths, dentists), you need Semrush. If you’re all-organic (coffee shops, bakeries, yoga studios), Ahrefs might be better, but you’ll miss the listing management. Ideally, use Semrush for PPC and local listings, and supplement with Ahrefs for content keyword research. The combined cost of $268/month is less than what most businesses waste on one bad ad campaign.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I use just Ahrefs for local SEO if I own a coffee shop in Austin?**

You can, but you’ll be missing tools for local listings, review monitoring, and Google Business Profile management. Ahrefs is excellent for keyword research and backlinks. For a coffee shop, that means you can find local search terms and see who links to competitors. But you’ll need to manually update your Google Business Profile and Yelp. If that’s fine, Ahrefs works. If you want one dashboard to manage your online presence, Semrush is better.

**Q: Is Semrush worth $139/month for a small hair salon with a $300 marketing budget?**

Depends on your goals. If you’re already spending $200/month on Google Ads with no tracking, Semrush might pay for itself by showing you which keywords to bid on. I’ve seen hair salons save $100/month in wasted ad spend by switching to Semrush’s keyword recommendations. If you don’t run ads and only need basic keyword research, Ahrefs Lite at $129 might be overkill too. Consider Semrush’s free trial first to see if the local SEO features actually improve your booking rate.

**Q: Which tool is better for tracking local rankings — my store in Nashville?**

Both track local rankings. Semrush lets you set a specific location (zip code or city) and device type. Ahrefs also has a rank tracker with location filters. I prefer Semrush for local because its reports include Google Maps pack positions, not just organic web results. Ahrefs focuses on organic web ranking. If you own a yoga studio, you probably care more about appearing in the local pack than ranking #2 on Wikipedia. Semrush shows both.

**Q: Do I need both Semrush and Ahrefs, or can I get by with one?**

You can get by with one, but you’ll miss data. I ran a test for a Chicago bakery using only Semrush. It missed a backlink from a local food blog that Ahrefs caught. But the bakery didn’t care because their local listings were perfect. If your business relies heavily on backlinks (like a blog-driven pet grooming site), you might need both. If you’re a plumber who only cares about local pack and ads, Semrush is enough.

**Q: How accurate are the local keyword volumes in these tools?**

Not perfect. Semrush and Ahrefs both estimate search volumes. For local terms (e.g., “Denver pet grooming”), the numbers are often underreported because they sample data from Google’s API. I’ve seen actual search volume be 2x higher. Use volumes as directional, not exact. The trend — whether volume is rising or falling — is more useful. For pricing, I trust Semrush’s local volume estimates slightly more because they incorporate location-specific data from their own clickstream panel.

**Q: Can I use these tools to spy on my competitors’ Google Ads spend?**

Semrush gives you an estimate of competitor ad spend and top keywords. Ahrefs does not. If you want to see what ads your local competitor is running, Semrush’s “Advertising Research” is the only option here. I used it for a dentist in NYC who was losing patients to a competitor running “Invisalign NYC” ads. Semrush showed the competitor was spending $4,000/month on that keyword. The dentist realized he couldn’t outbid them, so he targeted “clear aligners NYC” instead — cheaper, less competition. He got a 20% conversion rate on a $200/month ad budget.
