Through exchanges like Vistar Media and operators like Lamar Advertising's programmatic platform, you can now buy digital billboard time starting at CPMs of **$5–$9 — with no minimum spend required**.

A $500 DOOH budget can now buy 55,000–100,000 impressions on physical screens in your local market. This guide explains exactly how.

## What DOOH Inventory Types Exist

DOOH screens aren't just highway billboards. There are four major inventory categories, each with different audience profiles, CPMs, and dwell times:

| Category | Examples | Typical CPM | Dwell Time | Best For |
|----------|----------|-------------|------------|----------|
| **Roadside** | Highway billboards, urban digital displays | $5–$15 | Seconds (drive-by) | Brand awareness, directional ("turn left in 500m") |
| **Transit** | Bus shelters, subway stations, airports, rail | $8–$20 | 2–15 minutes | Commuter reach, longer message |
| **Retail** | Mall kiosks, grocery store screens, pharmacy displays, gas station screens | $10–$25 | 1–5 minutes | Purchase-moment targeting, promotional offers |
| **Venue** | Gym screens, restaurant displays, office lobbies, sports bars | $12–$30 | 10–30 minutes | Captive audience, contextual relevance |

**Best for local service businesses:**
- Coffee shops: Transit (morning commuter screens near your location) + gas station screens (captive audience within 1 mile)
- Hair salons / spas: Mall retail screens + venue screens in complementary businesses
- Fitness studios: Gym venue screens in competitor locations + transit screens near residential neighborhoods
- Pet groomers: Retail screens near pet supply stores, vet clinics

## Dynamic Creative: The Feature That Makes DOOH 10x More Effective

Static DOOH is a billboard. Dynamic DOOH is a real-time media channel that adjusts to context automatically.

### Weather-Triggered Creative

**How it works:** Define creative rules in your ad server (Broadsign, Vistar's creative tools) that activate based on local weather conditions fetched in real time.

**Real brand examples:**
- **Sprite** — ads activated only when local temperature exceeded a threshold
- **Aperol** — promoted warm-weather cocktails at 66°F+
- **McDonald's Qatar** — summer campaign activated at 35–45°C, delivered 7% sales lift
- **Dunkin'** — hot coffee creative in the morning, cold brew in the afternoon, weather-adjusted

**Impact data:** Weather-adjusted DOOH creative drives a **57% performance increase** vs. static creative (Clear Channel UK analysis). Contextually relevant messaging increases effectiveness by **17% on average** across all conditions.

---

### Dayparting

Show different messages at different times of day without changing the media buy. A single campaign for a coffee shop can run:

- **6:00–9:00am:** "Morning espresso — open now, 3-minute walk from here"
- **11:30am–1:30pm:** "Lunch break? Cold brew and sandwiches ready"
- **3:00–5:00pm:** "Afternoon slump? We're your fix — open until 7pm"
- **Evening (screens near entertainment venues):** "Wind down after work — dessert + coffee combos"

Samsung ran **105 creative variants** for day vs. night content (6am–6pm and 6pm–6am) in one campaign. Dayparting is available on all major programmatic DOOH platforms at no additional CPM cost — it's a creative and scheduling decision, not a premium feature.

### Location and Event Triggers

Dynamic creative can activate based on:
- **Traffic conditions** — "Traffic on I-35? Take the shortcut through our drive-through"
- **Sports scores / local events** — "Playoffs tonight — watch the game with us"
- **Proximity to your business** — Different messages on screens 0.3 miles away vs. 2 miles away
- **Inventory / real-time offers** — Connect your POS or inventory system to update DOOH creative based on what's in stock or available

## DOOH Attribution: Proving It Worked

The biggest objection to DOOH has always been measurement. "How do I know a billboard drove someone to my store?" Programmatic DOOH now has real attribution tools.

**Mobile device ID matching:** The programmatic platform logs which mobile devices were in the vicinity of the DOOH screen when your ad ran. It then tracks whether those devices subsequently appear at your business location. This foot traffic attribution is standard in Vistar Media, Place Exchange, and StackAdapt campaigns.

**Cross-device retargeting:** Platforms can serve a follow-up digital ad (mobile display, social, CTV) to the specific device that was near your DOOH screen. Someone walks past your billboard, sees your ad, and then gets a retargeted display ad on their phone 30 minutes later — creating a coordinated two-touchpoint awareness sequence.

**Combined CTV + DOOH campaigns:** The highest-performing local campaigns run DOOH and CTV simultaneously — the DOOH reaches people physically near your business; CTV reaches them in their homes. Both channels reinforce the same message across different contexts. StackAdapt handles this in a single campaign dashboard.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is programmatic DOOH and how is it different from traditional billboard advertising?

Traditional billboard advertising means signing a 4-week contract with a single operator for a specific billboard location — fixed price, fixed placement, static creative. Programmatic DOOH uses automated bidding to buy digital screen time across many operators simultaneously, with dynamic targeting, real-time creative updates, and CPM-based pricing. You can run for any duration, adjust creative daily, and target specific locations, times, and audiences — starting with no minimum spend on platforms like Lamar's programmatic inventory.

### How much does programmatic DOOH cost for a small business?

Lamar's programmatic inventory starts at $5 CPM with no minimum — meaning $100 buys approximately 20,000 impressions on digital billboards near your location. Retail and transit screens run $10–$25 CPM; venue screens (gyms, restaurants) $12–$30 CPM. A $500/month total DOOH budget spread across roadside + retail + venue formats typically delivers 60,000–100,000 targeted impressions.

### What creative specs do DOOH ads require?

Static images: 16:9 aspect ratio (most screens), minimum 1920×1080px resolution, JPEG or PNG format. Short looping videos: 10–15 second loops with no audio (DOOH screens are typically silent in public spaces), MP4 format. Dynamic creative requires a compatible ad server (Broadsign, Vistar's creative tools) to manage condition-based template switching. Keep copy minimal — 7 words or fewer for roadside; slightly more for transit/retail formats.

### Can I target specific streets or zip codes with DOOH advertising?

Yes. Programmatic DOOH lets you target by specific screen locations, zip codes, radius around your business, or geographic area (city, DMA). StackAdapt and Vistar Media both support pin-level targeting — you can exclude screens that are outside your delivery radius and focus spend on screens within 1–2 miles of your business.

### How do I measure whether my DOOH campaign is working?

Primary method: mobile device ID matching. Programmatic platforms track which mobile devices were near each DOOH screen when your ad ran, then measure whether those devices subsequently visited your location. Foot traffic attribution reports are standard in StackAdapt and Vistar campaigns. Secondary method: cross-channel lift — run CTV and DOOH simultaneously and measure foot traffic uplift vs. baseline in the campaign period.

### What's the minimum duration for a DOOH campaign?

With programmatic buying, there's no minimum duration — you can run a single-day campaign. Traditional billboard contracts require 4-week minimums. For small businesses, a 2–4 week campaign with at least $300–$500 in spend is typically needed to build enough frequency for the audience to notice and remember the message.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Buying Bus Shelters When You Need Curbside

A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas — let's call it Red River Roasters — spent $1,800 on a 4-week programmatic DOOH campaign targeting bus shelters within a 2-mile radius of their location. The thinking made sense: commuters waiting for the bus are bored, looking at their phone, might want coffee.

The problem? Nobody waits at a bus shelter in Austin for more than 90 seconds in July. The dwell time is zero. The CPM was $11 because transit inventory is priced for captive audiences — but there was no captive audience. They got 163,000 impressions. They also got exactly zero redemption on the "show this ad for a free pastry" offer they ran.

**What they should have done:** The same $1,800 buys about 225,000 impressions on roadside LED boards along the main commuter routes (Mopac, I-35 frontage roads) at a $7–$8 CPM. Those boards get 3–5 seconds of attention at a red light, which is more than a bus shelter gets. A gas station top-of-pump screen campaign at the same budget would have put the ad directly in front of someone idling with a credit card in hand.

**The fix:** Match the inventory type to the behavior, not the demographic. Bus shelters work in Chicago in February. They don't work in Austin in July. Red River Roasters switched to a mix of roadside digital boards and convenience store pump-top ads for their next $1,200 campaign. They ran a QR code to a mobile order page. The store tracked 42 orders from that code in three weeks. That's roughly $380 in incremental revenue from a $1,200 spend — not amazing, but a hell of a lot better than zero.

### Mistake #2: Running the Same Creative on Every Screen

A pet groomer in Nashville — Paws & Claws — bought a programmatic DOOH campaign through Vistar Media targeting "pet owners within 5 miles." They uploaded one generic creative: "Book your appointment today! Call 555-0123."

The campaign ran across gym screens, mall kiosks, convenience store pumps, and roadside digital billboards. The CPM was $6.50. The $1,000 budget bought about 153,000 impressions.

Here's what actually happened: The gym screens played between sets at 7 PM, when people are sweaty and not thinking about dog grooming. The mall kiosks ran mid-afternoon on a Tuesday, when foot traffic was low. The gas pump screens ran at 8 AM, when people are rushing to work. The roadside board ran during lunch hour.

They got 12 calls. Two booked an appointment. The campaign lost money.

**What they should have done:** One creative across four different environments is lazy. The fix was splitting the $1,000 into four $250 campaigns with environment-specific messaging:

- Gym screens: "Your dog misses you. Drop them off before your workout."
- Gas pumps: "Drive-thru drop-off on your way home. No appointment needed between 3–5 PM."
- Mall kiosks: "Saturday grooming spots still available. Walk-ins welcome."
- Roadside: "Pet groomer on 21st Avenue. Book online now."

The next $1,000 campaign used Booksy's integration with their booking calendar plus the creative variants above. They tracked 37 confirmed bookings from the campaign — roughly $2,590 in revenue at their average ticket of $70 per groom. The same budget, better creative targeting, 5x the return.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Dayparting Entirely

A hair salon in Portland, Oregon spent $900 on a programmatic DOOH campaign running on street-level digital screens in the Pearl District. They ran it Monday through Friday, 8 AM to 8 PM, for two weeks. Their thinking: "Our clients work 9–5, so we want to be top of mind when they're commuting."

The problem is that most of those $7 CPM impressions ran during the "desk hours" gap — 10 AM to 2 PM — when the screens are mostly seen by tourists and people on lunch errands who already have a haircut appointment elsewhere. The campaign generated 128,000 impressions and exactly 3 click-throughs to their website.

**What they should have done:** Dayparting in DOOH is free. The Portland salon should have run the campaign only during:
- 6–9 AM (commute, planning the day)
- 4–7 PM (commute home, making decisions)
- All day Saturday

Those time blocks have higher intent. The same $900 budget compressed into those windows would have cost the same CPM but delivered the impressions when people are actually making decisions about after-work or weekend appointments.

The salon ran a second $900 campaign with dayparting. They also added a 20% discount code only available through the ad. The result: 19 booked appointments tracked through the discount code alone. At $85 per ticket, that's $1,615 in revenue. Not a home run, but a positive ROAS. The first campaign was a waste. The second one paid for itself.

## How to Actually Track DOOH Performance (Without Guessing)

Most small business owners I talk to say the same thing: "I can't track a billboard." That's true for traditional outdoor. It's not true for programmatic DOOH.

Here are four methods that actually work, ranked from least to most reliable.

### Method 1: Unique URL + UTM Parameters

This is the baseline. Every DOOH campaign you run gets a unique landing page URL with a UTM source of "dooh" and a campaign name. If your website gets 20 visitors and 17 of them are from other channels, you know the DOOH isn't driving clicks — but it might be driving awareness.

The catch: DOOH click-through rates are usually 0.02%–0.08%. If your campaign delivers 100,000 impressions, you're looking at 20–80 clicks. That's not nothing, but it's also not a lot of data. You need at least 2–3 weeks of run time before the numbers mean anything statistically.

### Method 2: Promo Code or Offer Code

This is more reliable for brick-and-mortar businesses. Put a specific code on your DOOH creative — "DOOH20" for 20% off, or "WASH10" for $10 off a service. Track how many times that code is used.

A dog daycare in Denver ran "DAYCARE20" on digital billboards near downtown. They tracked 47 redemptions over 30 days. The average spend per visit was $32. That's $1,504 in revenue from a $600 campaign. The code was never used on any other channel.

**Which tools work best with this:** Square's QR code builder and Mailchimp's landing pages. Both let you generate unique codes and track redemptions without writing code. If you're using Booksy for appointment booking, set up a custom promo code inside the platform — it's free and tracks automatically.

### Method 3: QR Code to a Specific Landing Page

QR codes on digital billboards work about 60% as well as they do on print — the motion hurts response rates — but they're still useful. Use a dynamic QR code (free from QR Code Monkey or similar) that redirects to a page you can change later.

The trick: Make the landing page mobile-optimized and one-click. If someone scans a QR code on a gas pump and your page takes 6 seconds to load, they're gone. Use Google Sites or Carrd for fast, free landing pages.

### Method 4: Phone Number Tracking

Old school, but reliable for service businesses. Get a Google Voice number or use a call tracking service like CallRail. Put that number on your DOOH creative. Any calls to that number came from the billboard. This is especially useful for plumbers, electricians, and contractors who don't get walk-ins.

A locksmith in Chicago ran a $500 DOOH campaign with a unique number. They received 22 calls over two weeks. Average job value: $185. That's $4,070 in revenue. Cost per call: $22.73. That's cheaper than Google Ads in most markets.

## When to Skip DOOH Entirely (Honest Advice)

Not every business needs DOOH. I've consulted with three small business owners in the last year who should not have spent money on it. Here's when you should skip it.

**When your business is appointment-only and you don't have walk-in traffic:** A private yoga studio in Los Angeles spent $800 on DOOH targeting "fitness enthusiasts within 2 miles." They got 0 bookings. Why? Because their sessions required pre-registration and the website didn't load well on mobile. The billboard drove curiosity, but the booking experience killed the conversion. Fix the website first.

**When you need phone calls, not impressions:** A mobile car detailer in San Diego ran a DOOH campaign targeting "car owners in La Jolla." They got impressions. They got zero calls. Why? Because mobile detailers need to be available to pick up the phone when someone calls. The owner was detailing cars with his phone on silent. He missed every call. A $500 campaign wasted because the backend didn't work.

**When your geo-target radius is under 1 mile:** DOOH programmatic platforms can target down to a 0.5-mile radius, but the inventory available gets thin. If you run a coffee shop in a dense downtown area, a 0.5-mile radius might have 3–4 screens. A 2-mile radius might have 30–40. The smaller radius costs the same CPM but gets fewer impressions, making your cost per acquisition higher than Facebook ads.

**When you're running a digital-only brand:** If you don't have a physical location in the city where the DOOH runs, don't bother. DOOH is best for driving foot traffic to a specific place. If you're selling a Shopify product and run a billboard in Chicago, you're paying for brand awareness in a city where you have zero local presence. That's a luxury, not a strategy.

## The No-Minimum Trap (and How to Beat It)

Programmatic DOOH platforms advertise "no minimum spend." That's true — you can buy $500 worth of impressions. But here's what they don't tell you:

**Most platforms charge a $150–$300 creative review fee** for the first campaign. This is the cost of having their team check your file for resolution, aspect ratio, and content compliance. Some platforms charge per creative file. If you have three creative variants (which you should), that's $450–$900 in fees before you've bought a single impression.

**There's also usually a $50–$100 monthly platform fee** on managed accounts. If you're buying $500/mo, that's a 10–20% overhead cost on top of your CPM.

**How to avoid it:** Ask the platform or your DSP (Vistar, Hivestack, etc.) for a "test-and-learn" campaign. Many will waive the creative fee if you commit to a second campaign within 60 days. Some will also waive the monthly fee if you buy through a self-serve portal rather than a managed account.

**The real minimum spend:** For a small business, the effective minimum is about $800–$1,000 for the first month. That covers the creative fee ($150–$300), the platform fee ($50–$100), and enough impressions ($500–$600) to generate a meaningful test. Anything under that and you're paying more in fees than in actual media.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I target specific addresses, like my competitors' locations?**

Yes, through geo-fencing. Most DSPs let you draw a radius around any point. You can target within 0.5 miles of a competitor's location. The CPM won't change — it's based on the inventory in that area, not the name of the location. Just be aware that a small radius means fewer screens and fewer impressions. A 0.5-mile radius around a Starbucks in downtown Chicago might have 2–3 screens. A 0.5-mile radius around a suburban Starbucks might have zero.

**Q: How do I know if anyone actually saw my ad?**

You won't get guaranteed viewability like you do with Google Ads. DOOH vendors report "delivered impressions" based on the screen's location and foot traffic data. Lamar and Clear Channel use Placed Analytics to estimate foot traffic lift, but that's a separate product. For small budgets, the best proxy is your own tracking — promo codes, QR codes, unique phone numbers. If none of those show lift, the impressions might be hitting empty sidewalks.

**Q: What's the minimum budget that actually works?**

For a single test campaign, $800–$1,000. That gets you enough impressions (100,000–150,000) and a long enough run time (minimum 7 days, ideally 14) to see if there's a signal. Do not put $300 into a 3-day campaign. That's like buying one Facebook ad and judging the whole platform. Give it time and volume.

**Q: Can I pause a campaign mid-run?**

Yes, if you're buying programmatically. Unlike traditional outdoor where you're locked into a 4-week contract, programmatic DOOH allows real-time adjustments. If your code isn't getting redemptions on day 3, you can pause, change the creative, and restart. Most platforms support daily optimization. The exception is roadside digital billboards on fixed rotation — those sometimes lock you into the full flight. Ask before you buy.

**Q: Is programmatic DOOH better than Facebook ads for my local business?**

It depends on what you're selling. Facebook has better targeting — you can hit "dog owners in Austin who like Blue Buffalo" — but DOOH has higher attention. A Facebook ad gets scrolled past in 0.4 seconds. A digital billboard at a gas pump gets 30 seconds of idle attention. For impulse buys (food, coffee, services) in a specific location, DOOH often beats Facebook on cost per foot traffic. For complex sales (consultations, high-ticket services), Facebook wins.

**Q: What happens if my ad runs next to something inappropriate?**

DOOH inventory is screened by the platform and the venue. You won't get placed next to adult content or political ads. The bigger risk is proximity — your ad for a pet groomer might run on a screen 20 feet from a competitor's static billboard. That's not a conflict; it's just competition. You can ask your DSP for "brand safety" exclusions, but for small business DOOH, this is rarely a real problem.
