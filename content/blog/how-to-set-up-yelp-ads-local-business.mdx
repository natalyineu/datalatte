If your coffee shop is losing customers to the next-door chain or your fitness studio can’t fill weekend classes, Yelp Ads might be your missing piece. Unlike Google Ads, Yelp targets users already looking for local services—people ready to spend money at *your* business. But most small owners skip it, assuming it’s too expensive or complicated.

## Step 1: Set Up Your Yelp Business Account

Before running ads, your Yelp Business Profile must be complete. This is free and acts as your storefront for potential customers. Start at yelp.com/business, and verify your address using a postcard or phone call. 

Add high-quality photos of your space (a clean shot of your salon’s waiting area, for example). Write your “About” section in plain English—avoid jargon. A pet groomer in Austin, TX, increased walk-ins by 15% simply by listing their USDA-certified products in the description.

<Callout type="tip">Wait 24–48 hours for Yelp to verify your business. Ads won’t run until it’s approved.</Callout>

---

## Step 2: Create a Yelp Ad Campaign

Log into your Yelp Ads dashboard and click “Create Campaign.” Choose your goal: “More Visits” or “More Calls.” If you own a yoga studio, “More Visits” makes sense. Hair salons might prefer “More Calls” to book appointments.

Set your budget. Start with $25–$50/month for testing. A coffee shop in Seattle used $30/month to target a 2-mile radius and gained 40 new monthly customers. Your campaign will auto-pause if you go over budget.

<Callout type="warning">Don’t set a daily budget unless you want steady traffic. Weekly budgets give Yelp more flexibility to optimize for cheaper clicks.</Callout>

---

## Step 3: Target the Right People

Yelp Ads let you target users by location, age, and past behavior. For example, if you’re a dog walker in Toronto, target 25–45-year-olds within a 5-mile radius of your office. Exclude areas with competing services if you can.

Use “Interest-Driven Targeting” to reach users who’ve searched for similar services. A barbershop in Chicago boosted conversion rates by 20% by adding the term “cheap men’s haircut” to their interest list.

---

## Step 4: Optimize Your Ad Spend

Review your campaign daily for the first two weeks. Look for: 

- CPC (cost per click): Aim for $2–$3 for local services
- CTR (click-through rate): 2%+ is good
- Conversion rate: 5–10% is typical for small businesses

If your CPC is too high (> $4), narrow your location radius from 5 miles to 3. If CTR is low (&lt;1%), rewrite your headline from “Best Hair Salon in Town” to “$30 Off First Cut for New Clients.”

## Step 5: Track and Adjust

After 30 days, check which ads drove the most conversions. A yoga studio in Boston found “Weekday Morning Classes” outperformed “Weekend Workshops” by 30%. Shift budget to top-performing times.

Use Yelp’s “Conversion Tracking” to see if ads lead to appointments or purchases. If you sell coffee, install their pixel on your website to track online orders.

<Callout type="coffee">I recommend running Yelp Ads alongside Google Local Services Ads for maximum reach—but only if your monthly budget allows $100–$150 total.</Callout>

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How much does a Yelp ad cost?**  
Yelp Ads use cost-per-click pricing. Most local businesses pay $2–$4 per click, with $25–$50/month budgets testing well.

**Can I target people outside my city?**  
No—Yelp limits targeting to a 15-mile radius. If you run a pet grooming franchise, create separate campaigns for each location.

**How long does it take for Yelp ads to work?**  
Give campaigns 2–3 weeks to optimize. A coffee shop in Denver saw results after 14 days, but it took 28 days for a barbershop in Atlanta.

**Why is my ad not getting clicks?**  
Check your ad relevance. If your title says “Cheap Dog Grooming” but your rates are average, users will skip your ad. Match your headline to your pricing.

**Can I pause and restart campaigns?**  
Yes. Pausing during holidays or low-demand periods (like summer for ski schools) saves money. Yelp retains your ad history when you restart.
