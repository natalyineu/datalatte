## Summer Marketing Strategies for Local Businesses

To help you make the most of the summer season, we've put together 20 actionable marketing campaigns tailored to your local business. Whether you own a coffee shop, salon, pet grooming business, or fitness studio, these ideas will help you attract more customers and drive sales.

## Campaigns for Increased Foot Traffic

Summer is the perfect time to draw in more foot traffic to your business. Here are a few ideas to get you started:

* Host a summer-themed event, such as a outdoor concert or movie night (free or low-cost)
* Offer limited-time discounts or promotions to customers who visit during off-peak hours
* Partner with local businesses to create a joint promotion or loyalty program

## Leveraging Social Media for Summer Marketing

Social media is a powerful tool for reaching new and existing customers during the summer months. Consider the following strategies:

* Share summer-themed content, such as photos of your products or services in action
* Utilize Instagram Stories and Facebook Live to engage with your audience
* Run targeted social media ads to reach customers who are looking for summer deals

## Email Marketing for Summer Promotions

Email marketing is an effective way to keep your customers informed about your summer promotions and events. Here are a few tips:

* Create a summer-themed email campaign to promote your products or services
* Offer exclusive discounts or promotions to your email subscribers
* Use email marketing automation to personalize your messages and improve engagement

## Optimizing Your Online Presence

Having a strong online presence is crucial for attracting new customers during the summer months. Consider the following:

* Optimize your Google Business Profile to reflect your summer hours and promotions
* Use local SEO techniques to improve your search engine rankings
* Create a summer-themed landing page to promote your products or services

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running a Generic “Summer Sale” That Attracts the Wrong People

**The Story:** A coffee shop in Portland, Oregon called *Brew & Bloom* decided to run a “20% off all iced drinks” promotion every afternoon in June. The owner spent $800 on Instagram ads targeting anyone within 5 miles. Here’s what happened: foot traffic went up 35% in the first week. But the new customers were price-sensitive one-timers—they ordered the cheapest drink, took up a table for 45 minutes on their laptop, and never came back. Regulars were annoyed by the wait times. Revenue per customer dropped by 22%, and the owner lost $1,200 in net profit that month.

**What went wrong:** The promotion rewarded the wrong behavior. It attracted bargain hunters, not people who would stick around for a loyalty habit.

**The fix:** Switch to a “Buy 5 Iced Lattes, Get the 6th Free” punch card. No discount on single visits. That targets frequency and repeat visits, not one-time deals. The owner tested this in July—spent $0 on ads, just put a printed card on the counter. Repeat visit rate from new customers went from 12% to 41% in 30 days.

**The outcome:** $3,800 in incremental revenue from punch card redemptions over 8 weeks. The owner now runs a punch card every summer, no paid ads needed.

### Mistake #2: Posting on Social Media Without Any Connection to Actual Sales

**The Story:** A hair salon in Nashville, Tennessee called *Blunt & Curl* hired a social media intern for $600/month. The intern posted 4 times a week—hair transformation reels, meme reposts, “tag a friend” contests. Engagement looked great: 200+ likes per post, comments up 60%. But after 3 months, the owner checked her Booksy booking data. Not a single new client had come from Instagram. The $1,800 she spent (3 months of intern pay) produced exactly zero booked appointments.

**What went wrong:** Vanity metrics. Likes don’t equal bookings. The content was entertaining but didn’t include a clear, trackable call to action.

**The fix:** Pivot to a “Book Directly from Instagram” approach. Use Instagram’s “Link in Bio” tool linked to the Booksy booking page. Run one story per week offering a “$10 off your first cut if you mention this story.” Tag the specific stylist who did the work, not the salon. Track each booking source with a custom promo code.

**The outcome:** In month 4, the salon booked 17 new clients directly from Instagram—$2,550 in revenue. The intern now spends 70% less time on content and 30% on responding to DMs with booking links. The owner fired the “post 4x per week” rule and replaced it with “post once, link everywhere.”

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Local Search Optimization Because “Summer Traffic Will Just Show Up”

**The Story:** A pet groomer in Austin, Texas called *Suds & Snips* relied on word-of-mouth for years. Summer rolled around, and the owner noticed her Google Business Profile calls dropped 40% compared to the previous July. Competitors were ranking above her for “dog groomer Austin” and “Austin pet grooming.” She checked her profile—it still had “Closed” listed for Saturday hours (she had changed them 6 months ago), no summer photos, and zero reviews posted in the last 30 days. She lost an estimated $2,100 in July because potential customers called the competitor listed first.

**What went wrong:** She assumed Google would update itself. It doesn’t. Unmanaged profiles lose ranking, and lost ranking means lost revenue.

**The fix:** Spend 15 minutes every Monday updating the Google Business Profile. Add summer hours, post a photo of a freshly groomed dog, reply to every review (even the negative ones—public replies boost trust). Use keywords like “Austin summer pet grooming” in posts.

**The outcome:** In August, the profile’s “calls” metric went from 23 to 52. The groomer booked 8 new clients from search alone—$1,600 in revenue. She now sets a recurring 15-minute calendar reminder every Monday.

## Why Your Summer Email List Is Your Most Profitable Asset

Most small business owners treat email marketing like a chore. They send one blast a month—usually a discount code—and wonder why nobody opens it. I’ve seen this pattern at a dozen local businesses. The uncomfortable truth is that email, when done right, beats social media by a factor of 5-7x on conversion rate. But only if you treat it like a conversation, not a coupon dump.

Here’s a specific example. A fitness studio in Chicago called *Sweat & Shift* tested two approaches last summer. In June, they posted a “Summer Kickoff” offer on Instagram—$50 off a 10-class pack. They spent $300 on ad promotion. They got 12 sign-ups, which cost them $25 per lead.

In July, they did an email-only campaign. They had 980 subscribers from their check-in system (collected at booking via Square). They sent a single email: “Your July class schedule is live—reply to this email if you want a free guest pass for a friend.” The email cost $0 to send via Mailchimp (their free tier at the time). They got 34 replies. Of those, 22 people actually showed up with a guest. The studio sold 11 class packs to the guests—$1,870 in revenue.

The difference wasn’t the offer. It was the channel. The email list was full of people who had already paid them money. Social media was full of people who scrolled past a sponsored post.

If you don’t have an email list yet, start building one tomorrow. Put a sign-up sheet at your counter. Add a checkbox on your online booking form. Offer a “5% off your next visit” in exchange for an email address. Use a tool like Mailchimp or ConvertKit (free for small lists). Then send one email per week—not a sales pitch, just a useful update. “Here’s what’s happening this week” works better than “Buy now while supplies last.”

The summer season is 12-14 weeks. That means you have time to send 12-14 emails. Even a 15% open rate on a list of 500 people means 75 people are reading your updates. If 5 of them book or visit, you’ve recouped your entire annual email tool cost.

Stop overthinking. Collect the emails. Send the emails. Track the bookings. That’s the whole playbook.

## Using Google Local Services Ads Instead of Search Ads

If you’re a service-based local business—plumber, electrician, cleaner, dog walker—you are probably wasting money on Google Search ads. I’ve seen it firsthand. A house cleaning business in Denver was spending $1,200/month on standard Google Ads. They were getting clicks, but the leads were low-quality: people comparing prices, asking for quotes, never booking. Their cost-per-lead was $38, and only 1 in 6 actually paid for a cleaning.

Then they switched to Google Local Services Ads (LSAs). These are the “Google Guaranteed” ads that show up at the very top of search results, before the regular ads. You pay per lead, not per click. If the lead doesn’t book, you don’t pay.

The business spent $500/month on LSAs. Their cost-per-lead dropped to $15. Their booking rate went from 16% to 62% because the people clicking LSAs are ready to hire—they’re not browsing.

Here’s what you need to know: LSAs require a background check and license verification (depending on your industry). That’s actually a feature, not a barrier. It means your competitors doing shady work can’t easily get into the program. You get the “Google Guaranteed” badge, which builds instant trust.

The setup takes about 30 minutes. Go to ads.google.com, select “Local Services Ads,” and follow the prompts. You’ll need your business license and insurance info. Budget minimum is usually $100-$200/month. You can adjust it up or down weekly.

For a pet groomer or hair salon, LSAs work less well—those categories aren’t always available in every city. But for service businesses, this is the single highest-ROI ad channel I’ve recommended to clients. Test it with $300 for one month. If you get fewer than 5 paying customers, drop it. But the Denver cleaning business more than doubled their revenue in 90 days from LSA leads alone. That’s not a theory. That’s what actually happened.

## Why Partnership Marketing Works Better Than Discounts

I’m skeptical of “strategic partnerships” because most small businesses treat them like a press release. They partner with a neighboring business, post a photo together on Instagram, and call it a day. Nobody books anything.

But I’ve seen one model work consistently: revenue-sharing cross-promotion. No discounts. No coupons. Just a simple split.

Here’s how it looks in practice. A yoga studio in Denver called *Mountain Flow* partnered with a nearby juice bar called *Root & Stem*. The deal: Every new student who mentions Root & Stem when they book their first class gets a free juice after. The juice bar pays the yoga studio $5 for every new student referred—covers the cost of the juice plus profit—and the yoga studio sends an email to their list once a month promoting the juice bar.

In year one, Mountain Flow sent 37 new students to Root & Stem. That’s $185 in referral revenue for the studio, plus 37 new students who paid for classes. The juice bar gained 37 new customers who spent an average of $9 per visit, many of them returning weekly. Total incremental revenue for both businesses: approximately $8,400.

The reason this works: it’s trackable. Both businesses know exactly where each customer came from. There’s no guesswork, no “brand awareness” fluff.

You can apply this to any pair of complementary local businesses. A hair salon partners with a nail studio. A pet groomer partners with a dog daycare. A coffee shop partners with a bookstore. Use a tool like Square’s free referral tracking or just keep a notebook at the register. The key is making it easy to mention the partner’s name and having a simple handshake on the revenue split.

Don’t complicate this. Pick one partner. Agree on a $5 or $10 referral fee. Run it for 60 days. If it generates less than $200 in extra revenue for both sides, drop it. But I’ve never seen a well-executed partnership fail to beat that number.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My business is already slow in summer. Why should I spend money on marketing when I’m barely breaking even?**

That’s exactly the time to market. Summer slowdowns are often predictable—you know June and July are quiet. A targeted campaign in early June can pull forward demand from August and September. A coffee shop in San Diego used a “Beat the Summer Slump” campaign: $10 off any prepaid $100 gift card. Sold 84 cards in 10 days. That’s $8,400 in cash upfront, plus every card brought someone back who otherwise wouldn’t have visited. You lose margin on the discount, but you gain cash flow and guarantee visits.

**Q: Should I run Google Ads or Facebook Ads for my local business?**

For most local businesses, start with Google. People search for “coffee shop near me” or “hair salon [your city]” when they’re ready to buy. Facebook works for building brand awareness, but the click-to-booking conversion rate is lower. A bakery in Portland tested both: $500 on Google Ads (10% conversion rate to in-store visit) vs. $500 on Facebook (1.2% conversion rate). Google won. Start there, test for 30 days, then decide.

**Q: Is it worth paying for Yelp ads?**

Rarely for the average small business. Yelp’s ad model is expensive—sometimes $5-$10 per click—and the lead quality is mixed. A pet groomer in Austin spent $400/month on Yelp ads and got 3 bookings over 6 weeks. That’s $133 per booking. Meanwhile, her Google Business Profile generated 12 bookings in the same period for free. Spend your money on optimizing your Google profile and running LSAs first. Yelp ads are a distant third choice.

**Q: How much should I budget for a summer campaign?**

A safe starting point is 5-10% of your expected summer revenue. If you expect $20,000 in revenue from June to August, set aside $1,000-$2,000 for marketing. That should cover one LSA campaign, one email series, and a few social posts. Track every dollar. If a channel costs more than $50 per new customer, cut it. A salon in Nashville spent $1,200 over 8 weeks and generated $4,600 in new client revenue—a 3.8x return. That’s a reasonable benchmark.

**Q: I don't have time to manage all these channels. What's the minimum I should do?**

Three things. One: update your Google Business Profile with summer hours and photos (15 minutes, once). Two: send one email per week to your list (30 minutes, weekly). Three: put a referral card on your counter that says “Bring a friend—you both get 10% off” (5 minutes, once). That’s it. No social media required. I’ve seen a dog groomer in Chicago do exactly this and increase revenue by 22% in July. The minimum works if you do it consistently.

**Q: What if I try a campaign and it fails?**

Then you learned something specific and measurable. That’s better than guessing. I ran a “Summer Sunset Hour” campaign for a coffee shop in Denver—20% off all drinks after 5pm—and it barely moved the needle. Revenue increased by 3% but staffing costs went up 8%. So we killed it after 2 weeks. Net loss: $180. But we learned that happy hour discounting doesn’t work for that business. The next test (a punch card for iced drinks) produced $2,300 in 30 days. Failure is data. The only real failure is running something for 3 months and not tracking whether it worked.
