## Understanding Your Audience
To create effective email marketing campaigns, you need to understand your audience. Who are your customers? What are their pain points? What motivates them to book an appointment with you? For example, let's say you're a pet groomer in New York City, and your target audience is busy professionals who own dogs. You could create an email campaign that highlights the convenience of your services, such as online booking and flexible scheduling.

<Callout type="tip">Use segmentation to tailor your emails to specific groups within your audience. For example, you could create separate lists for dog owners and cat owners, and send targeted promotions to each group.</Callout>

## Building Your Email List
Building a strong email list is crucial for any successful email marketing campaign. You can build your list by collecting email addresses from your customers, either in-person or through your website. Make sure to offer incentives, such as discounts or free consultations, to encourage people to sign up. You can also use social media to promote your email list and attract new subscribers.

## Creating Effective Email Campaigns
Once you have a solid email list, it's time to start creating effective email campaigns. This means crafting compelling subject lines, writing engaging content, and using eye-catching visuals. For example, let's say you're running a promotion for a limited-time discount on dog grooming services. Your email campaign could include a subject line like "Limited Time Offer: 20% Off Dog Grooming," and an image of a happy dog with a clean coat.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating Every Pet Owner the Same

I watched a groomer in Austin, Texas, named Maria burn through $1,200 in Mailchimp credits over four months. She had a list of 900 people, and she sent the exact same email to every one of them. Golden retrievers, cats, guinea pigs, that one client with a bearded dragon — all got the same "Pamper Your Pup" promotion.

Three months in, her open rate had dropped to 11%. Her unsubscribe rate was climbing. She told me she was "doing email marketing" and it "wasn't working."

The fix was uncomfortable: she had to admit she didn't actually know what her cat owners wanted. We pulled her booking data. Turns out her cat grooming clients rebooked every 6-8 weeks, not 4-6 like dog owners. They didn't care about nail trim discounts. They cared about "stress-free handling" because their cats hated car rides.

She split her list into three segments: dog owners, cat owners, and "other small pets." Each got a different promotion. Dog owners got a "Summer Shedding Special" ($10 off deshedding treatments). Cat owners got a "Quiet Morning Appointment" invite (first slot of the day, no barking dogs in the waiting area).

The result: within six weeks, her open rate climbed back to 28%. She booked $3,800 in additional revenue over two months — the cat owners alone accounted for $1,100 she would've missed entirely.

The lesson: one-size emails are fast to write and useless to send. Your time is better spent writing three short emails than one long one.

### Mistake #2: Email Only When You Want Something

A groomer in Denver named James had a textbook problem. His email list had 1,400 people, but he only sent emails when he had a promotion running. "Valentine's Day Grooming Special." "Fourth of July Makeover." "Back-to-School Pet Bath."

His open rate hovered around 14%. He couldn't figure out why people unsubscribed after every campaign.

I asked him: "When's the last time you sent something that helped them, not sold to them?"

He didn't have an answer.

The fix: we added a 3-email nurture sequence for new subscribers. Week one: "How Often Should Your Dog Actually Get Groomed?" (no promotion) Week two: "Three Signs Your Cat Needs a Grooming Session Before It's Obvious" (informational) Week three: "We're Running a Shedding Special — Here's Why It Matters" (promotional, but frontloaded with value)

Then we added a quarterly "Pet Care Tips" email — seasonal advice specific to Denver's climate (dry winters, muddy springs). No offer. Just expertise.

His open rate went to 23% in two months. But the real win: his promotional emails started converting at 2x their previous rate. People didn't feel sold to. They felt informed, and then they bought.

The uncomfortable truth: if you only talk to your customers when you want their money, they'll learn to stop listening.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the Post-Grooming Follow-Up

A groomer in Portland, Oregon, had a solid business — $180,000 annual revenue, decent reputation, loyal clients. But she was leaving money on the table after every appointment.

Here's what she did: the client picks up the freshly groomed dog, pays, leaves. That was the end of the interaction until the next time the client called.

She had zero automated follow-up. No thank-you email. No "here's how to keep your dog's coat looking good for the next two weeks" tip sheet. No review request. No reminder to rebook in six weeks.

The fix cost $0 in extra software (she already had Mailchimp). We set up a simple 2-email sequence triggered the day after an appointment:

Email 1 (Day 1): "Thanks for trusting us with [pet name]." One photo of the groom, three quick coat-care tips, and a soft CTA to leave a Google review.

Email 2 (Day 14): "It's been two weeks — here's how [pet name]'s coat is probably looking now." A gentle nudge that rebooking is due in 4-6 weeks. A direct link to her booking page.

This 14-day gap mattered because most pet owners don't think about grooming until they're standing in a pile of fur. The email arrived before the mess became a problem.

Outcome: her rebooking rate went from 38% to 57% in three months. That's 19% more clients coming back consistently. At an average ticket of $65, and with 180 clients per month, that's roughly $2,200 in additional monthly revenue from people who would've slipped through the cracks.

Most groomers obsess over getting new clients. The clients you already have are sitting there, willing to pay you again, and you're not asking them.

### Mistake #4: Not Collecting Email Addresses at Point of Sale

This one kills me, because it's the easiest fix that nobody implements.

A groomer in Chicago ran a mobile grooming van. She had a website with a pop-up form, a Facebook page, and a Google Business profile. She was collecting about 15-20 emails per month.

I asked: "What happens when someone pays you?"

She pulled out a Square terminal. The customer taps their card, signs, and walks away. No email capture.

I showed her Square's built-in feature: you can toggle "Ask for email receipt" and set it to automatically add that email to a marketing list. It takes three seconds. Customers who pay by card are already holding their phones. They tap "Yes" without thinking.

She turned it on that same day. In the first month, she collected 87 emails from people who had already paid and already experienced her service. Compare that to 15 from her website pop-up.

She then sent those 87 people a welcome email with a "Thank You for Your Visit" offer — $10 off their next groom if they book within 4 weeks. Twelve people booked. That's $780 in revenue from one switch she made in ten minutes.

The takeaway: you don't need more traffic. You need to stop leaking the people who already hand you money.

## Using SMS Alongside Email (Without Annoying People)

Here's a scenario I've seen play out at three different clients: a pet owner books an appointment, forgets about it, doesn't show up, and the groomer loses $65-80 in potential revenue from that empty slot.

Email reminders help, but people check email less frequently than text messages. Open rates for SMS in pet services hover around 80-90%. Email sits at 20-30% for most groomers.

A groomer in Nashville, Tennessee, started using Booksy (a booking platform popular in pet services) integrated with Mailchimp. She set up a 3-touch sequence before every appointment:

- 48 hours before: SMS reminder ("Your grooming appointment with Sarah is confirmed for Saturday at 10 AM. Reply Y to confirm.")
- 24 hours before: Email with prep instructions ("Please make sure Fido has been walked before arrival")
- 2 hours before: SMS with address and parking instructions

She saw her no-show rate drop from 18% to 4% in six weeks. That reduction alone was worth roughly $500/month in recovered revenue for a shop doing 200 appointments per month.

But here's the part most guides skip: you can use SMS for more than reminders.

After each appointment, she sends one SMS: "How was your grooming experience? Reply 1-5 with any notes." That generates response rates of about 35% — far higher than email surveys. She uses those responses to catch problems early. One client replied "3, my dog seemed stressed" and she personally called them within 20 minutes to apologize and offer a free add-on service next visit. That client now spends $320/year with her instead of leaving a bad review.

The catch: SMS costs money. Mailchimp charges about $0.01-0.02 per text, depending on volume. That's roughly $20-40/month for a small groomer sending 2,000 texts. If it prevents even one no-show per month, it's paid for itself.

Tools I've actually seen work for pet groomers: Booksy, Vagaro, Square Appointments (has built-in SMS reminders), Mailchimp SMS add-on, and for larger lists, Klaviyo.

## Automating Your "We Miss You" Campaign

Every groomer has a segment of clients who used to come every 6-8 weeks, then stopped. Maybe they moved. Maybe they found someone cheaper. Maybe their dog died. You don't know unless you ask.

Most small business owners do nothing about these lapsed clients. They assume the relationship is dead.

A groomer in Denver (different from James above) had a list of 340 clients who hadn't booked in 90 days or more. She was sitting on a goldmine of people who already knew, trusted, and liked her work. But she was ignoring them.

We built a simple automated campaign in Mailchimp targeting anyone who hadn't booked in 90+ days.

The sequence:
- Email 1 (Day 90): "It's been a while — here's what's changed at our shop" (new pricing, new staff member, updated safety protocols)
- Email 2 (Day 105): "We genuinely miss [pet name]. Here's a $15 off coupon — no strings attached."
- Email 3 (Day 120): "Last chance — this coupon expires in 7 days."

If they didn't respond after email 3, we moved them to a "seasonal" list and only emailed them twice a year with major promotions.

Out of 340 people, 47 booked appointments within 45 days. At an average ticket of $72, that's $3,384 in revenue from people she'd written off.

She spent maybe 2 hours setting up the automation. It's been running in the background ever since.

The cost of not running this sequence: she was losing roughly $15,000-20,000/year in revenue from lapsed clients.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I only have 50 email addresses. Is it worth sending emails to that small of a list?**
Yes. A list of 50 engaged subscribers will outperform a list of 5,000 who never open. I've seen a groomer with 38 emails generate $600 in additional revenue from a single "last-minute appointment available" email. Start now. Grow later.

**Q: How often should I actually email my clients?**
Every groomer is different, but here's a framework that works: one post-groom follow-up (within 24 hours), one monthly tip email (educational, not salesy), and one promotional email per month. Three emails per month total. Anything more risks annoying people. Anything less risks being forgotten.

**Q: What if people unsubscribe?**
People unsubscribe for reasons that have nothing to do with you: they moved, their pet died, they switched to a different type of service. A 1-2% monthly unsubscribe rate is normal for a healthy list. If you're losing 5%+ per month, your emails are too frequent or too salesy.

**Q: How do I get more email addresses without spamming people?**
Train your front-desk staff. "Would you like to receive a text or email reminder for your next appointment?" is a natural question that leads to consent. Also put a sign-up form on your booking confirmation page. And run a simple promotion: "Join our email list and get $10 off your next groom."

**Q: What's the real ROI for a small groomer spending 2 hours per week on email?**
I've tracked this across six pet grooming clients. Average result: $20-40 returned for every $1 spent on email software and time combined. That's better than any paid ad platform I've tested for this vertical. Google Ads typically returns $2-3 per $1 spent for small groomers. Email is 10x more efficient.

**Q: Should I use Mailchimp or is something else better?**
For a groomer with under 2,000 subscribers, Mailchimp's free plan works fine. If you're doing SMS marketing too, Booksy or Square Appointments might handle both booking and email for less. For larger lists (5,000+), Klaviyo is worth considering. But for most small groomers, Mailchimp is good enough. Don't overthink the tool. Focus on the content.
