## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched coffee shops and small businesses burn money on email marketing in ways that make me genuinely angry. Not because they’re stupid — they’re not. But because almost every mistake comes from bad advice someone gave them at a conference or in a Facebook group. Let me save you the tuition.

### Mistake 1: Buying a “Targeted Email List” Instead of Building One

**The story:** A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas — let’s call it “Brew & Tumble” — spent $500 on a “local coffee drinkers” email list from a guy on Upwork. 4,000 emails. Sounded like a deal. They imported the list into Mailchimp and sent their first campaign expecting a flood of new customers.

Open rate: 3.2%. Click rate: 0.1%. Bounce rate: 22%. Spam complaints: 47.

That last number is the painful one. Mailchimp flagged their account. They got temporarily suspended. It took three days and multiple phone calls to get it reinstated. Meanwhile, their actual subscribers — the people who actually wanted their emails — didn’t receive updates for a week.

**Why it didn’t work:** Mailchimp (and most modern email platforms) monitor sender reputation. When you blast a purchased list, you’re sending to people who never opted in. Spam complaints spike. Your deliverability tanks for everyone. The “500 cheap leads” ended up costing them around $1,200 in lost sales from the week their emails went dark.

**What I’d do instead:** Put a sign-up form on your Square tablet or Toast POS. Every time someone pays with a card, the checkout screen asks: “Want emails for a free drink on your 5th visit?” That’s a warm lead. They just bought coffee from you. They like your product. A shop in Denver I worked with did this and grew a list of 800 real subscribers in 60 days. Cost: zero dollars. Open rate on their first campaign: 34%.

### Mistake 2: Sending the Same Email to Your Whole List

**The story:** A pet groomer in Portland, Oregon — “Paws & Claws” — had 1,200 subscribers. Every Friday, she sent a generic email: “Here’s this week’s grooming special. Book now.” She was proud of the consistency. But 18 months in, her open rate had dropped from 28% to 11%. She assumed email marketing was dying.

**The real problem:** 60% of her list had dogs under 20 pounds. 25% had large breeds. The remaining 15% were cat owners. Her “weekly grooming special” was a nail trim and bath combo — great for small dogs, irrelevant for cat owners, and mildly annoying for people with large breeds who needed a different service entirely.

**The fix:** We segmented the list into three groups based on past service history from her Booksy scheduling data. (Booksy exports customer data. Use it.) Each group got a different email: small-dog owners got the bath special, large-breed owners got a discount on de-shedding treatments, cat owners got a friendly “we also do cats” note with a 10% off cat grooming coupon.

**The outcome:** Open rate went from 11% to 24% in three weeks. More importantly, revenue per email sent increased from $0.42 to $1.87. That’s a 345% improvement. She was sending 4 emails per month to 1,200 people, so monthly email revenue went from roughly $2,000 to $9,000. Segmentation didn’t cost her a dime — just 45 minutes of sorting data.

### Mistake 3: Sending Too Often With Nothing to Say

**The story:** A hair salon in Nashville — “Blow & Go” — ran a promotion every Tuesday for two years. “10% off any service if you book by Thursday.” It worked for the first six months. Then it stopped working. Then it started annoying people. Unsubscribe rate hit 4.7% in a single month. That’s catastrophic.

**Why it failed:** Constant discounts train customers to never pay full price. More importantly, the owner never sent anything *useful*. No haircare tips. No behind-the-scenes photos of the team. No introduction to a new stylist. Just a weekly coupon. People felt like a transaction, not a community.

**The fix:** We cut the promotional emails to twice a month. The other two emails became content: “How to style your bangs without a flat iron” (with a 30-second video shot on an iPhone), “Meet Sarah — our new color specialist,” and “What to ask for when you want a balayage (not a highlight).” The promotional emails when they did come? More effective, because subscribers weren’t numb to them.

**The outcome:** Unsubscribe rate dropped to 0.3% within a month. Promotional email click-through rate doubled. Average ticket size on promotion days went from $65 to $82 — people who felt connected to the salon were willing to add on a service rather than just hunt for the cheapest option. Annual revenue impact: roughly $3,800 from that shift alone.

### Mistake 4: Forgetting to Automate the Welcome Sequence

**The story:** A fitness studio in Chicago — “Sweat Theory” — spent $300/month on Facebook ads driving people to their email sign-up. They got about 200 new subscribers per month. And then… nothing. No automated welcome email. No “here’s your first class free” follow-up. No introduction to the instructors. Just radio silence until the weekly newsletter went out — sometimes seven days later.

They couldn’t figure out why only 12% of new subscribers ever booked a first class.

**The fix:** A four-email automated sequence set up in Mailchimp (took two hours):

- Email 1 (immediate): “Welcome to Sweat Theory — here’s your free class pass code: SWEAT10”
- Email 2 (24 hours later): “What to expect at your first class” (with photos of the studio)
- Email 3 (48 hours later): “Meet your instructors” (short bios, why they teach)
- Email 4 (72 hours later): “Still haven’t booked? Here’s a direct link to our class schedule — pick a time that works.”

**The outcome:** First-class booking rate went from 12% to 41% in 30 days. That’s an extra 58 new members per month from the same ad spend. At an average lifetime value of $400 per member, that’s $23,200 in additional projected revenue per month. The email sequence cost nothing to build beyond the two-hour setup.

## How to Connect AI Email Tools With Your POS and Booking Systems

Most small business owners I meet have this reaction when I mention AI email tools: “I barely have time to brew the cold brew. I’m not learning software.”

Fair. But here’s the thing — you already have the data. You just aren’t using it.

If you use Square for payments or Toast for your coffee shop POS, you are sitting on a goldmine of purchase history data. The same goes for Booksy or Vagaro if you run a salon or grooming business. Every transaction tells you exactly who bought what, when, and how often.

Here’s how you actually connect this to AI-assisted email without a computer science degree:

**Step 1: Export your customer list.** Square and Toast both let you download a CSV of all customers with their email addresses and purchase history. It takes about five minutes.

**Step 2: Upload to Mailchimp or Klaviyo.** Mailchimp has a free tier up to 500 contacts. Klaviyo starts free and scales from there. Both will ask you to map fields — email, name, total spent, last purchase date. Do it.

**Step 3: Let the AI segment for you.** Klaviyo’s AI will automatically group customers by purchase frequency and recency. It’ll create segments like “bought in the last 30 days” and “hasn’t visited in 90 days.” Mailchimp has a similar feature in its “Customer Journey Builder.” You don’t need to decide the groups — the algorithm finds the patterns.

I worked with a cafe in Denver that used this exact setup. The AI noticed that customers who bought a pastry with their coffee on the first two visits were 3x more likely to become weekly regulars. So Klaviyo’s AI created a segment for “first-time pastry buyers” and triggered a coupon for a free croissant on their third visit within seven days. That automation alone increased repeat visit rate by 22%.

**Step 4: Set conditional triggers.** If a customer hasn’t purchased in 60 days, the AI sends a “We miss you” email with a personalized offer based on their last order. If a customer has bought five times in three months, the AI flags them as a VIP and sends a loyalty perk. You set the rules once. The AI executes them on autopilot.

**Step 5: Monitor the one number that matters.** Ignore open rate. Ignore click rate. The only number I care about is revenue per email sent. If your AI-assisted campaigns aren’t generating more than $1 per email sent (for a small list), something is wrong with the offer or the segmentation. Fix that before you worry about anything else.

## Using AI to Write Subject Lines That Don’t Sound Like AI

Here’s the uncomfortable truth: most AI-generated subject lines are terrible. They’re generic, they’re too clever, and they sound like a robot who just discovered puns.

The tools — ChatGPT, Copy.ai, Jasper — all produce variants of the same bland output. And then everyone in your industry uses them, so every coffee shop in your city sends emails with the same “Brew-tiful Savings Inside” subject line.

**What actually works:** Train the AI on your specific customers.

When I worked with a salon in Austin, we fed ChatGPT a transcript of three actual conversations the owner had with clients: their complaints about split ends, their excitement after a good cut, their hesitation about trying a new color. Then we asked the AI: “Write subject lines in the voice of the clients, not the business.”

The winning subject line for their rebooking campaign: “I look 10 years younger and I’m not even mad about it.” That was a direct quote from a client’s Google review. They stole it. Open rate: 38%. Normally their average was 17%.

**The method:**

1. Collect five actual customer quotes from reviews, emails, or in-person conversations.
2. Paste them into ChatGPT or Claude and say: “These are quotes from my customers. Write 10 subject lines that match this tone of voice. No puns. No emojis. No ‘don’t miss out.’” 
3. Edit whatever comes back. Remove the adjectives. Shorten it by 50%. The best subject lines are five words or fewer.
4. A/B test two variants per campaign. Mailchimp does this automatically. Send one version to 20% of your list, the winner goes to the remaining 80%.

**What to avoid:** “We have exciting news” — nobody cares. “Last chance to save” — overused to death. Anything with “unlock” or “exclusive access” — makes small businesses sound like they’re running a cryptocurrency scam.

**Real example from a coffee shop in Nashville:** They had a seasonal pumpkin latte that sold out every year. Their normal subject line was “Pumpkin Spice is Back.” Open rate: 14%. We rewrote it using a customer quote: “It tastes like a sweater.” Open rate: 29%. Sales that week: $1,800 above their average October week. The AI wrote 35 variations. A human picked the right one.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I have 200 email subscribers. Is AI worth it for a list this small?**

Yes, for one specific reason: automation. You’re manually sending emails right now, which means you send less often than you should. Set up a welcome sequence once using an AI tool — it’ll run for years. For a list that size, start with Mailchimp’s free plan. The time savings alone pays for itself by week two.

**Q: Won’t AI make my emails sound robotic?**

Only if you let it. The trick is editing. Take the AI’s first draft, remove 40% of the words, and add one specific detail that only you would know — like “the espresso machine that broke last Tuesday” or “that one regular who always orders a cortado.” The AI gives you structure. You give it soul.

**Q: What email platform actually works for a single-location coffee shop?**

Mailchimp is the most straightforward. It’s free up to 500 contacts, the automations are simple to set up, and the AI subject line testing is built in. Klaviyo is better if you want deep purchase history segmentation, but it charges once you hit 50+ contacts. For a small coffee shop, start with Mailchimp. Migrate to Klaviyo when you hit 1,000+ subscribers and need better reporting.

**Q: How do I get people to actually give me their email without offering a discount?**

Instead of a discount, offer something they can’t get in the store. A weekly email with three coffee brewing tips. A recipe for the syrup you use in your lavender latte. A heads-up when your seasonal roast drops. Discounts train people to wait for a sale. Valuable information trains them to open.

**Q: How often should I send emails if I’m using AI to help write them?**

Once a week minimum if you want to stay top of mind. Twice a week if you have something to say. More than that and you’re testing your subscribers’ patience. AI makes it faster to write, but it doesn’t change the fact that people unsubscribe when you email them every day with nothing useful.

**Q: What’s the biggest mistake you see business owners make with AI email tools?**

Thinking the AI will do everything. It won’t. The AI doesn’t know your customers. You do. I see too many people generate a campaign in five minutes, hit send, and wonder why nobody opens it. The extra 20 minutes you spend reviewing, editing, and adding a personal detail is what makes the email worth reading.
