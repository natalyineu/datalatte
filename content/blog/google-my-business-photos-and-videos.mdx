## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Posting 3 Photos and Calling It Done

A coffee shop owner in Portland, Oregon, spent $4,200 on a website redesign but uploaded exactly three photos to Google My Business: a distant shot of the storefront, a blurry latte art, and a menu photo taken with a phone from 2017. The listing sat that way for eight months.

The problem? Google's algorithm treats listings with fewer than 10 photos as incomplete. More importantly, customers scrolling past that listing see the same three images every time. There's no visual reason to choose this shop over the one with 40 photos showing the interior, the outdoor seating, the pastry case, and the barista actually smiling.

I asked the owner what he expected to happen. He said he thought "photos were just decoration." No. Photos are the first sales conversation you're not in the room for.

**The fix:** We uploaded 28 photos in one afternoon. Exterior from three angles, interior from every seat, close-ups of the three best-selling drinks, a shot of the pastry display, the chalkboard menu, the parking situation, and a photo of the barista team (consent obtained, obviously).

**The outcome:** Within six weeks, the listing went from appearing in the local pack for three search terms to showing for 11. Calls from Google Maps increased from roughly 4 per week to 19. The owner estimated that brought in about $1,800 in additional monthly revenue. He bought a $300 used camera lens for future updates. Still using that same lens three years later.

### Mistake 2: Stock Photos on a Local Business Listing

A hair salon in Nashville paid a freelance designer $600 for a website package that included "GMB images." The designer uploaded five stock photos: a generic pair of scissors, a generic hair dryer, a generic woman with perfect highlights, a generic salon chair, and a generic shampoo station. None of the photos showed the actual salon, the actual stylists, or any actual client work.

The owner didn't notice for four months. She just saw the listing existed and assumed that was sufficient. Meanwhile, a competitor who opened six months later was booking appointments through Google within three weeks. The competitor had uploaded real photos.

I've seen this mistake at three different clients across different industries. Google's system is not stupid. Stock photos trigger lower engagement signals — fewer clicks, less time on the listing, more back-outs to the search results. The algorithm notices.

**The fix:** We removed every stock photo. The owner spent one morning taking photos of the actual salon space: the waiting area with the plant she'd owned for eight years, the shampoo sinks, a before-and-after set of a client's color treatment (with permission), the stylists at work. Total investment: zero dollars. Total time: about 90 minutes.

**The outcome:** The salon's listing started showing in the top-three local pack for "hair color Nashville" within five weeks. According to the owner's booking software (she used Booksy at the time), appointments sourced from Google increased from roughly 8 per month to 23. At an average ticket of $85, that's an extra $1,275 monthly, with zero media spend. She canceled the designer's retainer.

### Mistake 3: Uploading Photos That Don't Match the Experience

A barbecue restaurant in Austin uploaded photos of the dining room with the lights dimmed, a filtered shot of brisket that made it look purple, and a video of the owner chopping wood. None of these matched what a customer would actually experience when walking in the door.

Here's what happened: I analyzed their listing data for a three-month period. The listing had decent views — about 1,200 per month — but the click-to-call rate was 2.1 percent, well below the 4 percent benchmark for restaurants in that area. People were looking but not acting.

The connection? When customers saw the listing, they couldn't tell if this was a sit-down restaurant, a food truck, or a catering company. The photos were confusing. People don't call or navigate to a place when they can't visualize what they're walking into.

**The fix:** We created a structured photo set: the exterior (so people know they're at the right place), the ordering counter (so people know how to order), two shots of the dining area (so people know seating is available), three plated food photos (well-lit, no filters), one shot of the smoker (because barbecue people care about this), and a short video of a brisket being sliced (30 seconds, vertical, no music).

**The outcome:** Click-to-call rate rose from 2.1 percent to 6.8 percent over eight weeks. Direction requests — the "get directions" button — increased by 140 percent. The owner told me foot traffic from Google searches added about $3,200 in sales over a two-month period. The video cost nothing. He shot it on his current phone.

### Mistake 4: Posting Videos That Are Too Long

A yoga studio in Denver uploaded a 12-minute full class recording as a Google Business Profile video. The owner thought "more content equals better results."

I checked the analytics on that listing. The video had been viewed 47 times. Average watch time: 14 seconds. People were dropping off before the warm-up ended.

Google's video limit is 30 seconds for GBP. Technically you can upload longer, but the platform truncates the preview. The full video sits there unwatched, taking up space that could be filled by something useful.

**The fix:** We replaced the long video with three 30-second clips: one showing the studio space during a quiet moment, one showing a 30-second flow sequence (fast forward of a full class), and one showing the owner talking directly to camera for 20 seconds about what new students should expect. Three videos, total production time: one afternoon.

**The outcome:** The short clips accumulated 210 views in the first month. Average watch time: 22 seconds. The owner told me that two new members specifically mentioned the Google video as the reason they showed up. Membership revenue from those two sign-ups: $1,560 over six months. She now films a new 30-second clip every two weeks.

## Why Photos Beat Any Google Ad You'll Run for Local Discovery

I manage Google Ads for some clients. It works. But there's a math problem that most agency people don't tell you about: the click-to-visit conversion rate on local search ads versus organic local pack results.

Here are real numbers from a pet grooming business in Chicago. The client was spending $900 per month on a local search campaign targeting "dog grooming Chicago" and surrounding neighborhoods. The ads were generating about 15 clicks per day at $2.40 per click. Of those clicks, roughly 2 percent resulted in a booking. That's about 9 bookings per month from ads, at a cost of $100 per booking.

Meanwhile, the organic local pack listing — which cost nothing beyond the time to manage it — was generating about 28 clicks per day, with a booking rate of 4.5 percent. That's about 38 organic bookings per month. The organic traffic required no ad spend.

I'm not saying you should cancel your ads. I'm saying you should look at the listing first. Every dollar you spend on ads to send people to a weak listing is partially wasted. A listing with 40 photos, a video, and regular updates will outperform a listing with 4 photos even if you're running a $2,000 monthly ad budget behind it.

I tested this directly at that Chicago pet groomer. We spent two weeks fixing the listing — adding 35 photos, a tour video, and updating the description — before making any changes to the ad campaign. The organic conversion rate improved from 3.1 percent to 4.5 percent with no additional ad spend. The ad campaign itself also improved because the quality score on Google Ads is connected to the landing experience. When we finally adjusted the ads, the cost per click dropped from $2.40 to $1.85 without changing bids.

The photos didn't just help the organic listing. They made the ads cheaper to run. That's a return you won't see in any standard ROI calculator.

## Video: The Underused Leverage Point Most Business Owners Skip

I'll be honest: most small business videos on Google are bad. Not bad as in poorly produced. Bad as in boring and useless. A static shot of the cash register. A 45-second video of someone walking through an empty store. A video shot in portrait that somehow uploaded in landscape and looks like you're watching through a keyhole.

Here's what actually works based on what I've tested across 30+ listings:

**Vertical video.** It takes up more screen space on mobile. Shoot it vertical. No exceptions.

**Under 30 seconds.** Google truncates at 30. If your video is longer, the preview only shows the first 30 seconds anyway. Why pay for footage nobody sees?

**Show the thing people come for.** A coffee shop in Austin shot a 22-second video of beans going into the grinder, the espresso shot pulling, and the drink being handed over. That's it. Three actions, 22 seconds. That video got 320 views in the first month. Compare that to the 47 views on the yoga studio's 12-minute class video from earlier.

**Show a person talking to camera.** A hair salon owner in Seattle recorded a 28-second video standing at the front desk saying: "We're open Tuesdays through Saturdays. Walk-ins welcome on weekdays. Saturdays fill up two weeks in advance — book online at the link in my profile." That video drove 15 appointment bookings in the first three weeks she published it. She told me clients said, "I liked that you just told me how it works."

**Don't overproduce.** I've watched agencies charge clients $2,500 for a "GMB video package" that includes a drone shot of the parking lot. Nobody needs a drone shot of your parking lot. Use your phone. Hold it steady. Good lighting. No background music that drowns out the space. That's it.

I've helped business owners create effective GBP videos in under 30 minutes with nothing but a smartphone, a window for light, and a willingness to talk. The barrier isn't equipment. It's deciding that it matters enough to do.

## How Often to Update (And What Happens When You Don't)

A pet supply store in Nashville had the same photos on their listing for 14 months. They were good photos — taken professionally, well-lit, showed the inventory. But they showed the old layout before a renovation that moved the checkout counter and added a new section for cat supplies.

I checked their Google Business Profile insights for month 14 versus month 1. Here's what I found: direction requests had dropped 60 percent. Total photo views had plateaued at roughly 450 per month. The listing was functionally stagnant.

Why does this matter? Google's freshness signal is real. Listings that show recent activity — new photos, updated hours, posts, reviews with responses — get priority in the local pack. It's not the only factor, but it's one that's entirely within your control and costs nothing except a few minutes.

I've seen this pattern at multiple clients: the listing gets set up, performs well for three to six months, then slowly declines as competitors update their listings and the GMB listing sits unchanged. It's not that Google penalizes you for not updating. It's that the algorithm finds the most current and active listing to serve first. If your competitor uploaded 10 new photos last week and you haven't touched yours since March, the algorithm has a clear tiebreaker.

**What I recommend clients actually do:**

Schedule 10 minutes twice per month. That's it. Ten minutes. Upload three to five new photos. Delete any photos that no longer represent the business. If you have a seasonal menu or seasonal service, add a photo showing it. If you rearranged furniture, show that. If your staff changed, update the team photo.

A fitness studio owner in Denver — different one, not the yoga studio — set a recurring calendar reminder every two weeks. She'd take a photo of the class mid-session (with permission), a photo of the front desk, and a photo of whatever she was selling most that month. Three photos, twice per month. She told me that after three months of this, her listing's weekly view count went from roughly 200 to 600. She attributed about 15 percent of her new member sign-ups that quarter to increased local search visibility. At $149 per month per membership, that's real revenue.

Compare that to the photo investment: approximately 20 minutes per month. That's a better hourly return than almost anything else you can do for your business.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need professional photos, or will my phone work?**

A good phone camera is sufficient. I've seen listings outperform competitors with professional photos because the phone photos showed the actual business. Professional photos that look like a stock library can actually hurt because customers don't trust them. The exceptions: if your business is visually detail-dependent (a jewelry store, a bakery with intricate cakes, a tattoo shop), spend $200-400 on a session with someone who knows how to light product photography. But for most local businesses — coffee shops, hair salons, pet groomers — your phone is fine if you use natural light and hold the camera steady.

**Q: How many photos do I actually need before it makes a difference?**

The minimum I recommend is 20. Listings with fewer than 10 perform measurably worse in click-through rates. The sweet spot based on what I've tracked across clients is 30-50 photos. More than 50 and you risk showing duplicates or low-quality images that dilute the set. You don't need hundreds. You need a thoughtful selection that answers every question a customer might have before they walk in the door.

**Q: What if I don't have anything new to take photos of every month?**

You do. The exterior during a different season. A busy moment during a Saturday rush (with permission from visible customers). A new menu item. A staff member's birthday. A before photo before you open and an after photo when the space is full. A photo of a delivered product in a customer's home (with permission). If you genuinely have zero changes in a given month, rotate out older photos and re-upload them. The act of uploading signals freshness.

**Q: My listing already has photos. Can't I just leave it alone?**

You can. But your competitors are updating theirs. I tracked a specific listing in Denver that stopped updating for 12 months. Their ranking for "coffee shop near me" dropped from position 2 in the local pack to position 5. A new shop that opened six months ago and uploaded photos biweekly took position 2. The listing that didn't update lost approximately 300 monthly views and an estimated 15-20 customers per month. Is leaving it alone worth that?

**Q: Should I delete old photos, or keep them?**

Delete photos that no longer represent the business. Old menus with old prices. Photos showing equipment you replaced. Photos of staff who no longer work there. Photos taken when the lighting was bad and you've since improved it. Keep photos that show historical authenticity if they're still accurate — a photo from your opening day is fine if the space still looks the same. But if the space has been renovated, remove the old photos. Customers arriving to find a space that looks nothing like the listing will leave before they order.

**Q: Do Google Business Profile videos really matter, or is that overrated?**

They matter more than most business owners realize. Less than 15 percent of local business listings have any video at all. If you have one, you're automatically more visible than 85 percent of competitors in your area. The algorithm gives video an engagement boost — listings with video get more clicks, more direction requests, and more phone calls. I've seen a single 30-second video outperform 20 photos in terms of conversion rate. Yes, they matter.

**Q: Can I use photos from my website or social media?**

Yes, but optimize them first. Download the original file, don't use a screenshot. Make sure there's no Instagram or Facebook branding on the image — Google doesn't like watermarks. Crop to a square or 4:3 ratio. Most website photos are landscape and will get cropped awkwardly by Google's thumbnail system. If the photo worked on Instagram but looked good because of a filter, remove the filter first.
