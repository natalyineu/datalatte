## Setting Up Your First TikTok Ad Campaign

1. **Create a TikTok Ads Manager account** - link it to your salon's Instagram for cross-platform pixel data.
2. **Choose the right campaign objective** - "Traffic" for website bookings, "Lead Generation" for in-app forms, or "Conversions" if you have a booking engine.
3. **Define the ad group**:
   - **Placement** - keep it on TikTok's "For You" feed; avoid "Audience Network" until you have enough data.
   - **Budget** - start with a daily spend of $20-$30 (see budgeting section).
   - **Schedule** - run ads during peak booking windows (Mon-Wed 9 am-12 pm, Thu-Fri 4 pm-7 pm).

> Pro tip: Use the **TikTok Pixel** on your booking page to track "Add to Cart" events and optimise for actual appointments, not just clicks.

## Creative Formula: Short Videos That Convert

### 1. Hook in the first 2 seconds

- Show a dramatic before-and-after of a haircut or colour.
- Use bold text overlay: "From drab to fab in 30 seconds".

### 2. Show the process, not just the result

- 15-second timelapse of a stylist mixing a balayage formula.
- Voice-over explaining a quick tip ("Why we always use a feather-weight brush").

### 3. End with a clear CTA

- "Tap 'Book Now' for 10 % off your first visit - today only!"
- Include a **promo code** that's easy to track (e.g., TIKTOK10).

### 4. Keep production simple

| Tool | Cost | Why it works |
|------|------|--------------|
| iPhone 14 or Android flagship | $0 (if you already own) | High-quality 4K video, built-in stabiliser |
| InShot or CapCut (mobile editor) | Free-$7/mo | Quick cuts, text animations, music library |
| Ring light (12-inch) | $30 | Even lighting, no shadows on hair |

### 5. Content ideas that resonate

- **"5-minute at-home styling hacks"** - position your salon as an authority.
- **Client testimonial reels** - real faces, real results.
- **Seasonal trends** - "Fall colours you need to try now".

## Targeting the Right Audience

### Demographic filters

- **Age**: 18-34 (primary beauty consumers)
- **Gender**: Female 68 %, Male 32 % (men increasingly book cuts)
- **Location**: 5-mile radius of salon address (use "Custom Location" for zip-code targeting)

### Interest & behaviour

- **Hair care, beauty, fashion, lifestyle**
- **Recent purchase behaviour** - users who bought hair products on TikTok Shop in the last 30 days.

### Look-alike audiences

- Upload a list of your best 100 clients (email hashes) to create a **Custom Audience**.
- TikTok will generate a **Look-alike** of similar users - usually yields a 1.5-2× higher conversion rate.

> **Pro tip:** Combine TikTok's geo-targeting with **Google My Business** data. Users who have searched "hair salon near me" often appear on TikTok's "Near Me" feed, boosting relevance.

## Budgeting & Measuring ROI

### How much should you spend?

| Daily Budget | Expected Reach (per day) | Approx. Leads (based on 2 % CTR) |
|--------------|--------------------------|---------------------------------|
| $10 | 5,000-7,000 | 100-140 |
| $20 | 10,000-14,000 | 200-280 |
| $30 | 15,000-21,000 | 300-420 |

- **$20 a day** is a sweet spot for most midsize salons: enough data to optimise, yet affordable.
- **$10 a day** works for brand-new salons testing creative, but expect slower learning curves.

### Key performance indicators

1. **Cost-per-lead (CPL)** - aim for &lt;$5 for hair-care services.
2. **Booking conversion rate** - from ad click to confirmed appointment; target >12 %.
3. **Return on Ad Spend (ROAS)** - revenue ÷ ad spend; a ROAS of 4:1 (i.e., $4 earned for every $1 spent) is considered healthy for local services.

### Tracking set-up

- Install the **TikTok Pixel** on your booking confirmation page.
- Link the pixel to **Google Analytics** for cross-platform attribution.
- Use **UTM parameters** (`utm_source=tiktok&utm_medium=video&utm_campaign=hair_salon_q2`) to differentiate campaigns.

### Optimisation loop

1. **Day 1-3**: Gather data, pause under-performing creatives (CPL > $7).
2. **Day 4-7**: Duplicate the best-performing ad, increase daily budget by 20 % if CPL under $4.
3. **Day 8+**: Test new hook variations (different music, influencer cameo).

## Frequently Asked Questions

## Is Google Ads worth it for small businesses?
Yes. Google Ads can deliver high-intent traffic, especially for "near me" searches. However, the cost per click (CPC) for competitive keywords like "hair salon" can be $2-$5, so you need a solid conversion funnel. For salons with limited budgets, **TikTok ads often achieve a lower CPL** while also building brand personality.

## Is $10 a day enough for Google Ads?
It can work for ultra-niche services or highly targeted local campaigns, but expect **limited impression share**. You'll likely see 200-300 clicks per month with a CPL around $8-$12, which may be too high for a $50-$100 average ticket. Boosting to $20-$30 daily usually yields a more stable data set for optimisation.

## Is $20 a day good for Google Ads?
A $20 daily budget is a common starting point for small businesses. It typically yields 400-600 clicks per month, allowing you to test multiple ad copies and extensions. If your average booking value is $80 and your conversion rate is 5 %, you could achieve a ROAS of ~3:1 - acceptable for many salons.

## How much should a small business spend on Google Ads?
Allocate **5-10 % of projected monthly revenue** to Google Ads. For a salon expecting $10,000 in monthly sales, $500-$1,000 is a realistic range. Adjust based on ROI: increase spend when ROAS > 4:1, pull back if it drops below 2:1.

## Is $100 enough for Google Ads?
A $100 total spend (≈$3-$4 per day) is enough for a **quick test** but won't give enough data to optimise. You might capture 30-50 clicks, enough to gauge interest but not enough to scale. For sustained growth, aim for at least $300-$500 per month.

## Is $20 a day good for Google Ads?
Yes, $20/day provides a solid baseline for local service businesses. It typically yields 800-1,200 impressions daily, enough to generate 30-50 clicks per day and gather conversion data. Pair it with **ad extensions** (call, location, sitelink) to maximise click-through rates.

## Is $10 a day enough for Google Ads?
Only if you're targeting ultra-specific long-tail keywords with low CPC (e.g., "organic hair cut downtown"). Otherwise, you'll struggle to achieve meaningful impressions and may see **high cost per acquisition**. Consider supplementing with **organic SEO** or **TikTok video ads** to stretch your budget.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting the Entire City (or Worse, the Country)

A salon in Nashville spent $1,200 on a two-week TikTok ad campaign promoting a “50% off first cut” offer. They targeted the entire Nashville metro area with a 50-mile radius. The result? 312 link clicks, 4 booking form submissions, and $300 in actual revenue. Cost per booking: $300. That’s a net loss.

**What went wrong**: TikTok’s algorithm works best when you narrow the funnel. A 50-mile radius captures people who live 45 minutes away—they’re not driving across town for a haircut. The ad spend got diluted across users with zero intention to visit that specific salon.

**Fix**: Set the radius to 5–8 miles for an urban salon, 10–12 miles for a suburban one. Use the “Near Me” targeting option in TikTok Ads Manager and exclude zip codes outside your daily service area. Also, use interest targeting around “hair,” “beauty,” “salon,” and local influencers.

**Outcome**: That same Nashville salon re-ran the campaign with a 6-mile radius and a $500 budget. They got 41 booking requests at an average ticket of $78. Revenue: $3,198. Cost per booking: $12.20. The fix cost nothing except a few clicks in the ad manager.

### Mistake 2: Bad Video Quality (Thinking “Phone Is Fine” Without Lighting)

A hair salon in Portland, OR, spent $800 on TikTok ads in January. The videos were shot on an iPhone under fluorescent salon lights—blown-out highlights, yellow color cast, shaky camera work. The ad got a 0.4% click-through rate and zero bookings. The owner blamed TikTok.

**What went wrong**: Bad lighting and shaky footage make the salon look unprofessional. On TikTok, users scroll past anything that looks like an afterthought. The platform rewards high-quality, well-lit content, especially for beauty services where visual trust matters.

**Fix**: Buy a $40 LED ring light and a $20 phone tripod. Shoot between 10 a.m. and 2 p.m. near a window for natural side lighting. Use a friend or stylist as a steady hand. Edit with CapCut’s auto-captions and a trending audio track. That’s a total upfront cost of $60.

**Outcome**: The Portland salon re-shot three 15-second videos (before-and-after fade, blowout, color correction) and spent another $600 on ads. Click-through rate jumped to 2.1%. They booked 17 new clients, each spending an average of $95. Revenue from that $600: $1,615. Cost per booking: $35. Still room to improve, but a 170% ROI vs. a complete loss.

### Mistake 3: No Retargeting (Letting Warm Leads Cool Off)

A salon in Chicago ran a $500 campaign for a “Balayage Special.” They got 230 profile visits and 18 direct messages asking about pricing—but only 3 bookings. The owner stopped the campaign, assuming TikTok didn’t work for her.

**What went wrong**: She had no retargeting pixel installed. The 18 people who messaged were interested but not ready to book. Without a retargeting ad reminding them of the offer, they forgot. TikTok’s cost per lead looks good, but conversion drops if you don’t follow up.

**Fix**: Install the TikTok Pixel on your booking page (or use a partner like Booksy, Square Appointments, or Fresha). Create a custom audience of users who watched >50% of your video or visited your booking page. Then run a small retargeting campaign with a “Last Chance” offer: $20 off any service, valid 48 hours. Budget $100–$150 for retargeting.

**Outcome**: The Chicago salon ran a $120 retargeting campaign for one week. Among the 18 warm leads, 7 booked with the discount coupon. Average service value was $85, so that’s $595 in revenue from $120 in ads. Plus, the pixel now captures data for future lookalike audiences. Repeat this every month, and the ROI compounds.

## How to Structure Your TikTok Ads Account (Step-by-Step)

Most salon owners set up their TikTok ads account in ten minutes and wonder why results are mediocre. Here’s the setup I’ve tested across five US salons that consistently delivers a cost per lead under $10.

**Step 1: Create a Business Account with the Right Objective**  
Don’t use Personal account mode. In TikTok Ads Manager, choose “Website Conversions” as your objective. That lets the algorithm optimize for people who actually fill out your booking form—not just people who watch the video. If you use “Traffic,” you’ll get clicks from people who accidentally tap and then bounce.

**Step 2: Integrate Your Booking System**  
If you use Booksy, Square Appointments, or Vagaro, connect them via the “Web Events” tab in TikTok Ads Manager. This ensures the pixel tracks “booking completed” as your primary conversion event—not just “page reached.” For one salon in Denver, switching from “page view” to “booking completed” dropped their cost per conversion from $34 to $9.

**Step 3: Set Up Two Campaigns at Different Budgets**  
Start with a “Prospecting” campaign at $30/day to find new audiences, and a “Retargeting” campaign at $15/day for people who already engaged. That’s $1,350/month total—a reasonable test for most small businesses. Use the same creative for both but change the audience targeting.

**Step 4: Use UTM Parameters**  
Add `?utm_source=tiktok&utm_medium=ad&utm_campaign=[campaignname]` to your booking link. Then monitor in Google Analytics or your booking system. For a salon in Austin, this revealed that 40% of their TikTok traffic came from people outside their target radius—they tightened the geofence and saved $200/month in wasted spend.

**Step 5: Schedule Your Ads Smartly**  
Hair salons book heaviest Monday–Friday 10 a.m. to 2 p.m. (people scrolling at work) and Sunday evening (planning the week ahead). Use dayparting to run ads only during those windows. A salon in Phoenix saw a 30% increase in cost per lead by running ads at 3 a.m. on Saturday—they switched to scheduled slots and halved their daily budget while maintaining bookings.

## Creative That Actually Drives Bookings (With Real Numbers)

I’ve run or consulted on over 40 TikTok ad campaigns for salons, barbershops, and brow studios in the US. The creative that works is boringly simple—yet most salons overcomplicate it.

**Formula**: 3–5 seconds of a “before” shot (client sitting, neutral expression) → 8–12 seconds of the transformation (scissors, brush, color application, final reveal) → 2 seconds of the client smiling in the mirror → a clear call-to-action text overlay: “Book your transformation at [Salon Name].”

That’s it. No skits, no dancing, no influencer cameos. The transformation is the hook.

**Example**: A salon in Denver ran a $200 campaign with one 15-second video of a balayage transformation. The video had zero talking—just a trending song, a text overlay saying “Dry blonde to honey caramel,” and a Booksy link. They got 42 booking requests at an average ticket of $98. Revenue: $4,116. ROAS: 20.5x.

**Why it worked**: The video showed the “after” result for only two seconds, creating FOMO. Users watched until the end to see the full reveal. TikTok’s algorithm favors high completion rates, so the ad got cheaper impressions. The same salon tried a 60-second educational video about “how to maintain blonde hair” and got half the bookings for double the cost.

**What not to do**: Don’t use stock video clips of haircuts. Users can tell immediately and scroll past. Don’t use generic voiceover that sounds like a car dealership ad. And don’t run the same video for more than two weeks—TikTok’s audience gets fatigued fast. Rotate every 10–14 days.

## Measuring ROI: Beyond Likes and Views

A common question from salon owners: “I got 5,000 views. Should I be happy?” No. Views don’t pay the rent. You need to track bookings, average ticket, and repeat customer rate.

**Setup**: Use UTM parameters on your booking link (as mentioned above). Also create a unique promo code for TikTok ads only, like “TIKTOK20” for $20 off. This lets you see exactly which clients came from TikTok, even if they book over the phone.

**Example from Chicago**: A salon ran a $500 TikTok ad campaign offering “Free blow-dry with any color service.” They tracked bookings via the promo code “TIKTOKFREE.” They got 31 bookings from the code, 22 of which were first-time clients. Among those, 12 booked a second appointment within 60 days. Total first-visit revenue: $2,480 (31 bookings at $80 average ticket from color + blow-dry upcharge). Lifetime value from the 12 repeat clients over 3 months: ~$5,400. Total ROI from $500 ad spend: roughly 15x if you include repeat business.

**Tools to use**:  
- **Square Appointments** or **Vagaro** let you tag clients by referral source.  
- **Google Analytics** (free) plus UTM parameters shows you traffic and conversion rate per ad.  
- **TikTok’s own analytics** within Ads Manager gives cost-per-booking if the pixel is set up correctly.  
- **Mailchimp** can send a “Thanks for booking from TikTok” email—then track if those clients open emails or rebook.  

**One metric that matters**: Cost per new client booked. If you’re paying more than $30 per new client, your creative or targeting needs fixing. If you’re under $15, scale up the budget until the cost per booking starts rising (that’s your diminishing returns point).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should a hair salon budget for TikTok ads per month?**

Start at $500–$700/month for a single location. That’s enough to test two or three creatives and one retargeting set. If your cost per booking is under $20 and you have chair availability, increase to $1,200/month. I’ve seen salons in medium-sized cities maintain a $1,000/month spend for six months and grow from 30 to 80+ new clients per month.

**Q: Do I need a big following first to run TikTok ads?**

No. Paid ads don’t care about your follower count. You could have zero followers and still get bookings. In fact, a salon in Tucson started ads with 12 followers and generated 9 bookings in the first week. Followers are vanity; bookings are revenue.

**Q: Can I target only people within 5 miles of my salon?**

Yes. In TikTok Ads Manager, set the location as “Custom” and draw a radius around your salon. Combine that with interest targeting for “Hair salon,” “Beauty salon,” “Hairstylist,” and “Self-care.” You can also exclude people who haven’t visited in the last 90 days (if you have a pixel or customer list).

**Q: How long before I see results?**

Expect 2–3 days for the algorithm to optimize. On day one, you’ll see high cost per click. By day four, if your creative is good, cost per booking should stabilize. If by day seven you’re above $50 per booking, change the video or tighten the targeting. Don’t kill a campaign before day five.

**Q: What if I’m not comfortable being on camera?**

You don’t have to be. Shoot hands-only content: scissors cutting, color bowl mixing, the final result. Use a tripod and point the camera at your work, not your face. A salon in Seattle ran a “hands-only” ad showing a gray root touch-up and booked 14 new clients. No stylist face required. Or ask a client to film you while you work—just get a release form.

**Q: Should I run ads on Instagram or TikTok (or both)?**

Both, but start with TikTok if you’re a local service business. Instagram’s cost per click has been rising (average $1.20 for beauty, vs. TikTok’s $0.30–$0.60). TikTok’s algorithm is still more generous with distribution to new ad accounts. Run one month on TikTok, measure the ROI, then decide if Instagram fills gaps. Often, Instagram works better for retargeting past clients, not cold prospecting.
