**Getting Started with TikTok Ads**

If you're new to TikTok, the first step is to set up a business account. You'll need to create a username, add a profile picture, and write a compelling bio. This will help you establish a presence on the platform and make it easier to track your ad performance.

To create an ad, follow these steps:

1. Go to the TikTok Ads Manager and select the ad format that best suits your business goals.
2. Choose your target audience based on factors like location, age, and interests.
3. Set your budget and bidding strategy.
4. Upload your ad creative, which can include videos, images, or even live streams.
5. Review and launch your ad campaign.

**TikTok vs Instagram: Ad Pricing**

When it comes to ad pricing, TikTok and Instagram have different models. TikTok uses a cost-per-click (CPC) model, where you pay each time someone clicks on your ad. Instagram, on the other hand, uses a cost-per-thousand impressions (CPM) model, where you pay per 1,000 times your ad is displayed.

**Measuring Success on TikTok**

When it comes to measuring success on TikTok, there are several metrics to keep an eye on:

1. Ad reach: This measures how many people saw your ad.
2. Ad clicks: This measures how many people clicked on your ad.
3. Conversion rate: This measures how many people completed a desired action (like making a purchase or filling out a form).
4. Return on ad spend (ROAS): This measures how much revenue you generated compared to your ad spend.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Tourists Instead of Neighbors

A coffee shop in Austin, Texas called *Forthright Coffee* spent $2,800 over three months on TikTok ads in early 2025. They targeted people "interested in coffee" within a 50-mile radius of downtown Austin. Sounds reasonable, right? 

What actually happened: Their ads got decent views — 34,000 impressions — but only 11 people scanned their QR code for a drink discount. Of those 11, exactly three showed up, all of whom were tourists staying at an Airbnb two blocks away. Their average ticket was $8.50. Total revenue from ads: $25.50. Return on ad spend: less than one percent.

The problem: A 50-mile radius in Austin captures San Antonio, Round Rock, and every tourist staying within those cities. The algorithm optimizes for whoever converts cheapest — and tourists watching TikTok in their hotel room are cheap to convert. They'll click your ad once, maybe even show up, but they won't return on Tuesday morning for their regular latte.

The fix: Change targeting to people who have "visited" your area in the last 30 days, combined with a tight 5-mile radius. Add a layer excluding users whose home location is outside your metro area. For TikTok, use custom audiences uploaded from your Square or Clover POS data. Upload a list of email addresses from customers who visited more than twice in the last six months. TikTok's algorithm will find lookalikes who actually live nearby.

After the fix, Forthright Coffee narrowed their radius to 3 miles and uploaded their 600 repeat customer emails. They spent the same $933/month. Over the next two months, 58 new customers walked in specifically because of the TikTok ad. Average ticket rose to $14 because these were locals buying pastries and lunch sandwiches, not just a drip coffee. Total revenue: $812. Cost per acquisition dropped from $933 to $34. Not glorious, but functional. More importantly, those customers stuck around. Their retention rate at 90 days was 41%.

### Mistake #2: Running Instagram Ads for Services Nobody Books on Instagram

A pet grooming studio in Nashville called *Barks & Bubbles* put $1,500/month into Instagram ads from June to August 2025. They used video carousels showing before-and-after grooming transformations. The ads were beautiful. Great lighting. Happy golden retrievers. 

The problem: They booked zero appointments from Instagram in three months. Zero.

What I heard when I spoke to the owner: "Instagram is visual, so it should work for grooming, right?" Wrong. The owner later said she got plenty of "cute dog!" comments and even a few DMs asking for pricing. But nobody filled out the Booksy booking link in her bio. She assumed those DMs would convert to bookings. They didn't.

The fix: Stop advertising a service on a platform where the user's intent is entertainment, not task completion. People on Instagram don't want to open a scheduling link. They want to scroll past your golden retriever and feel a warm emotion. 

Instead, we redirected the entire Instagram budget into Google Local Services Ads. Cost: $800/month, plus $200 into Yelp Ads targeting "pet grooming Nashville." The remaining $500 went to TikTok ads pushing a direct "Book Now" button with a 15% discount code for first-time customers — not a "learn more" or "visit website" button. A direct CTA.

Results in the first 30 days: Google Local Services Ads brought in 22 booked appointments worth $1,870 in revenue. Yelp brought in 7 appointments worth $595. TikTok brought in 14 appointments worth $1,190. Total: $3,655 on $1,500 spend. Instagram got cut entirely.

The uncomfortable truth: Instagram ads work great for businesses where the purchase decision is impulsive and the CTA is simple — buy a t-shirt, sign up for a webinar, download a recipe. They're terrible for local services that require people to coordinate their schedule, check their availability, and commit to a specific time slot. Instagram is a discovery engine, not a booking engine.

### Mistake #3: Using the Same Creative on Both Platforms

A yoga studio in Portland, Oregon called *Flow & Restore* ran identical video ads on TikTok and Instagram in September 2025. The video: a 45-second slow pan across a peaceful studio, soft music, a voiceover about "finding your center." Budget: $600 per platform.

On Instagram, the ad performed fine. 2.1% CTR, cost per lead of $12, booked 19 intro class slots.

On TikTok, the same ad bombed. 0.3% CTR. Zero bookings. $600 spent. Nothing.

The difference wasn't the audience. It was the format. TikTok users are trained to skip slow content within 0.3 seconds. A 45-second slow pan is TikTok poison. They need fast cuts, text overlays, someone talking directly to camera, a hook in the first two seconds.

The fix: Create platform-native creative. For TikTok, we shot a 15-second video where the instructor stood in front of a mirror, looked at the camera, and said: "If you think yoga is boring, I have 60 seconds to change your mind." Then cut to five quick shots of a vinyasa flow with bass-heavy music, ending with "First class free — link in bio." Raw. Aggressive. No soft lighting.

For Instagram, we kept the original slow studio tour, but added a carousel format with five slides explaining "5 Signs Your Yoga Studio Is Too Intense for You" — designed for saves and shares.

Results: TikTok creative change cost $200 to reshoot. The new ad spent $600 over two weeks and booked 34 first-time visitors at $25 each (intro class was normally $30, but the ad offered $25). Total revenue: $850. ROI turned positive. Instagram kept running the original ad with consistent results.

The lesson: If you film one piece of content and publish it everywhere, you're wasting money on at least one platform. Probably both.

## What Your Competitors Don't Tell You About Frequency

Most guides say "test different audiences" and call it a day. Here's what you'll actually discover when you run TikTok and Instagram ads for six months, which nobody warns you about:

People get annoyed. Fast.

I worked with a hair salon in Denver called *Lather + Blade* that ran Instagram ads to a retargeting audience for 14 consecutive weeks. Same creative. Same audience. By week five, their click-through rate dropped 73%. By week nine, negative comments started appearing on the ad: "Why does this keep showing up?" "I already book there." "Stop."

They didn't notice because they weren't looking at the comments section of their own ads. I checked. Twelve people had left negative feedback. That's bad for business and worse for your ad account's health score.

The fix: Set frequency caps immediately. On Instagram, cap frequency at 3 per person per week. On TikTok, cap it at 2. TikTok's algorithm punishes oversaturation faster. Also, rotate creative every 14 days minimum. You don't need completely new videos every time — swap the hook, change the CTA, use a different angle. Small changes reset the audience's boredom clock.

Lather + Blade implemented frequency caps and a two-week creative rotation. Their CTR stabilized at 1.8%. Their cost per booking dropped from $47 to $29 in three weeks. And nobody told them to stop running ads anymore.

## How to Actually Measure ROI Across Both Platforms

If you're a coffee shop or a salon, you can't track conversions the same way an e-commerce store does. You don't have a "purchase" event firing when someone buys a latte. This is where most local business owners get stuck and give up on TikTok or Instagram entirely.

Here's the method that worked for a fitness studio in Chicago called *West Loop Strength Lab*.

They ran $2,000/month across both platforms. Instead of relying on the platform's own conversion tracking (which is notoriously bad for offline businesses), they did three things:

1. **Dedicated landing pages with distinct phone numbers.** They bought two cheap VoIP numbers through Google Voice ($10 total). They put one number on the TikTok landing page and one on the Instagram landing page. Any call to either number went to the front desk. The front desk asked every caller: "Did you find us through TikTok or Instagram?" Took three seconds and gave them clean attribution data.

2. **QR codes in the ad creative.** Not in the caption — in the video. "Show this QR code at the front desk for your free week." Each platform had a different code generated through Square's QR code tool (free). When someone showed up and scanned, the system logged which platform sent them.

3. **Promo codes tied to each platform.** TikTok promo: FREETIKTOK. Instagram promo: FREEGRAM. Different but equal value. When someone used the code during checkout, they could track which platform delivered the customer.

The result: In Q4 2025, West Loop Strength Lab determined that TikTok customers had a 31% higher 90-day retention rate than Instagram customers, even though Instagram delivered slightly more initial visits. That insight completely changed their budget allocation. They shifted 60% of spend to TikTok because the lifetime value was higher.

Without this tracking, they'd have cut TikTok and doubled down on Instagram. That would have been a $1,200/month mistake.

## When to Use Email and SMS (Not Just Ads)

If you're spending on TikTok and Instagram but ignoring email and SMS, you're leaving money on the table. I'll be blunt: most local businesses don't need to spend more on ads. They need to spend better on retention.

I watched a pet groomer in Scottsdale, Arizona spend $1,100/month on Instagram ads to acquire new customers, while her existing customers churned at 60% because she had no follow-up system. She'd groom a dog once, never see that owner again, and then pay Instagram to find a new one.

We connected her Square POS to Mailchimp ($25/month) and set up two automated flows:

- **Day 1 after visit:** "We hope [pet name] is still feeling fresh! The next groom is due in about 4 weeks. Book here."
- **Day 21 after visit:** "Book [pet name]'s next appointment this week and receive 10% off — the brush is calling."

She also added an SMS sequence through SimpleTexting ($30/month) for appointment reminders. That reduced her no-show rate from 18% to 4%.

Within 60 days, her repeat customer rate went from 40% to 67%. She didn't need more Instagram ads. She needed to talk to the customers she already had.

Here's the math: She was spending $13,200/year on Instagram customer acquisition. After implementing email and SMS, she reduced ad spend to $600/month because her rebooking engine was feeding itself. She saved $5,400/year on ads and gained $8,200 in additional revenue from repeat bookings. Total improvement: $13,600.

Your TikTok and Instagram ads should feed your email list. If you're not collecting email addresses and phone numbers in your ad funnel, fix that first.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I only have $500/month to spend. Should I put it all on TikTok or Instagram?**

Put $300 on Google Local Services Ads or Yelp depending on your industry. Use the remaining $200 to test one platform — not both. Pick the platform where your actual customer type already hangs out. If your customers are under 35 and you sell something visually interesting (food, haircuts, fitness), pick TikTok. If your customers are 35–55 and you sell something that requires trust (auto repair, dental, legal), Instagram or Facebook is safer. You cannot split $500 between two platforms and expect to learn anything meaningful. You'll get 37 impressions and no conversions and conclude "ads don't work." They work. You just spread yourself too thin.

**Q: How do I know if my ad creative is bad or if the platform just isn't working for my business?**

Run a $50 test on each platform with the exact same creative. If one platform generates 10 clicks and the other generates 0, your creative isn't the problem — the platform's audience intent is mismatched. If both platforms generate zero clicks, your creative is the problem. Fix the creative before you adjust the budget. I've seen business owners fire their ad agency because "Facebook doesn't work for restaurants" when in reality their video looked like a hostage video shot in a dark kitchen.

**Q: Do I need to be on TikTok myself or can I just run ads?**

You don't need to be a TikTok content creator to run ads. But you should at least understand the format. If you've never scrolled TikTok for 20 minutes, you won't recognize bad creative. Pay someone who actually uses the platform to shoot your ad video. Do not repurpose your 2019 Facebook video with a voiceover about your "grand opening." It will look dated and the algorithm will not serve it. Budget $150–$300 for a local videographer who shoots vertical video. That single investment will outperform $2,000 in bad ad spend.

**Q: How long should I run a campaign before deciding it doesn't work?**

Minimum 14 days for either platform. TikTok's algorithm needs about 3–5 days just to find your audience. If you kill the campaign on day 6 because you got 2 clicks and spent $40, you never actually ran the test. Let it run for two weeks at a minimum daily spend of $15–$20. If after 14 days you have fewer than 100 link clicks or zero booked appointments, pause and change your targeting or creative. Do not double the budget hoping it will suddenly work. It won't.

**Q: Can I use the same promo code on both platforms and just split the budget?**

No. You can't prove which platform drove the conversion, and then you can't optimize. If you run $500 on TikTok and $500 on Instagram with the same promo code, and you get 10 customers, you don't know where 8 of them came from. That means your next $1,000 will be spent just as blindly as the first $1,000. Use separate promo codes, separate phone numbers, or separate landing pages. It's annoying to set up. It pays for itself in the first month of data.

**Q: What about Yelp? Should I just skip TikTok and Instagram and use Yelp instead?**

Yelp works well for businesses people actively search for — plumbers, dentists, mechanics. It's terrible for businesses people discover passively — coffee shops, yoga studios, hair salons. If you're a service business where the customer has a problem they need solved right now (leaky pipe, tooth pain), Yelp is worth your money. If you run a business where someone might think "I should try a new place for lunch," TikTok and Instagram are better. Most businesses should run a small test on each platform and let the data decide. I've seen a carpet cleaner in Phoenix get 80% of their new customers from TikTok ads because they filmed funny "gross carpet before/after" videos. Nobody searches for carpet cleaning for fun. But they watched the video, laughed, and saved it for later. That's TikTok working where Yelp couldn't.

**Q: What happens if TikTok gets banned in the US?**

If TikTok gets banned, the attention moves somewhere else — YouTube Shorts, Instagram Reels, or whatever replaces it. The principle doesn't change: short-form vertical video is now the dominant advertising format for local businesses. If you learn how to make good short-form ads, the platform is just distribution. Build the skill, not the platform loyalty. I've been through three major platform shifts in my career (MySpace, Facebook, Instagram, now TikTok). The businesses that adapted quickly survived. The ones who said "I don't do video" went out of business or sold to someone who did.
