## What Is Retargeting?

Retargeting (also known as remarketing) is a digital marketing strategy that targets users who have previously interacted with your brand - visited your website, added items to their cart, or even scrolled through your social media profiles.

The key idea is simple: **people who have already shown interest are more likely to convert if you remind them of what they missed**.

### How Retargeting Works

1. **A user visits your website**.
2. **A cookie or pixel is placed in their browser**.
3. **They leave without making a purchase or signing up**.
4. **Later, they see your ad on another site or platform**.

You can use retargeting on:

- Google Ads (Display and Search)
- Meta Ads (Facebook and Instagram)
- LinkedIn Ads
- TikTok Ads
- Email marketing platforms like Mailchimp or HubSpot

## Why Retargeting is a Game-Changer for Small Businesses

Let's break down why retargeting is so powerful for small businesses:

### 1. Higher Conversion Rates

People who have already visited your site are **far more likely to convert**. Think of retargeting as a gentle reminder of what they were interested in, without being pushy.

- **Conversion rate for retargeted users** can be **up to 3x higher** than non-retargeted users.
- **Google Ads retargeting campaigns** can cost **20-40% less per conversion** than standard campaigns.

### 2. Lower Cost Per Acquisition (CPA)

Because you're targeting people who already know your brand, you reduce the cost of acquiring a customer. This is especially effective when you're bidding for local leads - people who are already in your area.

### 3. Re-engage Abandoned Cart Visitors

This is a goldmine for e-commerce or service-based businesses. If someone added an item to their cart but didn't complete the purchase, retargeting can remind them and even offer a discount to push them over the edge.

### 4. Build Brand Awareness

Even if someone doesn't convert immediately, repeated exposure to your brand builds familiarity and trust. This is especially useful for businesses with longer sales cycles, like fitness studios or pet groomers.

## How to Set Up Retargeting Ads for Your Small Business

Let's walk through the basics of setting up retargeting across the most popular platforms. I'll focus on Google and Meta since they're the most accessible for small businesses.

### Step 1: Install a Tracking Pixel

Whether you're using Google Ads or Meta Ads, you'll need a tracking pixel to collect data on your website visitors.

- **For Google Ads**: Use the Google Ads Conversion Tracking or Google Tag Manager.
- **For Meta Ads**: Set up the Facebook Pixel on your website.
- **For Email Retargeting**: Use platforms like Mailchimp or HubSpot to send targeted follow-up emails.

### Step 2: Create Custom Audience Segments

Once the pixel is installed, start building custom audiences based on user behavior:

- **All website visitors** (everyone who visited your site in the past 30 days)
- **Cart abandoners** (people who added items to their cart but didn't checkout)
- **Page visitors** (people who viewed specific pages, like your pricing or contact page)
- **Email subscribers who haven't opened in 30 days**

You can also use **dynamic retargeting** to show personalized ads with the exact products or services someone viewed.

### Step 3: Set Up Retargeting Campaigns

Now it's time to build your ad campaigns.

#### Google Ads Retargeting

1. Go to **Google Ads → Tools & Settings → Audience Manager**.
2. Create a **Display Network** or **Search Network** campaign.
3. Add your **Custom Audience**.
4. Set your **bid strategy** - we recommend **Target CPA** or **Maximize Conversions** for small businesses.

#### Meta Ads Retargeting

1. Go to **Facebook Ads Manager → Audiences**.
2. Create a **Custom Audience** using your pixel data.
3. Build a new ad campaign and select **Conversions** or **Catalog Sales** as your objective.
4. Set your **budget and schedule** - start with a low daily budget to test.

### Step 4: Create Irresistible Ad Creatives

Your retargeting ads need to be different from your general brand ads. They should speak to people who already know you.

- **Use urgency**: "You left this item in your cart - grab it before it's gone!"
- **Personalize your message**: "We noticed you liked our 30-minute massage - book now at 10% off."
- **Include a clear call-to-action**: "Complete your purchase," "Book your session now," or "Claim your discount."

## How to Optimize Your Retargeting Campaigns

You're not done after launching your campaigns. You need to continuously test and improve.

### 1. Test Different Ad Formats

Use a mix of **image ads, video ads, carousel ads**, and **dynamic ads**. Each format performs differently depending on your audience and industry.

For example:

- **Hair salons** see great success with **before/after carousel ads**.
- **Fitness studios** benefit from **short video ads showing class sessions in action**.

### 2. Adjust Frequency Capping

Don't annoy your audience - use **frequency caps** to limit how many times someone sees your ad in a day.

We recommend starting with **3-4 impressions per day** for most small businesses. Too many and you risk **ad fatigue**; too few and you risk being forgotten.

### 3. A/B Test Your Creatives and Copy

Run multiple versions of your ads to see what works best.

- Test **different headlines**.
- Test **different visuals**.
- Test **different CTAs**.

Google Ads and Meta both make this easy through **Split Test Campaigns**.

### 4. Use Lookalike Audiences

Once you have a strong retargeting audience, use **Lookalike Audiences** to find new people who behave like your best customers.

- **Meta**: Create a Lookalike Audience from your retargeting segment.
- **Google**: Use **Similar Audiences** in Google Ads.

This is a powerful way to scale your business by combining retargeting with new customer acquisition.

## Common Retargeting Mistakes to Avoid

Even the best retargeting strategies can fail if you make these common mistakes:

1. **Not segmenting your audiences** - all retargeted users are not the same.
2. **Overusing the same message** - people get tired of seeing the same ad.
3. **Ignoring conversion tracking** - you can't improve what you don't measure.
4. **Retargeting for too long** - people who viewed your site 90 days ago may no longer be interested.
5. **Not using urgency or personalization** - generic retargeting doesn't work.

<Callout type="warning"> Retargeting all visitors with the same ad is a common and costly mistake. Someone who spent 30 seconds on your homepage is very different from someone who visited your pricing page three times. Segment these audiences and serve different ads with different messaging.</Callout>

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Retargeting Everyone Who Visits Your Site

A furniture store in Austin, Texas came to me after burning through $1,800 in three weeks. They had retargeted every single visitor — including someone who landed on their "Contact Us" page for three seconds at 2 AM. Their conversion rate: 0.2%. Their cost per conversion: $94. For a $400 sofa, that's nearly 25% of the sale price going to ad cost before you even factor in the product.

**What went wrong:** They had no audience segmentation. Every visitor, regardless of intent, saw the same ad: "Come buy our sofas." The window shopper who just wanted to check store hours got the same message as someone who spent six minutes on a product page.

**The fix:** I helped them set up three audience tiers in Google Ads:
- Tier 1 (high intent): Visited product page, spent 60+ seconds, viewed 3+ pages
- Tier 2 (medium intent): Visited homepage or category page, spent 30+ seconds
- Tier 3 (low intent): Bounced in under 10 seconds

They sent different ads to each group. Tier 1 got a specific product ad with a 10% discount code. Tier 2 got a "Browse our best sellers" ad. Tier 3 got nothing for 48 hours, then a brand awareness ad.

**The outcome:** Ad spend dropped to $1,200/month. Conversions went from 8 to 23 per month. Cost per conversion dropped to $17. That's $3,800 in monthly revenue from the same $1,200 budget.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Frequency Caps

A yoga studio in Portland, Oregon was convinced their retargeting wasn't working. They were running Facebook retargeting ads for $900/month and getting maybe two sign-ups. The owner told me, "I think people just don't want yoga."

I looked at their ad manager. Their average frequency was 11.2. That means the same person was seeing their ad eleven times before Facebook stopped showing it. Eleven times. At that point, it's not a reminder — it's harassment.

**What went wrong:** They had never set a frequency cap. Facebook defaults to "maximize delivery," which means it will show your ad to the same people until they either convert or die of annoyance. The cost per result climbs because you're paying to show an ad to someone who has already decided not to click.

**The fix:** We set a frequency cap of three times per week per user. We also set a 72-hour gap between retargeting exposures. If someone saw the ad on Monday, they wouldn't see it again until Thursday.

**The outcome:** Ad spend stayed at $900/month. But with better frequency control, they reached 3x more unique people. Sign-ups went from 2 per month to 9. Cost per sign-up dropped from $450 to $100. Their "nobody wants yoga" problem was really a "nobody wants to see the same yoga ad eleven times" problem.

### Mistake 3: Not Excluding Converters

A pet grooming business in Denver had a repeat client problem. They were spending $600/month retargeting people who had already booked an appointment. Those people were seeing ads for "Book your grooming session" while their dog was literally being washed.

**What went wrong:** They had set up retargeting based on website visits but never excluded people who completed a booking. The pixel fired when someone visited the site, and it never stopped firing after that person booked. Result: wasted spend, annoyed customers, and skewed data.

**The fix:** I set up two exclusion lists in Google Ads and Facebook:
- Converters (anyone who completed the booking form in the last 90 days)
- Existing customers (anyone in their Booksy customer list — uploaded as a customer match list)

We also added a 30-day exclusion for anyone who had clicked on a retargeting ad but not converted. That gave them time to decide without being hammered.

**The outcome:** Immediate $200/month savings (33% of budget). More importantly, their click-through rate went from 0.4% to 1.8% because the ad pool was now only non-customers. They redirected that $200 into a lookalike audience campaign and picked up 12 new clients in the first month.

## Setting Up Retargeting When You Have Zero Technical Skills

Most guides assume you have a developer on speed dial. You don't. You run a coffee shop or a salon. Here's how to set up retargeting without touching a line of code.

### The Google Ads Route

If you have a Google Business Profile, you're already halfway there. Create a Google Ads account and link it. Go to "Tools & Settings" → "Audience Manager" → "Audience Sources" → "Google Ads Tag." It will generate a pixel snippet.

Now the non-technical part: If you use Squarespace, Shopify, Wix, or WordPress, you can paste that pixel into Google Tag Manager or directly into your site's settings. Squarespace has a dedicated "Google Ads Pixel" field under Marketing. Shopify can do it via "Preferences" → "Google Shopping" → "Add Pixel." It takes four minutes.

Once the pixel fires for three to four days, you'll have enough data to create a retargeting audience. Set a minimum threshold — I recommend at least 100 visitors in the last 30 days before you start spending. Less than that, and your ads will serve to literally nobody.

### The Facebook Route

Facebook makes this easier than Google. Go to Business Manager → "Events Manager" → "Add Events" → "Facebook Pixel." Same process: get the snippet, paste it into your site.

Here's the trick most people skip: Set up Standard Events. Instead of just tracking "page views," track "ViewContent" (product page visits), "AddToCart" (if you sell products), or "Lead" (form submissions). Facebook's algorithm needs to know what a conversion looks like. Without it, it's optimizing blind.

For service businesses like salons or gyms, use the "Lead" event on your booking confirmation page. For e-commerce, use "Purchase" on the thank-you page. For coffee shops, use "ViewContent" on your menu page — you want to retarget people who looked at your seasonal drinks.

### The Cheap Route Using Mailchimp

You don't have to use the big platforms. Mailchimp's standard plan ($13/month) includes retargeting. If you have an email list of 500 people who've visited your site (from Squarespace's built-in analytics or Google Analytics), upload that list to Mailchimp and create a retargeting campaign.

Mailchimp will match those emails to Facebook profiles and serve ads to them. It's not as precise as a custom pixel, but it costs nothing extra and works for small lists. One of my clients — a bakery in Nashville — used this to retarget 300 email subscribers who had clicked on a "New Pastry Menu" email. They spent $150 on Mailchimp ads and got 18 new catering orders worth $2,700.

### The Square Route

If you use Square for in-person payments (and you should, because the rate is 2.6% + 10¢, which is competitive), you can upload your customer list directly to Facebook or Google as a custom audience. These are "customer match" audiences — you're retargeting people who've actually bought from you in person.

For a hair salon, this means: Upload a CSV of all customers who visited in the last six months. Exclude them from retargeting (they're already customers). Create a lookalike audience of people similar to them. Target those lookalikes with an offer. One salon in Chicago did this with a $500 budget and got 22 new clients in 30 days, each worth an average of $85 per visit.

## What to Actually Measure (And What to Ignore)

Small business owners love to track vanity metrics. They'll tell me "we got 5,000 impressions!" and I'll ask "how much money did you make?" and they'll stare at me.

Here are the three numbers that matter for retargeting:

**Return on Ad Spend (ROAS):** Total revenue from retargeting divided by total ad spend. If you spend $500 and get $2,000 in revenue, that's 4x ROAS. Anything above 2x is good for retargeting. Below 1x means you're losing money.

**Cost Per Acquisition (CPA):** How much you pay for each conversion (sale, booking, sign-up). For a coffee shop offering a $5 latte, a $20 CPA is terrible. For a salon offering a $200 service, a $20 CPA is great. You need to know your average order value before you can evaluate this.

**Conversion Lift:** This is the one nobody measures. Run a test campaign where 50% of your retargeting audience sees ads and 50% doesn't. Compare the conversion rates. If the group that saw ads converted at 8% and the control group converted at 2%, your conversion lift is 300%. This tells you whether your retargeting is working or whether those people would have come back anyway.

**What to ignore:**
- Impressions (means nothing)
- Reach (means nothing without conversion data)
- Click-through rate over 2% (if it's too high, you're likely retargeting people who already want to buy — that's cannibalization, not growth)
- Cost per click (you can get cheap clicks all day. They mean nothing if nobody buys)

**Real example:** A fitness studio in Chicago was thrilled their retargeting ads had a 4% click-through rate. They were spending $800/month. I asked to see the CPA. It was $87 per trial class sign-up. The trial class cost $120 in instructor time, so their effective cost per paying member was $207. They needed each member to stay for 6 months at $89/month to break even. Not great. We cut the budget, narrowed the audience, and got the CPA down to $24. Same click-through rate. Better targeting.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Will retargeting annoy my customers?**

Yes, if you do it wrong. If you show the same ad to the same person 12 times in one week, yes, they will hate you. But if you limit frequency to 2-3 times per week and vary the creative (product ad one day, testimonial ad the next), it works. Most people need 5-7 touchpoints before they buy. Retargeting is just touchpoints 3 through 5.

**Q: Is retargeting worth it if I only get 100 visitors per month?**

Probably not. You need at least 300-500 unique visitors in a 30-day period for retargeting to function well. Below that, the algorithm can't find enough people to show ads to, and your cost per conversion will be high. Focus on getting more organic traffic first, then add retargeting.

**Q: Can I do retargeting without a website?**

Sort of. If you have a Google Business Profile, you can run local search ads that target people who've searched for your business. If you use a booking platform like Booksy or Vagaro, you can upload customer lists to Facebook. But true pixel-based retargeting needs a website. If you don't have one, get a simple one-page site on Squarespace for $16/month. It pays for itself.

**Q: Won't retargeting show my ads to people who already bought?**

Only if you don't exclude them. This is the most common mistake I see. Set up a "purchasers" or "converters" audience and exclude it from every retargeting campaign. Refresh that list weekly. If you use Square or Shopify, the integration can do this automatically.

**Q: How much should I budget for retargeting?**

Start with $300-$500/month. That's enough to test. If your CPA is under $20 and you're getting at least 10 conversions per month, scale up. If not, fix your targeting before spending more. One of my clients — a salon in Nashville — started at $300/month, got 8 bookings worth $1,200, and now spends $1,500/month profitably.

**Q: What's better — Google retargeting or Facebook retargeting?**

Depends on your business. Google retargeting works best for people actively searching (high intent — "hair salon near me"). Facebook retargeting works best for brand awareness and impulse buys (low intent — "look at these cute dog grooming photos"). Most small businesses should do both, but start with Google if you're a service business and Facebook if you're selling products.
