## Which Google Ads campaign type works best for a pediatric office?

A Search campaign is the backbone for any pediatric practice because parents type specific terms like "pediatrician near me" or "child doctor open now."

- **Search**: Shows text ads at the top of Google results. Ideal for intent‑driven traffic.
- **Local Service Ads**: Only in select U.S. states, but they appear with a "Google Guaranteed�� badge, boosting trust.
- **Display**: Use sparingly to retarget families who visited your site but didn’t book.

**Example:** A 3‑chair clinic in Austin, TX started with a $500/month Search campaign and saw 30 qualified calls in the first two weeks, compared to zero before.

If you’re tight on budget, focus on Search and set geo‑targeting to a 10‑mile radius around your office. Add a call‑only extension so parents can dial directly from the ad.

<Callout type="tip">Start with a single "appointment‑booking" keyword list. Expand only after you see which terms convert.</Callout>

## How much should you spend and how do you bid?

Pediatricians typically see a **Cost‑Per‑Click (CPC)** of $2.50‑$4.00 for health‑related terms. A realistic starter budget is $1,000‑$1,500 per month, which yields roughly 300‑500 clicks.

- **Manual CPC**: Gives you control, good for testing.
- **Maximize Conversions**: Let Google automate bids after you have conversion data (e.g., phone calls).

<Callout type="warning">Don’t set a "low‑budget" limit like $50/month. Google needs enough spend to gather data; otherwise you’ll waste money on irrelevant clicks.</Callout>

## Writing ad copy that gets parents to call

Parents care about safety, convenience, and bedside manner. Your ad copy should mirror those concerns in under 90 characters.

- **Headline 1:** "Board‑Certified Pediatrician – Same‑Day Appointments"
- **Headline 2:** "Open 7 Days – Accept New Patients"
- **Description:** "Experienced doctors, kid‑friendly office, free wellness check for first visit."

Add **call‑only** and **location** extensions. Use **structured snippets** to list services: "Vaccinations, Well‑Child Visits, Sick Visits."

**Real‑world example:** A boutique clinic in Sydney, Australia swapped a generic "Pediatric Care" headline for "Kids’ Health – Walk‑In Today!" and saw a 42% lift in click‑through rate (CTR) within one week.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does a typical pediatric practice spend on Google Ads per month?
Most small to mid‑size clinics start with $800‑$1,500 monthly. This budget generates 250‑500 clicks and roughly 15‑30 qualified leads, depending on location and competition.

### Can I run Google Ads without a dedicated marketing team?
Yes. With clear goals, a simple keyword list, and conversion tracking, you can launch a basic campaign in a day. For ongoing optimization, consider a part‑time consultant or a managed service.

### What’s the difference between "Search" and "Local Service Ads" for doctors?
Search ads appear as text results based on keywords. Local Service Ads show a "Google Guaranteed" badge and are billed per lead, but they’re only available in select regions and require a background check.

### How quickly can I expect to see new patients book appointments?
If you use call‑only extensions and a fast‑loading landing page, you can start receiving calls within 24‑48 hours of launch. Conversions usually improve after the first two weeks of data collection.

### Do I need a separate website for my pediatric practice?
A dedicated landing page that highlights your services, office hours, and a clear "Book Now" button works best. It should load in under 3 seconds and be mobile‑friendly.

### Is it worth running ads on Facebook or Instagram as well?
Yes, especially for brand awareness and retargeting. Parents often browse social media while waiting for a child’s appointment, so a coordinated [Meta Ads management](/services/meta-ads) strategy can reinforce your Google Ads efforts.

### How do I measure the return on ad spend (ROAS) for a pediatric clinic?
Track the cost per lead (CPL) and compare it to the average lifetime value of a patient (often $1,200‑$2,000). A ROAS above 3:1 is generally considered healthy for a medical practice.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Ads on Every Keyword That Seems Relevant

A pediatrician in Nashville told me she wanted to rank for "everything a parent might search for." She ended up bidding on terms like "baby fever," "toddler rash," and "child vaccine schedule" — broad match, no negatives. Her $1,200 monthly budget was gone in 11 days. Most clicks came from people looking for symptom checklists, not appointments.

**What went wrong:** She paid for information seekers who had no intention of booking. Her cost per lead hit $48. That's painful for a small practice.

**The fix:** I helped her create three tight keyword groups. "Booking intent" keywords (pediatrician accepting new patients, child doctor near me, pediatrician open Saturday). "Condition" keywords with a modifier (strep throat test pediatrician, ear infection child doctor). And a negative keyword list that blocked terms like "symptoms," "home remedies," "causes," and "treatment at home."

**Outcome after 60 days:** Same $1,200 budget. 42 booked appointments. Cost per lead dropped to $12. She stopped showing up for people who wanted WebMD and started showing up for people who wanted an exam room.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Negative Keywords Until You Waste Money

A clinic in Portland, OR ran a Search campaign for three months. They were getting clicks, but the phone wasn't ringing. I checked their search terms report. Someone had searched "cheap pediatrician no insurance," clicked the ad, and cost the practice $4.20. Another searched "pediatrician that takes Medicaid" — also not a patient they could accept.

Over three months, they had spent $1,700 on clicks from people who could never become paying patients. Their actual cost per qualified lead was $94. Most guides skip this part — don't.

**The fix:** Every Monday, pull your search terms report. Add anything irrelevant as a negative keyword. Also add terms like "free," "discount," "coupon," "Medicaid," "sliding scale" if you don't offer those. Set up a script that automatically adds search terms with zero conversions after 30 clicks to your negative list.

**Outcome:** Reduced wasted spend by 38% in the first month. That $1,700 in three months became $640. The savings went into more effective keywords. Their cost per lead dropped from $94 to $27.

### Mistake #3: A Landing Page That Makes Parents Leave

A children's clinic in downtown Chicago ran a solid Search campaign. Good keywords, smart bidding, decent ad copy. Their click-through rate was 6.2%, which is strong. But their conversion rate was 1.1%. Nearly 99% of people who clicked the ad left without booking.

I visited their website. The "book appointment" button was buried under three paragraphs about the doctor's education. The form asked for insurance member ID, group number, and a primary care referral — before you could even pick a time. On mobile, the button was half the size of my thumb.

**What went wrong:** The ad promised "book online in 2 minutes." The website delivered a 15-minute data entry session.

**The fix:** Build a dedicated landing page with exactly one goal: schedule a visit. Remove insurance questions from the initial form — collect that at the appointment. Use a tool like Booksy or Square Appointments for a real-time calendar widget. Put the booking button above the fold on mobile. Add a phone number that's clickable with one tap.

**Outcome:** After the redesign, conversion rate went from 1.1% to 7.3%. That means for every 100 people who clicked the ad, 7.3 booked instead of 1.1. For a practice spending $2,000 a month on ads, that's roughly 23 new patients instead of 3. Revenue impact: about $3,800 in additional first-visit revenue per month.

## How to Write Ad Copy That Parents Actually Click On

Most pediatric Google Ads read like a yellow pages listing. "Dr. Smith has 15 years of experience. Call today." That doesn't work because it doesn't speak to the moment.

Think about when parents search for a pediatrician. It's rarely a calm Tuesday afternoon. It's 9:47 PM and their child has a fever of 102. It's Saturday morning and the rash appeared overnight. It's Monday and daycare called about a cough. They're anxious, tired, and looking for a signal that you can help, now.

I tested ad copy for a pediatric office in Denver. They had been running "Experienced pediatric care in a warm, friendly environment." The ad's click-through rate was 2.1%. I rewrote it to target specific scenarios:

**Headline 1:** Sick child? Same-day appointments available
**Headline 2:** New patients welcome — book online now
**Description:** Your child is our priority. Walk-ins accepted until 6 PM. Most insurances accepted. Book in under 60 seconds.

Click-through rate went to 5.8%. Conversion rate from the landing page also improved because the ad set a specific expectation — same-day, fast, no insurance hassle — and the landing page delivered on it.

**The uncomfortable truth:** Generic praise about your practice doesn't motivate action. Specific solutions to specific problems do. If you're not sure what parents in your area are worried about, ask your front desk staff. They hear it every day. "Can you see my son today?" "Do you take Blue Cross?" "Is there a long wait?" Build your ad copy around those exact questions.

Use ad extensions. Call extensions are obvious — make sure your phone number is clickable. Location extensions show your address on the map. Structured snippet extensions let you list services like well-child visits, vaccinations, sick visits, annual physicals. A pediatrician in Austin added structured snippets and saw a 14% increase in click-through rate because parents could immediately see they offered the specific service needed.

## Tracking Appointments (Not Just Clicks)

Here's a conversation I have at least once a month. A business owner tells me their Google Ads campaign is "working" because they got 300 clicks last week. I ask how many booked. Silence.

Clicks are vanity. Appointments are revenue.

For a pediatric practice in Houston, I set up proper tracking in about 90 minutes. Here is exactly what we did:

1. **Connected their Square Appointments account to Google Ads.** This automatically tracks when someone books online after clicking an ad. Most booking platforms — Booksy, Vagaro, Acuity, Square — have a direct integration or a Google Tag Manager option.

2. **Set up call tracking with CallRail.** Not all parents book online. Many call. CallRail assigns a unique phone number to your ad traffic so you can see exactly which keywords and ads generated phone calls. We discovered that "pediatrician open now" generated 4x more calls than clicks. Without call tracking, you'd never know.

3. **Created a conversion action for "form submission."** Some parents fill out a contact form instead of booking directly. That's still a lead. Assign a value to it — for pediatrics, a new patient is worth roughly $165 in first-year revenue based on average visit frequency. If you don't know your numbers, ask your practice manager. If you don't have a practice manager, guess conservatively.

4. **Set up a Google Sheet that auto-updates with lead source data.** Use Zapier or a simple integration. The sheet shows: date, source (Google Ads, Yelp, organic, referral), keyword (if applicable), whether they booked, and the dollar value. This gives you a weekly view of what's actually working.

**The result:** The Houston practice was spending $600 a month on Google Ads. Before proper tracking, they thought it was generating 15 appointments. After tracking, they saw it was actually 32. They also discovered that Yelp was sending zero calls but they were paying $200/month for Yelp Ads. They killed that budget, added it to Google Ads, and went from $600 to $800/month with 44 appointments.

If you're not tracking appointments, you're guessing. And guessing is expensive.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I start with a $200/month budget and still get results?**

Yes, but you need to be ruthlessly focused. Set geo-targeting to a 3-mile radius. Use only exact match keywords with high intent — "pediatrician accepting new patients near me" not "children's doctor." Bid aggressively during your office hours only. Expect maybe 8-12 clicks per day. If your landing page converts at 5%, that's roughly one appointment every two days. Not earth-shattering, but better than zero. Scale up once you see consistent conversions.

**Q: Should I use Google Guaranteed / Local Service Ads?**

If you're in a state where Local Service Ads are available, yes — but know the trade-off. Google charges per lead (not per click) and the leads can be lower quality. A pediatrician in Phoenix tried it and got calls from people who weren't even in her city. The badge does build trust, but you're paying for every lead regardless of whether they book. I'd test it with a small budget — $300/month — and compare cost per booked appointment against your Search campaign. Run both for 30 days, then pick the winner.

**Q: I don't get many phone calls from ads. Is it the ads, or is it my practice?**

Nine times out of ten, it's the ads or the targeting, not the practice. Check your search terms report first. Are you showing up for terms people actually use when they're ready to book? "Pediatrician open Saturday" is a booking query. "Child fever 102 treatment" is not. Also check your ad schedule — if you're only showing ads during lunch hours when parents are busy, you'll miss calls. Run ads 7 AM to 9 PM, at least initially, and see when the calls come in.

**Q: My competitor is spending a lot on Google Ads. Should I try to outbid them?**

No. You cannot outspend a bigger practice. Outsmart them instead. Target specific niches they ignore: Spanish-speaking families, Saturday-only availability, a specific vaccine brand, free newborn consult, same-day sick visits. Write ad copy that calls out your unique angle. Bid on your practice name and your competitor's name (yes, you can do this) with an ad that says "Looking for [competitor name]? We're accepting new patients today." It's aggressive but effective.

**Q: Do parents actually click on ads, or do they just use organic results?**

They click on both. Google's own data shows that ads increase total clicks by 89% for branded searches. Meaning when someone searches for "pediatrician near me," the organic results get more clicks if there's also an ad present. The ad doesn't just steal traffic — it expands the pie. If you're not running ads, your organic listing is getting fewer clicks than it could. Also, the top two organic results now look very similar to ads. The line is blurring. You need to be in both places.

**Q: How long until I see results?**

You'll see clicks within hours. You'll see your first phone call or booking within 24-48 hours if your targeting is tight. But real results — a steady flow of 20+ new patients per month from ads — take 4-6 weeks of optimization. The first two weeks are data gathering. Weeks 3-4 are pruning what doesn't work. By week 6, you should have a campaign that's producing at a predictable cost per acquisition. Anyone who promises instant floods of patients is selling something you shouldn't buy.
