## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched small business owners throw money at Microsoft Audience Network and walk away muttering that it doesn’t work. Nine times out of ten, the platform worked fine. The campaign setup didn’t. Here are the mistakes I keep seeing — along with the fixes that actually turn the spend into revenue.

**Mistake #1: Targeting “Coffee Lovers” in Portland and Wondering Why Nobody Showed Up**

A coffee shop owner in Portland, Oregon came to me after spending $1,200 over three months on Microsoft Audience Network. She’d set her audience interest to “coffee lovers.” The result: 14,000 impressions, 43 clicks, zero new customers. She was ready to burn the platform to the ground.

The problem? “Coffee lovers” includes people in Portland who drink Starbucks at their desk five days a week while working remote jobs in tech. They don’t need a local roaster. They need a vending machine. She was paying to show ads to people who weren’t in the market for a new coffee shop experience — they already had one on every corner.

The fix: I switched her targeting to “local coffee roasters” combined with “small business owners” and people within a 3-mile radius of her shop. Then I uploaded her email list from Square (people who’d visited at least twice in the last six months) and created a lookalike audience.

Her next month: $400 spend, 28 new customers tracked through a promo code she’d always used but never bothered to measure. Revenue from that campaign: $1,860. She ordered a second espresso machine. No regrets.

**Mistake #2: A Hair Salon in Austin Used the Same Image for Search Ads and Display**

A hair salon in Austin, Texas spent $600 on Microsoft Audience Network and got exactly one booking. The owner told me she’d used “that green hair model photo” — the same stock image from her Google Search Ads campaign — because it was what her agency had handed her three years ago.

Here’s the thing: that image worked for search ads because people were already looking for “hair salon Austin.” They’d type it in, see a friendly image, and click. On the Audience Network, nobody was searching. The ad appeared in their peripheral vision while they read a blog post about how to fix a leaky faucet. A generic stock image of a woman with green hair blended into the noise. Worse, the ad copy said “Book your appointment today” — which works when they’re already looking, not when they’re three paragraphs into a plumbing tutorial.

The fix: I created three ad variations. One showed a photo from inside her actual salon — the brick wall, the mirrors, a client getting a blowout (with permission). The second showed a before-and-after of a local client’s balayage, tagged with the neighborhood. The third used a simple text overlay on a photo of her storefront, saying “Walk-ins welcome Wednesdays.”

We tested for two weeks. The storefront photo with the neighborhood tag got five bookings directly. The salon interior photo got two. The before-and-after got zero — probably because it looked too polished to be real. Total spend: $320. Bookings from MSAN: 7, average ticket $135. Revenue: $945. Not life-changing, but a 3x return on ad spend in a month where she’d have otherwise wasted the budget.

**Mistake #3: A Pet Groomer in Denver Forgot About Mobile**

A Denver-based pet groomer running from her living room (expanding to a storefront soon) spent $500 on a Microsoft Audience Network campaign targeting “pet owners” and “Denver metro.” She got 12 clicks. Twelve.

I pulled up the campaign and checked the device report. 89% of impressions were on desktop. Her ad looked fine — a cute Golden Retriever holding a “Get 20% off your first groom” sign. But the landing page? A desktop-optimized site from 2019. On mobile, the “Book Now” button was hidden behind a pop-up offering a newsletter signup. The entire page load time on 4G was 6.2 seconds.

Here’s what she missed: Microsoft Audience Network shows your ads on MSN, Outlook, Edge, and partner sites. A massive chunk of that traffic comes from people checking email on their phones during lunch. They’re on mobile. Her ad was optimized for a 27-inch monitor.

Fix: I rebuilt her landing page in 90 minutes using Carrd ($19/year plan). One page, one CTA — “Book Your First Groom for $35.” No pop-ups. Image compressed to 200kb. We also adjusted targeting to “people using mobile devices” in the device settings of her campaign, which Microsoft actually lets you control (most people don’t know this).

Next month: $400 spend, 34 booked appointments tracked through Booksy integration. Revenue: $2,720 at $80 average ticket. She rented the storefront.

**Mistake #4: Using the Same Budget Strategy for MSAN as Google Ads**

I see this one at every single client handoff. A business owner has been running Google Display Network campaigns at $10/day for six months. They decide to try Microsoft Audience Network. They set it to $10/day. Then they wonder why it “doesn’t scale.”

Microsoft Audience Network has a smaller user base than Google’s Display Network — that’s the trade-off for cheaper clicks and better targeting. Some days, you’ll spend $3. Some days, $12. The algorithm needs budget flexibility to actually find your audience. Locking it to a strict daily cap kills performance because Microsoft’s system can’t compete for the good placements during peak hours.

A bakery in Nashville was doing this exact thing. $300/month on MSAN, spending $10/day, running for three months. She’d gotten exactly eight conversions. I shifted her to a weekly budget of $300 with no daily cap. She spent $240 in the first week — all of it on Friday and Saturday mornings when people were planning weekend treats. The remaining two weeks only spent $60 total. Total conversions that month: 23. Revenue from the campaign: roughly $2,500 based on average order size of $108 tracked through Square.

The daily cap was artificially throttling her reach. When I removed it, the platform started working.

## Which Local Businesses Benefit Most (And Which Should Skip It)

Microsoft Audience Network isn’t the right fit for every small business. I’ve tested it across dozens of categories in the US, and the results vary more than most guides admit.

**Who gets the best returns?**

Service-based businesses with a defined local radius and a repeat-purchase model. Pet groomers, hair salons, auto detailers, dental practices, HVAC contractors. These businesses benefit from MSAN’s ability to target by demographic (age, income, homeownership) combined with location. If you’re a plumber in Chicago and you target homeowners in ZIP codes with older housing stock, you’re going to find people who need a pipe fixed, not people browsing Instagram for cute wallpaper ideas.

A dentist in Denver ran MSAN for three months targeting “adults 25-45, homeowners, within 5 miles.” Monthly spend: $600. Monthly new patient bookings: 12-15. Average lifetime value of a new dental patient in Denver is around $900 (cleaning, x-rays, fillings over 12 months). That’s a 15x+ return.

Restaurants should approach with caution unless they have a specific promotion (lunch specials during the work week, for example). The platform doesn’t handle last-minute “I’m hungry now” intent as well as Google Search or even Instagram geofencing. One pizza place in Portland tried it for three months to promote their Tuesday night deal. Spend: $900. New customers tracked through Yelp check-ins and a unique coupon code: 14. Revenue from those customers: roughly $840. Not a win.

**Who should skip it entirely?**

Businesses that rely on impulse clicks for very low-ticket items ($15 or less). If you sell stickers, printable PDFs, or $5 coffee, the customer acquisition cost on MSAN is going to be higher than the conversion value. The platform’s strength is in consideration and intent — it’s not built for rapid impulse buys.

Also skip it if your customer base skews under 25. Microsoft’s audience (via LinkedIn, Outlook, MSN) tends to be older — 35-55 is the sweet spot. If you’re running a streetwear brand targeting college students, spend your money on TikTok and Instagram. I’ve seen this mismatch kill campaigns for two different clients in Los Angeles.

**The middle ground: test with $300, then decide**

I tell every client the same thing: run MSAN for one month at $300 total. No daily cap. One clear audience. Track everything with a promo code or landing page. If you can’t get a 2x return on ad spend in the first month, pull the budget and try something else. Don’t force it. The tool works for the right businesses in the right setup. It does not work as a “set it and forget it” channel for every industry.

## How to Track Results Without a Data Analyst

Small business owners in the US usually have two systems: a POS (Square, Toast, Clover) and maybe Mailchimp for email. Nobody has a full-time data analyst. Here’s how to track Microsoft Audience Network performance without one.

**Step 1: Use a dedicated promo code per ad group**

Don’t use a generic “SAVE20” code for your entire campaign. Create unique codes inside your POS system: “MSANCOFFEE1” for one audience, “MSANCOFFEE2” for another. Square lets you create unlimited discount codes with no setup fees. When someone comes in and uses MSANCOFFEE1, you know exactly which campaign drove that visit. This is the cheapest and most accurate tracking method available — and I’d say 80% of business owners skip it because they “forgot” or “it seemed complicated.” It takes 10 minutes.

A yoga studio in Austin did exactly this. She ran two separate MSAN campaigns — one targeting women 35-55 interested in wellness, the other targeting new movers in the area. She used code MSANWELL1 for the wellness group and MSANMOVE1 for the new mover group. After three months, she found the new mover campaign was driving 4x more first-class visits. She tripled the budget on that audience and paused the wellness one. That decision came from a $0 tracking method.

**Step 2: Set up Microsoft Clarity (free)**

Microsoft Clarity is a heatmapping and session recording tool that costs nothing. Connect it to your website in about 15 minutes. It will show you where people click, how far they scroll, and whether they rage-click when a pop-up blocks the booking button. I’ve watched small business owners discover that 63% of their mobile visitors were hitting “back” before the page loaded — because their site was bloated with images. Clarity shows you that in visual form, not a spreadsheet.

**Step 3: Use the UTM parameter microsoft.com provides**

Inside your Microsoft Ads dashboard, click “Shared Library” then “URL Options.” Set up auto-tagging with UTM parameters. Add source=microsoft_ads, medium=audience_network, and the campaign name. Then look at those values inside Google Analytics 4 (also free) under Acquisition > Traffic Acquisition. This takes 12 minutes.

A hair salon in Chicago told me she couldn’t tell if MSAN was working because “the phone just rings.” I set up UTM tags linked to a call tracking number from CallRail ($30/month starter plan). Within two weeks, she saw that 8 out of 17 calls from the MSAN campaign converted to bookings. She’d been assuming the calls were from Google Ads. They weren’t.

**Step 4: Look at assisted conversions, not just last-click**

Microsoft Audience Network is often a top-of-funnel channel. Someone sees your ad while reading MSN news, doesn’t click, then Googles your business name a week later. In most reporting systems, Google gets the credit for the conversion and MSAN looks pointless. View-through conversions (Microsoft Ads > Reports > View-Through Conversions) will show you how many people saw your ad and later converted. This won’t be perfect, but it’s better than ignoring the channel entirely. For one Denver-based pet groomer, view-through conversions added 40% more attributed revenue than last-click alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I own a coffee shop in Austin. My customers don't use Bing. Why would I advertise on Microsoft’s network?**

Your customers probably aren’t typing “coffee near me” into Bing. But they are checking Outlook on their phones, reading MSN weather, scrolling Yahoo News, and using apps that are part of Microsoft’s Audience Network. That’s where your ad shows up. The targeting isn’t based on search queries — it’s based on profile data Microsoft gathers from Outlook, LinkedIn, and Bing search history. If you target “coffee drinkers, within 2 miles, ages 25-50,” your ad will show to people who match that profile while they’re doing something else online. The same way a billboard catches someone on their way to work.

**Q: How is this different from Google Display Network? Isn't it the same thing?**

They’re structurally different. Google Display Network shows your ads on millions of websites and apps, but the targeting is mostly based on cookies and browsing behavior — which is getting less reliable. Microsoft Audience Network relies more on user profile data (age, location, income, job title) from Microsoft accounts and LinkedIn. For a local business, that means you can target “homeowners in ZIP code 78701” with higher accuracy than Google can right now. I’ve tested both for a Denver-based cleaning service. Google Display spent $500 over two months and brought in one call. Microsoft Audience spent $400 and brought in six booked cleanings. Your results will vary, but the profile-based targeting is a real advantage.

**Q: What’s the minimum budget I should start with?**

Start with $300 for one month. No daily cap — set a monthly budget. If you can’t get at least $600 in measurable revenue (tracked through a promo code or call tracking) in that first month, turn it off and try something else. Do not start with $50 a month and expect to learn anything. The sample size is too small, the algorithm won’t optimize, and you’ll walk away thinking it doesn’t work when really you starved the machine.

**Q: Can I target people within a small radius — like a 1-mile area?**

Yes, but the results depend on population density. If you’re in Manhattan, one mile covers enough people to get impressions. If you’re in rural Texas, one mile might get you seven people. I’ve seen a business in Nashville use a 1.5-mile radius to great effect for a lunch special — they were targeting office workers. A landscaping company outside Phoenix used an 8-mile radius and still struggled to find enough volume. If your area is low-density, increase the radius or combine it with a demographic (high-income homeowners) to avoid wasting budget on irrelevant users.

**Q: How do I know if MSAN is actually driving people or if I’m just wasting money?**

Track three things: unique promo codes, call tracking (even a free Google Voice number you rotate for the campaign), and view-through conversions inside Microsoft Ads. If you can’t tie even 5 conversions to the campaign in the first month, the targeting or creative needs fixing — not the platform. Also check your Google Analytics to see if traffic from “microsoft.com” or “msn.com” is showing up in the referral reports. Sometimes the impressions come through but don’t click — but they still lead to a search later.

**Q: Does this work for e-commerce businesses, or just local services?**

It works better for local services than for e-commerce, but I’ve seen exceptions. A small home goods brand in Portland used MSAN to target “people who recently moved” based on change of address data — they sold throw pillows and organizers. Their ROAS was 3.1x over three months. A skincare brand in California tried it and got 1.4x ROAS — not bad, but not better than their Instagram ads. If you’re a direct-to-consumer brand with fast shipping and a low return rate, test it at $500/month. If you sell products with a two-week delivery window and lots of returns, the math gets tight fast.
