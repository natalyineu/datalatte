### Why Google Ads Work for HVAC Companies

When your local dry cleaner’s AC breaks at 9 AM on a 98°F day, they don’t search "best HVAC companies." They search "emergency AC repair near me." That’s why HVAC Google Ads need to target hyper-local, urgent intent.

Start by auditing your current keywords. If you’re only bidding on broad terms like "HVAC services," you’re competing with 10+ local businesses for the same $30–$50 CPC. Instead, use keyword clusters like:
- "AC repair [City Name] 24/7"
- "Furnace repair urgent [Zip Code]"
- "HVAC emergency service [Town Name]"

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running the Same Ads All Year

A client in Austin, Texas—let's call them Lone Star HVAC—called me in a panic last October. They had been spending $4,200 a month on Google Ads since April. Summer was great: AC repair keywords were driving leads at $18 each, and their installers were slammed. Then September hit, and their cost per lead shot to $67. In October, it hit $94. They were still running the same "emergency AC repair Austin" keywords.

Here's what happened: Austin cools down in fall. Nobody needed AC repairs. But Lone Star had paused their furnace and heater keywords back in March because "nobody needs heat in Texas, right?" Wrong. October nights in Austin drop to the mid-50s. People turn on their furnaces, and furnaces that haven't been touched since last March often break.

The fix was obvious but painful: I paused 70% of their AC keywords, launched "furnace repair Austin" and "heater maintenance Round Rock" campaigns, and cut their daily budget from $140 to $60. I also added negative keywords like "portable AC" and "window unit" because people searching those aren't calling for service.

Outcome: Within two weeks, their cost per lead dropped from $94 to $24. They booked $8,300 in November heating repairs. Their total spend for that month was $1,800. That's a 4.6x return on ad spend, compared to the 0.7x they were scraping in September.

If you're in a four-season market, you need at least two campaign structures—one for cooling, one for heating—and you need to flip them at the right time, not when you remember to. Set calendar reminders: switch campaigns when the average high drops below 65°F.

### Mistake #2: Not Using Negative Keywords (And Wasting $1,200 a Month)

A medium-sized HVAC company in Nashville called me after they'd been running ads for eight months. Their CPA was $120 on a $95 average ticket. They were losing money on every click. I audited their search terms report and found the problem in five minutes.

They were bidding on "HVAC repair Nashville" and getting clicks from people searching:
- "How to repair my own HVAC"
- "HVAC repair cost DIY"
- "HVAC repair training near me"
- "Used HVAC parts Nashville"

Twenty-eight percent of their clicks came from DIYers, students, and bargain hunters. That's $1,200 a month flushed on people who were never going to call a professional.

The fix: I added 47 negative keywords in one go: "DIY," "how to," "training," "school," "certification," "used," "parts," "repair cost" (people searching this are price shopping and rarely convert), "free," "manual," "troubleshooting," "leaking water," (they'll just watch a YouTube video), and "no AC" (this one sounds counterintuitive, but in Nashville, "no AC" searches are mostly renters asking landlords for repairs—they don't make the call). I also set up a negative keyword list for competitor brands like "Trane repair" and "Carrier repair"—if you're not a certified dealer for those brands, the customer won't call you.

Outcome: Their CPA dropped to $38 within two weeks. They were spending $800 less per month on wasted clicks. Their booking rate from the remaining clicks actually improved because the traffic was higher intent. Net gain: about $1,800 per month from lower spend plus higher conversion rates.

### Mistake #3: Sending All Traffic to a Generic Homepage

A Denver-area HVAC company—four trucks, solid reputation—was getting 80 clicks a week with a 2% conversion rate. Their landing page was their homepage: "Family-Owned HVAC Services in Denver Since 1998." Generic, no urgency, no phone number above the fold.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: when someone searches "emergency furnace repair Denver" at 10 PM on a Sunday in January, they don't care that you're family-owned. They care if you'll answer the phone and show up tonight.

I built them three simple landing pages on a subdomain using Google Ads' native landing page builder (free, takes an hour):
- /ac-emergency — headline: "AC Broken? We'll Be There in 90 Minutes."
- /furnace-repair — headline: "Furnace Not Working? Same-Day Service Available."
- /hvac-maintenance — headline: "Annual Tune-Up $89. Includes Free Safety Inspection."

Each page had: the phone number in the top right, a one-click-to-call button, three bullet points of what happens next ("1. Call us. 2. We diagnose over the phone. 3. Truck dispatched within 90 minutes."), and a simple form that asks for name, phone, and issue—nothing else.

Outcome: Conversion rate went from 2% to 7.4%. Cost per lead dropped from $45 to $16. That's a 64% reduction. In the first month, they booked $14,200 in service calls from ads that previously had been generating maybe $4,000.

I've seen this mistake at three different clients across the US. The fix is always the same: match the landing page to the search intent. If they searched for "emergency," the page should scream urgency. If they searched for "tune-up," the page should sell value and schedule ease.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Call Tracking and Attribution

A company in Portland, Oregon, was spending $2,800 a month on Google Ads and telling me "it's not working." Their conversion tracking showed 12 form fills a month—about 0.4% conversion rate on clicks. They were ready to shut down the campaign.

I asked to see their call tracking data. They didn't have any. Most HVAC calls come in by phone, not form fills. In fact, for local service businesses, 60-80% of conversions happen over the phone.

I set up Google Ads' own call reporting (included free with Google Ads—no extra tool needed) and added a call-only ad extension. Within two weeks, we found that for every form fill, they were getting 14 phone calls. Most of those calls lasted 3+ minutes, which means the customer was actually interested. Of those calls, about 30% booked service.

The real numbers: they were getting 168 calls per month from ads, not 12 leads. At a 30% booking rate, that's 50 booked jobs. Average ticket: $295. Monthly revenue from ads: $14,750. Spend: $2,800. ROAS: 5.2x.

Without call tracking, they would have killed a profitable campaign because they were only measuring one channel of conversion.

If you're not tracking calls, you're flying blind. Google Ads gives you call reporting for free. Activate it. Also, ask customers "how did you hear about us?" at the point of booking. A simple checkbox on your booking form that says "Google Ad" saves you from guessing.

## Call Tracking and Landing Page Optimization Is Your Secret Weapon

Most HVAC owners I talk to think Google Ads is about getting clicks. It's not. It's about getting calls from people who are ready to spend money. If you're not optimizing for the phone call, you're doing it wrong.

Here's a setup that works across every city I've tested it in—Chicago, Nashville, Denver, Austin:

**1. Use Google Ads call extensions with call reporting.**
Go into your campaign settings. Under "Call Reporting," turn on "Report calls as conversions." Set the minimum call duration to 60 seconds. This filters out pocket dials and wrong numbers. For HVAC, I usually set it to 120 seconds because a real conversation about diagnosing a broken furnace takes at least two minutes. Google counts that as a conversion. Now your dashboard shows actual leads, not just form fills.

**2. Create call-only ads for mobile.**
Sixty-five percent of HVAC searches happen on mobile. When someone in Chicago searches "furnace repair near me" on their phone at 6 AM in January, they don't want to fill out a form. They want to press a button and hear a human voice. A call-only ad removes the middle step. The user clicks the ad, their phone dials your number, and you answer.

I tested this with a client in Chicago. Their call-only ad had a 4.8% click-to-call rate versus their regular ad's 1.9% click-to-landing-page rate. They went from 22 calls a week to 67. Revenue from ads jumped from $3,400 a week to $9,100. The setup took 20 minutes.

**3. Use Google Ads' location extensions with your actual address.**
This sounds basic, but I'm constantly surprised by how many HVAC companies skip it. Location extensions show your address, phone number, and a "Get Directions" button under your ad. For local searches, this increases click-through rate by 10-15%. It also signals to Google that your business is physically relevant to the searcher, which can lower your cost per click.

I tested this with a small HVAC company in Nashville. Adding the location extension dropped their average CPC from $22 to $17 and their CTR went from 3.1% to 4.6%. That's not a coincidence. Google's algorithm rewards ads that show local relevance.

**4. Set up call tracking at the keyword level.**
If you use a tool like CallRail, Nimbata, or even Google Voice (free for one number), you can assign different phone numbers to different ad groups. This tells you exactly which keywords drive calls. For example, "AC repair Chicago" might generate 30 calls a week with a 40% booking rate, while "AC tune-up Chicago" generates 50 calls but only a 15% booking rate. That means you should shift budget toward "repair" keywords.

You don't need a fancy tool for this. A simple spreadsheet tracking which phone number gets the most calls will give you 80% of the insight for 0% of the cost. Just rotate two numbers: one for summer campaigns, one for winter campaigns.

## Integrating HVAC Google Ads with Your Local Service Tools

You can't run Google Ads in a vacuum. The ad gets the call. What happens after that determines if you get the job. Here's how to connect ads to your actual business operations.

**Yelp Integration**
Yelp is a double-edged sword for HVAC companies. It drives calls, but it's expensive and their sales team will call you weekly. If you already have a Yelp Business page (and most HVAC companies do because customers leave reviews there), connect your Google Ads to your Yelp profile using the location extension feature. This shows your Yelp rating (if it's 4 stars or above) directly in your ad. I've seen a 12% increase in CTR from this alone.

Do not run Yelp ads as a replacement for Google Ads. Yelp's cost per lead for HVAC in the US averages $45-75. Google Ads, optimized correctly, can get you to $15-30. Yelp is a supplement, not a solution.

**Square and ServiceTitan Integration**
If you use Square Appointments or ServiceTitan for scheduling, set up a Google Ads conversion action that tracks when someone books an appointment through your scheduling link. You can do this with Google Ads' "Website" conversion tracking. Paste the tracking tag into your scheduling page. Now you can see exactly which clicks turned into booked appointments, not just form fills.

A client in Nashville using ServiceTitan saw that 42% of their Google Ads leads that started a booking actually completed it. The other 58% dropped off. They added a "call us instead" button on the booking page and those drop-offs started calling directly. Booking completion rate went to 68%.

**Mailchimp or Constant Contact for Retargeting**
Not every call turns into a job. People call, ask about pricing, then say "let me think about it." They forget. You can retarget them using a Customer Match list in Google Ads.

Export your contact list from Mailchimp or Constant Contact (if they called but didn't book). Upload it to Google Ads as a customer match list. Create a retargeting campaign with a specific ad: "Still need HVAC service? Call us before 5 PM for same-day availability." Use a $10 daily budget. I've seen retargeting bring back 8-12% of lost leads.

**Google Business Profile (GBP)**
This isn't a tool you buy, but it's the most important free integration. Your GBP listing shows up in local search results alongside your ads. If your GBP listing has bad photos, outdated hours, or unresolved reviews, your ads will underperform.

I worked with a Denver HVAC company that was spending $3,200 a month on ads but getting a 1.8% CTR. I looked at their GBP listing. Three photos. No recent posts. Two negative reviews from 2022 with no responses. We spent a weekend updating: 20 new photos (before-and-after of installs), weekly posts ("Tip: Change your filter every 30 days."), responses to every review (thank you for positive, solve the issue for negative). CTR on their ads went from 1.8% to 3.4% in three weeks. Google was rewarding the engagement.

**Booksy or Similar Booking Platforms**
Some HVAC companies use Booksy or similar booking tools. If you do, integrate it with Google Ads' conversion measurement. Booksy has a Google Ads integration that tracks when someone books through their platform. This gives you double attribution: the ad click and the booking. It's not perfect, but it's better than nothing.

## Measuring the True ROI Beyond the Last Click

Here's where most HVAC owners get fooled. They look at Google Ads' dashboard and see a cost per lead of $28. They think that's the final number. It's not.

**You need to account for lifetime value, not just the first visit.**

A typical HVAC service call in the US averages $180-350 for a repair. But that customer's lifetime value is much higher. If they book you for a repair, they'll likely call you for:
- The same repair next season ($180-350)
- Annual tune-ups ($89-150 each)
- A full system replacement in 3-7 years ($3,500-8,000)
- Referrals to neighbors and friends (priceless)

I tracked this with a Nashville client. Their first-call average was $205. But of customers who called from Google Ads, 31% booked a service contract within 12 months. Those contracts paid $89 per visit, twice a year. And 8% of those customers eventually booked a full system replacement at $4,200 average.

When you calculate the lifetime value of a Google Ads lead for this client, it's roughly $680 per customer, not $205. That changes the math completely. They could afford a $50 cost per lead and still be profitable.

**Track offline conversions using call recordings.**
Google Ads lets you upload offline conversion data. If you use call recording (check if your state allows it), you can listen to calls and tag which ones resulted in a booked job. Upload that data to Google Ads. Now the algorithm learns which keywords and ads produce real appointments, not just phone calls.

This takes maybe 30 minutes a week. Listen to 10-15 calls, mark them as booked or not. Upload the data. Google Ads will start optimizing toward booked jobs instead of just calls.

**Use Google Analytics with Enhanced Ecommerce (sort of).**
For HVAC, Enhanced Ecommerce won't work because you're not selling products online. But you can set up Google Analytics goals for "Phone Call Clicked" and "Form Submitted." Then build a segment that shows users who completed these goals. See which ad groups, keywords, and locations drive those users. This tells you where to invest more budget.

In Denver, this analysis showed that "furnace repair" keywords generated 3x more booked jobs than "AC repair" keywords, despite having the same cost per lead. The client shifted 60% of their budget to furnace keywords in winter and saw a 35% increase in booked revenue.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads per month for my HVAC business?**

Start with $500-$1,000 per month for a single service area. If you cover multiple cities or regions, scale to $1,500-$3,000. Any less than $500 and you won't get enough data to optimize. In smaller towns, $500 can work fine. In competitive markets like Chicago or NYC, expect to pay $25-40 per click for emergency keywords, so $1,000 might only get you 25-40 clicks—enough for testing but not scaling.

**Q: How long before I see results from Google Ads?**

Calls can start within 24 hours of launching. But meaningful data—knowing which keywords work, which ad copy converts, what your real cost per lead is—takes about 2 weeks. Do not judge performance on day one. Wait until you have at least 50 clicks and 5 conversions before making budget decisions.

**Q: Should I run ads year-round or just during peak seasons?**

Both, but with separate strategies. Summer (AC) and winter (heating) deserve 70% of your budget. The shoulder seasons (spring, fall) deserve 30% for maintenance and tune-up campaigns. If you stop ads completely in March, you lose the "tune-up before summer" traffic. That's a mistake I see all the time.

**Q: What's the #1 thing I should do differently from competitors?**

Track calls, not just form fills. Most HVAC companies track form submissions and ignore phone calls. If you're the one tracking calls, you'll have 3x more conversion data than your competitors. That means Google's algorithm will optimize your campaigns better.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads myself or do I need a specialist?**

You can run them yourself if you have 5-10 hours per week to learn and manage. Google's interface is not that complicated. The tricky part is keyword research, negative keyword management, and call tracking setup. If you don't have the time, hire someone who knows local service ads specifically—not a generalist agency that treats HVAC like ecommerce.

**Q: What's the biggest mistake HVAC owners make with Google Ads?**

Not using negative keywords. I said it earlier, but it's worth repeating. I see $500-1,500 per month in wasted spend from clients who didn't exclude DIYs, students, and price shoppers. The fix takes 30 minutes.
