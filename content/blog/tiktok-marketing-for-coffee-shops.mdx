**Step 1: Set Up Your Account**

Before you start creating content, you need to set up your account. This includes adding a profile picture, bio, and contact information. Your profile picture should be your logo, and your bio should include a brief description of your coffee shop and any relevant keywords.

**Tip:** Use a high-quality profile picture that represents your brand.

**Step 2: Create Engaging Content**

Content is king on TikTok, and you need to create content that engages your audience. This includes short videos, photos, and even live streams. When creating content, keep the following tips in mind:

* Use high-quality visuals and audio.
* Keep your videos short and sweet (15-60 seconds).
* Use music and sound effects to enhance the viewing experience.
* Use captions and subtitles to make your content more accessible.

**Warning:** Don't overpost! TikTok users are bombarded with content, so make sure you're posting at the right times to maximize engagement.

**Step 3: Leverage Hashtags**

Hashtags are a great way to increase the visibility of your content on TikTok. When using hashtags, keep the following tips in mind:

* Use relevant hashtags that are specific to your coffee shop and the type of content you're posting.
* Use a mix of popular and niche hashtags to reach a wider audience.
* Don't overdo it! Using too many hashtags can look spammy.

**Example:** Use hashtags like #coffee, #coffee shop, #coffeeperks, or #coffeeoftheday to reach a wider audience.

**Step 4: Collaborate with Influencers**

Influencers can help you reach a wider audience on TikTok. When collaborating with influencers, keep the following tips in mind:

* Choose influencers who align with your brand values and target audience.
* Collaborate on content that is relevant to your coffee shop and the influencer's audience.
* Make sure you're clear about your brand's expectations and goals.

**Coffee:** DataLatte recommends collaborating with local influencers to create content that showcases your coffee shop and the local community.

**Step 5: Run a TikTok Ad Campaign**

TikTok ads can help you reach a wider audience and drive sales. When running a TikTok ad campaign, keep the following tips in mind:

* Set clear goals and objectives for your ad campaign.
* Use high-quality visuals and audio to create engaging ads.
* Target your ads to specific demographics, interests, and behaviors.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Posting the Same Thing Every Day Until You Go Insane

I worked with a coffee shop in Austin called Brew & Battery. The owner, Sarah, heard that "TikTok rewards consistency." So she posted the exact same format every single day: a 10-second shot of someone pouring latte art. Same angle, same lighting, same background music. For 47 days straight.

Her account got 89 followers. Three of them were her parents and her ex-boyfriend who felt bad for her.

The problem isn't consistency. The problem is that Sarah treated TikTok like a Instagram grid post schedule from 2016. She was making content for the algorithm, not for humans. Nobody needs to see a 47th latte pour unless you're doing something different with it.

**The fix:** Sarah and I sat down and looked at what was actually happening in her shop. She had a pastry supplier who kept showing up late, a barista who could free-pour a perfect flamingo, and a regular who came in every Tuesday and ordered a "surprise me." She started filming those stories instead. The latte art became one video a week, not seven.

**The outcome:** In eight weeks, she hit 3,200 followers and $1,800 in attributed sales from people who walked in saying "I saw that video about the flamingo." She posts four times a week now. Two are planned. Two are whatever happened that day.

### Mistake #2: Trying to Go Viral Instead of Making One Sale

A pet groomer in Nashville messaged me after spending $500 on TikTok ads. She wanted to know why she got 340,000 views but zero bookings. Zero. Not a single person walked through her door.

Here's what happened: She filmed a 15-second video of a poodle getting a blow-dry. It was cute. It got shared. The comments were all "omg so fluffy" and "this made my day." But not one comment said "where are you located?" Because nowhere in the video did she say she was in Nashville. Her bio just said "Professional Pet Grooming" with no city. Her link wasn't clickable.

She optimized for views. She got views. That's the only thing she got.

**The fix:** We changed the strategy to one thing: every video needed to answer "where is this and can I book it?" We added a pin on her profile with the exact address. Every video caption started with "Nashville — East Side." She started including a CTA in the video itself: "Tag your friend who needs a spring groom before their HOA fine hits."

**The outcome:** Next $500 ad spend brought in $3,800 in bookings over three weeks. Same budget, completely different math. The views dropped to 28,000. The revenue went up 8x. I'll take that trade every time.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the People Who Already Know You

A hair salon owner in Portland told me she was "all in on TikTok" and spending 12 hours a week filming and editing. Her account was growing. She was getting 300–500 new views per video. But her appointment book was still half empty on Thursdays.

I asked her one question: "Do your existing clients know you're on TikTok?"

Silence. Then: "Well, I post about it on my personal Facebook."

She had 400 active clients in her Square system with phone numbers and emails. She had sent exactly zero texts or emails telling them to follow her. She had no signage in the salon. Her booking confirmation page didn't mention TikTok.

**The fix:** We set up a two-week promotion. She texted her entire client list through Square: "Follow us on TikTok this week and mention this text for $10 off your next color service." She printed a small stand-up sign for the front desk. She added a "Follow us on TikTok" button to her booking confirmation email in Mailchimp.

**The outcome:** She gained 230 followers in two weeks — most of them people who had already paid her money before. Those clients booked faster, tipped higher, and started sending their friends. The Thursday slot filled up in three days. Cost: $0 in ad spend, maybe $12 for the sign.

## When Your TikTok Strategy Actually Needs a Budget

Most guides tell you TikTok is free. That's true if you have unlimited time and a supernatural ability to create viral content on command. Most of us don't.

Here's what actually worked for a small fitness studio in Denver called Sweatbird. The owner, Marcus, was spending $800/month on Google Ads and getting decent results — about $2,400 in new client revenue. He wanted to try TikTok but didn't want to throw money at another platform blind.

We tested a $300/month TikTok ad budget for 60 days. Here's the specific setup that worked:

**Spark Ads only.** We didn't run standard in-feed ads. We took his three best organic posts (the ones with the highest watch time and share rate) and boosted them as Spark Ads. This means the video still looks like a regular post — it shows your profile name, not a brand tag. People trust it more.

**Location targeting with a 5-mile radius.** Denver is big. We didn't need people from Aurora driving 40 minutes for a 6 AM class. We narrowed it to the five ZIP codes surrounding his studio.

**One CTA per video.** Marcus's mistake early on was trying to do too much. "Follow for tips, book a free class, share with a friend" — that's three asks. Nobody does three things. We picked one: "Book a free class" with a link in bio.

**The numbers:** $300/month in TikTok ads brought in $1,100 in new client revenue after 60 days. That's a 3.6x return. Not earth-shattering. But compared to his Google Ads return of 3x, it was slightly better — and the TikTok clients were younger, stayed longer, and referred more friends.

One catch: TikTok ads manager is terrible for reporting. It will show you rosy numbers that don't match reality. I've seen it claim a 12x ROAS on a campaign that actually generated two calls and zero sales. Cross-reference everything with your actual booking system. For Marcus, we used his Square appointment data. If it wasn't booked through Square, it didn't count.

## Repurposing TikTok Content Into Your Other Channels

Here's where most coffee shops and salons leave money on the table. They film a TikTok, post it, and move on. That video could be working for you on three other platforms.

A coffee shop in Chicago called Daily Grind was posting TikTok content and doing nothing else with it. The owner, Jenna, was spending about four hours a week filming and editing. I asked her: "What happens to that video after you post it?"

"Nothing. It just sits there."

**Here's the repurposing system we built:**

**Instagram Reels.** This is the easiest win. Download your TikTok video without the watermark (there are free tools for this — I use Snaptik, not sponsored, I just don't like paying for things that should be free). Upload it to Instagram Reels. Change the caption slightly to fit Instagram's audience — less hashtag noise, more location focus. Daily Grind gained 400 Instagram followers in the first month without filming anything new.

**YouTube Shorts.** Yes, YouTube has Shorts now. Yes, people actually watch them. We uploaded the same content there and added a link to Jenna's website in the description. It drove about 50 clicks per week — not huge, but those clicks were from people actively searching for coffee in Chicago, not passively scrolling.

**Email newsletters.** This one surprised Jenna the most. She uses Mailchimp for a weekly newsletter to about 1,200 subscribers. We started embedding one TikTok video per email — just a screenshot with a play button overlay that links to the video. The open rate stayed the same, but the click rate on that one link was 8%, compared to her usual 2-3% on other links.

**Booking confirmation pages.** This is the one nobody does. Jenna uses Square for bookings. We added a line in the confirmation email: "While you wait, here's a 30-second video of our barista pulling the perfect shot." It's a small touch. But 12% of people who clicked that link ended up adding a pastry or an extra drink to their order — tracked through Square's upsell feature.

**The time math:** Jenna went from 4 hours of filming for one platform to 4.5 hours of filming for four platforms. The extra 30 minutes was downloading, reformatting captions, and scheduling. Her reach increased by about 3x without increasing her content budget.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I don't have time to make TikTok videos. I'm running a business. How much time does this actually take?**

Two hours a week, maximum. That's one hour to film and one hour to edit and schedule. If you're spending more than that, you're overproducing. Film three to four videos in one session. Use the same background, the same lighting, and batch your content. Then use a scheduling tool like Later or Buffer to spread them out over the week. I've seen business owners try to film daily and burn out in three weeks. Batch filming works. Do that instead.

**Q: I tried TikTok ads and lost money. What did I do wrong?**

You probably didn't track the right thing. TikTok will show you "cost per result" that looks good, but the result is usually a view or a profile visit — not a booking. Link your ad to a specific landing page with a unique phone number or booking code. If you're using Booksy or Square, create a promo code that only appears in that TikTok ad campaign. If nobody uses the code, the ad didn't work, no matter what the dashboard says.

**Q: What if I get negative comments or trolls on my videos?**

Ignore them or delete them. You own the comment section. A troll who comments "your coffee is overpriced" on a video that got 15,000 views is annoying but not a crisis. What matters is whether the video brought in customers. Reply to genuine questions, thank people who tag their friends, and delete anything that's rude or off-topic. The algorithm doesn't penalize you for deleting comments. I've had clients worry about this way too much. Nobody is checking your comment ratio.

**Q: Should I use a personal account or a business account?**

Business account. Here's why: you get analytics, you get the link-in-bio feature, you can run ads, and you can see which videos are actually driving profile visits. The only downside is the suggested music library has some restrictions, but that barely matters for local service businesses. I've had clients refuse to switch to a business account because they "like the aesthetic better." That's fine if you want to guess whether your content is working.

**Q: Do I need expensive equipment? I heard you need a ring light and a microphone.**

No. Film on your phone. Use natural light or overhead lighting. Do not buy a ring light until you've posted 20 videos and proven you'll stick with it. I've watched business owners spend $200 on equipment, film three videos, and quit. Your phone camera from 2021 or later is good enough. The content matters more than the production quality. A video of a barista messing up an order and laughing about it will outperform a professionally lit slow-motion pour shot every time.

**Q: How often should I post if I want to see results without it taking over my life?**

Four times a week is the sweet spot for most local businesses. Three is okay if you're consistent. Two is maintenance mode. One is not enough to build momentum. Post on Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, and Saturday — those are the highest-engagement days for local service content. Monday is dead. Sunday is hit or miss. Stick to four times a week for 90 days, then evaluate. If you can't commit to that, don't start. You'll get disappointed and blame the platform.
