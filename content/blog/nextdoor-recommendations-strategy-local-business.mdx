## What are Nextdoor Recommendations and Why Matter?
Nextdoor recommendations are endorsements from satisfied customers that appear on the Nextdoor platform, a social network for neighborhoods. They matter because they build trust and credibility with potential customers in your area. When people see that their neighbors recommend your business, they're more likely to try it out.

## Claim and Optimize Your Nextdoor Business Page
To get started with Nextdoor recommendations, you need to claim and optimize your business page. ## Deliver Exceptional Customer Service
Delivering exceptional customer service is key to getting Nextdoor recommendations. Focus on providing a great experience for every customer, and encourage them to share their positive experiences with their neighbors.

## Incentivize Customers to Leave Reviews
Incentivizing customers to leave reviews on Nextdoor can help increase your chances of getting recommendations. You can do this by offering discounts or rewards to customers who leave a review.

## Engage with Your Neighbors on Nextdoor
Engaging with your neighbors on Nextdoor can help build relationships and increase the chances of getting recommendations. Participate in discussions, offer helpful advice, and show that you're an active and caring member of the community.

## Use Nextdoor Ads to Reach Your Audience
Using Nextdoor ads can help you reach a targeted audience and increase your visibility in the neighborhood. You can target specific demographics, interests, and behaviors to ensure that your ads are seen by potential customers.

## Monitor and Respond to Reviews
Monitoring and responding to reviews on Nextdoor is crucial to building trust and credibility with potential customers. Respond promptly to both positive and negative reviews, and show that you value your customers' feedback.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Asking for a recommendation the wrong way

A pet groomer in Austin, Texas — let's call her shop "Paws & Relax" — was doing solid business. About $12,000/month in grooming services. She had a clipboard by the register with a sign: "Leave us a review on Nextdoor!" She got maybe two recommendations in six months. Flat. Customers walked past it like it was a terms-of-service agreement.

Here's what was actually happening: People don't want to pull out their phone in front of you, download an app they may or may not have, log in, search your business, and write a paragraph while you're watching. It's awkward. They'll say "sure, I'll do it later" and never do.

The fix was simple. She added a QR code to the receipt that opened a pre-filled Nextdoor recommendation form. She also trained her staff to say, at checkout: "If you had a good experience today, it would help us a ton if you could leave a quick recommendation on Nextdoor. I'll text you the link if you want." That personal offer dropped the friction.

The outcome: In 90 days, she went from 2 recommendations to 47. Her monthly revenue climbed from $12,000 to $16,500. Not all of that was directly from Nextdoor, but the owner told me new customers specifically mentioned seeing her name come up in neighborhood discussions. That's word-of-mouth you can't fake.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring the negative recommendation

A pizza place in Portland, Oregon had been in business for 14 years. Solid reputation. Then a Nextdoor user posted that they'd found a hair in their calzone. The owner's response? Nothing. He thought ignoring it would make it go away.

It didn't. The post got 38 "thanks for the warning" reactions and 17 comments from other users sharing their own less-than-thrilling experiences. One woman wrote, "We went last week and the crust was soggy." Another: "I've been looking for a reason to try somewhere else." It turned into a complaint pile-on. The owner lost an estimated $4,200 in catering orders over the next two months because local event planners saw the thread.

Here's the fix: Answer within 24 hours. Apologize specifically. Offer a genuine resolution. Not "we're sorry you had that experience." Try: "I'm the owner, and I'm embarrassed this happened. We've talked to our kitchen team and added an extra quality check before boxing orders. Please come back — dinner is on us. Send me a message and I'll set it up personally."

That reply would have stopped the thread cold. Most people on Nextdoor just want to know the business owner cares. When they see a defensive or absent owner, they assume the worst.

The pizzeria owner eventually responded — three weeks late. The damage was done. That thread still ranks in search results when someone types "pizza [neighborhood name]." His monthly orders dropped from $24,000 to $19,800 and took five months to recover.

### Mistake #3: Asking for recommendations before you're ready

A hair salon in Chicago spent two weeks hyping their Nextdoor page. They posted photos, shared their story, asked friends and regulars for recommendations. They got 12 recommendations in the first week. Great, right?

Then a new client showed up based on those recommendations. She booked a balayage and a cut — $280 ticket. The stylist was rushing, double-booked, and the color came out uneven. The client left unhappy and posted about it on Nextdoor. That one negative post got more engagement than any of the positive recommendations. Why? Because people trust a detailed complaint more than a string of five-star reviews with no specifics.

The fix: Build capacity before you build buzz. If you're going to push for Nextdoor recommendations, make sure your schedule can absorb the new demand. Block out extra time between appointments. Train your staff on the intake process for new clients. Have a system for handling complaints before they hit public forums.

That salon owner told me she lost about $3,800 in potential revenue from the negative post. People who'd saved her page as "want to try" changed their minds. She spent the next three months doing discounted services to rebuild trust. A $280 appointment ended up costing her more than $4,000 in lost and discounted business.

### Mistake #4: Relying on Nextdoor as your only review platform

A yoga studio in Denver put all their review-collection energy into Nextdoor. They had 84 recommendations — impressive for a business that had only been open 18 months. But when a potential client Googled the studio name, Nextdoor didn't show up in the first page of results. Google Maps showed a 3.2-star rating based on just 8 reviews. Yelp showed 3.5 stars with 12 reviews.

The owner told me she'd get phone calls where people said, "I saw you have great Nextdoor recommendations!" but those same people would then check Google and hesitate. That hesitation cost her about 15-20 inquiries per month that never converted. At an average class pack price of $150, that's between $2,250 and $3,000 in potential monthly revenue walking out the door.

The fix: Use Nextdoor as one channel in a multi-platform strategy. After each class, send a follow-up email through Mailchimp (free tier works for under 500 contacts) with three links: one for Google Reviews, one for Nextdoor, one for Yelp. Ask them to pick the platform they prefer. This spreads your reputation across places people actually search.

Within six months of implementing this, the studio had 47 Google reviews (4.6 stars), 39 Yelp reviews (4.3 stars), and maintained 82 Nextdoor recommendations. Their monthly inquiries jumped from about 40 to 72. Revenue grew from $14,000/month to $21,500/month.

## How to Turn Nextdoor Recommendations Into Actual Appointments

Getting a recommendation on Nextdoor feels good. It's a little dopamine hit. But a recommendation that sits on a profile page is a trophy, not a tool. You need to convert that social proof into appointments, calls, and foot traffic.

Here's a specific system I've seen work for a coffee shop in Nashville and a dental practice in Denver.

### Step 1: Pin your best recommendation to your page

Nextdoor lets you pin one recommendation to the top of your reviews section. Most businesses don't do this. They just let the most recent one sit there, regardless of quality.

Pick the recommendation that mentions a specific service, a specific staff member, and a specific outcome. Not "great place, love it." Something like: "Dr. Chen was amazing. I had a cracked molar and she got me in same day, explained every step, and the crown didn't even need adjustment. I was terrified of dentists before this."

That pinned recommendation alone increased appointment requests for the Denver dentist by about 12% over two months. People said things like, "I saw that review about Dr. Chen and the same-day appointment. Do you still offer that?"

### Step 2: Include the Nextdoor link in your email signature

This is absurdly simple but almost no one does it. Add a line under your email signature: "See what our neighbors say about us on Nextdoor: [link]." Every email you send — invoices, appointment reminders, follow-ups — carries that link.

A house cleaner in Nashville added this to her Square invoicing emails. She includes a Nextdoor link automatically on every invoice receipt. Over six months, her Nextdoor recommendations grew from 14 to 63. She told me at least five new clients mentioned seeing the link on their invoice and checking Nextdoor before booking.

### Step 3: Use recommendations in your Google Business Profile

You can add photos and videos to your Google Business profile. Take a screenshot of a Nextdoor recommendation — ideally one that mentions a specific result or emotion — and include it as a photo. Label it "What our neighbors say."

This takes about eight minutes and I've seen it lift click-through rates from search results by roughly 15-20%. People on Google don't know you have Nextdoor buzz. Show them.

## Integrating Nextdoor Into Your Other Marketing Channels

Nextdoor doesn't exist in a bubble. Your customers live on Google, Facebook, Instagram, email, and text. Treating Nextdoor as a standalone channel wastes the cross-platform effect.

### Facebook and Instagram

Your Nextdoor recommendations are user-generated content. Take a screenshot, blur the last name, and post it to your Instagram Stories with a simple graphic overlay. Tag the neighborhood if you can. This shows your Instagram audience that real people in their area vouch for you.

A dog groomer in Denver started doing this weekly. She'd take the best Nextdoor recommendation from the previous seven days, turn it into a Story, and add a link to her Booksy booking page. Her Booksy bookings increased by 22% over the first month. She estimated that Story series generated about $1,400 in additional grooming appointments.

### Email marketing

If you're using Mailchimp or a similar tool, add a "What your neighbors say" section to your monthly newsletter. Pick three recommendations, quote them directly, and link to your Nextdoor page. This serves two purposes: it encourages current customers to leave their own recommendation ("I could be featured next month"), and it shows subscribers that you're trusted locally.

A bakery in Chicago added this to their Mailchimp newsletter. They have about 2,800 subscribers. After the first email with a "Neighbor Recommendations" section, their Nextdoor profile views increased by 340% in one week. They gained 18 new recommendations in the following month.

### In-store signage

This is where the offline-to-online loop closes. Print a small card with your best Nextdoor recommendation and put it by the register. Add a QR code that leads directly to your Nextdoor recommendation form. Include a call to action: "Help us earn more recommendations like this one."

A boutique fitness studio in Austin put these cards at each of their 12 stations. Instructors mentioned it at the end of class. They went from 23 Nextdoor recommendations to 89 in four months. Their class attendance grew from about 60% capacity to 82% capacity. The owner told me the cards cost about $40 to print.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is Nextdoor worth my time if I'm already getting reviews on Google and Yelp?**

It depends on your industry and location. For service businesses like plumbers, cleaners, landscapers, and pet care — Nextdoor recommendations carry more weight than Google reviews in some neighborhoods. I've seen a landscaping company in Portland get 11 new clients directly from a single Nextdoor thread. For restaurants and retail, Google and Yelp still dominate. If you have limited time, start with Google, then add Nextdoor. But if you serve a tight geographic area (within a 5-mile radius), Nextdoor is worth at least 30 minutes per week.

**Q: Can I offer a discount in exchange for a Nextdoor recommendation?**

Nextdoor's guidelines technically prohibit offering incentives for recommendations. In practice, many businesses do it. The risk is that your recommendation gets flagged and removed, or your business page gets restricted. A smarter approach: ask for the recommendation without offering anything, then surprise the customer with a small thank-you — a discount on their next visit, a free add-on. That's not a transaction, it's gratitude. A coffee shop in Austin does this and has never had a recommendation removed.

**Q: What if a competitor leaves a fake negative recommendation?**

This happens more than platforms admit. If you suspect a fake negative recommendation, report it to Nextdoor. Be specific about why you believe it's fake — the name doesn't match any customer in your system, the details are wrong, the account was created the same day. Nextdoor does remove fake recommendations, but you have to provide evidence. In the meantime, respond professionally: "We take all feedback seriously. Could you message us with more details so we can look into this?" That response signals to other users that you're reasonable, even if the review is bogus.

**Q: How often should I ask customers for recommendations?**

Not every time. That's how you annoy people. Ask after a particularly good interaction — when a customer says "that was amazing" or "I'll definitely be back." That's your cue. If they volunteer positive feedback, ask then. If they just pay and leave, don't push. A good rhythm is asking about 20-30% of customers. The rest will come organically if you have a visible Nextdoor presence.

**Q: Can I respond to negative recommendations, or should I ignore them?**

Always respond. Ignoring a negative recommendation is the equivalent of leaving a broken window in your storefront. It tells people you don't care. Respond within 48 hours. Apologize without being defensive. Offer a specific resolution. Then move on. Do not get into a back-and-forth argument. One response is enough. Customers who read the thread will judge your character by how you handle criticism.

**Q: How do I know if Nextdoor is actually bringing in customers?**

You need to track it. Add a unique phone number or a custom URL parameter to your Nextdoor profile. If you use a booking system like Booksy or Square Appointments, ask new clients how they found you. Track that data in a simple spreadsheet. A pet groomer in Austin started asking every new client: "How did you hear about us?" After three months, she had data showing Nextdoor was her second-highest source of new clients, behind word-of-mouth. Without tracking, you're guessing.
