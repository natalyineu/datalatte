## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting “depression therapy” as a Broad Match Keyword

A therapist in Austin, Texas came to me after running Google Ads for three months. She'd spent $1,200 and gotten exactly two phone calls — both from people who lived 45 minutes away and were looking for a different type of therapy. Her mistake? She used “depression therapy” as a broad match keyword. Google showed her ad to anyone searching for depression-related terms, including people looking for free support groups, academic articles, and medication options.

**What went wrong:** Broad match gives Google permission to match your ad to searches that are loosely related. For mental health, this is a disaster. Someone searching “depression medication side effects” is not your client. Someone searching “free depression support groups Austin” is not your client. You're paying for clicks from people who will never book an intake session.

**The fix:** Switch to phrase match and exact match keywords with geographic modifiers. Instead of “depression therapy,” use phrases like “depression counseling in Austin TX” or “anxiety therapist Downtown Austin.” Add negative keywords: “free,” “support group,” “cure,” “medication,” “online,” “articles,” “symptoms of.” Build a list of 50–100 negative keywords before you launch a single ad.

**The outcome:** After restructuring her campaign, that Austin therapist was spending $800/month and booking 12–15 new intakes per month. Her cost per lead dropped from roughly $48 per call to $14 per scheduled session. She told me she wished she'd fired her previous agency sooner. I ordered a second coffee I did not need. No regrets.

### Mistake #2: Writing Ad Copy That Sounds Like a Therapy Session

A psychologist in Portland, Oregon wanted her ads to “feel warm and inviting.” Her headlines said things like “You Deserve to Feel Better” and “Healing Begins Here.” Google rejected four versions of her ad for “unsubstantiated health claims.” She spent two weeks going back and forth with Google support, missed an entire month of ad visibility, and lost roughly $1,800 in potential revenue.

**What went wrong:** Google's healthcare advertising policies are specific. You cannot promise outcomes. “Feel Better” implies a guaranteed result. “Healing Begins Here” suggests your practice cures conditions. These get flagged — every time. And the appeals process is slow.

**The fix:** Write ad copy that describes what you do and who you serve. “Therapist in Portland, OR — Specializing in Anxiety and Depression. In-Person & Virtual Sessions. Call Today.” “Cognitive Behavioral Therapy in Portland — Accepting New Clients. Weekday and Weekend Appointments.” Direct. Specific. No emotional promises. Let your Google Business Profile reviews speak to outcomes.

**The outcome:** Her ads were approved in 24 hours. She ran consistently for six months, averaging 8–10 new clients per month at $600 in ad spend. Her approval rate went to 100% and she stopped losing sleep over policy violations.

### Mistake #3: Not Setting Up Conversion Tracking and Winging It

A marriage counselor in Denver was spending $1,000/month on Google Ads. He told me the campaign was “okay” — he was getting some calls. When I looked at his account, he had zero conversion actions set up. Not one. He was looking at click counts and hoping for the best.

**What went wrong:** Without conversion tracking, you're flying blind. You don't know which keywords drive actual bookings. You don't know which ads produce calls versus tire-kickers. You can't optimize. This therapist was unknowingly spending 40% of his budget on keywords like “marriage counseling worksheets” — searches from people who wanted free resources, not a therapist.

**The fix:** Set up Google Ads conversion tracking for form submissions and phone calls. Use Google's call tracking number or a service like CallRail so you see which keyword generated each call. If you use a booking system like Booksy, SimplePractice, or TherapyNotes, integrate UTM parameters so you can tie ad clicks to scheduled appointments in your CRM.

**The outcome:** After setting up proper tracking, we saw that “premarital counseling Denver” had a 14% conversion rate (form submissions divided by clicks) and “marriage help near me” had a 2% conversion rate. We shifted 70% of his budget to the high-performing keyword. His cost per client dropped from $85 to $31. Within 60 days, he was booking 20+ new clients per month on the same $1,000 budget.

### Mistake #4: Sending Ad Traffic to a Generic Contact Page

A therapist in Nashville spent $2,200 over three months sending Google Ads traffic to the “Contact Us” page on her website. Her conversion rate was 1.8% — meaning 98 out of every 100 people who clicked her ad left without taking action.

**What went wrong:** A generic contact page asks visitors to fill out a form with “Name, Email, Phone, Message.” That's too many steps and too vague. People who click a mental health ad are often anxious or hesitant. They need clear direction and minimal friction.

**The fix:** Build a dedicated landing page for each ad group. The page should have: one headline matching the ad, a bullet list of what you treat, your location and hours, a simple form asking only for name and phone number, a testimonial from a client (with permission), and a clear call-to-action button that says “Schedule a Free 15-Minute Call.” No navigation menu. No links to other pages. No blog posts.

**The outcome:** Her conversion rate went from 1.8% to 11% in the first month. That's not a typo. She went from 2–3 leads per month to 18–22 leads per month on the same ad spend. Her cost per lead went from $67 to $11. She told me she felt stupid for not doing this sooner. I told her she wasn't stupid — most agencies don't bother to build landing pages for mental health clients because it's more work than just running the ads.

## Building a Landing Page That Converts (Without Feeling Salesy)

Let me be direct: most mental health professionals I work with hate the idea of a “sales page.” It feels icky. I get it. But here's the uncomfortable truth — a well-designed landing page isn't about manipulation. It's about reducing friction for someone who's already struggling to reach out.

Your landing page needs to answer five questions in under 10 seconds:

1. Do you treat what I have?
2. Are you in my area?
3. Can I afford you?
4. Is it easy to book?
5. Will I feel judged?

A therapist in Chicago tested two versions of her landing page. Version A was the standard “About Me” page — her photo, her philosophy, her education. Version B was a simple page with: a headline matching her ad (“Anxiety Therapy in Lincoln Park — Accepting New Clients”), a list of conditions she treated (anxiety, panic disorder, social anxiety, OCD), her insurance partners listed as logos, a single testimonial from a client, and a three-field form asking for name, phone, and best time to call.

Version B generated 7x more booked sessions. Seven times.

If you're using Squarespace or WordPress, you can build a landing page in under two hours. Use a simple template, remove the navigation, and add a clear call-to-action. Integrate a booking calendar from Booksy or Calendly so people can schedule their free consult call without emailing you first.

Every second you make someone wait to book is a second they talk themselves out of calling.

## Tracking What Matters (So You Know This Isn't a Money Pit)

I had a client in Seattle — a group practice with three therapists — who was convinced Google Ads didn't work. They'd spent $3,400 over six months and seen “maybe five or six” new clients. When I looked at their account, they were using Google's default call tracking number — the one Google assigns automatically. That number only shows in the ad. But their website also had their direct office number listed everywhere. Guess which number clients called?

They had no idea how many people actually called from the ad versus from their website directory listing. They were measuring clicks and assuming calls.

Here's what you actually need to track:

**Phone calls:** Use a unique call tracking number from Google Ads (free) or a service like CallRail ($30–50/month). Route ad calls to that number only. Do not list it anywhere else. This gives you exact data on which keywords produce phone leads.

**Form submissions:** Set up Google Ads conversion tracking on your landing page's “thank you” page — the one people see after submitting your form. This tells you which ads and keywords generate form leads.

**Booked appointments:** This is the gold standard. If you use SimplePractice, TherapyNotes, or Practice Better, add UTM parameters to the links in your ads and landing page. Most booking systems let you see referral source data in your dashboard. If you're tech-averse, ask your clients “How did you find us?” and write it down for 60 days. I've seen therapists discover that 40% of their new clients came from one specific keyword they'd been ignoring.

**Revenue per client:** Track this in a Google Sheet. Average session fee × average number of sessions per client ÷ cost per lead gives you your return on ad spend. If your average client books 8 sessions at $150/session, that's $1,200 in revenue. If you're spending $40 to acquire that client, you're making $1,160 per client. That math works.

A therapist in Denver who started tracking all three of these metrics found that her cost per booked client was $37. She increased her ad spend from $500 to $1,500/month and maintained that cost, adding 40 new clients per month. She told me she couldn't believe she'd been guessing for two years.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a solo practitioner. Is $500/month even worth it for Google Ads?**

It depends on your city and what you charge. In Denver, Austin, or Nashville, $500/month should get you 6–10 new client calls if your campaign is set up correctly. If your average session fee is $120, one new client paying for 5 sessions covers your ad spend for the month. Two clients make it profitable. Three make it worth your time. The risk isn't the money — it's running a bad campaign and blaming the channel.

**Q: What if someone clicks my ad and isn't a good fit? Do I have to take them as a client?**

No. You can screen during the initial phone call. Ask what they're looking for, what their schedule is, whether they have insurance you accept. If it's not a fit, refer them elsewhere. Google doesn't require you to accept every lead.

**Q: Can I mention specific conditions like PTSD, anxiety, or depression in my ad copy?**

Yes, but carefully. Google allows you to target keywords for these conditions. Your ad copy can state “Anxiety Therapy in Chicago” or “PTSD Treatment for Veterans.” What you cannot do is promise outcomes — “Stop Panic Attacks in 2 Weeks” or “Cure Your Anxiety.” Stick to describing what you treat and where you're located.

**Q: I tried Google Ads once and got a bunch of spam calls. How do I stop that?**

Two fixes. First, enable Google's click fraud protection in your campaign settings. Second, add negative keywords like “free,” “symptoms,” “test,” “quiz,” “article,” “worksheets,” “support group.” Spam clicks often come from broad match keywords showing to people searching for free resources. Also, set your location targeting to a 10–15 mile radius around your office and exclude zip codes where you don't want clients.

**Q: Should I send people to my Psychology Today profile instead of my website?**

No. Psychology Today profiles don't allow conversion tracking, you can't control the landing page experience, and visitors have to click away to another site to book. You lose control of the journey. Build a simple landing page on your own site.

**Q: Do I need a separate ad for each type of therapy I offer?**

Yes, but start small. Run one ad group for your most common service — anxiety therapy, for example. Once that's profitable, add a second ad group for depression therapy. Do not launch with five ad groups and a $300/day budget. Start with one group, $20/day, and scale what works.
