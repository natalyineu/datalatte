## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Posting What You *Think* Is Cool Instead of What Customers Actually Want to See

A coffee shop in Austin, TX — let's call it "Brew & Beat" — spent three months posting slow-motion pour-over shots set to lo-fi beats. Beautiful videos. Good lighting. The owner told me she was "building the aesthetic." After 12 weeks of consistent posting, she had 43 followers and exactly zero customers who said they found her on TikTok.

I asked to see their comments. The only engagement was from other coffee shop owners saying "nice pour." That's a red flag the size of Texas.

**The fix:** She switched to a simple format: one employee, on camera, saying "Here's the drink you should order if you hate cinnamon but want something cozy." Then she showed the drink being made in 15 seconds. No fancy transitions. No aesthetic pouring.

**The outcome:** That single video hit 28,000 views in four days. She got 17 new customers the following week who specifically mentioned the video. Her monthly TikTok-driven revenue went from zero to roughly $1,400 — from a video that took her eight minutes to film.

**What to do instead:** Ask yourself: would a random person in your city care about this? If the answer is "maybe if they're really into coffee technique," scrap it. Film the answer to one specific question a customer asked that week. That's your content.

### Mistake #2: Trying to Be Funny When Your Brand Isn't

A hair salon owner in Nashville told me she was "doing the trending audio challenges" and couldn't understand why she had 200 views per video. I watched three of them. She was clearly uncomfortable, the timing was off, and the punchlines didn't land. She was trying to be a TikTok comedian when her actual business strength was precision cuts and color matching.

She had spent $500 on a ring light and microphone setup, plus roughly 10 hours per week filming and editing content she hated making. The return: roughly $0 in new bookings.

**The fix:** I told her to stop trying to be funny and start being useful. Film a 30-second clip of correcting a bad at-home dye job. Show the before and after. Say three sentences about why professional color is different from box dye. No joke required.

**The outcome:** First video got 12,000 views. She booked eight new clients from it over two weeks, with an average ticket of $180 each. That's $1,440 in revenue from one video that took 20 minutes to shoot.

**What to do instead:** If your personality doesn't naturally lean toward humor, don't force it. Useful content performs as well as funny content — sometimes better, because people save it. A saved video means the algorithm shows it to more people.

### Mistake #3: Posting Once a Week and Expecting Results

A pet grooming studio in Portland, OR — "The Furry Barber" — posted one video per week for two months. Each video was a well-shot transformation of a matted doodle to a clean, happy dog. Great content. Zero growth. Owner was frustrated because "the algorithm is broken."

I asked: how often are you posting? Once a week. I asked: how many times are you scrolling TikTok yourself? "Probably an hour a day."

Here's the uncomfortable truth: TikTok doesn't care about your quality if you're not posting frequently enough for the algorithm to figure out who should see your content. One video per week on TikTok is like sending one sales email per month and wondering why nobody buys.

She was also spending $200/month on Facebook ads that she hated but felt obligated to run.

**The fix:** I told her to post daily for 30 days. Not perfect videos. Not even good videos. Just consistent, useful content. One dog transformation per day. No intro, no outro, no logo animation. Just the before, the work, the after. She also paused the Facebook ads and redirected that $200 into TikTok's Promote tool for her best-performing organic posts.

**The outcome:** By day 18 of daily posting, she had a video hit 150,000 views. Her TikTok account went from 89 followers to 2,400 in that month. New customer bookings from TikTok generated $3,800 in revenue the following month. She reactivated her Facebook ads later — but only after she had a content library to retarget people from.

**What to do instead:** Post minimum once per day for 60 days before you decide TikTok doesn't work. Batch film on a slow Monday. Film ten dogs in two hours. That's ten days of content. You don't have time? Neither did she. She found it when she saw the revenue.

## How to Actually Track ROI from TikTok (Without Going Insane)

Here's the question every business owner asks me at this point: "Great, I got views. How do I know if they turned into money?"

Most social media guides skip this part because it's boring and difficult. I'm not most guides.

You need three things:

**1. A trackable call-to-action in every video**

Do not post a video that ends with nothing. Not "come visit us." Not "link in bio." Something specific. I tell my clients to use a unique offer code. Example: "Show this video at the register and get $1 off any latte." That code — call it "TIKTOK1" — is now trackable in your Square or Toast POS system.

A bakery in Denver did exactly this. They printed a small sign next to the register that said "Did you see our TikTok? Show the video for 10% off." In the first month, 22 customers used it. Average spend was $8.50. That's $187 in attributable revenue from one month of posting. Doesn't sound huge until you realize each video took 10 minutes to make.

**2. UTM links for anything with a website**

If you're driving people to a website — catering menu, online ordering, gift cards — use Google's Campaign URL Builder. It's free. Create a unique link for TikTok. Paste it in your bio. Check Google Analytics weekly to see how many people clicked it and what they did.

A fitness studio in Chicago did this. They posted a video about their "first class free" offer. The UTM link tracked 43 clicks in one week. Of those, 11 actually booked a class. At $29 per class average, that's $319 in directly attributable revenue. Took them six minutes to set up the tracking.

**3. A simple customer question**

I ask every client to train their front-of-house staff to ask one question: "How did you hear about us?" If the answer is TikTok, write it on the receipt. Tally at the end of the month. Is it perfect? No. Is it better than guessing? Yes.

One coffee shop in San Diego did this and discovered 14% of their monthly customers mentioned TikTok. That gave them the confidence to double down on content instead of pulling the plug when a video underperformed.

**What not to do:** Don't obsess over TikTok's native analytics. The dashboard shows views, likes, and shares. Those are vanity numbers if you can't connect them to dollars. Don't track followers either. I've seen accounts with 50,000 followers and zero sales. I've seen accounts with 800 followers drive $4,000 in monthly revenue. Followers do not pay your rent.

## The Content Calendar That Doesn't Burn You Out (or Waste Your Time)

If you're a solo owner or a small team, you don't have time to "create content." You have time to run your business. The trick is combining the two.

Here's the exact system I use with clients. It works for coffee shops, salons, pet groomers, and fitness studios.

**Batch day: Monday morning (or your slowest day)**

Spend 90 minutes filming everything for the week. That's it. Not per day. Per week. Film 7–10 videos in one session.

**The formula for each video:**

- 5–15 seconds
- One specific thing
- No introduction
- End with the offer or reason to visit

**Examples for a pet groomer:**

- Monday: "This is what happens when you skip brushing for three months." (show matted fur, cut it off, show clean dog)
- Tuesday: "Three signs your dog needs a nail trim — number two will surprise you." (yes it's clickbait, it works)
- Wednesday: Customer brings in their dog. Dog is happy. "This is Gus. He comes every six weeks. Here's why."
- Thursday: "The one tool we use that you shouldn't try at home." (show professional clippers vs drugstore clippers)
- Friday: "Book before Friday and get $5 off any bath service."

**Why this works:** You're not creating content. You're documenting what you already do. The pet groomer already grooms dogs. The coffee shop already makes drinks. The salon already cuts hair. The fitness studio already trains people. You're just showing it.

**The tools I actually use (not sponsored, just tested):**

- **CapCut** (free) — edit on your phone. Trim, add text, add the audio. Takes 90 seconds per video.
- **Canva** (free tier) — make a simple weekly template for text overlays. Same font, same color, so customers recognize your content instantly.
- **Your phone** — and a $15 tripod from Amazon. No ring light necessary unless you're filming in a basement.
- **Mailchimp** — once a month, send a short email to your list: "We're on TikTok now. Here's our three best videos this month. Watch them or don't. Just letting you know." A yoga studio in Seattle did this and got 40 new TikTok followers from one email. Zero additional effort.

**What to avoid:** Don't plan a content calendar more than a week out. TikTok trends change every 48 hours. If you're planning content for next month, you're wasting time on things that will feel stale by the time you post them.

## When to Spend Money on TikTok Ads (and When to Save It)

I get asked about TikTok ads constantly. The answer is simple: don't run ads until you've posted 60 organic videos and proven you can make content people actually watch.

If you can't get 500 organic views on a video, paying TikTok to show it to people is like pouring gas into a car that doesn't have wheels. You'll move, but not far, and you'll be out the money.

**When ads make sense:**

You have a video that organically hit 10,000+ views. It's bringing in customers. You want more of that. Use the Spark Ads feature (it lets you boost organic content from your account — feels native, not like an ad).

A coffee shop in Brooklyn did this. Their organic video about a seasonal lavender latte hit 14,000 views and brought in 30 customers over two weeks. They spent $150 to boost that same video. It reached another 22,000 people and generated $1,100 in additional sales. That's a 7.3x return.

**When ads don't make sense:**

You have three videos, none broke 200 views, and you're considering a $500/month ad budget. Don't. That $500 is better spent on a better sign outside your door or a promotion that brings people in physically. Fix the content first. Then spend.

**Budget guideline I use with clients:**

- If your monthly revenue is under $10,000: spend $0 on TikTok ads. Spend time on organic only.
- If your monthly revenue is $10,000–$30,000: spend $200–$500/month on Spark Ads for your top 3 performing videos each month.
- If your monthly revenue is above $30,000: you can test $1,000/month, but cap it. I've watched too many small businesses burn $3,000/month on TikTok ads with no tracking system in place.

The uncomfortable truth: most small businesses should not run TikTok ads yet. They haven't built the foundation. Post 60 videos. Get 1,000 followers organically. Then add fuel to the fire.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need to dance or use trending audio?**

No. I said that clearly earlier, but people still ask. Trend audio helps the algorithm, but you can just search "viral trending audio" in TikTok and pick one that fits your vibe without dancing. Place the audio under your video. Done. You are not required to dance.

**Q: How much time will this take per week?**

Three hours maximum after you get the hang of it. One batch filming session of 90 minutes. One hour of editing across the week (10 minutes per video, 6 videos). Thirty minutes for responding to comments and engaging with other local businesses. That's a total of 3 hours per week. If you don't have 3 hours, you're spending time somewhere else you could cut.

**Q: What if I don't get views for the first month?**

That is normal. I have not met a single small business owner who posted on TikTok and immediately got views. Every single one of my clients hit a wall for the first 2–4 weeks. The ones who kept going broke through. The ones who quit after 10 videos swore TikTok was a waste of time. It took one coffee shop in Cincinnati six weeks to get their first video over 1,000 views. That video eventually hit 50,000 and drove $2,300 in revenue. Patience is not a strategy. Consistency is.

**Q: Should I make a separate account for my business or use my personal one?**

Separate business account. Always. Do not post about your coffee shop from your personal account where you also post about your cat, your dinner, and your opinions on parking enforcement. TikTok's algorithm needs a clean signal. Give it one. Set up a Business account (free) so you get access to analytics and the Promote button if you want it.

**Q: Can I just repost my Instagram content to TikTok?**

You can. You should not. Instagram Reels and TikTok have different cultures. Instagram content tends to be more polished. TikTok rewards raw, immediate, in-the-moment content. If you repost a heavily produced Instagram Reel to TikTok, it will feel stiff. Take 30 seconds and film something new. Your phone is in your pocket. I promise you can do this.

**Q: What do I do if a customer shows up and says they saw my TikTok?**

Thank them. Ask them what they liked about the video. Write it down. Then film a follow-up video based on that feedback. The algorithm loves when you make content that directly responds to your audience's behavior. A barista in Portland did this — a customer said "I came because you showed that drink with the honey foam." She filmed a "you asked for it" video the next day with a slightly different drink. It outperformed the original by 3x.
