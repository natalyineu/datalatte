## Choosing the Right Platform for Your Business

When deciding between Google Ads and Facebook Ads, consider your business goals, target audience, and budget. If you're a local business owner, Google Ads may be a better choice, as it allows you to target users who are actively searching for products or services like yours. Google Ads also offers more precise targeting options, such as location, language, and device targeting.

On the other hand, Facebook Ads is a great choice for businesses that want to reach a wider audience, including people who may not be actively searching for their products or services. Facebook Ads also offers more advanced targeting options, such as lookalike targeting and custom audiences.

**Tip:** If you're a local business owner, consider using Google Ads to target users who are searching for products or services like yours. This can help you drive more qualified leads and sales.

## Setting Up Your Campaigns

Once you've decided which platform to use, it's time to set up your campaigns. Here are some tips to keep in mind:

* **Set clear goals:** Define what you want to achieve with your ad campaigns, whether it's driving website traffic, generating leads, or boosting sales.
* **Target the right audience:** Use precise targeting options to reach the people who are most likely to be interested in your products or services.
* **Create compelling ad copy:** Write ad copy that resonates with your target audience and encourages them to take action.
* **Optimize for mobile:** Make sure your ads are optimized for mobile devices, as more and more people are using their smartphones to search for and purchase products online.

**Warning:** Don't forget to set clear budget limits and track your ad spend closely to avoid overspending.

## Measuring and Optimizing Your Campaigns

Once your campaigns are up and running, it's time to measure and optimize them for maximum ROI. Here are some tips to keep in mind:

* **Track your metrics:** Use tools like Google Analytics to track your ad metrics, including click-through rate, conversion rate, and return on ad spend.
* **Analyze your data:** Use data to identify areas for improvement and make adjustments to your campaigns accordingly.
* **Test and iterate:** Continuously test and iterate on your ad campaigns to ensure they're performing optimally.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Running Google Ads Without Negative Keywords

**The story:** A hair salon in Austin, Texas called “Locks & Lather” spent $1,200 in a single month on Google Ads. The owner, Jessica, was furious when she got exactly zero booked appointments from it. She called me asking if Google Ads was a scam.

I pulled up her account. The problem was obvious in five minutes: she was showing up for searches like “hair cutting scissors,” “hair products near me,” “how to cut your own hair,” and — this hurt to read — “salon chair for sale.” Google happily spent her budget showing her ad to people who wanted to buy equipment, not book a service.

**The fix:** We built a negative keyword list of 80+ terms. “Scissors,” “shears,” “products,” “for sale,” “Amazon,” “how to,” “DIY,” “at home,” “clippers,” “supplies.” Then we added phrase-match and exact-match keywords for what actual clients would type: “hair salon Austin downtown,” “blowout Austin TX,” “balayage specialist Austin.”

**The outcome:** Same $1,200 monthly budget. The next month she booked 22 appointments directly attributed to Google Ads. At an average ticket of $85 per visit, that’s $1,870 in revenue from the same spend. She was profitable within the first week.

Most guides skip negative keywords because they’re not sexy. But that oversight burns through small business budgets faster than almost anything else. If you run Google Ads and haven’t touched your negative keyword list in 90 days, you’re bleeding money.

### Mistake 2: Running Facebook Ads With a Cold Audience and a “Book Now” Button

**The story:** A pet grooming business in Nashville called “Paws & Claws” put $800 into Facebook Ads targeting dog owners within 10 miles. The ad was a simple photo of a fluffy poodle with the caption “Book your appointment today!”

They got 14 clicks. Zero bookings. The owner told me Facebook was a waste of money.

**What actually happened:** Facebook showed that ad to people scrolling on their phones at 9 p.m. on a Tuesday. Nobody in that audience had ever heard of Paws & Claws. Asking a cold stranger to pull out their credit card and book a $65 grooming appointment from a single photo is like proposing on a first date. It almost never works.

**The fix:** I restructured the campaign into two stages.

Stage one: A $300 “awareness” campaign. We used a video of a golden retriever getting a bath, with the caption “We make bath time easy — no wet floors, no muddy paws.” We asked people to watch the video (30 seconds or more). Cost per view: $0.03. We got about 10,000 video views over two weeks.

Stage two: A $500 “retargeting” campaign. We showed a special offer ad only to people who watched at least 50% of that video. The offer: “First grooming appointment $45 (normally $65).” We also used Facebook’s “Book Now” button that connected directly to their Booksy booking page.

**The outcome:** Stage two generated 38 bookings in 30 days. At $45 each for the first visit (and an estimated 60% retention rate for regular $65 visits), the campaign brought in $1,710 in initial revenue, with recurring revenue projected at $2,470 over the next three months. Total ad spend: $800. Total return from first visits alone: 2.1x.

The mistake most small businesses make is treating Facebook like Google. Google catches people who are already looking. Facebook has to warm them up first. You cannot skip the warm-up.

### Mistake 3: Using the Same Ad Creative for Three Months

**The story:** A fitness studio in Portland, Oregon named “Solid Ground CrossFit” ran the same ad — a photo of someone doing kettlebell swings with “Join today! First class free!” — for fourteen weeks straight. Their cost per lead started at $4.50 in week one. By week ten, it was $18.20 per lead. By week twelve, they paused the campaign because they thought their audience was “saturated.”

No. The audience didn’t get saturated. The ad got ignored.

Facebook’s algorithm shows your ad to the same people multiple times if your budget is small. After about three views per person, the response rate drops off a cliff. It’s called ad fatigue, and it’s predictable.

**The fix:** We built a rotation of six ads. Three static images (different exercises, different coaches, different backgrounds) and three short videos (15–20 seconds each). We told Facebook to rotate them evenly and to pause any ad that fell below a 0.5% click-through rate. Every two weeks, we swapped out the worst-performing ad and tested a new one.

**The outcome:** Cost per lead dropped back to $4.80 within two weeks. Monthly leads went from 22 (at $18 each) to 54 (at $5 each). Same $1,000 monthly budget. Revenue from new memberships went from roughly $2,200/month to $5,400/month. The owner started writing ad copy himself once he saw the pattern. You don’t need a creative agency — you need a system.

## When to Use Both (And How Not to Waste Money)

I get asked this constantly: “Should I just pick one?” The answer is almost never yes. Most local businesses should run both, but with a clear division of labor.

**Google Ads is for capturing intent.** Someone wakes up with a hangover and types “haircut near me.” Google Ads puts you in front of them. The job of Google Ads is to show up when they’re already looking. You want to be the first result, not the fourth. You want your ad to say “Open at 9am, $45 wash and cut” — because that’s exactly what they need to hear at that moment.

**Facebook Ads is for creating intent.** Nobody wakes up and types “I need a new gym” unless something triggered that thought. Facebook lets you plant that trigger. Run a video of someone crushing a workout at 6 a.m. Run a testimonial from a client who lost 20 pounds. Run a photo of a dog looking happy after a groom. Facebook primes the pump. Google catches the water.

Here’s the specific split I recommend for a small business budget:

**If you have $1,000/month total:**
- $600 on Google Ads (all search, no display, no Performance Max until you know what you’re doing)
- $400 on Facebook Ads (video awareness + retargeting with offer)

**If you have $500/month total:**
- $300 on Google Ads
- $200 on Facebook Ads (retargeting only — skip the cold awareness until you can spend more)

**The tool stack matters too.** Connect Google Ads to your Square or Booksy account so you can track actual bookings, not just clicks. Use a simple UTM parameter on your Facebook Ads so Google Analytics (or whatever you use) shows which platform drove each lead. I’ve watched too many business owners spend $2,000/month and have no idea which ad led to which booking because they never set up tracking. That’s not marketing. That’s gambling.

Also: Yelp is not a substitute for either platform. I’ve seen coffee shops in Denver spend $500/month on Yelp ads and get three phone calls. Yelp works for certain categories (plumbers, electricians, moving companies). For most retail and service businesses, Google and Facebook will give you better returns. Spend your Yelp budget on cleaning up your profile and responding to reviews. Don’t pay for their ad platform unless you’ve tested it and it works.

## How to Test a New Platform Without Wasting the Month’s Rent

Every week I get an email from someone who saw a TikTok about Google Ads and dumped $2,000 into a campaign with no strategy. Then they call me asking why it didn’t work. I’ll save you that call.

Here’s the test framework I use with every new client:

**Step 1: Set a hard budget floor.** If you’re not comfortable losing $500, don’t start with a platform. That’s your learning cost. Some campaigns lose money for the first two weeks while the algorithm figures out who converts. That’s normal. If you bail after three days, you never gave it a chance.

**Step 2: Pick one platform. Not both.** Run Google Ads for 30 days on its own. Or run Facebook Ads for 30 days on its own. Do not split $500 across both. You won’t get enough data on either one to make a decision.

**Step 3: Define one metric that matters.** Not impressions. Not clicks. Not “engagement.” One specific action: a booked appointment, a phone call that lasts over 60 seconds, a form submission, or an in-store visit. If you can’t track that action, fix your tracking before you spend another dollar.

**Step 4: Run the test for 30 days minimum.** If you spent $500 and got three bookings worth $600 total, that’s not a failure. That’s a 1.2x return. You have data. You can improve the ad, the landing page, or the offer. If you spent $500 and got zero bookings, that tells you something too — your targeting or offer is wrong.

**Step 5: Decide based on cost per acquisition, not total spend.** A $100 booking is fine if your average customer spends $400 over six months. A $5 click that never converts is a problem regardless of how cheap it seems.

I tested this exact framework with a coffee shop in Chicago called “Brew & Batter.” They wanted to spend $1,200/month on Facebook Ads. I convinced them to start with $500 on Google Ads first, targeting “coffee shop near me” and “best latte Chicago.” Month one: $500 spend, 12 new customers who specifically mentioned the ad, average spend per visit $8.50. That’s $102 in direct tracked revenue. Doesn’t sound great until you realize those 12 people came back an average of 4 times each over the next two months. Lifetime value: roughly $408. Return on the $500: 0.8x in month one, 3.2x by month three.

If they’d quit after week one, they’d have missed that.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I run Google Ads myself or do I need to hire someone?**

You can absolutely run them yourself — Google makes it easy to spend money. The hard part is spending it well. I’ve seen business owners succeed on their own if they’re willing to learn negative keywords, match types, and conversion tracking. If you’re not willing to spend two hours a week on your account, hire someone who will. The middle ground is using Google’s “Smart Campaigns,” which are better than nothing but worse than a well-managed manual campaign.

**Q: Will Facebook Ads work for a coffee shop or is it just for ecommerce?**

Yes, Facebook works for coffee shops if you stop trying to sell coffee directly and start selling the experience. Run ads for a loyalty program (buy 10 get 1 free). Run an ad for your seasonal drink menu. Run a video of your barista pouring latte art. Target people within 2 miles of your shop who have “coffee” or “food” interests. Expect to pay $0.50–$1.50 per click in most US cities. The goal is foot traffic, not online orders.

**Q: How much should I spend to start?**

$500–$1,000 per month per platform is the minimum to get meaningful data in most US metro areas. If you’re in a small town with low competition, you might get away with $300. Below that, the platforms don’t have enough budget to optimize properly. You’ll get sporadic results and you won’t know why.

**Q: Which platform is better for a hair salon?**

Google Ads, by a wide margin. People search for “hair salon near me” and “blowout [city name]” thousands of times a month in any decent-sized city. Facebook works for retargeting and building awareness, but Google will give you the fastest path to booked appointments. Connect Google Ads to your Booksy or Vagaro account so you can track which searches lead to actual bookings.

**Q: Should I run ads on Yelp instead of Google or Facebook?**

Only if you’ve already tested Google and Facebook and they failed. Yelp’s ad platform works best for high-urgency service businesses — plumbers, electricians, towing companies — where someone needs help immediately. For coffee shops, salons, gyms, and pet groomers, Google and Facebook will almost always outperform Yelp on cost per lead. Focus on getting good reviews on Yelp and let those do the work for free.

**Q: How long until I see results?**

Google Ads: You’ll see clicks within hours. Real bookings may take 1–2 weeks while the algorithm optimizes. Facebook Ads: Expect 2–4 weeks to build enough retargeting data. Anyone promising “instant results” is selling you something that won’t last. A well-run campaign builds momentum over 60–90 days.
