## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Ignoring Negative Reviews (Austin, TX)

A roofer in Austin called me after his leads dropped by 60% over six months. He had great Google reviews — a 4.8 average — but he was ignoring the two recent 1-star reviews. One mentioned a leak that wasn't fixed properly. The other complained about a crew showing up late three days in a row. His response? Silence.

**What went wrong:** Google's algorithm weights recency. Two bad reviews in a row with no response tanked his local ranking. He also lost three jobs because prospects specifically mentioned those reviews during calls. One guy said, "I saw the leak complaint and got nervous." That's $12,500 in lost jobs from two reviews.

**The fix:** I told him to respond to both reviews within 24 hours. Acknowledge the problem publicly, apologize without being defensive, and explain what they fixed. For the leak job, he sent a crew back for free. For the lateness, he fired the crew lead. Then he asked his last five happy customers (the ones who paid on time and thanked him) to leave a review.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, his average rating was back to 4.7. His Google Map pack ranking moved from position 6 to position 3. Traffic from that alone increased. The $12,500 in lost jobs was recovered within 45 days. Cost to fix? Zero dollars. Time invested? Two hours.

**What to do instead:** Set a calendar reminder every Monday. Check your Google Business Profile and Yelp reviews. Respond to every review, positive or negative, within 48 hours. For negative ones, offer a specific fix in your response. Do not argue. Do not blame the customer. Just fix it and move on.

### Mistake #2: Running Google Ads Without Geo-Targeting (Nashville, TN)

A Nashville roofer called me frustrated. He'd spent $1,200 on Google Ads the previous month and gotten exactly one lead — someone asking if they could install gutters in Memphis, 200 miles away. He was ready to fire Google Ads entirely.

**What went wrong:** He set up his campaign himself. Selected "roofing services" as the keyword. But he forgot to set a location radius. Google's default is the entire country unless you change it. So his ads were showing in Memphis, Knoxville, and even Chattanooga. Worse, he had broad match keywords enabled, so his ads triggered for "metal roof supplies" and "roofing contractor salary." People who never needed a roofer clicked his ads. He paid $2.50 per click for nothing.

**The fix:** I rebuilt his campaign with:
- A 20-mile radius around Nashville only
- Phrase match keywords: "roof repair Nashville," "roof replacement Nashville," "emergency roof leak Nashville"
- Negative keywords: "how to," "salary," "jobs," "materials," "supplies"
- Location bid adjustments: +15% for ZIP codes he'd worked in before
- A budget of $500/month instead of $1,200 because he didn't need to waste money on irrelevant clicks

**The outcome:** That $500 generated 3 leads in the first 10 days. One was a $1,200 repair job. One was a $4,500 full replacement. The third didn't convert, but 2 out of 3 is better than 1 out of all that wasted money. He made $2,700 in revenue from $500 in ad spend. That's a 5.4x return. He stopped threatening to fire Google Ads.

**What to do instead:** Start with a $300-$500 budget. Narrow your radius to 15-25 miles depending on your market. Use only phrase match and exact match keywords. Add negative keywords before you launch. Review search terms report weekly and add more negatives. The goal isn't to show up everywhere. It's to show up for the people who will pay you.

### Mistake #3: No Phone Call Tracking (Denver, CO)

A Denver roofer had no idea where his leads came from. He'd ask every caller, "How did you find us?" and most said "Google." But was it Google Ads, organic search, or Google Maps? He couldn't tell. So he was spending $800/month on Google Ads, $200/month on Facebook, and $150/month on Yelp. Total: $1,150/month with zero attribution.

**What went wrong:** He had one phone number on his website and ads. Every call went to the same place. He couldn't distinguish between an organic call and an ad call. So when someone asked for a quote, he didn't know if his Google Ads were working or if his Yelp profile was driving the lead. He was flying blind and overspending.

**The fix:** I set up call tracking using CallRail ($30/month for the basic plan). We assigned different numbers to:
- Google Ads landing page
- Organic Google Business Profile
- Yelp profile
- Facebook ads
- His van wrap (with a separate number printed on the vehicle)

**The outcome:** Within 30 days, he learned that 60% of his calls came from his Google Business Profile (organic), 25% from his van wrap, 10% from Google Ads, and 5% from Yelp. He was spending $200/month on Facebook ads that generated exactly one call in 30 days — a wrong number. He paused Facebook immediately. He reduced Google Ads to $400/month because organic was doing the heavy lifting. He kept the van wrap number running because it was free passive advertising. Total monthly spend dropped from $1,150 to $550. Calls actually increased 10% because he wasn't competing with himself in Google Ads for keywords that showed up in organic search.

**What to do instead:** Spend $30/month on a call tracking service before you spend anything else. Know which channel generates calls. Kill the channels that don't. Double down on the ones that do. This single step will save you money immediately.

## How to Turn Every Roof Job Into a Referral Machine

Referrals aren't luck. They're a system. Most roofers I meet say they "get referrals sometimes." That's not a system. That's hoping.

Here's a system that worked for a roofer in Portland, OR.

After every job completion, he did three things:

1. **Sent a handwritten thank-you note** with a $25 Amazon gift card. Cost per card: $27.50 with stamp.

2. **Followed up two weeks later** asking if everything was holding up. If the answer was yes, he asked, "Who among your neighbors or family might need a roof inspection before winter?"

3. **Created a "Referral Bonus" card** that gave existing customers $150 off their next service for every new customer who booked a paid job. He printed these on thick cardstock and left three with every homeowner.

The result? In 6 months, 18 referrals turned into 13 new jobs. Average job value: $3,800. Total new revenue from referrals: $49,400. Cost of the gift cards and bonus discounts: $1,950. Net gain: $47,450.

The key is timing. Most roofers ask for referrals right after getting paid. The customer just wrote a check. They're not thinking about you. Wait two weeks. Let the new roof settle. Let them enjoy not having a leak. Then ask.

Also: make it easy. Don't say "tell your friends about me." Give them a specific card to hand out. Say "just give this to anyone who mentions a leaky roof." A physical card works better than a text message. People trust a paper card more than a link.

## Offline Marketing That Still Works for Roofers

Everyone tells you to focus on digital. They're not wrong, but they're not complete. In my experience at OMD managing multi-million dollar campaigns, the best small business marketing mixes digital with offline. Here's what I've seen work for roofers specifically.

### Vehicle Wraps Are a No-Brainer

A roofer in Chicago wrapped his truck. Cost: $1,800 for a full wrap. He drives through neighborhoods where he's currently working. People see his truck, take a photo of the number, call him. He tracked 22 calls from his wrap in one year. Average job value: $2,400. Total revenue from the wrap: $52,800. Return on investment: 29x. Cost per call: $81. That's cheaper than Google Ads.

**The catch:** You need a clean truck. If your truck is dirty, the wrap looks desperate. Pressure wash it every two weeks. Park it facing the street when you're on a job.

### Direct Mail to Storm-Affected Areas

After a hailstorm in Dallas, a roofer I worked with sent 500 postcards to homes in the affected ZIP code. The postcard showed damage photos from that specific storm. Headline: "Hail damage? We can help with insurance claims." Cost per postcard: $0.45 including postage. Total cost: $225.

Within 10 days, he got 8 calls. That's a 1.6% response rate, which is high for direct mail. Four became jobs. Average insurance payout: $6,200. Total revenue: $24,800. Cost: $225. Return: 110x.

**The catch:** You need to send within 72 hours of the storm. Wait a week and competitors have already picked the low-hanging fruit. Keep pre-designed templates ready. Buy a mailing list of homes in ZIP codes you want to target. Print and mail same day.

### Partner with Home Inspectors

A roofer in Nashville partnered with a home inspector he met at a networking event. The inspector referred every client with roof issues to him. In exchange, the roofer gave the inspector $100 per signed job. Average job: $5,200. Referral cost: $100. Net per job: $5,100.

Over 12 months, the inspector sent 22 referrals. 15 became jobs. Total revenue: $78,000. Total referral fees paid: $1,500. Net profit: $76,500.

**The catch:** You need to vet your partners. Not all inspectors are good. The bad ones refer you to clients with unrealistic expectations. Find an inspector who's been in business at least 3 years and has good reviews. Buy them lunch. Explain your pricing and quality standards. Make sure they understand what kind of work you do best. Hand them 50 business cards with a note: "Refer me and I'll take care of you."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on lead generation per month?**

Start at $500-600 total across all channels. That's $300 for Google Ads, $30 for call tracking, and the rest for vehicle wraps, direct mail, or referral bonuses. Track everything for 90 days. If you see a 3x return or better on any channel, increase it. If a channel costs more than it returns, kill it immediately. Do not "give it more time" if it's bleeding money.

**Q: Do I really need a website? My customers find me on Google Maps.**

Yes, you need a website. A one-page site with your phone number, service area, and 5 before/after photos is fine. Cost: $500 from a local freelancer, $20/month for hosting. You need a place to send people from ads, Yelp, and Facebook. A Google Business Profile alone isn't enough because you can't control what Google shows. Your website is your home base.

**Q: Yelp keeps calling me to advertise. Is it worth it?**

Almost certainly not for a roofer. In my experience, Yelp ads convert poorly for service businesses with higher ticket prices ($2,000+). The leads are often price shoppers who call three roofers and take the cheapest quote. You're paying $300-600/month to compete with bottom-feeders. Instead, claim your Yelp page for free, respond to reviews, and post photos. That's it. Do not pay Yelp a dime for advertising.

**Q: How do I compete with the big roofing companies that outspend me?**

You don't outspend them. You out-local them. Big companies run broad campaigns that waste budget on irrelevant clicks. You can target specific ZIP codes, mention neighborhoods by name in your ad copy, and respond to reviews within hours. Big companies have marketing managers in different cities who don't know the local streets. You live there. Use that.

**Q: What's the one tool I should buy first?**

CallRail or similar call tracking software. It's $30/month. It tells you exactly which ad, which keyword, and which channel generated each call. Without it, you're guessing. With it, you can double down on what works and cut what doesn't. I've seen it save small businesses $500/month in wasted ad spend within the first 60 days.

**Q: Should I run ads year-round or only during storm season?**

Run ads year-round at a low budget ($300/month) and increase during storm season ($800-1,000/month). The reason: off-season leads are usually for maintenance, repairs, or inspections. These are lower ticket ($500-1,500) but they keep your pipeline warm. During storm season, you want to be top of search results immediately. If you pause your ads for six months, Google resets your campaign learning data. You'll pay more per click when you restart. Keep a low base budget running continuously.

**Q: What's the biggest waste of money I should avoid?**

Buying a "done for you" lead generation service that guarantees a specific number of leads. I've seen roofers pay $1,000/month for 10 "guaranteed leads" that turned out to be people who clicked a button accidentally or were looking for a completely different service. Read the fine print — most of these services define a "lead" as any click or form submission, not a qualified human who needs a roof. Run your own campaigns. You'll have more control and better results.
