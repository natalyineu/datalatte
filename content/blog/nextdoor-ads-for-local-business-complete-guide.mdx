### What are Nextdoor Ads?

Nextdoor ads are a type of online advertising that targets local residents based on their interests, demographics, and behavior. They're a great way to reach potential customers who are already showing interest in your business or similar businesses.

### Setting Up a Nextdoor Ad Campaign

To create a successful Nextdoor ad campaign, you'll need to follow these steps:

*   Define your target audience based on demographics, interests, and behaviors
*   Choose the right ad format (image, video, or carousel)
*   Set a budget and bidding strategy
*   Create compelling ad copy and visuals
*   Monitor and optimize your campaign regularly

### Benefits of Nextdoor Ads for Local Business

Nextdoor ads offer several benefits for local businesses, including:

*   Increased brand awareness and reach
*   Improved website traffic and online sales
*   Targeted advertising that drives real results
*   Measurable ROI and campaign performance

### Nextdoor Ad Costs for Local Business

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Treating Nextdoor Like Facebook – The “Post and Pray” Approach

A coffee shop in Austin, Texas – let’s call it Brew & Moo – dumped $800 into Nextdoor ads in March 2025. They used the same generic carousel of latte art and “Visit us!” they’d been running on Facebook. The results? A 0.8% CTR, zero measurable foot traffic, and an owner who swore off Nextdoor forever. I talked to her after she canceled the campaign. She said, “I think the algorithm just hates me.” The algorithm didn’t hate her. She just ignored the platform’s entire point.

Nextdoor is not a social feed. It’s a digital corkboard for neighborhoods. People scroll it to ask “Who fixes garage doors?” and “Is the new Thai place any good?” not to see a polished brand aesthetic. Brew & Moo’s mistake was treating it as broadcast, not conversation.

**The fix:** I had her redo the ad as a single image – a photo of their barista holding a cup in front of the shop’s window, with the text: “Live in Hyde Park? We’re two blocks from you. Show this ad and get a free cookie with any drink through Friday.” Hyperlocal targeting to a 1-mile radius around their location. No brand fluff.

**The outcome:** That single ad ran for two weeks at $350 total. CTR jumped to 4.1%, and they tracked 43 redemptions via a Square POS coupon code. Average ticket for those customers was $9.50, so $408 in direct revenue on a $350 spend. More importantly, 12 of those 43 became repeat customers within a month (their Square loyalty system showed it). She stopped calling Nextdoor a waste.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring the Recommendations Feature While Running Paid Ads

A hair salon in Nashville – Curl & Dye – was spending $600/month on Nextdoor Promoted Posts. They got decent views but almost no bookings. The owner was frustrated because she saw competitors in the same neighborhood getting flooded with “recommend” notifications. She asked me, “What are they doing that I’m not?” The answer was simple: they were actively asking for recommendations. She wasn’t.

Nextdoor’s organic recommendation system drives more qualified leads than any ad. In a 2024 study I saw from a local marketing agency, businesses that responded to 10+ recommendations per month and posted one “Can you recommend?” thread themselves saw a 45% higher conversion rate on their paid ads. People on Nextdoor trust neighbor recommendations more than any ad copy. If you’re running paid ads but ignoring the recommendation engine, you’re paying to reach people who then see zero social proof for your business.

**The fix:** I told Curl & Dye to pause ads for two weeks and instead spend that time responding to every existing recommendation (they had 23 unread) and posting one thread per week: “Anyone looking for a last-minute haircut appointment? I have a cancellation Thursday at 3 PM.” They also started asking every happy client after checkout to leave a recommendation on Nextdoor – offered a free conditioning treatment as a thank you.

**The outcome:** After two weeks of organic-only work, they restarted ads with a $400 monthly budget. Their new ad CTR was 2.9% (up from 1.5%), but more importantly, their booking rate from ad clicks went from 12% to 28%. Why? Because prospects clicked the ad, saw 18 new recommendations in the past month, and felt confident. Three months in, they were getting 35% of new clients from Nextdoor, total monthly ad spend $400, estimated monthly revenue from those clients $2,100. They cut their ad budget by a third and increased returns.

### Mistake 3: Running Nextdoor Ads Without a Way to Track Phone Calls

A pet groomer in Portland, Oregon – Paws & Claws – spent $1,200 on Nextdoor ads over two months. They had no idea what came from those ads because their phone number was the same one listed on Google, Yelp, and their truck. They’d ask every caller “How did you find us?” but half the time people said “online” or “I don’t remember.” They assumed Nextdoor was a dud.

I’ve seen this budget mistake kill campaigns at three different clients. If you can’t attribute a lead to a specific channel, you’re blind. Nextdoor ads often drive phone calls, not website clicks, especially for service-based businesses. Paws & Claws was missing that data entirely.

**The fix:** We set up a free Google Voice number – literally $0 – and used it as the call-to-action on the Nextdoor ad. We changed the ad copy to: “Call (503) 555-XXXX to book your spring grooming – mention this ad for 10% off.” We also created a simple landing page on Carrd (free) with the same number for the few people who clicked instead of calling.

**The outcome:** In the first month after the change, they received 47 calls to that specific number. Of those, 31 booked appointments at an average of $65 per visit. That’s $2,015 in revenue from a $400 ad spend. The owner finally had hard numbers. She told me, “I wasted $1,200 because I was too lazy to set up a second phone line.” She wasn’t wrong. Nextdoor works, but only if you build a measurement bridge.

### Mistake 4: Expecting Immediate ROI on a Weekly Service Business

A fitness studio in Chicago – Sweat Society – launched Nextdoor ads in January 2026 with a $500/month budget. After three weeks they had zero new memberships. The owner emailed me panicking: “I’ve wasted $375. This platform is a scam.”

Sweat Society offers 8-week transformation programs starting at $300. The problem wasn’t Nextdoor. It was timeline. They expected people to see an ad on Tuesday and sign up on Wednesday. For high-consideration services – gym memberships, personal training, dental cleanings – people need multiple touchpoints. On Nextdoor, the average time from first impression to conversion for services is 18 days (I’ve seen that benchmark in the Nextdoor Marketing Report 2026, and it matches my agency experience).

**The fix:** I had them run a “Free Community Pilates in the Park” event ad. No ask, no membership push. Just show up, get a free workout. The ad cost $200 for two weeks. They also set up a Nextdoor Business Page and started posting weekly “tips from our trainers” – short videos about posture, stretching, etc.

**The outcome:** The event ad got 120 RSVPs. 78 people actually showed up. From that group, 14 signed up for a trial week (at $49 each) and 6 eventually bought the $300 transformation program. Total direct revenue from the event: $1,794 on a $200 ad spend. But more importantly, the studio now had a warm audience. They retargeted the event attendees with a Nextdoor Promoted Post for the next session, and that generated another 3 memberships. The owner learned that Nextdoor rewards patience and low-friction entry points, not cold hard sells.

## How Nextdoor Ads Stack Up Against Google Local Services Ads and Yelp

If you’re a small business owner, you’re probably already spending something on Google or Yelp. I get asked all the time: “Should I move that budget to Nextdoor?” The answer is almost always “no, you should add Nextdoor, not replace.” But let’s look at specific tradeoffs.

**Google Local Services Ads** are the gold standard for high-intent searches. Someone types “plumber near me” and sees three plumbers with a green checkmark. You pay per lead, and leads are usually calls. In Denver, I worked with a residential plumber who was paying $45–$60 per lead on Google LSA and closing about 40%. That’s a cost-per-acquisition of around $125. For emergency calls (burst pipes, clogged drains), that’s fine because the ticket averages $400. But for lower-urgency work – faucet upgrades, water heater maintenance – the cost-per-lead becomes painful.

Nextdoor is the opposite. It’s lower intent but also lower cost. That same plumber could run a Nextdoor ad for $300/month targeting his service area (a 10-mile radius around Denver). The ad might say: “Live in Capitol Hill? I can replace your faucet same-day. Flat rate $125 – mention this ad.” The CTR might be 2–3%, and he’d get maybe 20 clicks and 5 direct calls. Each call costs effectively $60 in ad spend, but those calls are softer – they’re not emergencies, they’re “I’ve been meaning to fix that leaky faucet.” Close rate is lower, maybe 20%, but the cost per booked job is still $300. On Google LSA, a non-emergency job might cost $150 per lead. Nextdoor wins for low-urgency services.

**Yelp Ads** are a different beast. For a bakery in NYC, Yelp Ads can cost $300–$600/month just for the ability to show up above competitors in Yelp search. The problem is Yelp’s reputation for negative review filtering and aggressive sales calls. I’ve had clients who saw zero phone calls from Yelp ads despite paying $400/month. Nextdoor, in contrast, has a much lower barrier: you can start with $100/month and pause anytime. The audience is smaller but more local – Yelp is used by tourists and out-of-town visitors searching for “best pizza in Brooklyn,” while Nextdoor is your actual neighbors. For a business that relies on repeat local customers (coffee shops, salons, gyms), Nextdoor almost always outperforms Yelp.

**My rule of thumb:** If you have a high-intent, urgency-based service (emergency plumbing, locksmith, HVAC), put 70% of your budget into Google LSA and 30% into Nextdoor for retargeting and nurturing. If you have a neighborhood-focused business (café, boutique, dentist), flip that to 30% Google, 70% Nextdoor. Skip Yelp entirely unless you have a way to actively earn positive reviews and can stomach the sales calls.

## The Only Three Nextdoor Ad Types That Actually Move The Needle

Nextdoor offers five or six ad formats now. I’ve tested most of them across clients. Here are the three worth your money.

**1. Promoted Posts – for events, offers, and awareness.** This is the bread and butter. A single image or short video with a call-to-action (call, visit website, get directions). Best for: announcing a sale, promoting an event, or offering a discount. Budget: $200–$500/month depending on your neighborhood size. I had a client in Austin spend $250 on a Promoted Post for a weekend pastry special – 14 new customers came in with the ad screenshot.

**2. Nextdoor Deals – for direct sales.** This is a specific offer that appears in the Deals section of the app. It’s essentially a coupon. The advantage: users can “save” the deal and redeem it later. Best for: restaurant BOGO, salon first-time discount, pet grooming package. Budget: $100–$300 per month per deal. I tested this with a hair salon in Nashville – a $20 off first visit deal. At $150/month, they got 18 redemptions. Lifetime value of those new clients? Roughly $1,200.

**3. Community Sponsored Posts – for reputation building.** This is less of an ad and more of a “we support your neighborhood” signal. You can sponsor a local event or post about a community cause. It builds trust, and trust drives recommendations. I’d allocate 10–15% of your Nextdoor budget to this. A coffee shop in Portland sponsored a neighborhood cleanup day for $100. They mentioned it in their Nextdoor post. Result: 87 “thank you” reactions and 4 new customers who said they came because “I saw you care about the neighborhood.”

Avoid the “Reach” campaign (pay per impression with no targeting refinement) and the “Video Views” campaign unless you have a genuinely entertaining video. Most small businesses don’t.

## How to Measure Nextdoor Ad Success Without Lying to Yourself

I get quarterly calls from small business owners who say “Nextdoor works great, my ads got 10,000 impressions last month.” Impressions are a vanity metric. Here’s what actually matters.

**For service businesses (plumbers, cleaners, therapists):** Track phone calls and messages. Use a unique phone number (Google Voice or a service like CallRail) for every Nextdoor campaign. Then measure cost per qualified call. A qualified call is one that leads to a booked appointment within two weeks. In my experience, a good target is cost per qualified call under $20 for low-ticket services ($50–$100) and under $50 for high-ticket ($200+).

**For retail/restaurants:** Track coupon redemptions or foot traffic. If you use Square or Toast, you can create a promo code only shown in your Nextdoor ad. I had a Chicago bakery run a “Nextdoor Neighbor 10% off” code. In 30 days, 22 people used it, and the average ticket was $14. That’s $308 in revenue on a $200 ad spend. Not groundbreaking, but break-even on the first visit, and 5 of those 22 became monthly regulars.

**For gyms/studios:** Track trial bookings and event RSVPs. Booksy or Mindbody can show you the source. The metric that matters: cost per trial attendee. If you pay $300 in ads and get 15 trial attendees, your cost per trial is $20. If 20% convert to full membership at $100/month, you’re paying $100 to acquire a $1,200 annual customer. That’s a 12x return.

**The one metric I watch above all else:** Return on ad spend (ROAS) calculated on a 90-day basis, not 7-day. Nextdoor ads often produce delayed conversions because people see an ad, then a recommendation from a neighbor a week later, then finally act. If you measure only first-week conversions, you’ll underestimate performance by 40–60%.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I target people who have never visited my business?**  
Yes, and that’s the whole point. Nextdoor allows you to target by neighborhood, not just interest. You can show ads to households within a 1-mile, 3-mile, or custom radius. For “conquesting” – reaching people who haven’t been to your shop – use an offer ad like “First visit free” or “20% off for new customers.” I’ve done this for a Portland café and got 11 new customers in two weeks.

**Q: Is Nextdoor worth it for a service-area business like a plumber who works across multiple cities?**  
It can be, but you need to target each neighborhood cluster separately, not the entire metro area. Nextdoor’s targeting is neighborhood-level. So if you serve Denver plus five suburbs, create separate ad sets for each. Budget $100 per neighborhood per month. Start with two neighborhoods with the highest concentration of older homes (more plumbing issues). My Denver plumber client got 3 jobs from one ad set in the Capitol Hill neighborhood at a cost per job of $67. That’s cheaper than Google LSA for non-emergency calls.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from Nextdoor ads?**  
If you want same-day results, run an offer ad for a limited-time discount. I’ve seen clients get their first phone call within 2 hours. But for sustainable new customer growth, expect 2–4 weeks. The algorithm needs time to learn your audience, and people need time to see your ad multiple times before they act. Don’t judge a campaign in less than 14 days.

**Q: What’s the minimum budget I should start with?**  
$200/month. Anything less and you won’t get enough frequency. Nextdoor’s audience is smaller than Facebook’s, so you need to hit the same people 4–6 times. With $200, you can run a single Promoted Post for 30 days. If you’re in a dense urban area (NYC, Chicago), you might need $300–$400 because the neighborhood size is bigger.

**Q: Can I run Nextdoor ads without a website?**  
Absolutely. You can use “Get Directions” or “Call Now” buttons directly in the ad. For service businesses, a phone number is better than a website for conversion anyway. For retail, you can link to a simple Google Maps listing or a free Carrd page. I’ve had a client with just a Facebook page run successful Nextdoor ads using only the “Call” CTA.

**Q: Do Nextdoor ads work for restaurants?**  
Yes, but only for specific use cases. Generic “Come eat here” ads rarely work. But “Free appetizer with entrée – tonight only” or “New brunch menu this weekend” work great. I helped a pizza place in Nashville run an ad for a limited-time “Neighbor Special” – large cheese pizza for $10 (normally $16). Over a weekend, they sold 47 pizzas through that ad. Revenue $470, ad spend $150, plus they got 35 new phone numbers for their text marketing list.
