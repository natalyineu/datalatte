## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched small business owners throw money at marketing until it hurt. Most of the time, the problem wasn’t budget. It was strategy. Here are three screw-ups I’ve seen personally, with the fixes that actually turned things around.

### Mistake 1: Running Google Ads Without Location Targeting

**The story:** A coffee shop owner in Austin, Texas called me after spending $1,400 on Google Ads over six weeks. They’d gotten 38 clicks, zero in-store visits they could track, and one phone call from a guy asking if they sold gluten-free muffins (they did not).

**What went wrong:** They had set their ad radius to 30 miles. That covered all of Austin, plus Round Rock, Cedar Park, and half of nowhere. Their ad was showing to people 45 minutes away who had no intention of driving across town for a $5 latte. The owner had also clicked “maximize clicks” without setting a daily cap. Google spent their budget by 10 a.m. every day on the most expensive keywords in the broadest area.

**The fix:** I narrowed the radius to 1.5 miles. That’s the distance most people will walk or drive for a morning coffee. I changed the campaign to target people “regularly in” that area—not just people who searched for coffee but someone who happened to be driving through. I set a hard daily budget of $20, swapped to manual CPC bidding starting at $0.75, and added negative keywords like “recipes,” “beans wholesale,” and “near me” (because those searches already imply they’re looking for something different).

**The outcome:** In the next 30 days, they spent $620. They tracked 14 redemptions of a “free pastry with any drink” offer in the ad extension. Average ticket was $6.50 per visit. That’s $91 in direct revenue from the ad, plus an estimated 34 additional visits from customers who saw the ad but didn’t show the offer. The shop owner estimated an extra $1,800 in gross revenue that month. Cost per visit dropped from $36 to roughly $12. Not glamorous. Profitable.

### Mistake 2: Paying for “SEO Packages” That Do Nothing

**The story:** A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon told me she was paying $500 a month to an SEO agency. She’d been on the contract for 11 months. Her Google Business Profile showed 47 views in that entire period. She had one new review (from her mom). Her organic traffic was basically flat.

**What went wrong:** The agency had written three blog posts. The first was “5 Hairstyles for Summer.” The second was “How to Care for Blonde Hair.” The third was “Why You Should Visit Portland.” None of them had local keywords. None of them linked to her booking page. The agency had also “optimized” her website by stuffing the footer with “salon Portland Oregon hair color cut” repeated six times. Google ignored it. The agency was billing her for low-effort content that did nothing for local searchers.

**The fix:** I told her to cancel the contract immediately. Then I walked her through a three-hour audit of her actual Google Business Profile. Her business category was set to “Hair Salon” — fine — but her services listed were “Haircuts” and nothing else. No mention of balayage, highlights, bridal styling, or even “men’s cuts,” which is what 30% of her actual bookings were. I had her add 12 specific services, update her hours (they were wrong for Saturdays), and add real photos of her work — not stock images. Then I showed her how to ask for reviews after every appointment using a simple text template. No agency needed.

**The outcome:** Within 60 days, her Google Business Profile views went from 47 to 410. Calls from that profile went from zero to 12 per week. She started booking 4 to 6 new clients per week directly through Google search. She was paying $0 per month for SEO. The only cost was 15 minutes of photo editing and sending three texts a day. I don’t have precise revenue numbers, but she told me her May was the best month in three years. She also stopped calling me for help, which I consider a win.

### Mistake 3: Using a Generic Email Newsletter That Nobody Opens

**The story:** A pet grooming business in Denver had 1,400 email addresses. They’d collected them over two years via a clipboard at the front desk. They used Mailchimp’s free plan and sent one email a month: a PDF of their monthly calendar with dates and prices. Open rate averaged 11%. Click rate was 2%. They had no idea if anyone actually booked after reading it.

**What went wrong:** The email had no subject line personalization. No segmentation. No offers. It was just information. Their customers already knew when they needed grooming — they weren’t scrolling a calendar to figure it out. The owner also sent the same email to everyone, including people who hadn’t visited in 14 months. Some of those people had moved. Some had gotten a dog that died. The list was rotting.

**The fix:** I cleaned the list. Removed anyone who hadn’t opened an email in 6 months (about 530 addresses). Then I set up two simple automations. First: a “We Miss You” email sent to anyone who hadn’t booked in 8 weeks — $10 off their next groom, link to book directly. Second: a post-appointment email asking for a review, with a link to Google and a photo of their freshly groomed dog. That second email also had a “Book Your Next Appointment” button pre-filled with 6 weeks out (the average frequency for repeat customers).

**The outcome:** The “We Miss You” automation brought back 38 lapsed customers in the first month. Average spend was $65 per visit. That’s $2,470 in revenue from a single automated email sequence. The post-appointment review request generated 22 new Google reviews in 30 days — more than they’d gotten in the previous year combined. They upgraded to Mailchimp’s $13/month Essentials plan to support the automations. Their monthly send cost went from $0 to $13. ROI on that was roughly 19,000%. Rough math, but you get the point.

## Why Your Google Business Profile Is Worth More Than Your Website

Most small business owners obsess over their website. They spend months agonizing over font choices and hero images. Meanwhile, their Google Business Profile lists the wrong hours, has three blurry photos, and hasn’t been updated since 2022.

I’ll say it plainly: for a coffee shop, hair salon, or pet groomer, your Google Business Profile drives more revenue than your website. Here’s why.

When someone searches “coffee shop near me” on their phone, Google shows a local pack with three results. No websites. Just the profile photo, star rating, distance, and a “Call” button. If your profile is incomplete, you don’t show up. If you show up but have 2.5 stars and someone else has 4.6, you lose.

I worked with a coffee shop in Chicago called (let’s say) Bridgeport Brew. Their website was fine. Clean, responsive, had a menu PDF. But their Google Business Profile had their address listed as “1234 S. Halsted” instead of “1234 South Halsted Street” — and Google Maps couldn’t parse it properly. People were getting directions to a parking lot two blocks away. They had 17 reviews over three years. All five stars, but the volume was too low to compete with the Starbucks half a mile away.

We spent one Saturday morning fixing it. Corrected the address format. Added real photos — not the one stock latte shot they had, but 30 photos of their interior, their pour-over setup, their pastry case, and their barista (who was not thrilled but was a good sport). We added the correct attributes: “has outdoor seating,” “has Wi-Fi,” “accepts Apple Pay.” We also added posts — basically mini-ads that show up in your profile — promoting their seasonal drink specials and a “buy 10 get 1 free” punch card program.

Within 90 days, their profile views went from 230 per month to 1,100. Direction requests went from 14 per month to 87. Reviews came in faster because I told them to buy a cheap tablet stand for the counter, put a sign next to it that said “Loved your latte? Leave us a Google review and grab a free cookie,” and link directly to the review URL. That generated 34 new reviews in 60 days. Their average rating stayed at 4.8. Their ranking in the local pack went from position 5 or 6 to position 2, and sometimes 1 on slower days.

Total cost: $0 for the tablet stand (they already had an old iPad), maybe $20 in cookies. The increase in foot traffic was measurable — their Square transactions went up 22% in that quarter. The owner told me their average day went from 120 transactions to 148. At $7.50 average ticket, that’s $210 extra per day, or about $6,300 per month.

Your website is a brochure. Your Google Business Profile is your front door. Most business owners have the front door locked.

## Retargeting Customers Who Walked Past Your Shop

Here’s a truth that makes people uncomfortable: most of the people who walk past your shop will never come in. They’re busy. They’re distracted. They’ve got AirPods in and an agenda. You have maybe two seconds to change their mind.

Retargeting via foot traffic data is how you get a second chance.

I’m not talking about creepy surveillance. I’m talking about using Google Ads’ Customer Match and location-based lists to show ads to people who have been physically near your shop but didn’t enter.

A fitness studio in Nashville tried this. They ran a promotion: “first class free.” They printed a sign, hung it in the window, and waited. Not much happened. So I set up a Google Ads campaign targeting a 0.2-mile radius around their studio — basically the block they were on — and served a display ad to people who’d been in that area in the last 7 days. The ad said: “You passed us. We saw you. First class is on us.” (I know, borderline aggressive. It worked.)

The campaign cost $400 over 30 days. It generated 23 new visitor sign-ups through their booking system. 12 of those converted to paid memberships at $99/month. That’s $1,188 in recurring monthly revenue from a one-time $400 spend. The studio kept running it. By month three, they had 31 new members from that single retargeting campaign. The only cost was the ad spend and the time it took to set up the location list — about 20 minutes.

For coffee shops, this looks different but works the same way. Set a 0.1-mile radius around your shop. Create a Google Ads display campaign with a simple offer: “$1 off any drink — show this ad.” Target anyone who was in that radius in the last 14 days but hasn’t visited. It catches the commuters who walk past every morning and grab Starbucks instead. It catches the people who see your shop, think “I should try that,” and then forget.

Some business owners feel weird about this. They think it’s pushy. I get it. But here’s my perspective: you’re not tracking their phone. You’re using anonymized location data that Google already has. You’re just giving people a reason to walk through a door they already considered opening. That’s not pushy. That’s helpful.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I’m a single coffee shop. Do I really need Google Ads, or can I just use Instagram?**

Instagram is great for building a vibe. It’s terrible for getting someone off their couch when they’re already craving coffee at 8 a.m. Google Ads captures search intent — people actively looking for coffee near them right now. If you can only do one thing, do Google Ads with location targeting first, then use Instagram to make your shop look good when they search your name. I’ve seen shops spend $200/month on Google Ads and get 40+ visits. I’ve never seen a single Instagram post generate that kind of walk-in traffic.

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads per month for a local coffee shop?**

Start at $300 to $500, but only if you have tight location targeting and a clear offer. If you’re in a small town, $200 might be enough. If you’re in downtown Manhattan, $500 won’t last a morning. The important number isn’t what you spend — it’s your cost per visit. If you’re spending more than $10 per visit and your average ticket is $7, you’re losing money. Adjust your targeting or your offer until that number drops.

**Q: Will Mailchimp automations work for a small pet grooming business with only 200 email addresses?**

Yes. Even 200 addresses are enough to start. Segment them: people who came in the last 30 days, people who came in the last 60 days, and people who haven’t been in for 90+ days. Send a “We Miss You” offer to that third group. I guarantee at least 10 of them need a groom and forgot to book. That’s $650+ in revenue from an email that took 10 minutes to set up. The size of your list matters less than how you use it.

**Q: Do I need a separate website, or can I rely on my Google Business Profile?**

You need both, but prioritize the profile. A one-page website with your menu, hours, address, and a contact form is fine. You don’t need an expensive multi-page site. Spend the money on properly setting up your Google Business Profile, getting real photos, and collecting reviews. That will drive more revenue than a custom-designed website with a blog section you never update.

**Q: I tried asking for reviews, but customers keep forgetting. What do I do?**

Stop expecting them to remember. Make it automatic. Square, Booksy, and most POS systems can send a review request via text or email immediately after payment. For coffee shops specifically: print a short link on your receipt: “Review us at goo.gl/xyz and get 10% off your next drink.” Or use a QR code on a tent card at the register. But the most effective method I’ve seen is to ask right after the transaction, when the customer is still holding the drink. “Hey, if you enjoyed that, we’d love a Google review. It really helps us.” Hand them a card with the link. The success rate on in-person asks is about 1 in 20. That’s fine. It adds up.

**Q: I’m not tech-savvy. How do I set up retargeting without hiring someone?**

Google provides walkthroughs for creating location-based audiences. It’s literally five clicks: go to Google Ads, click “Audience Manager,” click “Create,” choose “Locations,” select your radius, and name the list. Then create a simple display campaign using that audience. The ad can be one image with your logo and an offer. You can do this in under an hour. If an hour feels too long, pay a freelancer $150 to set it up once. Then you never touch it again except to pause or increase the budget.

**Q: Is Yelp worth paying for?**

Almost never, unless your customers are the type of people who still use Yelp as their primary review platform (think: tourists or older demographics). For most local businesses, Google reviews drive more traffic. Yelp’s sales team will call you relentlessly and pitch their ad packages. I’ve seen businesses spend $300/month on Yelp and get three clicks. I’d rather put that $300 into Google Ads. Focus on Google reviews. If you get 100 reviews on Google with a 4.5+ rating, you will outrank most of your competitors in local search. That’s free.
