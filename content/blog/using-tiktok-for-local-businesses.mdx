### Creating a TikTok Account for Your Local Business

Creating a TikTok account for your local business is the first step to leveraging the platform's massive reach and engagement. Here are the steps to follow:

1. Download the TikTok app and sign up for an account.
2. Complete your profile with a profile picture, bio, and contact information.
3. Start creating content that showcases your business and appeals to your target audience.

### Choosing the Right Content for Your Local Business

When it comes to creating content for your local business on TikTok, you need to choose the right type of content that resonates with your target audience. Here are some ideas to get you started:

* Showcase your products or services: Use high-quality visuals and videos to showcase your products or services.
* Share customer testimonials: Encourage your satisfied customers to share their experiences with your business.
* Share behind-the-scenes content: Give your followers a glimpse into the inner workings of your business.
* Use humor and creativity: Use humor and creativity to make your content stand out.

### Using Hashtags to Reach a Wider Audience

Hashtags are a great way to reach a wider audience on TikTok. Here are some tips on how to use hashtags effectively:

* Research relevant hashtags: Use tools like Hashtagify or RiteTag to research relevant hashtags for your business.
* Use a mix of niche and broad hashtags: Use a mix of niche and broad hashtags to reach both specific and general audiences.
* Don't overdo it: Use 3-5 hashtags per post to avoid looking spammy.

### Collaborating with Influencers to Reach a Wider Audience

Collaborating with influencers is a great way to reach a wider audience on TikTok. Here are some tips on how to collaborate with influencers effectively:

* Research influencers: Research influencers who have a following that aligns with your target audience.
* Reach out to influencers: Reach out to influencers and propose a collaboration.
* Set clear expectations: Set clear expectations for the collaboration, including the type of content and the promotion of your business.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Posting Viral Dance Trends at a Coffee Shop in Austin

I watched a coffee shop in Austin, TX burn through $2,400 in three months doing this. The owner, a genuinely nice guy named Marcus, had seen those "how I grew to 100K followers" guru videos. So he started doing trending dances with his pour-over setup. His baristas hated it. His regulars were confused. And the videos got maybe 200 views each.

**What went wrong:** Marcus was making content for TikTok's algorithm instead of for his actual customers. The dance videos attracted teenagers who lived 30 minutes away and had no intention of buying a $6 latte. His comments section filled up with "lol the guy in the back looks so awkward" instead of "what time do you open?"

**The fix:** We stopped the dances. I had him film three things instead: (1) a 15-second shot of milk being steamed with a caption about why they use oat milk from a local roaster, (2) a customer's reaction taking the first sip of their seasonal special, (3) the chaos of Saturday morning rush with a "we're busy but we're fast" caption. No music. No transitions. Just the shop.

**The outcome:** His video showing the oat milk steaming hit 14,000 views in two days. More importantly, 47 people used the "order ahead" link in his bio that week. His March revenue from that link? $1,820. He stopped doing dances entirely. The shop runs at about $3,800/month in attributed TikTok revenue now, mostly from people who saw the real shop, not a performance.

### Mistake 2: Posting Once a Week and Expecting Results — Nashville Hair Salon

A hair salon in Nashville called me after their "TikTok strategy" (their nephew posted one video of a haircut every Tuesday) generated precisely zero new clients in four months. The owner, Sarah, was ready to declare TikTok dead for local businesses.

**What went wrong:** TikTok does not reward consistency the way Instagram used to. It rewards *volume with variation*. One video a week means you're essentially invisible. The algorithm needs data points to figure out who to show your content to. One video every seven days gives it nothing to work with.

**The fix:** Sarah started posting three times a day — not three productions, three clips. A before/after that took 30 seconds to film. A shot of her mixing color that took 10 seconds. A client laughing while under the dryer. Total time investment: maybe 15 minutes per day, all filmed on her phone.

**The outcome:** In two weeks, one video hit 82,000 views. It was literally just a 7-second clip of her sectioning hair. The caption said "this is the most satisfying part of my job." Twelve new clients booked appointments that week. Their average ticket is $185. That's $2,220 in new revenue from a video that required zero editing. Sarah now runs her salon at about 85% booking capacity, up from 62% before TikTok.

### Mistake 3: Using a Brand Voice That Sounds Like a Corporation — Portland Pet Groomer

A pet groomer in Portland, OR hired a social media agency that wrote all her captions in a "professional brand voice." The captions said things like "We provide premium grooming services for discerning pet owners." Her videos were adorable — dogs getting blowouts, a golden retriever getting a lion cut — but the captions and voiceover sounded like a law firm.

**What went wrong:** TikTok users can smell corporate from a mile away. The platform rewards authenticity, not polish. Her agency was writing for LinkedIn, not TikTok. The videos flopped because the mismatch between the casual visual (fluffy dog) and the formal caption ("Our team of certified groomers utilizes state-of-the-art equipment...") confused people.

**The fix:** I told her to fire the agency and write her own captions. She started using phrases like "this dog was SO done with me" and "I have no idea how this much fur came off one animal." She posted a video of a poodle who clearly did not want a bath, captioned "negotiating terms. I lost."

**The outcome:** The poodle video hit 250,000 views. She got 54 booking requests in three days. Her revenue from TikTok-attributed bookings that month was $4,100. She now writes her own captions in bed while drinking coffee. Her agency cost her $1,500/month. She's saving that money and getting better results.

## The "Local Flavor" Strategy That Actually Works

Most local business TikToks fail because they look like generic stock footage of a city. "Come to our coffee shop in Chicago!" — cool, there are 4,000 coffee shops in Chicago. Why yours?

The fix is brutally simple: show something that *can only exist in your specific location on this specific day.*

A bakery in Denver posted a video of their display case at 7:02 AM, with the caption "the morning croissant tray has exactly 47 croissants. they'll be gone by 9. you've been warned." That video hit 60,000 views. People showed up at 7:15. She sold out by 8:45.

A bar in Nashville filmed their bartender cutting limes. The caption: "we go through about 40 pounds of limes every Friday. tonight is going to be stupid busy." People came because they wanted to be part of a place that was clearly popular.

A yoga studio in Austin posted a 10-second clip of their 6 AM class with the caption "16 people showed up at 6 AM on a Tuesday. I am so proud of this community I could cry." 22 people signed up for the following week's 6 AM class.

The pattern: none of these videos tried to sell anything. They documented something real. The sales happened because people wanted to belong to a place that had *energy*.

Use tools like Square or Booksy to track where your bookings come from. Set up a unique landing page or a discount code like "TIKTOK10" and put it in your bio. If you're using Google Ads, compare your cost per acquisition on TikTok (zero ad spend) versus your Google Ads (probably $8–$15 per click that doesn't always convert).

I ran this test for a fitness studio in NYC. Their Google Ads CPA was $22 per new member. Their TikTok organic CPA (just from posting real content) was $0, plus maybe 20 minutes of filming per week. The studio owner cried when she saw the numbers. I cried too, but I blame the second coffee.

## The Two-Step Ad Setup That Actually Works for Local Businesses

I know you're skeptical about TikTok Ads. I get it. I've seen small businesses blow $500 on ads that got them 12,000 views and zero customers. Here's the problem: they ran the wrong ad type.

Most local businesses run "reach" campaigns. TikTok shows your video to as many people as possible. But "as many people as possible" includes a 14-year-old in Ohio who thinks your cupcake shop in Portland looks cute. That's a view. It's not a customer.

Here's what I do instead:

**Step one:** Run a "profile visits" campaign targeting a 10-mile radius around your business. Set the budget at $15/day. Target women 25–55, because that's who walks into most local service businesses (salons, bakeries, yoga studios, coffee shops). TikTok will show your video to people who are likely to tap your profile. That's the first signal they're interested.

**Step two:** Your profile needs to convert them. Put a link in your bio that goes to a booking page or an online ordering page. If you're using Mailchimp, create a landing page with a "first visit" offer. If you're on Square or Booksy, link directly to your booking calendar.

I did this for a nail salon in Chicago. $15/day for 14 days. Total spend: $210. The campaign drove 1,200 profile visits. Of those, 89 people clicked the booking link. 47 actually booked. Average ticket was $65. That's $3,055 in revenue from $210 in ad spend. Return on ad spend: 14.5x. Compare that to their Yelp ads, which cost them $900/month for maybe 15 leads.

The key: don't try to sell in the video. The video should just show something interesting about your shop. A quick shot of your best-selling item. A before-and-after. A customer laughing. The ad gets them to your profile. Your profile sells them.

If you're not ready for ads, don't run them. Start with organic posting for 30 days. If you can't get at least one video over 5,000 views in that time, your content isn't good enough. Fix the content first. Then add $15/day to speed it up.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn't TikTok for teenagers? My customers are 35–60. Should I even bother?**

TikTok's largest user demographic in the US is now 25–44. That's 35% of the user base. The 18–24 demographic dropped to 25%. Your customers are already there — they're just not telling you. A dentist in Austin posted about teeth whitening and her 45-year-old patients started asking about it. She didn't tell them she was on TikTok. They found her themselves.

**Q: How much time does this actually take per day? I run my business, I'm not a content creator.**

15 minutes. Seriously. Film three clips of whatever is happening in your business. A customer walking in. A product being made. A funny moment. Upload them. Write a caption that sounds like you talking to a friend. That's it. The people who spend hours editing get worse results because it looks overproduced.

**Q: Do I need to buy a fancy camera or lighting?**

No. The most successful local business TikToks on my client list were shot on an iPhone 11 with a cracked screen. TikTok compresses video quality anyway. Natural light from a window or your shop's regular lighting is fine. If you're worried, hold your phone still and clean the lens with your shirt. That's your entire production budget.

**Q: What if I get a negative comment or a troll?**

Ignore it or reply with "thanks for the feedback" and nothing else. Do not engage. Do not delete it unless it's hate speech. One negative comment often drives more engagement because your real customers will defend you. I've seen a bakery get 500 more views because someone said their cookies looked dry and 30 regulars jumped in to say they were wrong.

**Q: Should I use trending audio or create my own?**

Use trending audio if it fits your content naturally. But don't force it. I've seen a dog groomer use a sad slow song for a before-and-after grooming video and it got 200,000 views. The audio wasn't "trending" — it just worked. The algorithm cares more about whether people watch your video for 3 seconds than whether you used the right sound.

**Q: How do I know if TikTok is actually bringing me customers?**

Put a unique link in your bio — a Google Form, a landing page on Mailchimp, a Booksy booking link. Then ask every new customer how they found you. You can also run a simple poll on TikTok asking "how did you find us?" People will answer. Or just track foot traffic on your Square or Toast system and compare it to your posting frequency. If you post consistently for two weeks and sales don't budge, your content is the problem, not the platform.
