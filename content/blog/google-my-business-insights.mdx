## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Claiming the Listing and Then Forgetting It Exists**

The owner of a salon in Austin, Texas, let's call her Jenna, set up her Google Business Profile in 2019. She verified it, uploaded the logo, and considered it done. Two years later, her profile showed a phone number disconnected three months ago, her hours still said "Temporary Closure" from the early pandemic, and her only photo was a blurry shot of a half-empty shampoo shelf.

When I checked her insights, she had 1,200 views in the previous month. That's not bad for a small salon. But 400 of those clicks were to directions — and her map location was pinned a block off. Another 90 people clicked her phone number, which went nowhere. She lost real walk-ins, probably $2,000 to $3,000 a month in missed appointments, simply because she set it and forgot it.

**Fix:** Check your profile once a week. Update hours before holidays. If your phone number changes, change it the same day. Respond to reviews within 48 hours, even if it's just "thanks." Treat your GBP like a second website, not a sign you hung up once in 2019.

**Outcome:** Jenna fixed her map pin, updated her hours, added twelve new photos over a month, and started responding to reviews inside 24 hours. Her direction requests went up 40% in six weeks. She booked 17 new clients the following month. At an average ticket of $75, that's $1,275 in new revenue — from a free profile.

**Mistake #2: Stuffing Your Business Name with Keywords**

There's a pet groomer in Nashville who renamed herself "Best Dog Grooming Nashville Tennessee — Affordable Pet Wash — Open Sundays." Google penalized her within three weeks. Her profile stopped showing up for "dog grooming Nashville" entirely. She'd been visible on page one, then dropped to page four. That's where profiles go to be ignored.

**Why it happened:** Google sees your business name as a trust signal. When you load it with keywords, you're telling Google you're trying to game the system. And Google does not like being gamed.

**Fix:** Your business name is the name on your door. That's it. If you're "Bella's Pet Spa," put "Bella's Pet Spa." Not "Bella's Pet Spa — Best Dog Grooming Nashville." You can add keywords to your description, your posts, your products, and your Q&A. Leave the name alone.

**Outcome:** The owner removed the keyword stuffing and submitted a reinstatement request. It took ten days. Her ranking came back in three weeks. She told me her booking rate went from "dead quiet" to nine appointments in the first week back on page one. At roughly $60 per groom, that's $540 she would have lost every week if she'd left the name alone.

**Mistake #3: Posting Random Content with No Purpose**

A fitness studio owner in Portland was proud of posting to her GBP every day for six months. I respect the consistency. But she was posting things like "Happy Tuesday!" with a generic sunrise photo, or "Don't forget to drink water" with a stock photo of a glass. Zero connection to her business. Zero CTA. Zero value.

Her insights showed impressions were solid — 3,400 in a month. But clicks to website? Fifty-three. Phone calls? Eleven. She had visibility, but nobody acted. She was posting noise, not signals.

**Fix:** Every post needs a job. Promote a specific offer — "$49 first month, ends Friday." Announce a new class — "New 6 AM spin class starting Monday, space limited." Show a client result (with permission) — "Samantha lost 12 pounds in 8 weeks with our program. Start yours today." Include a button. Google lets you add "Book," "Call," or "Learn More." Use it.

**Outcome:** She shifted to three posts a week, each with a specific offer and a button. Her clicks to website jumped from 53 to 192 in four weeks. Phone calls went from 11 to 34. The owner told me that one post about a "Summer Challenge" got her 21 signups at $149 each — $3,129 from a single GBP post.

**Mistake #4: Ignoring the Q&A Section Entirely**

There's a coffee shop in Denver that had a great problem — people were asking questions on their Google profile. "Do you have oat milk?" "Is there outdoor seating?" "Open on Thanksgiving?" The owner never answered a single one. Strangers answered them instead. One person answered the oat milk question with "I think so." Another said the shop was "usually open on Thanksgiving but call first." Both were wrong.

The shop had oat milk. They were closed on Thanksgiving. But random users shaped the public perception.

**Fix:** Be the one who answers. Check your Q&A tab weekly. If you see a wrong answer, click the three dots, flag it, and post the correct one yourself. You can also pre-empt this by adding your own FAQs. Google lets you seed questions and answer them. Do this for your top five questions before a customer ever types them.

**Outcome:** The Denver coffee shop owner took 20 minutes to clean up the Q&A and add three of her own. She also added her menu as a photo with prices. Her direction requests went up 18% over two months. Not a fortune, but for a 20-minute fix? Worth more than most $500 ad campaigns.

## How to Turn Insights Into Actual Ad Decisions

Most guides tell you insights are "valuable data for understanding your audience." That's like saying a hammer is useful for holding in your hand. Let's talk about hitting something with it.

Your GBP insights show you three things worth caring about: where your views are coming from (search vs. maps), what search terms people used, and what actions they took (website clicks, calls, direction requests). Here is how you turn each one into a money decision.

**If you have high search views but low maps views:** People are finding you through Google Search, not Google Maps. That usually means your profile is visible but not optimized for local discovery. Test adding location-based keywords to your description. For a salon in Chicago, "women's haircut on Belmont Avenue" outperforms "hair salon" because it tells Google exactly where you are and what you offer.

**If a specific search term keeps showing up:** Let's say your insights show people are finding you with the query "24 hour gym Denver." But you are not a 24-hour gym. You close at 10 PM. You have two options: remove anything that suggests you're open late, or add a new service tier. A client in Boulder chose option two, started offering 6 AM to midnight access with a premium fee, and added $800 a month by meeting demand that was already there.

**If call volume is high but direction requests are low:** People are interested enough to call but not motivated enough to visit. That could mean your location is inconvenient, your offer is unclear, or your photo shows nothing worth driving to. Run a test: post a photo of a busy, happy space with real customers (not stock) and a post that says "Come see us today — mention this post for 10% off." Track direction requests for two weeks. If they go up, you found the problem.

**Where ads come in:** Once you know the search terms people are using, you don't have to guess — you can put ad dollars directly behind those terms. Open Google Ads, target a 5-mile radius around your business, and use the exact phrases from your insights. One coffee shop in Portland spent $350 on a one-month ad campaign targeting the phrase "best espresso downtown Portland." They tracked 43 new customers who mentioned the ad. At $6 average spend per visit, that was a healthy return.

## How to Use Third-Party Tools Without Screwing Up Your Profile

Google Business Profile does not exist in a vacuum. You are also dealing with Yelp, Booksy, Mailchimp, Square, and a dozen other tools that all want your attention. The question is whether they help or hurt your local SEO.

**Yelp:** Yelp and Google are not friends. Yelp reviews do not help your Google ranking. But they do help your business if customers trust Yelp in your category. For a hair salon, Yelp drives real bookings. For a pet groomer in Austin, Yelp generated 30% of her new clients despite her Google profile doing the heavy lifting for rankings. Her approach: respond to every Yelp review like you would on Google, and never quote a Yelp review on your Google profile. That violates Google's guidelines.

**Booksy and Other Booking Platforms:** If you use Booksy for appointment scheduling, do not put "Booksy" in your Google business name. I have seen "Lisa's Nails (Book on Booksy)" in profiles. Stop. Put your booking link in the appointment URL field of your GBP dashboard. That way, when a user clicks "Book," they go directly to your Booksy page. Your profile stays clean, Google is happy, and you keep your bookings.

**Square and Mailchimp:** If you use Square for payments, you can connect your Square account to your Google profile to show product inventory and pricing. This works well for coffee shops and small retail. For a coffee shop in Denver that sells beans online, adding Square inventory to the profile increased in-store pickup orders by 22% over two months. Mailchimp is useful for email follow-ups after a customer leaves a review — set up a simple automation that sends a "thanks for visiting" email with a discount code. This does not affect your GBP directly, but repeat customers drive more reviews, which boosts your ranking.

**CallRail:** If you use a call tracking tool like CallRail, set up a separate phone number for your Google profile. This lets you track exactly how many calls come from Google vs. other channels. A fitness studio in NYC did this and discovered that 65% of their phone calls came from Google, but only 30% of their marketing budget was allocated there. They shifted $1,500 a month from print ads into GBP optimization and ad spend. Calls dropped slightly at first, then recovered within three weeks — but the cost per lead dropped from $12 to $4.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I check my Google Business Profile insights?**

Once a week. Set a recurring calendar reminder for Wednesday morning. Look at three things: total views, top search queries, and the action breakdown (website clicks vs. calls vs. requests for directions). If anything drops more than 20% from the previous week, investigate. Did you miss a review? Did you stop posting for a week? Did a competitor open nearby? Weekly checks catch problems before they become trends.

**Q: Can GBP insights tell me specifically who is searching for my business?**

No. Google does not give you individual customer data. You get aggregated stats like "250 people found you by searching 'haircut Austin' this week." That is still useful — it tells you what terms people are using and which neighborhoods they're searching from. If most of your views come from a specific ZIP code, that is a signal. Maybe open a second location there, or run ads targeting that area.

**Q: Do Google reviews affect my ranking more than insights data?**

Yes. Reviews are a direct ranking signal. Insights are diagnostic. A profile with 50 five-star reviews and outdated information will still rank well — but it will rank better with accurate info. Prioritize asking every happy customer for a review. Do not pay for reviews or offer discounts in exchange. Google detects that pattern and will suspend your profile. I've seen it happen three times. It's not a fun reinstatement process.

**Q: What if my insights show very few views — like under 50 a month?**

That means you have a bigger problem than insights can solve. First, make sure your profile is verified. Second, check that your category is correct. A "coffee shop" and a "café" are different categories in Google's system, and people search for them differently. Third, check if there are other listings with similar names in your area that might be splitting attention. I once worked with a bakery in Portland that had 30 views a month. Turned out there were three other bakeries with nearly identical names within a two-mile radius. We added "Southeast Portland" to their description and added their exact street name. Views tripled in six weeks.

**Q: Can I use tools to automate posting on Google Business Profile?**

Yes, but be careful. Tools like Hootsuite and Buffer now support GBP posting. They work fine for scheduling regular updates. The risk is that Google has flagged some third-party tools for spammy behavior, and automated posts sometimes show up with slightly different formatting or missing buttons. Stick with Google's own app for high-stakes posts like new hours, promotions, or holiday announcements. Use tools for less critical content, like "here's what we did this week" updates.

**Q: I have multiple locations. Do I need separate insights for each?**

Yes. Google treats each location as its own profile. Set up a separate dashboard for each one, or use a multi-location tool like BirdEye or Reputation to see them in one view. Do not put two locations on one profile. I've seen a yoga studio in Chicago try to list both its Lincoln Park and Wicker Park locations on a single profile. Google suspended both. Took three months to get them back.

**Q: Do insights from Google Business Profile help with my SEO beyond local search?**

Indirectly, yes. If people find your profile, click through to your website, and spend time there, that signals to Google that your website is relevant. It's a weak signal compared to backlinks and on-page content, but it adds up. A small plumbing company in Denver saw their organic search traffic for non-local terms go up 15% after they optimized their profile. Not a direct link, but correlation worth paying attention to.

They are the ones who check once a week, make one small change, and repeat. That consistency is what moves the needle — not a perfect profile launched on day one.
