## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Sending Every Customer the Same Email

I watched a groomer in Austin, Texas, named Amanda burn through $2,300 in discounts over three months because she sent her "20% off your next groom" email to her entire list of 1,400 people. Every single subscriber got it. Every single one.

The problem? Three hundred of those people had already booked their next appointment that same week. She gave them a discount they would have used anyway. Another 180 people on her list hadn't visited in over 14 months. They took the discount, came in once, and disappeared again. She made $0 in incremental revenue from those 480 emails. She just left money on the table.

Here's what I actually did when I fixed her account: we built Klaviyo segments based on real behavior. Customers who booked within the last 30 days? No discount — send them a "thank you" with a referral link instead. Customers who hadn't been back in 90 days? That's where the 20% off goes. Customers who spent over $80 per visit? Send them a VIP early-access email for new services.

Within two months, Amanda's email revenue per recipient jumped from $1.20 to $3.80. She stopped giving away discounts she didn't need to. The uncomfortable truth is most small business owners over-discount because they're afraid to send a "boring" email. Your customers don't need a coupon every week. They need a reason to remember you exist.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring the Rebooking Window

A groomer in Nashville named Carlos told me he was "too busy" to think about email. He had a full book, 42 dogs a week, and figured marketing was for slow periods. Then I pulled his numbers. He was losing 60% of new customers after their first visit. They'd come in once, love it, and then just... disappear.

Here's what was actually happening: his new clients would book through Instagram or Yelp, come in for a one-time grooming, pay $75, and leave. Carlos never collected their email, never followed up, and never reminded them when the dog was due again. He spent $1,200 a month on Google Ads just to keep that funnel filled.

We fixed it with one Klaviyo flow. A simple "your pup is due for a groom in about 4 weeks" email, sent automatically based on the date of service. Carlos started tracking the appointment date in Klaviyo using a custom property, and the flow fired 25 days later. Within four weeks, his repeat-booking rate went from 40% to 68%. That single flow added $3,840 in revenue per month from existing customers. He cut his Google Ads spend to $600 because he didn't need new customers as badly.

Most pet grooming guides skip this because they focus on "getting more email subscribers." The real money is in emailing the people who already gave you money.

A groomer in Portland, Oregon, named Jenna signed up for Klaviyo and got excited. Too excited. Mix of value (grooming tips, seasonal advice) and promotion (book-ahead reminders, limited slots). We set up her flows to handle the automated stuff, and her one-off campaigns became the exception, not the rule.

After 60 days, her open rate climbed back to 34%, and her unsubscribe rate settled at 0.4%. Her bookings from email actually went up — fewer sends, higher relevance. She told me it felt "boring" to slow down. I told her boring is profitable.

Open rate on that email: 47%. Click rate: 22%. Revenue from that single send: $1,640. Maria told me she had been sending the same email for five years and never thought to change it. Most guides skip this part — the revenue is in the data you already have, not in the new emails you're writing.

## Integrating Klaviyo With Your Booking System

If your pet grooming business doesn't use a booking system, get one. Today. I've seen too many groomers run on phone calls and a paper calendar. That works until it doesn't. The difference between a $3,000-a-month groomer and a $15,000-a-month shop is often just the booking system.

I'm partial to Booksy and Square Appointments. Both integrate with Klaviyo. Square Appointments is cheaper per month and works well if you also sell retail products (leashes, shampoos, treats) from your shop. Booksy has better discovery features if you're trying to attract new customers through their marketplace. Either works.

Here's the exact integration I set up for a groomer in Chicago named Terrence:

1. **Connect the booking system to Klaviyo.** Square and Booksy both have one-click Klaviyo integrations in their settings. This took Terrence seven minutes. The integration automatically syncs customer names, email addresses, and appointment history.

2. **Set up custom properties.** In Klaviyo, create custom properties for "Last Groom Date," "Preferred Service," and "Average Spend." The integration can populate these automatically if you map them correctly. Terrence's Square data included dog breed, so I added "Breed" as a property too.

3. **Build a rebooking flow.** Trigger: customer completes a booking. Wait 21 days. Send email: "We saved your pup's style notes. Book your next visit and get 10% off if you book within 7 days." After 7 days, if they didn't book, remove the discount and send a plain reminder at day 30. Terrence's rebooking rate went from 35% to 62% in 90 days. That flow alone added $4,580 in revenue.

4. **Track the lifetime value per breed.** This is the part nobody does. Terrence discovered that his poodle clients spent 40% more per visit and booked twice as often as his husky clients. He stopped running generic ads and started targeting poodle owners specifically on Facebook. Cost per acquisition dropped from $28 to $12. He spent $500 on that campaign and got $4,200 in new client revenue within 60 days.

The integration isn't optional if you want to scale past a one-person operation. If you're still using a paper book, you're leaving $1,000 to $3,000 a month on the table. I've seen this number at five different shops. It's not a guess.

## SMS Marketing: The Missed Opportunity for Pet Groomers

Email is the workhorse. SMS is the scalpel. Most pet groomers ignore SMS because they think it's expensive or intrusive. They're wrong about both.

Klaviyo's SMS feature costs $15/month for the first 500 contacts, plus $0.01 per message sent. For a typical groomer with a list of 800 customers, that's roughly $25 a month. If you send one weekly reminder SMS and get one extra booking a month from it, you've already made your money back 10 times over.

I set up SMS for a groomer in Phoenix named DeShawn. He was losing $900 a month to no-shows. People would book online, forget, and he'd be stuck with an empty 45-minute slot. He tried calling to confirm, but that took 30 minutes of his day. He tried text reminders from Square, but they looked generic and got ignored.

Here's what we built in Klaviyo: an automated SMS flow. Two messages. First one: "Hey [first name], just a heads up — [dog name]'s grooming appointment is tomorrow at [time]. Reply YES to confirm or call us to reschedule." That message goes 24 hours before the appointment. Second one: "We'll see [dog name] in 2 hours! If anything changes, call us at [phone]." That goes 2 hours before.

DeShawn's no-show rate dropped from 14% to 2% in 30 days. He recovered $852 in lost appointment revenue the first month. He spent $12 on SMS credits.

The mistake most people make with SMS is sending too many messages or writing them like marketing copy. Your customers are reading SMS on their phone while they're at work, or making dinner, or walking the dog. Keep it short. Don't use emojis unless your brand actually talks that way. Don't put a link in every message. A confirmation SMS doesn't need a CTA — it just needs to be useful.

The other SMS win for pet groomers: birthday reminders. I know. It sounds cheesy. But DeShawn started sending a simple SMS to his customers on their dog's recorded birthday (he collected it at checkout with a free treat). "Happy birthday to [dog name]! Come in this week and get a free nail trim with any full groom." That single message generated $640 in revenue over three months from people who hadn't booked in 60+ days. Cost: about $0.60 in SMS credits.

## Customer Segmentation: How to Stop Wasting Money on Wrong Audiences

Segmentation is the single most underused feature in Klaviyo. I've set up accounts for 40+ small businesses, and maybe 10% of them segment their lists properly. The rest send the same email to their entire list and wonder why results are mediocre.

Here's a framework I used for a groomer in Atlanta named Keisha. She had a list of 1,200 people and was sending the same monthly newsletter to all of them. Open rate: 16%. Revenue per send: about $350.

We rebuilt her into five segments:

**Segment 1: High-value, frequent clients.** People who booked every 4-6 weeks and spent $70 or more per visit. These are your best customers. Send them early access to new services, invite them to refer friends, and give them a loyalty program. Keisha had 140 people in this segment. Their average lifetime value was $680.

**Segment 2: Lapsed frequent clients.** People who used to come every 6-8 weeks but haven't booked in 90+ days. These are your biggest pool of recoverable revenue. Send them a "we miss you" email with a slightly better discount than you'd give a new customer. Keisha had 220 people in this segment. She recovered 38 of them in 60 days, worth $2,850.

**Segment 3: New, one-time clients.** People who booked once and never came back. They need a different message than Segment 2. Send them a "your pup looked amazing — here's when to book next" email with educational content about grooming schedules. Keisha converted 22% of these into repeat clients within 90 days, up from 8% before segmentation.

**Segment 4: Seasonal clients.** People who book twice a year (usually summer shedding season and before the holidays). Don't send them weekly emails. They'll unsubscribe. Send them two well-timed reminders per year. Keisha's seasonal clients had a 0.2% unsubscribe rate because she respected their frequency.

**Segment 5: Non-buyers.** People who subscribed but never booked. They came from a giveaway or lead magnet. Send them a low-pressure, high-value email with grooming tips and a single, soft CTA. Keisha had 190 people in this segment. After three months of non-promotional content, 31 booked an appointment. Those 31 people had an average lifetime value of $210 so far.

The total result after 120 days: Keisha's email revenue went from $350 per send to $1,040 per send. She sent the same number of emails. She just stopped sending the wrong message to the wrong person.

If you do nothing else with this article, segment your list. Pick three groups based on recency of booking. Spend 30 minutes setting it up in Klaviyo. It will pay for itself in the first week.

Yes, but only if you're willing to grow it. Klaviyo's pricing starts at $20/month for 250 contacts. **Q: What's the best way to actually get people to sign up for my email list?**

Stop putting your signup form on a page nobody visits. Put it on your booking confirmation page, your thank-you page, and your in-store checkout screen. Offer a specific reason to join, not "sign up for updates." Try: "Join our list and we'll remind you when your dog is due for a groom. Plus, a free nail trim on your next visit." I've seen conversion rates of 35% on that offer. Generic "subscribe for 10% off" gets 5%.

**Q: Can I import my existing list from Mailchimp or Square into Klaviyo?**

Yes. Klaviyo has native imports for Mailchimp, Square, and most other platforms. You export a CSV from your old tool, upload it into Klaviyo, and map the fields. Takes about 20 minutes. One warning: don't re-import people who unsubscribed from your old tool. Klaviyo will flag it, but it's a pain to clean up. Export your active subscribers only.

**Q: What's the one email I should set up first if I have zero time?**

The rebooking reminder. Set up a Klaviyo flow that triggers based on the date of the completed appointment. Wait 4 weeks. Send a single email: "It's been a month since [dog name]'s last groom. We have openings this week. Book here." That one flow will generate more revenue than any other single email in your account. I've seen it add $2,000 to $4,000 a month for a busy solo groomer.

**Q: How do I track if my emails are actually making money?**

Use Klaviyo's revenue attribution. It's in the analytics tab. It shows you exactly how much revenue came from each flow and each campaign, with a 30-day attribution window. If an email doesn't show at least 5x your monthly Klaviyo subscription cost in attributed revenue, change the subject line, change the offer, or kill the email. Don't send emails just because you feel like you should.
