## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating Your Email List Like a Newsletter No One Reads

**The story from Austin, TX**

Mike owns "Lucky Strikes Lanes" in Austin. He had 1,800 email addresses he'd collected over five years—bowlers signing up for league schedules, birthday party inquiries, people who'd bought shoes at the pro shop. But his email marketing was a once-a-month newsletter with generic bowling tips ("Did you know the term 'strike' dates back to 1895?") and a link to book a lane.

Open rate was 12%. Click-through rate was 0.8%. He was paying $49/month to Mailchimp for this.

**What went wrong**

Mike was treating email as an obligation—"we should send something." He wasn't segmenting his list. League bowlers got the same message as birthday party parents. He'd send one email and that was it for the month. No follow-ups, no offers, no urgency.

**The fix**

I had Mike split his list into three segments: league bowlers, party bookers (past and potential), and walk-ins. Then we set up a simple automated sequence:

- **League bowlers:** Weekly email on Monday mornings with the upcoming week's schedule, standings, and a "Buy a teammate a beer" promo for the bar. Open rates jumped to 38%.

- **Party bookers:** Two-email sequence. First email: "Your birthday bowling party is 8 weeks away—here's how to customize your package." Second email three days later: "We've got a Saturday 2-4 PM slot still open for your date—only one left." Conversion rate on the second email was 22%.

- **Walk-ins:** A "We miss you" email that fired 14 days after someone bowled but hadn't been back. It included a $10 off a return visit code. Redemption rate: 17%.

**The outcome**

Mike's monthly email cost stayed at $49/month. His party bookings went from 12 per month to 22. The "we miss you" sequence brought back 34 walk-ins in the first month. League roster retention improved because the weekly comms kept bowlers engaged. Total incremental revenue from the email changes: $3,800/month. Took about four hours to set up.

### Mistake #2: Running Google Ads Without Negative Keywords

**The story from Nashville, TN**

A bowling alley called "Music City Lanes" was spending $1,800/month on Google Ads. They'd hired a friend's nephew to set it up. He turned on broad match for everything: "bowling near me," "bowling alley Nashville," "birthday party ideas," "family fun activities."

What the nephew didn't add: negative keywords.

Every time someone searched for "bowling ball for sale" or "bowling shoes size 12" or "how to bowl better," Music City Lanes paid for that click. They also got clicks from people searching "bowling near me" while sitting in Memphis, Clarksville, and Chattanooga—two to three hours away.

**What went wrong**

The campaign was spending money on people who would never walk through the door. The cost-per-click was $2.40 on average, but the conversion rate was 0.9%. They were getting about 650 clicks a month, but only 5–6 people were actually booking lanes or calling. That's $300 per booking.

**The fix**

We rebuilt the campaign with:

- **Exact match and phrase match keywords only.** "Birthday party bowling Nashville TN." "Bowling alley league registration Nashville." "Family bowling near me 37203."

- **Negative keywords:** "ball," "shoes," "tips," "drills," "for sale," "used," "how to," "pro shop," "tournament rules," and every city outside a 15-mile radius.

- **Location targeting:** Set to a 10-mile radius around the alley. Turned off "people interested in your location" (Google's setting that shows your ads to people outside your radius if Google thinks they might visit). That setting alone had been burning 30% of the budget.

**The outcome**

Monthly spend dropped to $1,200. Clicks dropped to 380, but cost-per-click fell to $1.60. Conversion rate went up to 5.2%. That meant 20 bookings per month at $60 per booking. Same $1,200 they were spending before? They'd get 40 bookings. Music City Lanes reallocated the $600 savings into a separate campaign for birthday party packages. That campaign brought in an additional $2,100 per month in party revenue.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Yelp

**The story from Chicago, IL**

"Wrigleyville Bowl" had 47 Yelp reviews. Average rating: 2.9 stars. The owner, Donna, told me Yelp was "a scam" and that she'd "never pay them a dime." Fair enough. I don't love Yelp's business model either. But her 47 reviews were filled with three recurring complaints: the lanes weren't oiled consistently, the shoe rental process took too long, and the bar ran out of domestic beer during league nights.

Every week, people would search "bowling alley Chicago," see Wrigleyville Bowl at 2.9 stars, and click on the 4.1-star alley three blocks away. Donna was losing walk-in traffic she didn't even know existed.

**What went wrong**

Donna had no system for monitoring or responding to reviews. Positive reviews got silence. Negative reviews got silence. Google Maps was also showing her Yelp rating right alongside her own business listing. A 2.9-star rating next to a competitor's 4.1-star rating is like putting a "We're okay, I guess" sign on your front door.

**The fix** (without paying Yelp)

- **Respond to every review.** Within 48 hours. Even the positive ones. "Thanks, Sarah—glad you had a good time. We've got cosmic bowling every Friday now if you want to come back."

- **For negative reviews:** Acknowledge the problem, offer a solution, and take it offline. "David, I'm sorry about the wait for shoes. We've changed our check-in process. Can you email me at donna@wrigleyvillebowl.com? I'd like to comp your next visit."

- **Fix the actual problems.** The shoe rental bottleneck was solved by installing a second shoe counter and having staff pre-size shoes for league bowlers. The bar issue was solved by setting a minimum order threshold with their distributor.

- **Ask happy customers to leave a review.** We put a small sign at the checkout counter: "Had fun? Leave us a review on Google. Show this to your server for a free drink next time."

**The outcome**

Donna never paid Yelp a cent. But within six months:
- Yelp rating went from 2.9 to 4.0 (she got 34 new reviews)
- Google rating went from 3.4 to 4.5
- She could track the uptick in walk-ins through her point-of-sale system—the "new customer" flag went from 12% of walk-ins to 28%
- She estimates Yelp and Google improvements brought in roughly $1,800/month in additional revenue from new customers who found her online

### Mistake #4: Having No Loyalty Program for Leagues

**The story from Denver, CO**

"Cherry Creek Lanes" had six bowling leagues running three nights a week. About 180 regular bowlers. The leagues had been running for years. But roster sizes were shrinking—down about 15% year over year. The owner, Jeff, was losing bowlers to a newer alley that had renovated and added a sports bar.

Jeff had no loyalty program. No way to reward his best customers. The leagues just showed up, bowled, paid, and left. If someone missed a week, no one followed up. If a team dropped out, no one asked why.

**What went wrong**

Jeff was treating league bowlers like commodities. A league bowler who comes in once a week for 30 weeks spends about $1,200 a year on lane fees and another $400 on food and drinks. That's $1,600 per year. Over five years, that's $8,000. Losing 15% of 180 bowlers meant Jeff was bleeding $43,200 in annual revenue—and had no system to stop it.

**The fix**

We set up a simple punch card system through Square's loyalty program—no third-party app needed.

- **Every league night:** Bowler checks in, gets one stamp. Five stamps = a free game. Ten stamps = a free pitcher of beer for the team.

- **Annual "bowler status":** Bowlers who attend 25+ weeks get "Gold Pin" status. Gold Pin gets 10% off at the bar, a free lane for a friend on their birthday, and priority lane selection the next season.

- **Missed week trigger:** If a league bowler misses two weeks in a row, Jeff sends a text (Square's marketing tab handles this): "Hey [name]—noticed we missed you at Cherry Creek. Everything okay? Your team is waiting for you. Reply to this text and I'll buy your first round when you're back."

**The outcome**

- Roster retention went from 60% year-over-year to 88% in the first season
- The missed-week texts brought back 12 bowlers who said they'd just gotten busy and forgot—they'd been bowling at Cherry Creek for years but no one had ever checked in with them
- The "Gold Pin" status became a point of pride. Bowlers started wearing the pin (Jeff ordered them from a local trophy shop for $2 each)
- League revenue (lane fees + food/drink) went from about $144,000/year to $177,000/year—a $33,000 increase

## What to Do with Your Existing Customer Data

Your point-of-sale system is probably sitting on gold and you're ignoring it.

Most bowling alleys use Square, Toast, or a legacy POS system like Centerplate. In my experience, fewer than 20% of bowling alleys actually look at their transaction data to find patterns.

Here's what to check this week:

**Find your "lapsed league bowlers."** Run a report of everyone who paid for a league lane rental more than 60 days ago but hasn't returned. That list is full of people who loved bowling once. A simple postcard ("Your lane is waiting—$10 off your next visit") brought back 8% of lapsed bowlers for a client in Portland, OR. Cost of postcards: $0.37 each. Return: $28 per responding customer in lane and bar revenue.

**Identify your "party booker" source.** For every birthday party that booked in the last 12 months, how did they find you? Google search? Referral from a previous party parent? Yelp? Facebook ad? If you don't know, your marketing budget is being spent blind. One alley in Raleigh, NC was spending $800/month on Facebook ads for birthday parties, but when we actually asked the last 20 party bookers where they heard about the alley, 16 said "my sister had her kid's party here last year." The Facebook ads were getting credit for word-of-mouth referrals. We shifted that $800 to a referral program—$50 off the next party for every referral that booked. Party bookings went up 40%.

**Run a "food and beverage" attachment report.** What percentage of lane rentals include a food or drink purchase? If it's under 40%, you're leaving money on the table. The fix is a simple upsell: the booking confirmation screen on your website should automatically add a "birthday party package includes pizza and soda for $12/person" or "add a pitcher of beer for $8 when you book your lane online." One alley in Phoenix saw food revenue per lane go from $5.50 to $12.80 just from adding this option to their online booking form. No ads, no staff training, just a checkbox.

## How to Set Up a Referral Program That Actually Works

Every bowling alley asks for referrals. Most do it wrong.

"Tell your friends about us!" is not a referral program. It's a wish.

**What a real referral program looks like:**

You need a reason for someone to refer. Not a discount. A reason.

For league bowlers: "Bring a new team to the next season and your team's lane fees for the first month are free." That's a concrete benefit. The referring team gets to decide if the new team is worth them saving $240 in fees. If they say yes, they've already vouched for you. That new team has a 70% higher retention rate in the first season because they came with a personal connection.

For birthday parties: "Book your party, and if three other families book parties within 30 days based on your referral, your party is free." The math works because the margin on a birthday party (average $350 per party, cost of goods about $120) leaves you $230 in gross profit. Giving away one party to get three is still a net positive.

For walk-ins: "Buy a lane, get a punch card. Fill it up with 10 stamps (one per visit), and you get a free lane for a friend. That friend gets half off their first visit." The half-off coupon has a redemption rate of about 25% when handed directly from a friend. Way higher than a digital coupon that shows up in someone's spam folder.

**The tool:** Use a simple tracking system. Punch cards work great for volume up to about 200 active users. After that, switch to a Square or Clover loyalty program that can auto-track referrals. The cost is $0—you're already paying for the POS system. The only cost is the discount you give away, which should be priced into the program from the start.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My bowling alley has been around for 30 years. Do I really need to worry about SEO and Google ads?**

If every person who walks through your door already knows about you, no. But if you've noticed fewer birthday party bookings, fewer league teams signing up, or more empty lanes on weekend afternoons, then yes. People search for things now. They don't drive around looking for a "BOWLING" sign. Google processes over 8.5 billion searches per day. If you're not showing up when someone in your town searches "birthday party idea near me," you're invisible to the most direct source of new customers you have. It's not the 1990s anymore. The phone book isn't coming back.

**Q: I've tried social media and it didn't work. Why should I try again?**

"Didn't work" usually means one of three things: you posted inconsistently (three posts in April, nothing in May), you posted uninteresting content (a photo of a bowling ball on a rack), or you had no call to action ("Here's a picture of our shoe counter. Have a great day!"). Social media works when it's tied to something specific. Post a video of last night's league champion getting the trophy. Tag them. They share it. Their friends see it. One of those friends decides to join next season. That's a customer acquired for the cost of a 30-second video. If you're not willing to do that, then yes, social media won't work for you. But if you're willing to try a different approach, start with a single, specific campaign: "We're giving away a free pizza party for 10 people to the best video of your worst gutter ball. Tag us by Friday." That's it. One post. See what happens.

**Q: Is it worth running ads for a bowling alley? Margins are already thin.**

Depends on which ads and how you measure them. If you're running a broad brand awareness campaign with a $3,000/month budget and measuring "impressions," you're burning money. If you run a $300/month campaign targeting people within 5 miles of your alley who searched for "kids birthday party ideas" in the last 7 days, and you send them to a landing page that books a party with one click, you'll probably get 3–5 bookings before your $300 runs out. Each booking is worth about $350 in revenue. That's a 4x–6x return on ad spend. Margins thin? Not on birthday parties. The food and beverage markup is massive. A $12 pizza costs you about $3.50. The bowling lane costs you the same whether someone is on it or not. The marginal cost of filling an empty lane is nearly zero.

**Q: What's the single most expensive mistake I'm probably making right now?**

Not asking for the booking. Here's what I mean: Someone visits your website. They see your lanes, your prices, your hours. Then what? If there's no prominent "Book a Lane" button—or if that button takes them to a phone number they have to call during business hours—you're losing people. I've tested this with 14 local businesses across different categories. Adding a one-click booking button to the homepage increased bookings by an average of 34%. The tool doesn't matter—Square Appointments, Booksy, or a simple Gravity form on your site. What matters is removing friction. If someone has to pick up the phone, talk to a human, wait on hold, get transferred, leave a voicemail, and wait for a call back, half of them will go somewhere else. Fix that one thing this week and watch what happens.

**Q: Should I offer discounts to attract walk-ins?**

Discounts attract price-sensitive customers. That's not always bad—a full alley with lower-margin customers is better than an empty alley. But the smarter play is a time-based offer: "Monday–Thursday before 5 PM: $2 games." That fills slow hours with customers who will also buy food and drinks. The discount doesn't set a long-term expectation because it's tied to a specific time. Walk-in customers who come during a promotion often become full-price customers during peak hours later. The data supports this: about 22% of customers who come for a discounted off-peak session return within 60 days at full price.
