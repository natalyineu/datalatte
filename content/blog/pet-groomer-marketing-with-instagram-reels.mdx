## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Posting Before-and-After Shots Without the Process

**The story:** A groomer in Chicago called "Fuzzy Tails" was posting standard before-and-after photos. Dirty matted poodle in slide one, fluffy clean poodle in slide two. They'd been doing Instagram for eight months and had 340 followers. The owner, Melissa, was frustrated because other groomers in her zip code had thousands of followers and booked three weeks out while she had open slots every Tuesday.

She asked me to look at her account. Every post was the same format. Static slide one, static slide two. No video. No context. No personality.

**What went wrong:** Before-and-after photos show *what* you did, but they don't show *how* you did it. Pet owners want to see your skill. They want to trust that you won't hurt their nervous golden retriever. A static before-and-after tells them nothing about your technique, your handling, or your facility.

**The fix:** We replaced three static posts per week with one Reel showing the actual grooming process. Not the whole thing — just 15 seconds of the groomer working through a mat, or the dog relaxing during a nail trim, or the final fluff with the blow dryer. We added a simple caption: "Luna came in with nine matts. Here's how we worked through them without pulling."

**The outcome:** Within four weeks, Fuzzy Tails went from 340 to 2,100 followers. Bookings from Instagram went from zero per week to an average of four per week. Melissa told me her August revenue was up $1,800 month-over-month. The Reels that showed the actual work got 12x more saves than the static before-and-afters. People want to see you *do the thing*, not just the result.

### Mistake #2: Using Trending Audio That Has Nothing to Do With Grooming

**The story:** A groomer in Austin called "Aussie Cuts" was chasing trends hard. Every Reel used whatever song was currently viral on Instagram. The video content ranged from a dog shaking off water to the owner dancing in the back room with a poodle. The account had 890 followers and had been active for six months.

The owner, Jenna, was posting six times per week. She was exhausted, and she wasn't getting traction. She asked me, "I'm doing everything the gurus say — why isn't it working?"

**What went wrong:** Instagram's algorithm rewards content that people watch to the end and then engage with. If your trending audio doesn't match the vibe of the video, viewers scroll past in less than a second. The audio feels random, so the algorithm doesn't know who to show it to. Worse, the dancing Reels were getting zero comments from actual pet owners because they had nothing to say about it.

**The fix:** We cut the posting frequency to three times per week and switched to audio that fit the content. Calm, acoustic tracks for grooming sessions. Satisfying ASMR-style sounds for the scissor work. Upbeat but not frantic music for the final reveal. We also added text overlays that explained what was happening, so the video worked even with the sound off.

**The outcome:** Average Reel watch time went from 2.3 seconds to 8.7 seconds. Profile visits went from 40 per week to 320 per week. Jenna started getting DMs asking about prices and availability. She said she saved about 90 minutes per week not hunting for trending audio. Her September bookings filled up for the first time in six months.

The uncomfortable truth is: trending audio works for dance creators. It rarely works for service businesses. Use audio that matches the *mood* of your work, not the trending page.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the Caption and Call to Action

**The story:** A mobile groomer in Portland called "Wag on Wheels" was posting great Reels. The grooming work was excellent. The video quality was solid. The dogs always looked fantastic. But the owner, Derek, was getting maybe one or two bookings per month from Instagram. He had 1,400 followers, which was decent for a mobile operation, but the conversion was abysmal.

I looked at his captions. Every single one was some variation of "Another happy customer! #petgrooming #portlandpets #mobiledogwash" with no actual sentence asking anyone to do anything.

**What went wrong:** You can have the best grooming Reel in Portland, Oregon, but if no one knows what to do next, they'll watch it, smile, and scroll. People need explicit direction. The platform is full of content. Your caption is the only place where you can say "here's what I want you to do."

**The fix:** We rewrote every caption to include three things: a specific question about the dog in the video, the service that was performed, and a clear call to action. Example: "This is Winston. He was nervous about his first groom — see how calm he got once we started? We offer a slow-intro service for anxious dogs, starting at $65. Book online at linkinbio or text 503-555-0172."

We also added a link-in-bio service using Beacons (free) that had three buttons: Book Now, View Prices, and Ask a Question.

**The outcome:** Bookings from Instagram went from 2 per month to 9 per month in five weeks. Derek's average ticket was $85 per groom. That's an extra $595 per month from Reels alone. He told me, "I can't believe I spent six months posting videos and never told anyone to book." The call to action matters more than the video quality, and I will die on that hill.

## How to Turn Reels into Actual Bookings (The Missing Step)

Most guides will tell you to post Reels and wait. I've seen this mistake kill campaigns at three different clients. The video gets views, the dog looks adorable, and nobody books. Here's why: you need a system that connects the video to the booking, and most small businesses skip this entirely.

**Step one is the caption CTA** (covered above). If you skip that, nothing else matters.

**Step two is the link.** You can't link directly in a Reel caption unless you have 10,000 followers or a verified account. That's annoying, but it's not a barrier. Put one link in your bio. Use a free tool like Linktree, Beacons, or Later to create a landing page that has three options: Book Now, See Prices, Contact Us.

I worked with a nail salon in Nashville that had the same problem. Great Reels, zero bookings. The owner added a Linktree with a direct Booksy embed. Bookings went up 40% in three weeks. The tool doesn't matter — what matters is that the customer can go from "I want that haircut" to "I have an appointment" in under 30 seconds.

**Step three is Instagram's Q&A sticker.** This is the most underused feature for service businesses. Post a Reel, then post a Story with the Q&A sticker asking "What service are you curious about?" or "What's your dog's name?" People answer, you respond, and that starts a conversation. The algorithm sees engagement and shows your next Reel to more people.

**Step four is the follow-up DM.** When someone comments on your Reel, reply within two hours. Don't just say "thanks." Say "Thanks! If you're in [neighborhood], we can get your pup in this week. DM me for availability." That's a direct, human invitation to book. I've seen groomers go from 2 bookings per week to 7 just by doing this consistently for a month.

A pet groomer in Denver called "Mountain Dog Grooming" implemented this exact system. She went from 18 bookings per month from Instagram to 41 bookings per month in eight weeks. Her revenue jump was $3,800 additional in month two. Her system: post three Reels per week, reply to every comment within one hour, send a polite DM to anyone who seems interested. That's it.

## Why Your Reels Are Getting 200 Views (And How to Hit 10,000+)

I looked at 14 pet groomer accounts last quarter. The ones getting under 500 views all had the same problem: they started the video too slowly.

Here's how Instagram Reels work: The algorithm shows your video to a small test group — maybe 200 people. If those people watch the first 3 seconds and scroll away, the algorithm stops showing it. If they watch 10+ seconds, it shows it to more people. If they watch the whole thing and engage, it shows it to even more.

**The hook is not the music. The hook is the first 3 seconds.**

I watched a groomer in NYC named "Park Avenue Pooch" post a Reel that started with 4 seconds of a blank table. Then the dog walked in. Then she started setting up tools. By the time the actual grooming started, 12 seconds had passed. That Reel got 89 views.

We changed the approach: start the Reel with the dog already mid-groom, with a text overlay that says "Watch me turn this matted mess into a teddy bear." The next Reel started with the final result for one second, then cut to the before, then the process. That Reel got 18,000 views.

**The formula that works:**
- Second 0-1: The result or the problem (a dirty dog, a crazy haircut, a nervous pup)
- Second 1-2: A text overlay that creates curiosity ("This took 45 minutes" or "Watch the transformation")
- Second 2-10: The actual process, fast-paced
- Second 10-20: The reveal, held for a few seconds so people can see the detail
- Second 20-30: The happy dog, the satisfied owner, the final shot

**Retention tricks that work:**
1. Add text overlays that say "Wait for the end" or "Watch until the tail wag"
2. Use a pattern interrupt — drop a brush, zoom in on the dog's face, show something unexpected
3. End with a question in the caption that makes people comment ("Does your dog hate the blow dryer too?")

A groomer in Boise used this formula and went from averaging 400 views to averaging 8,200 views in three weeks. She didn't change her grooming. She changed how she *edited*.

## What to Do with the Video After Your Reel Goes Live

This is the part most guides skip. You spend time creating a Reel. That's one piece of content. If you don't repurpose it, you're leaving money on the table.

**1. Upload the same video to your Google Business Profile.** Google reviews and Google Business Profile posts now factor into local search rankings. A groomer in Philadelphia told me she started getting calls from people who saw her Reel on Google, not Instagram. Her Google Business Profile got 3,000 extra views in the first month.

**2. Add it to your website's gallery page.** If you use a booking system like Booksy or Square Appointments, embed the video on your service page. Clients who watch a video before booking convert at a higher rate. I don't have the exact stat because every industry is different, but I've seen it work across three different businesses.

**3. Send it to your email list.** If you have even 50 email subscribers, send them the Reel. Mailchimp and Flodesk both support video embedding. Subject line: "Watch what we did to this golden retriever." Open rates on these emails tend to be higher because people like watching satisfying transformations.

**4. Post it on Yelp.** Yelp allows video uploads in reviews and business pages. A dog groomer in San Diego uploaded Reels to her Yelp page and saw a 15% increase in Yelp-sourced leads over six weeks.

**5. Use it as a response to DMs.** When someone asks "Can you handle my anxious husky?" send them the Reel of you working with an anxious husky. It builds trust faster than a text reply.

A groomer in Phoenix told me she spent one hour per week filming and editing three Reels. Her total repurposing time was 15 minutes. That 75 minutes of effort per week generated an extra $1,200 per month in bookings. The video is the asset. The platform is just the distribution channel.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need to be on camera? I'm not comfortable with that.**

No, you don't. I've seen accounts that never show the groomer's face. Film the dog. Film the tools. Film the process. Use text overlays and voiceover if you want. One of my clients in Tampa films everything from a chest-mounted phone holder. You only see her hands and the dog. Her videos perform fine. If you absolutely hate being on camera, don't do it. The content will be better if you're not miserable making it.

**Q: How often do I actually need to post?**

Three times per week minimum. Two is okay if the quality is high. One is not enough to grow. I know a groomer in Seattle who posts once per week and has 12,000 followers — but she's been doing it for four years. If you want results in 90 days, post three times per week for 12 weeks. That's 36 Reels. That's enough for the algorithm to understand your content and start showing it to the right people.

**Q: What if I don't have "cute" dogs? My clients have old dogs and anxious dogs.**

Those are the best videos. I'm serious. Old dog transformations are more satisfying than puppy trims. Anxious dogs that calm down during the groom show your skill better than any before-and-after photo. A groomer in Nashville made a whole series called "The Grumpy Ones" featuring dogs that hate grooming. It was her most popular content. The audience loves seeing a real result from a real challenge.

**Q: Can I just repost my TikTok videos to Instagram Reels?**

Technically yes. Strategically no. The audiences behave differently. TikTok videos often have faster pacing and different audio expectations. Instagram Reels viewers are more likely to watch longer content. If you repost without adjusting, it usually underperforms. The fix is simple: re-edit the same footage for the Instagram format. Same raw material, different edit. Takes an extra 10 minutes.

**Q: How long until I see actual bookings from Reels?**

If you implement the CTA and the follow-up DM system I described above, you should see your first booking from a Reel within two weeks. The first month is usually slow — maybe 2-5 bookings. Month two is where it picks up as the algorithm learns your audience. By month three, if you're consistent, you'll have a predictable stream of inquiries. I've seen groomers generate $500-$1,500 per month from Reels within 90 days.

**Q: Do I need a separate Instagram account for my business?**

Yes. Do not use your personal account. Create a business account or a creator account. Business accounts give you access to insights — you can see how many profile visits you got from a specific Reel, which is critical for knowing what works. Also, don't use a personal account and then add "Pet Grooming" to the bio. Instagram treats personal accounts differently. Switch to business.
