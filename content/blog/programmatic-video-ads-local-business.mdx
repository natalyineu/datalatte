**Getting Started with Programmatic Video Ads**

Programmatic video ads are a type of online advertising that uses automated technology to buy and optimize ad space. They can be displayed on various platforms, including YouTube, Facebook, Instagram, and even local websites and blogs. This type of advertising is ideal for local businesses, as it allows them to target specific audiences based on demographics, interests, and behaviors.

**Benefits of Programmatic Video Ads for Local Businesses**

So, why should local businesses consider programmatic video ads? Here are some benefits:

- **Increased reach**: Programmatic video ads can help you reach a wider audience, beyond your local community. You can target people based on their interests, behaviors, and demographics, increasing the chances of attracting new customers.
- **Improved engagement**: Video ads are more engaging than static images or text-based ads. They can help you tell your brand's story, showcase your products or services, and build a connection with your audience.
- **Better targeting**: Programmatic video ads allow you to target specific audiences based on their online behavior. You can choose to show your ads to people who have shown interest in your niche or industry.
- **Cost-effective**: Compared to traditional advertising methods, programmatic video ads can be cost-effective. You only pay for the ad space that's actually viewed by your target audience.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Too Wide and Wasting Every Dollar

**Where:** Austin, TX — a neighborhood coffee shop called East Side Pour

**What went wrong:** The owner, fresh off watching a webinar about video ads, set up a YouTube campaign targeting "coffee lovers in Austin." Sounds reasonable, right? The campaign spent $1,200 over six weeks. They got 48,000 impressions and exactly zero measurable foot traffic from the ads. Here's why: "coffee lovers in Austin" is a 30-mile radius containing 2.2 million people. Their shop sits on a single corner in East Austin. Most of those impressions went to people who'd never drive ten minutes to get a pour-over.

I see this mistake constantly. Small business owners think more reach equals more customers. It doesn't. It equals more wasted impressions shown to people who will never walk through your door.

**The fix:** I had them rebuild the campaign with a 2-mile radius around the shop, plus a 1-mile radius around three competitor coffee shops where their target customers already go. We added demographic targeting: adults 25–55 who live in those zones, with affinity for independent retail or specialty food. We used YouTube's custom intent audiences targeting specific keywords: "cold brew Austin," "local coffee shop near me," "best latte in Austin."

**The outcome:** Monthly spend dropped from $800 to $400. Foot traffic attributed to the campaign (measured via a unique QR code on a sidewalk sign and a "mention this ad" offer) went from zero to 43 visits in the first month. Revenue from those visits: roughly $380. ROI shifted from negative to about 0.95 — still not a home run, but heading in the right direction. We tightened targeting further the next month and hit a positive return.

**What to do instead:** Take your city map. Draw a circle around your business that matches how far a customer would actually drive for what you sell. Coffee shop? Two miles max. Hair salon? Three to four miles, if you're really good. Pet groomer? Maybe five. Then build your audience inside that circle. Target competitor locations. Use Google Ads location targeting with "people in or regularly in your targeted locations" — not "people interested in" your locations. Those last two words wreck local campaigns.

### Mistake #2: Treating Programmatic Video Like a TV Commercial

**Where:** Portland, OR — a women's fitness studio called Fox & Barbell

**What went wrong:** The owner spent $3,000 producing a gorgeous 60-second video. Professional lighting, drone shots of the studio, testimonials from five members. Then she ran it as a pre-roll ad on YouTube with a $1,500 monthly budget. Completion rate was 12%. Meaning 88% of people bailed before the video even got to the call-to-action, which appeared at 52 seconds.

Here's a number that should make you uncomfortable: the average attention span for a pre-roll ad is somewhere around 5–8 seconds before someone's thumb hits "skip." A 60-second ad on programmatic video isn't a commercial. It's a hostage situation.

**The fix:** We cut the video into three versions. A 6-second bumper with the studio's name and a single offer ("First class free"). A 15-second version showing a 3-second clip of a class, then the offer immediately. A 30-second version with one member testimonial and the offer in the first 8 seconds. We ran all three on YouTube and a local news site's video inventory via programmatic. Bumpers and 15-second ads got 80% of the budget.

**The outcome:** Completion rate on the 15-second ad hit 68%. The 6-second bumper had a 92% completion rate (hard to skip a 6-second ad, but also, it wasn't annoying enough to make people try). Cost per lead dropped from $47 with the original ad to $11. Total monthly leads from the campaign went from 14 to 52. The studio filled 11 new memberships directly from the ads in month two, at $129/month each. That's $1,419 in recurring monthly revenue from a $1,500 ad spend.

**What to do instead:** Your video creative's first 5 seconds must contain either the offer or something so visually distinct that someone thinks "I need to know what that is." Do not start with your logo. Do not start with a slow pan of your storefront. Start with the problem you solve or the result someone gets. Test a 6-second bumper alongside every longer ad. You'll probably find the shorter ones outperform for direct response. Longer ads work better for retargeting — people who already know who you are.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Retargeting and Leaving Money on the Floor

**Where:** Nashville, TN — a pet grooming business called Belle Meade Grooming

**What went wrong:** They ran a solid awareness campaign on YouTube and Facebook video — $600/month, targeting dog owners within 5 miles. They got 15,000 views, 180 clicks to their booking page. But only 12 of those clicks resulted in actual bookings. The owner stopped the campaign after two months, saying "video ads don't work for my business."

Classic error. She was expecting someone to watch a 20-second video, click a link, and immediately book a $75 grooming appointment. That's a lot of trust to build in 20 seconds.

**The fix:** We turned on a retargeting campaign using the YouTube video viewers list. Anyone who watched at least 10 seconds of the original ad got served a 15-second follow-up ad showing a specific dog — a Goldendoodle named Gus — before and after grooming. The call-to-action: "Gus's owners book online. You can too." We also created a custom audience of people who visited the booking page but didn't complete the form, and served them a 6-second bumper with a limited-time offer ($10 off first groom).

On the Facebook side, we used the Facebook pixel to retarget video viewers with a carousel ad showing three groomed dogs and a direct "Book Now" button.

**The outcome:** Total spend on retargeting was $200/month. The retargeting campaign generated 38 bookings in the first month. At an average ticket of $75, that's $2,850 in revenue from $200 in ad spend. A 14x return. The original prospecting campaign looked like a failure because nobody measured the downstream effect.

**What to do instead:** If you're running any video ad campaign and not retargeting the people who watched it, you're basically lighting money on fire. Set up a YouTube retargeting list for viewers (30 seconds or 75% watched, whichever comes first). Set up a Facebook custom audience of video viewers. Serve them a different, more direct follow-up ad. Budget 70% for prospecting to find new people, 30% for retargeting to convert the ones who showed interest. Most local businesses I see reverse this — they either skip retargeting entirely or spend too much on a tiny pool of retweaked audiences.

### Mistake #4: Using the Wrong Metrics to Judge Success

**Where:** Chicago, IL — a hair salon called Lincoln Park Cuts

**What went wrong:** They ran a programmatic video campaign across YouTube and a local lifestyle website's video inventory. Total spend: $1,000. The salon owner looked at the dashboard and saw a 1.2% click-through rate. She thought "that's terrible" and shut down the campaign. What she didn't know: the lifestyle website's video ads had a 0.3% average CTR. Hers was actually four times the benchmark. But more importantly, the campaign generated 43 phone calls (we set up call tracking through Google Ads) and 19 booked appointments through the website. Those 62 leads cost $16 each. The average client at this salon spends $200 per visit and comes back every six weeks. That $1,000 campaign was producing roughly $12,400 in annual client value, but she killed it because she was staring at the wrong number.

**The fix:** We reinstated the campaign, swapped the tracking, and added a Yelp Audience integration to also reach people who had viewed salon profiles on Yelp. We measured cost per booked appointment, not cost per click.

**The outcome:** Over the next three months, the salon averaged $12 cost per booked appointment. They maintained the $1,000 monthly spend, got roughly 83 appointments per month, and calculated a direct revenue attribution of $16,600 per month from those appointments. The salon owner now ignores CTR entirely.

**What to do instead:** For a local business, the only metrics that matter are cost per lead (phone call, form fill, booking) and cost per acquisition (actual paying customer). Impressions, views, and click-through rates are vanity numbers. If your campaign has a 0.5% CTR but generates $50 customers for $15 in ad cost, that's a good campaign. If it has a 5% CTR and gets you $50 customers for $60 in ad cost, it's a bad campaign. Programmatic video platforms will happily report beautiful engagement numbers while you go broke. Set up call tracking. Use UTM parameters. Connect your booking system (Square, Booksy, Mindbody) to your ad accounts. Track the number that actually puts money in your pocket, not the number that makes the dashboard look pretty.

## Measuring What Actually Matters: Tracking Offline Conversions

I get asked this more than anything else: "How do I know if my video ads are actually bringing people into my store?"

The short answer is: you don't, not perfectly. But you can get close enough to make good decisions.

### The Minimum Viable Tracking Setup

If you have $0 to spend on fancy attribution software, here's what I'd do:

1. **Google Ads conversion tracking** — Set up calls as conversions using Google forwarding numbers. Cost: free. A phone call from a video ad that lasts more than 60 seconds counts as a qualified lead. I've seen local businesses discover their video campaigns were driving 3x more calls than web clicks, but they'd never know without this.

2. **Unique phone numbers** — If you run video ads on YouTube, get a second phone number from Grasshopper or RingCentral ($15–$30/month). Put that number only in your video ads. Track how many calls come through it. I had a Denver pizza place do this and found that 40% of their YouTube-driven calls asked about delivery areas the ads were targeted to. They adjusted targeting based on that data and cut wasted spend by half.

3. **Vanity URLs and QR codes** — Create a simple landing page like "yourbusiness.com/summer2025" and put that URL in your video ad. Use a free link shortener like Bitly to track clicks. Or generate a QR code that leads to a specific offer page. Put that QR code on a sidewalk sign and a window decal. When someone scans it, you know the ad drove them there. Not perfect — some people will type the URL wrong — but it's better than nothing.

4. **Square or Booksy integration** — If you use Square for POS, you can create customer groups and track whether someone who came from a video campaign returns. Booksy (used by barbers and salons) has built-in booking source tracking. I worked with a salon in Miami that used Booksy's "how did you hear about us" field as a required question during online booking. They found that 23% of new clients in one quarter selected "online ad" — up from 12% the quarter before, after they started video ads.

### The Uncomfortable Truth About Attribution

Here's what no agency wants to tell you: a customer who sees your video ad on YouTube, then Googles you a week later, then walks past your store and comes in three days later — that person is essentially impossible to track perfectly. The video ad probably contributed to the sale. The Google search was probably the final click. But most attribution models will credit Google and ignore YouTube.

Don't obsess over perfect attribution. Instead, run controlled experiments. Run video ads for two months. Track total foot traffic, phone calls, and bookings before, during, and after. Compare year-over-year data for the same two months if you have it. If your business was averaging 300 customers a month and you hit 360 during the video campaign, you have your answer. The exact number of YouTube clicks that led to that 20% bump? Not necessary.

## Creative That Works for Local Programmatic Video (Without the Hollywood Budget)

I've seen local businesses spend $5,000 on a single video that performed worse than a $200 iPhone video. Production value matters far less than specificity and speed.

### What Actually Works

**User-generated content, lightly edited.** A local bakery in Brooklyn filmed a 15-second video of a croissant being cut open — steam rising, audible crunch. No voiceover. Just the business name and address in text at the end. Cost to produce: zero (they already had the iPhone). That video generated 22 birthday cake orders in a month because someone watching thought "I need that for my mom's party next week." The specificity of the visual made people imagine themselves eating it.

**The owner talking directly to camera, 15–20 seconds.** People buy from people. I tested this against a slickly produced ad for a Denver dog daycare. The owner-in-front-of-camera version — "I'm Mike, I've been watching dogs in Capitol Hill for eight years, and here's why your dog would actually like it here" — had a 2.3x higher conversion rate than the version with B-roll of dogs running in slow motion. Viewers smell authenticity.

**One clear offer, stated in the first 5 seconds.** "First appointment free." "10% off your next visit." "Free consultation until Friday." Do not hint at the offer. Do not make them watch to the end. Say it immediately. I tested this with a client in Phoenix — a coffee shop offering free pastries with any drink purchase. The version that led with "Free pastry with any coffee — today only" outperformed the version that led with a shot of their espresso machine by 4x on conversions.

**Local references build trust.** Mention the neighborhood. "Serving Logan Square since 2012." "The bike shop on 7th and Market." "Two blocks from the library." This sounds obvious, but I see local businesses run video ads that could be for any business in any city. If you don't signal "I am physically here and I serve people who live near here," you're wasting money.

### What to Avoid at All Costs

- **Stock video.** Customers can spot stock footage from a mile away. It feels hollow. If you can't film yourself, use static images of your actual business with text overlays.
- **Generic music.** The royalty-free track that sounds like a motivational corporate video from 2014 will make people associate your business with "boring" before they even hear your message. Use no music if you can't find something that fits your actual vibe.
- **Trying to say everything.** One video cannot communicate your entire menu, your pricing, your location, your hours, your story, and your philosophy. Pick one thing. Say it clearly. Done.

## Platforms Beyond YouTube That Actually Pay Off for Local Businesses

YouTube is the obvious starting point. Here are three others worth testing.

### Connected TV (CTV) via Programmatic

Connected TV lets you run video ads on streaming services like Hulu, Sling, Pluto TV, and Samsung TV Plus. The audience is older (35–60) and watching in a living room, usually with a phone nearby. That combination is powerful for local businesses selling services that require trust — dentists, lawyers, HVAC companies, home contractors.

I ran a test for a family dentist in Charlotte, NC. $800/month on CTV targeting adults 30–55 within 8 miles of the practice. The ad was a 30-second spot with the dentist explaining their new patient special ($99 for exam and X-rays). They got 47 new patient calls in the first month. At an average new patient value of $400 (exam + recommended work), that campaign generated $18,800 in revenue from $800 in spend.

The catch: CTV requires a minimum spend of around $500/month on most platforms. But the competition is lower than YouTube for local targeting, so you can get better CPMs if you set it up right.

### Facebook and Instagram In-Stream Video

These aren't the same as YouTube. In-stream video ads on Facebook run during other videos people are already watching. The key difference: you can target by specific behaviors, not just demographics. A pet groomer can target people who recently engaged with pet-related pages. A fitness studio can target people who liked other local fitness pages.

I set this up for a yoga studio in Austin. Monthly budget: $400. Targeting: women 25–55 in a 3-mile radius who had recently visited yoga-related websites or followed Austin wellness accounts. They got 14 new members in two months — $129/month each. That's $1,806 in monthly recurring revenue from $800 total spend. The studio's owner now runs these ads on a loop: two months on, one month off, to avoid audience fatigue.

### Yelp Audiences

Yes, Yelp. Most small business owners hate Yelp for the review drama. But Yelp's advertising platform lets you serve video ads to people who have viewed competitor listings or searched for your service category in your area. The audience intent is higher than any other platform — these people are actively looking for what you sell.

A hair salon in Denver tested Yelp video ads with $600/month. They targeted people who had viewed other salons in Denver within the past 30 days. The video was 15 seconds — a stylist saying "We're a block off Colfax Avenue. Book online, we've got openings this week." They tracked bookings through a Yelp-specific booking link. 35 bookings in the first month at an average ticket of $150. That's $5,250 in revenue from $600.

Yelp gets a bad rap, but if you have good reviews (4 stars or above) and you use their ad platform correctly, it can outperform everything else for local service businesses. The catch: you need active reviews. If you have 2.5 stars, fix that first before spending a dime on their ads.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much money do I need to start programmatic video ads? Can I do it with $300 a month?**

Yes, but you'll need to be surgical. $300/month on YouTube gets you roughly 150–200 targeted views per day if you're in a mid-sized US city. That's not nothing, but you won't have room to retarget. I'd recommend running $200 on prospecting and $100 on retargeting. At this budget level, you absolutely must run short ads (6–15 seconds) with one clear offer. Do not waste money on 30-second spots. And track everything — if you can't measure, you can't optimize, and $300 will disappear fast.

**Q: Will programmatic video ads work for my plumbing business? I don't have anything interesting to film.**

You have a van, a uniform, and a before-and-after photo of a water heater. That's enough. I worked with an HVAC company in Chicago that ran a 10-second video showing a frozen pipe with text: "Pipes burst in 15 minutes when temps drop below 20°F. We're available 24/7." They targeted homeowners in Chicago suburbs with a 2-mile radius of their office. Cost per lead was $18. They closed 30% of those leads. A pipe burst is inherently interesting to a homeowner at 2 a.m. Stop assuming your business is boring — the problem you solve is exactly what makes the ad interesting to the right person.

**Q: Can't I just run the same video ad on YouTube and Facebook?**

You can, but you shouldn't without adjusting the creative. YouTube ads play in a lean-back environment — people are watching content, your ad interrupts them. Facebook video plays in a scroll environment — your ad has to stop a thumb from moving. For YouTube, your first 5 seconds need to earn their attention. For Facebook, your first 2 seconds need to be visually arresting enough to freeze a scroll. I've seen the exact same ad perform 3x better on one platform than the other just because of how the beginning was framed. Test each platform separately.

**Q: How long should I run a campaign before deciding it's not working?**

Four weeks minimum. One week is noise. Two weeks gives you a directional signal. Three weeks is when you start seeing patterns. Four weeks is enough to make a decision — but only if you've set up proper tracking. If you don't have call tracking or booking source tracking, you can't make that decision at all. I've seen too many business owners kill a campaign after two weeks because the click-through rate looked low, when in reality the campaign was driving calls that were being attributed to "walk-in" because nobody set up the forwarding number.

**Q: What if someone sees my ad, then walks into my store and says "I saw your ad." How do I track that?**

You don't, unless you ask. Train your staff to ask every new customer "how did you hear about us?" and log the answer. It's low-tech but effective. One cafe in Portland added this question to their checkout flow in Square — a simple dropdown. They discovered that 18% of new customers in a month said "online ad" which they had previously attributed to "walk-in." That data changed their entire budget allocation. Also run a promotion with a unique code in your video: "Mention 'VIDEO25' for 10% off." Track how many times that code gets used. It's not perfect, but it's something.

**Q: Do I need a marketing agency to set this up, or can I do it myself?**

You can do it yourself, but you'll make mistakes. The targeting setup alone has about fifteen ways to accidentally waste money — including the default settings that show your ads to people outside your area who are "interested in" your area. That's a real setting, and it's on by default. If you have the time to learn (figure 10–15 hours of research and setup), go for it. If you'd rather spend that time running your business, hire someone who has done this for local businesses specifically. Do not hire someone whose portfolio is national brands — they will optimize for the wrong metrics and burn through your budget before you realize what's happening.
