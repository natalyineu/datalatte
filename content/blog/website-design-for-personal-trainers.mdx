### Designing a Website for Personal Trainers

When it comes to designing a website for personal trainers, there are a few key elements to consider. Here are some steps to follow:

1. **Keep it simple**: Avoid cluttering your website with too much information or too many images. You want to make it easy for clients to find what they're looking for.
2. **Use a clear call-to-action (CTA)**: Make it clear what you want clients to do next, whether it's booking a session or signing up for a free consultation.
3. **Highlight your services**: Make sure your website clearly outlines the services you offer, including any specialized training programs or packages.
4. **Add a blog**: A blog is a great way to showcase your expertise and attract potential clients who are searching for information on a particular topic.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Hiding Your Price Like It’s a State Secret

**The story:**
I worked with a personal trainer in Austin, TX — let’s call him Mike. He was strong on Instagram, had a clean website, and booked maybe two new clients a month. His site had a big “Let’s Talk!” button. That button opened a contact form with six fields, including “What’s your fitness history?” and “What are your short-term and long-term goals?”.

Mike was proud of that form. He thought it showed he was thorough.

What actually happened: people opened the form, realized they’d have to write three paragraphs about their relationship with cardio, and closed the tab. I checked his analytics. The page got 800 visits a month. Only 12 people submitted the form. That’s a 1.5% conversion rate.

**The fix:**
We replaced the form with a single line of text: “Small group sessions start at $35. One-on-one starts at $65. No contracts.” Right below that, a button that said “Book a Free 15-Minute Call” — took them straight to a calendly link. No password, no account needed.

**The outcome:**
Conversion rate went from 1.5% to 8% in six weeks. That’s roughly 64 booked calls per month instead of 12. If even half of those turned into clients at $65/session, twice a week, that’s an additional $3,380/month in revenue.

**Why this works:**
People who are shopping for a personal trainer are price-sensitive and decision-avoidant. If you force them to jump through hoops before they know if you’re in their budget, they leave. Show the price. Let them self-qualify. Then offer a low-friction next step.

### Mistake #2: The “I Can Train Anyone” Website

**The story:**
A female trainer in Nashville, TN had a website that said: “I help people get stronger, lose weight, and feel better.” Under that was a photo of her with a male client doing a deadlift, then a photo of a 55-year-old woman on a treadmill, then a photo of a teenager hitting a heavy bag.

She was trying to be everything to everyone. Her bounce rate was 73%.

**The fix:**
We cut the site down to one specific audience: women over 40 who want to build strength without injury. Every headline, every photo, every testimonial spoke to that person. She rewrote her homepage so the first sentence was: “You’re over 40, you’re tired of being told to ‘just eat less,’ and you want to deadlift your bodyweight without throwing your back out.”

**The outcome:**
Six months later, her client roster went from 12 to 28. Average session price: $70. That’s roughly $9,000/month in additional revenue. Her bounce rate dropped to 38%. She stopped getting calls from college kids wanting to “get ripped.” Those calls were time wasters anyway.

**Why this works:**
When your website says “I help everyone,” you help no one. Specific audiences trust you because you clearly understand their problem. A 55-year-old woman with joint pain does not want to see a photo of a 22-year-old doing box jumps. She wants to see someone who looks like her, who talks about her actual concerns — like hip mobility and hormonal changes.

### Mistake #3: Making the Booking Process a 12-Step Program

**The story:**
A trainer in Portland, OR had a site with a “Book Now” button. I clicked it. It took me to a separate scheduling page where I had to create an account, select a package, pick a time slot, enter my credit card, agree to three terms-of-service documents, and then confirm via email.

I gave up at step five.

I called her and asked, “How many people actually complete this process?” She said she didn’t know. She was using a scheduling tool her friend recommended. She’d never tested the flow herself.

I did a quick audit. Of 450 people who clicked “Book Now” in a month, 28 completed a booking. That’s a 6% drop-off at the first step alone.

**The fix:**
We switched to a tool called Booksy, which lets clients book a session in three clicks: select service → pick time → confirm. No account required. No credit card upfront for the first session. We also added a “Book via Text” link on her mobile site that opened her SMS with a pre-written message: “Hi [trainer name], I’d like to book a free intro call.”

**The outcome:**
Month one: 94 bookings. That’s a 235% increase. She was skeptical until the first week hit and she had to turn people away. She raised her rates by $10/session the next month. No drop in bookings.

**Why this works:**
Every additional click in a booking flow is a chance for the client to second-guess themselves. Remove friction. Let them book the way they want — text, click, or a three-step calendar. Do not require an account for a first visit. That’s for established clients, not cold traffic.

## Where to Spend Your First $500 of Marketing Budget

If you’re a personal trainer with a decent website but nobody’s coming, your first $500 should go to two places — not three, not four, but two. I’ve seen too many trainers spread $300 across Facebook ads, Yelp, and a Google Ads campaign, then wonder why none of them worked. Pick one channel, do it well, then expand.

**Option A: Google Local Services Ads ($400/month)**

This is different from regular Google Ads. Local Services Ads appear at the very top of search results, right above the paid ads. You pay per lead, not per click. For personal trainers, leads typically cost $20-$40 in mid-size US cities like Denver or Charlotte.

I tested this with a trainer in Denver last year. He was getting zero organic traffic. After three weeks of Local Services Ads, he was getting 6–8 leads per week at $28/lead. About half converted. His monthly spend was $450. He added roughly $2,500 in new monthly recurring revenue within 60 days.

The catch: you need to pass a background check and have an active business license. If you’re a sole proprietor training out of a park, you might not qualify. But if you’ve got a legit setup, this is the highest-ROI channel I’ve seen for local service businesses.

**Option B: Yelp Ads ($100/month + optimization time)**

Yelp gets a bad rap, and some of it is deserved. Their sales team will call you weekly asking you to spend $500/month. Don’t.

But Yelp has a $100/month “self-serve” ad option that actually works if you set it up correctly. The trick is to claim your profile, add 10+ high-quality photos (not selfies), respond to every review (good and bad), and use the “Request a Quote” button.

A trainer in Chicago spent $100/month on Yelp ads for six months. Her monthly leads started at 2 per month and hit 12 per month by month four. She attributes about $1,200/month in new client revenue to Yelp alone. Yelp is not a volume play — it’s a credibility play. Many clients will check your Yelp page before booking even if they found you via Google or Instagram. A clean, active profile with good reviews seals the deal.

**Where not to put your first $500:**

- Facebook Ads for a brand-new page with 120 followers. You’ll burn cash.
- Instagram influencers in your city. They’re expensive and their audience rarely converts.
- A custom mobile app. You do not need an app. You need a text message and a link to your booking page.

## Email Marketing: The $0 Channel Trainers Ignore

Most personal trainers I meet have zero email list. They’re all-in on Instagram, fighting the algorithm for scraps. Meanwhile, email marketing has an average ROI of $36 for every $1 spent — and it works even better for local service businesses.

I helped a trainer in Columbus, OH set up a simple email flow in Mailchimp. She had 47 email addresses from people who’d filled out her “free consultation” form over the past year. We tagged them as “lead” and set up a three-email sequence:

1. **Day 1:** “Hey, I remember you were interested in training. I’ve got two openings this week if you want to try a session for $20.”
2. **Day 7:** “Three success stories from clients who started where you are.” (Just text and one photo each.)
3. **Day 14:** “Last call — my schedule fills up two weeks in advance. Here’s the link to book a free call.”

No fancy design. No graphics. Just text emails that sounded like they came from her.

Four of those 47 people booked within three weeks. That’s an 8.5% conversion rate from a dead list. Average spend per client: $60/session, twice a week. Those four clients added $1,920 in monthly recurring revenue. Total time spent setting this up: 45 minutes. Cost: $0 (Mailchimp free tier handles up to 500 contacts).

**The tool:** Mailchimp or MailerLite. Both have free tiers. Do not overthink this. Do not buy a $99/month automation tool. You need three emails and a link.

**The rule:** Start collecting emails from day one. Add a simple form to your website footer: “Get free fitness tips every Tuesday.” No one likes that? Fine. Offer a free “5-Minute Mobility Routine” PDF in exchange for an email. Every lead that doesn’t convert today is a lead you can email next month when you run a promotion.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a website if I’m getting clients from Instagram?**

Yes, and here’s why: Instagram owns your audience. If the algorithm changes, if your account gets hacked, if you post something that gets flagged — you lose everything. A website is the only piece of internet real estate you fully control. Also, clients who look you up on Google and find nothing assume you’re not serious. I’ve had three separate trainers tell me they lost potential clients because the person Googled them, found no website, and went with someone who had one. A one-page site costs $200-$500 from a decent freelancer on Upwork. This is not optional.

**Q: How often should I update my website?**

You should touch it once a month at minimum. That doesn’t mean a redesign. It means updating your testimonials, swapping out old photos, adjusting your pricing if it changed, and making sure your booking link still works. I checked a trainer’s site in Phoenix last year and her “Book Now” button linked to a scheduling page that expired. She hadn’t noticed for four months. She estimated she lost 8–10 bookings. That’s a lot of missed revenue from a 2-minute fix.

**Q: Should I put my prices on my website or make people call?**

Put your prices on your site. I know some trainers worry that competitors will copy them. So what? They can find out your prices by pretending to be a client anyway. By hiding your prices, you’re losing anyone who doesn’t want to waste time calling around. The trainer I mentioned from Austin saw a 6x increase in qualified leads just by adding a simple price range. A “Starting at $35” is better than nothing.

**Q: What’s the most important page on my website?**

Your “About” page. Seriously. For a personal trainer, people are buying you, not a workout program. They want to know who you are, what you believe, and whether you’ll judge them for being out of shape. Your About page should answer: why you became a trainer, what kind of clients you work best with, and one specific story about a client you helped. Do not write a generic bio. Write like you talk. A trainer in San Diego rewrote her About page to include the sentence: “I used to hate working out too, so I get it.” Her consultation requests doubled in a month.

**Q: Do I need a blog on my site?**

Only if you’re willing to write consistently. A blog with four posts from 2019 makes you look neglected. If you have time to write one 500-word post every two weeks, do it. It helps your SEO and gives you content to share on social media. If you don’t have time, don’t start. A clean site with great photos, clear pricing, and an easy booking system is better than a site with a dead blog.

**Q: Should I use a website builder like Wix or Squarespace?**

Yes, either works for a personal trainer. Don’t overthink this. Squarespace templates look cleaner out of the box. Wix has more flexibility for custom booking integrations. Both cost about $20-$30/month. The choice matters less than how fast your site loads and how easy it is to book. I’ve seen beautiful Squarespace sites that take 6 seconds to load on mobile — that’s terrible. Use Google’s PageSpeed Insights tool to check your speed. Aim for under 3 seconds.
