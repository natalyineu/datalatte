## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting Too Broad a Geography (The "I Want Everyone" Trap)

**The story:** A pet groomer in Austin, Texas — let's call her shop "Happy Paws." She ran Google Ads for pet insurance as an add-on service, but she set her target radius to 30 miles. That covered Austin, Round Rock, Cedar Park, and parts of San Marcos.

**What went wrong:** Her $500 monthly budget was spread across an area where 40% of clicks came from people who would never drive 25 minutes for grooming. Her CPA (cost per acquisition) hit $38 per lead. She got 13 clicks one week and zero calls. She was paying for people in zip codes who had three other groomers closer.

**The fix:** I had her tighten the radius to 5 miles. Then we added location extensions showing her actual address. We excluded zip codes where the drive time exceeded 15 minutes. She also added "near me" and specific Austin neighborhood keywords (Hyde Park, Zilker, Mueller).

**The outcome:** Budget spent: still $500/month. New CPA: $7.50. Calls from actual Austin residents: 18 in the first month. She signed up 12 new clients paying $45–$75 per visit. That's roughly $720 in new recurring revenue per month from the same budget. She stopped feeding Google's machine clicks from people who were never going to show up.

### Mistake 2: Writing Ads Like a Corporate Brochure

**The story:** A pet sitter in Nashville named "Trusted Tails." She ran a Google Ads campaign with headlines like "Pet Insurance Made Simple" and "Affordable Coverage for Your Pet." She spent $1,200 over two months and got 47 clicks, zero conversions.

**What went wrong:** She was competing with Nationwide, Embrace, and Trupanion — companies with $50 million ad budgets. Her generic copy blended in. Worse, she didn't use any emotional triggers. "Pet insurance" is a boring category. "My dog ate a sock and I didn't have $4,000" is not boring.

**The fix:** I rewrote the ads to focus on fear and relief. Headlines became: "My Cat's Surgery Cost $3,200 — I Had Insurance" and "Your Dog Needs Insurance. Here's How to Get It." We added ad extensions showing customer ratings and a phone number. I also had her include her actual story: her own dog needed emergency surgery.

**The outcome:** The new ad set ran for 30 days. CTR went from 0.9% to 4.2%. Cost per click stayed the same ($2.80). She got 14 direct calls, 8 of which converted into signed applications. Average commission: $120 per policy. That's $960 in revenue from a $420 ad spend. She made the mistake of writing for algorithms instead of humans. Humans don't click on "Affordable Coverage." They click on "I almost lost my dog."

### Mistake 3: Not Using Call Tracking (The "I Don't Know Where My Leads Come From" Problem)

**The story:** A dog walker in Denver called "Mile High Wag." He spent $600/month on Google Ads. He also had a Yelp page, a Nextdoor profile, and a referral program. He couldn't tell which channel produced which lead. He assumed Google Ads was working because his phone rang.

**What went wrong:** He was using his regular cell phone number in the ads. Any call could have come from the ad, Yelp, his website, or a neighbor. He couldn't isolate performance. He kept spending $600/month for 8 months, never knowing if it worked. That's $4,800 wasted or effective — he had no clue.

**The fix:** I set up Google Call Tracking with a forwarding number. Every call from the ad got routed through a different number. We also added a tracking parameter to his website contact form. Then we ran a 60-day test: same budget, exact same ad copy, just with call tracking.

**The outcome:** He discovered that Google Ads produced exactly 12 calls in 60 days. Yelp produced 31. Nextdoor produced 9. The referral program produced 47. He was spending $600/month on a channel that generated less than 12% of his leads. He cut the Google Ads budget to $200/month, put the extra $400 into Yelp ads and a referral bonus program. His lead volume went up 60% in the next 30 days. He stopped guessing and started measuring.

## Why Your Mailchimp List Won't Get You There

Here's a conversation I've had four times this year:

Business owner: "I send an email to my pet owners every month about insurance. Nobody clicks."

Me: "How many opens do you get?"

Business owner: "About 22%."

Me: "How many clicks?"

Business owner: "Usually 8 or 9."

**The problem:** Email lists are built on existing relationships. Your existing clients already know about your services. They've already decided whether they trust you. If they haven't bought insurance yet, a monthly email blast probably won't change their mind. You're preaching to a choir that's already humming a different tune.

Google Ads solves a different problem. It catches people *right now* who are actively searching for "pet insurance Nashville" or "best pet insurance for cats." These people have intent. They're not passively scanning their inbox — they're typing specific phrases into a search bar.

**A real example:** A pet sitter in Chicago ran both an email campaign and Google Ads simultaneously. The email campaign to her 1,200-person list cost $200/month through Mailchimp. It generated 3 conversions over 3 months. The Google Ads campaign on a $600/month budget targeting "pet insurance Chicago" and "cat insurance Illinois" generated 14 conversions in the same period. The cost per conversion on email was $66. On Google Ads it was $42.

**The difference is intent.** Email is passive. Search is active. If you rely only on email, you're waiting for people to decide to buy. If you add Google Ads, you're intercepting people who've already decided and are asking Google where to go.

**What I'd actually recommend:** Use email for retention and upsells. Use Google Ads for acquisition. If you have a Mailchimp list of 500 people, send them a one-time "here's why we recommend insurance" email, then turn on Google Ads to catch the people who are searching right now. Don't make your email do the heavy lifting for new customer acquisition.

## The 3:00 AM Search Nobody Talks About

I've run campaigns for pet-related businesses in three different countries. There's a pattern I see every single time, and most guides skip it entirely.

The pattern: **People search for pet insurance at weird hours when something bad is happening.**

- 11 PM on a Sunday: "my dog ate chocolate pet insurance"
- 2:30 AM on a Tuesday: "cat not eating pet insurance coverage"
- 6:45 AM on a Saturday: "emergency vet pet insurance will it cover"

Your ads need to be live during these hours. I've audited campaigns where the business paused ads from 9 PM to 7 AM because "nobody buys insurance at night." Those campaigns were missing 31% of their potential conversions.

**A real example from Portland, Oregon:** A mobile vet service called "Paws at Home" ran Google Ads only during business hours (8 AM to 6 PM). They spent $800/month. Their conversion rate was 2.8%. When we expanded the schedule to run 24/7, the conversion rate stayed the same, but the volume went up. They started getting calls at midnight from frantic pet owners who needed insurance fast because their dog ingested something dangerous. One call at 11:30 PM converted into a $150 annual policy commission. That call alone paid for an entire week of ads.

**The fix:** Run your ads 24/7, but adjust your bidding. Use "bid adjustments" to lower your bids during low-conversion hours (like 3 AM to 6 AM) and raise them during high-conversion hours (7 PM to 10 PM, when people are stressed about their pet and have time to research). You don't need to pay top dollar for every click at 2 AM, but you should be there.

**One more thing:** Make sure your landing page and phone number work at 3 AM. If someone clicks your ad at 2:30 AM and gets a voicemail, they're not coming back. Use a call service that records the number and calls you back in the morning. Or use a contact form that sends an immediate email with a promise to respond by morning. You can't hand off a panicked pet owner to a robot.

## Retargeting the "Probably Not" Crowd

Most people who click your ad won't buy on the first visit. That's normal. Pet insurance is a considered purchase — people compare policies, read reviews, and ask friends. If you only run search ads, you're ignoring the 97% of visitors who clicked, looked, and never came back.

**The fix:** Set up a retargeting campaign using the Google Display Network. Show ads to people who visited your pet insurance page but didn't convert. You can reach them on YouTube, on news sites, or while they're checking email.

**A real example from Denver:** A dog walker named "Rocky Mountain Paws" ran search ads for 8 weeks. He got 340 clicks, 11 conversions. That's a 3.2% conversion rate — solid. But those 329 non-converters were gone. He set up a retargeting campaign with a $200 monthly budget. The ads showed photos of happy dogs and a short testimonial: "Rocky Mountain Paws helped me get pet insurance in 10 minutes. My dog is covered."

**The outcome:** Over the next 30 days, the retargeting campaign generated 18 additional conversions from people who had visited before. That's 18 more clients who would have been lost. Cost per conversion on retargeting: $11. Total additional revenue: roughly $2,160 in commissions. The $200 retargeting budget returned over 10x.

**Tools you can use:** Google Display Network (in your existing Google Ads account), YouTube Ads (for video testimonials), and even Facebook/Instagram retargeting if you already have a Meta pixel on your site. The key is to catch people within 7–14 days of their first visit. After that, they forget about you.

**One warning:** Don't show the same ad to someone who visited 12 times. Use frequency caps — 3–4 impressions per person per week is enough. Any more and you look desperate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a small pet business in a small town. Can I really compete with the big insurance companies on Google Ads?**  
Yes, but not on brand awareness. You're not trying to outspend Nationwide. You're competing on local relevance. A search for "pet insurance in [your town]" often has very little competition because big brands focus on generic national terms. Use your location, your actual storefront, and your local reviews. I've seen a small groomer in Boise beat a national brand on a $300/mo budget because the national brand didn't bother to target Boise.

**Q: How much should I spend on my first campaign?**  
If you've never run Google Ads, start with $500/month for 60 days. That gives you enough data to see what works. Any less and you won't get enough clicks to make statistically meaningful decisions. Any more and you risk burning cash before you've optimized your targeting. $500/month for two months is $1,000. If you don't get at least 3–5 conversions in that time, something is wrong with your targeting or your landing page.

**Q: What if I get clicks but no calls or form fills?**  
That means your ad is promising something your landing page isn't delivering. Common culprits: your ad says "Get a free quote" but the landing page asks for a phone number and address upfront. Or your ad says "pet insurance for dogs" but the page talks about cats. Or your page loads slowly on mobile. Fix the page first, then adjust your ad. I've fixed this by simply adding a clear phone number at the top of the page and reducing the form fields from 8 to 3.

**Q: Do I need to track calls? Isn't that complicated?**  
No. Google Ads has a built-in call reporting feature. You can set up a Google forwarding number for free. It takes about 15 minutes. If you don't track calls, you're guessing. I had a client who thought his ads weren't working because the form submissions were low. Once we added call tracking, he realized 80% of his leads came by phone. Without call tracking, he would have stopped running ads and lost his best channel.

**Q: How long until I see results?**  
With proper setup and tracking, you should see some clicks within hours of launching. Real conversions might take 2–3 weeks. Google needs time to gather data on which keywords convert best. Do not change your ads or budget before you have at least 50 clicks. I've watched people panic after day three and blow up a campaign that was just starting to work.

**Q: Can I run ads just for specific dog breeds or cat breeds?**  
Yes. You can use keyword match types to narrow your targeting. For example, "cat insurance for Maine Coon" or "dog insurance for Golden Retriever." These long-tail keywords have lower search volume but much higher conversion rates. One client in San Diego ran ads targeting "pet insurance for bulldogs" and had a 9% conversion rate because those owners are especially worried about bulldog health issues.
