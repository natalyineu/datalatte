## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Hiding Your Prices Behind a "Call for Quote" Button

A cleaning company in Austin, Texas, called Sparkle & Shine (name changed) spent $3,200 on a Squarespace website that looked beautiful. Soft blues, photos of smiling teams, the whole thing. But every service page ended with "Contact Us for a Custom Quote."

The owner, a woman named Rachel, was terrified of scaring people off with prices. She thought if she showed them, competitors would undercut her. Instead, prospects landed on her site, saw no prices, and bounced to Molly Maid or The Cleaning Authority — who listed theirs right on the homepage.

I checked her Google Analytics. Her site had a 1.8% conversion rate. She was getting about 400 visitors a month and booking maybe seven jobs. Average job value was $180.

**The fix:** We listed base prices. A three-bedroom, two-bath standard clean: $149. Window add-on: $40. Deep clean starts at $249. No fine print, no "starting from" asterisks.

**The outcome:** Her conversion rate went to 4.7% in six weeks. She lost some leads who didn't like the prices, but the leads she kept were serious. Monthly bookings went from 7 to 19. That's $2,160 extra per month — $25,920 annually — from a change that took one afternoon.

If you're worried about competitors seeing your prices: they already know what you charge. They can send a friend to request a quote. Stop pretending it's a secret.

### Mistake #2: Stock Photos of People Who Are Not Your Team

A cleaning business in Denver ran a website with a hero image of a woman in scrubs holding a spray bottle, smiling at a kitchen counter. Generic, but fine, right? Except the woman wasn't their employee. The kitchen wasn't in Denver. It was a Shutterstock photo.

The owner, Marcus, told me clients would show up and say, "I thought you were a bigger company" or "Where's the lady from the website?" He thought the photo made him look professional. It made him look fake.

Worse: his Google reviews mentioned the mismatch between the "stock photo vibe" and the actual two-person crew that showed up. Two people wrote that they almost cancelled because they expected a larger operation.

**The fix:** Marcus spent $350 on a local photographer named Jen who shot his actual team (three people) cleaning a client's home (with permission). He swapped all photos — the website, Google Business profile, Yelp, even his Facebook cover image.

**The outcome:** Within two months, his repeat booking rate went from 22% to 41%. Clients told him they liked seeing the same faces they'd meet at their door. His close rate on new inquiries jumped from 34% to 52%. That's real trust, not a stock photo trust.

Don't have a team? Take a photo of your own van, your own mop bucket, your own hands cleaning a counter. Real is better than polished.

### Mistake #3: Asking for a Phone Call Before You've Earned It

A one-person cleaning operation in Portland ran a Google Ads campaign spending $1,200 per month. She was getting clicks — about 60 per month. Her landing page had one call to action: "Call to Book Your Free Estimate."

Problem: she was cleaning houses during the day. She couldn't answer the phone. Voicemails piled up. By the time she called back (even within an hour), those leads had moved on to the next Google result.

Of those 60 clicks, she booked exactly two jobs. Cost per acquisition: $600. Average job: $200. She was losing $400 every month.

**The fix:** We added an online booking system (I used Booksy, but Square Appointments works too). The landing page now had: a price calculator for standard cleans, a calendar showing available slots, and a text-to-book option. Phone was still available, but not required.

**The outcome:** Of the same 60 clicks, 12 people booked online immediately. Conversion rate jumped from 3.3% to 20%. Her cost per booking dropped to $100. She had to adjust her schedule, but she was actually getting customers instead of voicemail.

The uncomfortable truth: most small business owners overestimate how much their customers want to talk to them. People want convenience. If your website makes them work, they'll go somewhere else.

## The Booking Flow: Where Most Cleaning Sites Lose Money

Let me walk through a specific booking flow I see broken constantly.

A cleaning company in Nashville had a website that looked fine. But to book a standard clean, a customer had to:

1. Click "Book Now"
2. Fill out a form with name, email, phone, address, square footage
3. Wait for a call back within 24 hours
4. Confirm the time over the phone
5. Enter credit card info over the phone

That's five steps. Three of them require active back-and-forth with a human being.

Here's what the same company's competitor did (and they were killing it):

- Customer clicks "Book Now"
- Sees a calendar with available times
- Picks a slot
- Enters address and credit card
- Done. Confirmation email sent.

That's three steps, zero human interaction required for the initial booking. The competitor was a two-person operation with a $99 Squarespace template. They were booking 35+ jobs per week during peak season. Average ticket: $150. Weekly revenue: $5,250. All from a site that cost $20 a month.

The company with the manual flow was doing 8 jobs per week at $180 average. Weekly revenue: $1,440.

Both offered the same service quality. The difference was friction.

If you're using a contact form and a phone call, you're leaving money on the table. Set up Square Appointments, Booksy, or even Calendly with a Stripe payment link. Test it yourself. Book your own service and count the clicks.

## Local SEO for Cleaning Services: Stop Wasting Money on "SEO Packages"

I've seen cleaning businesses pay $500–$1,000 a month to "SEO agencies" that promise page one rankings. Most of those agencies send a monthly report full of "keyword rankings" for terms nobody searches for.

Here's what actually moves the needle for a cleaning service in a US city.

**Google Business Profile is everything.** A cleaning company in Chicago was ranking on page four of Google for "house cleaning Chicago." They were paying an SEO company $800 a month. I looked at their Google Business Profile: no photos, one review from 2019, and the services listed were "general cleaning." No categories for deep clean, move-out clean, or post-construction.

We fixed the profile. Added categories. Uploaded 30 photos (before/after shots of real jobs). Set up Q&A and answered common questions. Asked every client for a review with a specific link.

Within 45 days, they jumped to the local map pack for "apartment cleaning Chicago" and "deep clean Chicago." Their calls from Google went from zero to 12–15 per week. They dropped the SEO agency the same week.

**Your website's location pages matter.** If you serve multiple neighborhoods, create separate pages. Not "We serve all of Dallas." Create "House cleaning in Oak Lawn," "House cleaning in Uptown Dallas," "House cleaning in Lakewood." Each page should mention the neighborhood by name, show a map, and include local landmarks.

A cleaning company in Dallas did this. They had nine neighborhood pages. Each one was thin — maybe 400 words with photos. Within three months, they were getting page one rankings for all nine. Their monthly organic traffic went from 200 visitors to 1,400. That's not from backlinks or content marketing. That's from telling Google exactly where they worked.

**Yelp still matters, but differently.** I know Yelp gets a bad rap. But for cleaning services, especially in the US, it's the second-largest source of inquiries after Google. A cleaning company in Houston was getting 30% of their new leads from Yelp. They had 47 reviews, 4.7 stars. They didn't pay Yelp a dime for advertising. They just responded to every review — thank you for positive ones, "we're sorry, please email us to make this right" for negative ones — and encouraged happy clients to leave reviews.

Google Ads can work for cleaning, but only if you have conversion tracking set up properly. Most small business owners set up Google Ads without conversion tracking, meaning they're paying for clicks and guessing whether those clicks become jobs. If you can't tell me your cost per booking from Google Ads, turn off the campaign until you fix tracking.

Use a tool like CallRail or a simple Google Ads conversion tag that fires when someone books online. Spend $200 on ads with tracking. If the cost per booking is under $50, scale up. If it's over $100, fix your landing page first.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a website if I get all my leads from Yelp or Nextdoor?**

Yes. Here's why: when someone finds you on Yelp and then types your business name into Google to see if you're legit, they land on your website. If your website is bad — no pricing, broken mobile layout, a 2018 copyright in the footer — they assume your cleaning service is also bad. Your website is your credibility anchor, even if nobody navigates to it directly. I've seen six-figure cleaning businesses that get 80% of leads from Yelp, but they still invest in a clean site because it closes the deal.

**Q: How much should I spend on a cleaning service website?**

Between $500 and $3,000 for a decent one. Not $12,000, not $99. A good template-based site — Squarespace, Wix, or Showit — with custom photos, clear pricing, and online booking will run you $1,500 to $2,500 if you hire a decent freelancer. Don't pay an agency $5,000 for a custom WordPress build. You don't need a custom build. You need a site that loads fast, works on phones, and makes it easy to book.

**Q: Should I include a blog on my cleaning service website?**

No. Not until you have 50+ recurring clients and you're maxed out on local leads. A blog won't help you rank for "house cleaning Austin" faster than a well-optimized Google Business Profile and neighborhood pages. When a client asks me about blogging, I tell them to spend that time asking for reviews and updating their photos. That drives actual business. Blogging is a long-term play that most small service businesses don't need.

**Q: How do I get more reviews without begging?**

Automate the ask. Use a tool like Mailchimp or a simple text automation to send a review request 24 hours after a cleaning. The message: "Hi [Name], we cleaned your home yesterday. If you're happy, could you leave us a review here? [Link to Google review]. If something wasn't right, please reply to this text so we can fix it." Include a direct link to your Google review page — not your website, not your Yelp page. Direct link. One click. A cleaning company in Phoenix did this and went from 12 reviews to 87 in three months. They send the text manually from a Google Voice number. Costs nothing.

**Q: My competitor has a fancy website with animations. Do I need that?**

No. Animations slow down your page load time, which hurts your Google ranking and frustrates mobile users. The cleaning company with the plain, fast site that has pricing and online booking will beat the competitor with the animated hero video every single time. I watched a single-page site in Atlanta with zero animations and a $199 template generate $3,800 in monthly recurring revenue from weekly cleanings. The owner spent the money on photos and a booking system, not on a parallax scroll effect.

**Q: Should I offer discounts for first-time customers on my website?**

Only if you test it. I've seen cleaning businesses offer 20% off first cleans and attract price shoppers who never book again. I've also seen a "$49 deep clean" promo (limited to one room) that turned into $300+ recurring clients. The difference is in how you frame it. Offer a low-risk trial that shows your quality, not a discount that trains people to wait for your next sale. A cleaning company in Seattle offered a "free kitchen deep clean with any first booking" instead of a discount. They upsold kitchen-only cleanings to 35% of those first-time clients within two months.
