## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Paying for Contacts You Never Email

A salon owner in Nashville came to me six months into using Klaviyo. She had 4,200 contacts in her list and was paying $60/month. The problem? Nearly 3,000 of those contacts hadn't opened a single email in over a year. She was paying to store names that were doing nothing but burning a hole in her budget.

Here's what happened: She'd collected emails from every walk-in for two years, never cleaned the list, and never set up a re-engagement sequence. When she finally looked at her deliverability rates, they were hovering around 72%. Mailchimp and Klaviyo both punish you for that — your sender reputation drops, and even your active subscribers stop seeing your emails.

The fix was uncomfortable but straightforward. We ran a re-engagement campaign for 14 days. Anyone who didn't open got a final "we'll miss you" email with a 20% discount code. No response? Gone. She lost about 2,800 contacts. Her monthly bill dropped to $32. And her open rates jumped from 22% to 41% in three weeks.

The dollar cost of ignoring this? She was paying roughly $340 extra per year for dead weight. More importantly, her active campaigns were underperforming because her metrics were diluted. After the cleanup, her appointment booking emails drove 18% more conversions — roughly $1,200 in additional monthly revenue from the same number of active subscribers.

**Do this instead:** Every 90 days, run a list hygiene check. Remove anyone who hasn't opened in 6 months. It feels painful. It's not. Smaller, engaged lists outperform bloated ones every single time. I've seen this pattern at four different clients. The ones who clean their lists consistently spend less and earn more. The ones who don't end up wondering why their open rates look like a flatline.

### Mistake #2: Choosing the Platform That's "Easier" For You, Not For Your Customers

A coffee roastery in Portland started with Mailchimp because the owner had used it for a newsletter five years ago. She knew the interface. It felt comfortable. The problem was her business model: customers bought beans online, visited the shop for pour-overs, and signed up for a weekly subscription box. Her customer data lived in Square, her website was on Shopify, and her loyalty program ran through a third-party app.

Mailchimp couldn't talk to any of these systems without clunky Zapier workarounds. She was manually exporting CSV files every Monday to update her segments. One week she forgot, and 140 subscribers got the wrong email — subscription customers received a "come try our new seasonal blend" offer that they were already receiving in their boxes. She had to send apology emails and comp three months of coffee to make it right. That cost her $860 in lost product and shipping.

The uncomfortable truth is that Mailchimp's simplicity works great for a newsletter. It works poorly for businesses where customer behavior changes across channels. A coffee shop, a hair salon, a fitness studio — these businesses need to know who walked in yesterday, who bought online last week, and who hasn't visited in three months. That's not a newsletter use case. That's an e-commerce CRM use case.

She switched to Klaviyo. The migration took a weekend. She set up a single integration with Square and Shopify, and suddenly every purchase, every visit, every subscription update flowed into her segments automatically. Her first automated campaign — a "we haven't seen you in 30 days" offer — brought 47 people back into the shop in two weeks. That's roughly $1,400 in incremental revenue she wasn't capturing before.

**Do this instead:** Before you pick a platform, map your customer's actual journey. Where does the data live? Square? Yelp? Booksy? Your website? If you need more than one integration, Mailchimp will frustrate you. If you're sending one monthly newsletter and nothing else, Mailchimp is probably fine. But most small businesses I work with are leaving money on the table because they chose the easy interface instead of the right data structure.

### Mistake #3: Using the Wrong Pricing Model for Your Growth

A pet groomer in Austin signed up for Mailchimp's free plan when she started. Two years later, she had 1,800 contacts and was paying $59/month. Her business was growing — she was adding about 80 new contacts monthly from her Booksy bookings and walk-in clients. But she was also losing about 30 contacts per month to disengagement.

Here's the problem: Mailchimp charges by contact count, not by how many emails you send. She had 1,800 contacts but was only emailing about 600 of them regularly (the ones who actually came in for grooming). She was paying for 1,200 contacts that had visited once and never returned. That's $480 per year for nothing.

Klaviyo also charges by contact count, but with a twist: you can segment your list into active and inactive contacts. Inactive contacts cost significantly less. When she migrated, her monthly bill dropped to $35 because Klaviyo's pricing distinguishes between engaged and unengaged subscribers.

The bigger issue was growth trajectory. She planned to expand to a second location in six months. With Mailchimp, adding another 1,000 contacts would push her to roughly $90/month. With Klaviyo, the same growth would cost around $55. Over two years, that's a $840 difference.

**Do this instead:** Pick the platform whose pricing aligns with how you'll actually use it. Mailchimp works when your list stays relatively flat. Klaviyo works when you're growing fast or have a high percentage of inactive contacts. If you're a fitness studio adding 200 emails per month from class sign-ups, Klaviyo's tiered pricing will save you real money. If you're a consultant with a stable list of 500 contacts, Mailchimp's free plan is perfectly fine.

## The Hidden Costs That Nobody Talks About

### Migration Pain: What It Actually Takes to Switch

I've migrated more than a dozen small businesses from one platform to another. The migration itself isn't technically difficult — both platforms have import tools. But the hidden cost is almost always the same: lost email history.

When you move from Mailchimp to Klaviyo, your open rates, click rates, and engagement history don't transfer. Klaviyo sees your contacts as new, even if they've been opening your emails for three years. That means your automated flows — welcome sequences, re-engagement campaigns — start from zero data. You lose the ability to segment based on past behavior.

For a hair salon in Chicago, this meant her "clients who haven't booked in 6 months" segment was empty for the first 90 days after migration. She had to manually recreate her top 50 clients' booking history. It took her 12 hours of work across three evenings. That's the real cost of switching: your time, not the platform fee.

The fix is to run both platforms in parallel for 30 days before fully switching. Export your Mailchimp engagement data into a spreadsheet, and use that spreadsheet as a reference for your first few segments in Klaviyo. It's not elegant, but it saves the confusion.

### Template and Design Costs

Mailchimp's drag-and-drop editor is genuinely good. Their templates look clean with almost no effort. Klaviyo's editor is more powerful but less forgiving. If you're not comfortable with basic HTML, you will spend more time formatting emails in Klaviyo.

A bakery owner in Denver spent $450 hiring a freelancer to rebuild her email templates when she switched from Mailchimp to Klaviyo. Her Mailchimp templates didn't transfer cleanly, and she didn't have the patience to learn Klaviyo's block editor. That's a real expense that rarely appears in comparison guides.

If you're considering Klaviyo, budget $200–$500 for template setup unless you're comfortable building from scratch. Platforms like Canva offer email template exports that work with both services, but you'll still need to tweak them.

### The Opportunity Cost of Not Using Automation Properly

This is the one nobody talks about because it's hard to measure. Mailchimp's automation capabilities exist but are limited. Klaviyo's automation engine is genuinely powerful. But most small businesses I meet aren't using either one effectively.

A fitness studio in Denver had Klaviyo for eight months and was only sending manual blast emails. They weren't using any automated flows — no welcome sequence, no re-engagement, no birthday offers. They were paying $83/month for a platform that could have automated 60% of their email marketing. The owner told me she "didn't have time to set it up." She had time to manually send the same welcome email to every new sign-up, though. That took her about 2 hours per week.

I set up three flows in one afternoon: a welcome sequence for new members, a re-engagement sequence for lapsed members, and a referral ask after the third class. Those three flows generated $2,100 in additional class bookings over the next 60 days. Her manual email time dropped to zero.

Here's the math: $83/month for Klaviyo, plus 2 hours/week of her time at roughly $50/hour (her own valuation). That's $483/month in total cost. After automation, her cost dropped to $83/month, and she gained $2,100 in revenue. The platform wasn't the problem. The underuse was.

## When You Actually Need Both Klaviyo and Mailchimp

I don't often recommend running two email platforms. It's messy, redundant, and your data gets split. But there are two specific scenarios where having both makes financial sense.

### Scenario 1: You Run a Physical Business and an E-Commerce Side

A boutique in NYC sells clothing in-store and through an online shop. Their in-store customers mostly book appointments through Yelp and pay via Square. Their online customers buy through Shopify.

The in-store customers don't want weekly emails about new arrivals. They want appointment reminders and the occasional "come see our new collection." The online customers want product drops, restock alerts, and abandoned cart emails. These are fundamentally different communication patterns.

For the in-store customers, Mailchimp's simplicity works perfectly. The owner sends one monthly newsletter and appointment reminder emails through Mailchimp's free plan (under 500 contacts). For the online customers, she uses Klaviyo to run abandoned cart sequences, post-purchase follow-ups, and product recommendations based on browsing behavior.

The total cost is about $45/month — $0 for Mailchimp (free plan) and $45 for Klaviyo. If she used Klaviyo for everything, she'd be paying for contacts that don't need advanced automation. If she used Mailchimp for everything, she'd lose the e-commerce automation that drives 30% of her online revenue.

### Scenario 2: You Have a Seasonal Business

A landscaping company in Portland has 1,400 contacts. They send emails heavily from March through October, then go quiet from November through February. Mailchimp charges by contact count year-round. Klaviyo charges by contact count but also factors in your email volume.

During the off-season, they paused their Klaviyo account and moved their inactive list to Mailchimp's free plan. When spring hit, they reactivated Klaviyo and imported the list back. Total annual cost: about $200 instead of $600.

This is an edge case, and it requires careful list management. But if your business has clear high seasons and low seasons, running both platforms lets you avoid paying for unused capacity.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I just stay on Mailchimp's free plan forever?**

You can, but you're likely leaving money on the table. Mailchimp's free plan limits you to 500 contacts and 1,000 sends per month. That's fine for a very small business sending one newsletter. But you can't run automation, segmentation, or A/B testing. For a coffee shop or salon, that means you can't send automatic birthday offers, re-engagement emails, or welcome sequences. I've seen free-plan users miss out on roughly $200–$400 per month in repeat business because they couldn't automate simple follow-ups. If you're under 500 contacts and sending less than 1,000 emails a month, stay. Otherwise, upgrade.

**Q: Will migrating to Klaviyo actually increase my revenue?**

Not by itself. Klaviyo is a tool, not a magic wand. Your revenue will increase if you use the features that Mailchimp doesn't offer — specifically, advanced segmentation based on purchase behavior and automated flows that trigger based on real-time actions. A pet groomer in Austin added $1,800 in monthly revenue after migrating because she set up a "book again" reminder that went out 6 weeks after each appointment. Mailchimp could have done this with enough effort, but Klaviyo made it simple enough that she actually did it. Migration alone won't increase revenue. Using the platform's strengths will.

**Q: What happens to my existing Mailchimp templates and automation?**

Your templates don't transfer. You'll need to rebuild them in the new platform. Your automation rules (welcome sequences, birthday emails) also need to be recreated. Your contact lists transfer easily via CSV export, but engagement history — open rates, click rates, purchase history — does not transfer automatically. Plan for a weekend of setup work, or budget $300–$500 to hire someone to handle it.

**Q: Which platform has better deliverability?**

Both are good. Klaviyo has slightly better deliverability for transactional emails (order confirmations, shipping updates) because their infrastructure is built for high-volume automated sending. Mailchimp is better for newsletter-style blasts. Realistically, your deliverability depends more on your sending habits than the platform. If you're sending to people who haven't opened in a year, your deliverability will suffer on either platform. Clean your list, send consistently, and both will perform well.

**Q: I use Yelp and Booksy. Which platform works better with these?**

Klaviyo integrates natively with both Yelp and Booksy. Mailchimp requires third-party connectors like Zapier, which add cost and complexity. If appointment booking data is central to your email strategy — reminders, follow-ups, re-engagement — Klaviyo is the easier choice. A hair salon in Portland saved about 3 hours per week by switching from Mailchimp/Zapier to Klaviyo's native Booksy integration. Those 3 hours were worth about $60/hour to her — $180/week in saved time.

**Q: Is Klaviyo worth the cost if I only have 200 contacts?**

No. If you're under 500 contacts and not running e-commerce, Mailchimp's free plan is perfectly adequate. Klaviyo's paid plans start at around $20/month for 500 contacts. You're not getting enough value from automation at that size to justify the cost. Grow your list to 1,000+ with Mailchimp, then evaluate whether you need Klaviyo's segmentation. Most small businesses hit the wall around 800–1,200 contacts, where manual sending becomes unsustainable and automation becomes necessary.
