### Start With 3–5% of Gross Revenue (Yes, Even If It Feels Low)

If you’re a solo coffee shop owner in Phoenix making $20,000/month, that’s $600–$1,000/month to test ads, SEO, and automation. This isn’t “optional spending” — it’s your life raft against competitors like Starbucks.

**How to split that $600:**
- Google Ads: $250/month
- Facebook Ads: $150/month
- SEO tools: $100/month
- Email marketing: $100/month
- Buffer for A/B testing: $100/month

<Callout type="tip">**Start with $200/month if you’re under $10k/month revenue.** Double your budget only when you’re consistently making 3:1 return on ad spend (ROAS).</Callout>

### Allocate Based on What You Want to Fix, Not What Feels “Trendy”

A $500/month fitness studio in Toronto isn’t going to outspend Peloton. But if your class cancellations are high, spend 60% of your budget on marketing automation tools like ConvertKit. If walk-ins are low, spend 70% on Google Ads.

<Callout type="example">*Case study:* A 3-chair hair salon in Sydney boosted bookings by 40% by spending $300/month on Google Ads and $200/month on a local SEO tool. Their biggest mistake? Starting with $500 all on Instagram Stories — low conversion, high cost.</Callout>

**Here’s why Google Ads is #1:**
- 48% of local searches lead to a purchase
- You only pay when someone clicks (no wasted spend)
- Can be paused at any time

<Callout type="warning">Avoid spending more than 20% of your budget on Instagram or TikTok unless your audience is teens (e.g., nail salons in high schools zones).</Callout>

### Track Conversions Like a Business, Not an Artist

You wouldn’t cook a cake without a recipe. So why run ads without tracking? Use free tools:
- Google Analytics 4 (track website conversions)
- Facebook Pixel (see which ads get bookings)
- CallRail (track if phone calls from ads become clients)

<Callout type="coffee">*DataLatte hack:* Tie each campaign to a specific goal. “I want 10 new yoga clients in 30 days” ≠ “I want more visibility.”</Callout>

### Adjust Your Budget Every 4 Weeks (Even If You’re Busy)

A $250/month coffee shop in Austin spent 6 weeks testing Google Ads, then doubled down on the winning ad. Result: +50% in morning walk-ins after optimizing for “best coffee near me.” Their mistake? Waiting 3 months to review results.

**Budget review checklist:**
1. Which channel has highest cost per customer?
2. Which ad has lowest cost per booking?
3. Are competitors outbidding me on keywords?

<Callout type="tip">Use the 80/20 rule: 80% of your budget should go to your top 2 channels. The rest is for testing.</Callout>

### Frequently Asked Questions

**How much should I spend on Google Ads if I’m new?**  
Start with $100/month. Use broad keywords like “dog groomers near me” and pause any ad that doesn’t get 2+ bookings in 2 weeks.

**Is it worth paying for SEO tools?**  
Yes, if you’re a studio in Chicago spending 10+ hours/month on Facebook. Try SEMrush or Ahrefs for $50/month to find local keywords like “yoga for seniors in Evanston.”

**Can I skip Facebook Ads entirely?**  
Yes if your customers are 65+ (e.g., senior fitness). No if you’re a pet groomer in Austin targeting millennials.

**What if my budget is only $50/month?**  
Focus on free tools: Google My Business (must-have), Canva for social posts, and a referral program offering $10 off for “2 friends who book.”

**How do I know when to increase my budget?**  
When 1) your best ad has a cost per customer lower than your profit margin and 2) you have the capacity to serve more clients.
