	+ Potential for increased revenue
	+ No upfront marketing costs
* Cons:
	+ Commission fees eat into your revenue
	+ Limited control over customer relationships
	+ Dependence on ClassPass's marketing efforts

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating ClassPass Like a Lead Generation Machine

**The story:** A yoga studio owner in Austin, Texas signed up for ClassPass thinking it would be a pipeline to fill their classes with warm bodies who'd eventually convert to members. They ran an unlimited promo — $49 for the first month, which ClassPass took 30% of. The owner watched 90 new students walk through the doors in week one. Felt great. Then week two: 40. Week three: 12. By week four, the studio was packed with one-time visitors who never returned, while regulars couldn't get into their usual classes because the slots were burned on ClassPass bookings.

**What went wrong:** They assumed any body in a class was a potential long-term member. ClassPass users are trained to hop from studio to studio. The owner didn't capture emails, didn't run a post-class follow-up, and didn't limit how many ClassPass spots were available per class. They let the platform dictate their capacity and their brand experience.

**The fix:** Cap ClassPass bookings at 20% of each class's capacity. Install a check-in tablet that requires an email address to sign in — not optional. Send a follow-up email within two hours of the class with a specific offer: "Come back this week for your second class free, and we'll throw in a mat rental." Run this for 60 days, then measure what percentage of ClassPass first-timers took the offer and converted to a paid membership.

**The outcome:** Revenue from ClassPass dropped from $1,800/mo to $1,200/mo because fewer slots were available. But the conversion rate from first-timer to paid member jumped from 3% to 22%. Net revenue from the ClassPass channel went from $54/mo in converted member revenue to $264/mo. Not life-changing, but the owner stopped treating ClassPass like a lottery ticket and started treating it like a sampling program.

### Mistake #2: Building a Membership That Competes on Price Instead of Experience

**The story:** A Pilates studio in Nashville decided their in-house membership needed to beat ClassPass on price. They priced unlimited at $99/mo, which was $30 less than the going rate in their neighborhood. They figured volume would make up for it. Six months in, they had 140 members and were losing money on every single one of them. Their rent was $6,500/mo. Their instructor costs averaged $50 per class. At $99/mo, each member needed to attend fewer than two classes per month for the math to fall apart. Most members were coming three to four times per week.

**What went wrong:** They priced based on what ClassPass charged per credit, not on their actual operating costs. They didn't factor in churn, peak-hour capacity limits, or the fact that heavy users would cancel if they felt they weren't "getting their money's worth." The membership was a race to the bottom that they were winning — straight into a hole.

**The fix:** Rebuild the membership structure around usage tiers. A 4-class-per-month plan at $80. An 8-class plan at $140. An unlimited plan at $200 with a 12-month commitment and a cancellation fee. The owner also added a "peak hour" surcharge — if you booked a 5:30 PM class and no-showed, you paid $15. This wasn't about punishing members; it was about protecting the revenue per available slot, which is the single number most studio owners don't track.

**The outcome:** Membership dropped from 140 to 89 in the first month. Monthly revenue? Went from $13,860 to $14,020. The owner lost 51 members but gained $160 in revenue. Three months later, membership stabilized at 112, revenue was $18,400, and the studio was actually profitable. The higher-tier members were more engaged, showed up more consistently, and churned at half the rate of the old $99 crowd.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the Data You Already Have

**The story:** A barre studio in Denver had been running for three years. They had 500 past clients in their Mailchimp list. They sent one email per week — a generic "come to class" blast with zero segmentation. Open rates hovered around 11%. The owner complained about how hard it was to fill 10 AM weekday classes. When I asked what the 10 AM regulars had in common, she shrugged. When I asked her to export her booking history and look at the data, she said she didn't have time.

**What went wrong:** She was sitting on a goldmine of behavioral data and ignoring it. She knew which clients booked 10 AM classes, which ones bought packages, which ones referred friends, which ones stopped showing up after 30 days. She just never looked. She was trying to market to everyone the same way, which meant she was marketing to no one.

**The fix:** Export 12 months of booking history from Booksy or Mindbody. Segment clients by:
- Class time preference (morning, midday, evening)
- Frequency (1-2x/week, 3-4x/week, occasional)
- Lapsed (no booking in 60+ days)
- Referrers (has tagged a friend in the last 6 months)

Build three email sequences:
1. A "We Miss You" sequence for lapsed clients — $20 off a 5-class pack, no expiration.
2. A "Morning Crew" sequence for 10 AM regulars — early access to schedule changes, a free smoothie after their 5th class.
3. A referral sequence for your top 20 referrers — "Bring a friend to your next class, you both get a free week."

**The outcome:** Open rates went from 11% to 34% within 60 days. The 10 AM weekday class went from averaging 4 students to averaging 12. The "We Miss You" sequence brought back 22 lapsed clients in the first month, generating $1,760 in package sales. Total cost of the campaign: zero dollars and about four hours of Excel work.

## The Hybrid Model: ClassPass for Acquisition, Your Membership for Retention

I've never seen a studio succeed by going all-in on one channel. The studios that grow consistently use ClassPass as a top-of-funnel sampling tool and their own membership as the core business. The trick is making the handoff intentional.

Set up a ClassPass landing page that's separate from your main website. The booking flow should include a checkbox: "Yes, send me studio updates and special offers." If someone checks yes, they go into a dedicated email sequence that's completely different from your regular newsletter. No "come to class" generic stuff. Instead: "Since you tried us on ClassPass, here's the truth about our membership pricing" and "Three things you can't do at any other studio in [city]."

I worked with a studio in Portland, Oregon that ran this exact setup. They allowed ClassPass bookings only for 9 AM and 12 PM classes. No evening spots, no weekend prime time. They made sure every ClassPass first-timer got a handwritten card at the end of class with a QR code linking to a "members-only" pricing page. That page showed a 30-day money-back guarantee — something ClassPass can't offer because they don't control the relationship.

In 90 days, the studio converted 18% of their ClassPass first-timers to a paid membership. Their ClassPass revenue was $2,100/mo. Their direct membership revenue from those conversions? $3,800/mo. The total cost of the program was $140 in card printing and a $50/mo Mailchimp upgrade.

## Marketing Your Own Membership Without Spending on Ads

I hear the same objection from studio owners: "I can't compete with ClassPass's marketing budget." You don't have to. ClassPass spends millions on national ads. You need to spend exactly zero dollars and beat them on relevance.

Here's the playbook, which I've seen work in Chicago, San Diego, and Raleigh:

**Step 1: Google My Business optimization.** Not the "fill out your profile" basic stuff. Specifically: create a "Memberships" Google Product category and load it with 10+ photos of actual members in your classes. Write descriptions that mention specific outcomes: "12-class pack for people who want to fix their lower back tightness." ClassPass doesn't get shown in local Google searches the way a well-optimized GMB profile does. I've seen studios double their phone call volume from GMB alone by adding membership-specific photos and posts.

**Step 2: Yelp review funnel.** Yelp is aggressive with fitness studios. Instead of fighting it, use it. Create a physical card that says "If you loved your class, tell us on Yelp. If something wasn't right, tell us in person." Give it to every new client for the first two weeks of their membership. When someone leaves a positive review on Yelp, respond within 24 hours and offer a "reviewer's bonus" — one free guest pass they can give to a friend. That guest pass is the single cheapest acquisition tool you have. It costs you a spot in a class that you were going to run anyway.

**Step 3: SMS-based referral system.** Email open rates are fine. SMS open rates are 98%. Use a tool like TextSanity or SimpleTexting to set up a referral sequence. When a member brings a friend, both get a credit toward next month's membership. The text message should come from a local number, not a 5-digit shortcode. "Hey Sarah, bring Jen to your next Tuesday class and you both get $20 off. Reply YES and I'll add the credit." This works because it feels human. ClassPass doesn't send texts that sound like a person.

One studio in Chicago did this exact three-step playbook and their direct membership grew from 47 to 132 in four months. Their ad spend: zero. Their monthly marketing cost: $29 for the SMS tool and $15 for card printing.

## When to Walk Away from ClassPass Entirely

Here's the uncomfortable truth: ClassPass is bad for some studios. I don't mean "it could be better." I mean it's actively hurting you.

You should cancel ClassPass if:

**Your average class size is under 12.** Every ClassPass booking is a slot you could have sold at full price to a direct member. If you're running small classes, you're giving away your most valuable inventory at a discount. I've watched a studio in Brooklyn lose $1,200/mo in potential revenue because they kept ClassPass slots open for classes that could have sold out at $25/head.

**Your retention rate is above 75%.** If your existing members stick around, you don't need ClassPass for fill. You need it to grow. But the math flips: if ClassPass users churn at 70% and your direct members churn at 25%, every ClassPass user on your roster is a net drag on retention metrics. Investors and acquirers look at retention rates. A ClassPass-heavy studio looks like a leaky bucket.

**You're in a city with high-density competition.** In New York, San Francisco, or Los Angeles, ClassPass users are trained to treat studios like a tasting menu. They're not looking for a home. They're looking for variety. Trying to convert them is like asking someone at a buffet to commit to eating only at your restaurant for the next year. It's not going to happen. In those markets, your marketing dollars are better spent on hyper-local Google Ads targeting people who live within a 10-minute walk of your studio. I've seen a studio in San Francisco's Mission District spend $500/mo on radius-targeted Google Ads and get a 4:1 ROAS on membership signups. That same $500 on ClassPass would have bought them about $1,000 in revenue — before commission.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much commission does ClassPass actually take?**

Between 20% and 40% of the class rate, depending on your contract and market. Smaller studios in competitive cities get the worst deal. Read your contract carefully — some versions have a "most favored nation" clause that requires you to give ClassPass your lowest available rate. If you offer a $10 intro special on your website, ClassPass can demand the same price. Negotiate this out before you sign.

**Q: Can I get my client data out of ClassPass?**

Barely. ClassPass gives you name and email of users who attended your class. That's it. No phone numbers, no demographic data, no booking history outside your studio. You cannot retarget them on Facebook. You cannot upload them to a custom audience. The data is theirs, not yours. This is the biggest hidden cost — you're building their audience, not yours.

**Q: Should I price my in-house membership lower or higher than ClassPass?**

Higher. Always. If your direct membership costs less per class than ClassPass, you've already lost the pricing game. Your membership should feel like a premium product with real benefits: priority booking, member-only classes, free workshops, guest passes. ClassPass is the discount option. Own that positioning. Charge $180-200/mo for unlimited and let ClassPass users feel smart for finding the deal. Then convert them when they're ready for something better.

**Q: How long does it take to see ROI from building my own membership?**

Depends on your starting point. If you have zero membership program and 200 active clients, expect 60-90 days to break even on setup costs (software, card printing, email setup). From there, the ROI compounds because your margins are 100% on every direct member dollar. ClassPass margins are 60-80% after commission. The breakeven point on swapping one ClassPass regular to a direct member is usually 3-4 months. After that, it's pure profit.

**Q: What if a competitor in my city uses ClassPass and I don't?**

You'll lose some short-term bookings. You'll gain long-term loyalty. Here's what I've seen happen in three separate markets: the studio that drops ClassPass and invests in direct marketing loses 15-25% of total class bookings in the first month. Six months later, they're at 90% of their old volume but making 40% more revenue because they kept the full class price. The studio that stays on ClassPass sees flat or declining revenue as commission rates creep up. In every case, the studio that owned their customer relationships won.

**Q: Do I need a booking app like Booksy or Mindbody to run my own membership?**

Yes, but don't overthink which one. Booksy is fine for small studios on a budget. Mindbody is overkill for a single location. The most important feature is automated payment processing for recurring memberships and a decent email integration. I've seen studios make this work with a simple Google Form and Square subscriptions. Ugly, but functional. Upgrade when you hit 50 members.
