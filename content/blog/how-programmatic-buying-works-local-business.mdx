## What is Programmatic Buying?
Programmatic buying is a way of buying ad space online using automated systems. It allows you to target specific audiences and demographics, increasing the effectiveness of your ads.
For example, if you own a coffee shop in New York City, you can use programmatic buying to target people who live or work in the area and have shown an interest in coffee.
This type of targeting is much more effective than traditional advertising methods, which often rely on broad demographics and hope for the best.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched small business owners throw good money at bad programmatic campaigns more times than I've had hot dinners in Warsaw. Here's the uncomfortable truth: most local businesses lose money on programmatic for the first 90 days. They don't have to. These are the three mistakes I see most often, with actual stories that will save you the tuition fee.

### Mistake 1: Targeting "Everyone Near Me" Like It's a Flyer Drop

**The story:** A coffee roaster in Portland, Oregon, spent $2,400 in six weeks on programmatic display ads. The owner, let's call him James, set a two-mile radius around his shop. No other targeting. The algorithm did what algorithms do: it served ads to every phone within that radius. Retirees playing Scrabble on their iPads. Teenagers watching TikTok in the park. A guy waiting for his girlfriend outside a yoga studio three blocks away.

James got 18,000 impressions. He also got three new customers. One of them asked if he sold decaf. That $2,400 bought him maybe $60 in coffee sales. He called me frustrated, saying programmatic was a scam.

**What went wrong:** Proximity targeting alone is lazy. It treats everyone within a radius as equally likely to buy. They're not. The retiree needs a low-acid roast. The teenager wants a $7 lavender latte once. The guy outside the yoga studio doesn't drink coffee because it raises his cortisol (he told James this).

**The fix:** Layer behavioral and contextual signals on top of location. Use Google's affinity audiences to find "coffee enthusiasts." Exclude people who've searched for "caffeine anxiety" or "how to quit coffee." Target commuters — people who pass through your neighborhood between 7–9 AM and 3–5 PM are your real audience, not the guy waiting for his girlfriend.

**The outcome:** James cut his monthly budget from $400 to $300. He added commute-time targeting and excluded anyone whose device hadn't moved in 15 minutes (eliminating the retirees and the yoga-waiter). His next six weeks: 112 new loyalty program signups, $1,860 in incremental revenue tracked via Square. Not a windfall. But $1,860 beats $60.

### Mistake 2: Using the Same Creative for Every Placement

**The story:** A hair salon owner in Nashville spent $1,200 on programmatic video ads through a managed service provider. The provider ran one 30-second clip: a woman getting a dramatic haircut set to upbeat music. It ran on cooking blogs, news sites, and a pet adoption forum. The salon got three booking requests. Total: probably $180 in services, assuming they upsold the blow-dry.

The owner, Maria, showed me the ad. It was lovely. It also bore no relationship to where it was showing. Someone reading a recipe for sourdough starter doesn't want to watch a stranger get a haircut. The cognitive gap is too wide. They scroll past.

**What went wrong:** Programmatic platforms buy placements based on audience data, but the creative needs to match the context. A video about a dramatic transformation works on beauty blogs. On a pet adoption site, you want something softer, maybe a before-and-after that shows how a haircut changed someone's confidence — not the loud scissors part.

**The fix:** Create three creative variants minimum. One for lifestyle and beauty contexts (show the process, the products). One for local news and community sites (feature your stylists, mention your address). One for general audiences (focus on booking convenience, not the artistry). Use free tools like Canva to create simple static ads if video is too expensive. The ad creative matters more than the targeting for most local campaigns.

**The outcome:** Maria created four image-based ads in one afternoon using Canva templates. One showed a "rainy day special" (Nashville gets 50 inches of rain a year — people want to know you exist when they can't do outdoor activities). She ran these for $800/month. In eight weeks, she got 47 new client bookings tracked through Booksy. At an average service ticket of $85, that's roughly $3,995. Against $1,600 in ad spend. She doesn't call programmatic a scam anymore.

### Mistake 3: Setting It and Forgetting It — The Dunning-Kruger Effect

**The story:** A fitness studio owner in Denver set up a programmatic campaign through the "easy setup" wizard in his Google Ads account. He chose "increase store visits," set a $500 monthly budget, and walked away. Three months later, he had spent $1,500. His Google Business Profile showed zero attributed store visits from ads.

He told me, "I set the goal to store visits. Why didn't it work?"

**What went wrong:** "Store visit" is a modeled metric, not a real one. Google estimates store visits based on a sample of users who turned on location history. For a local business, the sample size is often too small to optimize against. The algorithm had no real signal to learn from. It was flying blind, optimizing for phantom data.

**The fix:** Use a concrete conversion action, not a modeled one. Set up a call tracking number (free with Google Ads or cheap through CallRail). Track "click to call" as your primary conversion. If you use a booking system like Mindbody or Glofox, integrate it with your ad platform so booked classes become conversions. You need real, attributable actions. Store visits are a vanity metric for most local businesses.

**The outcome:** The owner switched to call tracking and booked-class conversions. He also installed a UTM-tagged phone number on his landing page. In 30 days, he tracked 22 phone calls from ads, 14 of which booked a trial class. At $99/month membership average, with a 60% trial-to-member conversion, that's about $831 in recurring monthly revenue from $500 in ad spend. The campaign was cash-positive by week three. He stopped trusting Google's "store visits" column. Good.

## How to Track Programmatic Results Without Losing Your Mind

Most local business owners don't need a $5,000 attribution platform. You need three things that cost nothing or close to it.

**Use unique phone numbers.** Services like CallRail charge around $30/month. You can also use Google Voice for free. Put a different number on your programmatic landing pages than on your website footer. Count the calls. If someone asks, "I saw your ad," flag the call. Keep a running tally. I've seen a pet groomer in Austin track $4,600 in revenue this way using nothing but a notebook and a free Google Voice number.

**Promo codes that actually get used.** Not "SAVE10." Be specific: "PROG2025" or "AD2025." Put it in your display creative and on your landing page. Make sure your POS system can track it. If you use Square, create a discount code and check the redemption report weekly. A bakery in Chicago found that 23% of programmatic-driven customers used a promo code in month one, but only 8% in month two — because the creative had gotten stale and nobody remembered the code from an ad they saw three weeks ago. They refreshed the code monthly. Problem solved.

**Landing pages with purpose.** If your programmatic ad goes to your homepage, you're burning money. Create a single-page site or use a tool like Carrd or Unbounce (starting at $19/month). Include your phone number, your promo code, and a clear call to action. Test one thing at a time: button color (red vs. green sounds stupid but can change click-through by 18%), headline (test "Skip the Wait" vs. "Same-Day Appointments Available"), and image (your smiling face vs. your product). Don't test three things at once. You'll have no idea what worked.

Most guides skip this part — don't. Tracking is the difference between knowing programmatic works and hoping it works. Hope is not a strategy.

## What a $500 Monthly Programmatic Budget Actually Buys You

I hear this question every week: "Is $500 enough?" The answer is yes, if you're honest about what $500 buys you. It does not buy a full-funnel, multi-touch, omni-channel assault on the market.

Here's what $500 gets a local business in a mid-sized US city:

**250,000 to 400,000 display impressions** across standard IAB placements. That's the equivalent of putting 400,000 flyers on cars. Except you're not littering. And you can target.

**About 2,000 to 4,000 clicks** to your site, assuming a click-through rate around 0.8% to 1.2%. That's realistic for well-targeted display. If your site converts at 3% (a reasonable number for a local service business), that's 60 to 120 leads.

**Zero qualified leads if your landing page asks people to fill out a 12-field form.** I am not joking. I watched a dentist in San Diego spend $500 and get zero booked appointments because his landing page asked for full name, email, phone, preferred time, insurance provider, referring doctor, whether they had a preferred hygienist, and a dropdown with 14 treatment categories. The man was asking for a commitment before a first date. He switched to a one-field form: "Best phone number." Bookings went from zero to nine in two weeks.

**Better options for the same money:**

- Run programmatic display for two weeks, stop, redirect the budget to retargeting for the next two weeks. The people who clicked but didn't book get a second ad. This alone can increase conversion rates by 50% to 70%.
- Skip premium placements. Your local news site wants $12 CPM. A programmatic open exchange might charge $2 CPM. For $500, that's 250,000 vs. 41,000 impressions. The premium site audience may be marginally better, but four times the reach usually wins for local awareness.
- Use the mid-month reset. Most programmatic campaigns run monthly. I've noticed performance drops after day 18 because the budget is exhausted or the algorithm has run out of new people to show. Pause on day 18, wait three days, restart. You'll get a fresh learning phase. It's a hack, not a strategy, but it works.

## How to Tell Your Programmatic Provider Is Bad (Before You Pay Them)

I've audited programmatic campaigns run by agencies who charge small businesses $2,000/month for "managed services." The results were embarrassing. Here are the red flags:

**They can't tell you what inventory you're buying.** If your provider says "we buy from Google Display Network and open exchange," ask for a list of the top 20 websites where your ads ran. If they hesitate or say "it varies," run. A good provider has a placement report ready.

**They report impressions and clicks only.** If your monthly report shows "2.5M impressions, 0.09% CTR, great brand awareness!" and nothing about leads, calls, or revenue, they are giving you a vanity report. Brand awareness is a luxury for local businesses with saturated markets. You need conversions.

**They recommend a budget under $300/month.** The minimum viable budget for programmatic display is about $500/month. Below that, the algorithm can't learn because it doesn't have enough data. The platform spends your money testing audiences, not converting them. A provider who suggests $200/month either doesn't understand the math or wants to collect a management fee regardless of results.

**They don't ask about your business.** Before taking a client, I need to know: What's your average ticket? What's your busiest time of day? Who are your best customers? How do they find you now? If a provider sends you an intake form that's just "business name, credit card number, start date," they're going to set up a generic campaign and hope you don't call them for 90 days.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn't programmatic just for big brands with huge budgets?**

No. The barriers are lower than they've ever been. You can start with $500/month through Google Ads' Display Campaigns, Amazon Ads, or platforms like Simpli.fi that specialize in local. The technology scales down. The targeting scales down. What doesn't scale down is the work you need to do on your landing page and tracking. A big brand can waste $50,000 learning lessons. You can't. So you need to be smarter, not richer.

**Q: How is this different from Facebook ads?**

Facebook targets people based on what they've told Facebook. Programmatic display targets people based on browsing behavior, device data, location history, and third-party data. Facebook is a walled garden — you reach people inside Facebook's ecosystem. Programmatic reaches people across thousands of sites. For a coffee shop or salon, I'd usually start with Facebook because the targeting is simpler. But programmatic becomes useful when you need to reach people who aren't scrolling social media — commuters reading news sites, for example.

**Q: Can I do this myself or do I need to hire someone?**

You can set up the campaign yourself in Google Ads in about 30 minutes. But you shouldn't. The setup wizard is designed to get you spending, not to get you results. It will default to "maximize conversions" without a proper conversion history, which means it will spend your money learning. If you have the time to learn (read: 10-15 hours of research and ongoing weekly optimization), DIY is fine. If you'd rather focus on running your business, pay a freelancer or agency who specializes in local programmatic, not a generalist who runs programmatic as part of a "full-service package."

**Q: How quickly will I see results?**

If your tracking is set up correctly before you launch, you'll see some data in the first week. But real traction — consistent leads at a cost per acquisition you can stomach — usually takes 30 to 60 days. The first two weeks are the learning phase. The platform is figuring out which audiences convert. Don't judge the campaign in week one. If after 45 days you're getting zero conversions, something is fundamentally wrong. Either your targeting, your creative, your landing page, or your tracking is broken.

**Q: What if my business is in a small town, not a city?**

It depends on population density, not town size. A town of 15,000 people with no other coffee shop is a better programmatic opportunity than a town of 60,000 with six Starbucks. Programmatic works best when you have a clear, addressable audience. If your town is small, your budget should be smaller too. Start at $300/month. Use a very tight radius — one mile. Target by device type (mobile only — people in small towns search on their phones). And consider Google's "custom intent" audiences rather than demographic targeting. You want people who have searched for your service in the last 30 days.

**Q: Is programmatic still worth it with iOS privacy changes and cookie deprecation?**

Yes, but it's harder. The loss of third-party cookies and Apple's App Tracking Transparency framework has made audience targeting less precise. You can no longer reliably retarget someone who visited your site three weeks ago if they're on iOS. The workaround: focus on contextual targeting (ads placed based on page content, not user history) and first-party data. If you collect emails or phone numbers, you can upload them as a customer match list. This is legal and effective. Use tools like Mailchimp or Square to export your customer list, then upload it to your ad platform. You'll reach people you already know, which has higher conversion rates anyway.
