## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Bidding on Generic Keywords Like “Video Production”

I had a videographer in Austin reach out after burning $1,200 in two weeks. He’d set up a single campaign targeting “video production” and “videographer” with broad match. Google happily served his ad to people in Seattle looking for wedding videographers, high school seniors wanting promo videos, and someone searching for “cheap video production” who expected a $50 animation.

His click-through rate was 1.2%, cost-per-click hit $4.80, and zero leads.

**The fix:** Switch to phrase match keywords that signal intent. For a corporate and event videographer, that means phrases like “corporate event videographer Austin,” “company video testimonial service,” or “conference highlight reel production.” Add negative keywords like “wedding,” “birthday,” “cheap,” “free,” and “amateur” to filter out tire-kickers.

**The outcome:** After restructuring into three tightly themed ad groups (corporate events, brand videos, and real estate video) with a $500 monthly budget, his cost-per-lead dropped from infinity to $22. Within 30 days, one lead turned into a $3,800 contract for an annual corporate video series.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Location and Radius Targeting

A Nashville videographer came to me frustrated. She’d spent $900 on Google Ads in a month and gotten two leads — both from people 200 miles away who wanted “destination wedding” coverage (she doesn’t do weddings). She thought the algorithm would figure out her service area.

It doesn’t.

**The fix:** Set a radius of 30 miles around your physical office or service area. Use location targeting in Google Ads to “Presence” only (people physically in the area, not interested in the area). Also enable location exclusions for areas you can’t serve. For event videographers, consider adding a separate campaign targeting event venues, conference centers, and business districts by name.

**The outcome:** She narrowed her radius to 25 miles around downtown Nashville, paused weekends, and ran ads only 9am–6pm Monday through Friday. Her monthly spend stayed at $900, but she got 11 leads in the next 30 days. Three turned into bookings worth $5,200 total. She also saved about $400 in wasted clicks from out-of-state searchers.

### Mistake 3: No Conversion Tracking — Flying Blind

A videographer in Portland told me his Google Ads account was “getting clicks” but he couldn’t tell what resulted. He had no call tracking, no form submission tracking, and no way to know if a visitor booked a consultation. He’d been running ads for six months with a $700/month budget and assumed the phone just didn’t ring much.

When I looked at his account, the campaign was optimized for clicks. Google happily delivered 150 clicks a month — but most were from people bouncing after 3 seconds because the landing page didn’t load fast.

**The fix:** Set up conversion tracking in Google Ads. For phone calls, use Google forwarding numbers (call extensions or call-only ads). For contact forms, install the Google Ads conversion tag via Google Tag Manager or use a tool like CallRail or WhatConverts. Then optimize your campaigns for “Conversions” instead of clicks — this changes how the algorithm spends your money.

**The outcome:** Within two weeks of turning on conversion tracking and switching the bid strategy to target CPA (cost-per-acquisition), his cost per lead dropped from roughly $40 (estimated guess) to $18. He found that the contact form got 4x more leads than phone calls, so he redesigned the form to be shorter and mobile-friendly. Monthly revenue from Google Ads went from zero to $3,200 within 60 days.

### Mistake 4: Running One Ad Group for “Videographer” and Calling It Done

A videographer in Denver set up one ad group with about 20 keywords — everything from “corporate videographer” to “drone footage” to “after movie” (whatever that means). He had one generic ad that said “Hire a Videographer” and sent everyone to his homepage. He spent $1,500 over three months and landed one small project worth $800.

**The fix:** Create separate ad groups for each service you offer. For example:
- *Corporate Event Videos* with keywords like “conference videographer Denver,” “corporate event highlight reel”
- *Brand Video / Testimonials* with “company testimonial video,” “brand story videographer”
- *Real Estate Video* if you do that (separate campaign entirely, different audience)

Each ad group needs its own ad copy referencing that specific service, and its own landing page (or at least a dedicated section on your site). Use responsive search ads with multiple headlines and descriptions so Google can test combinations.

**The outcome:** After restructuring into three ad groups with $400 total budget (spread based on what historically performed), he saw a 7% conversion rate on the corporate events ad group. In the first month he booked two corporate projects worth $2,400 each.

## How to Build a Landing Page That Converts Corporate Clients

Your ad can be perfect, but if the page it sends people to looks like a portfolio site for your cousin’s wedding, corporate decision-makers won’t fill out the form. I’ve seen this firsthand — a Chicago videographer spent $2,000 on ads that drove traffic to a homepage with a 4-second auto-playing music video, a contact form buried at the bottom, and no pricing clues. His conversion rate was 0.8%.

**Here’s what works instead.**

First, use a dedicated landing page — not your homepage. Tools like Carrd, Unbounce, or even a simple WordPress page with a form plugin. Cost: about $100 to set up if you hire someone on Fiverr, or free if you DIY with a template.

**Elements that actually move the needle with corporate clients:**

- **A headline that states the outcome, not the service.** Instead of “Videographer in Austin,” use “Corporate Event Video That Gets 5,000 Views on LinkedIn.” It sounds better because it promises a specific result.
- **Three portfolio examples — but only relevant ones.** If you’re targeting corporate events, show a one-minute clip from a conference, a testimonial video, and a brand story. Remove the wedding highlight reel and the music video. Each example should include a stat (“This video helped the client get 40% more meeting requests”).
- **Trust signals that corporate buyers care about.** Logos of past clients (even if they’re smaller companies — just list them), testimonials that mention measurable results (“We saw a 15% increase in event attendance”), and any industry awards or certifications.
- **A clear call-to-action that matches the ad.** If your ad says “Get a Free Quote,” the landing page should have a form with the words “Request Your Free Quote.” Don’t say “Contact Us” or “Book Now.” Be literal.
- **Remove navigation links.** Don’t let them wander off to your Instagram feed or blog. Give them one job — fill out the form or call the number.

**Real numbers from that Chicago videographer after the fix:**

We replaced the homepage with a simple landing page (three case studies, a testimonial video, and a form with five fields). His conversion rate went from 0.8% to 8.1%. Cost per lead dropped from $94 to $12. In the first 90 days, he booked four corporate projects worth $9,600 total. The landing page cost $150 to build on Carrd with a custom domain.

Also, add a phone number with call tracking. Many corporate clients prefer to call rather than fill a form — especially if they’re on a tight deadline for an event next week. Use Google forwarding numbers or a service like CallRail so you know which ad drove the call.

## Integrating Google Ads with Your Other Marketing Channels

Google Ads works best when it’s not operating in a vacuum. Most videographers run a Facebook page, collect emails in Mailchimp, and maybe have a Yelp listing for local searches. If you’re not connecting these, you’re leaving money on the table.

**Yelp Ads for Local Event Clients**

I worked with a videographer in New York City who was targeting “corporate event videographer Manhattan.” He was competing against fifty other companies. His Google Ads cost-per-click averaged $5.50. I suggested adding Yelp Ads, because event planners often search Yelp for videographers specifically. After setting up a $300/month Yelp campaign with location targeting to Midtown Manhattan and keywords like “corporate event video,” he got an additional seven leads in one month. Two converted, totaling $4,800 in revenue. His Yelp cost per lead was $42 — higher than Google, but still profitable. The key: make sure your Yelp profile has at least five recent reviews, business hours, and a clear “Request a Quote” button.

**Mailchimp Retargeting for Cold Leads**

Not everyone who clicks your ad books on the spot. Some will browse your portfolio and leave. You can capture those visitors with a simple Mailchimp integration using Google Ads’ Customer Match or a tool like Unbounce that syncs with Mailchimp.

Here’s a real scenario from a videographer in Dallas: He ran Google Ads for “corporate testimonial video Dallas” with a landing page that had a form. About 60% of visitors left without filling the form. We added a Mailchimp signup popup offering a free guide (“5 Questions to Ask Before Hiring a Video Agency”) — email address required. He collected 23 emails in a month. Then he set up a Mailchimp automation: Day 1 send, Day 3 send, Day 7 send — each with a different case study. Within two weeks, three of those 23 leads submitted a contact form through the follow-up email. Two became paying clients worth $2,800 and $3,400.

The cost of the Mailchimp account was $59/month, and the popup tool (Sumo or OptinMonster) was about $19/month. Total extra spend: $78/month. The return: $6,200.

**Square and Booksy for Booking**

If you offer a service that requires scheduling (e.g., filming an event on a specific date), integrate your Google Ads with a booking system like Square Appointments or Booksy. I had a videographer in Miami who ran a “conference reel special” ad. Instead of a generic contact form, his landing page had a Square booking widget that showed available dates and times. Prospects booked directly without a phone call. This cut his lead-to-booking time from three days to 15 minutes. His conversion rate on the landing page jumped from 7% to 14% because the friction of “wait for a response” disappeared.

**Facebook Retargeting**

Don’t ignore Facebook for retargeting. Set up a Facebook pixel on your landing page. After someone clicks your Google Ad and visits your landing page, retarget them with a Facebook ad showing a testimonial video from a similar client. A videographer in Austin tested this: he spent $150 on Facebook retargeting over 30 days and got two leads that converted into $3,100 in projects. The key is to keep the retargeting ad simple — just a 30-second video of a happy client saying “we got 20 qualified leads from that video.”

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I’m a one-person videography operation. Is Google Ads worth it for me?**

Yes, but only if you have the capacity to handle the leads. If you’re already booked solid, don’t run ads — you’ll just get frustrated. If you have open slots, start with $300–$500 per month. Track everything. I’ve seen solo videographers in Denver generate $4,000 in monthly revenue from a $500 ad spend. The key is to target very specific services (e.g., “corporate headshot video” or “one-minute testimonial”) so the leads are easy to fulfill.

**Q: How do I compete with big production agencies that have massive budgets?**

You don’t compete head-on. You out-niche them. The big agencies go after “video production” — broad, expensive keywords. You go after “corporate event sizzle reel,” “conference highlight video,” or “company culture video for startups.” These have lower search volume but much higher intent and less competition. I saw a New York videographer drop his cost-per-click from $7 to $2 by moving from “video production NYC” to “startup brand video NYC.”

**Q: What if I don’t have a website yet?**

Run ads directly to a landing page. You can build one on Carrd in under an hour for $19/year. Or use a free Google Business Profile and run Local Service Ads (though those are limited to certain categories). But a dedicated landing page with your portfolio, testimonials, and a form will outperform any Google Business Profile page. Don’t wait for a full website.

**Q: How long until I see actual leads from Google Ads?**

Usually 2 to 4 weeks. The first week is data gathering — Google needs to learn who clicks. Do not make major changes in the first 10 days. After that, if you’re not seeing at least one lead per $100 spent, revisit your keywords and landing page. A videographer in Nashville got his first lead on day 12 and closed it for $2,800. But if nothing happens by day 14, check your negative keywords and location settings.

**Q: Should I use broad match keywords for more reach?**

No. Broad match will show your ad for “video toaster repair” if you’re unlucky. Start with phrase match for your service keywords. Once you have 100+ conversions, you can experiment with broad match with a smart bidding strategy, but only if you have solid negative keyword lists. Otherwise, you’ll burn through your budget on irrelevant clicks.

**Q: Can I pause ads during my slow season and turn them back on later?**

Yes, absolutely. In fact, that’s smart. If you only do corporate events during Q4 or summer conference season, run ads 8–10 weeks ahead. Pause the rest of the year. When you unpause, Google will need a few days to re-optimize, but your history and quality score typically carry over. Just make sure your landing page is still live.
