### Local SEO for Physical Therapists

Local SEO is a type of search engine optimization (SEO) that focuses on improving your website's ranking in local search results. This is especially important for physical therapists, as most people search for medical services online. Here are a few tips to improve your local SEO:

* Claim and optimize your Google Business Profile (GBP) listing
* Use location-specific keywords on your website
* Get listed in online directories like Yelp and Healthgrades
* Encourage patients to leave online reviews

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Relying on Word-of-Mouth and Getting Complacent

I worked with a clinic in Austin, Texas — let's call them "South Congress PT" — that had been in business for eleven years. The owner, a skilled therapist named Dave, told me his referral base was solid. "My patients send me their friends. I don't need to do much else."

He was right, partially. His referral rate was good — about 30% of new patients came from existing ones. But here's what he missed: the clinic had been flat for three years. Revenue wasn't growing. The schedule had gaps in the afternoon that he accepted as normal. Meanwhile, two new clinics had opened within a mile, both running Google Ads and showing up ahead of him in local searches.

The uncomfortable truth: word-of-mouth is a ceiling, not a foundation. Dave didn't know how many potential patients were searching for "Austin physical therapist near me" and not finding him. When I pulled his Google Business Profile insights, he had 87 profile views in the previous month. The new clinic down the street had 340.

The fix wasn't complicated. We optimized his Google Business Profile — actual categories, response photos, posts twice a week. We set up a $500/month Google Ads campaign targeting "physical therapist Austin TX" and "sports injury South Austin." No broad keywords. Tight location radius.

The outcome: In the first 60 days, Dave saw 18 new patient calls directly from the ads. His schedule went from 65% full to 88% full. Monthly revenue increased by roughly $4,200. The first week he said, "I should have done this three years ago."

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Negative Reviews (or Responding Badly)

A chiropractor-run physical therapy clinic in Nashville, Tennessee, had a 3.8-star rating on Google. That sounds mediocre — and it is. But here's the problem: they had 42 reviews, and eight of them were one-star. Every single one-star review had the same theme: "They didn't listen to me." "Felt rushed." "Dismissed my concerns."

The owner, a well-meaning guy named Marcus, had not responded to a single one of those negative reviews. He told me he didn't want to "engage with negativity." That is a mistake I've seen kill local businesses in three different cities.

Google weighs review responses as a trust signal. A clinic that responds to negative feedback — professionally, not defensively — signals that they care about patient experience. A silent profile signals you don't care, or you don't know how to handle criticism.

The fix: We wrote specific responses to each negative review. Not canned copy, but actual acknowledgment. For the "felt rushed" reviews, Marcus wrote: "I'm sorry you felt that way. I've adjusted my scheduling to allow more time for initial evaluations. If you'd like to discuss further, please call me directly."

We also implemented a system using Yelp's free review response tools and Google's built-in alerts to flag new negative reviews within 24 hours.

The outcome: Over three months, Marcus's rating climbed to 4.6 stars. More importantly, his appointment request rate from Google Maps increased by 22%. The clinic that had been losing patients to a competitor with a 4.8-star rating started closing the gap.

### Mistake 3: Posting Generic Content on Social Media

A small PT practice in Portland, Oregon — "Eastside Rehab" — was posting three times a week on Instagram. The content: photos of the clinic lobby. A shot of someone on an exercise bike. A blurry picture of a whiteboard. The owner, Jenna, hired a college student to manage it. It cost her $400/month and generated exactly zero patient inquiries.

The problem wasn't that social media doesn't work for PT clinics. The problem was the content had no value. Nobody wakes up thinking "I wonder what the waiting room at Eastside Rehab looks like today." Patients search for answers, not ambiance.

The fix: Jenna started posting one educational video per week. Two minutes, shot on an iPhone, where she demonstrated how to stretch a tight hamstring or what to do for lower back pain in the morning. She titled them "3 stretches you can do at your desk" and "Why your hip hurts when you run." She added location tags and relevant hashtags.

She used Square's appointment scheduling integration — when someone commented asking how to book, the link was in her bio. She also started running Instagram ads ($300/month) promoting the top-performing video to a 10-mile radius targeting people aged 25–55.

The outcome: In six weeks, her video on "how to fix sleeping neck pain" got 12,000 views. She booked seven new patient consultations directly from Instagram. That's $2,100 in first-visit revenue from a channel she was about to abandon.

### Mistake 4: Not Having a Follow-Up System for Existing Patients

Here's one most guides skip: marketing isn't just about getting new patients. It's about preventing the gap between visits. A clinic in Chicago — "Wicker Park PT" — had a decent flow of new patients, but a terrible rebooking rate. People would come for three visits, feel better, and disappear. Six months later, they'd reinjure themselves and go to a different clinic.

The owner, a guy named Steve, had no system to remind patients to come back. No email sequence. No text reminders for maintenance visits. No recall campaign for the 300+ people who had been treated in the last year and never returned.

The fix: We set up a simple email sequence using Mailchimp. Three emails, spaced two weeks apart:
- Email 1: "It's been 90 days since your last visit. Here's a quick self-check for your [specific injury]."
- Email 2: "Three exercises to prevent re-injury (with video demonstration)."
- Email 3: "We have morning availability next week. Here's a direct booking link."

We also added a text reminder via Square's appointment system — not for the patient's next appointment, but for a suggested visit three months after discharge.

The outcome: In 90 days, Steve rebooked 14 past patients. Average visit value for those rebookings was $180 per session, with most booking a series of 4–6 visits. That generated roughly $3,800 in revenue from patients who had already been written off as lost. The campaign cost $35/month for the Mailchimp plan.

## What Actually Works for Local Search (Beyond the Obvious)

Everyone tells you to "claim your Google Business Profile." Fine. That's table stakes. But here's a specific move I've seen work across three different clinics: optimizing for the "near me" search with a service-based keyword structure.

A Denver clinic — "Mile High PT" — was getting buried in search results because they only targeted "Denver physical therapist." That keyword has high competition from the big rehab chains. But when we looked at search volume data from Google Keyword Planner, "Denver running injury physical therapist" and "Denver ACL rehab specialist" had far fewer advertisers bidding on them — and the people searching those terms were much closer to booking.

We rebuilt their Google Ads account into 12 ad groups, each targeting a specific condition + location. "Lower back pain Denver." "Knee rehab Cherry Creek." "Shoulder injury downtown Denver." We used phrase match and exact match only — no broad match to waste money on irrelevant clicks.

We also added structured data (FAQ schema) to their website. Not technically complex — most website builders do it through a plugin. But it meant their listings started showing rich results in search, with questions like "How long does PT for a torn meniscus take?" appearing directly in the search results.

The cost: $1,800/month ad spend. The result: average cost per lead dropped from $87 to $58. They were getting 8–10 new patient calls per week. Over 12 weeks, that's roughly 108 new patient leads. At a conservative 60% conversion rate, that's 65 new patients. Average revenue per new patient in their clinic was around $450 over the course of treatment. That works out to roughly $29,000 in revenue from $5,400 in ad spend.

One caveat: you need conversion tracking set up correctly. I've walked into clinics spending $2,000/month on Google Ads with zero tracking. They had no idea which keywords worked. If you don't have tracking, you're guessing. And in my experience, guessing with ad spend means you're wasting 40–60% of it.

## The Email List Your Clinic Should've Started Last Year

I'm going to say something that sounds like an exaggeration: the single most profitable marketing asset a physical therapy clinic can have is an email list of past patients. Not Instagram followers. Not Yelp reviews. An owned list of people who have already trusted you with their body.

A clinic in Brooklyn — "Park Slope PT" — had a list of 1,400 email addresses they had never used. They collected them through intake forms but never sent a single email. The owner told me she "didn't want to spam people."

Here's the reality: if someone gave you their email address during an intake, they expect to hear from you. Not hearing from you is worse than getting a relevant email.

We set up a monthly newsletter. Nothing fancy. One piece of injury-prevention content (with a video), one patient success story (with permission), and one offer — usually a "come back for a maintenance visit" discount or a referral incentive ("refer a friend and get $25 off your next visit").

We used Booksy for the appointment link integration — one click from the email to a booking page.

The first email went out to the full 1,400-person list. Open rate: 34%. Click rate: 8%. Within 48 hours, 12 people booked appointments. That's roughly $2,400 in revenue from a single email that cost $0 to send (Mailchimp's free tier covers that list size).

Over six months, the email list generated an average of $1,200/month in rebooking revenue. That's $7,200 over six months. The time investment: about two hours per month.

If you don't have an email list starting today, go set up a lead magnet on your website. Offer a free PDF: "5 Exercises for Lower Back Pain." Capture emails. Thank me later.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a solo practitioner with limited time. How much time does this actually take?**

Most of the strategies above require 2–4 hours per week total. The email newsletter is two hours per month. Google Ads requires a 30-minute weekly review once it's set up. Review responses take five minutes when a new one comes in. The biggest time investment is the initial setup — that's where I'd recommend paying a freelancer or agency for two weeks of focused work, then maintaining it yourself.

**Q: What's the minimum budget for Google Ads that actually works?**

I've seen $300/month work in smaller markets with specific keywords. In a city like Austin or Portland, $500–$800/month is the floor. Below that, you won't get enough data to optimize. One important note: don't start with a low budget if you can't commit to at least 90 days. Google's algorithm needs data. Pausing after two weeks is a waste of $200.

**Q: Do negative reviews really matter that much, or is that overblown?**

Yes, they matter. When I pulled search data for a clinic in Minneapolis, the clinics with 4.5+ stars had click-through rates roughly 35% higher than clinics with 4.0–4.2 stars. Even moving from 4.0 to 4.3 can increase your appointment request rate by 10–15%. Respond to negative reviews, fix the underlying issue, and ask happy patients to leave a review. It's not complicated, but most clinics don't do it.

**Q: Should I offer free initial consultations as a marketing tactic?**

It depends on your conversion rate. If you convert 70% of free consults to paid visits, it's probably worth it. If your conversion is below 40%, you're training people to shop around. I prefer a low-cost initial screening ($25–50) that covers your time and feels like a commitment to the patient. Free consultations attract tire-kickers. Paid screenings attract serious patients.

**Q: Is Yelp worth the effort for a physical therapy clinic?**

Yelp drives less volume than Google in most major US markets. But it drives higher-intent traffic. In a clinic we worked with in San Diego, Yelp accounted for about 12% of new patient inquiries, but those patients booked at a higher rate (72% vs. 58% from Google). Yelp is worth being on, but don't spend heavily on their ads until you've optimized Google first.

**Q: Can I do this myself, or do I need to hire someone?**

You can do the email list, review responses, and basic social media yourself. The Google Ads setup I'd recommend outsourcing to someone who specializes in local service businesses. Misconfigured Google Ads is one of the fastest ways to burn $1,000. I've fixed campaigns where the client's average cost per click was $12 for "physical therapist Boston" because they ran broad match with no negatives. Don't be that person.
