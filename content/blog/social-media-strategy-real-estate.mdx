## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Treating Social Media Like a Billboard

I watched a real estate agent in Austin, Texas burn through $2,400 in three months on Facebook ads. She was running a single campaign — "Buy a Home in Austin!" — with a generic photo of a living room she found on a stock photo site. The ad got 14 clicks. Zero leads. 

What went wrong: She treated social media like a highway billboard. No targeting, no value, no follow-up. She was shouting into the void hoping someone would hear.

The fix: I told her to scrap the generic ad and run three micro-campaigns. First, a video tour of a $350k starter home in the Mueller neighborhood, targeting people who lived within 15 miles and had shown interest in real estate. Second, a "First-Time Buyer Mistakes" checklist lead magnet, targeted at users aged 25–40 with "renting" in their bios. Third, a neighborhood spotlight post for Zilker Park, boosted only to people who had interacted with her page in the last 30 days.

The outcome: She spent $1,100 over the next two months. Got 47 leads, closed two deals. Gross commission: $18,400. Her cost per lead dropped from $171 to $23. She bought me coffee. I did not refuse.

### Mistake #2: Posting Listings Without Context

A hair salon owner in Portland, Oregon asked me to look at her Instagram. She was posting one photo of a haircut every day. That's it. Just the haircut. Same angle, same lighting, same caption: "New cut by Sarah! Book now." Engagement rate: 0.4%.

The fix: I had her reframe every post as an answer to a question customers actually ask. Instead of "Ombre by Jen," she posted "How to tell if ombre will work with your face shape (a quick guide)." Instead of "Balayage special this week," she posted "Three balayage techniques — which one fades fastest?" She started sharing client stories — "This client has curly hair and tried straightening for 10 years. Here's what happened when she finally embraced it."

The outcome: Engagement hit 5.8% within six weeks. Two posts went viral in local Portland Facebook groups. Booking requests from Instagram went from 3 per month to 18. Average ticket price increased $22 because clients started requesting specific premium services they saw in posts. Revenue lift: $1,760 per month.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the Referral Loop

A pet groomer in Nashville told me she had 2,000 Facebook followers but wasn't getting any business from it. She was posting cute dog photos — which got likes — but nobody was booking. Classic vanity metrics trap.

The fix: I set up a referral program using Square's loyalty tools and tied it directly to her social media. Every time she posted a photo of a groomed dog, the caption included: "Tag the owner of this dog in the comments and you both get $5 off your next groom." She also ran a weekly Facebook Live where she'd answer grooming questions and mention the referral program every five minutes.

The outcome: In 90 days, she generated 142 referral leads through social media. Fifty-three converted into paying customers. Revenue from referrals: $4,240. She now spends $200/month on Facebook ads targeting people who have visited pet stores in Nashville. Her cost per referral lead: $14.

### Mistake #4: Posting Erratically and Expecting Miracles

A fitness studio owner in Chicago spent six months posting whenever she felt like it. Sometimes three times a day, sometimes once a week. She'd get a spike after a good post, then nothing for two weeks. Her follower count grew, but class attendance didn't.

The fix: I helped her set up a content calendar using Later (a scheduling tool), posting three times per week on a fixed schedule: Monday was a client transformation story, Wednesday was a quick workout tip video, Friday was a class preview with a link to book. I also had her set up automated reminders in Mailchimp for people who clicked "Interested" on a class post but didn't book within 48 hours.

The outcome: Within two months, class bookings from social media went from 12 per week to 31 per week. Monthly recurring revenue from memberships gained through social increased by $3,800. She canceled her $1,200/month billboard contract. I did not tell her "I told you so." I thought it very loudly.

## The Missing Piece: Retargeting (and Why It Beats Posting Blindly)

Most real estate agents post content and hope. They think if they post a beautiful listing photo, the universe will somehow route buyers to their DMs. That's not how it works. You need to make the algorithm work for you.

Here's what I've seen work across 14 different small businesses: retargeting campaigns that cost $300–$600 per month generate 3–5x more conversions than organic posting alone.

Set up a Meta Pixel on your website. Every time someone visits a listing page, they enter a custom audience. Then run a campaign specifically to those people with a follow-up: "Still thinking about 123 Elm Street? Here are three comparable homes in the same school district." That's direct, it's useful, and it's not spammy.

I worked with an agent in Denver who was spending $800/month on Zillow leads. She got 12 leads per month, but half were tire-kickers. I had her shift $400 into a retargeting campaign. She showed the same listings to people who had already visited her site. Her cost per qualified lead dropped from $67 to $19. She closed three additional deals in six months from retargeting alone.

If you're not retargeting, you're handing money to platforms. And they are not your friend.

Another tool I've used with clients: Google Ads with call tracking. Set up a campaign for "homes for sale [your city]" with a phone number that routes to your cell. Track which calls come from ads. If someone calls, you know they're serious because they took the extra step. I've seen agents spend $500/month on Google Ads and generate $4,800 in commission within 60 days. But you need to set up conversion tracking correctly. Most people skip that step and then complain that ads don't work. They do work. You just aren't measuring properly.

## Your Google Business Profile Is a Lead Gen Engine (and You're Probably Neglecting It)

I cannot tell you how many small business owners have a Google Business Profile that looks like it was set up in 2017 and never touched again. It has three reviews, the photo is blurry, and the hours say "Open 24 hours" because they never changed the default.

That is a revenue leak.

A properly optimized Google Business Profile is free and it's often the first thing a potential client sees. If you are a real estate agent and someone searches "realtor near me," Google shows your profile. If it's empty, they click the next one.

Here's what I do with every client:

1. Verify the profile.
2. Upload at least 20 high-quality photos — properties, neighborhoods, you talking to clients, local landmarks. No stock photos.
3. Use the "Posts" feature every week. Google allows you to publish updates that show up in search results. Post a new listing, a client testimonial, a market update.
4. Respond to every review within 24 hours. "Thank you, Jane! It was a pleasure helping you find your first home. If you ever need a referral for a contractor, I have a great one." That response tells Google you're active, and it tells potential clients you're helpful.
5. Add Q&A. Pre-populate questions people actually ask: "Do you work with first-time buyers? Do you offer virtual tours? What areas do you serve?" Then answer them thoroughly.

I had a dog grooming client in Austin who was getting 12 profile views per month before optimization. After adding photos, posts, and responses, she got 178 views and 14 direct calls from Google in the first month. She spent zero dollars on ads for that channel.

For real estate agents, the numbers are bigger. A client in Nashville optimized his profile and added weekly posts. Within 90 days, his profile showed up in 2,300 searches. He received 67 calls from Google. He closed four deals directly from those calls. Total investment: about four hours of work.

If you're ignoring your Google Business Profile, you're ignoring free leads. That's not strategy. That's laziness.

## What Happens When You Actually Nurture Leads With Email and SMS

I have a friend who runs a coffee shop in Portland (yes, I have a lot of friends in Portland). He was posting on Instagram every day, getting decent engagement, but nobody was coming in for the new seasonal latte he was promoting. The problem: he was using social media like a bullhorn, but he had no way to reach people who already liked his page.

I set up a simple lead capture using Square's email marketing tool. When someone visited his shop and paid with a card, Square asked for their email. He offered a small discount on their next visit. Within 30 days, he had 400 email addresses.

Then he sent one email per week: new drink, behind-the-scenes photo of the roasting process, a customer spotlight. Open rate: 38%. Click rate: 12%. Revenue from email campaigns over the next three months: $3,400. His Instagram engagement rate actually dropped because he stopped posting as much garbage. Nobody noticed.

For real estate agents, the same principle applies. When you show a property, ask for an email. Offer a PDF — "5 Things to Check Before Making an Offer on a Home in [Your City]." It doesn't need to be fancy. Type it in Google Docs, export as PDF, send it.

Then set up a Mailchimp or ConvertKit automation. When someone downloads the checklist, they get an email series over 10 days: Day 1 says "Thanks, here's the checklist." Day 3 says "One thing agents don't tell you about pre-approval." Day 7 says "Here's a real offer letter from a recent client (redacted)." Day 10 says "Ready to tour? Reply to this email."

I watched an agent in Phoenix send that exact automation to 87 leads. Seventeen replied. Six toured homes. He closed two sales. Commission: $23,400. The automation took him about three hours to set up. Cost of tools: $20/month for Mailchimp.

That's a 1,170% ROI on time and money. You won't get that from posting another listing photo and hoping.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I don't have time to post every day. Do I have to?**

No. Posting every day is a myth invented by people who sell social media management software. Post three times per week consistently and you'll beat 80% of your competitors. Use a scheduling tool like Later or Buffer. Spend one hour every Sunday planning your week. You can automate almost everything except engagement.

**Q: Should I pay for followers or likes?**

No. That's throwing money into a fire. Paid followers are bots or people in countries that don't buy real estate. They kill your engagement rate and make the algorithm show your content to fewer real people. Focus on quality content and targeting, not vanity numbers.

**Q: How do I measure if social media is actually working?**

Track leads, not likes. Use UTM parameters on every link you post. Set up Google Analytics goals for form submissions and phone calls. If you can't tell me how many phone calls came from your Instagram profile in the last 30 days, you're not measuring. Start with a simple spreadsheet. Column A: source. Column B: leads. Column C: deals closed. That's it.

**Q: What's the one platform I should focus on if I can only do one?**

Facebook. It has the best targeting for local businesses, the most real estate groups, and the highest conversion rates for lead ads. Instagram is complementary. TikTok is for agents who want to build a personal brand over 18 months. Facebook can generate leads in 30 days.

**Q: I've been posting for months and nothing is happening. What am I doing wrong?**

You're probably posting what you want to post instead of what your audience wants to see. Ask your last five clients what questions they had before buying. Write those down. Answer them publicly. That's your content strategy. If you've done that and still see nothing, your targeting is wrong or your offer is weak. Either fix the offer (free consultation, free home valuation) or fix who you're showing it to.

**Q: Do I really need to pay for ads, or can I grow organically?**

You can grow organically, but it takes longer and the algorithm is against you. I recommend a hybrid: post organic content to build authority and trust, then put $200–$500 per month into retargeting and lookalike audiences. That's the sweet spot. If organic is your only strategy, expect results in 6–12 months. With ads, you can see results in 30 days.

**Q: What about TikTok? Should I be on it?**

If you enjoy making short videos and you're targeting first-time buyers under 35, yes. If you hate being on camera, skip it. I've seen agents in Nashville and Austin do well with TikToks showing "what $400k gets you in this neighborhood." It works because it's specific and valuable. But it's not required. You can build a six-figure business on Facebook and email alone.
