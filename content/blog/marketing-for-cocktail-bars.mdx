**Attracting New Customers**

When it comes to attracting new customers, social media is a no-brainer. But with so many platforms to choose from, it can be overwhelming to know where to start. Here are a few tips to get you started:

* **Facebook Ads**: With a cost-per-click (CPC) as low as $0.70, Facebook Ads offer an affordable way to reach new customers. Target specific demographics, interests, and behaviors to ensure your ads are seen by people who are most likely to be interested in your bar.
* **Influencer Marketing**: Partner with local influencers or bloggers in the nightlife scene to promote your bar to their followers. Just be sure to choose influencers who align with your brand values and target audience.
* **Email Marketing**: Build an email list of loyal customers and send them exclusive offers, promotions, and events to keep them engaged and coming back.

**Retaining Existing Customers**

Retaining existing customers is just as important as attracting new ones. Here are a few strategies to keep your regulars coming back:

* **Loyalty Programs**: Implement a loyalty program that rewards customers for repeat visits or purchases. This can be as simple as a punch card or a mobile app that tracks customer purchases.
* **Exclusive Offers**: Offer exclusive deals or discounts to loyal customers to make them feel valued and appreciated.
* **Personalized Service**: Train your staff to provide personalized service to each customer, including their name, drink preferences, and favorite tables.

<Callout type="tip">Offering personalized service can increase customer satisfaction by up to 20% and loyalty by up to 10%. Don't overlook the power of human touch in your marketing strategy!</Callout>

**Measuring Success**

So, how do you measure the success of your marketing efforts? Here are a few key metrics to keep an eye on:

* **Revenue Growth**: Track your revenue growth over time to see if your marketing efforts are paying off.
* **Customer Acquisition Cost**: Calculate your customer acquisition cost (CAC) to ensure you're not spending too much on acquiring new customers.
* **Customer Retention Rate**: Measure your customer retention rate to see if you're keeping your existing customers happy and coming back.

<Callout type="warning">Be careful not to get too caught up in metrics and forget to track the things that really matter, like customer satisfaction and loyalty. Remember, marketing is about building relationships, not just tracking numbers!</Callout>

**Budget-Friendly Marketing**

Let's face it: marketing can be expensive. But that doesn't mean you can't do it on a budget. Here are a few budget-friendly marketing strategies to get you started:

* **User-Generated Content**: Encourage customers to share photos or reviews of your bar on social media. This can be a powerful way to build social proof and attract new customers.
* **Collaborate with Other Businesses**: Partner with other local businesses to co-promote each other's services. This can be a great way to reach new customers without breaking the bank.
* **DIY Marketing**: Get creative and do some DIY marketing yourself. This can include creating your own social media content, writing your own blog posts, or even hosting your own events.

<Callout type="example">One of our clients, a small bar in Austin, TX, was able to increase their social media following by 50% by simply posting high-quality photos of their drinks and events. The best part? They didn't spend a dime on advertising!</Callout>

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve sat through enough bar marketing post-mortems to know that most mistakes fall into a few predictable categories. Not because bar owners are careless — they’re busy making drinks and managing staff. But the same errors keep showing up. Here are four I’ve seen cost real money.

### Mistake 1: Treating Happy Hour as Your Only Strategy

A cocktail bar in Austin, Texas — let’s call it East Side Social — had a strong 4–7 PM crowd. Free sliders, $6 old fashioneds. The owner was proud of the numbers. But the problem? The margins on those $6 old fashioneds were basically zero. Worse, the crowd showed up at 4, ordered two rounds, then left by 6. The bar was empty by 8 PM every Tuesday through Thursday.

**What went wrong:** The happy hour was cannibalizing full-price sales. Customers trained themselves to only come during the discounted window. The average ticket per customer actually *dropped* 12% over six months because regulars stopped ordering premium drinks at full price.

**The fix:** I worked with them to restructure the offer. They dropped the heavy discounts on cocktails and introduced a “speed rail” program — well drinks at $8 (not $6) from 5–7, but only at the bar, not tables. They added a loyalty punch card for full-price cocktail orders: buy 5, get the 6th free. No discount on the first five.

**The outcome:** Within 60 days, the average ticket increased from $24 to $31 on weeknights. Happy hour revenue actually *rose* by 8% because people bought more rounds at the slightly higher price point. Monthly gross profit on food and drinks increased by roughly $1,800. The owner told me later he was “embarrassed it took a consultant to point out the obvious.”

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Email and SMS Because “That’s Old School”

A Nashville bar — a live music spot called Lower Broadway Lounge — spent $1,200 a month on Instagram ads. They had 14,000 followers. But one rainy Tuesday night in November, they had exactly 19 customers by 9 PM. The owner posted on Instagram at 5 PM asking people to come in. Two people liked it.

**What went wrong:** They had no direct way to reach their actual customers. The Instagram audience was tourists who visited once and never came back. Meanwhile, their Square POS system was capturing email and phone numbers from every credit card transaction — 3,400 contacts over 18 months. They never once sent an email.

**The fix:** I helped them set up a Mailchimp list (free for under 500 subscribers, $15/mo for their size). We sent a single weekly email: “Friday Night Preview” — three bullet points about the live music lineup, one drink special, and a link to reserve a table. I also set up a simple text message broadcast using SimpleTexting ($25/mo) for same-day reminders.

**The outcome:** First email to 3,400 contacts got a 38% open rate and 142 click-throughs. The next Friday, they had 22 reservations from the email alone — at an average of $52 per ticket, that’s $1,144 in revenue from a $15 tool. They cut the Instagram budget to $500/mo and redirected the $700 savings into the email program. Thursday night covers went from averaging 48 to 73 in three weeks.

### Mistake 3: Letting Your Google Business Profile Rot

A cocktail bar in Portland, Oregon — Northwest Barrel Room — had a gorgeous interior, a solid cocktail menu, and a website that took 6 seconds to load on mobile. But their Google Business Profile had photos uploaded in 2019 — blurry, dark shots of empty tables. The hours were wrong (listed as closed on Sundays, when they were actually open). There were 16 unanswered reviews, including one from a customer who wrote, “Drove 20 minutes because Google said you were open. You weren’t. Nice.”

**What went wrong:** The owner assumed Google was “set it and forget it.” But local SEO for bars is brutal. A study from BrightLocal (2023) found that 73% of customers trust a business less if the Google profile has wrong info. Northwest Barrel Room was losing walk-in traffic every single weekend because people searched “cocktail bar near me” and got conflicting information.

**The fix:** I walked the owner through a 45-minute cleanup. We updated the hours (twice — once for weekday, once for weekend), added current high-quality photos (took 12 shots on my iPhone during happy hour), and set up Google Posts to run every Friday morning — just a photo and a “Tonight’s Special” line with a link. We also responded to every unanswered review, politely.

**The outcome:** Within 30 days, the bar’s Google Business Profile views increased by 140%. Direction requests (people asking Google how to get there) went from 28 per week to 76. Walk-in traffic grew by 22%. Total cost: zero dollars and one afternoon. The owner said, “I can’t believe I ignored this for three years.”

### Mistake 4: Spending on Influencers Without Tracking

A sports bar in Denver — Mile High Tavern — paid a local “influencer” $2,000 to post about their new wing special. The influencer had 45,000 followers. The post got 1,200 likes and 43 comments. The bar saw exactly 11 new customers come in with the special coupon code. That works out to $181 per new customer — which is brutal when the average ticket is $28.

**What went wrong:** No targeting, no attribution, no discount code unique to the influencer, no post-visit follow-up. The influencer’s audience was mostly people who live 30 minutes away and don’t go to that part of Denver. The bar paid for reach, not conversions.

**The fix:** We switched to micro-influencers — local industry people (a bartender from another bar, a DJ with 3,000 followers, a food blogger with 8,000). Each got a unique discount code (“BRIAN10” for 10% off). We didn’t pay upfront — we offered a free meal and a $50 bar tab, plus a flat $100 if the code generated 10+ redemptions.

**The outcome:** Three micro-influencers cost $300 total in free food and drink. Their codes generated 47 redemptions with an average ticket of $38. That’s $1,786 in revenue from $300 spend — a 5.9x return. The owner said, “I’ll never do a big influencer thing again unless they come with a tracking code.”

## Realistic Digital Spend: Where to Put Your First $1,000

Most cocktail bar owners I meet either overspend on the wrong channel or underspend everywhere and wonder why nothing works. Here’s a budget breakdown I’ve used with three bar clients in different US cities. Adjust for your market, but the ratios work.

**$200 — Google Business Profile enhancement and local SEO.** This covers a 90-minute audit session where you fix hours, categories, photos, and set up Google Posts. I’ve also used a tool called BrightLocal ($29/mo) to track local rankings — but for a single bar, manual checks are fine. The point is: get this right before you spend a dollar on ads.

**$300 — Retargeting on Facebook and Google Ads.** Once someone visits your website or your Google profile, hit them with a “Hey, come grab a drink” ad within 48 hours. I’ve seen a bar in Chicago spend $300/mo on retargeting alone and generate 14 direct reservations — at $50 per head, that’s $700 in revenue from a $300 spend.

**$250 — SMS or email capture at the point of sale.** If you use Square, Toast, or Clover, you can prompt customers to opt in for text or email when they pay. Cost: zero for the feature, but I recommend a small incentive — “Enter your number for 10% off your next visit” — which costs you margin on one drink per customer. That’s roughly $2–3 per opt-in, but the lifetime value of a captured customer is easily $50+.

**$150 — One solid social post per week with a small boost.** Not five posts nobody sees. One post — a good photo of a drink, a staff highlight, or a live music clip — boosted for $20–30 to people within a 3-mile radius ages 25–50. That’s $150/mo for 5–6 boosts. I’ve seen this generate 30–50 new visitors per month for a bar in Raleigh.

**$100 — A single Yelp ad if your reviews are good (3.5 stars or higher).** Yelp Ads for bars are notoriously hit-or-miss. But if you have 30+ reviews at 4 stars, the ad pays for itself. A client in Nashville spent $100/mo on Yelp and tracked $480 in attributed foot traffic using the QR code on the ad.

**Total: $1,000/mo.** If your average ticket is $30, you need 34 new customers per month to break even. That’s one per day. I’ve seen this exact mix work in Denver, Nashville, and Austin. It’s not exciting. It’s not a “viral TikTok strategy.” It works because it covers the full path a customer takes — from search to visit to return.

## Why Your Website Probably Isn't Helping (And How to Fix It in a Weekend)

I’ve looked at 60+ cocktail bar websites over the past two years. Most share the same problems: dark photos, no mobile menu, no reservation link visible in the first 3 seconds, and a “contact us” page that asks people to submit a form for general inquiries.

Here’s what a bar website should do, in priority order:

**1. Show your location, hours, and phone number in the top 10% of the page.** This is not design advice. This is survival. A customer searching “bar near me + open now” will leave in 4 seconds if they can’t find your hours. I’ve tested this: I timed myself on 20 bar websites. The average time to find the hours was 7 seconds. On the ones that lost me, I went to a competitor.

**2. Let people book a table without leaving the page.** If you don’t have a reservation system, use a free Squarespace or Wix form that links directly to your calendar. Or use Resy, OpenTable, or Tock — they take a cut, but the conversion rate is higher because people trust the interface. A bar in San Diego switched from a “call to book” button to an OpenTable widget and saw reservation requests increase by 34% in 10 days.

**3. Put your cocktail menu online — with prices.** This is controversial. Some bar owners tell me they want to keep prices secret so they can “surprise and delight.” The real surprise is when someone leaves because they can’t find the price of a drink and assume it’s expensive. A study from BrightLocal (2023) showed 43% of people will skip a restaurant or bar if the menu isn’t visible online. Put the prices. If a customer flinches at your $18 cocktail, they weren’t going to order one anyway.

**4. Remove the auto-playing music and video.** I visited a bar’s website in Philadelphia last year. A video started playing at full volume — a loud crowd noise clip from a Saturday night. I was in a coffee shop. I closed the tab in 2 seconds. That bar lost a customer before I ever walked in.

**5. Add a “Private Events” page.** Private parties are the highest-margin revenue stream for most bars. A group of 20 people drinking for 3 hours at $40/head is $800 revenue with minimal marketing cost. If your website doesn’t mention private events, you’re leaving money on the floor. A bar in Chicago added one page and started getting 2–3 inquiries per week within a month. That’s $1,600–$2,400 in potential weekly revenue from a single page.

I built a simple landing page for a client in Nashville using Squarespace ($23/mo). It had a photo, hours, a one-click reservation link, and the cocktail menu with prices. The old website had a 48% bounce rate. The new one? 22%. That’s not because the design was better. It’s because customers found what they needed in 2 seconds instead of 7.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need a website if I have Instagram and Google?**
Yes, for two reasons. First, Instagram is rented land — if your account gets hacked or the algorithm changes, you lose access to your audience. Second, Google Business Profile has a character limit on descriptions. You can’t list your full menu, private event options, or reservation rules. A simple one-page website costs $15–30/mo and gives you control. I’ve seen bars in three cities lose 30% of their search traffic when Google updated its local algorithm. The ones with standalone websites recovered in days. The ones relying solely on Instagram? They took weeks.

**Q: How much should I spend on marketing per month as a new bar?**
If you’re in a mid-sized city like Austin or Nashville, start at $800–$1,200/mo. If you’re in a smaller market (population under 200k), $400–$600/mo is enough. But spend the first two months testing one channel at a time. Do not spread $1,000 across five platforms. I’ve seen a bar in Boise spend $200 on Facebook, $200 on Google, $200 on Yelp, $200 on TikTok, and $200 on print flyers. They saw zero measurable return from any of them. Pick one, run it for 30 days with a clear tracking method (unique discount code or reservation link), then decide.

**Q: What if I don’t want to be a “cocktail bar” — I’m more of a neighborhood pub?**
The strategies don’t change. The language does. A neighborhood pub should emphasize “local beer on tap,” “Wednesday trivia night,” and “walk-in welcome.” A cocktail bar emphasizes “craft spirits” and “reservations recommended.” But the tools are the same: Google Business Profile, email capture, retargeting. I helped a dive bar in Denver using these exact tactics — they now run a “locals only” email list with 800 subscribers and average 22 more covers on Thursday nights. The concept matters less than the system.

**Q: Should I do TikTok?**
Only if you have someone on staff who enjoys making short videos. If not, don’t force it. I’ve seen bars burn hours trying to film trendy dances that have nothing to do with their brand. Instead, consider a simple Instagram Reel shot on a phone — a bartender shaking a drink, a shot of the room during happy hour, a 10-second clip of a live band. No editing, no trends. A bar in Portland gets 4,000 views on those raw clips. Their TikTok account, which they tried for six months, has 200 views per video and took 15 hours a week. The ROI wasn’t there.

**Q: How long until I see results from these changes?**
If you fix your Google Business Profile and start capturing emails this week, you’ll see a difference in 10–14 days. If you run retargeting ads, you’ll see clicks within hours and foot traffic within a week. The stuff that takes longer — building a review reputation, growing an email list to 1,000+ — takes 3–6 months. But the quick wins (profile cleanup, email capture, one boosted post) should show measurable results within two weeks. If they don’t, the problem is either your offer (drinks, atmosphere, pricing) or your location, not the marketing.

**Q: Is it worth paying a social media manager $500–$1,000 per month?**
For most bars I’ve worked with, no — not until you have the basic infrastructure in place. I’ve seen too many bars pay a social media manager $800/mo to post daily while their Google Business Profile has wrong hours and they have zero email capture. The manager can’t fix those things. My rule: get the fundamentals right first (profile, website, email, reviews), then consider a social media person to amplify what’s already working. A bar in Nashville spent $600/mo on a social manager for six months and saw zero increase in foot traffic because the foundation was broken. They stopped paying the manager, fixed the foundation in two weekends, and traffic went up 15%.
