## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Automating the Wrong Thing

A coffee shop owner in Portland, Oregon spent three weekends setting up an automation that posted their daily specials to Instagram, Facebook, and a blog simultaneously. Felt productive. Looked cool.

Here's what actually happened: Their specials changed at 11am when they sold out of the first batch. The automation still posted the 7am version. Customers walked in asking for a lavender latte that had been gone for four hours. Baristas spent more time explaining the mistake than the automation saved.

The fix: Before automating anything, track how much time you actually spend on each task for one week. This owner was spending 45 minutes a week on social posts. What was killing them? Manually entering email newsletter signups from Square into Mailchimp — 12 hours a month. They automated that instead.

The outcome: Recovered 11 hours per month. Lost two customers from the Instagram debacle (roughly $1,200 annual revenue). The email automation generated an additional $3,800 in repeat business over three months because people actually got their welcome discount.

**Rule: Automate the boring, repetitive data transfer. Not the creative decision-making.**

### Mistake #2: No Error Handling

A hair salon in Austin, Texas set up Make to automatically email a thank-you note with a booking link after every Booksy appointment. Great idea. Except they didn't account for cancellations.

The automation ran 15 minutes after the appointment end time. If someone canceled same-day, they still got the email. "Thanks for visiting! Book your next cut here." From a person who didn't visit. Who was annoyed. One customer emailed back: "I canceled because my mom had surgery. Thanks for the reminder."

Then there was the client who marked "no-show" who also got the email. Then the stylist who blocked time for personal training but accidentally marked it as a client slot.

The fix: Add a filter step that checks the appointment status before sending. Make has built-in "only if" conditions. Takes two minutes to set up.

The outcome: Complaints dropped from 4-5 per month to zero. The owner also added a cancellation trigger that sends a "Sorry we missed you — here's 15% off your next booking" message instead. Recovered $1,600 in canceled appointments over two months that rebooked.

**Rule: Every automation needs a "what if this goes wrong" backup. Test with a single contact before turning it on for real customers.**

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Data Hygiene

A pet grooming business in Nashville connected their Yelp booking system to a Mailchimp list. Every new booking added a contact automatically. Sounded clean.

Six months later they sent a "Holiday Grooming Special" email blast to 3,400 contacts. Three hundred bounced. Dozens unsubscribed. Several replied angry: "I brought my cat in ONCE in 2019. Stop emailing me."

The problem: The automation added every booking as a new contact — even repeat customers. The list bloated with duplicates. Some people showed up 12 times. The groomer had no way to track actual customer count.

The fix: Before adding a contact to Mailchimp, check if they already exist by email address. If yes, update their appointment date instead of creating a duplicate. Make lets you do this with a "Search" module before the "Create Contact" module.

The outcome: Cleaned the list from 3,400 to 1,200 actual customers. Open rates went from 12% to 34%. The holiday campaign generated $5,200 in bookings — triple what they'd done the year before without automation, because the emails actually reached people who wanted them.

**Rule: Automating bad data just makes bad data faster. Clean your source first.**

### Mistake #4: Over-Engineering the First Automation

A fitness studio owner in Chicago watched a YouTube tutorial and decided to build a "master automation." It would: take new Mindbody bookings → check their purchase history → send different emails based on whether they bought a class pack, membership, or single session → add them to a Facebook custom audience → log everything in Google Sheets → send a Slack notification to the owner → wait three days → send a follow-up.

It took her 14 hours to build. It broke five times in the first week. She abandoned it after two weeks.

The fix: Start with one trigger and one action. New booking → send confirmation email. That's it. Run it for two weeks. If it works, add one more step. Rinse, repeat.

The outcome: A simple booking confirmation automation saved her 8 hours per month in manual emails. She added a "no-show" reminder two months later. Total investment: 45 minutes of setup time. No breakdowns.

**Rule: Automations should grow with your confidence, not overwhelm you in week one.**

## The Automations That Actually Make You Money

### Email Sequences That Don't Annoy People

Most small business email automation advice comes from people who sell email automation software. They tell you to send five emails in the first week after someone subscribes. For a coffee shop? That's insane. Your customers don't want to hear from you five times.

Here's what works: One welcome email with a discount. One reminder if they don't use the discount within 14 days. One birthday email with a free drink. That's it. Three emails total per year unless they buy something.

I tested this at a yoga studio in Denver that was sending a weekly newsletter to people who'd only taken a single trial class. Unsubscribe rate was 8% per month. We cut back to: welcome email with class schedule, 14-day follow-up, birthday offer, and a "we miss you" email after 60 days of no visits. Unsubscribe rate dropped to 0.3%. Class bookings from email went up 40%.

The setup in Make takes 30 minutes:
1. Trigger: New contact in Mailchimp (or manually add from Square/Booksy)
2. Condition: Did they already buy something? If yes, add to "active" list. If no, add to "nurture" list.
3. Module: Add tag for first purchase date
4. Module: Send welcome email
5. Wait module: 14 days
6. Module: Check if discount was used
7. Condition: Used → add to active. Not used → send reminder

Total cost: $0 on Make's free plan. Mailchimp's free tier handles up to 500 contacts.

### Connecting Your Booking System to Google Ads

This is the automation that pays for itself in a week. And most small businesses skip it because they don't realize it's possible.

Scenario: A hair salon in Miami runs Google Ads for "haircut Miami" and "balayage Miami." Someone clicks the ad, books a cut through Booksy for $65, then adds full highlights at checkout for $180. Total revenue: $245.

Without automation: The salon doesn't know which ad led to the booking. They see "3 new clients this week" and hope the ads are working. They spend $1,200/month on Google Ads with no visibility into actual return.

With automation in Make:
1. Trigger: New booking in Booksy
2. Module: Search Google Sheets for that email address
3. Condition: Is the email in the "Google Ads lead" sheet? (Created when someone clicks the ad)
4. If yes: Log booking value, service type, and ad campaign name in a separate sheet
5. Module: Add Google Ads conversion tag for that amount

What this tells you: Which ad campaigns produce the highest-value bookings. One week of data showed "balayage ads" delivered average bookings of $210 versus "cut ads" at $55. The owner shifted 60% of her budget to balayage ads. Monthly ad spend stayed the same ($1,200). Revenue from ads went from $4,800/month to $7,200/month.

Tools needed: Booksy (or any booking system with webhooks), Google Sheets, Google Ads (with conversion tracking set up). Make's free plan covers this.

Most small businesses send a "thank you" email and call it a day. The data says that's leaving money on the table.

A pet groomer in Denver ($65 per visit, average client visits every 6 weeks) automated this sequence in Make:

## When Automation Fails (And How to Catch It Before It Hurts You)

Automations break. This is not a moral failing. It's software.

The problem is that small businesses often don't know an automation has broken until a customer complains. By then you've lost trust, money, or both.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: You will spend more time maintaining automations than building them in the first year. Plan for this.

### Set Up Your Own Warning System

In Make, add a "Text Me If This Fails" module at the end of every automation. It uses the "Send an SMS" function or an email module pointed at your phone's SMS gateway (like 5551234567@txt.att.net).

Every time the automation completes successfully, you get a text you can ignore. Every time it breaks, you get nothing — which means you check once a day and notice the gap.

A bakery in Portland used this after their "new order notification" automation silently broke for three days. They missed 12 catering orders worth $2,400. The "daily success check" costs 2 texts per day. Cost of missing orders? Far higher.

### Watch for Data Drift

This is the quiet killer. Your automation works perfect for six months. Then Square changes their API. Or Mailchimp renames a field. Or someone on your team changes a spreadsheet column header.

Suddenly, new contacts are missing their phone numbers. Or they're being assigned the wrong tags. Or nothing breaks visibly — the automation runs, it just writes garbage data.

The fix: Once a month, manually check 5-10 records that went through an automation. Compare input to output. If anything looks wrong, pause the automation and investigate before it compounds.

A coffee roaster in Austin discovered their order fulfillment automation had been assigning "shipping method: ground" to all orders for two months. A Square update had renamed the "shipping speed" field. All their wholesale customers (who paid extra for overnight) were getting ground shipping. Two accounts canceled. Total loss: $4,600 in annual recurring revenue.

**Rule: Trust but verify. Especially after your tools update.**

**Q: What if I mess up and send something embarrassing to customers?**

You will. Accept this. Here's how to minimize damage:
- Test every new automation with a fake email address first

The mistake isn't messing up. It's not having a rollback plan.

In every client I've worked with, automation freed up 5-10 hours per week per employee. Not one has used it to fire someone. Most used it to give their team better hours or pay raises.
