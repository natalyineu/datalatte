Television advertising used to require a minimum of $15,000–$50,000 just to enter the room. A 30-second local TV spot, production included, was a big-budget line item that small businesses couldn't justify.

Roku changed the calculation. With **85 million active US households** streaming on Roku devices and a self-serve ad platform with minimum campaigns as low as **$500**, streaming TV advertising is now genuinely accessible to local businesses.

The question is whether it actually works — and for which types of local businesses the CPM and brand exposure justify the investment over more measurable channels like Google Ads or Meta.

## What Roku Is and Why It Matters

Roku is the leading streaming platform in the US by active users — not a streaming service itself, but the operating system and hardware on which millions of Americans watch Netflix, Hulu, YouTube, Disney+, and hundreds of free ad-supported channels.

When someone watches anything on their Roku device — whether it's a paid subscription service or a free channel — Roku can serve ads. The platform collects viewing data across all these streams, building detailed audience profiles based on what people actually watch (not just what they claim to be interested in).

In 2026, US adults average **over 2 hours of CTV viewing daily**. Roku captures a significant share of that time — and can serve your business's ad directly on the living room TV.

## Roku's Advertising Products

### OneView (Self-Serve Platform)

OneView is Roku's self-serve DSP — the access point for small and mid-sized businesses. You create an account, upload your video ad, set targeting parameters, and launch. No agency required.

Through OneView you can target by:
- **Geography:** City, zip code, DMA (designated market area)
- **Audience segments:** Demographics, interests, purchase behaviour, viewing patterns
- **Device type:** Roku TVs, Roku streaming sticks, other connected devices
- **Contextual:** Ads served near specific types of content

The minimum campaign budget on OneView is $500, though meaningful reach typically requires $1,500–3,000/month for a local market.

### Managed Service

For businesses with larger budgets ($10,000+/month), Roku offers managed service with a dedicated account team, access to premium inventory, and more advanced audience capabilities.

### Roku Home Screen Ads

Ads that appear on Roku's home screen — the interface people see before selecting an app. High-visibility placement but limited to Roku's managed service.

## What Type of Video Ad You Need

Every Roku ad is a video. There is no static image format. This is the primary barrier for local businesses — you need a 15 or 30-second video before you can start.

The good news: production quality requirements for streaming TV are lower than you might think. The bad news: you genuinely need a video. Static images don't work in the pre-roll and mid-roll placements Roku uses.

**What works:**
- 15-second ads perform best for brand awareness — short enough to hold attention, long enough to communicate
- 30-second ads work better for direct response with a specific offer
- Authentic, local-feeling content often outperforms overly polished productions for small businesses
- Close-up shots of your product or space with a clear verbal call-to-action

**Budget-friendly production options:**
- Smartphone video with good lighting and an external microphone: $0–$200
- Freelance video editor on Fiverr or Upwork to polish raw footage: $50–$300
- Local videographer for a half-day shoot: $500–$1,500

<Callout type="tip">
Roku ads are non-skippable in most placements. This is a significant advantage over YouTube (where 70% of users skip after 5 seconds). Your full 15 or 30 seconds reaches the viewer — which increases the value of a well-constructed script and clear CTA.
</Callout>

## Which Local Businesses Get the Most from Roku Ads

**Fitness studios and gyms:** TV is an ideal format for showing the energy of your space. A 15-second clip of a class in action, with a "first class free" offer and your local address, can drive meaningful trial. Target health and fitness audiences within your geographic area.

**Restaurants and cafés:** Food looks incredible on TV. A 15-second sensory tour of your best dishes, ending with your address and "open until 10pm," works especially well for evening viewing when people are deciding where to eat.

**Event venues and entertainment:** Showing upcoming events with strong visuals builds awareness in a way that text-based digital ads can't match.

**Home services (contractors, cleaners, landscapers):** Streaming TV skews to homeowners with above-average household income — an ideal audience for home service businesses.

**Less suitable:** Pure e-commerce brands without a local presence get less value from geo-targeted TV; service businesses that rely on immediate response (plumbers needing emergency calls) may find Search Ads more efficient for direct ROI.

## Setting Up a Roku Campaign: Step by Step

**Step 1: Create your OneView account**
Go to oneview.roku.com and register. The process is similar to Google Ads account creation.

**Step 2: Upload your video creative**
Accepted formats: MP4, MOV. Minimum resolution: 1920×1080 (1080p). Accepted lengths: 15 or 30 seconds. Have a 15-second and 30-second version if possible — they suit different placement types.

**Step 3: Set geographic targeting**
For local businesses, target by DMA (your city's designated market area) or by zip code radius. Don't run nationally — localise aggressively.

**Step 4: Select audience segments**
Layer audience targeting on top of geography. Roku's data includes viewing behaviour, app usage, and modelled purchase data. For a coffee shop, try "coffee drinkers" + "morning routine content viewers" + local DMA.

**Step 5: Set budget and flight dates**
Start with a $500–1,000 test over 2–4 weeks. Roku's dashboard shows impressions, reach (unique households), completion rate, and for connected campaigns, attributed website visits.

<Callout type="warning">
Roku ads are an awareness channel, not a direct response channel. Don't measure success by immediate conversions or sales. Measure brand search lift (are more people searching your name?), website traffic from organic and direct sources, and foot traffic if you can track it. Give campaigns 4–8 weeks before judging performance.
</Callout>

## Cost Expectations and Realistic ROI

Roku's CPM for local targeting ranges **$15–35** depending on targeting precision and inventory quality. A $1,000 monthly campaign reaches 28,000–65,000 unique households, each seeing your ad in a TV environment.

For comparison: a $1,000 Google Ads campaign might generate 600–900 clicks (direct response, easy to measure). A $1,000 Roku campaign generates 30,000+ brand impressions in the living room (upper-funnel awareness, harder to attribute directly).

These aren't competing — they serve different stages of the funnel. For businesses already running efficient Google and Meta Ads and looking to scale awareness, Roku fills an upper-funnel gap that digital-only channels can't serve.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I target specific neighbourhoods with Roku Ads?

You can target by DMA (city region) and zip code, but not at the neighbourhood level. For very localised businesses (a single coffee shop wanting to reach only the adjacent 3 zip codes), zip code targeting is close enough.

### Does Roku let me track whether TV viewers actually visit my store?

Roku offers website visit attribution (tracking users who saw your ad and later visited your website). Physical store visit attribution is available through managed service partnerships with location data providers — not directly through the self-serve OneView platform.

### What happens if my video ad doesn't perform?

Roku's OneView platform lets you pause or adjust campaigns at any time. You're not locked into a commitment like traditional TV. If your completion rate is below 70% or your cost-per-website-visit is unsatisfactory, update your creative or targeting and test again.

### Is Roku better than YouTube for local business video advertising?

Different strengths. YouTube has 30x more targeting options (keywords, remarketing, interests at granular level) and better direct response capabilities. Roku has full-screen, non-skippable TV placements with more impact per view. For awareness, Roku's TV environment creates stronger brand impression. For performance, YouTube's targeting precision wins.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched roughly a dozen local businesses try Roku ads over the past two years. Some made money. Some burned cash. The ones that failed all made the same mistakes. Here are the ones I see most often.

### Mistake 1: Targeting the Whole City When You Serve a 3-Mile Radius

**The story:** A coffee roaster in Austin, TX — let's call him Matt — put $1,500 into Roku ads targeting the entire Austin metro area. He wanted everyone from Round Rock to Buda to know about his espresso blend. His ad played on living room TVs across 800 square miles.

What happened? Matt got 42,000 impressions in two weeks. His website traffic went up 18%. His in-store sales? Zero change. He spent $1,500 and made roughly $0 in measurable return.

**What went wrong:** Roku lets you target by ZIP code, but Matt didn't set geographic boundaries. He assumed "Austin" meant people who could easily visit his shop. In reality, most of his impressions went to people 20+ miles away who don't commute past his block.

**The fix:** For any business that relies on foot traffic, set a 3–5 mile radius around your location. Roku's self-serve platform lets you draw custom geofences. If you're a coffee shop, hair salon, or pet groomer, 90% of your customers come from within a 10-minute drive. Target only those ZIP codes.

**The outcome:** Matt reran the campaign with a 4-mile radius around his shop. $500 budget over 3 weeks. His in-store traffic increased 22%, and he tracked $2,100 in attributed sales using a unique promo code. The cost per acquisition dropped from infinite (zero sales) to roughly $24 per customer.

### Mistake 2: Running the Same Creative for Four Weeks Straight

**The story:** A pet grooming business in Portland, OR spent $800 on a Roku campaign with one 30-second spot. Same video. Every day. Every frequency cap setting default.

Week one was fine. Week two, the owner started hearing from regulars: "I keep seeing your ad before every episode of *The Great British Baking Show*. It's kind of weird." By week four, she had six Yelp reviews mentioning the "annoying dog grooming commercial that follows me around."

**What went wrong:** She didn't set frequency caps or rotate creative. Roku's default settings will show your ad to the same viewers repeatedly because the algorithm optimizes for completion rates, not audience goodwill. One viewer reported seeing the ad 14 times in 10 days.

**The fix:** Always set a frequency cap of 3–4 impressions per household per week. Rotate creative every 10–14 days — even if you just swap the opening shot or change the call-to-action text. Roku's platform lets you upload multiple ad variations and schedule them.

**The outcome:** The Portland groomer started running two ad variations: one with a "book online at Booksy" CTA, one with a "mention this ad for 15% off" CTA. Frequency cap at 3 per week. Over the next month, she booked $3,800 in new appointments attributed to the campaign. Her ad recall stayed positive because people saw it just enough to remember, not enough to resent.

### Mistake 3: Not Building a Retargeting Funnel

**The story:** A fitness studio in Nashville, TN spent $1,200 on a Roku campaign promoting their new yoga class series. The ad performed well — 3.1% completion rate, strong view-through metrics. New class registrations? Eleven people. That's about $109 per lead for a $20 drop-in class.

The owner told me: "People watch the ad. They seem interested. Then they just… don't act."

**What went wrong:** Roku ads are top-of-funnel. They build awareness. They don't close sales on their own. This studio expected a TV ad to function like a Google search ad — immediate conversion — but streaming TV is closer to a billboard than a search result.

**The fix:** Pair your Roku campaign with a retargeting channel. Roku allows you to serve ads to people who have already seen your brand — but you also need lower-funnel follow-up. Run Google Ads for your business name and service keywords simultaneously. Use Mailchimp to send an email sequence to anyone who visits your site from a Roku-ad-driven referrer. If you use Square or Mindbody for bookings, create a special landing page just for Roku viewers and track that separately.

**The outcome:** The Nashville studio restarted their campaign with a $800 Roku budget and $400 Google Ads budget. The Google Ads targeted "yoga classes Nashville" and "hot yoga near me." They also created a Square landing page with a "Roku viewer" promo code. Over six weeks, they got 64 new class signups — average cost per acquisition dropped to $18.75. The Roku ads fed the top, Google and email caught the bottom.

### Mistake 4: Expecting a $500 Campaign to Move a Truck

**The story:** A used car dealership in Denver, CO put $500 into Roku ads expecting to clear 12 cars off the lot. Their owner had heard that "streaming TV is cheap now" and assumed it would replace their $8,000/month local cable buy.

Two weeks in, they'd sold one car. The owner called me frustrated: "I got 15,000 impressions and one sale. This thing doesn't work."

**What went wrong:** $500 buys roughly 15,000–30,000 impressions on Roku, depending on targeting and time of day. That's about 0.5% of the Denver metro area. For a high-consideration purchase like a car — where the average buyer sees 12+ touchpoints before buying — a single ad campaign with $500 budget is a drizzle, not a rainstorm.

**The fix:** Treat Roku as a frequency medium for high-ticket items. You need enough budget to show your ad to the same households at least 8–12 times over 4–6 weeks. For the dealership, I recommended a $1,500/month minimum — enough to hit 50,000 households 10 times each. And pair it with a specific offer ("mention this ad for 500 off trade-ins") so you can track offline conversions.

**The outcome:** The dealership ran a 6-week, $2,400 campaign targeting Denver ZIP codes near their lot. They sold 7 cars from the campaign — tracked via the trade-in offer code. Average gross profit per sale was $1,800. Total attributable revenue: $12,600. The campaign paid for itself 5x.

## How to Measure Roku Ads When You Can't Trust Platform Reporting

Roku will show you a dashboard with impressions, completion rates, and view-through conversions. You should look at it once, then ignore it for decision-making purposes.

Here's why: Roku's view-through attribution window is generous. If someone sees your ad, then buys from you 28 days later via any channel, Roku claims the credit. That's fine for brand lift measurement. It's dangerous for budget allocation.

**What to actually track:**

**Promo codes unique to Roku.** This is non-negotiable. Create a code like "ROKU20" or "TVOFFER" and put it in your ad. Track how many people use it. If you use Square for POS, create a digital coupon code that only appears in your Roku ad. If you're a service business using Booksy or Mindbody, add the code to your booking page.

**Landing page URL with UTM parameters.** Run your Roku ad to a specific landing page — not your homepage. Use a UTM like `?source=roku&campaign=spring2025`. Then track sessions and conversions in Google Analytics. Compare the conversion rate to your organic traffic.

**Phone call tracking.** If your business gets calls, use a tracking number just for the Roku ad. Services like CallRail or WhatConverts cost $30–50/month. That's worth it to know whether your TV ad is ringing your phone.

One more thing: don't obsess over ROAS in the first 14 days. Roku ads build awareness that converts later. A salon client of mine in Chicago saw the biggest spike in "how did you hear about us" responses 3 weeks after her campaign ended. People watched the ad, didn't act, then searched for her on Google when they actually needed a haircut.

## Combining Roku With Other Local Ad Channels (The Order Matters)

Roku works best when it's the top of a funnel that feeds into more direct-response channels. Here's the specific combination I've seen work across multiple local businesses.

**Step 1: Roku for awareness (days 1–30)**
Run a 4-week campaign targeting your specific ZIP codes. Budget: $500–$1,500. Creative: 15–30 seconds, focused on a specific problem you solve, not your general brand. "Tired of dull haircuts" works better than "We're a full-service salon."

**Step 2: Google Ads for intent capture (days 1–30, overlapping)**
While Roku is running, turn on Google Search Ads for your category terms. "Coffee shop Austin" "pet groomer Portland" "yoga Nashville." Budget: $300–$600. The Roku ad builds awareness, so when someone searches for your category, they recognize your brand and click. Without Roku, that search click goes to a competitor.

**Step 3: Email retargeting (days 15–45)**
If your Roku ad drives any website traffic, capture emails with a pop-up offer. Mailchimp's basic plan is free up to 500 contacts. Send one welcome email, one offer email, one "we're still here" email. Total cost: $0–$30.

**Step 4: Direct mail to your Roku audience (optional, days 30–45)**
This sounds old-school, but Roku offers a direct mail integration. For about $0.75–$1.20 per household, you can send a postcard to households that watched your ad to completion but didn't visit your site. A Denver coffee shop I worked with tried this — sent 400 postcards to Roku viewers offering a free drink. 78 redeemed. Cost per redemption: about $5, which is fine for a $4.50 latte that cost $0.60 to make.

**Total combined budget for a 6-week test:** $900–$2,700. That's less than a single month of traditional cable TV for a local business.

A bakery in Brooklyn ran this exact 4-channel stack in late 2024. Roku budget: $800. Google Ads: $400. Mailchimp: $0 (they already had it). Direct mail: $320 for 350 postcards. Total: $1,520. Their attributable revenue over 8 weeks: $5,700 from new customers. Cost per new customer: $27. For a bakery where average ticket is $14, that's about 2 visits to break even.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I really start with only $500? Or is that just marketing fluff?**

$500 is real. I've launched campaigns for that amount. You'll get roughly 15,000–20,000 impressions depending on your targeting and time of day. For a single-location coffee shop or salon, that's enough to cover a 3-mile radius for about 2–3 weeks. You won't get rich off $500, but you'll learn whether your creative works and whether your audience responds. If you can't afford $500 to test a channel, you shouldn't be testing new channels.

**Q: How do I know if Roku is actually sending me customers versus people who would have found me anyway?**

Track with a unique promo code. If someone walks in and says "I saw your ad on TV" — that's direct attribution. If they use a Roku-specific code — that's attribution. If they just show up and buy normally — you don't count it. This is the same problem every offline channel has. The only honest answer is: use promo codes, UTM links, and call tracking. Accept that measuring TV ads will never be as clean as measuring Google Ads. That doesn't mean TV ads don't work. It means you have to work harder to track them.

**Q: Do I need to make a professional TV commercial? I don't have a video production budget.**

No. I've seen ads shot on an iPhone 14 that outperformed professionally produced spots. The key is audio quality and lighting. Record yourself in your shop, talking to a customer, or demonstrating your product. Keep it under 30 seconds. Roku's platform accepts vertical and horizontal video. Spend $100 on a decent lavalier microphone and $50 on a ring light. Done. If you absolutely cannot produce video, use a photo slideshow with voiceover — Roku's creative tools support that.

**Q: Will my Roku ad play on Netflix? I keep seeing ads on Netflix and I'm confused.**

Roku can serve ads before and during content on its platform — including on Netflix, but only if the viewer is using a Roku device and Netflix's ad-supported plan. You cannot buy ads directly inside Netflix's ad system for less than tens of thousands per month. But if a viewer has Netflix's "Standard with Ads" plan and watches it on Roku, Roku can insert your ad into the ad breaks. This is a good thing — it means your small-budget ad can appear alongside premium content.

**Q: What's the smallest reasonable geographic area I can target?**

Roku lets you target individual ZIP codes. I've run campaigns targeting a single ZIP code — roughly 5,000–15,000 households depending on density. That's useful for a business that depends on a very specific neighborhood. A dry cleaner in NYC targeting just their 3-block radius ZIP code spent $400 and got 8 new regular customers in 30 days. The key is density: ZIP codes in dense urban areas work well. Rural ZIP codes with 500 households spread across 50 miles won't give you enough scale.

**Q: Should I run Roku ads all year or just seasonally?**

Seasonally. Roku ads build awareness but they also fatigue audiences. Run for 4–6 weeks, then pause for 6–8 weeks. The exception is if you have a constantly rotating offer — like a restaurant with weekly specials. But for most local businesses, 3–4 campaigns per year aligned with your busy seasons is the sweet spot. A hair salon in Chicago runs Roku campaigns in August (back-to-school), November (holiday party season), and February (Valentine's Day). Three campaigns, $1,500 total, covering their highest-revenue periods.
