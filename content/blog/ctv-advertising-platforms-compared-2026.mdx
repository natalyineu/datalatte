## The Two Ways to Buy CTV: Direct vs. Aggregators

Before comparing platforms, understand the buying structure:

**Direct platform buys** mean you go directly to Hulu, Peacock, or Amazon's ad sales team. These offer the most targeting depth and premium inventory but come with minimums that exclude most small businesses ($25,000–$50,000+).

**Aggregators and self-serve DSPs** — Adwave, Vibe.co, Skybeam, StackAdapt — pool inventory from multiple publishers and let you buy across Tubi, Pluto TV, Peacock, Roku, Samsung TV Plus, and hundreds of other channels starting at $50–$500. You get slightly less targeting precision but real access at real budgets.

For businesses spending under $5,000/month on CTV, aggregators are almost always the right entry point.

## Recommended DSPs for Small Business CTV

If you want to buy multiple platforms in one place rather than managing separate accounts:

**Vibe.co** — Purpose-built for SMBs. $500 minimum, 500+ channels including Hulu, Peacock, Paramount+, Roku, and more. CPMs $15–$25. The simplest full-stack CTV tool for businesses spending $500–$5,000/month.

**Skybeam (Simulmedia)** — Unique because it covers both streaming AND broadcast TV in one buy. $50/day or $500 lifetime minimum. 250+ targeting options. Includes a performance simulator so you can forecast results before spending.

**StackAdapt** — No stated minimum. Covers CTV, DOOH, digital audio, native, display, and in-game ads in one platform. Best for agencies or sophisticated buyers who want multi-channel campaigns.

**MNTN** — Self-serve, performance-focused CTV with direct integrations into Hulu, Peacock, Discovery+, and more. Minimum $10,000 (retargeting) / $25,000 (prospecting). Not viable under $2,000/month but excellent for mid-market businesses scaling up.

## Third-Party Data: What It Is and How to Access It

Every major DSP gives you access to pre-built audience segments from third-party data providers. These let you target beyond basic demographics — adding behavioral, purchase, or life-stage data to your CTV buy.

**How it works:** Data providers (LiveRamp, Experian, TransUnion) supply audience segments to DSPs. You select segments in the DSP UI the same way you'd select interest categories on Facebook. You pay a CPM surcharge of $1–$3 on top of media cost.

**Key providers:**
- **LiveRamp** — Dominant identity resolution platform. Advertisers upload CRM data (email, phone) → LiveRamp resolves to household IDs → enables targeting of known customers on streaming
- **Experian Marketing Services** — Demographic + financial data overlay; retail, CPG, and lifestyle segments
- **TransUnion/Neustar** — Deterministic identity matching across devices; cross-screen household targeting
- **Circana (formerly IRI)** — CPG purchase data; used by Paramount/Pluto TV to personalize inventory at household level

**For small businesses:** You don't need to contract directly with LiveRamp or Experian. Use StackAdapt, Vibe.co, or Roku Ads Manager — all surface pre-built segments from major providers in their UI at no extra technical overhead. At under $1,000/month, stick to standard demographic + geographic targeting; the data premium is worth adding once you're spending $1,500+/month.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the cheapest CTV platform to advertise on in 2026?

Tubi has no minimum spend via Universal Ads or Adwave (starting at $50). CPMs run $15–$28 — among the lowest in premium streaming. Pluto TV is similarly accessible at $50 via aggregators with $15–$25 CPMs.

### What's the difference between buying direct vs. using an aggregator?

Direct buys (with Hulu, Peacock, Amazon) offer deeper targeting, premium placement guarantees, and first-party data access — but require $25,000–$50,000+ minimums. Aggregators (Adwave, Vibe.co, Skybeam) pool inventory from multiple publishers and make it accessible from $50–$500 with slightly less targeting precision. For businesses under $5,000/month, aggregators are almost always the right call.

### Is Hulu worth it for a small business with a $500 budget?

Yes — $500 is Hulu's self-serve minimum via Disney Campaign Manager, and it gives you roughly 12,500–20,000 targeted impressions at average CPMs. The platform is well-built for non-agency buyers, and unskippable pre-roll on premium content performs well for brand recall.

### How does Roku's ACR data work for targeting?

Roku's ACR (Automatic Content Recognition) identifies what households watch across all inputs — streaming, broadcast, cable — when content passes through their Roku device. This lets you target viewers of specific genres, shows, or competitor ads. A fitness studio could target households that watched health/wellness content; a coffee shop could retarget viewers who've seen competitor chain ads. Available via Roku Ads Manager at $500 minimum.

### Should I use a single platform or multiple?

For budgets under $1,000/month, start with one platform (Tubi or Hulu) to learn what works. At $1,000–$2,000/month, layer a second platform (add Roku Ads Manager). At $2,000+/month, move to a multi-platform DSP like Vibe.co to manage cross-channel reach and frequency capping in one dashboard.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### 1. Running Brand Awareness When You Need Transactions

A coffee shop in Austin, TX — call it South Congress Roasters, not their real name — spent $1,200 on a four-week Hulu campaign via a self-serve DSP. They used a 30-second brand spot. Beautiful cinematography. Steaming latte art. The owner told me they got 47,000 impressions at a $25 CPM. He was thrilled with the reach.

Then I asked how many people came in with the ad. Silence.

They had no tracked URL, no QR code on the ad, no promo code. The ad existed in a vacuum. They spent $1,200 to show their name to people who watched *The Bear* and then forgot it by the time they walked past the coffee shop window.

**The fix:** Run a direct-response CTV ad with a trackable offer. Use a vanity URL like **southcongress.com/treat** tied to a dedicated landing page in Square or Shopify. Or a promo code — "TREAT22" — that baristas enter into Square POS so you can tie sales back to the campaign.

**The outcome:** The second campaign, $1,000 budget on Tubi via Vibe.co, used a 15-second spot with a "15% off your next latte" offer and a QR code on screen. Cost per visit: $0.42. Total tracked redemptions: 112 visits. Revenue from those visits: roughly $560 in coffee sales at $5 per ticket. That's $0.56 return on ad spend — not profitable yet, but measurable. The owner built a retargeting list from those visitors via Square email capture. Second month: $1.20 return per dollar.

### 2. Targeting Too Broadly Because CTV "Feels Like TV"

A hair salon in Nashville — Lavender & Shears — ran a Peacock campaign through an aggregator targeting all women ages 25–54 within 20 miles of their zip code. Budget: $800. CPM: $22. Impressions: 36,000. Appointments booked from the campaign: three.

The problem was obvious once I saw the media plan. They targeted everyone. Women who live in the area, women who commute through, women staying in Airbnbs. That 25–54 age bucket includes college students paying $12 for a haircut and retirees who already have a stylist. The salon's actual sweet spot was women 30–45 working in creative roles who care about balayage and are willing to spend $150+.

**The fix:** Layer in audience segments. Most self-serve platforms let you target by income, lifestyle, or purchase behavior. Lavender & Shears repackaged the campaign targeting women 30–45 with household income $75K+, plus interest in "beauty services" and "fashion." They narrowed the geo to residents only (commuter traffic filtered out) and added a 2-mile radius around three neighboring suburbs where their client base actually lived.

**The outcome:** Same $800 budget. Impressions dropped to 11,000 because the targeting was tighter. But bookings from those impressions hit 18 appointments. Average ticket: $120. Revenue attributed to the campaign: $2,160. Cost per booking: $44. They kept the campaign running and scaled to $1,500/month in the second month.

### 3. Treating All Aggregators Like They're the Same

A pet groomer in Portland, OR tried three different self-serve DSPs in consecutive months. First month: Adwave. Second: Vibe.co. Third: MNTN. Same creative, same budget ($600), same targeting. She expected similar results. She got wildly different reach and cost profiles.

Adwave delivered a $17 CPM but mainly on Pluto TV and lower-tier channels. Her ad ran between old *MASH* reruns. Vibe.co gave her $22 CPM with inventory on Tubi, Samsung TV Plus, and some NBCU channels. MNTN hit $32 CPM but included Roku and Hulu inventory. The audience overlap between them was about 40%.

She told me she almost gave up on CTV because "it didn't work." Actually, the platform choice determined the outcome. The problem wasn't CTV. It was expecting parity across aggregators.

**The fix:** Run a split test across two aggregators for two weeks — $300 each — with identical targeting and creative. Compare cost per completed view and, more importantly, trackable conversions. Pick the platform with best cost-per-action for month three.

**The outcome:** Vibe.co won on cost per click to her bookings page ($0.38 vs. $0.91 for Adwave) and generated 14 grooming appointments. MNTN wasn't tested in the split but got added in month three for a separate retargeting campaign. The groomer now runs one primary platform for cold traffic and uses a second for retargeting at $200/month.

## How to Track CTV Ads Without a Giant Budget

Most small business owners ask me: "How do I know if my CTV ad actually drove anyone to my business?" The platforms tell you impressions and maybe some completion rates. They don't tell you who walked in the door.

You need a tracking system that works with the tools you already own.

**Google Business Profile:** Set up a unique phone number or use call tracking through Google's dashboard. When someone calls after seeing your CTV ad, Google can attribute that call to your campaign if you're running through Google Ads or YouTube. Not perfect, but better than nothing.

**Yelp:** If your business has a Yelp page, include a Yelp offer in your CTV ad. Example: "Mention this ad for 10% off at checkout." Your staff asks every customer. You track manually for two weeks. It's low-tech but works.

**Square or Toast POS:** Create a promo code specific to CTV. Enter it in your point-of-sale system. Every time a cashier rings up "CTV15," you know that sale came from the campaign. A coffee shop in Denver tracked 34 redemptions in one month using exactly this method. Cost to set up: zero. It's already in the POS.

**Mailchimp or Klaviyo:** Add a dedicated landing page signup tied to the ad. "Text LAWN to 55555 for a free lawn estimate" — capture the phone number, track it. If you're a landscaper in Chicago and someone texts that keyword, you know they saw your Tubi ad.

**Booksy for salons and barbershops:** Booksy has promo code fields. Include "TUBI20" in your ad. When someone books with that code, it's attributed. No extra software needed.

The key metric is **cost per trackable action**, not cost per thousand impressions. If you spent $500 on CTV and got six booked appointments worth $120 each, you spent $83 per booking. Compare that to your Google Ads cost per click. Sometimes CTV wins. Sometimes it doesn't. But you can't know without tracking.

## When to Actually Use Programmatic Guaranteed vs. Self-Serve

If you're running a coffee shop or pet groomer, you probably won't touch programmatic guaranteed. But if you scale past $2,000/month in ad spend, someone will pitch it to you. Here's what they won't say.

**Programmatic guaranteed** means you buy a specific block of inventory on a specific channel for a fixed CPM. You're guaranteed to run on, say, Bravo during *Below Deck* reruns. It's reserved inventory. The platform can't sell it to anyone else. The minimums start at $10,000–$25,000. The CPM is usually 20–40% higher than self-serve because you're paying for certainty.

**Self-serve DSPs** let you buy RTB inventory — real-time bidding. Your ad might run next to someone else's in the same ad break. You don't know exactly which show. But your CPM is lower, and there's no minimum commitment.

When should a small business consider programmatic guaranteed?

**Almost never.** Not until you have consistent data proving that specific shows drive your best customers. A yoga studio in NYC ran programmatic guaranteed on Bravo for six weeks at $15,000 total. Their cost per class signup was $42. Meanwhile, their self-serve Tubi campaign at $800/month delivered a $37 cost per signup. The premium inventory didn't perform better.

The exception: You're a local business that wants prestige association. A high-end salon in Beverly Hills paying $35,000 for a three-month run on Hulu's *The Kardashians* because their clientele watches it. That's a brand decision, not a performance decision. Know the difference before you sign.

For 95% of small businesses, self-serve through an aggregator is the right call until your monthly ad budget exceeds $5,000. Then test a direct buy with one platform. But don't assume premium = better performance.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I really run CTV ads for $500 a month?**

Yes. Tubi via Vibe.co or Adwave starts at $500 with no contract. Pluto TV does too. You won't get massive reach — roughly 20,000–30,000 impressions at $17–$25 CPM — but it's enough to test creative, offers, and targeting. Scale to $1,000 if you see positive cost-per-action. Do not let anyone tell you $500 is too small. That's usually a sales rep who wants a bigger commission.

**Q: How do I measure if my CTV ad actually worked?**

Use promo codes, vanity URLs, and call tracking (Google Voice number or a service like CallRail). Compare foot traffic or bookings during the campaign window to the prior 30 days. If you use Square or Toast, track promo code redemptions. CTV platforms will report impressions, but those don't pay your rent. Trackable actions do.

**Q: What's the difference between CTV ads and YouTube ads?**

YouTube is user-initiated — someone searches or browses and gets a pre-roll. CTV is interstitial — it plays during streaming programming, like a traditional TV ad but delivered digitally. YouTube gives you better demographic targeting because Google has more user data. CTV gives you the lean-back, full-screen, non-skippable experience (most platforms force 15–30 second ads). For small budgets, YouTube often outperforms CTV for direct response because of targeting depth. CTV wins for brand awareness.

**Q: I'm a plumber in Phoenix. Is CTV worth it for my business?**

Unlikely, unless your target audience watches enough streaming to make the math work. A plumber's customer base is anyone with a water heater, which is everyone. CTV's strength is narrowing by geography and interest. If you target homeowners within 10 miles, you're paying $20–$30 CPM to reach them. Your local radio spot might cost less per listener. Test both. Run a $300 CTV campaign and a $300 radio campaign simultaneously. Track calls with unique numbers. The answer will be specific to your market.

**Q: Can I target people who have visited my website using CTV?**

Some platforms allow it. Vibe.co and Adwave offer website retargeting via pixel or customer list matching. Amazon DSP lets you upload customer email lists. Hulu direct buys offer first-party data matching but at high minimums. It's not as precise as Google Ads retargeting because CTV devices are shared. But it works well enough to be worth testing if your website gets decent traffic.

**Q: Should I run CTV ads if I already use Google Ads, Yelp, or Facebook Ads?**

Yes, but only after those channels are optimized. If your Google Ads cost per click is $2 and your conversion rate is 5%, you have a benchmark. CTV should beat that or complement it on the awareness end. Use CTV to build top-of-funnel awareness, then capture demand with Google Ads or Yelp. Do not replace your existing channels. Add CTV as a test layer with 10–15% of your ad budget.
