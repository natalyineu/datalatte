### 2. How to Set Up Your Google Ads Campaign (Step-by-Step)

#### Step 1: Choose the Right Campaign Type
For yoga studios, **Search and Performance Max** campaigns work best. Search campaigns drive immediate traffic, while Performance Max uses AI to optimize across Google’s entire ad network.

#### Step 2: Define Your Audience
Use **Geo-Targeting** to focus on a 10–15 mile radius around your studio. Pair this with **Demographic Targeting** to reach users aged 25–54 (the largest yoga demographic) and exclude competitors’ websites.

#### Step 3: Build Keyword Groups
Start with 8–12 high-intent keywords per ad group. For example:
- "beginner yoga classes [City]"
- "yoga for seniors [City]"
- "yoga near me [Zip Code]"

**Pro Tip:** Use Google’s Keyword Planner to find "exact match" keywords with low competition but high search volume. In 2026, the average CPC for "yoga near me" is $3.15, but CPC drops by 30% for long-tail terms like "yoga for back pain [City]."

### 5. Track Results Like a Pro (Tools & Metrics)

#### Key Metrics to Monitor
- **Cost-per-lead (CPL):** Aim for $10–$15/qualified lead.
- **Click-through rate (CTR):** 2.5%+ is good for yoga ads.
- **Conversion rate:** 5–8% is typical for local services.

#### Tools to Use
- **Google Analytics 4:** Track website behavior (e.g., how many users watch your "yoga for beginners" video).

- **Google Ads Performance Report:** Focus on "Top Conversion Events" like form fills and phone calls.

**Example:** A Miami studio used UTM tracking to discover that 60% of conversions came from mobile users searching "yoga near me" at 6 PM on weekdays. They adjusted ad scheduling to focus on 5–7 PM, cutting CPL by 20%.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Bidding on Generic Keywords Like “Yoga Classes”

A studio owner in Nashville, Tennessee, came to me after burning $1,200 in two months on Google Ads with exactly one new student to show for it. She had set her campaign to target “yoga classes” and “yoga near me” — broad match, no negatives. She was competing against every yoga studio, gym, and national chain within 50 miles. Her average CPC was $4.10, and most clicks came from people who were just browsing.

**What went wrong:** Broad match keywords without modifiers or location targeting act like a firehose aimed at the entire internet. You pay for clicks from people who have zero intention of signing up — students looking for free YouTube videos, tourists checking schedules, or competitors spying on ad copy.

**The fix:** Switch to phrase match and exact match, and layer in geo-targeting with a 5-mile radius around the studio. Use long-tail keywords like “hot yoga Nashville downtown” or “beginner yoga class near me East Nashville.” Add negative keywords like “free,” “online,” “YouTube,” “app,” and “Pilates.”

**Outcome:** After restructuring, her cost-per-lead dropped from $18 to $7, and she got 12 new student sign-ups in the next month on a $500 budget. That’s a 40% increase in leads with 60% less spend.

---

### Mistake #2: Forgetting to Exclude Competitor Brand Terms

A yoga studio in Portland, Oregon, was running Google Ads for six months and couldn’t figure out why their CTR was high but conversion rate was abysmal. I looked at their search terms report and found they were showing up for searches like “CorePower Yoga Portland” and “YogaWorks schedule.” Those searchers wanted a specific brand — they would never convert.

**What went wrong:** Google’s broad match often triggers on competitor brand names even if you didn’t bid on them. If you don’t add competitor brand terms as negative keywords, you’re paying for clicks from people who are already loyal to someone else. One click costs you $3–4 and leads nowhere.

**The fix:** Run the search terms report weekly for the first month. Identify every competitor, teacher name, or studio name that shows up. Add them as negative keywords at the campaign level. Also add misspellings — “yogaworks” without capital letters, “core power” as two words.

**Outcome:** The Portland studio saved an estimated $400 per month in wasted clicks. Their conversion rate jumped from 1.2% to 3.8% because every click was now from someone actually looking for *their* studio — or at least open to options.

### Mistake #3: Writing Mobile-Unfriendly Ad Copy

A Chicago studio spent $900 in July and got 23 clicks — and zero calls, zero form fills, zero anything. Their ad said: “Visit our website for class schedules and pricing.” That was it. No phone number, no address, no call-to-action button. 85% of yoga searches happen on mobile — and on a phone screen, a generic link to a website is a dead end.

**What went wrong:** Mobile users want immediate action — call to book a spot, get directions, or see a schedule without scrolling. If your ad doesn’t include call extensions, location extensions, or a clear mobile-specific CTA like “Book Your Free Trial” with a tap-to-call button, you’re losing 60% of potential leads.

**The fix:** Add call extensions (your studio phone number), location extensions (Google Maps pin), and a sitelink extension that goes directly to a booking page (e.g., via Square Appointments or Booksy). Write ad copy for mobile: short headlines under 30 characters, use “Call Now” or “Tap to Book,” and include urgency — “Only 5 spots left for today’s 6 PM class.”

**Outcome:** After adding call and location extensions, the Chicago studio saw a 60% increase in phone call leads. Their cost-per-lead went from $39 to $14. Their ad CTR increased from 2.1% to 4.8%.

### Mistake #4: Running One Ad for Every Class Type

A studio in Denver had a single responsive search ad that said “Join our yoga classes — all levels welcome.” They were offering hot yoga, restorative yoga, prenatal yoga, and Vinyasa flow, but the ad lumped everything together. Their CTR was below 1%, and they were spending $1,500 a month to get 10 sign-ups.

**What went wrong:** Different search intents need different ad copy. Someone searching “prenatal yoga Denver” wants safety, comfort, and a teacher trained in prenatal care. Someone searching “hot yoga Denver” wants sweat, intensity, and a schedule. If your ad is generic, neither group feels you understand them. Quality Score drops, CPC rises, and conversions suffer.

**The fix:** Create separate ad groups for each class type. For “prenatal yoga,” use headlines like “Prenatal Yoga Classes — Gentle & Safe” with a landing page that talks about instructor certifications and class timing. For “hot yoga,” use “Hot Yoga Denver — 105°F, All Levels Welcome” with a page that lists class intensity and temperature. Use ad customizers to insert the specific class name dynamically if possible.

**Outcome:** The Denver studio restructured into 5 ad groups with dedicated landing pages. Their overall CTR went from 0.9% to 2.8%. Cost per sign-up dropped from $150 to $65. Within two months, they were spending the same $1,500 but getting 23 new students instead of 10.

## Budget Strategies That Don’t Waste Money

When I ask yoga studio owners what they’re spending on Google Ads, I get answers ranging from “$200 a month” to “I don’t know, I just put a credit card in.” If you’re running ads without a clear budget strategy, you’re essentially flushing cash down the toilet. Here’s what actually works.

**Start with a $500–$1,000 monthly test budget.** I know that sounds like a lot for a small studio, but $300 or less won’t generate enough data to optimize. At $500, you get roughly 150–200 clicks per month (at $2.50–$3.00 average CPC). That’s enough to see which keywords convert and which don’t within 3–4 weeks. Run the test for at least 30 days before making any major changes.

**Use dayparting.** Most yoga classes happen in the morning (6–8 AM) and evening (5–7 PM). Don’t waste budget showing ads at 2 PM on a Tuesday. Set your ad schedule to run from 5 AM to 9 AM and 4 PM to 9 PM, seven days a week. You can adjust based on your actual class times. A studio in Austin did this and immediately cut wasted spend by 35% — they were paying for clicks at midnight that never booked.

**Cap your daily budget.** Set a daily limit of $20–$30. Google’s algorithm will spend up to 2x your daily budget on high-traffic days, but it evens out over the month. If you set a $500 monthly budget with a $16 daily cap, you’ll stay on track. Don’t let Google automatically increase your budget — that’s a fast track to an $800 invoice you didn’t expect.

**Allocate 70% to branded and non-branded high-intent keywords, 30% to retargeting.** Most studio owners put all their money into new student acquisition. But retargeting people who visited your site but didn’t book is where the money really lives. A studio in Denver spent $200/month on retargeting ads (using Google Ads audience lists) and got 8 additional sign-ups from people who had visited their schedule page twice and left.

**Use conversion tracking before you spend a dime.** Install the Google Ads conversion tracking tag on your booking confirmation page (Square, Booksy, Mindbody all support this). Without it, you’re flying blind. A studio in Portland realized they had been paying for 300 clicks with zero conversions — but actually 12 people had booked through a different path. Once they installed proper tracking, they saw their true CPA was $35, not infinity.

## Ad Copy and Extensions That Actually Get Clicks

Nobody reads a yoga ad and thinks, “That sounds nice.” They either see a reason to book or they scroll past. Your ad copy has about 2 seconds to grab someone who’s standing in line for coffee, half-watching a TikTok, or sitting in traffic. Here’s the copy structure I use with every yoga studio client.

**Headline 1:** Lead with a benefit and a location. “Hot Yoga Austin TX — Try Your First Class Free” beats “Yoga Classes in Austin” every time. The free trial removes risk; the location confirms relevance.

**Headline 2:** Add a differentiator. “Beginner-Friendly, 105°F, 60-Min Sessions” or “Award-Winning Teachers, Small Classes.” Don’t say “best yoga studio” — nobody believes that. Give a specific, verifiable claim.

**Headline 3:** Use a secondary CTA. “Book Online Now” or “Call for Same-Day Spot.” If someone’s ready to commit, let them take action immediately.

**Description lines:** Two 90-character lines. Lead with a pain point or a timeline. Example: “Tired of crowded yoga classes? We cap at 12 students. Book your first class for $15 today.” Second line: “Located at 123 Main Street, 2 blocks from the Pearl District. Call (555) 123-4567.”

**Extensions you must add:**  
- **Call extension** (studio phone — make sure it’s answered during class times, or use a virtual receptionist)  
- **Location extension** (Google Maps pin — this alone can boost CTR by 15%)  
- **Sitelink extensions** (link to your class schedule page, your “About Our Teachers” page, and your special offers page)  
- **Callout extensions** (“Free Mat Rental,” “New Student Special,” “5 AM Class Available”)  
- **Structured snippet extensions** (header: “Class Types” — values: Hatha, Vinyasa, Hot, Restorative, Prenatal)

**A real example:** A studio in NYC changed their ad from “NYC Yoga Studio — Join Today” to “Yoga Near Times Square — Free First Class. 6 AM & 7 PM Hot Yoga. Book Now.” Their CTR went from 1.4% to 5.2% in two weeks. They added a call extension and got 15 calls per week — 8 of which booked a trial class.

**Pro tip:** Write 4–5 different headlines and descriptions in your responsive search ad. Google will test combinations. But don’t let it write headlines for you with automated suggestions — use only the ones you craft. Turn off “auto-created assets” in campaign settings. Otherwise, Google will add “Great quality” or “Buy now” and tank your Quality Score.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads per month?**  
For a single-location yoga studio, start with $500–$800 per month. That’s enough to get real data without hemorrhaging cash. If you’re in a high-cost city like San Francisco or Manhattan, expect a higher CPC (around $4–$5) and budget $1,000–$1,200. Run for 60 days, then adjust based on cost per sign-up. If your average student lifetime value is $200 (6 months of classes at $35/month), you can afford a CPA up to $80. But I’d aim for $30–$50 to leave room for profit.

**Q: Should I bid on my own studio name?**  
Yes, but only if you’re not already ranking #1 organically for your own brand. If a competitor is bidding on your name, you need to defend that traffic. Bidding on your brand name is cheap (CPC $0.50–$1.00) and prevents competitor ads from stealing people who already know you. If you’re ranking #1 organically for your studio name, you can skip this — but check first. I’ve seen studios lose 20% of direct traffic to competitors who bought their brand keyword.

**Q: Google Ads vs. Facebook Ads — which is better for a yoga studio?**  
Depends on what you want. Google Ads captures people who are already searching for a yoga class — high intent, lower volume. Facebook Ads lets you target interests (e.g., people who like “meditation” or “wellness”) but they’re in scroll mode, not search mode. I recommend starting with Google Ads for sign-ups, then using Facebook Ads for retargeting and event promotion (workshops, retreats). A studio in Chicago used Google Ads for 70% of their budget and Facebook for 30%, and Google delivered 3x the sign-ups per dollar spent.

**Q: How long until I see results?**  
Expect 2–4 weeks of data collection before you can make informed decisions. Google needs time to learn which keywords convert. Don’t start changing bids after three days. After 30 days, you’ll have enough data to kill underperforming keywords, raise bids on winners, and test new ad copy. A studio in Denver saw their first sign-up on day 4, but the campaign didn’t become profitable until week 6.

**Q: Do I need a website, or can I use a booking link as my landing page?**  
You need a dedicated landing page — not your homepage and not a raw booking link. A simple one-page site with your class schedule, pricing, address, a phone number, and a booking button works fine. Use Squarespace, Wix, or even a free Google Sites page. The key is relevance: if someone clicks “Hot Yoga Austin,” take them to a page about hot yoga, not your homepage with 10 unrelated tabs. A studio in Austin built a one-page site in an afternoon and saw conversion rates jump from 1% to 4%.

**Q: What if I run out of budget before the month ends?**  
That’s a good problem — it means your campaign is working. But if you’re running out because of high CPC, not high volume, you’re likely bidding too aggressively. Lower your max CPC bid by 10–15% and see if volume stays consistent. Also check your search terms report — you might be wasting money on irrelevant clicks. When a studio in Nashville ran out of budget in 20 days, I found they were bidding on “yoga pants” (a search term they didn’t exclude). After adding that negative, their budget lasted the full month with the same number of sign-ups.
