## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Everyone in a 50-Mile Radius

I worked with a CrossFit gym in Austin, Texas last year. Owner named Derek. He was spending $2,400/month on Google Ads and getting maybe two new members. He was frustrated. Ready to quit Google Ads entirely.

I looked at his account. He had location targeting set to 50 miles around his gym. Austin traffic is a nightmare. Nobody is driving 50 miles to do burpees. His ads were showing to people in Round Rock, Cedar Park, even San Marcos — towns with their own gyms, their own loyalties, their own reasons to stay home.

**What he was actually doing:** Competing with every gym in Central Texas for clicks.

**The fix:** We shrunk his radius to 5 miles. Then we added location exclusions for zip codes where we knew competitor gyms had strong retention. We also layered in "people who are in your targeted locations" vs "people who show interest" — because Google's default setting includes people who *Google* Austin but live in Dubai.

**The outcome:** His cost per lead dropped from $48 to $14 in three weeks. Monthly spend went from $2,400 to $900. He got 14 new members in month two — at $64 per acquisition instead of $1,200. Derek texted me a photo of his coffee mug that said "Worth It." That was the whole message. I understood.

### Mistake #2: Using Broad Match Keywords for a Local Business

A martial arts studio in Portland, Oregon — let's call her Jenna — was running ads on "martial arts classes." Broad match. Google, being Google, decided "karate movies," "self-defense keychain," and "Bruce Lee workout routine" were all perfectly reasonable searches to trigger her ad.

She spent $1,200 in six weeks. Zero sign-ups. Zero calls. Zero emails.

**What was happening:** Broad match gives Google permission to interpret intent. And Google's interpretation is generous to the point of being useless for a small local business. Someone searching "is karate good for kids" isn't ready to sign up. They're doing research. Broad match burns budget on window shoppers.

**The fix:** We switched to phrase match and exact match only. Keywords like:
- "karate classes Portland" (exact)
- "kids martial arts near me" (phrase)
- "adult jiu jitsu Beaverton" (exact)
We also added negative keywords: "movie," "history," "documentary," "video game," "YouTube." You'd be surprised how many people search "karate documentary" and click an ad for a real dojo.

**The outcome:** $580/month spend. 8 new sign-ups in month one. Average membership value is $120/month. That's $960 in monthly recurring revenue from $580 in ad spend. Jenna's been running this for seven months. She told me it's the only marketing channel that reliably pays for itself.

### Mistake #3: Sending All Traffic to a Generic Homepage

A yoga studio in Denver — small, five instructors, rent was killing them — was sending every Google Ads click to their homepage. The homepage had a hero image, a mission statement, a class schedule, a teacher bio section, and a "contact us" button somewhere near the bottom.

Their bounce rate was 82%. Cost per conversion was $97. They were bleeding money.

**The problem:** When someone clicks an ad for "hot yoga Denver at 6 PM," they don't want to hunt through your homepage to find the schedule. They want to see the exact class, the time, the price, and a big "Reserve Your Spot" button. The homepage is for people who already know you. The landing page is for people who clicked an ad.

**The fix:** We built a single landing page for each ad group. One for "hot yoga classes," one for "beginner yoga," one for "yoga for athletes." Each page had:
- The class name and time in the headline
- A photo of the actual instructor (not a stock photo of a model in tree pose)
- Price — upfront, no "call for pricing"
- A two-step booking form (name + email, then pick a date)
- Social proof: "Join 140+ students who started this year"

**The outcome:** Conversion rate went from 2.1% to 7.4%. Cost per new student dropped to $22. Monthly spend was $800. They filled 31 new student spots in the first month. The studio owner told me she "finally understood why people bother with Google Ads." High praise from someone who was one month away from canceling.

### Mistake #4: No Conversion Tracking (Seriously)

A cycling studio in Nashville. Owner had been running Google Ads for four months. Couldn't tell me how many members came from ads. Couldn't tell me which keywords worked. Couldn't tell me if the $1,100 he was spending each month was generating anything.

He was flying blind. Actually, worse than blind — he was assuming it worked because "people said they saw the ad."

I opened his Google Ads account. No conversion tracking set up. None. Zero. Not even a phone call tag.

**The problem:** Without conversion tracking, Google optimizes for clicks. Not sign-ups. Not calls. Not bookings. Clicks. So Google happily shows your ad to people who click but never convert, because that's what you're telling it to optimize for.

**The fix:** Set up Google Ads conversion tracking in 45 minutes. Tagged the "thank you" page after booking. Set up call tracking with a Google forwarding number. Connected it to his Square account so we could see which members used an offer code from the ad.

**The outcome:** First month with tracking revealed that 80% of his budget was going to keywords that never converted. "spinning classes" — $340 spent, zero conversions. "indoor cycling Nashville" — $220, one conversion. "cycling studio near me" — $180, seven conversions. We shifted budget to what worked. Same $1,100/month got 22 new members instead of 4. He called it "the best hour I ever spent."

## How to Structure Your Campaign for Maximum ROI

If you're spending $500 to $2,000/month on Google Ads, you don't need a complex multi-campaign structure. You need three campaigns, done right.

**Campaign 1: Branded Search.** This is people searching for your gym name. Yes, you might already show up organically. But running a brand campaign lets you control the message — "New members get 30% off first month" — and it prevents competitors from bidding on your name. Bid low, like $0.50 to $1.00. Budget $100/month. This is cheap insurance.

**Campaign 2: Non-Branded Service Search.** This is where the money goes. Keywords like "yoga classes Austin," "personal trainer Chicago," "karate for kids Dallas." Use phrase and exact match. Send to dedicated landing pages. Set daily budgets based on your target CPA.

**Campaign 3: Remarketing.** People visited your site but didn't book. This campaign shows them ads for the next 14 days. Use a discount offer — "First class free, use code FREECLASS" — or a social proof angle — "1,200 members and counting. Join them."

**A real example:** A Pilates studio in Chicago ran this three-campaign structure. Total spend: $1,500/month. After three months, they were getting 35 new members per month at $43 each. Their average membership is $180/month. That's $6,300 in monthly recurring revenue from $1,500 in ad spend.

The owner told me: "I was spending $2,000/month on Facebook ads and getting five members. I moved half that budget to Google and tripled my results." She still runs Facebook, but now it's for brand awareness, not direct response.

## Integrating Google Ads With Your Booking System

This is where most guides stop. They tell you to set up ads. They don't tell you what happens after someone clicks.

If someone clicks your ad and has to fill out a contact form, wait for an email, then call to book, you've lost them. Every extra step drops conversion by 15 to 25 percent.

**The fix:** Connect Google Ads directly to your booking system.

For fitness studios using **Mindbody** or **Zen Planner**: You can create a Google Ads conversion action that tracks when someone books a class. Then use the "visitor who booked a class" audience to build lookalike audiences. This is advanced, but it works.

For smaller operations using **Square Appointments** or **Booksy**: Create a dedicated booking link for each ad group. Use UTM parameters so you know exactly which keyword drove the booking. Then track that as a conversion.

For gyms using **Club OS** or **Pushpress**: Many of these platforms have Google Ads integrations built in. If yours does, use it. If not, manually tag your URLs and review the data weekly.

**Real example from a boxing gym in NYC:** They used Google Ads with a direct link to their Booksy booking page. The ad said "Book Your First Class — $20," and the link went straight to a Booksy page with that class selected. Their conversion rate was 11.2%. A similar campaign that sent people to a generic contact form had a 3.8% conversion rate. Same ad copy, same keywords, different destination. That's the difference.

## Seasonal Campaigns and Event Promotion

Sports clubs have natural peaks and valleys. January — New Year's resolution crowd. May — people want to get in shape for summer. September — back-to-school routine for parents.

Google Ads can target these windows aggressively, then scale back in slower months.

**For membership growth:** Run campaigns from December 26 to February 15. January is the most competitive month for fitness ads. Cost per click goes up 30 to 50 percent. But conversion rates also go up, because intent is high. The key is to have a compelling offer — "Join in January, get February free" — and a clear landing page.

**For event attendance:** Let's say you host a "New Member Open House" or a "Bring a Friend Workout." Run ads for three weeks before the event. Use a keyword like "free workout [city]" or "trial gym class [city]." Set the ad schedule to morning and evening, when people are planning their day.

**A specific example:** A climbing gym in Austin ran a "Summer Climbing League" event. They spent $600 on Google Ads over three weeks. Keywords: "climbing competition Austin," "bouldering league," "rock climbing club." They got 87 registrations. Each registration paid $45. That's $3,915 in direct revenue from $600 in ad spend. They also got 34 new monthly members from people who showed up for the event. Those members will pay $85/month for an average of six months. That's an additional $17,340 in revenue, attributable to a $600 campaign.

The owner said: "I thought events were just for community building. Now I treat them as acquisition channels."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is Google Ads too expensive for a small sports club on a tight budget?**

It depends on your city and your offer. In Austin, a $500/month budget can work for a yoga studio. In Manhattan, $500 gets you maybe 10 clicks. The math changes. But here's the rule: if you can't make $500/month work, don't start with $5,000. Start small, test for two weeks, and scale what works. I've seen a $400/month campaign in Portland generate 12 new members. That's $33 per acquisition. Can your membership fee cover that? If yes, it's not too expensive.

**Q: How long does it take to start seeing results?**

You'll see clicks and impressions in hours. You'll see conversions in days to weeks. The first two weeks are data collection — your account has no conversion history, so Google is still learning. Don't judge performance in week one. By week three, you should have enough data to know if the campaign works. If you're getting clicks but no conversions, your landing page or offer is the problem, not the ads.

**Q: Can I run Google Ads myself, or do I need an agency?**

You can absolutely run them yourself if you have the time to learn. Google's interface is not intuitive. It's built for power users. If you spend 10 hours setting it up and checking it weekly, you'll get decent results. If you'd rather spend those 10 hours coaching, teaching, or running your business, hire someone. But vet them. Ask how many sports clubs they've managed. Ask for a specific example of how they improved a campaign. If they say "it depends on your goals," move on.

**Q: What's the biggest waste of money in Google Ads for sports clubs?**

Broad match keywords, by far. And sending traffic to a homepage. Those two mistakes account for probably 70% of wasted spend I see in small business accounts. The third biggest waste is running ads without conversion tracking. You can't improve what you can't measure.

**Q: Should I use Google Ads or Facebook Ads for my sports club?**

Both, but for different things. Google Ads captures demand — people actively searching for "yoga classes near me." Facebook Ads creates demand — showing an ad to someone who didn't know they wanted yoga classes until they saw your video. If you have to pick one, start with Google. The intent is higher. The conversion rates are better. Once Google is profitable, then add Facebook.

**Q: What if my gym is in a small town with low search volume?**

This is a real problem. You can't create search volume where it doesn't exist. In that case, consider expanding your radius or adding broader keywords like "fitness tips" or "home workout ideas" that let you build remarketing audiences. Then use remarketing to get those people into your gym. But honestly, if your town has 5,000 people and 12 are searching for "gym [town]" per month, Google Ads probably isn't your best channel. You're better off with local Facebook groups and referral programs.
