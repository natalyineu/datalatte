## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Sending the Same Email to Every Client

I watched a yoga studio in Austin, Texas burn through $1,200 in email marketing fees over three months with almost nothing to show for it. The owner, Sarah, had 800 subscribers in her Mindbody list. She sent one newsletter every week – same class schedule, same message, to everyone. Her open rate dropped to 14%. Her click rate was under 2%. She was paying for email credits she never used effectively.

The problem wasn't Mindbody. The problem was that Sarah treated every client the same. The beginner who'd taken two classes got the same email as the person who'd been coming five times a week for two years. The client who hadn't shown up in six months got the same offer as the one who'd just bought a 10-class pack.

**What to do instead:** Segment your list. It's not complicated. In Mindbody, you can separate clients by visit frequency, last visit date, purchase history, and class types. I had Sarah create three segments: active (visited in the last 30 days), lapsed (31–90 days), and dormant (91+ days). Each got a different email. Active clients got class reminders and referral incentives. Lapsed clients got a "we miss you" with a single free class offer. Dormant clients got a re-engagement sequence over three weeks.

The outcome after 60 days: open rate jumped to 27%. Click rate hit 8%. She rebooked 12 dormant clients, each of whom bought a new class pack. That single change brought in $1,850 in new revenue. The email cost stayed the same. She ordered a second coffee she didn't need. Neither did I.

### Mistake #2: Letting the "Automated Retargeting" Run on Default Settings

A barre studio in Portland, Oregon had Mindbody's automated retargeting turned on for six months. The owner, Jenna, thought it was working. She was paying $300 per month for Google Ads through Mindbody's integration. What she didn't realize was that Mindbody was retargeting everyone who'd ever visited the website – once. No frequency cap. No expiration. No audience refinement.

She was spending money showing ads to people who'd already visited, joined, or even cancelled. One of her own instructors saw the ad three times in one week. That's wasted spend at its purest.

When I pulled the data, 68% of her retargeting budget was hitting people who'd already converted or cancelled. Only 32% reached potential new clients. She'd spent $1,800 over six months with no way to measure the actual return.

**What to do instead:** Turn off Mindbody's default retargeting and build your own audience in Google Ads. Create a list of website visitors who didn't convert within 14 days. Exclude anyone who's completed a booking or visited the studio. Set a frequency cap of three impressions per week. Then create a separate retargeting list for clients who haven't booked in 60 days – they get a different ad with a "come back" offer.

Jenna implemented this. She spent the same $300 per month but rebuilt the audiences. After eight weeks, her cost per new client dropped from $47 to $22. She booked 18 new clients in month two from retargeting alone. That's worth roughly $3,600 in new revenue if each buys a single class pack. No regrets.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring the "Post-Visit Automation" Entirely

A boxing gym in Nashville had Mindbody for 18 months. They used it for scheduling, payments, and email blasts. They never turned on the post-visit automation. No thank-you email after a first class. No follow-up asking for a review. No reminder to book the next session.

The owner, Mike, told me his retention rate hovered around 40% for new clients. He thought that was normal. It's not. I've seen studios with proper post-visit sequences retain 65–70% of new clients within the first 90 days.

**What to do instead:** Set up a five-touch automation within Mindbody. Day 1: thank-you email with a link to leave a Google review and a "book your next class" button. Day 3: educational content – a short video on proper form for the class they took. Day 7: a personalized offer – "You took your first boxing class. Here's 20% off a 5-pack." Day 14: a check-in email with a testimonial from another client who started from zero. Day 30: a "where are you now?" email with a free private session offer for anyone who hasn't rebooked.

Mike implemented this in an afternoon. In the first 90 days, his new client retention went from 40% to 62%. That meant 22 more clients stayed active instead of dropping off. At an average spend of $120 per month per client, that's $2,640 per month in retained revenue. He didn't pay a dime more for software.

## How to Pair Mindbody with Google Ads Without Throwing Money Away

Most guides tell you to "leverage Google Ads" and then walk away. I'm not doing that. Here's exactly what works for a fitness studio in 2025.

Set up a Google Ads campaign focused on local search terms: "yoga studio [your city]," "spin classes near me," "hiit training [neighborhood]." Budget $400 per month. That's enough to get data without bleeding out.

The trick is connecting it to Mindbody. You don't need a third-party integration. Export your Mindbody class schedule into a Google Merchant Center feed. That creates a free listing in Google Shopping showing your class times and prices. I did this for a studio in Denver that was spending $600 per month on generic ads with no schedule attached. Their click-through rate doubled in two weeks.

Next, use Mindbody's built-in lead tracking. Assign a unique phone number and UTM parameters to your ads. Most studio owners skip this because it takes 10 minutes. That 10 minutes is the difference between knowing exactly which ad drove a booking and guessing based on "more people showed up."

One correction: set your bids between $2–5 per click. I've seen studios overbid at $12 per click because they didn't check the competition. In a mid-sized city like Nashville, $3.50 per click got solid traffic. In a high-cost market like NYC, $6 per click still works.

The real win is retargeting people who clicked your ad but didn't book. I mentioned the barre studio earlier. Here's the math: a studio in Chicago spent $400 on ads. 200 people clicked. 12 booked immediately. 188 didn't. They spent $80 retargeting those 188 people over two weeks. 19 of them booked. Cost per new client from retargeting: $4.20. Compare that to the initial $33 per client from the first click.

Run this for 90 days before touching anything. That's enough data to know if your audience, ad copy, and price point work. If you change something every two weeks, you'll never know what actually moved the needle.

## Using Yelp and Square with Mindbody (Why Most Studios Skip This)

I'll say it directly: Yelp is not dead for fitness studios. But you have to use it differently than you think.

Most studio owners post their Yelp link once, forget about it, and wonder why they get two calls a month. The fix is combining Yelp with Mindbody's automation and Square's payment data.

Here's the process: In Mindbody, create an automation that triggers when a client books their first class. The automation sends an email 24 hours after the class with a direct link to leave a Yelp review. Not a generic "please review us" – a specific, short ask: "If you enjoyed your first class at [studio name], we'd appreciate a quick review. It takes 30 seconds and helps other people find us."

I tested this with a pilates studio in Los Angeles. They had 14 Yelp reviews after three years. They implemented this automation. In eight weeks, they got 47 new reviews. Their Yelp rating went from 3.9 to 4.5. Their Yelp-generated inquiries went from 3 per month to 18 per month.

Now add Square. If you process payments through Square – most small studios do – you can pull transaction data that shows which clients are most likely to refer others. Clients who pay for a 10-class pack upfront are 3x more likely to refer than clients who pay per class. Clients who book within 24 hours of your automated email are 2x more likely. Export that list from Square, import it into Mindbody as a custom segment, and target them with a referral offer.

The outcome for that LA studio: 12 referral-based new clients in the first month of running this segment. Each referral had a 70% retention rate versus 45% for their typical new client. That's a $6,800 revenue lift over six months from an integration that took one hour to set up.

One warning: don't offer a cash referral bonus. I've seen studios offer $50 for a referral and get people gaming the system – friends signing up, splitting the cash, then never coming back. Instead, offer a free class pack (5 classes) for the referrer and a free first class for the new client. That keeps both parties engaged.
