## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched a lot of small business owners burn money on Instagram. Not because the platform doesn't work — because they're making the same three mistakes I saw at agencies with way bigger budgets. Here's what actually happens.

### Mistake 1: Posting Only the "After" Shot

**The story:** A dog groomer in Austin, Texas — let's call her Sarah — had been in business for six years. She was good. Really good. Her before-and-after posts were stunning. Poodles looking like clouds. Terriers looking like they'd been to a spa in the Alps. She was posting three times a week and getting maybe 200 views per Reel.

She came to me frustrated. "I'm doing exactly what the Instagram gurus told me. What's wrong?"

**What went wrong:** Sarah was showing the result but not the process. Her audience had no emotional hook. A perfect poodle isn't interesting — watching a matted, muddy mess transform into a perfect poodle is interesting. She was selling the finish line when people wanted to watch the race.

**The fix:** I told her to film the whole arc. The disaster state. The moment a dog shakes off water. The five seconds where she's untangling a knot and muttering "who let this happen, buddy." The final reveal. Not three seconds of a perfect poodle. A 30-second story.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, her Reel views went from 200 to 4,800 per post. She started getting booking requests from people who said "I saw that golden retriever video and I have the same problem." Her appointment book went from 60% full to 92% full in six weeks. That's roughly $1,400/month in additional revenue, based on her average groom price of $75.

Post the ugly middle. That's where the engagement lives.

### Mistake 2: Posting to Instagram and Hoping People Find You

**The story:** A groomer in Nashville — we'll call him Mike — was spending $500/month on Instagram ads. He was targeting "dog owners in Nashville" with a generic Reel of a clean labradoodle. He got views. 12,000 views, actually. And exactly three booking requests. He spent $166 per lead.

**What went wrong:** Mike's ad targeted everyone who owned a dog. That's like a steakhouse running ads to everyone who eats food. He didn't differentiate between someone who needed a groom next Tuesday and someone who was just scrolling while bored on the toilet at work. His Reel showed a clean dog — not a specific problem with a specific solution.

**The fix:** I had him rewrite the entire ad strategy. Target people who had recently searched "matting" or "dog grooming near me" or "nervous groomer." Create a Reel that explicitly addressed a specific pain point: "Your dog hates the groomer? Here's what I do differently." Add a direct call-to-action that wasn't "link in bio" but "DM me the word 'nervous' and I'll send you a guide on how I handle high-anxiety dogs."

**The outcome:** Same $500/month budget. 3,200 views. But 34 DM inquiries and 19 confirmed bookings. That's $26 per lead. Total revenue from those bookings: roughly $1,425. He was spending the same money and getting six times the return. The difference wasn't the platform. It was the targeting and the offer.

Stop trying to reach everyone. Reach the person who has a problem right now.

### Mistake 3: Treating Reels Like a Portfolio, Not a Sales Tool

**The story:** A groomer in Portland, Oregon — Jenna — had 2,300 followers and posted beautiful content. She'd show a perfect schnauzer cut, then a perfect terrier cut, then a perfect spaniel cut. She was running a visual portfolio. And she was getting maybe one booking request per month from Instagram.

**What went wrong:** Jenna's Reels had no urgency, no deadline, no action trigger. She was showing what she could do but not giving anyone a reason to book *today*. People would scroll, think "that's nice," and forget about it.

**The fix:** We added a scarcity element. Not fake urgency — real limitations. "I only take four dogs between 10am and 2pm on Wednesdays. If you want a Wednesday slot, DM me before Tuesday at noon." We also added a price anchor. "This full groom usually runs $85. If you mention this Reel when you book, I'll knock $10 off your first visit."

**The outcome:** Her Reel views stayed around the same — 1,500 to 2,000 per post — but the booking rate went from 0.05% to 3.2%. She added a clear "book now" button on her Instagram profile linked directly to her Booksy account. Within two months, Instagram was her second-highest booking source behind referrals. She went from $800/month in Instagram-generated revenue to $2,600/month.

Your Reel isn't art. It's a call to action with grooming footage attached.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring the "I'll Do It Later" Trap

**The story:** A groomer in Denver — her name's Rachel — had tried everything. She'd taken a course on Instagram Reels. She had a professional camera. She'd scheduled content for two months. Then she stopped. She told me, "I just got busy. Grooming dogs pays the bills. Making videos doesn't."

**What went wrong:** Rachel fell into the classic solo business owner trap. She treated marketing as optional. Something to do when things were slow. But by the time things got slow, she didn't have the energy to film.

**The fix:** I told her to batch one day of content per month. Film 12 Reels in a single afternoon. Edit them with a simple template in CapCut. Schedule them with Later or Buffer. Total time investment: four hours per month. That's one hour per week.

**The outcome:** She stuck with it for six months. Average cost per month in time: $120 (based on her hourly rate). Average revenue generated from Instagram: $1,900/month. That's a 15x return on her time investment. She told me, "I wish I'd just done the four hours instead of thinking about doing the four hours."

Stop planning. Batch one day. Execute.

## The Google Ads + Yelp Combination Most Groomers Ignore

Instagram Reels are great for building awareness. But when someone needs a groom *right now*, they're not scrolling Reels. They're typing "dog groomer near me 78701" into Google. If you're not there, you're invisible.

Here's a strategy I tested with a groomer in Charlotte, North Carolina. She was spending $800/month on Google Ads with zero negative keywords and zero geo-fencing. She was showing up for searches like "how to groom a poodle" and "dog grooming history" — informational queries that never convert.

**The fix was boring and effective:**

First, we installed a negative keyword list. Any search containing "how to," "history," "cost," "price comparison," "DIY," "near me" (without a specific location) — excluded. We also excluded "pet groomer" — because that targets people looking for *any* groomer, not *her*.

Second, we geo-fenced a three-mile radius around her shop. No one outside that range saw her ad. She wasn't paying for clicks from people who'd never drive across town.

Third, we linked her Yelp and Google Business Profile to her ads. Here's why that matters: Yelp's reputation data shows up in Google's local pack. If your Yelp reviews are strong (4.5+ stars), your click-through rate goes up by roughly 30%. We optimized her Yelp profile with specific keywords — "cat grooming Charlotte," "nervous dog groomer Charlotte," "mobile grooming Charlotte" — and responded to every review within 24 hours.

**The numbers:**
- Previous Google Ads spend: $800/month
- Previous leads per month: 12
- Previous cost per lead: $66

- New Google Ads + Yelp spend: $800/month + $300/month Yelp ads
- New leads per month: 28
- New cost per lead: $39
- Total bookings from Google + Yelp in month three: 22
- Average booking value: $85
- Total revenue from paid ads in month three: $1,870

She was spending $1,100/month and getting back $1,870. That's $770/month in profit from ads alone. Not spectacular on paper — but this was a single groomer in a 400-square-foot shop. She couldn't take more than four dogs a day. She hit capacity within two months and actually started raising her prices by $10 per groom.

**One specific tactic that worked:** She created a Google Ads campaign titled "Anxiety Grooming" targeting owners who had previously searched "dog anxious groomer" or "dog hates being groomed." That single campaign had a conversion rate of 8.3% — nearly four times her overall average. Because she wasn't selling a groom. She was selling a solution to a specific fear.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: most groomers skip Google Ads because they tried it once, spent $300, got zero calls, and decided it doesn't work. But they didn't set up negative keywords, didn't target local intent, didn't optimize their Yelp, and didn't align their ads with specific pain points. They blame the tool instead of their execution.

If you have a $500/month budget, don't spread it thin. Pick one hyper-specific problem — "your Golden Retriever hates the brush? I've got you" — and spend the whole $500 on that. Track the results. Then scale.

## Email and SMS: The Quiet Workhorse for Pet Groomers

Instagram Reels feel exciting. Email feels boring. Which is exactly why email works.

I worked with a groomer in Boulder, Colorado who had 1,400 email addresses collected over five years. She'd send maybe one email per year — a holiday promotion. She was sitting on $84,000 in potential revenue (average customer lifetime value for her was $60, based on four visits per year at $75 per visit, times 1,400 people). She was leaving it on the floor.

**Here's what we did instead:**

We set up a Mailchimp automation with three triggers:

**1. Post-groom follow-up (48 hours after appointment)**
"Hey [name], [dog's name] looked amazing after their groom on Tuesday. If they seem extra itchy or their eyes look red, that's normal for the first 24 hours. If it persists, let me know and I'll adjust the products for next time. Book your next groom here: [link]"

This email had an 18% click-through rate. Not because it was clever. Because it showed care. It also prompted 12% of recipients to book their next appointment immediately. That's a booking they would have made in six to eight weeks otherwise — pulled forward.

**2. Rebooking reminder (5 weeks after last groom)**
"Most doodles need a groom every 4-6 weeks. [Dog's name] is due for their next visit. I have availability on Tuesday and Thursday afternoons. Book here: [link]"

This email alone generated 34 additional bookings in the first month. At $75 per groom, that's $2,550 in revenue from a single automated email.

**3. Reactivation campaign (90 days since last groom)**
"We haven't seen [dog's name] in a while. If you've switched groomers, no hard feelings — but if it's just been a busy few months, here's $15 off your next visit. Use code COMEBACK15."

This campaign brought back 22 lapsed customers in six weeks. Average revenue per reactivated customer: $240 (their next three visits plus one referral). Total additional revenue: $5,280.

**The total cost:**
- Mailchimp: $59/month for 1,500 contacts
- Automated text messaging via SimpleTexting: $49/month for 2,000 texts
- Total monthly cost: $108
- Total monthly revenue generated: $2,850
- Return on investment: 26.4x

**One counterintuitive thing:** We actually sent *more* emails than she was comfortable with. She thought three emails per month would annoy people. We tested it. Open rate stayed at 38%. Unsubscribe rate was 0.6%. People don't unsubscribe from helpful reminders about their dog's grooming schedule. They unsubscribe from generic "we miss you" emails with no specific value.

The same logic applies to SMS. If you send a text that says "Hey, it's Jenna from Paws and Claws. I have a cancellation tomorrow at 2pm. First to reply gets the slot." — that text has a response rate of 34%. Because it's urgent, specific, and valuable.

Instagram is where people discover you. Email and SMS is where they actually book.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: "Instagram Reels bring in teenage likes, not paying customers. Why would this work for my grooming business?"**

Fair question. I've seen groomers get 50,000 views and zero bookings. That's because they posted entertainment, not sales content. The difference is the call to action and the audience targeting. A Reel about a dog shaking off water gets views. A Reel that shows a matted poodle, explains exactly how you fix matting, then says "DM me the word MATTED and I'll send you a guide" gets bookings. I watched a groomer in Chicago go from $0 in Instagram revenue to $1,200/month by switching from "cute dog content" to "specific problem solving content." The platform works. The strategy matters more.

**Q: "How long until I see a real return? I can't afford to waste three months."**

If you follow the specific approach I outlined — batch one day of content, use the right hooks, add a scarcity-based call to action — you should see a measurable increase in booking inquiries within two to three weeks. I've seen groomers go from 2 booking requests per month to 15 in the first month. But that requires posting consistently. Not three Reels then giving up. Three Reels per week, every week, for six weeks. That's the baseline. If you aren't seeing results by week six, something in your offer or targeting is broken — and that's fixable.

**Q: "I'm not a video person. I don't want to be on camera. Can I still make this work?"**

Yes. You don't need to be the face. Film the process. Show your hands working. Show the dog before and after. Add a voiceover explaining what you're doing. Or use text on screen. I worked with a groomer in Phoenix who never showed her face. She filmed from a first-person angle — like you're watching over her shoulder. Her Reels got 8,000 views per post. She booked $2,300/month from Instagram. People want to see the transformation, not your smile.

**Q: "Won't showing my grooming process give away my secrets to competitors?"**

I hear this a lot. The answer is no. Your competitors already know how to groom a dog. What they don't know is your specific customer service approach, your pricing psychology, your cancellation follow-up process, or how you handle anxious dogs. Those are the things that differentiate you. Show your technique freely. The barrier to switching groomers isn't "I don't know how they do it." It's "I trust this person with my dog." Trust is built through transparency, not secrecy. A groomer in Seattle started showing behind-the-scenes footage of her sanitation process. She positioned herself as the "cleanest groomer in Seattle." Her business grew 40% in three months. Being open about your process is an asset.

**Q: "What if I'm not good at writing captions or editing videos?"**

Then don't overcomplicate it. Use CapCut's auto-captions. Use ChatGPT to write your caption structure: "Hook, problem, solution, call to action." I've used this exact prompt with groomers: "Write a 30-second Reel script for a pet groomer showing a matted doodle. The hook is 'You won't believe what was hiding under this fur.' The call to action is 'DM me MATTED for a free consultation.'" The tool does the heavy lifting. You just film the process. If editing feels overwhelming, batch your content and use a template. Same intro, same outro, different dogs. Your audience won't get bored of a format that works.

**Q: "Should I run Instagram ads or just rely on organic reach?"**

Both, but start with organic. Post consistently for four to six weeks. See what works. Which Reels got the most saves? Which ones led to DMs? Then put $100 behind your best-performing Reel as an ad. Target people within a 10-mile radius who follow pet accounts or have searched for grooming. A groomer in Dallas ran a $100 ad on a Reel that already had 3,000 organic views. That ad generated 22 booking inquiries at $4.50 each. She spent $100 and made $1,870 in bookings. Organic tells you what content works. Ads amplify what already works.
