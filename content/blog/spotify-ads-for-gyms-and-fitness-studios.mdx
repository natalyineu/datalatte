## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting "Everyone Who Exercises"

A studio owner in Nashville came to me after burning $2,400 on Spotify Ads over two months. She was getting plenty of impressions, but exactly zero new memberships. Zero.

Her targeting? "Fitness" interest category, people within 15 miles of her studio, ages 18–65.

That's not targeting. That's throwing money at a map.

The problem: Spotify's interest-based categories are broad. "Fitness" on Spotify includes someone who ran once in 2019 and now listens to running playlists while commuting. It includes someone who added one yoga track to a study playlist. It includes your cousin who downloaded a 5K app and forgot to delete it.

The fix was specific:

- We built three separate campaigns targeting by **listening behavior**, not interests. Spotify lets you target by what people are actually playing right now — specific workout playlists, genre-specific exercise mixes, or "high intensity" tagged tracks.
- We layered **location radius targeting** — a tight 3-mile radius for her main studio, 1-mile radius for her boutique yoga space, and a 5-mile "commuter zone" that covered the neighborhoods her current members came from.
- We created separate ad groups for morning (6-9am run playlists), lunch (quick sweat playlists), and evening (wind-down yoga playlists). Same studio. Different hooks.

Outcome: She spent $1,100 the next month (she was scared to go back to $1,200). Got 38 website visits that turned into 11 trial class bookings. Four became monthly memberships. Monthly recurring revenue from that single campaign: roughly $680. The ads paid for themselves by week two.

Her mistake wasn't Spotify. It was expecting a firehose to fill a teacup.

### Mistake #2: Running a Radio Ad That Happens to Be on Spotify

A CrossFit box in Denver spent $3,000 producing a "professional" audio ad. It had a voiceover artist, music drops, a fade-out tagline. Sounded beautiful. Zero gym tours booked.

The owner told me, "It's basically the same ad I run on the local radio station."

That's the problem.

Radio ads assume a passive listener who's half paying attention in their car. Spotify ads during a workout hit someone who is actively in a specific state: sweating, pushing through a set, trying not to pass out.

You can't sell them a "join our community" tagline in that moment. You need to say something that lands in the three seconds between reps.

Here's what we changed for the Denver gym:

- **Rewrote the script** to open with something the listener was literally doing: "You're three minutes into a set and your arms are shaking. That's exactly where you're supposed to be."
- **Cut the production budget** to $400. Used the owner's actual voice, recorded on a decent mic in a quiet room. The minor imperfections sounded more honest.
- **Added a specific, immediate action**: "Show this ad at the front desk and skip the drop-in fee. Today only."

Outcome: 14 people showed up in the first week. They weren't ready to buy a membership yet — but the owner converted three into two-week trials, and two of those became members. The ad recouped its total cost ($3,400 including production and spend) in about six weeks.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Retargeting Completely

A barre studio in Austin was getting decent click-through rates on Spotify Ads — about 1.2%, which is fine for audio — but nobody was booking.

The owner showed me her Spotify Ads dashboard. She was spending $900 a month. She was getting about 150-200 clicks a month to her website. And then... nothing. No remarketing. No follow-up. No email capture.

She was basically inviting people to look at her front door and then hoping they'd let themselves in.

Here's what actually works when you advertise fitness on Spotify:

- **You need a bridge.** Spotify doesn't give you an email address. It gives you a click. If that click lands on a generic homepage, you lose them. We built a dedicated landing page with one thing: a free trial offer in exchange for an email. Used Mailchimp to automate the follow-up sequence.
- **Run a retargeting campaign on Facebook/Instagram.** Spotify's audience data and Facebook's conversion tracking work together if you set up the pixel correctly. People who heard your Spotify ad but didn't click get a different message on Instagram three days later.
- **Use Spotify's own pixel.** It's clunkier than Meta's, but it lets you track whether someone who heard your ad later searched for your studio on Google. That's conversion data most small businesses leave on the table.

For the Austin studio: We added a retargeting layer for an extra $200/month on Meta. Nine people clicked the Spotify ad, didn't book, saw a Facebook ad the next week, and booked. Three became members. The $200 spent on retargeting generated about $900 in first-month membership fees.

## The Specifics of Scriptwriting for Workout Audio

Writing a 30-second audio ad for Spotify is not copywriting. It's a different muscle.

Here's the structure I've used with fitness clients that consistently outperforms:

**The first 5 seconds** — Name the feeling they're having right now. "Your legs are burning." "You're trying not to look at the clock." Not "you deserve a great workout." Too vague. They don't feel "deserving" mid-burpee. They feel pain.

**The next 10 seconds** — Offer a specific problem fix. "Most people hold their breath during the hardest part of a set. Next rep, exhale hard on the way up." This is advice they can use immediately, even if they never book with you. That builds trust.

**The final 15 seconds** — The offer. Keep it small. Not "join our premium unlimited membership." Too much commitment for someone who's sweaty and distracted. Try: "Come try one class free. Just say the word 'Spotify' at the front desk. Zero pressure. We'll even have a towel ready."

I tested this against a standard "professional" ad for a studio in Portland, OR. The "feeling + quick tip + low-friction offer" version got a 3.1% conversion rate (click to landing page visit). The polished version got 0.7%.

## How to Track Spotify Ads Without Going Insane

Small business owners ask me this constantly: "How do I know if the ad actually brought someone in?"

You can't count on Spotify's dashboard alone. It tells you impressions and clicks. It doesn't tell you who walked through your door.

Here's the system I set up for a client in Chicago that actually works:

**Step 1: Unique landing page per campaign.** Use a subdomain or a specific URL parameter. Something like `yourstudio.com/spotify` or `yourstudio.com/?ref=spotify01`. Don't link to your homepage. You'll never know where they came from.

**Step 2: Track the referral in your booking system.** Booksy and Mindbody both let you add custom intake questions. Add one: "How did you hear about us?" with Spotify as a dropdown option. Yes, some people won't remember. But if you're seeing 15-20% of new bookings selecting "Spotify" over 3-4 months, the data is real.

**Step 3: Phone call tracking.** Use a tool like CallRail ($30-$50/month) to assign a unique phone number to your Spotify campaign. Every call from that number is a tracked conversion. For a Nashville client, this revealed that 40% of her Spotify-driven leads called rather than booked online. She would have missed them entirely without the tracking number.

**Step 4: Compare against Google Ads.** If you run both, look at cost per actual booking, not cost per click. A Denver gym was spending $4.50 per click on Google Ads and $1.80 per click on Spotify. But Google's conversion rate was 8% and Spotify's was 3%. The actual cost per booking was roughly the same ($56 vs $60). Neither was a clear winner — but without the tracking, she would have killed Spotify based on click metrics alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can Spotify Ads actually compete with Google Ads for local fitness studios?**

Yes, but not in the way you think. Google Ads captures people who are *already searching* for a gym. That's high intent, but it's a small pool — you're fighting every other studio in your city for the same 200 people typing "yoga near me" each month. Spotify Ads puts your brand in front of people who aren't looking for you yet but are in a mindset where your offer might land. For most of my clients, Spotify doesn't replace Google — it adds a second revenue stream from people who didn't know they needed you.

**Q: How much should I spend to see if Spotify Ads will work for my studio?**

Start with $500-$600 a month. That's enough to get meaningful data in 30-45 days without putting your rent at risk. Do not commit to a three-month minimum with an agency or consultant until you've run at least one full, tracked month. If you get zero bookings in month one, it's probably a targeting or offer problem — not a platform problem. Fix those first.

**Q: I own a hair salon, not a gym. Does any of this apply?**

Yes. The "mid-workout" angle in this article is specific, but the mechanics work for any business where people are doing something repetitive and could use a distraction. Hair salons can target people listening to "chill vibes" playlists or "getting ready" playlists. Coffee shops can target morning commute playlists. Pet groomers can target "dog walking" playlists. The principle is the same: find the playlist that matches the moment someone would naturally think about your service.

**Q: How long does it take to set up a Spotify Ads campaign?**

The account setup takes 20 minutes. Building a decent campaign structure — audience targeting, ad groups, tracking setup, landing page — takes 4-6 hours the first time. You can cut that to 90 minutes once you know what you're doing. The part most people underestimate is the ad itself. Writing and recording a 30-second audio ad that doesn't sound like a used car commercial takes a few rounds of revision.

**Q: What happens when someone skips my ad?**

They skip it. That's fine. Spotify's ad model charges you only when someone doesn't skip. If they skip in the first 5 seconds, you pay nothing. If they listen past 5 seconds and skip, you pay. That means your ad needs to earn its keep in those first few seconds. If your open is boring, the skip rate spikes and your budget goes to waste. Test two or three different openings in your first month.

**Q: Do I need a professional voice actor?**

No. I've seen studio owner recordings outperformed polished VO ads by 2:1. The catch is the recording quality — don't record in a tiled bathroom or next to a fan. Get a $60 USB microphone like a Blue Yeti Nano, record in a room with carpet and curtains (or under a blanket if you're desperate), and do two or three takes while standing up. You sound different when you're standing. More energy. More believable.
