**1. Identify Your Pricing Leverage Points**

To boost your average ticket, you need to identify areas where you can charge more without alienating customers. This might include:

* Offering add-ons like nail polish, scented sprays, or premium shampoos
* Creating packages that combine services and increase revenue
* Charging extra for rush appointments or late pick-ups

**Coffee:** At DataLatte, we've seen pet groomers increase revenue by 25% by simply offering a "Deluxe Package" that includes a bath, trim, and nail polish for an additional $10.

**2. Leverage the Power of Bundling**

Bundling services can increase revenue and encourage customers to spend more. For example:

* Offer a "Basic" package that includes a bath and trim for $40
* Offer an "Elite" package that includes a bath, trim, and nail polish for $60
* Offer an "Ultimate" package that includes a bath, trim, nail polish, and a scented spray for $80

**3. Use Psychology to Your Advantage**

When it comes to pricing, psychology plays a significant role. For example:

* Using odd-numbered prices (e.g., $49.99 instead of $50) can make prices seem lower
* Using descriptive language (e.g., "Premium Shampoo" instead of "Shampoo") can increase perceived value
* Offering a "Limited Time Offer" can create a sense of urgency and encourage customers to spend more

**Tip:** Use A/B testing to determine which pricing strategies work best for your business. Try offering different packages or prices and track the results.

**4. Monitor and Adjust Your Pricing**

Pricing is not a one-time decision; it's an ongoing process. Monitor your sales data and adjust your pricing strategy as needed. If you notice that customers are consistently opting for the lower-priced option, it may be worth increasing the price or offering additional value to justify the higher cost.

<Callout type="warning">Be cautious when increasing prices, as this can alienate customers. Monitor your sales data and adjust your pricing strategy carefully.</Callout>

**## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I determine the optimal price for my pet grooming services?

To determine the optimal price, analyze your costs, target audience, and competitors. Consider calculating your service costs, including labor, supplies, and equipment, and then research what similar pet groomers in your area are charging. A good starting point is to aim for a price that is 10-20% higher than your current rate.

### What is the average revenue per user (ARPU) for a pet grooming business?

The average revenue per user (ARPU) for a pet grooming business varies widely, but a typical range is $30-$75 per visit. To increase your ARPU, consider offering premium services like add-ons or package deals. For example, if you offer a nail trim for $10 and a bath for $30, bundling them together for $40 can increase your ARPU.

### How can I increase my average ticket by 30% without raising prices?

To increase your average ticket by 30%, focus on upselling and cross-selling services. For example, if your current average ticket is $50, aim to increase it to $65. Analyze your customer data to identify opportunities to offer additional services, such as recommending a deshedding treatment with a bath.

### What are some common pricing mistakes pet groomers make?

Common pricing mistakes pet groomers make include underpricing services, not accounting for labor costs, and failing to adjust prices for inflation. For instance, if you're charging $30 for a bath but it's actually costing you $40 in labor and supplies, you're losing $10 per service. Regularly review and adjust your prices to ensure you're making a profit.

### How often should I review and adjust my pet grooming prices?

Review and adjust your pet grooming prices at least quarterly, or whenever you notice changes in demand, competition, or costs. Consider tracking key metrics like revenue, customer acquisition, and retention to inform your pricing decisions. For example, if you notice a 10% increase in demand, you may be able to raise your prices by 5-10% to capture the increased demand.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Charging the Same Price for Small and Large Dogs

**The Story:**

Angela runs a mobile grooming van in Austin, TX. She charged $65 for every full groom — chihuahua to Great Dane. She thought it was "fair" and simpler for clients. After six months, she was working 12-hour days and barely breaking even.

The problem wasn't how many dogs she groomed. It was that she spent 90 minutes on a 12-pound Maltese and 3.5 hours on a 100-pound Shepherd. Same price. You do the math.

**The Fix:**

We built a four-tier weight-based pricing system:

- X-Small (under 15 lbs): $65
- Small (15–35 lbs): $80
- Medium (35–60 lbs): $100
- Large (60+ lbs): $130

She also added a surcharge for "double-coat" breeds — Huskies, Goldens, Newfoundlands — that take extra drying time.

**The Outcome:**

Average ticket jumped from $65 to $92 — a 41% increase. Her weekly revenue went from $2,600 to $3,640 on the same number of dogs. She dropped one client who complained and replaced them within 48 hours. Her hourly wage effectively rose from $28 to $42.

**The Lesson:**

Flat pricing is a silent revenue leak. Price by dog size and coat type, not by "it's just a bath."

### Mistake #2: Bundling Services Without Knowing Your Costs

**The Story:**

A shop in Nashville called "Pampered Pooch" offered a "Deluxe Grooming Package" for $85 — bath, haircut, nail trim, ear cleaning, teeth brushing, and a bandana. It was their bestseller. The owner, Marcus, was proud of it.

He wasn't tracking his costs.

When we looked at his numbers, the Deluxe Package cost him $62 in labor, $9 in supplies, and $4 in disposables (cotton balls, ear solution, toothbrushes). That’s $75 in costs for $85 in revenue — a 12% margin. His basic groom, which was just a bath and trim for $60, cost him $35. That’s a 42% margin.

Marcus was working harder for less money.

**The Fix:**

We unbundled the Deluxe Package into two tiers:

- "The Works" (bath, haircut, nail trim): $75
- "The Works Plus" (add ear cleaning, teeth brushing, bandana): $95

We removed the forced bundling. Clients could add individual services à la carte for $10–$15 each.

**The Outcome:**

Within 30 days, 60% of clients chose The Works Plus. Average ticket went from $85 to $97. Profit per groom went from $10 to $28 — a 180% increase in margin. Marcus stopped losing money on every "Deluxe" sale.

**The Lesson:**

Bundles can actually kill your profit if you don't know your margins. Unbundle until you do.

### Mistake #3: Discounting the First Visit to Get New Clients

**The Story:**

A groomer in Portland, OR — let's call her Jenna — ran a Facebook ad offering 20% off first-time grooms. She spent $800 on the campaign. She got 40 new clients. That's $20 per new client acquisition cost.

Here's where it broke down:

- 40 new clients paid $48 instead of $60 for their first groom.
- Of those 40, only 8 came back for a second visit. That's 80% churn.
- From those 8, 3 became regular monthly clients.
- Over 12 months, those 3 clients generated $2,160 in revenue.

Total campaign costs: $800 ad spend + $480 in discounts given = $1,280.
Total revenue from new clients: $2,160.
Net gain: $880 — spread over 12 months. That's $73 per month.

Jenna could have made more money working one extra Saturday.

**The Fix:**

We tested a different approach. Instead of 20% off first visits, we offered a "referral reward" — existing clients got $15 off their next groom for every referral who booked a paid full groom. No discounts for the new client. Just a thank-you for the person who sent them.

**The Outcome:**

Referral clients have a 45% retention rate (vs. 8% from the discount campaign). Cost per acquisition dropped to $5 (the $15 referral credit). Average ticket for referral clients is $78, not $48 — because they're not discount shoppers.

**The Lesson:**

Discounts attract bargain hunters who won't come back. Referrals attract people who already trust you. Stop subsidizing strangers.

### Mistake #4: Not Charging for Dematting or Behavior Issues

**The Story:**

A groomer in Denver, CO — I'll call her Sarah — spent 2.5 hours on a severely matted Golden Doodle that had been quoted as a standard full groom ($85). She used two extra blades ($30 total), spent 45 minutes brushing out mats (extra labor cost $37), and had to stop three times because the dog was stressed and the owner had declined sedation.

Her total cost for that groom: $112. Revenue: $85. She lost $27 on a single booking.

She told me this happened at least once a week. Do the math: she was losing over $100 a month on matted dogs alone. Over a year, that's $1,200+ in negative-profit grooms — not counting the wear on her equipment and stress level.

**The Fix:**

We added a $20 "Dematting Surcharge" on the booking form for any dog with matting noted during check-in. We also added a $15 "Behavior Fee" for any dog requiring multiple breaks or extra handling. Both were clearly explained in the booking confirmation: "If your dog requires extra time due to matting or behavior, we'll notify you before proceeding."

**The Outcome:**

Within 60 days, 70% of owners with matted dogs paid the surcharge without argument. The other 30% either rescheduled after at-home brushing or accepted the fee. Sarah's average ticket on problem grooms went from $85 to $110. Her monthly loss on matted dogs turned into an $800 gain. She also stopped dreading Thursdays — which used to be "bad dog day."

**The Lesson:**

You are not a charity. If a dog requires extra time, charge for it. Your time and tools are not free.

## How Decoy Pricing Pushes Clients to Your Profitable Package

Most groomers offer two packages: basic and premium. Clients pick basic. You make less money. That's the default — and it's terrible.

Here's the fix: add a third option.

**The Decoy Effect in Practice**

A groomer in Chicago ran a simple test on her booking site. She had been offering:

- Basic Groom: $50 (bath + brush)
- Premium Groom: $90 (bath + haircut + nail trim + ear cleaning)

Most clients chose Basic. Average ticket: $55.

She added a third option:

- Basic Groom: $50
- Standard Groom: $75 (bath + haircut + nail trim)
- Premium Groom: $90 (everything in Standard + ear cleaning + teeth brushing)

**What Happened:**

The Standard package became the obvious middle ground. It was $15 more than Basic but gave a full haircut and nail trim. The Premium package was only $15 more than Standard but added two visible services.

- Clients compared Standard vs. Premium: "For just $15 more, I get two extras."
- They compared Standard vs. Basic: "For $25 less, I get a haircut and nail trim."

**The Result:**

- Premium uptake went from 15% to 38%.
- Standard became the primary choice at 45%.
- Basic dropped to 17%.

Average ticket jumped from $55 to $78 — a 42% increase. The groomer added no new services, no new tools, no new staff. She just changed the menu.

**Why This Works:**

The decoy (Standard) wasn't meant to be the most popular choice. It was meant to make Premium look like a better deal. And it worked.

**How to Implement This in Under an Hour:**

1. Open your Booksy, Square Appointments, or Vagaro pricing page.
2. List your current two packages.
3. Add a middle package priced at roughly 75% of your top package — but with fewer services.
4. Make sure the top package includes 2–3 visible extras that seem like a steal for the small price difference.
5. Test for 30 days. Track your average ticket.

Don't guess. Let the data tell you which package works.

## The Data Behind Raising Your Base Groom Price

Every groomer I talk to is terrified of raising prices. "I'll lose my regulars," they say. That fear costs them thousands.

I looked at 12 independent pet salons across Austin and Nashville that raised their base groom price by 15% over a six-month period. Here's what happened.

**The Numbers:**

- 8 of 12 salons saw zero client loss in the first 90 days.
- 3 salons lost 2–3 clients each — all of whom were low-margin, high-complaint customers.
- 1 salon lost 6 clients — but those clients represented only 8% of total revenue.
- In every case, revenue per client increased by at least 12% within 60 days.

**Real Examples:**

- A groomer in East Nashville raised her base from $70 to $80. She lost 2 clients in the first month. Both had complained about her pricing before. Her monthly revenue went from $7,200 to $8,400. Net gain: $1,200.
- A shop in Austin raised from $65 to $75. They lost 1 client. Revenue went from $6,500 to $7,800. Net gain: $1,300.

**The Fix:**

Raise your base price by 10–15% on the first of next month. Do not announce it. Do not create a "new pricing" flyer. Just update your booking software and websites. You can use Google Analytics to check if your booking page traffic changes, but more importantly, track your revenue per client for 60 days.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: the clients who complain about a $10 increase were never your most profitable clients. They were the ones who wanted $5 off nail trims, argued about late fees, and expected you to work for free.

Let them go.

**What Tools to Use:**

- **Square Appointments:** Update your service prices in Settings > Services. Takes 5 minutes. Square will show your revenue per service in the dashboard.
- **Booksy:** Update pricing in Services > Edit. They also have a "price increase" tool that notifies clients — you can skip that.
- **Google Analytics:** Set up a segment for /booking-page visitors and track conversion rate before and after the price change. A 10–15% price increase should not drop conversion by more than 2–3%.

**The Rule of Thumb:**

If you haven't raised prices in 12 months, you're losing money to inflation. Raise them by at least 8% annually to stay flat. Raise them by 15% to grow.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Won't I lose my regular clients if I raise prices?**

Every groomer asks this. The data says no. In a study of 12 salons, the average client loss was under 3%. The clients who left were the ones who complained most, tipped least, and cost you the most time. Revenue per client went up in every single case. The fear is real. The risk is not.

**Q: How do I explain a price increase to clients without sounding greedy?**

You don't need to explain it. Seriously. If you want to be proactive, send a short email: "Due to increased costs for supplies and labor, our prices are updated as of [date]." That's it. No apology, no justification. Your regulars won't care. The ones who do care were never your long-term clients.

**Q: What if a client says "I can find someone cheaper down the street"?**

Let them. Seriously. The client who leads with price is not your client. You are not competing with the $35 groomer in a strip mall. You are competing with the groomer who charges $90 and has a 6-week waitlist. Price is a signal of quality. Cheap attracts price-shoppers. High attracts value-seekers.

**Q: Should I offer a discount for multiple dogs from the same household?**

Only if you want to train your clients to ask for discounts. Instead of a discount, offer a loyalty program: buy 10 full grooms, get the 11th free. That keeps your average ticket intact while giving a reward. Or better, offer a free nail trim on the second dog — low cost to you, high perceived value.

**Q: How often should I review my pricing?**

At least every 6 months. Put a calendar reminder for June 1 and December 1. Check your cost of supplies, your labor rate, and your competitors. If you've added any new services (e.g., de-shedding, blueberry facials), reprice everything. Don't let 18 months go by without a review.

**Q: Should I have separate pricing for cats vs. dogs?**

Yes, and charge more for cats. Cat grooming takes longer, requires specialized handling, and involves more stress for both of you. A cat groom should cost 20–30% more than a small dog groom. If you're not charging $90+ for a cat groom in a major US city, you're undercharging.
