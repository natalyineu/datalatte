## Fix Your Google Business Profile First

Your Google Business Profile (GBP) is the foundation of restaurant SEO. 83% of local searches start on Google Maps, not the search engine. For a coffee shop in Seattle, fixing GBP errors increased their map visibility by 300% in six weeks.

Start by claiming your profile if you haven’t already. Add high-quality photos (interior, menu items, happy customers). Update your operating hours—especially if you’re closed on holidays. Use the "Posts" section to share specials like "$2 off iced coffee Monday–Thursday."

<Callout type="tip">Include **"Call To Action" buttons** like "Make Reservation" or "Order Online" in your GBP. They boost engagement by 25%.</Callout>

Add 10–15 relevant categories. A sushi bar might list "Sushi Restaurant," "Japanese Food," and "Takeout." Avoid stretching categories—Google penalizes overstuffing.

## Build Citations Like a Pro

Citations are online mentions of your restaurant’s name, address, and phone number (NAP). They’re critical for local rankings. A pet groomer in Toronto saw a 50% drop in citations after switching platforms—her search ranking plummeted. Avoid this by auditing your citations monthly.

Start with **DataLatte’s citation builder** to list your business on sites like Yelp, Yellow Pages, and TripAdvisor. Ensure your NAP is consistent across all listings. If you change addresses, update your GBP first, then update every citation.

<Callout type="warning">Inconsistent NAP data can take **6–8 weeks** to fix in search rankings. Use a tool like BrightLocal to track errors.</Callout>

Focus on local directories. A yoga studio in Melbourne listed on Mindbody and Yoga Alliance increased studio sign-ups by 40%. Niche citations matter more than quantity.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Picking the Wrong Google Business Profile Category

**The story:** A taco truck owner in Austin, Texas, texted me in a panic. His truck had been parked at the same spot on South Congress for eight months, but his Google Maps ranking kept dropping. Walk-ins were flat. He’d even run a $500 Google Ads campaign—zero calls from it. His GBP listed “Mexican Food Truck” as the primary category.

That’s the problem.

**What went wrong:** A “Food Truck” category doesn’t trigger the right local search queries. When someone in Austin types “tacos near me” or “best tacos South Congress,” Google favors restaurants, taco shops, and fast food categories—not food trucks. His profile was invisible for the exact searches that bring customers to his curb.

**The fix:** I switched his primary category to “Taco Restaurant.” Then added secondary categories: “Mexican Restaurant,” “Takeout,” “Caterer,” and “Lunch Restaurant.” We also added a “Catering” service button, since office lunch orders are half his weekday business.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, his truck appeared in the top three Google Maps results for “tacos South Congress.” Phone calls for catering increased from zero per week to twelve. He told me his average catering order is $85. That’s $1,020 per week in new, tracked revenue—from a free category change.

**Your move:** If you’re a coffee shop, do not list “Cafe” as your primary category if you also serve breakfast sandwiches. Pick “Breakfast Restaurant” or “Coffee Shop”—whichever drives the most walk-ins. Check your top three competitors’ categories. Copy the ones that apply. Don’t guess.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Photo Metadata and Freshness

**The story:** A hair salon in Portland, Oregon, had been open for three years. Their GBP had 47 reviews and a 4.6-star average—solid. But they were ranking #9 for “hair salon Portland.” The salon owner told me she updated her photos every six months or so. I checked her profile. She had 12 photos total. Six were from 2020.

**What went wrong:** Google rewards fresh, high-quality images with location metadata. Old photos signal neglect. Worse, photos without geotags (the location data embedded in the file) don’t help Google confirm your physical presence. Google treats stale profiles like closed businesses.

**The fix:** I asked the salon owner to take 7 new photos every week for one month: two interior shots with clients in chairs (with permission), one shot of products on the shelf, one street-front shot, one shot of a finished hairstyle, and two shots of the team. Before uploading, I made sure each photo had location data enabled on the phone’s camera settings. We uploaded 28 photos in a month. All geotagged.

**The outcome:** The salon jumped from #9 to #2 in local Maps results for “hair salon Portland.” Bookings through the Google “Book Appointment” button went from 3 per week to 19 per week. The owner said her average appointment is $65. That’s an extra $1,040 per week in bookings, directly attributed to a photo strategy that cost $0.

**Your move:** Enable location services on your phone’s camera before taking GBP photos. Upload at least 5 new photos per week. Delete any photo older than 12 months. If you use an iPhone, make sure “Precise Location” is on for the Camera app. Android users: check that “Location tags” are enabled in the camera settings.

### Mistake #3: Letting Yelp Listings Rot While You Focus on Google

**The story:** A pet groomer in Chicago had a spotless GBP profile—verified, active, fully optimized. She was spending $300/month on Google Ads for “dog grooming Chicago.” Her GBP ranked #1. But when I checked her Yelp page, it listed her phone number from 2019, a different address (residential, not her shop), and the last review was from 2021. She had no idea. She told me, “I don’t do Yelp. That’s for restaurants.”

**What went wrong:** Yelp still drives significant traffic for local service businesses, especially in cities like Chicago. When her Yelp listing had the wrong address, Google saw a citation inconsistency—a major ranking signal. Google cross-references your NAP (name, address, phone) across directories. When Yelp says you’re at a different address, Google gets confused. Her GBP ranking dropped from #1 to #4 in six weeks.

**The fix:** We claimed her Yelp listing, corrected the address, updated the phone number, added 10 photos, and responded to the three most recent reviews. I recommended she spend 10 minutes per week monitoring Yelp and replying to reviews. We also added her Yelp URL to her website’s “Find Us” footer.

**The outcome:** Within a month, her GBP ranking returned to #1. Yelp direct bookings increased from 0 to 8 per week. Average groom is $75. That’s $600 per week from a directory she’d ignored. The owner told me she was “pissed she’d wasted a year avoiding Yelp.”

**Your move:** Check your Yelp, Facebook, Bing Places, Apple Maps, and Yellowpages listings. Fix any NAP inconsistencies. Respond to reviews—even the bad ones. Spend 15 minutes per week on this. It’s not glamorous. It works.

### Mistake #4: Writing Your Google Posts Like a Press Release

**The story:** A fitness studio in Denver offered hot yoga and HIIT classes. Their GBP posts read like corporate announcements: “We are pleased to announce our new class schedule for the fall season.” They posted once a month. Their Google Posts engagement rate was 0.2%.

**What went wrong:** Google Posts are mini-advertisements. They’re not newsletters. They need a hook, an offer, and a clear call to action. Generic language gets ignored. The studio owner told me she “didn’t want to sound pushy.” That’s the opposite of what a Google Post needs.

**The fix:** I rewrote their posts to be direct and urgent. Examples:

- “Your first HIIT class is free this week. Show up at 6 AM. Sweat guaranteed. Book here.”
- “Hot yoga at 7 PM. 20 spots left. Bring a towel and a friend. First-timers get 50% off.”
- “We’re closed Thanksgiving. But Wednesday morning class is $10. Reserve your mat.”

We posted twice per week for three months.

**The outcome:** Google Posts engagement (clicks and calls) went from 0.2% to 4.7%. Class bookings through GBP increased by 63%. The studio owner said her monthly class pass revenue went from $3,800 to $5,200—a $1,400 increase from writing posts that didn’t sound like a PR firm wrote them.

**Your move:** Write your Google Posts like you’re talking to a friend who’s walking past your shop. Use a number. Use a deadline. Use a price. Use the word “free” if you can. Post at least twice a week.

## How to Use Google Ads for Walk-Ins Without Setting Money on Fire

Most small business owners I talk to have a Google Ads story that starts with “I tried it once” and ends with “I spent $500 and got nothing.” That’s not because Google Ads doesn’t work. It’s because most restaurant, salon, and studio owners set up their campaign wrong.

I’ve managed eight-figure Google Ads budgets at GroupM. The mistakes I see at the local level are the same mistakes I saw at the agency level, just with smaller dollar amounts. Here’s how to run Google Ads for walk-ins without lighting your budget on fire.

**First, target the right radius.** If you’re a coffee shop in Nashville, don’t target “Nashville.” Target a 2-mile radius around your shop. People drive 30 minutes for a specific restaurant, but not for drip coffee. If you’re a pet groomer, a 5-mile radius works. Use location targeting in Google Ads and set it to “Presence or interest.” That way you only show ads to people who are physically in your area, not people who searched for your city from another state.

**Second, use location extensions.** Link your Google Business Profile to your Google Ads account. This shows your address, phone number, and a “Get Directions” link directly in the ad. A coffee shop in Austin saw a 23% increase in “Get Directions” clicks after adding location extensions. That directly translates to more butts in seats.

**Third, use call-only ads for specific hours.** If your hair salon is open 9 AM to 6 PM, run call-only ads during those hours only. No website visit. No form fill. Just a “Call Now” button. One pet groomer in Denver told me she gets 8–12 calls per day from her call-only ad campaign. Average appointment is $85. She spends $200/month on the campaign. Her return is roughly $680–$1,020 per week.

**Fourth, don’t use broad match keywords.** Broad match will match your “coffee shop” ad to someone searching for “café near me” or even “espresso machine repair.” That burns your budget. Stick to phrase match and exact match. For a coffee shop, use keywords like “coffee shop [your neighborhood],” “latte [your neighborhood],” and “best coffee near [street name].”

**Fifth, set a daily budget you can afford to lose.** Start with $10–$15 per day. Run the campaign for 30 days. Track calls, direction requests, and website clicks. If you’re not seeing a return after 30 days, pause it. Do not set it to “maximize conversions” when your campaign has zero conversion data. That’s a fast way to spend $300 on irrelevant clicks.

One bakery in Portland spent $450/month on Google Ads with zero tracking. After I helped her set up call tracking and a “Get Directions” goal, she discovered her campaign was generating 14 direction requests per week. Her average sale is $22. That’s $308 per week from $112.50 in ad spend. Three hundred percent return. But she never would have known if she didn’t set up measurement first.

If you don’t want to track, don’t bother with Google Ads. You’ll just be paying Google for the privilege of guessing.

## Reputation Management: How to Get More Reviews Without Begging

Every coffee shop owner I’ve worked with has said the same thing: “I ask customers for reviews, but they forget.” The fix isn’t to ask more nicely. It’s to make the process invisible.

**First, use a direct link to your Google review page.** No one will search for your business name, scroll to the review section, and write a review. They’ll get distracted by Instagram. Create a short link using a tool like Bitly or a custom URL (e.g., yourbusiness.com/review) that redirects to your Google review page. One salon in Chicago put this link on every appointment confirmation email. They went from 3 reviews per month to 18 reviews per month.

**Second, use Square or Mailchimp to automate review requests.** If you use Square for payments, enable the “Ask for a Review” feature. It automatically texts the customer after their transaction. A pet store in Austin enabled this and went from 12 reviews to 89 reviews in six months. Square processes the payment, Square sends the text. Zero effort from the owner.

**Third, respond to every review—good and bad.** This is the single highest-leverage activity for local SEO. A coffee shop in Portland saw their GBP ranking improve from #5 to #2 in three months after the owner started replying to every review within 48 hours. The replies didn’t need to be long. Just: “Thanks for stopping by, Sarah. Glad you liked the cold brew. See you next week.” For negative reviews: “Sorry about the wait, Mark. We’ve adjusted our morning staffing. Hope you’ll give us another shot.”

**Fourth, don’t buy reviews.** I’ve seen this backfire at three different businesses. Google detects fake review patterns and suppresses your GBP. One Nashville diner bought 15 five-star reviews from a service. Within two weeks, Google removed their GBP from Maps entirely. It took four months and three appeals to get it reinstated. Not worth it.

**Fifth, ask for reviews when the customer is happy.** The best time to ask is immediately after a positive interaction. A hair stylist in Denver asks clients right after they see their haircut in the mirror. She hands them her phone with the review link already open. She gets 2–3 reviews per day that way. That’s more than most coffee shops get in a month.

I had one client—a coffee shop in Austin—who set up a tablet at the register with a “Leave us a review” prompt before you tap your card. They got 47 reviews in the first month. But they also got 12 one-star reviews from people who didn’t like being bothered. So test this carefully. What works for a salon might annoy a coffee shop customer.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it take to see results from local SEO?**

I usually tell clients to expect 6 to 12 weeks for noticeable movement in Google Maps rankings. If you fix your GBP, add 15 high-quality photos, and start posting twice a week, you might see a shift in 4 weeks. But sustainable rankings take longer. One bakery in Denver saw a 40% increase in walk-ins after 8 weeks of consistent effort. If you’re not seeing any change after 90 days, something is broken. Re-check your NAP consistency, your categories, and your review velocity.

**Q: Do I need a website for local SEO, or is Google Business Profile enough?**

You need both. If you only have a GBP, you are renting real estate from Google. You don’t own it. They can change the algorithm, remove your listing, or bury you in the “more places” section. A website gives you independence. Plus, Google uses your website as a citation signal. A simple one-page site with your address, phone number, hours, and a menu is fine. Use Squarespace or Wix. Don’t overthink it. One coffee shop in Portland spent $6,000 on a custom website they didn’t need. A $100 Shopify site would have worked.

**Q: How many reviews do I need to rank #1?**

There’s no magic number. A cafe in Austin with 47 reviews and a 4.8-star average ranked #3 behind a chain with 212 reviews and a 4.0-star average. Google weights review volume heavily, but recency and response rate matter too. My rule of thumb: aim for 50 reviews minimum, respond to all of them, and add 5–10 new reviews per month. That puts you in the top three in most mid-size US cities.

**Q: Should I try to remove fake negative reviews?**

Only if they violate Google’s policy—profanity, off-topic, or from someone who clearly never visited. If a competitor leaves a one-star review saying “The food is terrible” when they’ve never been, flag it. Google may take it down, but don’t count on it. I’ve flagged 12 fake reviews for clients. Two were removed. The rest stayed. Instead of obsessing over fake reviews, focus on getting more real ones to drown them out.

**Q: Is Yelp worth my time if I’m a hair salon or pet groomer?**

Yes, but don’t obsess over it. Yelp drives traffic, especially for service businesses in cities like Chicago and Portland. But Yelp’s algorithm is not as transparent as Google’s. Spend 10 minutes per week monitoring Yelp. Fix any incorrect information. Respond to reviews. Do not pay for Yelp advertising until your organic Yelp presence is solid. I’ve seen too many businesses pay $300/month for Yelp ads and get two phone calls.

**Q: What’s the one thing I should do today that gives the biggest immediate impact?**

Fix your operating hours on Google Maps. I cannot tell you how many coffee shops and salons I’ve seen list “Closed” on holidays when they’re actually open, or “Open 24 hours” when they close at 6 PM. One diner in Denver lost 30% of their weekend walk-ins because their hours said they opened at 10 AM when it was actually 7 AM. Update your hours. Add holiday hours. Check it once a week. This takes five minutes and can directly increase foot traffic tomorrow.
