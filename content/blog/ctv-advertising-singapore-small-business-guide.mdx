## The Singapore Streaming Landscape

**meWATCH** Mediacorp's national streaming platform, Singapore's most-used CTV environment with strong local content across English, Mandarin, Malay, and Tamil. CPMs run S$22–S$36.

**StarHub TV+ ** A major telco streaming platform combining local and international content, popular among Singaporean households. CPMs run S$24–S$38.

**Amazon Prime Video Singapore** Ad-supported tier with strong reach among Singapore's high-income, high-device-penetration households. CPMs run S$28–S$42.

**Pluto TV (via programmatic)** Free ad-supported streaming accessible through regional programmatic exchanges. CPMs run S$14–S$22.

## ASAS (Advertising Standards Authority of Singapore): Compliance for Singapore CTV Advertisers

**ASAS (Advertising Standards Authority of Singapore)** is the body local businesses need to understand before launching a CTV campaign in Singapore. Singapore's advertising self-regulatory body under the Consumers Association of Singapore. CTV ads must comply with the Singapore Code of Advertising Practice, with strict requirements around racial and religious sensitivity given Singapore's multicultural population, plus specific rules on health and financial product claims.

**VAT/tax note**: 9% GST. Any pricing claim in your creative should be VAT-inclusive to avoid compliance issues with the platform's ad review team.

## Local Business Sectors with Strong CTV Potential in Singapore

**Coffee shops** can use CTV's broadcast-quality production value to compete with national chains on the household screen — showing atmosphere, product, and address in a context viewers already trust.

**Fitness studios** benefit from CTV's ability to combine geographic and interest-based targeting, reaching engaged local audiences without the waste of broad social reach campaigns.

**Hair salons** and **restaurants** round out the local categories seeing the strongest early CTV adoption in Singapore, particularly when campaigns are scheduled around relevant seasonal demand windows.

## Budget Guidance for Singapore Small Businesses

- **Test campaign**: 4–6 weeks on the lowest-cost platform in your market (see table above) with national or single-city targeting. Designed to validate the channel before committing further budget.
- **Core local campaign**: 2–3x the test budget, running on the leading local broadcaster platform with city-level targeting and 2–3x weekly frequency per household.
- **Premium campaign**: Combine two platforms — a trusted local broadcaster app plus a global platform like Amazon Prime Video — with income or interest-based audience layering.
- **Seasonal burst**: A focused 4–5 week campaign timed to your business's highest-demand period of the year, with budget concentrated rather than spread evenly across 12 months.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does CTV advertising cost in Singapore?

Average CPMs run around S$28 across major platforms, though this varies by platform and targeting precision. Lower-cost FAST (free ad-supported streaming TV) platforms offer the cheapest entry point for businesses testing the channel for the first time.

### Do I need local-language creative for Singapore CTV campaigns?

It's not always a legal requirement, but it substantially improves performance. Localised references — neighbourhood names, regional cultural touchstones, or simply a native-accent voiceover — build the trust that makes broadcast-quality CTV worth the premium over cheaper digital formats.

### Is CTV better than social media ads for local businesses in Singapore?

They serve different purposes. CTV builds brand trust and awareness through broadcast-quality creative in a household context; social ads are better for direct response and granular retargeting. Most successful local campaigns in Singapore use CTV for awareness and pair it with Google Ads or Meta Ads for the direct-response layer.

### What's the minimum budget to start CTV advertising in Singapore?

Programmatic access through FAST platforms typically allows testing from a few hundred SGD per month — far below the five-figure minimums that traditional linear broadcast TV historically required.
