## What Is Local Targeting on TikTok?

Local targeting means showing your ads only to people in a specific geographic area — usually a few kilometers or miles from your business. It's perfect for:

- Driving foot traffic
- Promoting a local event
- Reaching past customers
- Testing new locations

TikTok allows you to target users by:

- **Location radius** (e.g., 1 mile, 10 miles)
- **Zip code or postal code**
- **City or region**
- **Points of Interest (POI)** like local shopping centers or parks

## How to Set Up Local Targeting in TikTok Ads

Here’s the step-by-step to set up local targeting for your TikTok ad campaign.

### Step 1: Choose the Right Campaign Objective

TikTok offers several campaign objectives, but for local businesses, the best options are:

- **App Installs** (if you have an app)
- **Clicks or Web Conversions**
- **Store Visits**
- **Engagement (e.g., Video Views)**

If you're running a promotion for in-store visits, **Store Visits** is the most powerful objective.

### Step 2: Set Your Targeting Radius

In the **Audience** section of your TikTok Ads Manager:

1. Click on **Location** under the **Demographics** tab
2. Select **Custom Location** (not the default "Everyone")
3. Enter your business address or choose a location radius from your business

You can also add **multiple locations** if you have more than one store.

> 💡 Pro tip: Start with a **1-mile radius** to test. Once you see performance, you can expand the radius to 3-5 miles if needed.

### Step 3: Add Layered Targeting

Local targeting is most effective when combined with other audience segments:

- **Age & Gender** (e.g., 18–45 years old, female)
- **Interest Segments** (e.g., "Coffee Lovers" or "Pet Owners")
- **Lookalike Audiences**
- **Custom Audiences** (upload customer email lists)

For example, one of our clients — a small coffee shop in Austin — used a 1-mile radius, targeting 25–45-year-olds who follow coffee influencers and live within 2 miles of their store.

## What to Avoid When Using TikTok for Local Targeting

Even with the best tools, there are common mistakes to avoid.

### Mistake 1: Over-Targeting

Some business owners go too narrow — targeting only a 0.5-mile radius — and end up with no impressions.

Start with **at least a 1-mile radius**, and adjust based on performance.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Schedule

TikTok users are most active in the evenings and on weekends. If you’re targeting local customers, run your ads during:

- **Weekdays 6 PM – 10 PM**
- **Saturdays 11 AM – 8 PM**
- **Sundays 12 PM – 6 PM**

This improves relevance and ad performance.

### Mistake 3: Not Using Geo-Fencing

Geo-fencing is not the same as location radius. It’s a more advanced tool that lets you target people who have visited a competitor’s location. TikTok allows this for:

- Competitor stores
- Local events
- Points of Interest

It's more advanced, but if you want to steal customers from nearby competitors, it's worth exploring.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Targeting Too Wide, Then Wondering Why Nobody Walks In

A boutique fitness studio in Austin, Texas — let’s call it “Sweat & Soul” — came to me after burning $1,200 in three weeks on TikTok ads. The owner was furious. She’d gotten 14,000 impressions and exactly zero class bookings.

I looked at her campaign. She’d set a 15-mile radius around her studio. That covers all of Austin, plus Cedar Park, Round Rock, and half of Kyle. Her ad was showing to people who’d never drive 40 minutes for a 6 AM spin class.

**The fix:** We dropped the radius to 2 miles. Then we added a “Points of Interest” layer targeting the three apartment complexes within walking distance, plus the two office parks within a 5-minute drive.

**The outcome:** In week one, she spent $380 and got 17 class bookings — $1,700 in revenue. Her cost per booking dropped from $400 to $22. She now runs three separate ad sets, each targeting a different POI cluster. Never went back to the 15-mile nonsense.

**Rule of thumb:** If you’re a foot-traffic business, your radius should be walkable or a short drive. Coffee shops: 1 mile. Hair salons: 3 miles. Pet groomers: 5 miles max. Anything wider and you’re paying to show ads to people who will never show up.

### Mistake #2: Using the Same Creative for Local and National Audiences

A pet groomer in Portland, Oregon spent $800 on a campaign that showed her standard “We groom all breeds” video to a 3-mile radius. She got clicks but no calls. The video was generic — a dog getting a bath, nice music, no mention of Portland or her shop name until the last two seconds.

TikTok users scroll fast. If they don’t know *why* this ad matters to them specifically, they’re gone.

**The fix:** We shot three 15-second videos. One showed her grooming a golden retriever outside her shop with the street sign visible. Second video: “Hey Portland — if your dog smells like a wet sock, come see us on Hawthorne.” Third video: a customer’s before-and-after with the shop’s neon sign in the backgroundcars parked out front.

**The outcome:** The “Hey Portland” video alone drove 23 appointment bookings in two weeks. Total spend: $450. Revenue from those bookings: $3,100. The generic video had produced zero appointments at twice the spend.

**Why it works:** Local targeting is wasted if your creative doesn’t scream “I am literally down the street from you.” Mention the neighborhood. Show the storefront. Use local landmarks. Make it impossible for someone to watch and think “this isn’t for me.”

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Retargeting — And Letting Warm Leads Go Cold

A hair salon in Denver ran a solid local campaign. They got 2,000 profile visits and 40 DM inquiries in a month. Then they stopped. No retargeting. No follow-up. No “hey you visited our page, here’s 10% off” ad.

Three months later, they started from scratch. Same spend. Same struggle.

**The fix:** I set up a TikTok retargeting pixel on their Booksy booking page. Anyone who visited the page but didn’t book got added to a 7-day retargeting audience. We served them a simple ad: the salon’s busy Saturday vibe, with text overlay: “Still thinking about that haircut? We saved you a chair.”

**The outcome:** Retargeting cost $0.18 per click vs. $0.85 for cold traffic. Within two weeks, the retargeting campaign booked 14 appointments worth $2,100. The salon now runs retargeting as a permanent campaign at $15/day. It’s their highest-ROI ad set.

**The lesson:** Local targeting gets them in the door. Retargeting gets them to actually open it.

### Mistake #4: Not Matching TikTok Campaigns With Google Business Profile

A coffee shop in Nashville spent $600 on TikTok ads promoting their new cold brew. The ad was great. But when people searched “Nashville coffee shop” on Google, the shop’s Google Business Profile showed “closed” (wrong hours), had no photos from the last year, and listed an old phone number.

**The fix:** We updated the Google Business Profile first. Added current hours, fresh photos of the cold brew, and a link to their online ordering system. Then we launched the TikTok campaign pointing to that same ordering page.

**The outcome:** The TikTok ad drove 200 clicks. 38 of those turned into online orders — $760 in cold brew sales. But the Google Business Profile updates alone (without the TikTok spend) had already increased walk-in traffic by 22% in the previous week. The two channels worked together. The TikTok ad was the spark. The Google profile was the landing strip.

**What you should do:** Before you spend a dollar on TikTok local ads, audit your Google Business Profile. Is the address correct? Hours accurate? Photos recent? Reviews responded to? If not, fix that first. Otherwise you’re paying to drive people to a closed door.

## How to Combine TikTok Local Ads With Other Platforms (Without Wasting Money)

### Google Ads + TikTok: The One-Two Punch

Most small business owners treat Google Ads and TikTok like competing channels. They’re not. They serve different stages of the same funnel.

Here’s the setup I’ve used for a Chicago pizza place:

**TikTok** runs a $35/day local awareness campaign targeting a 2-mile radius. The ad shows a 15-second video of a pizza being pulled from the oven, steam rising, cheese stretching. The call-to-action is “Order Now” — linking to their Square online ordering page.

**Google Ads** runs a $25/day “pizza near me” search campaign. It targets anyone searching for pizza delivery or takeout within that same 2-mile radius.

The TikTok ad creates the craving. The Google ad catches the person who then types “pizza delivery” into search.

**The numbers:** Over 8 weeks, TikTok drove 1,400 link clicks at $0.32 each. Google drove 220 clicks at $1.80 each. But the Google clicks had a 14% conversion rate vs. TikTok’s 3%. Combined, the two channels generated $11,200 in revenue on a $4,200 ad spend. Neither channel alone would have hit that number.

**The catch:** You need separate tracking. Use UTM parameters on the TikTok link and set up conversion tracking in Google Ads. Otherwise you’ll have no idea which channel actually made the sale.

### Email (Mailchimp) + TikTok: Retargeting Your Existing List

If you have an email list of past customers, you’re sitting on gold. TikTok lets you upload that list and create a “Custom Audience” — then show ads specifically to those people.

A yoga studio in Brooklyn did this. They had 1,800 email subscribers, mostly people who’d taken one class and never returned. They uploaded the list to TikTok, excluded current members, and ran a 7-day retargeting campaign offering “Your second class is free — bring a friend.”

**The numbers:** $280 spent. 43 new class bookings. $1,720 in revenue from the “bring a friend” passes that turned into memberships. The studio now runs this campaign every quarter.

**How to do it:** Export your email list from Mailchimp or your CRM. Upload it to TikTok Ads Manager under “Audiences” > “Custom Audience” > “Customer File.” TikTok will match the emails to user accounts. Then create an ad set targeting only that audience.

**One warning:** Don’t upload more than 10,000 emails at once unless you’re prepared for a high match rate. TikTok will find themcars.

### Yelp + TikTok: Closing the Trust Gap

TikTok ads can drive curiosity. Yelp reviews close the deal.

A dentist in Austin (different from the fitness studio) ran a $500 TikTok campaign promoting their new patient special: cleaning + exam for $99. The ad performed well — 12,000 views, 400 clicks. But only 8 people booked.

Why? The landing page had no social proof. No reviews. No testimonials.

**The fix:** We added a Yelp widget to the booking page showing their 4.8-star rating and three recent reviews. Then we ran a separate TikTok ad featuring a patient’s testimonial (with permission) talking about how nervous they were and how the dentist made them comfortable.

**The outcome:** The testimonial ad cost $320 and drove 22 bookings. The original ad with the Yelp widget added drove 14 bookings. Combined: 36 new patients worth $7,200 in revenue (cleaning + follow-up treatments).

**The lesson:** People trust other people more than they trust your ad. Put reviews front and center. If you don’t have reviews on Yelp or Google, start collecting them before you spend on ads.

## How to Measure What Actually Matters (Not Vanity Metrics)

Most small business owners look at the wrong numbers. They see 50,000 impressions and think “wow, that’s great.” Meanwhile their phone isn’t ringing.

Here’s what I actually track for local TikTok campaigns:

**Cost Per Visit (CPV)** — Not “cost per click.” A click could be someone accidentally tapping their screen. A visit is someone who lands on your website or booking page and stays for at least 5 seconds. TikTok’s pixel can track this. Set it up.

**Cost Per Booking/Call** — This is the only number that matters. Divide total spend by number of appointments, orders, or phone calls. For a coffee shop, that might be $2.50 per online order. For a hair salon, $15 per booking. If your CPB is higher than your average profit per customer, pause the campaign.

**Foot Traffic Lift** — If you have a physical location, track foot traffic before and during the campaign. A simple method: count customers for one week before the campaign, then count during the campaign week. The difference (minus any seasonal factors) is your lift. I’ve seen campaigns generate a 30-60% lift in walk-ins for coffee shops and bakeries.

**Return on Ad Spend (ROAS)** — Total revenue from campaign ÷ total ad spend. For local businesses, I aim for 3x minimum. 5x is good. 10x is exceptional. Anything below 2x means your targeting or creative needs work.

**What NOT to track:** Impressions (unless you’re doing brand awareness), video views (people watch, doesn’t mean they buy), and “engagement” (likes and comments are nice but don’t pay rent).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I’m a small business with a $500/month marketing budget. Is TikTok worth it, or should I stick with Google Ads?**

It depends on your business type. If you’re a service business with high-intent searches (plumber, dentist, locksmith), Google Ads will likely outperform TikTok at $500/month. If you’re a retail or hospitality business where impulse matters (coffee shop, boutique, bakery), TikTok can work better. I’ve seen a donut shop in Portland spend $300/month on TikTok and get 80+ orders. Same budget on Google got them 12 calls. Test both with $250 each for two weeks, then double down on the winner.

**Q: How long does it take for TikTok’s algorithm to optimize for local audiences?**

About 3-5 days. TikTok’s algorithm learns fast. Give it at least 50 conversions (clicks, visits, or bookings) before you judge performance. If you’re spending $30/day, that’s usually 4-7 days. Don’t kill a campaign after 24 hours — you haven’t given it enough data.

**Q: Can I target people who’ve visited my business before but haven’t come back?**

Yes. If you have their phone number or email (from a loyalty program, booking system, or POS), you can upload that list to TikTok as a Custom Audience. Then create an ad set targeting only those people. Exclude current customers. Offer a “we miss you” discount. I’ve seen this bring back 15-25% of lapsed customers within 30 days.

**Q: Do I need to show my face in the video? I’m camera-shy.**

No. You can show your product, your space, your team (with permission), or even a customer’s result (with permission). A pet groomer in Denver never appears on camera — she films the dogs before and after, with text overlays and music. Her ads perform better than her competitors’ talking-head videos. That said, face-to-camera videos tend to build trust faster. If you can stomach it, try one. If not, don’t force it.

**Q: What’s the minimum daily budget for TikTok local targeting to actually work?**

$20/day is the floor. $30-50/day is the sweet spot for most small businesses. Below $20/day, TikTok’s algorithm doesn’t have enough budget to optimize properly. You’ll get impressions but few conversions. Above $100/day, you risk oversaturating a small local audience. I’ve seen a salon in San Diego spend $150/day and hit the same 2,000 people five times in one week. That’s annoying, not effective.

**Q: Should I use TikTok’s “Promote” button or the full Ads Manager?**

Use the full Ads Manager. The “Promote” button is TikTok’s simplified version — it’s fine for boosting a single video to a broad audience, but it doesn’t give you local targeting, custom audiences, or conversion tracking. Ads Manager takes 20 minutes longer to set up. That 20 minutes will save you hundreds of dollars in wasted spend.
