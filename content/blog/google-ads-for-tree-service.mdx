## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Bidding on Broad Keywords and Ignoring Negatives

A tree service in Austin, TX—let’s call it Austin Tree Guys—launched a Google Ads campaign in early spring. The owner bid on “tree removal” as a broad match keyword. He got 230 clicks in two weeks and exactly zero booked jobs. When I looked at the search terms report, it was a graveyard: “how much does tree removal cost,” “tree removal near me cost,” “DIY tree removal,” “tree removal price list.” People were comparison shopping or watching YouTube tutorials, not hiring. He had spent $1,200 on clicks from people who were never going to call.

The fix was uncomfortable but simple. I added a negative keyword list: “cost,” “price,” “how to,” “DIY,” “free estimate” (yes, some searchers want free estimates and then ghost you), “per hour,” “equipment rental.” Then I changed the match type from broad to phrase match for core terms. For “storm damage tree removal,” I kept exact match. We also added “emergency” and “24/7” as high-intent positives.

Within two weeks, wasted spend dropped by 70%. Cost per lead went from $45 to $18. Austin Tree Guys started getting calls from homeowners with actual fallen branches. The owner told me, “I thought broad match was casting a wider net. It was casting a net full of holes.”

**Takeaway:** Check your search terms report every three days during storm season. Add negatives like it’s your job. It is.

### Mistake #2: Sending All Ads to a Generic Homepage

Portland Tree Pros in Portland, OR had a clean homepage: company story, service list, a photo of a guy holding a chainsaw. But every Google ad—whether for stump grinding, emergency storm cleanup, or residential pruning—went to the same page. Their conversion rate hovered below 1%. They were paying $6 per click to send people to a page that didn’t match their intent.

I built a standalone landing page specifically for storm damage cleanup. It had:
- A headline that matched the ad: “Storm Damage? We’ll Clear It Today.”
- A list of common storm scenarios (broken limbs, leaning trees, power line clearance).
- Two buttons: “Call Now” and a short form (name, phone, address).
- A photo of a crew working in actual Portland weather (grey, wet, dramatic).

We ran a two-week A/B test: homepage vs. landing page. The landing page converted at 8.2% vs. 0.9% on the homepage. In the first week, Portland Tree Pros got 15 leads from that page. Three turned into jobs worth a total of $3,800. The page cost $150 to build using Unbounce and a stock photo.

**Takeaway:** If your ad says “storm damage cleanup” and your landing page says “Welcome to Our Tree Service,” you’re burning money. Match the message exactly.

### Mistake #3: Not Tracking Phone Calls

A tree service in Nashville, TN—Nashville Canopy—was spending $2,000 a month on Google Ads. The owner told me he was busy, phones were ringing, so it must be working. But he couldn’t tell me which calls came from the ads, which from his yard sign, and which from his Yelp page.

I set up call tracking using CallRail. We added a Google forwarding number to the ad extension, a different number on the website, and another on the Yelp profile. In 30 days, the data was ugly: 40% of the calls tagged as “from Google Ads” were actually from Yelp. The actual call volume from ads was half what he thought. Meanwhile, his Yelp Ad spend was low but generating better quality leads.

We shifted $500 from the Google Ads budget to Yelp Ads, and adjusted the Google campaign to focus on high-intent emergency keywords. Within a month, Nashville Canopy’s cost per booked job from Google Ads dropped from $120 to $65. Overall lead volume stayed flat, but revenue per lead increased because the Yelp callers had higher average job sizes ($1,200 vs. $850).

**Takeaway:** If you’re not tracking calls separately by source, you’re flying blind. Spend the $30/month on a call tracking tool. It will pay for itself in the first week.

### Mistake #4: Running the Same Bids All Year

Denver Tree Works in Denver, CO kept their max CPC at $5.75 all year. During the quiet months (January–March), that was fine. But when spring storms hit in April–May, competitors in Denver ramped up bids to $8–10 for “emergency tree removal.” Denver Tree Works got shoved to the bottom of the auction. Their impression share dropped to 18%.

The fix: I set up a seasonal bid adjustment. From April 1 through June 15, we increased max CPC by 40% for “storm” and “emergency” keywords. We also added dayparting: 7 AM–10 AM and 4 PM–7 PM got a +25% bid boost because those were the hours homeowners noticed damage on their way to work or home.

Result: impression share jumped to 62% during storm season. Cost per click actually went down 25% because the higher bids improved quality score (Google rewards relevance and expected CTR). Denver Tree Works booked an extra 12 storm jobs over the six-week period, totaling $14,400 in revenue. The extra spend was $1,800. Net win.

**Takeaway:** Static bids are lazy. Google Ads is a live auction. If you don’t raise your hand when everyone else does, you’ll lose the table.

## How to Use Ad Extensions to Steal the Lead

Ad extensions are free real estate that Google gives you right below your text ad. Most tree service companies ignore them. That’s a mistake. I’ve seen a single callout extension increase CTR by 15% for a tree service in Chicago.

Here’s what to set up, and in what order of priority:

**1. Call extensions** – A clickable phone number right in the ad. Mobile users tap to call. Desktop users see the number. Use a call tracking number so you can attribute the lead. A tree service in Chicago added this and saw 30% more phone calls in the first week.

**2. Callout extensions** – Short phrases that highlight differentiators. Example: “24/7 Emergency Service,” “Free Estimates,” “Licensed & Insured,” “Same-Day Response.” I worked with a tree service in Nashville that added “Storm Damage Specialists” in callouts during severe weather. Their click-through rate on that ad group hit 8% vs. the usual 3%.

**3. Structured snippets** – These let you list services in a grid. Choose “Service” as the header, then list: Tree Removal, Stump Grinding, Storm Cleanup, Pruning, Emergency Response. Google shows them as a tidy list. It makes your ad look bigger and more credible.

**4. Location extensions** – If you have a physical address (even if it’s a yard or office), link your Google Business Profile. The ad shows your address and a “Get Directions” link. For a tree service covering multiple towns, use location extensions with radius targeting. A company in Portland set a 30-mile radius and saw 20% more calls from people who wanted same-day service.

**5. Price extensions** – Only use these if you have fixed prices. For example, “Tree Removal – Starting at $350.” Be careful: if your prices vary wildly by job, this can backfire. I’ve seen it work for stump grinding ($75–$150) but not for emergency storm cleanup.

**One pro move:** For storm season, create a separate ad group with “Storm Damage Cleanup” in the headline and add callout extensions like “Crew on Standby” and “Insurance Claim Help.” Pair it with a call extension that goes to a dedicated storm hotline. A tree service in Denver did this and saw a 50% higher conversion rate on storm ads vs. their generic ads.

**Tools to help:** Google Ads itself offers a “recommendations” tab that will suggest extensions. But I don’t trust Google’s default suggestions—they often recommend every extension under the sun. Pick three or four that match how your customers actually decide. Then test one change per week.

## The One Thing Most Tree Services Forget: Google Business Profile Integration

You’re running Google Ads. But are you syncing them with your Google Business Profile (GBP)? Most tree services don’t. They treat them as separate worlds. That’s a leaky bucket.

Here’s why it matters: When someone searches “tree removal near me,” Google shows a Local Map Pack with three businesses. If you have a fully optimized GBP with reviews, photos, and updated hours, you can appear in that pack *for free*. Your Google Ads can appear above it or beside it. But if your GBP is missing or half-baked, you lose that free visibility.

I consulted for a tree service in Austin that spent $1,500/month on Google Ads but had a GBP listing with three old photos and a generic “Tree Service” category. No reviews posted in six months. They were paying for clicks while free local traffic went to competitors.

We cleaned up their GBP:
- Added categories: Tree Service, Arborist, Landscaping.
- Uploaded 12 photos (before/after storm cleanup, crew photos, equipment).
- Added a service menu with prices (e.g., “Storm Damage Cleanup – Call for Quote”).
- Encouraged recent customers to leave reviews (we used a Mailchimp email blast with a direct review link).
- Set the primary phone number to match the call tracking number used in Google Ads.

Within three weeks, their GBP appeared in the map pack for 40% of relevant local searches. Organic calls went up about 15%. The surprising part: these organic leads had a higher conversion rate than ad leads because they had already seen the business’s reviews and photos. The client was spending $1,500 on ads to get leads that cost $60 each. The organic GBP leads cost $0.

**The real win:** You can export your Google Ads location extension data and cross-reference it with your GBP insights. If you see a lot of clicks coming from an area where your GBP is weak, fix it. A tree service in Portland did this and realized their GBP was missing the “emergency” service area. After adding it, they got an extra 8 calls per week without spending a dollar more.

**Tools you need:** Google Business Profile Manager (free), CallRail (to track calls), and a simple email tool like Mailchimp to automate review requests. If you’re using Square for payments, you can even set up a review request after invoicing.

One warning: Do not stuff fake reviews. Google will ban your profile. But you already know that, because you run a real business.

## How to Match Your Budget to Storm Season Reality

I’ve seen tree service owners set a monthly budget of $500 and wonder why they only get two leads. Or they set $5,000 and burn through it in a week with bad targeting. Here’s the framework I use with clients:

**1. Know your break-even cost per lead.** If your average job is $800 and your margin is 40%, you can afford to spend up to $320 to get a job. But aim for less. I tell clients: if your cost per lead (CPL) exceeds 20% of your average job value, you’re overpaying. For an $800 job, that’s $160 per lead.

**2. Scale up when CPL drops.** A tree service in Chicago started at $500/month. Their CPL was $95. Good, not great. After refining keywords and negatives, CPL dropped to $55. At that point, I recommended doubling the budget to $1,000. They did, and volume doubled. CPL stayed at $55. They were getting 18 leads a month for $1,000.

**3. Use storm season as a multiplier.** In calm months, you might need $1,000/month to keep your crew busy. In storm season, you can afford to spend more because demand spikes and you can charge premium prices. I worked with a tree service in Nashville that normally spent $800/month. During a spring storm surge, they increased to $2,500/month for six weeks. Their cost per lead actually dropped because of higher search volume and better quality score from relevant landing pages. They booked $38,000 in revenue from those six weeks.

**4. Don’t set it and forget it.** Google Ads will happily spend your budget on low-quality clicks at 3 AM. Set a daily cap. Use dayparting (9 AM–7 PM, local time). For emergency services, keep ads on 24/7 but use a lower bid overnight unless you have a 24/7 dispatch.

**5. Consider a two-budget approach.** One budget for “normal” tree service (pruning, removal, stump grinding) and one for “storm emergency.” The storm budget gets higher bids, call extensions, and a dedicated landing page. The normal budget is more conservative. A tree service in Austin used this method and saw their overall ROAS go from 3:1 to 6:1.

**Real numbers:** A client in Portland started at $350/month. After three months of tweaks, they scaled to $1,200/month. Their average CPL was $42. At $42, with an average job value of $950, they were getting a 22:1 return. That’s not typical, but it’s possible if your landing page and ad copy are tight.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Will Google Ads work for a small town with only 10,000 people?**  
Yes, but with a smaller radius and tighter keywords. A tree service in a town of 12,000 in central Illinois runs Google Ads with a 15-mile radius and spends $400/month. They get 8–12 leads per month. The key is to use exact match keywords like “tree service [town name]” and exclude nearby cities that are too far for you to drive. Also, leverage your Google Business Profile—in small towns, the map pack is often more effective than paid ads.

**Q: How much should I spend per day as a starting budget?**  
Start at $20–$30 per day. That’s roughly $600–$900 per month. If your CPL is below $100 after two weeks, you can increase. If it’s above $100, pause and fix your keywords, landing page, or targeting before scaling. I’ve seen a $20/day budget generate 10 leads a month with good setup.

**Q: Do I really need a dedicated landing page? Can’t I just use my website’s contact page?**  
You can, but it will cost you. I tested this with a client in Denver: the contact page converted at 1.2%, while a storm-specific landing page converted at 7.5%. That means for every 100 clicks, the contact page gets you 1.2 leads, and the landing page gets you 7.5 leads. If you’re paying $6 per click, that’s $500 vs. $80 per lead. The landing page costs $150–$300 to build. It pays for itself in the first three leads.

**Q: What if I don’t want to manage the ads myself?**  
Then hire someone who specializes in local service ads, not a generalist. Ask them for a case study from a tree service or home service business. Expect to pay $500–$1,000 per month for management, plus ad spend. A good manager will save you that much in wasted spending within the first month. I’ve seen clients double their leads after firing a general agency and hiring a niche specialist.

**Q: How long until I see results?**  
You’ll see clicks and calls within hours after the ads go live. But meaningful data takes 7–14 days. Google needs time to gather conversion data and optimize. In the first week, you might get high CPL. That’s normal. Don’t panic. Make small changes every 3 days (adjust keywords, pause poor performers). By week three, you should have a clear picture of your CPL and conversion rate. If it’s still high, it’s a sign your landing page or keyword strategy needs a redo.

**Q: Can I just use boosted posts on Facebook instead?**  
You can, but Facebook is less effective for urgent, location-based needs. People don’t go to Facebook to find someone to remove a fallen tree at 3 PM. They Google it. Facebook works for building brand awareness and retargeting past customers. I recommend a dual approach: Google Ads for immediate leads, Facebook for nurturing and seasonal reminders. But if you only have $500, put it on Google Ads first. I tested this with a tree service in Portland: $500 on Google Ads generated 11 leads; $500 on Facebook boosted post generated 2 leads and 37 comments from people’s neighbors.
