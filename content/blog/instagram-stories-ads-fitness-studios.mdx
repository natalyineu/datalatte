## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Running Ads to Everyone Within 50 Miles

**The story:** A hot yoga studio in Austin, Texas, spent $1,200 in June on Instagram Stories Ads targeting anyone within a 50-mile radius. The owner figured more reach meant more bodies in class. After 30 days, they'd gotten 14,000 impressions, 182 link taps, and exactly zero new members. Their cost per swipe-up was $6.59 — and every single one of those taps bounced because the ad showed a "Sweat & Flow" class that launched at 6 AM on a Tuesday.

**What went wrong:** 50 miles around Austin covers Round Rock, Cedar Park, Kyle, and parts of San Antonio. That's 2.5 million people, most of whom will never drive 45 minutes for a 6 AM class. The ad also made no mention of the start time. People clicked, saw the schedule, and closed the page.

**The fix:** I had them rebuild the audience to a 3-mile radius centered on the studio's address. Then they created three separate ad sets — one for morning classes, one for evening, one for weekend workshops. Each ad showed the actual start time, the instructor's name, and a "first class free" offer that expired in 48 hours.

**The outcome:** Same $1,200 budget the next month produced 17 new members. That's $70 per acquisition versus $0 returns. Their monthly recurring revenue from those 17 people — assuming an average $89/month membership and three-month retention — came to $4,539 over the first quarter. The original campaign generated exactly zero.

### Mistake #2: Using Still Photos from Your Phone

**The story:** A CrossFit gym in Denver ran Stories Ads using four photos of their equipment and one blurry shot of someone doing deadlifts. The owner had taken them on an iPhone during the 5 PM rush. The ad copy said "Join Denver's toughest box!" The campaign ran for two weeks at $500 total. They got 47 link taps and one person who signed up for a trial — then no-showed.

**What went wrong:** The photos looked like a Craigslist listing. No faces, no movement, no sense of what it actually feels like to be in that gym. Instagram Stories is a video-first format. Still images get scrolled past. The ad also used the word "box" — which CrossFit people understand but new members read as "a cardboard container."

**The fix:** I told the owner to film three 15-second clips using vertical video. One clip showed the 6 AM crew finishing a WOD — sweaty, smiling, high-fiving. Another showed a coach correcting someone's squat form. The third was a simple talking-head from the owner saying "Show up tomorrow at 6 AM. Your first class is on me. Click the link to claim it." Total filming time: 20 minutes. No editing software needed.

**The outcome:** With the same $500 budget, they got 312 link taps and 14 trial sign-ups. Nine of those converted to full memberships within two weeks. The owner later told me that campaign alone paid for two months of rent. Cost per acquisition dropped from $500 to $55.

### Mistake #3: Running Ads Without a Proper Landing Page

**The story:** A Pilates studio in Nashville bought a "new member special" Instagram Stories Ad — $49 for your first month. The owner designed the ad in Canva, linked directly to their generic website homepage, and spent $800 over two weeks. Thirty-three people clicked. Two signed up. The owner was ready to swear off Instagram forever.

**What went wrong:** The homepage didn't mention the $49 offer anywhere. When someone clicked, they landed on a page with class schedules, pricing tiers, instructor bios, and a contact form. No link to claim the deal. No urgency. No way to book the $49 trial without digging through three pages. Most people left within eight seconds.

**The fix:** I had them set up a dedicated landing page using Carrd (free, takes 20 minutes). The page had:
- A headline that matched the ad exactly: "Your First Month of Pilates — $49"
- A photo of the studio's actual reformer machines
- A single button: "Claim Your $49 Month"
- The button linked directly to their Mindbody booking page with the offer pre-applied

**The outcome:** That $800 budget generated 24 new members at $49 each for the first month — $1,176 in immediate revenue. But the real number was the retention: eighteen of those 24 stuck around for full-price month two at $129/month. That's $2,322 in month-two revenue alone, meaning the ad essentially returned $3,498 over 60 days on an $800 spend. The original homepage campaign returned $98.

## Why Your Ad Creative Needs to Show the Actual Experience

Most fitness studios make the same mistake: they sell the *idea* of getting fit instead of the *feeling* of being in their studio. I saw a barre studio in Portland, Oregon, run an ad with a stock photo of a woman in yoga pants looking at a sunset. The caption said "Find your strength." It got one click in three days.

They replaced it with a 10-second clip of a real class — someone laughing after losing balance, the instructor clapping, the music playing. Same budget. Different result: 84 clicks, 12 trial sign-ups.

Here's the hard truth: people don't sign up for "fitness." They sign up for a specific room, with specific people, at a specific time. Your Instagram Stories Ad needs to show them that room. Film the 9:15 AM crowd on a Tuesday. Film someone struggling through the last rep. Film the owner saying "see you tomorrow" at the door.

Cost to film this: zero dollars, assuming you have a phone and one employee who can hold it steady for 90 seconds. You don't need a videographer. You don't need a ring light. You need real people doing real things in your actual space.

I ran an A/B test for a bootcamp in Chicago. Ad A was a polished graphic with class times and pricing. Ad B was a 12-second clip of the 5:30 AM class finishing burpees — shaky, loud, imperfect. Ad B outperformed Ad A by 340% on swipe-ups and 280% on conversions. The "ugly" video generated $3,800 in new membership revenue over three weeks.

Stop polishing. Start filming.

## Retargeting People Who Almost Signed Up

The biggest waste of ad spend I see is the one-hit wonder approach. A small business spends $500, gets 100 clicks, three sign-ups, and calls it a failure. But what about the 97 people who clicked but didn't book?

They were interested. They were *this close*. Then they got distracted, or the page didn't load, or they wanted to ask their partner, or they forgot. Those people are not lost. They're waiting.

I worked with a yoga studio in Brooklyn that ran a "first class free" Stories Ad. It got 400 clicks and 11 bookings. The owner was happy — $36 per booking. I asked if she'd retargeted the other 389 clickers. She hadn't.

Here's the fix: Set up a Facebook Pixel on your website. It takes maybe 10 minutes using the Facebook Pixel helper extension for Chrome. Then create a retargeting ad specifically for people who clicked your previous ad but didn't book. The ad copy should be straightforward: "You checked us out. Your free class is still waiting. Book it this week."

The Brooklyn studio spent $150 on retargeting — three days, showing the ad only to those 389 people. Twelve more people booked. That's a cost per acquisition of $12.50, compared to the original $36. Total additional revenue from those 12 people — assuming a 40% conversion to membership at $99/month — comes to roughly $475 in month-one revenue and $1,900 over six months.

You don't need to reinvent your offer for retargeting. You just need to remind people that they were already interested. Most studios skip this step because they don't know it exists. That's fine — means more air for you.

To set this up: go to your Facebook Ads Manager, create a new campaign, choose "traffic" or "conversions," and under Audience, select "Custom Audience." Choose "Website" and then "People who visited specific pages." Enter the URL of your booking page. Set the window to 30 days. Exclude anyone who completed the booking. That's it.

If you're using Booksy or Mindbody, both allow you to install a Facebook Pixel directly. Square also offers this integration. Total setup time for a non-tech person: about 30 minutes, including YouTube tutorials.

## Integrating Your Booking System with Your Ads

Here's something most guides skip: your Instagram Stories Ad is only as good as the booking link it leads to. If someone has to click through five pages to book, they'll leave. If your booking system doesn't load on mobile, they'll leave. If they have to create an account before they can see pricing, they'll leave.

I've tested this across fifteen different studios. The studios using direct booking links in their ads (meaning: click the ad, see a class time, book in two taps) had conversion rates between 8% and 15%. Studios that sent people to a generic "view our schedule" page had conversion rates around 2%.

A CrossFit gym in Seattle was using a Wix site with an embedded Google Calendar. People clicked the ad, landed on the calendar, then had to email to book. The owner told me "it worked fine." It wasn't working fine. We swapped their ad link to a Booksy booking page where someone could pick a class, enter their name and phone, and get a confirmation in under 30 seconds. Same ad. Same budget. Bookings went from 14 to 47 in one month.

The tools I see work best for fitness studios:
- **Booksy**: $30/month, integrates with Instagram, allows same-day bookings, sends automatic reminders (reduces no-shows by 30-40%)
- **Mindbody**: more expensive ($129/month) but has better reporting, allows package sales, integrates with Mailchimp for email follow-ups
- **Square Appointments**: free for the basic plan, integrates with Square payments, works well if you already use Square for other transactions

Whichever you use, test the mobile booking flow yourself. Open your ad on your phone. Click the link. Try to book a class. If it takes more than four taps or requires a password, fix it. That friction is costing you members.

I watched a Pilates studio in Los Angeles lose 60% of their ad clicks because their booking page required a credit card before showing available times. They changed it to show times first, ask for payment at confirmation. Their conversion rate went from 1.8% to 6.2%. That's the difference between spending $500 to get four members versus spending $500 to get thirteen members.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I tried Instagram ads once and got zero sign-ups. What did I do wrong?**

Almost certainly one of three things: your audience was too broad (you targeted everyone within 20 miles instead of the 3-mile radius where people actually drive), your offer was unclear (a generic "try us" doesn't work — "free first class" does), or your booking link was broken. I've tested this at five different studios and the problem is almost never "Instagram doesn't work." It's always one of these three. Fix them one at a time and test again with $100 before you spend more.

**Q: Do I need a big budget? What's the minimum I should spend?**

I won't let a client spend less than $100 for a three-day test. Below that, you don't get enough data to know what's working. For a real campaign that produces results, budget $400-$800 per month. That sounds like a lot, but if one new member pays $99/month and stays for six months, you need roughly four new members per month to break even. Every member after that is profit. A Denver barre studio I work with spends $600/month and gets 12-15 new members. That's a monthly return of roughly $1,200 to $1,500 in recurring revenue.

**Q: How do I know if someone actually signed up because of my ad?**

Set up a UTM parameter on your ad link. It's a short code you add to the URL that tells Google Analytics where the traffic came from. Go to Google's Campaign URL Builder, paste your booking link, add "utm_source=instagram" and "utm_medium=stories" and "utm_campaign=june2025". Use that link in your ad. Then in Google Analytics, you can see exactly how many bookings came from that specific campaign. If you don't use Google Analytics, at least ask new members "how did you hear about us?" and track it in a spreadsheet. The studios that do this systematically get better results because they stop spending money on what doesn't work.

**Q: Can I run these ads myself, or do I need to hire someone?**

You can absolutely do this yourself. I taught a hair salon owner in Austin to run Stories Ads in one hour. The key is keeping it simple: one audience (3-mile radius), one clear offer ("free first class"), one direct booking link, and a 15-second video shot on your phone. Do not try to run five different campaigns with three different audiences. Start with one. Run it for three days. Look at the numbers. Adjust. If you have $500/month to spend, spend all of it on one well-executed campaign instead of spreading it across five mediocre ones.

**Q: What if I'm a one-person studio and don't have time for this?**

Film the video during your slowest class on a Tuesday. It takes 90 seconds. Setting up the ad takes 20 minutes the first time, then 10 minutes after that to duplicate and adjust. The biggest time waste is overthinking. Most studio owners I've worked with spend more time worrying about whether the ad is perfect than it would take to just launch the damn thing. Launch an imperfect ad, see what happens, then improve it. A mediocre ad that's live is better than a perfect ad that's still in your drafts.

**Q: Should I run ads on TikTok instead? Everyone says that's where the audience is.**

TikTok can work, but for a local fitness studio, Instagram has better targeting for location-based ads. TikTok's audience is broader and harder to pin down to a 3-mile radius. I've seen studios waste $1,000 on TikTok ads and get viral views but zero local sign-ups. If you have money to experiment, sure, try TikTok. But if you need to fill classes next week, Instagram Stories Ads with location targeting will outperform TikTok every time for a local business. The one exception is if your studio teaches something visually unique that has viral potential — aerial yoga, trampoline fitness, that kind of thing. For standard Pilates, barre, CrossFit, yoga, or strength training? Instagram.
