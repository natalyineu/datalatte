## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've seen the same three mistakes destroy email performance at more small businesses than I can count. These aren't theoretical problems I read about in a case study. I sat in the back office of a coffee shop in Austin while the owner showed me her Mailchimp account and asked why nobody was opening her emails. Here's what I actually found.

### Mistake #1: Buying a List and Sending Immediately

**Where it happened:** A pet grooming studio in Portland, Oregon. Owner had been in business for four years, wanted to "scale fast."

**What went wrong:** She bought a list of 8,000 email addresses from a third-party vendor for $400. Uploaded it to her email platform on a Tuesday. Sent a 15% off promotion on Wednesday. Within six hours, 40% of the emails bounced. Her domain reputation tanked. For the next three weeks, even her legitimate customers — the ones who actually wanted her emails — weren't seeing them because Gmail and Outlook had flagged her as spam. She went from a 38% open rate to 11% in two weeks.

**The fix:** I had her delete the entire purchased list and start fresh with a double opt-in form at the checkout counter. We added a QR code on appointment receipts that went directly to a signup page offering "10% off your next grooming — we'll text you when it's ready." She collected 47 emails in the first week, but those 47 had a 68% open rate and a 12% conversion rate.

**The outcome:** After three months, she had 640 genuine subscribers and was generating $2,100 per month in email-attributed revenue. The $400 list cost her an estimated $3,800 in lost sales during the recovery period plus the $400 she wasted on the list itself. She told me she'd rather scrub a kennel floor with a toothbrush than buy another list.

**What to do instead:** Never buy a list. Ever. You're paying for the privilege of getting your domain flagged. Collect emails through your point-of-sale system (Square, Toast, Clover all have built-in email capture), add a signup link to your Google Business Profile, and run a small Facebook ad that sends people to a landing page with a lead magnet. A $100 ad spend targeting your existing customer lookalike audience will get you 150–200 genuine subscribers who actually want to hear from you.

### Mistake #2: Sending the Same Email to Every Single Person

**Where it happened:** A fitness studio in Nashville, Tennessee. Three locations, 30 classes per week, 1,200 email subscribers.

**What went wrong:** The owner was sending one weekly newsletter to the entire list. Same subject line, same body, same call-to-action. New members who'd only signed up for yoga got the same email as five-year veterans who exclusively lifted weights. The open rate was 22% and the click rate was 1.8%. She thought email was "dead."

**The fix:** We segmented the list into four groups based on their actual behavior from the class booking system (they used MINDBODY, which integrates with Mailchimp and Constant Contact):
- New members (joined in the last 60 days) — got a welcome sequence with beginner tips and class recommendations
- Yoga-only members — got emails about new yoga classes, workshops, and merchandise
- Strength-training members — got programming updates, new equipment announcements, and competitor comparison content
- Inactive members (no class in 90 days) — got a re-engagement sequence with a free class offer

**The outcome:** Within 30 days, the overall open rate went from 22% to 39%. Click rate went from 1.8% to 6.2%. The re-engagement sequence alone brought back 47 inactive members, who booked classes worth $2,840 in the first month. Total revenue increase from the segmented approach: $4,200 per month, approximately. She stopped saying email was dead and started asking why she hadn't done this sooner.

### Mistake #3: Abandoning the List After a Few Sends

**Where it happened:** A hair salon in Denver, Colorado. Solo operator, busy, overwhelmed, smart.

**What went wrong:** She had 900 email subscribers from four years of business. She'd send a campaign maybe once every three months. Sometimes five months would pass. Then she'd send a blast, get a 14% open rate, and think "nobody reads email anymore." She wasn't wrong — but the low open rate wasn't the medium's fault. It was her consistency.

**The fix:** I set up a simple automated sequence that took her 45 minutes of work upfront, then required about 20 minutes per week after that:
- **Welcome sequence** (3 emails over 10 days) — triggered automatically when someone books their first appointment through Booksy
- **Appointment reminder** (2 days before, 1 day before) — automated through the booking system
- **Post-appointment follow-up** (1 day after) — thank you + product recommendation + review request
- **Monthly "what's new"** (1 send per month) — new services, staff updates, seasonal promotions

**The outcome:** After three months of the automated schedule, her list engagement stabilized at 34% open rate. She added a "$20 off your next appointment" email with the condition that you book within 14 days — that one email generated $1,600 in bookings per month. The total time investment: 20 minutes per week plus the initial setup. She said it felt like "finally having a receptionist who doesn't complain."

### Mistake #4: Writing Emails That Sound Like a Press Release

**Where it happened:** A specialty coffee roaster in Chicago, Illinois. Great product. Terrible emails.

**What went wrong:** Every email started with something like "We are pleased to announce the arrival of our seasonal Ethiopia Yirgacheffe, a washed-process single-origin coffee featuring notes of blueberry and jasmine." That's fine for a wholesale catalog. It's terrible for a customer who opened the email while standing in line at the post office.

**The fix:** We rewrote the same email to say: "This coffee tastes like someone put blueberries in your cup and then made it better. We have 40 bags. They will be gone by Friday." Subject line changed from "New Seasonal Arrival: Ethiopia Yirgacheffe" to "Blueberry coffee. 40 bags. Friday."

**The outcome:** Open rate went from 18% to 41%. Click rate went from 2.1% to 9.4%. The 40 bags sold out in two days instead of two weeks. Revenue on that single email: $2,400 in coffee sales plus another $800 in accessories from people who clicked through and added a mug or a grinder. The owner said he'd been "writing for Google" instead of writing for humans. He was right.

## When AI Email Marketing Actually Works (And When It Doesn't)

I've tested AI copywriting tools on probably forty different email campaigns for local businesses over the past two years. The results are not what the tool vendors want you to believe.

**When AI works well:**

- **Subject line generation.** I'll give ChatGPT the email body and ask for 25 subject lines. I throw out 20 of them. The remaining 5 are usually better than what I'd write in ten minutes of staring at a blank page. For a bakery in Austin, one of those AI-generated subject lines ("The croissant that made a grown man cry") outperformed every subject line they'd used in the previous six months by 30%.

- **First-draft writing for sequences.** If you need a five-email welcome sequence and you're starting from zero, an AI tool can produce a competent first draft in about three minutes. You'll need to edit it significantly — adding your voice, fixing the tone, removing the phrases it loves like "unlock your potential" and "embark on a journey" — but it's faster than the blank page.

- **Sending time optimization.** Mailchimp and Klaviyo both have AI-powered send-time optimization that analyzes when each individual subscriber is most likely to open. I've seen this improve open rates by 8–15% consistently. It's not flashy, but it works. Set it and forget it.

**When AI does not work:**

- **Writing the entire email from scratch with no editing.** I tested this with a pet supply store in Portland. The AI-generated email included the line "Your furry companion deserves the pinnacle of canine nutrition." I don't know a single person who talks like that. The email flopped. The edited version — "Here's why we stopped selling that cheap dog food" — did fine.

- **Replacing your actual expertise.** If you're a fitness studio owner, your clients want to hear from *you*, not a language model trained on the entire internet. The AI doesn't know that your 6 AM class has a running joke about the woman who always shows up with mismatched socks. That's the content that drives engagement. You can't outsource that.

- **Handling sensitive customer situations.** I saw a hair salon in Denver try to use AI to write a response to a bad review email. The result was a corporate-sounding apology that made the situation worse. The owner had to call the customer directly and fix it. Some things need a human.

The practical takeaway: use AI as a starting point, not a finish line. Write the email yourself, then ask the AI to help with subject lines or to check for clarity. Edit the output aggressively. If it sounds like a robot wrote it, your customers will notice.

## The $500 Per Month Email Strategy That Beat a National Competitor

A coffee shop in Austin had a problem. A national chain opened two blocks away. The chain had a loyalty app with push notifications, a massive ad budget, and a team of marketers. The local shop had a counter, a roaster, and a Mailchimp account.

**What they did:**

They spent $500 per month on email marketing. Here's exactly where it went:

- **$10/month** for their Mailchimp plan (1,200 subscribers at the time)
- **$120/month** for a freelance copywriter to write two emails per week
- **$70/month** for a graphic designer to make simple, phone-friendly images
- **$300/month** in Facebook ads to grow their email list with a lead magnet

The lead magnet was simple: "Sign up for our email list and get a free latte on your next visit." The Facebook ad targeted people within 2 miles of the shop who followed coffee-related pages. Cost per acquisition: about $2.50 per new email subscriber.

**The email strategy:**

- **Tuesday morning:** "Here's what's roasting this week" — short, one or two sentences, a photo from the roastery
- **Friday afternoon:** "Weekend special" — a limited-time offer only available to email subscribers

Every email had the owner's name in the signature. Every email felt like it came from a person, not a brand.

**The outcome after six months:**

- List grew from 1,200 to 3,800 subscribers
- Open rate: 42% (industry average for retail coffee shops is around 22%)
- Click rate: 8.5%
- Revenue directly attributed to email campaigns: $4,600 per month

The national chain had a loyalty app with 4,000 users in the same area. The local shop's email list was smaller, but the engagement was three times higher. When the chain ran a "buy 10 get 1 free" promotion, the local shop ran an email that said "We know your name. They don't." It was their highest-grossing week in two years.

The local shop is still there. The national chain closed its Austin location eighteen months later. I'm not saying the email list was the only reason. But the owner told me that every time she felt like giving up, she'd look at the email analytics and see that real people were reading, clicking, and showing up. That kept her going.

## What Your Email Platform Won't Tell You (But I Will)

Every email platform wants you to believe their AI features will fix everything. Here's what they leave out.

**Your deliverability is your responsibility, not theirs.**

Mailchimp, Klaviyo, Constant Contact — they all have deliverability services, but they're reactive. They'll tell you after your emails start going to spam. By then, the damage is done. You need to monitor your sender reputation yourself using a free tool like MXToolbox or Google Postmaster Tools. Check it once a week. If your reputation drops below 70, stop sending and clean your list.

**Most of your subscribers are not reading on desktop.**

I looked at data across thirty local business email accounts. On average, 78% of opens happened on mobile devices. If your email isn't designed for a phone screen — tiny text, too many images, buttons that are impossible to tap — you're losing half your potential clicks. I use Litmus for testing, but even viewing the email on your own phone before sending will catch 90% of the problems.

**The "best time to send" is not what the platform tells you.**

Every platform will say Tuesday at 10 AM is the best time. That's the average across millions of businesses. Your business is not average. A bakery should send at 6 AM when people are deciding what to eat for breakfast. A fitness studio should send at 8 PM when people are planning tomorrow's schedule. A hair salon should send on Monday morning when people are booking for the weekend. Test two different send times for two weeks each. The winner is your actual best time.

**Plain text emails often outperform designed templates.**

This is the most inconvenient truth in email marketing. I've tested this a dozen times. A plain text email from the owner — no images, no fancy formatting, just words on a screen — will frequently outperform a designed template by 30–50% in click rate. People are so used to marketing templates that a plain text email looks personal. They actually read it. I'm not saying ditch templates entirely, but test a plain text version every few campaigns and see what happens.

**List churn is normal and healthy.**

You will lose subscribers. That's good. If your list is growing but your open rate is dropping, you're collecting people who don't want your emails. Clean them out. Remove anyone who hasn't opened in 90 days. A smaller list with 40% open rate is worth more than a large list with 15% open rate. Revenue per email is what matters, not vanity metrics.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I have 200 email subscribers. Is it even worth doing email marketing?**

Yes, if those 200 people are actual customers who gave you their email. A salon in Denver started with 180 subscribers and generated $1,200 in email-attributed revenue in the first month. That's $6 per subscriber per month. Scale that to 500 subscribers and you're at $3,000. The list size isn't the problem. The problem is not sending anything.

**Q: Can't I just use social media instead?**

You can, but you don't own your social media audience. Instagram changes its algorithm, your posts stop being seen, and you have zero control. Email is the only channel where you own the relationship. I saw a coffee shop in Portland lose 40% of their Instagram reach overnight after an algorithm update. Their email list? Unaffected. They sent an email that afternoon and still got a 38% open rate.

**Q: How much time does this actually take?**

If you're doing it right, 30 minutes per week. That's one 15-minute block to write the email and one 15-minute block to review and schedule it. The sequences — welcome emails, post-purchase follow-ups, appointment reminders — get set up once and run automatically. The people who say email takes too much time are usually the ones designing custom templates every week and rewriting the same content. Stop doing that.

**Q: Will AI make my emails sound like robots?**

Only if you let it. The AI is a tool. You're the writer. Generate a draft, then rewrite it in your voice. Read it out loud. If you wouldn't say it to a customer standing in front of you, delete it and try again. I've seen great results when people use AI for structure and write the substance themselves.

**Q: What's the one thing I should do today if I'm not doing email at all?**

Set up a capture form at your checkout. Square, Toast, and Clover all have integrations with email platforms. Add a signup link to your booking confirmation page if you use Booksy or MINDBODY. Offer a small incentive — $5 off, a free item, early access to a promotion. Do it today. Not next week. You're losing money every day that passes without collecting emails from the people who are already giving you money.

**Q: How do I know if my emails are actually working?**

Ignore open rate for a minute. Look at revenue. Track how many people click through and actually buy something. Most email platforms have basic ecommerce tracking. If you're using Square or Shopify, connect it to your email platform. Then you'll see exactly how much money each campaign generates. A local bakery in Chicago found that their emails were responsible for 18% of their weekly revenue. They didn't know that until they connected the data. Don't guess. Measure.
