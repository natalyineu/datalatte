## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Buying Ahrefs Before You Own Your Google Business Profile

I had a call with a salon owner in Austin, Texas. She was frustrated. She’d spent $399 on a six-month Ahrefs plan, ran a site audit, found 47 broken links, fixed them all, and… nothing. No increase in calls. No new bookings. She was ready to cancel everything.

I asked one question: “Who controls your Google Business Profile?”

Silence.

Turns out, the previous owner had set it up with their personal Gmail. The current owner had never reset the listing. The phone number was wrong, the hours hadn’t been updated in 14 months, and there were no photos. Meanwhile, a competitor two blocks over had 87 reviews and answered every question within an hour. Our client was invisible in the map pack even though her website technically outranked the competitor.

**The fix**: Before you spend a dime on Ahrefs, claim and verify your Google Business Profile. Update every field. Respond to every review (yes, the bad ones too). Add real photos of your salon, your team, and your work. Then run the Ahrefs site audit to clean up the technical stuff.

**The outcome**: After she took back control of the profile, her GMB-driven phone calls went from zero to 22 per month within six weeks. Each call averaged a $65 booking. That’s $1,430 in monthly revenue from the single action of fixing the profile — more than the annual cost of Ahrefs. She still uses Ahrefs, but only after the basics were solid.

### Mistake 2: Targeting Keywords That Don’t Match How People Actually Search

A salon owner in Nashville told me she was “obsessed with ranking for ‘hair color’.” She wrote a 2,500-word blog post about hair color trends. She built backlinks from a local fashion blog. Three months later, she was on page 8 of Google for “hair color.” It drove exactly zero traffic.

Here’s the problem: the phrase “hair color” is a generic category term. It’s used by people browsing Pinterest, not by someone sitting in their car looking for a salon to fix a bad DIY dye job. The intent is wrong.

**The fix**: Use Ahrefs’ Keywords Explorer to find phrases that include both a service word and a location or intent qualifier. For a hair salon, the money terms are things like:

- “women’s haircut Nashville”
- “balayage specialist Nashville”
- “salon open Sunday Nashville”
- “cheap color correction near me”

When I worked at a boutique agency, we had a client in Portland, Oregon who targeted “purple hair Portland” — a super niche term that had only 80 searches per month but a very high conversion rate. Every person searching that was ready to book. That page ranked #1 in three weeks and brought in an average of $950 per month in color services.

**The outcome**: The Nashville salon owner stopped writing about “hair color” and created one page for “blonde highlights Nashville.” Within two months, that page was ranking #3 and generating 12 calls per week. At an average service value of $90, that’s $1,080 a week in attributable revenue. The page took four hours to write.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Competitor Backlinks – Or Overestimating Their Value

A hair salon in Denver hired a freelance SEO “expert” who told them the key to local rankings was building “tons of backlinks.” They spent $1,200 on a package that promised 50 backlinks from “high-authority directories.” The backlinks were from spammy sites nobody had ever heard of. I’ve seen this pattern at three different clients. People think backlinks are a numbers game. For local businesses, they’re not. A link from the Denver Chamber of Commerce, a local blogger who actually visits your salon, or the neighborhood newspaper is worth more than fifty links from generic “beauty” directories.

**The fix**: Use Ahrefs’ Site Explorer on your top three local competitors. Look at their backlinks one by one. Which ones are real, relevant, and local? A good example: a competitor in Denver had a link from a local sports team’s website because they donated services for a charity event. Another had a link from a mom blog that reviewed their kids’ haircuts.

Replicate those. Reach out to the same sources — but offer something different. The local sports team doesn’t need another free haircut for a raffle; they might need a booth at an event. The mom blog might want a “bring your kid for a free trim” offer to share with readers.

**The outcome**: The Denver salon ditched the spammy link builder, did a local citation cleanup (they found 14 duplicate NAP listings on Yelp alone), and landed two real backlinks: one from a wedding planner website (they offered a “bridal party updos” package) and one from a local real estate agent’s blog (they wrote a quick post about “how to make your new home salon-ready”). Their Ahrefs Domain Rating went from 12 to 24 in four months. Their GMB ranking moved from position 8 to position 3 for “Denver hair salon.” Estimated revenue increase: $3,800 per month.

### Mistake 4: Forgetting That Ahrefs Is a Tool, Not a Strategy

I met a salon owner in Chicago who had been using Ahrefs for a year. He showed me his dashboard — rank tracking was set up, site audit was green, keyword lists were long. He was proud. But when I asked how many new customers came from organic search in the last quarter, he didn’t know.

**The outcome**: The Chicago salon owner cut his keyword list from 200 to 12. He stopped obsessing over vanity metrics. He noticed that “glueless wigs Chicago” was ranking #5 but had zero traffic because the page was a one-liner. He expanded the page with photos, pricing, and a booking button. ## How to Use Ahrefs for Local Keyword Research (Without Overanalyzing)

Most local SEO guides tell you to “do keyword research” and then leave you with a list of 500 general terms like “haircut” and “salon.” That’s useless. Here’s the exact process I use with salon owners — no PhD in SEO required.

**Step 1: Start with three seed phrases.**

Open Ahrefs’ Keyword Explorer. Type in:

- “hair salon [your city]”
- “haircut [your city]”
- “balayage [your city]”

Replace [your city] with wherever you’re located. For a salon in Austin, that’s “hair salon Austin,” “haircut Austin,” “balayage Austin.”

**Step 2: Filter for local intent.**

Look at the “Questions” tab inside Keyword Explorer. Ahrefs surfaces the actual questions people are asking. For a hair salon, these will be things like:

- “What salons are open on Sunday in Austin?”
- “How much does a balayage cost in Austin?”
- “Best hair salon for curly hair in Austin?”

These are golden. They indicate someone is actively shopping, not just browsing. A salon in Portland used this method and found “balayage Portland cost” — they wrote a transparent pricing page answering that exact query and ranked #1. They got 300% more click-throughs because they were the only salon that gave a straight price instead of “call for a consultation.”

**Step 3: Steal from competitors — but only the good stuff.**

Run your top three competitors through Ahrefs’ Site Explorer. Go to “Organic keywords.” Sort by position (1–5). Look at which keywords are driving traffic to their site. Are there any you haven’t targeted? A Denver salon saw that a competitor was getting traffic for “Denver hair extensions” even though they didn’t list extensions on their own site. They added an extensions page, optimized it with local intent, and started ranking on page one within six weeks.

**Step 4: Prioritize based on commercial value, not search volume.**

Volume is a trap. A keyword with 50 searches per month can be worth more than one with 1,000 searches, if every searcher is ready to book. Use Ahrefs’ “Clicks” metric — it shows how many people actually click through to a website. And look at the search results: if the top result is a Yelp page or a large directory, you probably don’t want to compete. But if the top result is another local salon, you have a chance.

**Real example**: A barbershop in Nashville found “fade haircut Nashville” had 130 searches but only 30 clicks per month — because the search results were dominated by Instagram pages and local directories. They created a dedicated page with before/after photos, a menu of fade styles, and pricing. Their page started ranking #3 within 10 weeks. They gained an estimated 18 appointments per month from that single keyword, averaging $40 each — $720 monthly.

## Pairing Ahrefs With Tools You Probably Already Use

Ahrefs is great at telling you *what* to do. But you still need to *do* it. And you have tools already in place that can make the execution faster. Here’s how to connect them.

### Booksy / Vagaro / Schedulicity

Your booking software is a goldmine of local SEO data. Export your last 90 days of appointments. Which services are booked most? Those are your highest-converting offerings. Now go into Ahrefs Keyword Explorer and check search volume for those services + your city.

**Example**: A salon in NYC had 40% of their appointments for “keratin treatments.” They didn’t have a dedicated page for it. Their booking software showed customers who booked keratin treatments often asked about “frizz control” – that’s a secondary keyword. They used Ahrefs to find “keratin treatment New York” had 320 searches monthly. They built a page optimized for that keyword and linked it from their booking confirmation email. Within two months, that page was driving 25+ visits per week. Revenue per keratin treatment: $200. That’s $5,000 a month from a page they never would have built without cross-referencing their booking data and Ahrefs.

### Google Business Profile Insights

Inside your GMB dashboard, you can see exactly which search queries led people to your listing. Copy the top 10 queries. Paste them into Ahrefs’ Keywords Explorer. Are any of those high volume but not on your website yet?

**Example**: A salon in Chicago noticed “glueless wigs” was a top search term on their GMB. They didn’t offer wigs, but they did install wigs for clients. They added a “wig installation Chicago” page with pricing and photos. Ahrefs showed the search volume was 90/month with low competition. They ranked #1 in 5 weeks. They started getting 6–8 wig installation inquiries per week. Average ticket: $150. That’s $900–$1,200 per week.

### Mailchimp / Email Marketing

Segment your email list by what people have booked. Send a survey: “What services are you looking for that you can’t find?” Collect those answers. Use Ahrefs to check if there’s search demand for those services in your area.

**Reality check**: Most salons email about promotions. Few email to ask what’s missing. A salon in Austin did this and found 14% of their subscribers wanted “organic hair color” – a niche. Ahrefs showed “organic hair color Austin” had 40 searches per month. They added it as a service, built a page, and within 10 weeks they were the #2 result. The organic color appointments averaged $180 each, and they got about 8 per month from that page. $1,440 a month from a 15-minute email survey.

### Yelp and Other Review Sites

Ahrefs can’t track your Yelp ranking directly (different domain), but you can use Ahrefs’ “SERP history” to see which keyword searches bring up Yelp pages. If Yelp is ranking for “hair salon [city]” above local salon websites, you need to optimize your Yelp page. And you can use Ahrefs to see which competitors have high-traffic Yelp pages. Reverse engineer what they’re doing differently (more photos? more reviews? pricing listed?).

**Example**: A salon in Portland noticed that Yelp pages were the top 3 results for “hair salon Portland.” Their own website was buried. They used Ahrefs to analyze the Yelp pages of competitors — one competitor had 400+ reviews, another had 200. The salon had 68 reviews. Their fix: a 30-day push to get customers to leave reviews on Yelp (not just Google). They offered a free conditioning treatment for a review. After 30 days, they had 120 new reviews. Their Yelp page started ranking higher, and their website (linked in Yelp profile) also got a boost. The effort cost $500 in treatments but generated an estimated $4,200 in new bookings from Yelp alone over the next 6 months.

## Tracking Rankings: What Actually Matters for a Hair Salon

I’ve seen too many salon owners obsess over their Ahrefs rank tracker showing “position 1 for ‘hair salon’” — only to realize that keyword drives zero calls because it’s an informational query. Local ranking isn’t one number. It’s a set of metrics that connect to revenue.

### The Only Keywords That Matter

Set up rank tracking in Ahrefs for these 10–15 keywords:

1. “[service] [city]” — e.g., “balayage Austin”
2. “[service] near me” — e.g., “curly haircut near me”
3. “[city] hair salon” — e.g., “Austin hair salon”
4. “[specific neighborhood] hair salon” — e.g., “East Austin hair salon”
5. “best [service] in [city]” — e.g., “best color correction in Austin”
6. “[problem] [city]” — e.g., “bad haircut fix Austin”
7. “[city] [service] pricing” — e.g., “Austin balayage cost”
8. “[salon name]” (branded) — to see if competitors are outranking your own name

**Do not track** generic terms like “hair color,” “cut and style,” “salon services.” Those are too broad. They’ll give you false confidence or false panic.

### How Often to Check

Weekly. Any more often and you’ll drive yourself crazy with daily fluctuations. Any less and you won’t catch problems early. I schedule 15 minutes every Monday morning to check the tracker and note any changes of 3+ positions.

### What to Do When Rankings Drop

A salon in Portland OR saw their #1 position for “blonde highlights Portland” drop to #5 in one week. They panicked. Instead of randomly changing things, they checked Ahrefs’ site audit: no new errors. Then they looked at the SERP: a new competitor had published a comprehensive guide on blonde highlights. The solution wasn’t to add more keywords — it was to update their own page with fresh photos, new pricing, and a FAQ section. They did that on a Wednesday. By the following Monday, they were back to #2. The revenue impact was roughly $1,200 in the month it took to fully recover.

### When Rankings Mean Nothing

If you rank #1 for “hair salon [city]” but nobody books from that page, something is wrong. The page might have a weak call-to-action, a confusing phone number, or a form that doesn’t work on mobile. Track the conversion rate, not just the ranking. Ahrefs can tell you where you rank; your booking software tells you if it matters.

I had a client in Nashville who ranked #3 for “nashville highlights” but only 2% of page visitors booked. We added a “Book Now” button at the top of the page and a pricing table. The booking rate went to 8%. Same ranking, 4x more revenue.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need Ahrefs, or can I get by with free tools?**

You can stumble with free tools, but you’ll spend twice as long and still miss things. Ahrefs’ site audit catches technical issues that Google Search Console doesn’t flag (like missing meta descriptions or broken internal links). Its keyword explorer shows actual search volumes and click-through rates — free tools like Google Keyword Planner give you ranges like “100–1k” which are almost useless for a salon. If you’re serious about local SEO and have a budget, Ahrefs pays for itself if you follow the steps above. If you’re under $2,000/month revenue, start with GMB and free tools, then upgrade.

**Q: Can’t I just pay someone on Fiverr to do all this for $50?**

You can. And you’ll get what you pay for. I’ve audited dozens of salons who hired cheap freelancers. The freelancers built spammy backlinks or stuffed keywords into invisible text. One salon in Austin had their site penalized and lost all GMB visibility for six weeks. It cost them $4,500 in lost bookings to fix. The $50 “expert” disappeared. If you’re going to hire someone, check their case studies and ask for a specific breakdown of work for a local business. A generic “I do SEO” pitch from a freelancer who’s never worked on a hair salon is a red flag.

**Q: How long until I see results from Ahrefs-driven SEO?**

It depends on where you start. If your website is clean and you have a verified GMB, expect to see ranking movements within 4–8 weeks for low-competition keywords (like a specific service in a mid-sized city). For competitive terms like “hair salon Austin,” it could take 4–6 months of consistent work. I had a Chicago salon that went from no organic traffic to 120 visitors a month from SEO in 8 weeks — but they were already in a niche (men’s grooming) with low competition. The average salon I work with sees a 15–25% increase in organic bookings within three months.

**Q: Will Ahrefs help me get more reviews on Google or Yelp?**

Indirectly, yes. Ahrefs can show you which competitors are getting the most review traffic (their GMB profile ranking high for “hair salon with reviews” type searches). But the tool can’t write review requests for you. Use Ahrefs to identify review-rich competitors, then create a process: follow-up texts after appointments with a direct link to your GMB review page. I have a client in Denver who added a QR code on their checkout iPad. Reviews went up 3x in two months.

**Q: My salon is mobile-only — I don’t have a full website. Does Ahrefs still work?**

Ahrefs works best on a proper website. If you only have a Booksy profile or a Facebook page, Ahrefs can still help you analyze competitors’ backlinks and keywords, but you won’t be able to run a site audit or track rankings for your own page. My advice: build a simple one-page website using Squarespace or Wix (cost: ~$20/month). Even that gives you a base to optimize with Ahrefs. I’ve seen a mobile-only salon in Nashville go from zero web presence to a single-page site with a booking button. Their GMB ranking improved because they had a real website to link to. Within two months, they added $1,800/month in online bookings.
