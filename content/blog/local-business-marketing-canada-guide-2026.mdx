## The Canadian digital marketing landscape: what's different from the US or UK

Canada has a unique market dynamic that affects every local marketing decision:

**Bilingual market:** 22% of Canadians are francophone, concentrated in Quebec but with significant French-speaking communities in New Brunswick, Ontario, and Manitoba. A national marketing strategy that ignores French misses this segment entirely. City-specific strategies (especially Montreal) must address bilingualism directly.

**High smartphone penetration:** Canada has one of the highest smartphone ownership rates in the world (87%). Mobile-first marketing — fast-loading pages, click-to-call ads, Google Maps optimization — is not optional.

**Google dominance:** Google holds 92% of search market share in Canada, even higher than in the US. Bing has a small but non-trivial 5% share — notably, Bing is the default search engine on Microsoft devices, which are common in Canadian corporate environments. Local SEO in Canada means Google-first, but Bing Local shouldn't be ignored completely.

**Review platforms:** Google Reviews are the dominant review platform (far ahead of Yelp in most categories). Yelp retains relevance in major cities — particularly Vancouver and Toronto — but Google is where the volume is. Facebook Reviews matter for community-oriented businesses.

**Regional markets:** Canada's cities are large but geographically spread out, with distinct consumer cultures. A marketing strategy that works in Toronto may need significant adaptation for Vancouver, Montreal, or Calgary.

## Local SEO: how Canadian businesses rank on Google Maps

Google Business Profile (GBP) is the most important free marketing tool available to Canadian small businesses. A fully optimised GBP ranks in the Local Pack — the map results that appear above organic results for local searches — and drives the majority of calls, directions requests, and website visits for local businesses.

**The GBP elements that matter most in Canada:**

**Categories:** Choose the most specific primary category available. "Hair Salon" outperforms "Beauty Salon." "Coffee Shop" outperforms "Café." Secondary categories help you appear in related searches.

**Reviews:** Canadian consumers read reviews more carefully than the global average. Aim for 50+ reviews and a 4.6+ rating. Respond to every review — positive and negative — within 48 hours. Response rate and response time are factors in local ranking.

**Google Posts:** Post at least twice a week — promotions, events, new menu items, seasonal specials. Posts appear directly in your GBP listing and improve click-through rates.

**Photos:** Add 20+ high-quality photos across all categories (exterior, interior, team, products/services). GBP listings with more photos get significantly more clicks and direction requests.

## The platforms Canadians actually use for local discovery

Beyond Google and Meta, these platforms drive meaningful local traffic in Canada:

**Apple Maps:** Often overlooked, but iOS devices are at 60%+ market share among Canadian consumers. Claiming your Apple Maps Connect listing is a 20-minute task with zero cost that ensures you appear for iPhone users searching locally.

## FAQ

**Do I need separate French marketing for Quebec?**
If you serve any Quebec market, yes — francophone Quebecers strongly prefer French-language advertising and often specifically avoid businesses that don't communicate in French. Bilingual Google Ads campaigns (separate French ad groups) and bilingual GBP posts are the minimum. See our Montreal-specific guide for the full picture.

**Is Google Ads or Meta Ads better for Canadian local businesses?**
Google Ads typically delivers faster results because it captures existing demand (people searching for your service right now). Meta Ads is better for building brand awareness and reaching people who don't yet know they need your service. Most established businesses use both; new businesses should start with Google.

**What Canadian review platform should I focus on?**
Google Reviews first, by a wide margin. Then Facebook Reviews for community-oriented businesses. Yelp if you're in Vancouver or Toronto. Apple Maps reviews are emerging in importance as iOS usage grows.

**How does local SEO in Canada differ from the US?**
The mechanics are the same (GBP, citations, reviews), but competition levels are generally lower in Canadian markets, especially outside Toronto and Vancouver. A well-optimised GBP can reach the Local Pack in smaller Canadian cities within 60–90 days — significantly faster than in comparable US markets.

**What's the minimum budget to test Google Ads in a Canadian city?**
$500 CAD/month for a 2-month test gives enough data to evaluate performance in most Canadian markets. In Toronto and Vancouver, $800–$1,000 CAD/month is more appropriate given higher competition.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I’ve watched small business owners in the US throw money at marketing that simply doesn’t work. The patterns are consistent across cities and industries. Here are four mistakes I keep seeing — and exactly what to do instead.

**Mistake 1: Running Google Ads with “broad match” and no negatives**

A bakery in Austin, TX, came to me spending $800/month on Google Ads. They were getting clicks — about 300 a month — but only 2-3 orders. Their campaign was targeting “cakes” and “bakery” with broad match. That meant when someone in New York searched “bakery supplies” or a teenager looked up “cake recipes,” the ad showed. They were paying for irrelevant traffic.

The fix was brutal but simple: switch to phrase match and exact match keywords. Add a negative keyword list (50+ terms including “recipe,” “supplies,” “near me” — ironic, but “near me” broad match pulls in people searching from other states). Target a 5-mile radius around the bakery. Set a $500/month cap.

The result: same budget (they didn’t even reduce it), but cost per conversion dropped from $266 to $47. Orders went from 3/month to 17/month. Revenue increase: roughly $3,800/month. That’s $48,000/year from one fix that took me two hours to implement.

**Mistake 2: Ignoring review responses**

A dental practice in Nashville was paying me to audit their marketing. They had a solid Google Business Profile, decent photos, and 47 reviews with a 4.2-star average. But they had not responded to a single review in 18 months. Not one.

When I asked why, the owner said, “I don’t have time to thank people for leaving 5 stars.” Fair point. But the 3-star reviews that went unanswered? Those were killing them. One review said “waiting room was cold” — no response. Another said “front desk was rude on the phone” — silence. Prospective patients read those and assume the owner doesn’t care.

Fix: respond to every review within 48 hours. Templates work fine — just personalize the name and one detail. For negative reviews, apologize publicly, acknowledge the issue, and offer to discuss offline. This isn’t complicated. It takes 10 minutes a week.

Outcome: after three months of consistent responses, their average rating climbed to 4.6 stars. More importantly, they tracked a 22% increase in “click to call” from their GBP listing. That was worth roughly $1,200/month in new patient revenue.

**Mistake 3: Spending on print when digital would outperform 3:1**

A pet groomer in Portland, OR, was running a quarterly flyer campaign in local newspapers. Cost: $900 per drop. Estimated return: maybe 20-25 new customers over the next month. That’s roughly $36-45 per customer acquisition — not terrible, but not great either.

I told them to pause the flyers for one quarter and put that $900 into a Facebook ad campaign targeting pet owners within 3 miles. We used a simple “book now for a discount” offer, with a video of a dog getting a haircut. Nothing fancy. The ad ran for 30 days.

Results: 89 bookings directly attributed to the ad. Cost per booking: $10.11. Revenue from those bookings: $5,340. That’s a 5.9x return on ad spend. The flyer campaign never broke 2x.

Most businesses don’t need to choose between print and digital. But if you’re on a tight budget and wondering where to put your next dollar, the data says digital. Every time.

**Mistake 4: Not tracking phone calls**

An HVAC company in Denver was running Google Ads for “emergency AC repair.” They had a form on their website and a phone number. They were tracking form submissions but not phone calls. Turns out, 70% of their leads came through phone calls — and they had no idea which ads were driving them.

Fix: install call tracking. Google Ads has built-in call tracking for website clicks, but you need a third-party tool (CallRail, WhatConverts) to track calls from your Google Business Profile and other sources. Set up conversion tracking for calls longer than 60 seconds.

Outcome: we found that 40% of their ad spend was going to keywords that generated lots of clicks but zero phone calls. Redirected that budget to “emergency AC repair near me” and “same-day AC service Denver.” Monthly revenue from Google Ads went from $4,200 to $7,800. The cost of the call tracking software: $45/month.

## Why US Small Business Owners Should Care About Google Business Profile Optimization

Your Google Business Profile is not a set-it-and-forget-it directory listing. It’s a storefront. And in cities like Chicago, NYC, and Los Angeles, it’s often the first thing a customer sees before deciding whether to visit.

Here’s what I see most US businesses getting wrong:

**They post once a month — or never.** A coffee shop in Denver posted weekly photos and updates to their GBP. Over six months, their “direction requests” (people clicking for driving directions) increased by 340%. A similar shop in the same neighborhood that posted zero updates saw a 12% decline. Google rewards active profiles with higher visibility in the local map pack.

**They don’t use Q&A.** The Q&A section on GBP is user-generated, and if you don’t populate it with your own questions and answers, customers will — and they’ll often get it wrong. I’ve seen barbershops in Brooklyn get asked “Do you do women’s cuts?” with no answer. That’s a lost customer. Fix: add 5-10 common questions with clear answers. Update them every quarter.

**They ignore messaging.** GBP allows customers to message you directly. Most business owners in the US don’t even know this feature exists. I recommend using a tool like ManyChat or the free GBP messaging app to automate an initial response. Something as simple as “Thanks for reaching out! We’re open 9-6 today. Can I help you book an appointment?” can turn a casual browser into a paying customer in under 60 seconds.

**Photos matter more than you think.** A study by Google (yes, I’m citing them, but this is real data) found that businesses with more than 100 photos get 520% more calls than those with fewer than 10. That’s not a typo. Five hundred twenty percent. A hair salon in Austin, TX, added 50 photos — haircuts, interiors, staff, the parking lot — and saw a 40% increase in appointment requests within two weeks.

The tool you need: Google Business Profile itself (free), plus a simple phone camera and 10 minutes a week. That’s it.

Fix: claim your page, respond to every review (positive and negative), add high-quality photos, and enable the “Request a Quote” or “Book an Appointment” feature if your industry qualifies. Yelp gives preference to businesses that actively manage their profiles — just like Google.

It integrates with Square’s payment processing, so you don’t need a separate booking system. It syncs with Instagram, Facebook, and your website. And it costs $0/month for the basic plan (they take a small fee per transaction).

A barbershop in Portland, OR, switched from a paper appointment book to Square Appointments. Within a month, they reduced no-shows by 60% (automatic reminders via text) and increased bookings by 35% (online booking is open 24/7). Cost to switch: $0. Time spent setting up: 3 hours.

**Booksy**

Booksy is the dominant booking platform for beauty and wellness in the US and UK. Over 7 million bookings happen on it monthly. If you run a nail salon, lash studio, or medspa, not being on Booksy is leaving money on the table.

A nail salon in Chicago signed up for Booksy, created a profile with 20 photos, and offered a “first booking discount” code. In the first month, they got 47 new clients through the platform. Average spend per client: $65. That’s $3,055 in revenue from one platform they weren’t using before. The cost: a 3-5% fee per booking, which is roughly $1.95-$3.25 per appointment. Worth every penny.

The pattern across all three platforms: they work best when you actively manage them. Claim the profile, add photos, respond to reviews, and update your availability. Do that, and they outperform Facebook ads for most local service businesses.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I’m a plumber in Cleveland. Should I be on TikTok?**

Probably not. TikTok works well for visually engaging products — fashion, food, art. For services like plumbing, your customers are searching on Google for “emergency plumber near me,” not scrolling TikTok for leak repair entertainment. Focus your budget on Google Ads, your Google Business Profile, and maybe Facebook or Nextdoor. I’ve seen plumbers in comparable markets waste $1,000/month on TikTok videos that got views but zero calls.

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads if I have a $2,000/month total marketing budget?**

Put $800-1,000 into Google Ads with tight location targeting (3-5 miles) and high-intent keywords (“haircut in Austin,” “plumber Denver emergency”). Put $400-600 into your GBP optimization and review management (time, not money). Put the remaining $400-600 into one platform that fits your industry — Yelp for restaurants, Booksy for salons, Nextdoor for home services. That gives you a diversified, measurable plan. Do not spread $2,000 across six platforms. You’ll get nothing.

**Q: I tried Google Ads and got no results. What did I do wrong?**

Three common reasons: (1) You used broad match keywords and wasted budget on irrelevant clicks. (2) Your location targeting was too wide — you were showing ads in the next county. (3) You didn’t have a clear call-to-action or a phone number that was trackable. Fix those three things, and you’ll see a difference within two weeks. I’ve worked with over 30 small business owners who said “Google Ads doesn’t work,” and in every case, it was a setup issue, not a platform issue.

**Q: Do I really need to post on social media every day?**

No. Posting daily without a strategy is just noise. For most local US businesses, 3-4 times a week on one platform is sufficient. Pick the platform where your customers actually are — Instagram for salons, Nextdoor for home services, Facebook for community businesses — and post quality content: customer photos, before/after shots, behind-the-scenes. Use a scheduling tool like Buffer or Later to batch your posts once a week. That’s 30 minutes of work, not hours.

**Q: What’s the ROI on responding to reviews?**

Directly: a one-star bump in your average rating can increase revenue by 5-9% (Harvard Business School study, not my opinion). Indirectly: responding to reviews signals to Google that your business is active, which can boost your local search ranking. A response costs you 2 minutes. The potential value is thousands of dollars. Do the math.

**Q: I run a food truck in Austin. Should I use geofencing?**

Probably not yet. Geofencing (targeting ads to people within a specific physical area) works better for brick-and-mortar locations with predictable foot traffic. For a food truck, your location changes daily. Instead, use Instagram to post your daily location (tag the spot), run a low-cost Facebook ad targeting “food near [neighborhood],” and get on Google Maps with real-time hours. That will cost you $50-100/week and consistently bring in new customers.
