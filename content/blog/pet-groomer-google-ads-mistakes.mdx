Here are the five I see most often.

## Mistake 1: Targeting the Whole City (or Worse, the Whole State)

Google Ads defaults to broad geographic targeting. If you don't manually set your radius, you may be showing ads to people 40 miles away who would never drive to you.

The fix: Set a radius around your grooming location that matches how far your actual clients travel. For most groomers, that's 5–10 miles. For mobile groomers, set a service-area polygon that matches your actual coverage zone.

Every click from outside your real service area is wasted money.

## Mistake 2: No Negative Keywords

If you're bidding on "dog grooming," your ad will also show for searches like "dog grooming school," "dog grooming scissors to buy," "dog grooming jobs near me," and "free dog grooming tips." None of those people want to book an appointment.

The fix: Build a negative keyword list before you launch. Start with terms like: jobs, training, school, DIY, scissors, clippers, salary, course, certificate, how to. Review your search terms report weekly in the first month and add new negatives as you discover them.

## Mistake 3: Sending Clicks to the Homepage

Your homepage is designed to explain your business to someone who doesn't know you. It has navigation, multiple sections, and lots of choices. That's the wrong destination for someone who searched "dog groomer near me" and clicked your ad.

The fix: Send ad traffic to a dedicated landing page with one job — get the booking. It should have your service area, key services, prices (or a range), photos, reviews, and one clear CTA: call or book online. No navigation. No distractions. Conversion rates improve dramatically.

<Callout type="warning"> Sending Google Ads traffic to your homepage instead of a dedicated booking landing page is one of the most common and costly mistakes. A focused landing page with one CTA can double or triple your conversion rate without increasing ad spend.</Callout>

## Mistake 4: No Conversion Tracking

If you don't have conversion tracking set up, you're flying blind. You can see clicks and impressions, but you have no idea which keywords are generating phone calls or booking form submissions — and which are just burning budget.

The fix: Set up at minimum two conversion actions: phone call clicks (when someone clicks your phone number on mobile) and form submissions (when someone submits your contact or booking form). Both can be set up through Google Tag Manager with no coding required. Once tracking is in place, you can see cost per lead by keyword and cut the underperformers.

## Mistake 5: Using Broad Match for Everything

Broad match keywords in Google Ads will trigger your ad for searches Google considers "related" — which often means vaguely, distantly related. Bidding on broad match "pet groomer" can show your ad for "pet hotel," "pet sitting jobs," and stranger things.

The fix: Use phrase match or exact match for your core keywords. Start tight and loosen as you gather data. Your core keywords should look like "dog grooming [city]," "pet groomer near me," and "mobile dog grooming [neighborhood]" — all on phrase or exact match.

## The Underlying Issue

Most of these mistakes happen because Google Ads accounts are set up quickly by someone who isn't a specialist, or by using Google's "Smart Campaigns" which hides all the controls and optimises for Google's revenue, not yours.

A proper account setup takes a few hours but saves hundreds per month in wasted spend. If you're running Google Ads and haven't looked under the hood in a while, it's worth an audit.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake: Running Ads Without Call Tracking

**The story:** A groomer in Austin, Texas — let’s call her Megan — was spending $800/month on Google Ads. She saw clicks and impressions, but had no idea how many calls came from those ads. Her phone rang constantly. She assumed the business was growing. When I looked at her account, the only conversion tracking was a “Thank You” page visit for online booking forms. She got maybe 10 bookings a month through that form. But the phone? Silence in the data.

**What went wrong:** Without call tracking, every phone call is a ghost. Google doesn’t count it as a conversion unless you set up call extensions with forwarding numbers or use a third-party tool. Megan had call extensions enabled but no tracking — so Google was reporting zero conversions from calls. She couldn’t optimize for what she couldn’t measure.

**The fix:** I set up call tracking using a simple Google Ads call extension with a Google forwarding number. That number forwards to her real line, and Google records each call as a conversion. Then I added a 60-second minimum duration — short calls are usually wrong numbers or spam. We also set up a “call only” campaign for mobile users who want to tap and dial directly.

**The outcome:** In the first month, Megan’s ads drove 47 tracked phone calls. Of those, 31 lasted longer than a minute. She booked 22 appointments from calls alone — nearly double her previous form-based bookings. Her cost-per-lead dropped from $27 to $12. She increased her budget to $1,200/month because she could see the return.

### Mistake: Using Broad Match Keywords on a Small Budget

**The story:** A groomer in Nashville, Tennessee — single location, two employees — was bidding on broad match “dog grooming.” His $500 monthly budget was burning through in 10 days. He’d get impressions for “dog grooming near me in Miami” and “how to become a dog groomer.” His click-through rate was 1.2%. He had zero conversions in two weeks.

**What went wrong:** Broad match allows Google to show your ad for anything it considers related. For a local service business with a tight budget, that’s like throwing money into a fan. Google wants to spend your budget — relevance is your problem.

**The fix:** I paused all broad match keywords. Replaced them with phrase match ([“dog grooming”], [“pet grooming services”]) and exact match ([“dog grooming Nashville”], [“dog groomer near me”]). Added a long list of negative keywords: “school,” “jobs,” “training,” “how to,” “supplies,” “salon,” “veterinarian.” Then I set the budget to “standard delivery” to pace spending across the month instead of “accelerated” which burns fast.

**The outcome:** Budget now lasts the full 30 days. Click-through rate jumped to 4.8%. Conversion rate from ad to booking form went from 0% to 9%. He’s spending $450/month and getting 18–22 new clients. That’s about $20 per client acquisition cost — he’s thrilled.

### Mistake: No Ad Scheduling — Wasting Money When You’re Closed

**The story:** A groomer in Portland, Oregon — mobile service, but she only works Monday through Saturday, 8 AM to 5 PM. Her Google Ads ran 24/7. Between 8 PM and 6 AM she was getting about 15% of her clicks. Every one of those clicks was a wasted dollar — she couldn’t answer the phone, couldn’t respond to texts, and her voicemail callback rate was maybe 10%.

**What went wrong:** Google doesn’t know your business hours unless you tell it. The default is “all day, every day.” For a service business where customers want immediate booking (especially pet owners calling same-day), showing ads at 2 AM is just flushing cash.

**The fix:** I set up ad scheduling to run only during her business hours: Monday–Saturday, 8 AM–5 PM. I also added a “phone only” ad schedule that started 30 minutes before opening (so calls could queue) and ended 30 minutes before closing (to avoid missed calls after hours). Then I applied a bid adjustment of +20% during her busiest period (10 AM–1 PM) to capture the lunch-rush crowd who book same-day appointments.

**The outcome:** Clicks dropped by 14%, but conversions increased by 40%. Cost per conversion fell from $35 to $18. She’s now spending $600/month and getting 33 new clients per month instead of 17. She also sleeps better knowing her ad budget isn’t running while she’s asleep.

### Mistake: Ignoring Google Business Profile Integration

**The story:** A groomer in Denver, Colorado — well-reviewed, 4.8 stars on Google — had a separate Google Ads campaign but never linked her Google Business Profile (GBP). Her ads showed generic location extensions with an address, but no reviews, no photo, no call button from the ad. When I searched her business name, her GBP listing had 200+ reviews. Her ads had zero social proof.

**What went wrong:** Google Ads can pull review stars and local business info directly from your GBP listing — but only if you link the two accounts. Without the link, your ads look like a random service page, not a trusted local business. Users see a generic ad and scroll past. Plus, Google gives a quality score boost for showing location and reviews, which lowers your cost per click.

**The fix:** I linked her Google Ads account to her GBP in the “Business data” section of Google Ads. Then I enabled seller ratings (Google automatically shows star ratings in extensions if you have enough reviews and the link is set). I also turned on “affiliate location extensions” (though that’s for agencies) and made sure her ad copy included her neighborhood: “Denver’s Highlands Dog Grooming – 4.8 stars.”

**The outcome:** Click-through rate on her location extension improved from 3.1% to 5.9%. Cost per click dropped from $3.40 to $2.10 because the quality score improved. Within two months, her monthly conversion count went from 28 to 41. She’s spending $900/month and estimates $3,800 in extra revenue from those additional bookings.

## Why Your Ad Copy Is Killing Your Chances (Even If Your Keywords Are Perfect)

I see groomers targeting the right keywords, setting the right radius, and tracking conversions — but then writing ad copy that sounds like a generic template. “Professional dog grooming services – Book now!” That tells me nothing. It tells the customer nothing.

Your ad copy is the only thing between a click and a bounce. In a local search, someone is comparing three groomers. If your headline says “Pet grooming near me” and the next groomer’s headline says “Same-day appointments – $35 full groom – 4.9 stars,” who gets the click?

**Real example:** A groomer in Chicago’s Wicker Park neighborhood. She was using the default headlines Google provided: “Grooming Services” and “Book Now.” Her click-through rate was 2.8%. I rewrote two headlines: “Wicker Park Dog Grooming – $45 Full Groom” and “Senior Dogs Welcome – Open 8-6 Mon-Sat.” Description line: “We treat your pup like family. 4.8 stars on Google. Same-day appointments available.” Click-through rate jumped to 6.2% within one week. Cost per click dropped from $2.80 to $1.90 because higher CTR = higher quality score.

**How to write good ad copy for a pet groomer:**
- Include your location in the headline (city, neighborhood, or area)
- Mention a specific price or starting price (if you have a standard service)
- Call out a special offer or unique differentiator: “No-cage drying,” “Mobile service,” “We take walk-ins”
- Use emotional triggers: “Your pup deserves the best,” “Stress-free grooming for anxious dogs”
- Include your rating if it’s good: “4.8 stars on Google”
- Add a call to action that matches intent: “Call to book” or “Get a free estimate”

Test two ad variations every month. Pause the loser. Write a new one. That one habit alone can improve your return on ad spend by 20-30% without increasing your budget.

## The Hidden Cost of Not Using Ad Extensions

I audited a groomer in Los Angeles — Sherman Oaks area. She was running a $700/month campaign. She had no call extension, no location extension, no sitelink extension, no callout extension. Her ad was a single headline, two description lines. That’s it.

Google Ads gives you free real estate — use it. Ad extensions can increase your click-through rate by 10-30% according to Google’s own data, but more importantly, they give customers the information they need to choose you without clicking. If someone sees your address, your phone number, and your review stars right in the ad, they’re more likely to call directly.

**What she missed:**
- **Call extension:** Without it, a user has to click the ad, load your website, find the phone number, and dial. Each extra step loses 20-30% of potential calls.
- **Location extension:** Shows your address, map, and directions. For a groomer, that signals “I’m nearby.”
- **Sitelink extensions:** Links to specific pages: “Book Online,” “Services & Prices,” “About Us,” “Contact.” Gives users a shortcut, improves CTR.
- **Callout extensions:** Bite-sized features: “Same-day service,” “Senior pet discounts,” “Certified groomers,” “Pickup & delivery available.”
- **Structured snippet extensions:** Lists like “Types: Full groom, Bath & brush, Nail trim, Teeth cleaning” — helps with relevance.

**The fix:** I added all relevant extensions. Took about 20 minutes. Her CTR went from 2.1% to 4.4% in two weeks. Cost per conversion dropped from $28 to $17. She’s now spending $900/month and getting 50+ conversions per month (calls + online bookings). That’s about $18 per new client.

**The moral:** Extensions are free. Not using them is like paying for a billboard and only writing your website URL — no phone number, no address, no reason to stop.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I’m a mobile groomer. Should I target the whole city since I can travel anywhere?**

No. Target only the areas you’re willing to drive to. If you’re in a city like Phoenix that’s 500 square miles, targeting the whole city means your ad shows to someone 30 miles away who expects you to come to them. For mobile groomers, I recommend a service-area polygon — draw the exact neighborhoods you serve. If you cover a 20-mile radius, set that. But be honest: if you only serve 10 zip codes in a city of 100 zip codes, target those 10. Otherwise you’ll get leads you can’t fulfill, or you’ll waste money on clicks from people outside your zone.

**Q: How much should I spend on Google Ads if I’m just starting out?**

For a single-location pet groomer in a mid-size US city, start with $500/month. That gives you enough data to learn what works without bleeding cash. If you’re in a high-cost market (New York, San Francisco, Seattle), you might need $800–$1,000/month to compete. The key is to start small, track everything, and scale the campaign that converts. Don’t start at $200/month — too few clicks to learn anything.

**Q: Do I really need a landing page? Can’t I just send people to my website homepage?**

Use a landing page. If you send ad traffic to your general homepage — which probably talks about your history, your philosophy, and a blog — you lose people. Create a simple page dedicated to that campaign: “Dog Grooming in [City] – Book Your Appointment Today.” Include your phone number, a short booking form, your address, your hours, and maybe three reviews. That’s it. I’ve seen conversion rates double just by switching from a homepage to a landing page.

**Q: What about Google’s “Smart Campaigns”? Are they worth it?**

Smart Campaigns are Google’s automated option for small businesses. They work if you want to set it and forget it with minimal control. But you lose the ability to target specific keywords, set your own budget allocation, or see detailed search term data. For a groomer who wants to grow, I recommend standard campaigns — you can start with Smart Campaigns for two weeks to see if you get any leads, but then switch to manual if you’re spending more than $500/month. The extra control usually pays for itself.

**Q: Should I run ads on weekends, or only weekdays?**

Test both. Most pet owners book appointments during the week, but weekend searches spike for same-day grooming. If your shop is open Saturday, run ads Saturday. If closed Sunday, turn them off Sunday. Use ad scheduling data in your account to see which days and hours have the highest conversion rates. You might find that Tuesday at 10 AM gets you three bookings, while Friday at 2 PM gets you none. Shift budget to the winning time slots.

**Q: I tried Google Ads and got zero bookings. What did I do wrong?**

Most likely one of three things: (1) You targeted too broadly, so you got clicks from people who can’t drive to you. (2) Your ad copy doesn’t match what people are searching for — they see “pet grooming services” but want “same-day dog grooming near me.” (3) Your landing page or phone process is broken. Call your own number from a friend’s phone. Does it ring? Does someone answer? Is your voicemail professional? Fix those three, and retest with a $300 budget for two weeks.
