## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Treating Your Google Business Profile Like a Digital Business Card

A groomer in Austin, Texas—let’s call her shop “Aussie & Trim”—had been in business for six years. She had a Google Business Profile (GBP) but hadn’t touched it since she set it up in 2019. No photos. No responses to reviews. No posts. Her phone rang maybe three times a week, and she assumed that was just the market.

When I looked at her profile analytics, the problem was obvious: her profile showed up for exactly three search terms, all of which were her business name. She was invisible for “Austin dog grooming,” “mobile pet groomer Austin,” or “cheap dog haircut Austin.” The fix took two hours: uploading 25 photos of recent grooms (including one of a poodle that looked like a small dinosaur), responding to every review with a specific thank-you, and answering the Q&A section where random users had asked pricing questions she’d ignored.

Within six weeks, her profile impressions went from 340 per month to 2,100. Her phone rang 12 times the following Monday. She booked an additional $2,400 in appointments that month—directly from GBP alone. Cost: zero dollars and a couple of hours she said she didn’t have.

**The fix:** Claim your GBP if you haven’t. Add photos every week. Respond to every review within 48 hours. Use the “Posts” feature to announce a new service or a breed-specific special. Google treats active profiles like they treat active restaurants—they show them first.

### Mistake 2: Building a Website and Assuming People Will Find It

A groomer in Portland, Oregon spent $4,800 on a Squarespace website. It looked beautiful: parallax scrolling, a video of a golden retriever getting a bow tie, a booking page with a calendar widget. Then she waited. And waited. After three months, she had exactly seven website visits—all from her mother.

The problem wasn’t the design. It was that she had zero local SEO on the site. No location-specific page titles. No schema markup for pet grooming services. No local backlinks. The site was a brochure that nobody could find on Google unless they typed “portlandoregonpetgroomingsquarespace.com” directly into the address bar.

I had her rewrite five pages with basic local SEO: title tags like “Best Dog Groomer in Portland, OR | Aussie Cuts,” service pages named “Poodle Grooming Portland,” and a Google Maps embed on the contact page. She submitted her site to Yelp, Nextdoor, and the Portland Pet Business Directory. Three months later, organic traffic hit 340 visits per month. She booked $3,200 in revenue from people who found her site through search.

**The fix:** If you’re spending money on a website, spend 20% of that budget on getting it found. Add local keywords to your page titles, get listed in local directories, and make sure your NAP (name, address, phone) is identical across every platform. Google penalizes inconsistency.

### Mistake 3: Running Facebook Ads Without a Landing Page

A groomer in Denver, Colorado spent $1,800 on a Facebook ad campaign targeting dog owners within 15 miles. The ad showed a before-and-after of a Shih Tzu that’d been transformed from a dust mop into a show dog. Cute, right? The ad got 4,200 impressions and 140 clicks.

Then those 140 people landed on her Facebook page. Not a booking page. Not a special offer page. Just her Facebook timeline, where the most recent post was a photo of her lunch. She got zero bookings. Zero leads. Zero email captures. She spent $1,800 to show 4,200 people a photo of a clean dog and a sandwich.

I told her to build a simple landing page using Carrd (free) with a headline that said “Denver Dog Grooming – First Visit $35 Off.” She pointed her Facebook ad to that page. The next month, she spent $600 and got 22 bookings, generating $3,800 in revenue.

**The fix:** Never send paid traffic to a generic social media profile or your home page. Build a simple, one-purpose landing page. One headline. One offer. One button. Track everything. If you can’t measure the return, you’re just donating money to Meta.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring the Power of Email Follow-Ups

A groomer in Nashville, Tennessee was booking 30 appointments a week but never collected email addresses. She thought it was “creepy” to ask. One of her clients moved away, another three switched to a cheaper groomer, and two just stopped showing up. That’s six lost clients in one month—nobody told her why, and she had no way to ask.

She started collecting emails at checkout with a simple “Join our email list for $5 off your next visit.” In three months, she had 140 emails. She sent one email per month: a reminder to book before the holidays, a photo of a recent creative groom (a labradoodle dyed to look like a tiger), and a “we miss you” offer to clients who hadn’t booked in 90 days.

Three months in, she saw a 22% rebooking rate from the email list. That generated an additional $1,100 per month from clients she would have otherwise lost.

**The fix:** Collect emails at every touchpoint. Use a tool like Mailchimp (free up to 500 contacts). Send one email per month. Don’t overthink it. You’re not writing a newsletter—you’re reminding people you exist.

## Why Google Ads Actually Work for Pet Groomers (If You Do It Right)

Most small business owners I meet have either never tried Google Ads or tried them once, spent $300, got one phone call, and swore them off forever. I get it. I’ve made that mistake myself. But Google Ads, when set up for a service business like pet grooming, can produce a 4x or 5x return if you’re willing to get specific.

Here’s the playbook I’ve used for groomers in Chicago and NYC.

First, never run a generic campaign with keywords like “dog grooming.” That’s too broad. You’ll spend money on clicks from people who are just browsing, not booking. Instead, use long-tail keywords: “affordable dog grooming in [your city],” “mobile pet groomer near me,” “best dog haircut in [your neighborhood].” These have lower search volume but much higher intent. Someone typing “best dog haircut in Lincoln Park” is ready to book. Someone typing “dog grooming” might just be curious.

Second, use location targeting with a radius of 5–10 miles. Anything wider, and you’ll get clicks from people who are too far to drive. I’ve seen groomers in Austin waste 40% of their budget on clicks from outside their service area.

Third, set up call tracking. Google Ads can show you which clicks turned into phone calls. If you’re not tracking calls, you’re flying blind. Use a tool like CallRail or a simple forwarding number. One groomer in Denver discovered that 70% of her bookings came from phone calls, not website forms. She optimized her ads for call clicks and dropped her cost per booking from $28 to $11.

A realistic budget for a pet groomer in a mid-sized US city: $500–$800 per month. At $2–$4 per click, you’ll get 125–400 clicks per month. If 10–15% of those turn into bookings (which is typical for a well-run campaign), that’s 12–60 new clients per month. At an average ticket of $65 per groom, that’s $780–$3,900 in revenue from a $500–$800 spend.

**The catch:** You have to monitor it weekly. Google Ads is not set-and-forget. If you don’t check search terms and add negative keywords (like “DIY dog grooming” or “free dog grooming”), you’ll waste money. If you don’t adjust bids based on performance, you’ll overpay. Spend 30 minutes per week on it, or hire someone who will.

## How to Build a Referral Program That Actually Generates Clients

Every groomer says they have a referral program. Most of them do not. They have a vague “tell your friends” line at the bottom of a receipt. That’s not a program. That’s hope.

A groomer in Phoenix, Arizona set up a real referral system using Square’s built-in referral tools. Here’s what she did:

- Existing clients got a $10 credit for every new client they referred who booked and completed a grooming session.
- New clients got $10 off their first visit.
- She promoted it on her booking confirmation page, in her email signature, and on a small sign at the checkout counter.

The result: In the first 90 days, she got 34 referrals. At an average revenue per groom of $55, that’s $1,870 in direct revenue from referred clients. More importantly, referred clients had a 68% retention rate after three months, compared to 42% for clients who found her through search. Referrals stick.

The key is making it easy. Don’t make clients remember a code. Use a tool like Square, Yelp Guest Manager, or a simple spreadsheet where you track the referring client’s name and apply the credit automatically. If you’re using Booksy or Vagaro, both have referral programs built in.

A few rules I’ve seen work:
- Cap the referral bonus at $50 per month per client. You don’t want someone referring 20 people in one week and you handing out $200.
- Track the source of every new client in your CRM. If you don’t know where clients come from, you can’t optimize.
- Say thank you. A handwritten note or a quick text costs nothing and makes the referring client feel appreciated.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I’m already fully booked. Why do I need marketing?**

If you’re fully booked but you have no waitlist, no email list, and no referral program, you’re one sick groomer or one bad Yelp review away from a 40% drop in revenue. Marketing isn’t just for getting more clients—it’s for protecting the clients you have. Build a waitlist. Collect emails. Create a referral system. That’s how you stay fully booked even when things go wrong.

**Q: Is Yelp worth it for pet groomers?**

It depends on your city. In cities where Yelp has high usage (San Francisco, NYC, Austin), having a Yelp page with 20+ positive reviews and regular photo updates can bring in steady leads. In smaller cities, Yelp is often a ghost town. Test it for two months: set up your page, respond to every review, add photos weekly. If you’re not getting at least one booking per week from Yelp after two months, pull your effort and focus on Google.

**Q: Do I really need a website? Can I just use Instagram and Facebook?**

You can, but you’re leaving money on the table. A website gives you control. You can’t control Instagram’s algorithm. You can’t control Facebook changing its ad policies. A simple one-page website with your services, pricing, location, and a booking link costs less than $200 per year to maintain. It’s your digital storefront. Instagram is the window display—the website is where you actually make the sale.

**Q: How much should I spend on marketing per month?**

A safe rule is 5–10% of your monthly revenue. If you’re doing $10,000 per month in grooming revenue, budget $500–$1,000 for marketing. Split it between Google Ads (40%), local SEO (30%), social media (20%), and email (10%). Adjust based on what’s actually working. If Google Ads is bringing in 3x return, put more there. If Instagram is a time sink, cut it.

**Q: I tried a loyalty program and it didn’t work. What did I do wrong?**

Probably one of two things. Either you made it too complicated (punch cards that get lost, points that expire, “buy 10 get 1 free” that nobody tracks), or you didn’t promote it well. A simple “every 5th groom is 20% off” that you mention at checkout and put on your booking page will work better than an elaborate points system. Keep it simple. Test it for 60 days. If you don’t see a 10% increase in repeat bookings, change the offer.

**Q: Should I offer mobile grooming?**

That’s a business model decision, not a marketing decision. Mobile grooming increases your overhead (van, gas, insurance) but also increases your average ticket (you can charge 20–30% more for the convenience). If you’re in a dense urban area where clients don’t have cars, mobile makes sense. If you’re in a suburb where people have minivans and drive everywhere, a fixed location with easy parking is fine. Don’t do both at once unless you have a second groomer.
