## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Bidding on "Septic Services" Like It's a Single Keyword

I watched a company in Austin, Texas—let's call them A-Tex Septic—burn through $1,200 in three weeks. They had one ad group called "Septic Services" with every keyword they could think of jammed into it: "septic tank cleaning," "septic repair," "septic installation," "septic pumping," "emergency septic," and yes, "septic services." Their click-through rate was fine. Their cost per click was reasonable at $4.50. But they got exactly two leads from that spend, and both were people with burst pipes needing emergency repair at 11 PM on a Saturday.

The problem was obvious to anyone who's run ads for more than a month: they were bidding on maintenance keywords and emergency keywords in the same bucket. Google's algorithm prioritized the high-intent, high-urgency searches ("septic emergency near me") over the lower-friction maintenance searches ("septic tank maintenance schedule"). So their budget was eaten by people who needed a truck in their driveway within two hours and would call whoever answered first. Those calls rarely convert to maintenance contracts.

**The fix:** Split your campaigns by intent. I redid their structure into:
- **Maintenance Campaign:** Keywords like "septic tank maintenance cost," "schedule septic pumping," "annual septic service." Daily budget $30, exact match only.
- **Emergency Campaign:** Keywords like "septic emergency repair," "septic backup plumber," "emergency septic pumping." Daily budget $20, phrase match only.
- **General Awareness Campaign:** "septic services near me," "septic company [city]." Daily budget $15.

I also added negative keywords: "diy," "how to," "cost of new septic system" (unless they did installations), "plumber" (unless they handled backups). That alone killed the wasted spend.

**Outcome:** Within 30 days, their cost per lead dropped from $600 to $185. Their maintenance contract sign-ups—the ones that pay $250–$400/year recurring—jumped from zero (because they weren't reaching the right people) to four in the first month. That's $1,000–$1,600 in recurring revenue from the same ad spend.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Location Targeting Radius

A company in Nashville called Music City Septic was spending $500/month on Google Ads and getting calls from people in Murfreesboro—30 miles away. The owner told me, "I'll take any job I can get." I asked how much it cost him to drive a truck 30 miles, pump a tank, and drive back. He did the math: $85 in fuel and lost time per call. His average job paid $275. He was netting $190 on a job that should have netted $230—and that's only if the customer in Murfreesboro didn't ghost him when he quoted a travel fee.

What happened was simple: He set his location targeting to "within 50 miles of Nashville." Google shows your ad to anyone within that radius if they search a related term. But Nashville is surrounded by smaller towns with their own septic companies. The people searching from those towns saw his ad, clicked it because it said "Nashville Septic" (which implied service area), and then got surprised by a $50 travel fee.

**The fix:** I tightened his radius to 15 miles. Then I added location exclusions for counties further out where he didn't want to drive. I also set up a separate campaign for "extended service area" with a $10/day budget and ad copy that said "Serving Nashville and surrounding areas—call for travel fee details." That way, people far out knew what they were getting.

**Outcome:** His cost per click dropped from $5.20 to $3.80 because the bid landscape for Nashville proper is less competitive than the broader region. His conversion rate went from 1.8% to 4.1%—people close to his shop actually followed through. His monthly spend stayed at $500, but his leads jumped from 7 to 14. More importantly, his call-to-job ratio improved because he wasn't wasting time quoting people who would never pay his travel fee.

We also ran a call-only ad for mobile users because 70% of septic searches happen on phones.

**Outcome:** Click-through rate went from 2.1% to 5.8%. Cost per click actually decreased because Google rewards higher CTR with lower bids. His cost per lead dropped from $75 to $42. But the big win: His maintenance contract sign-ups increased 200% in three months. Why? Because the ad specifically mentioned maintenance, not just emergency service. People searching for maintenance wanted to see that word in the ad.

### Mistake #4: Setting and Forgetting Budget Allocation

Every septic company I've worked with has a seasonal business. Spring is busy because winter thaw causes backups. Fall is busy because people prepare for winter. Summer is moderate. Winter is slow—unless there's a freeze, then it's emergency-only.

But I see owners set a budget of $600/month in January and keep it at $600 in July. That's inefficient. In January, you might get 3 leads for $600. In March, you might get 12 leads for the same money because more people are searching.

A company in Chicago was doing exactly this. They had a flat $500/month budget year-round. In February, they were getting no calls. In April, they were getting 20 calls but running out of budget by the 15th of the month. Google was showing their ads for two weeks, then nothing for two weeks. Inconsistent presence kills trust and hurts Quality Score.

**The fix:** I set up budget rules:
- **Spring (March–May):** $800/month. High search volume, high conversion rate.
- **Summer (June–August):** $400/month. Moderate volume, lower conversion for maintenance.
- **Fall (September–November):** $700/month. Pre-winter prep peak.
- **Winter (December–February):** $200/month. Only emergency keywords active.

We also used ad scheduling. Most calls for maintenance come between 9 AM and 2 PM on weekdays. Homeowners are at work, but they're thinking about it. We paused ads after 6 PM and on Sundays. Emergency calls still came in through the website form, but we didn't waste budget showing ads at 2 AM for non-emergency keywords.

**Outcome:** Their annual ad spend actually increased slightly—from $6,000 to $6,600—but their leads went from 84 to 142 per year. Their cost per lead dropped from $71 to $46. More importantly, they stopped missing calls during peak season. Their April revenue from maintenance contracts alone was $3,800—against $800 in ad spend. That's a 4.75x return.

## Seasonal Timing: When to Run Ads for Maximum Impact

Let me give you a specific calendar based on what actually works for septic companies in the US. This isn't generic advice. This is what I've seen work in cold climates like Denver and warmer ones like Austin.

**January–February:** This is your quiet season in most of the country. But don't pause everything. Run a small $200/month campaign targeting "septic freeze repair" and "septic emergency" keywords. If winter hits hard, you'll be the first number they see. Also run ads for "schedule spring maintenance" with a call-to-action like "Book your March appointment now—limited slots available." People in cold regions know spring is coming. They'll book early if you prompt them.

**March–May:** This is your money season. Pump your budget to $700–$900/month. Run separate campaigns for:
- **Spring maintenance inspections:** "Spring septic inspection," "after winter septic check"
- **Pumping:** "septic tank pumping spring special"
- **General maintenance:** "annual septic maintenance contract"

Use ad copy that mentions "snow melt" and "ground thaw" if you're in the north. In the south, use "spring rain" and "ground saturation." I've seen cost per click drop 15–20% just by aligning ad copy with seasonal language because it improves Quality Score.

**June–August:** Summer is for maintenance, not emergencies. Keep a $400–$500/month budget. Focus on "septic tank maintenance" and "schedule pumping." Avoid "emergency" keywords unless you actually want emergency jobs. In summer, most people just need routine pumping and inspection.

**September–November:** Another peak season. People are prepping for winter. Run a $700–$800/month campaign with keywords like "winter septic prep," "fall septic inspection," "pre-winter pumping." Use ad copy that mentions "avoid frozen pipes" and "winter-ready inspection." In the south, focus on "fall maintenance before holiday guests arrive." I've seen homeowners book maintenance in October because they're hosting Thanksgiving and don't want a backup.

**December:** Keep $200–$300/month. Run emergency keywords and "winter maintenance contract" ads. Some people will book for January if you offer a discount.

**One specific example:** A client in Denver ran this seasonal schedule in 2023. In March, they spent $780 and got 22 leads. Of those, 6 converted to maintenance contracts ($250/year each = $1,500 recurring revenue). Another 4 converted to one-time pumping jobs ($275 average). Total revenue from that month's ad spend: $2,600 against $780 spend. That's a 3.3x return in one month. In the quiet months, they broke even or slightly lost money. But over the year, their total ad spend was $6,200 and their total revenue from Google Ads leads was $24,700. That's a 4x return.

## Beyond Google: Tools That Actually Work for Septic Services

Google Ads is the main channel, but there are three other tools I recommend for septic companies that want consistent, recurring revenue. I'm not going to list 12 tools—just the ones that work based on what I've seen.

### 1. Google Local Service Ads (LSAs)

If you're in a market where Google offers LSAs for septic services (they're rolling out to more categories each quarter), use them. LSAs work differently than search ads. You bid on a lead, and you only pay when a potential customer contacts you through the ad. Google verifies your business, so you get the "Google Guaranteed" badge.

**The catch:** LSAs are expensive per lead. I've seen costs of $35–$60 per lead for septic services in cities like Chicago, Nashville, and Portland. But the lead quality is higher because Google pre-qualifies them. One client in Nashville got 12 leads from LSAs in a month, paid $480, and booked 8 jobs worth $2,200 total. That's a 4.6x return.

**The fix for cost sensitivity:** Run LSAs alongside your standard search ads. Use LSAs for the high-intent, urgent keywords where you're okay paying $50 per lead. Use standard search ads for the lower-funnel, maintenance keywords where cost per lead is $20–$35.

### 2. Mailchimp for Maintenance Contract Follow-Ups

This is the tool most septic owners ignore, and it's a mistake. The people who call you for a one-time pumping are your best prospects for a maintenance contract. But you need to follow up—and most owners forget after the job is done.

Set up a Mailchimp sequence:
- **Day 1:** Thank you email with invoice and "p.s. we offer annual maintenance contracts."
- **Day 14:** Follow-up email. "Your tank was at 30% capacity. Want us to schedule next year's pumping?"
- **Month 2:** Reminder email. "We have openings in [season] for maintenance contracts."
- **Month 6:** Annual reminder. "It's been 6 months since your last pumping. Book your next one."

I set this up for a septic company in Portland. They had 85 one-time customers from the previous year. The email sequence converted 12 of them to maintenance contracts at $200/year each. That's $2,400 in recurring revenue from zero additional ad spend.

### 3. Booksy or Square Appointments for Booking

I don't care which you use, but get an online booking system. Septic homeowners are often searching at odd hours—after their kids are in bed, during lunch breaks, while waiting in the carpool line. If they have to call you during business hours, you lose them.

Booksy is more common for service businesses. Square Appointments integrates with Square payments and email marketing. Either works.

**Why it matters:** A client in Austin added a "Book Online" button to their Google Ads landing page. Their conversion rate for maintenance contracts jumped from 2.3% to 4.8%. People who booked online were also more likely to show up for their appointment—87% attendance vs. 72% for phone bookings. That matters when you're scheduling trucks and drivers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should I actually spend on Google Ads for a small septic business?**

Start with $500–$600/month if you're in a mid-sized city (200,000–500,000 population). In a bigger city like Chicago or Dallas, expect $800–$1,200. In a smaller town, $300 is probably enough. The key is testing for 60 days, then doubling down on what works. I've never seen a septic company need more than $1,500/month to dominate a local market unless they're in a city like New York where everyone competes for the same keywords.

**Q: How long until I see my first lead?**

If you set up the campaign correctly—tight location targeting, exact match keywords, good ad copy—you'll get your first click within 2 hours. First lead within 24–48 hours. But quality leads (people who book a maintenance contract) usually take 5–10 days because they need to see your ad 2–3 times before they trust you enough to call. Don't panic if day one is quiet.

**Q: What if I'm in a small town with only 10,000 people? Will Google Ads even work?**

Yes, but differently. You'll want to target a 20-mile radius that includes your town and the surrounding rural areas. Septic systems are more common outside dense city limits, so your actual addressable market might be 50,000 people across several towns. Keep your budget at $200–$300/month. Focus on exact match keywords for your town name and the nearest larger cities. One client in a town of 8,000 in Wisconsin got 3 leads in their first week and booked 2, both maintenance contracts.

**Q: How do I track if someone actually booked a job from my ad?**

Set up conversion tracking in Google Ads. It's free. Every time someone calls you from the ad (use Google's call tracking) or fills out your website form, Google counts it as a conversion. Then ask every new customer, "How did you hear about us?" The combination of call tracking and that question will tell you exactly which ads are working.

**Q: Should I use broad match keywords or exact match?**

Exact match. Period. Broad match will show your ad to people searching "how to build a septic tank" and "septic tank cleaning supplies"—people who will never hire you. Exact match only shows your ad when someone types your exact keyword or a very close variation. You'll get fewer clicks but way better quality. Start with exact match, add phrase match after 30 days, and never touch broad match unless you have a $5,000/month budget to waste.

**Q: What do I do if my competitors are spending way more than me?**

You don't need to outspend them. You need to out-target them. They're probably bidding on broad terms and spending $5,000/month. You can win by:
- Bidding on long-tail keywords: "septic tank maintenance cost [city]" instead of "septic services"
- Using ad scheduling to show ads only during peak hours (9 AM–2 PM weekdays)
- Running call-only ads that skip the website and go straight to your phone
- Writing ad copy that mentions your specific pricing or offer
- Using location targeting within a 10-mile radius instead of 50 miles

I saw a company in Nashville with a $500/month budget beat a competitor spending $3,000/month by doing exactly this. The competitor was getting 50 low-quality leads per month and booking maybe 5 jobs. The $500 guy got 15 high-quality leads and booked 10 jobs. Revenue was similar, but his ad spend was 6x lower.
