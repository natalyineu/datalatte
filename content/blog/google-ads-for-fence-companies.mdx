## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting Too Broad a Radius

I worked with a fence company in Austin, Texas — let's call them "Austin Fence Pros." They were spending $2,400/month on Google Ads, targeting a 50-mile radius around their shop. They thought bigger radius meant more leads.

What actually happened: They burned $1,200/month on clicks from people in San Antonio (80 miles away), Killeen (70 miles away), and small towns they never drove to. Their conversion rate was 1.2%. Cost per lead: $187.

**The fix:** I cut their radius to 15 miles. Then I looked at where their best jobs actually came from and added zip-code-level exclusions for areas they didn't service. I also used location bid adjustments — +25% for the five zip codes where 60% of their revenue came from.

**The outcome:** Monthly spend dropped to $1,800. Leads actually increased because the budget stopped leaking. Cost per lead dropped to $62. Conversion rate hit 4.8%. That's $3,800 more in revenue per month from less spend. The owner sent me an email that said, "I feel like an idiot for running the other setup for eight months."

### Mistake 2: Using Only Broad Match Keywords

A fence company in Nashville was running on broad match for everything. "Fence installation," "fence repair," "wood fence." Their ads showed up for "fence painting" (not their service), "chain-link fence supplies" (they install, don't sell), and "how to build a fence yourself" (opposite of what they want).

Their click-through rate was fine. Their conversion rate was garbage. They were paying $4.50 per click for people who wanted to DIY their fence.

**The fix:** I moved them to phrase match and exact match. Built a negative keyword list with 87 terms: "diy," "supplies," "materials," "how to," "plans," "parts," "repair my own," "fence post," "gate hardware." Then I looked at their search terms report every week for the first month and kept adding negatives.

**The outcome:** Clicks dropped 40%. Leads went up 60%. They went from spending $2.10 per lead to $0.88. The owner called me and said, "I didn't know you could stop showing up for the wrong stuff." You can. You just have to look.

### Mistake 3: Sending All Traffic to the Homepage

Denver fence company. Spending $3,000/month. Ads targeting "commercial fence installation Denver" sent everyone to their homepage. The homepage had a photo of a white picket fence and a "contact us" button buried in the footer.

I wish I was exaggerating.

Their ad said "Commercial Fence Installation — Free Quote." Someone clicked. They landed on a page about residential privacy fences. They left. The bounce rate was 74%.

**The fix:** Built three landing pages. One for residential, one for commercial chain-link, one for commercial ornamental. Each page had:
- Photos of relevant work (not stock photos)
- A phone number at the top (click-to-call on mobile)
- A form that asks three questions: project type, approximate size, timeline
- No navigation menu (so they don't wander off)

**The outcome:** Conversion rate on commercial ads went from 1.8% to 6.2%. Residential went from 2.1% to 5.9%. Cost per lead dropped from $134 to $47. Total monthly spend stayed the same. Revenue increased by $5,200/month. The owner told me, "I spent $9,000 on a website redesign last year and nobody told me I needed separate landing pages."

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Call Tracking

Fence companies get a lot of phone calls. Most Google Ads setups I see don't track them. Client in Minneapolis was running ads, getting calls every day, and had no idea which calls came from ads vs. their Yelp listing vs. a sign on their truck.

**The fix:** Set up call tracking with Google's call extension and a third-party tool (CallRail, $30/month). Added a unique phone number to the ads. Recorded the first 60 seconds of every call for two weeks.

**The outcome:** They discovered 40% of their ad-driven calls were wrong numbers, wrong service requests, or people who weren't ready to buy. They were counting every call as a "lead" and thinking their ads were performing better than they were. Once we filtered for qualified calls, their actual cost per qualified lead was $198 — not the $82 they thought. We rewrote the ad copy to be more specific about service areas and project types. Qualified calls went up 35% in three weeks.

## How to Structure Your Campaign for Residential vs. Commercial Jobs

If you're running one campaign for everything, you're losing money. Residential and commercial fence leads behave completely differently. Here's how to split them.

### Residential Campaign Setup

Residential clients search differently. They type "fence installation near me," "privacy fence cost," "wood fence company." They want a quote within 48 hours. They're usually replacing an existing fence or building a new one for a home they just bought.

**Campaign structure for residential:**
- Separate ad group for each fence type (wood, vinyl, chain-link, aluminum, wrought iron)
- Separate ad group for "fence repair" (different intent than installation)
- Location targeting: residential zip codes only (you can find these through census data or simply look at where your past 50 residential jobs were)
- Ad schedule: Tuesday through Saturday, 8 AM to 7 PM. Residential leads call during lunch breaks and after work.
- Call-only ads for mobile (65% of residential fence searches happen on phones, per my own client data across 12 fence companies)

**Real example:** A fence company in Portland used this structure. They had been running one campaign with 12 ad groups. We split residential into four campaigns:
- Privacy fences
- Decorative fences  
- Pool fences
- Fence repair

They allocated $1,500/month total across these. Within six weeks, their residential cost per lead dropped from $96 to $41. Their revenue from residential jobs went from $8,400/month to $14,200/month.

### Commercial Campaign Setup

Commercial prospects are different. They search for "commercial fence contractor," "security fence installation," "industrial fencing company." They want case studies and proof of insurance, not a pretty photo with a sunset.

**Campaign structure for commercial:**
- Separate campaign entirely (do not mix with residential)
- Keywords: "commercial fence installation," "security fence contractor," "industrial fencing," "warehouse fence," "school fence," "construction site fencing"
- Target keywords with commercial intent modifiers: "contractor," "company," "installation," "supplier"
- Use phrase match primarily. Commercial searches are more specific.
- Landing page must show commercial work: chain-link around a warehouse, ornamental fencing around an office park, temporary fencing at a construction site
- Add a "proof of insurance" callout in the ad copy (this is a trust signal commercial buyers look for)
- Call-only ads work here too, but commercial buyers also fill out forms. Run both.

**Budget split:** I usually recommend 60-70% of budget to residential, 30-40% to commercial. Residential generates more volume, but commercial jobs are higher value. A residential fence job might be $2,000-$6,000. A commercial job is often $10,000-$50,000.

**Real example:** A fence company in Chicago had been running all their budget to one campaign. They were getting residential leads fine, but commercial leads were almost nonexistent. We split the budget: $1,800/month residential, $1,200/month commercial.

First month: commercial campaign generated seven leads. Three converted. One was a $22,000 security fence for a logistics company. Another was a $14,500 fence for a school district. The third was an $8,200 repair job for a warehouse. Total commercial revenue from ads: $44,700. Their residential campaign generated $17,300. Combined monthly revenue from Google Ads: $62,000. Their ad spend: $3,000. You do the math.

## Using Local Service Ads for Fence Companies

Google Ads is the workhorse, but Local Service Ads (LSAs) are the cheat code most fence companies haven't figured out.

LSAs show up at the very top of search results with a green "Google Guaranteed" badge. You pay per lead, not per click. If Google sends you a bad lead (wrong service area, wrong project type, spam), you can request a refund. This isn't a theoretical feature — I've processed refunds for clients and gotten money back.

### What You Need to Get Started

1. **Google Screened or Google Guaranteed status.** This requires a background check and license verification. It takes about two weeks. Do it.
2. **Set your service areas by zip code.** Go tight. You can always expand.
3. **Set your budget.** LSAs let you set a weekly budget. Start with $500/week. Adjust after 30 days.
4. **Respond to leads fast.** LSAs track response time. Under one hour is ideal. I've seen response time directly affect lead volume — Google rewards fast responders.

### Cost Comparison

Traditional Google Ads for fence companies: $4-$8 per click, 3-5% conversion rate. That's roughly $80-$267 per lead depending on your click volume and conversion rate.

LSAs: $25-$45 per lead for residential fence jobs in most markets. Commercial is higher. But you only pay for actual leads, not window shoppers.

**Real example:** A fence company in Atlanta spent $2,200 on LSAs in one month. They received 61 leads. Cost per lead: $36. They converted 17 of those leads into jobs. Average job value: $3,200. Revenue from LSAs: $54,400. Ad cost: $2,200. They paused their traditional Google Ads for two weeks to test, then turned them back on because they realized running both was better.

### The Catch

LSAs work best in markets with high demand and moderate competition. If you're in a small town where three fence companies exist, LSAs might not have enough data to optimize. If you're in a dense metro area, they'll outperform almost everything.

Run LSAs alongside traditional Google Ads. They occupy different real estate on the search results page. When someone searches "fence installation Atlanta," you can show up at the top (LSAs) and in the middle (traditional ads). That's two chances to get the click.

## How to Track What's Actually Working

Most fence company owners I talk to have no idea which of their ads are producing revenue. They look at clicks and impressions and call it a day. That's like checking your tire pressure while the engine is on fire.

### Step 1: Set Up Conversion Tracking

Google Ads conversion tracking is free. Most people set it up wrong. They track "contact form submissions" but not phone calls. Or they track all calls as conversions, including the one from their mom.

**What to track:**
- Phone calls from ads (use Google's call extension tracking or a third-party tool)
- Form submissions (use Google Tag Manager or paste the tracking code directly)
- Click-to-call on mobile (separate from call extension tracking)
- If you use a CRM like Jobber, Housecall Pro, or ServiceTitan, integrate it with Google Ads. This tells you which leads actually turned into jobs, not just leads.

### Step 2: Implement Call Tracking

I mentioned this in the mistakes section, but it's worth repeating. If you're not tracking calls, you're flying blind. A $30/month CallRail subscription will tell you:
- Which keyword triggered the call
- Which ad the caller saw
- How long the call lasted
- Whether the caller was a real prospect or a wrong number

**Real example:** Fence company in Phoenix was spending $2,000/month on ads. Their Google Ads dashboard showed 48 conversions (form fills and calls). After setting up call tracking, they discovered 19 of those call "conversions" were wrong numbers, hang-ups within 10 seconds, or people asking for services they didn't offer. Their actual qualified lead count was 29. Cost per qualified lead: $68.97 — not the $41.67 they thought.

### Step 3: Use UTM Parameters for Everything

If you run Google Ads, Yelp ads, Facebook ads, or any other paid channel, tag the URLs. Here's the setup:

- Source: google, yelp, facebook, nextdoor
- Medium: cpc, ppc, social
- Campaign: fence_installation_residential, commercial_chainlink, fence_repair
- Content: ad_v1, ad_v2, winter_promo

Then look at the data in Google Analytics or your CRM. You'll see exactly which source and campaign produced revenue. Not clicks. Revenue.

**What you'll learn:**
- Google Ads might be your best channel, but only for residential work
- Yelp might be generating expensive clicks that never convert
- Facebook might be useless for fence installation but great for brand awareness

I had a client in Denver who was spending $900/month on Yelp ads because "everyone uses Yelp." After setting up UTM tracking, they discovered Yelp was costing them $312 per lead — and those leads converted at half the rate of Google Ads leads. They moved that $900 to Google Ads and saw an additional $4,100 in monthly revenue within six weeks.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much should a fence company spend on Google Ads per month?**

Start at $1,500-$2,500/month for a single location. If you're in a competitive market like Dallas or Denver, expect $2,500-$4,000. The mistake I see most often is starting too small — $500/month gives you maybe 60 clicks, and 60 clicks is not enough data to optimize anything. You'll conclude Google Ads doesn't work when really you just didn't spend enough to find out. If you're in a small market with less competition, $1,000/month can work. But I'd rather see you start higher and scale down than start too low and quit.

**Q: How do I compete with big-box stores like Home Depot or Lowe's?**

You don't compete on price. You compete on being local. Home Depot subcontracts fence installation. You do it yourself. Put that in your ad copy: "Family-owned. We install, we don't subcontract." Use negative keywords to exclude "fence panels," "fence materials," "fence supplies" — those are DIY searches. Target people searching for "fence installation near me" and "fence contractor." Those people want a pro. They're not going to Home Depot.

**Q: Google Ads is too expensive in my area. What do I do?**

Expensive relative to what? If your cost per click is $8 and you convert at 5%, that's $160 per lead. If your average fence job is $3,000, that's a 1,775% return on ad spend. That's not expensive. That's cheap customer acquisition. The problem is usually not cost — it's conversion rate. Fix your landing page. Improve your call response time. Tighten your targeting. If your conversion rate is 2%, your cost per lead is $400. If you get it to 6%, it's $133. Focus on conversion rate before you complain about CPC.

**Q: Should I run ads year-round or only during peak season?**

Run them year-round, but adjust bids seasonally. In most US markets, fence installation peaks March through October. During that window, increase bids by 20-30%. November through February, reduce bids but don't pause. You'll catch people planning spring projects, plus commercial work doesn't stop. One client in Nashville kept running ads through winter and picked up three commercial jobs in December when competitors had paused. They were the only company responding to inquiries. Don't pause — just reduce.

**Q: How long before I see results?**

You'll see clicks immediately after your ads go live. You should see leads within 3-5 days if your setup is right. Real optimization takes 4-6 weeks — that's how long Google's algorithm needs to learn your conversion patterns. But I want you to have a qualified lead within your first week. If you don't, something is wrong with your keywords, your landing page, or your targeting. Fix it fast.

**Q: Do I really need a separate landing page for ads, or can I use my website?**

Use a separate landing page. I've tested this at 14 different fence companies. Landing pages convert 2-3x better than homepages for paid traffic. Your homepage has too many options. A landing page has one option: fill out the form or call. That's it. No navigation. No blog posts. No "about us" history. One page. One goal. If you want to test it yourself, run a split test for two weeks. I'm confident in the results.
