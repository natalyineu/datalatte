## ## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Buying HubSpot because you "might need it later"

I watched a 2-chair salon in Austin, Texas burn $1,800 in four months on a HubSpot Professional plan they didn't need. The owner, Rachel, ran a solid operation — 60–70 regulars, a waitlist for weekend appointments, and a decent Instagram presence. She signed up for HubSpot because a marketing friend told her "you'll grow into it."

She didn't.

What actually happened: Rachel spent 15 hours over the first month trying to set up pipelines she never used, custom properties for data she didn't collect, and workflows that automated... nothing. Her actual needs were: send appointment reminders, promote a monthly nail art special, and track who hadn't visited in 90 days. HubSpot was a power drill when she needed a screwdriver.

**The fix:** I walked her through migrating to ActiveCampaign's Plus plan at $49/month. We built three workflows in one afternoon: (1) appointment reminder 48 hours before, (2) re-engagement sequence for clients inactive 90+ days, (3) monthly newsletter with that month's specials. Total setup time: three hours. Total monthly contacts: 180.

**The outcome:** Rachel saved $1,320 over the next six months ($150/month difference × 6 months). Her re-engagement sequence brought back 12 clients who hadn't visited in 4+ months — worth about $960 in additional appointments. She told me later: "I spent more time cancelling HubSpot than I did setting up ActiveCampaign."

The lesson: If your contact list is under 1,000 and you're not running a sales team with multiple deal stages, don't pay for the Ferrari.

### Mistake #2: Importing a dirty list and blaming the tool

A pet grooming business in Portland, Oregon — three groomers, one location, 850 email addresses — signed up for ActiveCampaign and saw open rates drop to 11% within six weeks. The owner, Mike, blamed the platform. "ActiveCampaign's deliverability is trash," he told me in an email.

I asked to see his list. He'd been collecting emails in a notebook at the front counter for three years. Names, sometimes. Email addresses? Whatever the client scribbled. He'd imported every single one — including "gmail@gmail.com", "no@thankyou.com", and seventeen variations of his own email from testing.

**What actually happened:** ActiveCampaign's system flagged his account for high bounce rates. 230 out of 850 addresses bounced. Another 140 had never opened a single email in the entire time he'd been collecting them — dead addresses. The platform saw spam signals and started routing his emails to promotions folders.

**The fix:** We purged the list. Removed every address that had never opened, every obvious fake, every address older than 18 months with no engagement. That cut his list to 480 contacts. Then we ran a re-engagement sequence for the remaining borderline ones — if they didn't open two emails in 30 days, they got removed, too.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, open rates went from 11% to 38%. Click-through rates hit 6%. Mike's monthly newsletter promoting "$5 off any groom when you rebook at checkout" went from generating maybe two redemptions per send to averaging eleven. That's roughly $550 in additional monthly revenue — $6,600 annually — from a list that was half the size.

The lesson: ActiveCampaign and HubSpot both deliver fine. Garbage in, garbage out. Clean your list before you import. And for the love of god, stop collecting emails on paper.

### Mistake #3: Setting up automation but never checking it

A fitness studio in Nashville — 12 instructors, 350 active clients, $7,500/month in recurring revenue — had been running the same ActiveCampaign automation for 18 months. The owner, Jenna, was frustrated that "nobody reads the emails anymore."

I asked to see the automation. It was a welcome sequence she'd built during a free trial in 2024. New client books a class → receives email with studio rules → receives email with class schedule → receives email with membership pricing. Fine for year one.

The problem: The studio had changed location eight months prior. The welcome email still had the old address. The class schedule attached was from the previous winter. The membership pricing email quoted rates $30/month lower than what new clients actually paid. Every new client who joined got a sequence that told them the wrong location, the wrong schedule, and the wrong price.

**What actually happened:** Jenna had set the automation, checked it once, and never touched it again. 18 months later, it was running on autopilot, actively misleading every new person who joined. No wonder nobody read the emails — they were useless.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the entire sequence in one afternoon. Four emails, updated for the new location. Real class schedule. Current pricing. Added a trigger: if a new client didn't book a second class within 14 days, they got a personal text from the instructor (via ActiveCampaign's SMS add-on, $25/month extra).

**The outcome:** Second-class bookings jumped from 41% to 67% within two months. That 26% increase meant 20 additional retained clients over the period, worth about $600/month in recurring revenue. Total cost of the fix: $25/month for SMS and three hours of my time.

The lesson: Automations are not "set and forget." They're "set and check quarterly." If you can't remember the last time you looked at yours, it's probably broken.

## What Nobody Tells You About Migrating Between These Tools

Here's what I've seen at three different clients who switched from one to the other: migration is where the hidden costs live.

A dog daycare in Denver tried to move from ActiveCampaign to HubSpot because they wanted "better reporting." They exported their list — 1,400 contacts — and imported it into HubSpot. Two weeks later, they realized:

- All their tags became lists (which HubSpot charges extra for)
- Their automation history was gone — new contacts had to start from scratch
- They lost every email send count from the previous 18 months
- Their segments broke because ActiveCampaign's logic doesn't translate directly to HubSpot's

**The fix cost:** They paid a freelance HubSpot consultant $800 to rebuild everything. That consultant took three weeks. The owner spent another 10 hours on the phone with both support teams trying to recover data.

**Actual migration cost:** $800 consultant fee + 20 hours of the owner's time (at $50/hour, that's $1,000) + $1,200 in lost email revenue during the migration period when nothing was sending. Total: $3,000.

The outcome: Six months later, they moved back to ActiveCampaign. Same cost, different direction.

**What I recommend instead:** If you're under 2,000 contacts and your workflows are simple (welcome sequence, birthday email, re-engagement), migrating is probably not worth the headache. The tools are more similar than different at that scale. The cost of switching — in time, money, and lost momentum — usually outweighs the benefit.

But if you absolutely must switch (maybe you're moving from HubSpot to ActiveCampaign to save money), do this:

1. Export everything — contacts, tags, automations, email stats — into CSVs before you cancel
2. Rebuild your automations manually in the new tool (don't try to import them — it won't work)
3. Run both tools in parallel for 30 days before canceling the old one
4. Accept that you will lose historical data. Plan for it.

A boutique hotel in Savannah did this right. They moved from HubSpot ($499/month) to ActiveCampaign ($149/month) because they were only using about 40% of HubSpot's features. They rebuilt three automations in ActiveCampaign over a weekend, ran both tools for three weeks, and canceled HubSpot. Saved $4,200 annually. No consultant. No crisis.

The difference: they went in knowing what they'd lose and decided it was worth it.

## Integrations That Actually Matter for Local Businesses

Both tools claim to integrate with "thousands of apps." For a local business owner, that's noise. Here are the integrations you actually need to care about.

### Square

If you run a coffee shop, salon, or any retail with a Square POS, this is the most important integration you'll set up.

With ActiveCampaign + Square: Every customer who pays by card automatically enters your CRM. You can see what they bought, how often they visit, and how much they spend. Then you can automate: "You haven't visited in 60 days — here's a free pastry on us."

With HubSpot + Square: Same thing, but HubSpot's free tier doesn't include this integration. You need the Starter plan at $99/month.

**Real example:** A bagel shop in Brooklyn with 850 Square transactions/month imported all that purchase data into ActiveCampaign. They built a simple automation: any customer who spent over $50 in a single visit gets a "VIP" tag and receives a monthly "try our new flavor first" email. Open rate on those VIP emails: 52%. Monthly redemption rate: one in four recipients visited within the week.

**Cost:** ActiveCampaign Lite ($9/month) + Square integration (free) + the shop owner's time to build the automation (two hours).

### Booksy or Vagaro (for salons and studios)

If you run a hair salon, nail studio, or fitness studio using Booksy for booking, the integration with ActiveCampaign is straightforward. HubSpot doesn't have a native Booksy integration — you'd need a third-party tool like Zapier.

**Real example:** A nail salon in Dallas with four technicians used Booksy + ActiveCampaign to reduce no-shows by 33%. They set up: Booksy booking triggers an ActiveCampaign automation that sends a text reminder 48 hours before (via ActiveCampaign's SMS add-on) and an email 24 hours before. If the client doesn't confirm the appointment within 12 hours of the text, the system sends a follow-up asking to rebook.

**Cost:** ActiveCampaign ($49/month for Plus, which includes SMS) + Booksy integration (free) + 20 minutes to set up the automation.

**Outcome:** No-shows dropped from 14% to 9.4%. Over six months, that saved roughly $2,100 in lost appointments.

### Google Business Profile (via Zapier)

Neither HubSpot nor ActiveCampaign integrates directly with Google Business Profile. But here's where Zapier earns its keep.

Set up: When someone leaves a Google review (positive or negative), Zapier captures it and sends a notification to ActiveCampaign. If the review is 4 stars or above, the reviewer gets added to a "brand advocates" list and receives a thank-you email with a referral link. If the review is 3 stars or below, the reviewer gets a personal email from the owner asking what went wrong.

**Real example:** A pizza place in Austin used this setup. Every 5-star review triggered an email with a coupon for $5 off their next order. Over three months, they sent 47 such coupons. Redeemed: 19. That's $95 in discounts for $475 in incremental revenue.

**Cost:** Zapier ($20/month for starter plan) + ActiveCampaign ($49/month for Plus).

### Mailchimp (if you're switching)

If you're currently on Mailchimp and considering switching to ActiveCampaign or HubSpot, here's the honest truth: Mailchimp's free tier is fine for under 500 contacts. I've seen businesses run perfectly well on Mailchimp Free for years.

But if you're hitting 1,000+ contacts and need automations that actually work (Mailchimp's automations are clunky and limited), ActiveCampaign is the better value. A hair salon in San Diego migrated from Mailchimp Standard ($59/month for 2,000 contacts) to ActiveCampaign Plus ($49/month for unlimited contacts). They saved $120/year and got better automation.

The migration itself took about four hours — exporting contacts, re-creating their three automations, updating forms on their website. No data loss, no drama.

**Don't switch just because everyone else is.** Switch when your current tool actively prevents you from doing something you need.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I really run a business on a $9/month plan?**

Yes, if you have under 500 contacts and only need basic email sending. I've seen a two-person coffee roasting operation in Portland run on the $9/month ActiveCampaign Lite plan for two years. They had a list of 320 wholesale buyers, sent a monthly product update, and used the calendar integration to schedule everything in advance. The only reason they upgraded was because they hit 501 contacts and ActiveCampaign requires the next tier.

The catch: $9/month gets you no automations, no conditional content, no A/B testing. For a coffee shop that just wants to send a weekly "today's special" email, it's fine. For a salon that wants appointment reminders and re-engagement sequences, you'll need at least the Plus plan at $49/month.

**Q: Which tool has better deliverability?**

I've tested both at scale across multiple clients. Neither is meaningfully better. Both have deliverability issues if you: (1) buy email lists (don't), (2) import dirty data (don't), (3) send to inactive subscribers (don't).

Here's what actually matters for deliverability: your sender reputation, your domain authentication, and your engagement rates. If 40% of your list hasn't opened in 90 days, neither HubSpot nor ActiveCampaign will save you.

Both tools offer SPF, DKIM, and DMARC setup guides. Use them. If you're sending fewer than 5,000 emails per month and you're not blacklisted, deliverability will be fine on either platform.

**Q: What happens when I grow past 1,000 contacts?**

This is where the pricing diverges significantly.

**ActiveCampaign:** At 1,001 contacts, you're at $39/month (Lite) or $69/month (Plus). At 2,501 contacts, $69/month or $119/month. The pricing scales gradually.

**HubSpot:** At 1,000 contacts on Starter, you're at $99/month. Jump to 2,000 contacts? $199/month. The jump is steeper because HubSpot bundles contacts with features you might not need.

For most local businesses, ActiveCampaign stays cheaper until you hit 5,000+ contacts. At that point, HubSpot becomes more competitive if you're also using their full CRM, meeting scheduling, and reporting.

**Q: Can I test both before committing?**

HubSpot offers a free CRM with limited features. You can add paid features later. But the free version is stripped down — no automation, limited reporting, HubSpot branding on emails.

ActiveCampaign offers a 14-day free trial with full features. No credit card required. You can test automations, integrations, and sending before paying.

My recommendation: sign up for ActiveCampaign's trial. If you can't get your core workflow working within three hours, switch to HubSpot's free CRM and evaluate from there. If you can't get it working in either tool within three hours, the problem might not be the tool.

**Q: What if I need a proper sales CRM with deal tracking?**

Both tools have this. HubSpot's is better for B2B sales teams. ActiveCampaign's is adequate for service businesses that track leads through stages like "inquiry → booked → visited → returned."

If you're a dog groomer or fitness coach, ActiveCampaign's deal tracking is fine. If you're a construction contractor managing 50+ active leads with $10,000+ deal values, HubSpot's pipeline features justify the cost.

**Q: Is it worth hiring someone to set this up?**

For a basic setup (import contacts, build two automations, create three email templates), no. You can learn both tools in a weekend.

For anything more complex — CRM setup with custom pipelines, integration with Square or Booksy, migrating from another tool, building a multi-step lead nurturing sequence — yes, hire someone. Expect to pay $300–$800 for a proper setup. I've seen business owners waste $1,200+ by doing it wrong and having to redo everything later.

The threshold: if you've watched three tutorials and still can't build your first automation, you're better off paying for setup.
