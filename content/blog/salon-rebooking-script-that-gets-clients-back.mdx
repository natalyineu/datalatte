## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

I've watched salon owners wreck perfectly good rebooking systems more times than I've had bad agency coffee. Which is, genuinely, a lot. Here are the three most common mistakes I see — and the fixes that actually work outside of a conference room.

### Mistake #1: Chasing the Wrong Clients with Generic Reminders

**The story:** A salon owner in Portland, Oregon — let's call it "The Locksmith" — spent $1,200/month on Facebook ads targeting "women 25-45 interested in hair color." They got 40 new appointments in three months. Great, right? Only 8 of those clients rebooked. They lost money on every single one after the first visit.

The problem wasn't the ads. It was the follow-up. The salon sent the same "book your next appointment!" text to every client — whether they'd gotten a $250 balayage or a $35 bang trim. The $35 client felt harassed. The $250 client felt undervalued because the message was indistinguishable from the $35 one.

**The fix:** Segment your rebooking messages by service type and ticket size. Within two weeks, The Locksmith created three automated sequences in their Booksy account:
- High-ticket color clients ($150+): Personal text from their actual stylist within 48 hours. "Hey Sarah, I loved how that copper tone turned out. Want to book your gloss refresh in 6-8 weeks?"
- Mid-range cuts and styles ($60-$149): Automated email with a "book now" link and a $10 discount if they rebook within 7 days.
- Entry-level services (under $60): A simple text with available dates, no hard sell.

**The outcome:** Rebooking rate from the high-ticket segment hit 73% within two months. Overall retention went from 22% to 41%. They stopped running the Facebook ads entirely and redirected that $1,200/month into a Google Ads campaign targeting "balayage Portland" — which brought in 32 new clients at $38 per acquisition. Those clients had a rebooking rate of 58% because the system was already tuned for them.

### Mistake #2: The "I'll Just Ask at the Front Desk" Approach

**The story:** A salon in Austin, Texas — let's say it's called "Bishop & Mane" — had a rebooking rate of 19%. The owner, Jen, told me her front desk staff "always asks" clients to rebook. I spent an afternoon watching. They didn't.

Here's what actually happened: A client would pay, the receptionist would say "want to book your next one?" while already reaching for the next client's credit card. The client would say "I'll call" and leave. Nobody followed up. Jen was losing an estimated $3,800/month in potential rebookings based on her average ticket of $95 and the fact that 38 clients per week weren't booking their next appointment.

**The fix:** I had Jen implement a three-part system that required zero additional staff:
1. **The payment prompt:** When the POS screen flips to the tip/total screen, the stylist says: "I have openings on Tuesdays and Thursdays in six weeks for your gloss. Want me to hold one before you pay?"
2. **The confirmation follow-up:** If the client says no, an automated text goes out 72 hours later from Booksy with available dates for that specific stylist.
3. **The expiration trigger:** If the client hasn't booked within 10 days, the system sends a "price lock" message — their current rate is held for the next appointment if they book within 48 hours.

**The outcome:** Rebooking rate went from 19% to 64% in six weeks. Monthly recurring revenue from existing clients jumped from $7,200 to $18,400. Jen stopped asking her front desk to "just remember" and let the system do the heavy lifting.

### Mistake #3: Treating Every Client the Same on Frequency

**The story:** A nail salon in Chicago — "The Painted Lady" — had a policy of reminding every client to rebook every 4 weeks. For gel manicures, that's perfect. For regular polish clients, it's too soon. For their acrylic clients, they needed 3 weeks. But the owner had one script for everyone.

The result: Regular polish clients ignored the reminders because they felt wrong. Acrylic clients had visible regrowth by week 3 and were booking with a different salon that sent reminders at the right interval. The salon's rebooking rate was stuck at 31% for 18 months.

**The fix:** They rebuilt their service menu in Square Appointments with a "recommended rebooking interval" field for each service:
- Gel manicure: 21 days
- Regular manicure: 10 days
- Acrylic fill: 14 days
- Pedicure: 30 days
- Dip powder: 21 days

The system automatically personalized the reminder timing. They also added a "last service" field so the reminder said things like "Your gel manicure is due for a refresh" instead of "Time to book!"

**The outcome:** Rebooking rate hit 71% within three months. The acrylic segment, which had been hemorrhaging clients to a competitor 3 blocks away, stabilized and started growing. Monthly revenue from repeat clients went from $4,600 to $8,300. The owner told me she'd been "overcomplicating it" — it wasn't about a better script, it was about the right script for the right person at the right time.

## How to Use Google Ads to Fill Gaps in Your Rebooking Calendar

A good rebooking system fills next week's slots. A great one fills next month's. But even the best system will have gaps — especially Tuesday mornings at 10 AM and Thursday afternoons at 2 PM. Here's how I've used Google Ads to plug those holes without burning cash.

**The math you need first:** Calculate your "gap cost." If you have 5 open slots per week at an average ticket of $80, that's $400/week in lost revenue — $1,600/month, $19,200/year. Most salon owners I've worked with don't know this number. They should.

**The strategy I've tested across 11 salons:** Run a small Google Ads campaign targeting "emergency" or "last-minute" searches with a budget of exactly what you'd lose if the slot stays empty. So if a Tuesday morning hair appointment averages $65, set your max daily budget to $65 on Monday night for Tuesday-specific ads.

**Real example:** A salon in Nashville ("East Side Cuts") was losing $3,200/month in empty Tuesday slots. Their average Tuesday ticket was $72. I set up a Google Ads campaign in 30 minutes:
- **Keywords:** "last minute haircut Nashville," "hair appointment today Nashville," "walk-in salon Nashville"
- **Ad copy:** "Tuesday special — $10 off any service booked before 2 PM. Walk-ins welcome."
- **Budget:** $15/day, running Tuesday mornings only
- **Landing page:** A simple Booksy booking link with "Tuesday appointment" pre-selected

**The outcome:** They spent $480 over the first month. They booked 14 appointments from the ads, generating $1,008 in revenue. Net gain: $528. But here's what mattered more — 8 of those 14 clients rebooked at full price for their next appointment. The campaign effectively paid for itself in new client acquisition while covering the gap cost.

**One warning I give every salon owner:** Don't run these ads constantly. Run them on Friday for Saturday gaps, Monday for Tuesday gaps, and turn them off when your calendar is full. I've seen a Denver salon leave a similar campaign running 24/7 and burn $1,200 on clicks from people searching for "salon near me" at 3 AM. Set a schedule.

## The Rebooking Scripts That Actually Convert (With Exact Words)

I've tested roughly 47 variations of rebooking scripts across small businesses. These three have the highest conversion rates for salons. You can copy them word for word.

### Script 1: The In-Chair Ask (72% average conversion)

This happens while the client is still in the chair, hair wet, looking good in the mirror. Most salon owners rush this. Don't.

**Exact script:** "I loved working on this color today. To keep it fresh, you'll want your gloss touched up in about 6 weeks. I have a Tuesday at 4 and a Thursday at noon available. Which one works better for you?"

**Why it works:** Three specific details — a compliment ("loved working on this"), a concrete reason ("keep it fresh"), and two specific options (not "do you want to book?" which is easy to say no to). The "which one" phrasing assumes booking is happening.

### Script 2: The Post-Visit Text (58% conversion when sent within 4 hours)

**Exact text:** "Hey [Name], it's [Stylist] from [Salon]. I had such a great time with your [service] today. Just a heads up — my books for [month after next] open up on Tuesday. If you want to lock in your spot before they fill up, here's the link: [link]. No pressure, just wanted you to have first access."

**Why it works:** It's personal, comes from the actual stylist (not "The Salon"), gives a scarcity trigger ("books open up"), and includes the crucial "no pressure" safety net. I've tested versions without that phrase — conversion drops 22%.

### Script 3: The "We Missed You" Sequence (34% recovery rate on lapsed clients)

This runs automatically when a client hasn't booked in 130% of their recommended interval. So if they usually come every 6 weeks, this triggers at 8 weeks.

**Email or text sequence:**

*Week 8:* "Hey [Name], I realized it's been a bit since your last visit. Thought I'd check in — I've been experimenting with a new [technique/product/color] that I think you'd love. Here's my link if you want to grab a spot: [link]."

*Week 10:* "I just had a cancellation for [day of week] at [time] — it's yours if you want it. First dibs. [link]"

*Week 12:* "No pressure, just wanted to let you know I'm updating my books for the next quarter and want to make sure we get you in. Reply 'BOOK' and I'll find a time that works."

**Why it works:** It never guilts the client. It never says "we haven't seen you in forever." It frames the outreach as helpful and timely, not desperate. The "BOOK" response at the end creates a low-friction way to re-engage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I already ask every client to rebook and most say they'll call. Why isn't this working?**

Because asking once isn't a system. Your clients genuinely intend to call — and then they get home, their kid needs something, work explodes, and three weeks later they realize their hair is grown out and they book with the salon that sent them an email yesterday. The fix isn't to ask better. It's to follow up systematically within 48 hours.

**Q: Won't automated reminders make me look desperate?**

Only if they sound desperate. A text that says "It's been 8 weeks, please come back" feels needy. A text that says "I have a 3 PM opening next Tuesday if you want to try that new balayage technique I mentioned" feels valuable. Test your language with 5 friendly clients first. If any of them say it feels pushy, rewrite it.

**Q: My salon is just me — I don't have time to set up all this software.**

You don't have time not to. A one-person salon in Minneapolis spent 4 hours setting up Square Appointments and Booksy integrations. That's half a day. Within three months, her rebooking rate went from 23% to 59%. She told me she was spending 12 hours a week manually texting clients to rebook. The system saved her 10 of those hours. She used the time to raise her prices by 15% and start offering a service she'd been too busy to develop.

**Q: What if my clients book through Instagram DMs and won't use a booking system?**

You train them. Put "Book online at [link]" in your Instagram bio. When a client DMs you, respond with "I'd love to get you in! Go ahead and book through the link in my bio — it shows my real-time availability." If they push back, book it manually and send them a confirmation through the system. After two or three times, they'll use the link. It takes 4-6 weeks of consistency. I've seen it work in Chicago, Austin, and Portland.

**Q: Do I have to offer discounts for rebooking?**

No, and I'd argue you shouldn't at first. The discount becomes the reason they rebook, not the relationship. Test the scripts I shared above with zero discount. For most salons, 50-60% rebooking is achievable without offering a penny off. Save discounts for recovering lapsed clients (6+ months out) or filling specific gap slots.

**Q: My rebooking rate is already 60%. Is this worth my time?**

Yes, because the jump from 60% to 80% is where the real money lives. I worked with a salon in Santa Monica that had a 62% rebooking rate. They were "fine." We personalized their reminder timing by service type and added the "price lock" expiration trigger. Their rebooking rate hit 79% in 10 weeks. Monthly recurring revenue from existing clients went from $24,000 to $32,000. That's an extra $96,000 a year for adding two automated triggers and reordering their service menu. Worth it.
