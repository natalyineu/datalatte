Welcome email series are crucial for small local businesses to establish a strong connection with new customers, set expectations, and encourage repeat visits. A well-crafted series can significantly improve customer retention, boost loyalty, and drive sales. In this article, we'll share a 5-email template you can replicate for your local business.

**Crafting a welcome email series requires a strategic approach**. Here's a step-by-step guide to creating an effective series that resonates with your customers.

### Step 1: Initial Welcome (Email 1)

The first email should be sent immediately after the customer makes a purchase or signs up for a service. This email sets the tone for the series and establishes your brand's personality.

* Use a clear and concise subject line that includes a personalized message.
* Include a brief introduction to your business, highlighting your unique selling points.
* Offer exclusive deals or promotions to encourage repeat business.

Example: If you own a coffee shop, your initial welcome email might read:

"Thanks for stopping by [Coffee Shop Name]! We're thrilled to have you as part of our community. As a special welcome, enjoy 10% off your next purchase. Use code WELCOME10 at checkout."

### Step 2: Educational Content (Email 2)

The second email should focus on educating your customers about your products or services. This email aims to build trust and establish your expertise.

* Share valuable content, such as tips, tutorials, or industry insights.
* Highlight the benefits of using your products or services.
* Include a clear call-to-action (CTA) to encourage engagement.

Example: If you own a hair salon, your educational content email might read:

"Did you know that regular trims can help prevent split ends and breakage? At [Salon Name], we're passionate about helping you achieve healthy and beautiful hair. Book your appointment today and let our experts work their magic!"

### Step 3: Social Proof (Email 3)

The third email should focus on social proof, showcasing customer testimonials, reviews, or ratings. This email aims to build credibility and trust.

* Share customer testimonials, reviews, or ratings that highlight your business's strengths.
* Highlight any awards, recognition, or certifications your business has received.
* Include a clear CTA to encourage engagement.

Example: If you own a pet groomer, your social proof email might read:

"We're proud of our 5-star rating on Google! Our customers love us, and we love them too. Check out what [Customer Name] had to say about their experience with us: '[Pet Groomer Name] is the best! They're always so friendly and take great care of my furry friend.'"

### Step 4: Exclusive Offers (Email 4)

The fourth email should focus on exclusive offers, promotions, or loyalty programs. This email aims to encourage repeat business and increase customer loyalty.

* Offer exclusive deals, discounts, or rewards to loyal customers.
* Highlight any loyalty programs or membership benefits.
* Include a clear CTA to encourage engagement.

Example: If you own a fitness studio, your exclusive offers email might read:

"Hey fitness fans! We're excited to announce our loyalty program, [Program Name]. Earn points for every class you attend and redeem rewards like free classes, merchandise, and more. Join now and start earning!"

### Step 5: Retention and Follow-up (Email 5)

The final email should focus on retention and follow-up, checking in with customers and encouraging them to return.

* Send a personalized message to each customer, inquiring about their experience.
* Offer a clear CTA to encourage engagement or feedback.
* Highlight any upcoming events or promotions.

Example: If you own a coffee shop, your retention and follow-up email might read:

"Hey [Customer Name]! We hope you're enjoying our coffee and atmosphere. We'd love to hear about your experience with us. Take our quick survey and let us know how we can improve. As a special thank you, enjoy 10% off your next purchase. Use code THANKYOU10 at checkout."

**Investing in a welcome email series can have a significant impact on your local business**. By following this 5-email template, you can create a personalized and engaging experience for your customers, driving sales, increasing loyalty, and boosting retention.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Sending a Welcome Email That Sounds Like a Robot Wrote It

**The story:** A coffee shop in Austin, Texas — let’s call it “Brew & Co.” — spent $600 on a Mailchimp template and had their part-time barista write the copy. The welcome email read: “Thank you for your purchase. We look forward to serving you again. Please visit our website for more information.” No name. No offer. No personality. They sent it to 1,200 new subscribers over three months and got exactly 14 opens. That’s a 1.1% open rate. Industry average for welcome emails is 50%+.

**What went wrong:** They treated the welcome email like a receipt. No subject line that made you curious, no personalization (not even the customer’s first name), no reason to click. The barista meant well but had zero marketing experience. Worse, they didn’t test the email on mobile — the template broke on iPhones.

**The fix:** I rewrote the email with a subject line “Your first latte is on us” (they were already giving a free drink with first purchase, just not mentioning it). The body used the owner’s actual voice: “Hey [First Name], thanks for stopping by. I’m Sarah — I roast our beans myself. Come back this week and show this email for a free pastry with any drink.” Added a single button: “Claim My Free Pastry.” We split-tested two subject lines (the second was “Sarah’s personal coffee recommendation for you”) and picked the winner after 200 sends.

**The outcome:** Open rate jumped to 58%. Click-through rate went from zero to 22%. In the first month after the fix, 87 people redeemed the pastry offer. Average ticket with pastry add-on was $8.50. That’s $739.50 in incremental revenue from one email — against the $600 they wasted on the template. They canceled the template subscription and now write emails in-house.

### Mistake #2: Bombarding New Customers with Emails Every Day for Two Weeks

**The story:** A hair salon in Nashville — “Glamour Cuts” — wanted to “build a relationship” with new clients. They set up a 10-email welcome series in Constant Contact: one email every day for the first 10 days. Subject lines included “Day 1: Welcome!”, “Day 2: Meet Your Stylist”, “Day 3: Our Products”, “Day 4: Tips for Healthy Hair” … all the way to “Day 10: Book Your Next Appointment.” Unsubscribe rate after the third email was 34%. By day 10, they had 60% fewer subscribers than when they started. The series generated exactly one booked appointment.

**What went wrong:** Frequency killed them. New customers don’t want a daily lecture. They want a single, useful welcome — then maybe a follow-up a week later with a reason to come back. The salon owner assumed volume equaled engagement. Opposite. The “Day 4: Tips” email had no actionable tip — just a generic paragraph copied from a blog. Total waste of their $50/month Constant Contact plan.

**The fix:** We collapsed the 10 emails into three: (1) immediate welcome with a “10% off your next visit” code, (2) a “meet your stylist” email sent five days later (but only if they hadn’t booked yet — using automation logic), (3) a “we miss you” email after 14 days if no second visit. We also added a one-click “Book Now” button to every email instead of sending them to the website homepage.

**The outcome:** Unsubscribe rate dropped to 3%. Open rate on email #2 hit 45%. Booking rate for the series went from 1 in 1,200 to 23 in 1,200 — a 22x improvement. That’s roughly $2,300 in new bookings from the same audience. The monthly Constant Contact subscription stayed $50. No extra cost.

### Mistake #3: Forgetting to Segment New vs. Returning Customers

**The story:** A pet groomer in Portland, Oregon — “Furry Fresh” — used Square for appointments and payments. They set up a welcome email that offered “$15 off your first grooming.” Problem: the email went to every new Square contact, including people who had already been to the shop five times. One regular customer named Mike got the welcome email and replied, “I’ve been coming here for two years. Why do I need a welcome offer?” He felt undervalued. He left a one-star Yelp review saying the salon was “out of touch.”

**What went wrong:** No segmentation. Square has a built-in field for “first appointment date” — they never used it. The welcome series was triggered by any new contact, not by actual first-time purchase. So loyal customers got a “new customer” email that insulted their loyalty. Mike wasn’t the only one — the salon owner told me they got three angry emails and two bad reviews in the first month of the series.

**The fix:** Set up a segment in Square Marketing: “First appointment date is NULL” (meaning they’ve never been before). Then created a separate “VIP” series for customers with 3+ appointments — that series offered a free nail trim instead of $15 off. Also added a suppression rule: anyone who already had a completed appointment did not receive the welcome email at all.

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, the bad reviews stopped. The VIP series generated 14 additional nail trim appointments in the first month (average price: $12). No discount lost revenue on existing customers because we weren’t giving them unnecessary $15 off. Yelp rating recovered from 3.8 to 4.5 stars. Total cost: zero — the segmentation tools were already in Square.

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Mobile Formatting (Yes, It Still Happens)

**The story:** A yoga studio in Denver — “Mountain Flow” — designed a beautiful welcome email in Mailchimp with a full-width image of their studio, a paragraph of elegant text, and a “Schedule Your First Class” button at the bottom. They sent it to 340 new sign-ups. Open rate was decent: 32%. Click rate: 0.5%. The owner was baffled. She paid a freelancer $200 to design the email.

**What went wrong:** The email looked great on a desktop. On mobile — where 78% of their subscribers opened it — the image was huge and didn’t scale down. The button was below the fold, and the “click” area was tiny. Users had to pinch-zoom to tap it. On Android, the button didn’t work at all (Mailchimp’s default button vs. Android email client rendering). The freelancer never tested on a phone.

**The fix:** I showed her how to use Mailchimp’s mobile preview and told her to shrink the image to 50% width, stack the text above the button, and use a plain-text link as a backup. We also added a “Book now” text link at the very top of the email so users didn’t have to scroll. Switched from a generic button to a simple `<a>` tag with inline styling. Tested on iPhone, Android, and Outlook mobile.

**The outcome:** After the redesign, the same 340-person list got a second send (new subscribers only). Click rate went from 0.5% to 12%. In that batch, 41 people clicked the booking link. 9 actually booked a class. That’s $360 in first-class revenue (at $40 per intro class). The freelancer cost $200 — so the fix paid for itself in six bookings. The studio owner now tests every email on her own phone before hitting send.

## Why Timing and Frequency Matter More Than You Think

Most local businesses set up their welcome series and forget about it. They pick whatever timing feels right — send the first email immediately, second email two days later, third a week later — then never look at the numbers again. I’ve seen this at a half-dozen clients. The defaults are almost always wrong.

Take a laundromat in Chicago with a loyalty card program. They sent the welcome email at 8 AM on a Monday. Open rate: 9%. Why? Their customers were dropping off laundry before work — not reading email. When they moved the send time to 6 PM on Thursday (when people are home and scrolling on their phones), open rate hit 41%. The same email. Different time.

For most local businesses, the sweet spot is Tuesday through Thursday, between 10 AM and 2 PM local time. That’s when business owners themselves check email during a lull, and when consumers are on a coffee break. Mondays are too chaotic, Fridays are “I’ll read this later” (they never do), and weekends work only for very specific niches (like brunch spots on Saturday morning).

Frequency is even more neglected. You’re not The New York Times. You don’t need to email every day. My rule of thumb: three emails over two weeks, then nothing for at least 21 days unless there’s a specific offer. A pet supply store in Nashville tried a 7-email series over 10 days. Unsubscribes hit 22% after email #4. They slashed it to three and unsubscribes dropped to 4%. Revenue per subscriber actually went up because the remaining people were more engaged.

If you’re using Mailchimp or Constant Contact, set up A/B testing on send times. Send the same email at 10 AM and 2 PM to a small sample (200 each), see which gets more opens, then send the rest at the winner’s time. Do this for at least two emails in your series. You’ll often find the second email performs better at a completely different hour than the first — because people’s attention patterns shift after the initial dopamine hit of “new email from a business.”

Your email service provider should also allow “send time optimization” based on individual subscriber history. Mailchimp calls it “Send Time Optimization.” Square has it in their marketing tab. Use it. Otherwise you’re guessing.

## How to Track What’s Actually Working (Without Drowning in Data)

I once worked with a bike shop in Austin that had 15 columns in their spreadsheet — opens, clicks, bounces, forwards, complaints, revenue, cost per email, ROI, “engagement score” (they made that up) — and they still couldn’t tell me if the welcome series was worth the $1,200 they spent on a freelancer. Too much data is worse than none. You need three numbers and three only.

**Number one: unique open rate.** Ignore total opens. One person opening the email 10 times inflates the number. Unique open rate tells you how many people actually saw it. For a welcome email, expect 40–60%. Below 25% means your subject line is weak or your sending name is wrong (don’t use “noreply@” — use a person’s name like “Sarah from Brew & Co.”).

**Number two: click-through rate (unique clicks / unique opens).** This is your quality score. Welcome emails should hit 10–20% CTR. Below 5% means your call to action isn’t compelling or your offer is buried under too much text.

**Number three: conversion to desired action.** If the email’s goal is booking an appointment, track that specific event. Use a unique promo code (e.g., WELCOME10) in the email, then count how many times it’s used in Square or Booksy. A salon in Denver used this method: their welcome series had a $15 off code. Over 90 days, 47 people used it. Average service ticket was $85. That’s $3,995 in revenue directly attributable to the welcome emails. They knew exactly what it was worth.

Don’t track email revenue if you can’t directly link it. If you run a coffee shop and someone buys a latte after getting your email, but they might have bought it anyway — you’re overcounting. Use coupon codes or UTM links. Mailchimp has built-in Google Analytics integration: tag your links with “utm_source=mailchimp&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=welcome_series”. Then check your Google Analytics “Acquisition > Campaigns” tab. You’ll see exactly how many sessions and conversions came from that series.

If you don’t have Google Analytics (or you don’t know what that is), just use a unique phone number or offer code. A pet groomer in Portland printed a “Welcome email code: GROOM10” on their signup form. Every time someone used it, they knew the email did its job. Simple, no spreadsheets.

One last tracking tip: look at unsubscribe rate. If any single email in your series has an unsubscribe rate above 2%, fix it immediately. That email is annoying people. Shorten it, change the offer, or delete it entirely.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn’t one welcome email enough? Why do I need a whole series?**

It depends what “enough” means. One email can get someone to make a second purchase if your offer is strong. But the data shows a 3–5 email series generates 50–70% more repeat visits than a single email. That’s because one email is easy to ignore — especially if they opened it at a busy moment. A series gives you multiple chances. I’ve seen a single email generate $500 in repeat revenue, and a three-email series generate $1,800 from the same list. The extra two emails cost you nothing but time.

**Q: What if I don’t have an email list yet? How do I start?**

You start today. Put a signup form on your website, at your cash register, on your Instagram bio, and on your Google Business Profile. Use a tool like Mailchimp (free up to 500 contacts) or Square’s built-in marketing (free with payments). Offer an incentive: “Get $5 off your next visit when you join our email list.” A coffee shop in Austin got 200 signups in one week by putting a QR code at the register that led to a one-field form (“Enter your email”) with an automatic coupon. That cost them $1,000 in free drinks (200 x $5) — but those 200 people generated $3,800 in repeat revenue over three months. Positive ROI in week one.

**Q: Should I send welcome emails from my personal email address or a business address?**

Personal name, business domain. Use “Sarah@BrewAndCo.com” — not “sarah@gmail.com.” People trust a domain they recognize. But the sender name should be a real person: “Sarah from Brew & Co.” Not “Brew & Co. Customer Service.” Test it: send a welcome email from “Sarah from Brew & Co.” and watch open rates jump 20–30% compared to a generic name. People open emails from humans, not brands.

**Q: What about text messages? Should I do an SMS welcome series instead of email?**

Only if you have explicit opt-in for SMS. Text message open rates are 90%+ but people hate spam texts. If you own a pizza shop that takes orders via text, an SMS welcome could work: “Hi, this is Tony’s Pizza. Thanks for ordering. Text COUPON for 15% off your next pizza.” That’s fine. But don’t blast a 5-text series. One SMS, then move to email. A pet groomer in Nashville tried a 3-SMS welcome series. Unsubscribe rate hit 40% after the second text. SMS is for urgent offers, not relationship building.

**Q: How long should my welcome email series run? I don’t want to keep emailing forever.**

Total duration: 14 days max. Three emails over two weeks. After that, move them to your regular monthly newsletter or promotional list. If they haven’t made a repeat purchase in 60 days, trigger a “we miss you” email — but that’s separate. The welcome series itself should be short and intense. A yoga studio in Denver kept emailing weekly for three months. After the 8th email (same series), open rates dropped to 8% and unsubscribes spiked. The owner thought she was being persistent. Customers thought she was annoying.

**Q: What if my business is seasonal? Like a pool cleaning service that only gets customers in summer?**

Adjust your series timing. Send email #1 immediately when they sign up. Email #2 one week later with a “Prepare Your Pool for Summer” checklist (useful, not salesy). Email #3 two weeks later with a discount on a seasonal package. Then pause until the next season. Don’t send a “we’re open all winter” email to people who only care about summer — they’ll unsubscribe. A pool service in Phoenix tried that and lost 30% of their list. Instead, they sent a single “See you next spring” email in October with a early-bird booking offer. That generated $4,200 in pre-season bookings.
