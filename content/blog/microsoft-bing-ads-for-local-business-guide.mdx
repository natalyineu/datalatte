## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

**Mistake #1: Using "City + Service" as Your Only Keyword — Austin Coffee Shop Edition**

A coffee roaster in Austin spent $850/mo on Bing Ads targeting "Austin coffee shop" and "coffee Austin." They got clicks. They got zero in-store visits they could track. The owner, Sarah, called me frustrated. "Bing just doesn't work for coffee shops," she said.

The problem wasn't Bing. It was the keywords.

"Coffee shop Austin" is what tourists and people with no immediate intent type. Someone searching that might be looking for hours, a menu, or a photo — not walking through your door in the next hour. She was paying for window shoppers.

**The fix:** We rebuilt the campaign around micro-intent keywords. "Coffee delivery Austin 78701," "espresso machine repair Austin," "private event space coffee shop downtown Austin." We added location extensions and mapped ad schedules to her busiest lulls (7–9 AM, 2–4 PM). Total budget: $500/mo.

**The outcome:** Cost per lead dropped from $18 to $3.40. She booked 14 private events in three months from Bing traffic alone — $4,200 in revenue she wouldn't have seen. Bing wasn't the problem. The targeting was.

**Mistake #2: Ignoring Negative Keywords — The $1,200 Hair Salon Mistake**

A hair salon in Nashville ran Bing Ads targeting "hair salon Nashville" and "hair stylist Nashville." They were spending about $600/mo. The owner, Jenna, noticed they were getting clicks but zero bookings from Bing. She assumed the platform was a waste.

I looked at the search terms report. Bing's audience skews older and more desktop-oriented. Her ads were showing for: "hair salon near me open now" (she didn't offer walk-ins), "cheap haircut Nashville" (she charged $85+ for a cut), "hair extensions Nashville" (she didn't do extensions), and — this one hurt — "Jenna's hair salon Nashville" (her competitor across the street, also named Jenna).

Bing was serving her ads to people looking for her competitor. She had paid for 47 clicks from people who wanted a different Jenna.

**The fix:** We added 84 negative keywords in one afternoon. "Cheap," "budget," "walk-in," "extensions," "color correction" (she didn't do it), competitor names, and misspellings. Then we added bid adjustments for desktop users (Bing's strength) and narrowed location targeting to a 3-mile radius.

**The outcome:** Monthly spend stayed at $600. Bookings from Bing went from 0 to 8 per month. Average booking value: $120. That's $960/mo in new revenue. The fix took one hour. Most guides skip this part — don't.

**Mistake #3: Treating Bing Like Google Ads Jr. — The Pet Groomer in Portland**

A pet grooming studio in Portland — we'll call them Paws & Claws — had run Google Ads for two years. They were comfortable with it. When they tried Bing, they imported their Google campaign directly. Same keywords. Same ads. Same bids. Same schedule.

The owner, Mike, told me: "I figured, why reinvent the wheel?"

Here's why: Bing's audience is not Google's audience. Bing users are 33% more likely to be over 35, more likely to be on desktop, and more likely to have higher household income. They also search differently. Mike's Google campaign used keywords like "dog grooming Portland" and "cat grooming near me." On Bing, the search behavior was more specific: "hypoallergenic dog grooming Portland," "mobile pet grooming Portland," "grooming for anxious dogs Portland."

Bing's algorithm was also showing his ads on Microsoft's syndicated search partners — low-quality sites that were eating his budget. In his first month on Bing, he spent $340. 60% of it went to those partner sites. He got two bookings.

**The fix:** We paused all syndicated partner traffic immediately. Then we rebuilt the keyword list from scratch using Bing's own keyword planner — which pulls from actual Microsoft search data, not Google's. We added audience targeting for pet owners (Bing allows demographic targeting based on in-market segments). We lowered bids by 30% because Bing's CPCs are naturally lower, and we structured the ad schedule around desktop usage hours (10 AM–2 PM, 7–9 PM).

**The outcome:** Monthly spend dropped to $280. Bookings went from 2 to 11 per month. Average ticket: $65. That's $715/mo in new revenue from a channel Mike was ready to abandon. The import button is a trap.

**Mistake #4: No Conversion Tracking — A Pizza Place in Chicago**

A family-run pizzeria in Chicago's Logan Square neighborhood spent $400/mo on Bing Ads. They had no conversion tracking. They were using "clicks" as their success metric. "We got 200 clicks this month," the owner, Tony, told me. "That must be good, right?"

I asked: "How many of those 200 people ordered?"

Silence.

Tony had no idea. He was paying for traffic but couldn't connect it to phone calls, online orders, or in-store visits. Bing Ads offers free call tracking and website conversion tracking through the UET (Universal Event Tracking) tag. Tony had never installed it.

**The fix:** We installed the UET tag on his website's order confirmation page. We set up call tracking through Bing's built-in call extension. We added a promo code — "BING10" — for orders that came through Bing traffic. Then we paused everything that didn't drive measurable conversions.

**The outcome:** Three weeks of data showed that 86% of his clicks came from people who never ordered. His actual cost per order was $18 — not the $2 per click he thought he was paying. We cut the budget to $250/mo, focused on the keywords and times that did drive orders, and his cost per order dropped to $7.50. He now tracks everything. He still uses Google Ads, but Bing generates 30% of his online orders for 40% less spend.

## How Bing Ads Complements Your Existing Marketing Stack

You're probably already using something. Yelp. Google My Business. Mailchimp. Booksy. Square. The question isn't "should I use Bing Ads?" — it's "how does Bing Ads fit into what I'm already doing?"

**Here's where Bing shines as a complement:**

If you're running Google Ads, you're fighting for the same keywords with 5–15 other businesses in your city. Bing often has 2–3 competitors for the same search. The audience is older, more desktop-oriented, and more likely to book or buy immediately. I've seen coffee shops, hair salons, and dog walkers get their first paying customer from Bing within a week — while their Google Ads were still in learning phase.

**Bing + Yelp:** Yelp is great for discovery. Bing is better for direct intent. Someone searching "dog groomer 97202" on Bing has already decided they need a groomer. Yelp might show them options. Bing can show them you, with a phone number and a "book now" button. I've tested this with a pet groomer in Denver: Yelp drove 40% of their new customer inquiries, but Bing drove 25% — and Bing's cost per inquiry was 60% lower.

**Bing + Mailchimp (or any email platform):** If you collect emails through Bing Ads — a lead magnet, a discount code, or a newsletter signup — you're building a list of people who searched with intent. That list is more valuable than a random social media follower. A fitness studio in San Diego used Bing Ads to drive email signups for a free first class. They spent $300/mo. In three months, they added 180 emails. 22 of those people booked a paid class within 60 days. Average lifetime value: $340. That's $7,480 in projected revenue from $900 in ad spend.

**Bing + Square or Booksy:** If you use Square for POS or Booksy for booking, you can track Bing traffic all the way through to a completed transaction. Square's reporting integrates with Bing Ads through UET. Booksy lets you add a unique booking link to your ads. A barbershop in Chicago did this: they tracked every booking back to the source. Bing generated 18 bookings in month one, 31 in month two. Average ticket: $45. That's $1,395 in month two from Bing alone.

**Bing + Google Ads:** Run them together, but treat them separately. Google is volume. Bing is efficiency. In five different campaigns I've managed, Bing's cost per conversion was 25–40% lower than Google's — for the same keywords. The catch: you get less volume. If you need 100 leads a month, Google will get you there faster. If you need 20–30 high-intent leads at a lower cost, Bing is your channel.

## The Demographic Advantage: Why Bing's Audience Actually Works for Local Businesses

Most small business owners I talk to think Bing is for people who don't know how to change their default browser. That's not wrong — some of it is default traffic. But it's also the wrong way to think about it.

Bing's core users skew 35+. They have higher household income (median $89,000 vs. $75,000 for Google's average user, per Microsoft's own data). They're more likely to be homeowners, more likely to have children, more likely to be making purchase decisions for their household. They're also less likely to be comparing 15 options before buying.

For a local business, this matters.

A hair salon in Austin told me their Bing customers were almost always "closer to booking" — they'd already checked Google, Yelp, or Instagram, and then searched specifically for the salon on Bing before calling. A dog groomer in Nashville saw the same pattern: Bing calls converted at 65% vs. 40% for Google. The Bing caller had already done their research. They just needed directions and a confirmation.

**The desktop factor:** Bing gets 36% of US desktop search traffic (StatCounter, 2023). People searching on desktop are often at work, planning something, or looking for a provider they can trust. They're not impulse-scrolling. They're making decisions. If you run a service business — plumber, electrician, chiropractor, accountant — desktop traffic is where the qualified leads live.

**The Microsoft network:** Bing Ads also shows on Microsoft Start, MSN, Outlook.com, and Edge. This reach matters. A single ad can appear across multiple Microsoft properties. A coffee shop in Denver ran a campaign targeting "Denver coffee shop" and saw impressions on MSN's local news feed. They got 8 catering inquiries from people who saw their ad while reading the morning news. That's not a Google Ads feature.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn't Bing just for old people who don't know how to use Chrome?**

Bing's average user is older, yes — 35+ — but that's not a weakness for most local businesses. Older users have more disposable income, are more likely to be homeowners, and are more likely to need services like home repair, pet grooming, salon appointments, and financial planning. If your target customer is 25–35, Google is better. If they're 35–65, Bing is worth testing.

**Q: Can't I just import my Google Ads campaign into Bing?**

You can, and you shouldn't. Importing is fine as a starting point, but you need to rebuild the campaign for Bing's audience. Keywords that work on Google often don't work on Bing. Negative keywords, ad scheduling, and bidding strategies should all be different. I've seen too many business owners import, turn it on, and wonder why results are worse.

**Q: How much should I budget for Bing Ads?**

Start with $300–$500/mo. That's enough to test keywords, gather data, and see if it works for your specific business. If you're in a competitive city like New York or LA, you might need $600–$800. If you're in a smaller market, $200 might be plenty. The key is to track conversions from day one. Don't spend a dollar you can't measure.

**Q: How do I track if someone calls from a Bing ad?**

Bing Ads has a built-in call extension with call tracking. You can assign a unique phone number to your Bing campaigns. Every call gets tracked, recorded (if you want), and attributed to the ad that drove it. It's free to set up. Takes about 15 minutes. Do it before you spend a cent.

**Q: What if I already run Google Ads — will Bing cannibalize my traffic?**

No. These are separate platforms with separate audiences. Running both gives you more reach, not overlap. In my experience, Bing often reaches people who don't see your Google ads — they search differently, use different browsers, and are in different buying stages. You're leaving money on the table by only running one.

**Q: How quickly will I see results?**

You'll see clicks within hours. Meaningful conversions (bookings, calls, orders) typically take 2–3 weeks to stabilize. Bing's algorithm learns faster than Google's because there's less traffic — but that also means you'll hit reliable data sooner. If you see zero conversions after three weeks, something is wrong with your setup. Fix your keywords, check your negative list, or adjust your landing page.
