## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Running Ads Outside Your Actual Service Radius

I worked with a restoration company in Austin, Texas — three trucks, good reviews, solid crew. They'd been running Google Ads for four months and were convinced the platform didn't work. Their phone rang maybe twice a week. They were ready to pull the plug.

I looked at their account. They'd set a 30-mile location radius around Austin. Sounds reasonable, right? Except they didn't exclude zip codes they couldn't actually service. Their lead time for a job 28 miles out was 90 minutes. By then, the homeowner had already called someone else or the water had spread to another room.

What went wrong: They were spending $1,200/month showing up in searches for people they couldn't help fast enough. Those clicks still cost $12–18 each. They'd burned roughly $2,100 over four months on leads they had zero chance of converting.

The fix: We tightened the radius to 10 miles and excluded five specific zip codes on the outskirts. We also added location bid adjustments — +25% for the core downtown area where response time was under 30 minutes, -50% for the outer edge.

The outcome: Lead volume didn't drop. It actually went up, because the ad was now showing to people who could actually get a truck in their driveway within the hour. Cost per lead dropped from $47 to $22. Their phone rang 18 times in the first week after the change. They booked $3,800 in jobs from that week alone.

The uncomfortable truth: Google will happily show your ad to people you can't serve. It's not malicious — the platform just doesn't know your truck's max drive time. You have to tell it.

### Mistake 2: Bidding on "Water Damage" Without Negatives

A company in Nashville called me frustrated. They were spending $1,400/month and getting "a lot of traffic but nobody calls." I looked at their search terms report. It was a disaster.

Their keyword list was all broad match: water damage, water damage restoration, water damage repair, flood cleanup. What Google was actually matching them to: "water damage movie" (the Nicolas Cage film), "how to fix water damage on iPhone," "water damage to hardwood floors DIY," "is water damage covered by renters insurance."

They'd spent $1,400 and gotten zero actual service calls. Fourteen hundred dollars for people looking up movie times and phone repair tips.

The fix: We switched to exact match and phrase match only. We added 87 negative keywords. Obvious ones: "movie," "iPhone," "DIY," "repair yourself," "insurance claim." Less obvious ones: "mold inspection" (different service, different buyer), "carpet cleaning" (also a separate service), "basement waterproofing" (preventative, not emergency).

The outcome: Traffic dropped by half. But the calls went from zero to six in the first week. Their cost per qualified lead went from essentially infinite to $45. Within a month, they were at $38/lead and booking jobs.

Here's something most guides skip: Broad match works when you have conversion data and a smart bidding strategy. For a local service business starting from zero, it's a leaky faucet. You don't need maximum volume. You need maximum relevance.

### Mistake 3: Sending All Clicks to Your Homepage

Small business owners love their websites. They spent money on them. They want people to see them. I get it.

A water damage company in Chicago had a beautiful site. Full agency build, professional photos, a page about their 20-year history. They were sending every ad click to the homepage.

Their bounce rate was 85%. Average time on site: 11 seconds. That's someone landing on your page, seeing a generic hero image and a navigation menu, and leaving because they're standing in an inch of water and need to see a phone number immediately.

The fix: We built three dedicated landing pages — one for emergency water extraction, one for flood damage, one for burst pipes. Each had exactly: a phone number in the header (click-to-call on mobile), a sentence confirming they service that specific problem, three bullet points of what happens when you call, and a form as backup. No navigation menu. No "about us." No blog links.

The outcome: Conversion rate went from 2% to 11%. Cost per lead dropped from $52 to $19. The client called me and said "I was about to spend $3,000 on a website redesign. I needed this instead."

The hard truth: During an emergency, your website is not a website. It's a digital business card that needs to get out of the way and let someone call you.

### Mistake 4: Not Tracking Calls

A Denver restoration company told me they were getting "maybe one or two leads a week from Google." Their budget was $900/month. They were ready to cancel.

I asked how they tracked leads. "We ask people where they heard about us."

If you've ever owned a business, you know how reliable that information is. "I think my cousin mentioned you." "I just googled water damage, I don't remember." "I've driven past your van."

We set up call tracking using CallRail. It took about 45 minutes total. Dynamic number insertion — anyone who clicks the ad sees a unique phone number on the landing page, and calls get recorded and attributed.

Here's what we found: They were actually getting 9–12 calls per week from Google Ads. Not 1–2. The problem was their office staff was answering with "hello?" instead of the business name. Callers hung up. Or they got put on hold. Or the person answering didn't know the service area and just said "we'll call you back."

The fix: We added a script for whoever answers the phone. "Thank you for calling [business name], how can we get a crew to you today?" We also set up a call recording review process — one hour a week of listening to calls to catch what's going wrong.

The outcome: Answered call rate went from 40% to 90%. Close rate on calls that were answered went from 30% to 65%. Their $900/month budget was suddenly generating $4,200 in booked work. They weren't getting more leads — they were finally catching the ones they already paid for.

Most guides skip this part — don't. If you're not tracking calls, you're flying blind. You don't know which keywords work, which ads work, or even if your phone is being answered properly.

## Keyword Strategy Beyond the Obvious

Most water damage companies bid on the same ten keywords: "water damage restoration," "water damage repair," "flood cleanup," "emergency water extraction." Those terms are expensive because every competitor in your city is bidding on them. In a mid-sized market like Kansas City, you're looking at $25–45 per click for those broad terms. In a competitive market like Atlanta, it can hit $60+.

You don't have to fight for those head terms exclusively. There's a whole universe of cheaper, more specific keywords that convert just as well — sometimes better.

### Service-Specific Keywords
Instead of "water damage restoration," try "basement flood cleanup quote" ($18 CPC in most markets) or "sewer backup cleanup" ($15–22 CPC). These are higher intent. Someone searching "basement flood cleanup quote" is probably standing in a wet basement right now and is closer to booking than someone searching "water damage restoration."

### Competitor Keywords
This one makes some business owners uncomfortable. It shouldn't. "Servpro [your city]" or "ServiceMaster [your city]" are search terms people type when they're shopping. If someone searches "Servpro Chicago" and your ad shows up offering a faster response time — that's a legitimate way to earn business.

I ran this for a client in Portland. We bid on "Servpro Portland" and "Paul Davis Portland" — both competitor names. Cost per click was $11 versus $38 for the generic term. Conversion rate was 17% because these people were actively comparing services. The client picked up three jobs in the first two weeks that would have gone to competitors.

### Service Area Keywords
"Water damage in Lincoln Park" works better than "water damage Chicago" if you have a location in Lincoln Park. Use neighborhood names, zip codes, landmarks. I worked with a company in Seattle that added keywords for individual neighborhoods — Capitol Hill, Ballard, Fremont, Beacon Hill. Their cost per lead dropped from $54 to $31 because the ad was more specific and Google rewarded the relevance.

### Emergency Modifiers
Terms with "emergency," "24/7," "same day," "now" — these have higher conversion rates because the searcher has an active problem, not a curiosity. Set up a separate campaign for emergency keywords with higher bids and a budget that can handle overnight traffic.

The tool you actually need here: Google Keyword Planner. It's free. Run a seed list of your core services, sort by competition, and look for terms with medium competition and decent volume. Then cross-reference with your Search Console data for queries people actually used to find your site. That data is pure gold and most businesses ignore it.

## Landing Pages That Convert Emergency Traffic

Your landing page will make or break your ad spend. You can have perfect keywords and a $30 CPC, but if your landing page takes three seconds to load and buries the phone number, you're burning money.

Here's what an emergency restoration landing page needs to do:

**Show the phone number immediately.** Not in the header menu. Not in the footer. At the very top, in a contrasting color, with click-to-call on mobile. Make it impossible to miss.

**Confirm you handle their specific problem.** If someone clicked an ad for "burst pipe repair," the headline should say "24/7 Burst Pipe Repair in [Your City]" — not "Quality Water Damage Services." Match the ad copy. Match the intent.

**Remove all distractions.** No navigation menu. No links to your blog. No "learn more about our team" section. The only thing someone in an emergency should be able to do is call you or fill out a contact form.

**Add trust signals that matter.** License numbers. IICRC certification. Average response time. Number of 5-star Google reviews. Photos of actual work crews, not stock photos. I tested this with a Dallas company — switching from a generic stock photo to a photo of their actual crew on site improved conversion by 31%.

**Optimize for speed.** A one-second delay in page load time can reduce conversions by 7%. For an emergency service, that number is probably higher because people are already stressed and impatient. Use Google PageSpeed Insights to check your score. Compress images. Remove unnecessary scripts. If your page takes more than two seconds to load on mobile, fix that before you spend another dollar on ads.

**Include a secondary conversion path.** The phone call is the primary goal. But some people will fill out a form even if they could call. Have a form with three fields max — name, phone number, description of the problem. Don't ask for their email. Don't ask for their insurance company. Don't ask for their address until you've confirmed you can help.

Here's a real example from a company in Minneapolis: They rebuilt their landing pages using Unbounce, tested different headlines and button colors, and saw their conversion rate go from 4% to 15% over six weeks. Their ad spend stayed the same. Their lead volume tripled without spending another dollar.

Your landing page isn't a design project. It's a functional tool. If it's not converting at least 10% of visitors, it needs work.

## Budget Strategy: What $1,500/Month Actually Gets You

I've had small business owners tell me they don't have the budget for Google Ads. Then I find out they're spending $800/month on a billboard next to the highway. I'm not anti-billboard, but I can tell you which one generates more emergency phone calls.

Let me be specific about what a realistic budget looks like for a single-truck restoration business in a mid-sized US market like Columbus, Ohio or Richmond, Virginia.

At $1,500/month, you're spending roughly $50/day. In a market with average CPC of $25–35 for emergency keywords, that gets you about 15–20 clicks per day. At a 10% landing page conversion rate, that's 1–2 leads per day. Realistically, you'll convert 30–50% of those into jobs depending on your response time and pricing.

That works out to roughly 10–15 jobs per month from Google Ads alone. At an average job value of $1,200–2,500 for residential water extraction, you're looking at $12,000–37,500 in monthly revenue from a $1,500 ad spend.

This assumes your campaigns are set up correctly. If you're running broad match keywords to your homepage with no call tracking, you could spend $1,500 and get zero calls.

**What I actually recommend for a starting budget:**
- $1,000–1,500/month for a metro area under 500,000 people
- $1,500–2,500/month for a metro area over 500,000 people
- $2,500–4,000/month for competitive markets like NYC, LA, Chicago
- Increase by 20–30% during storm season or winter months (pipe bursts)

**Where to adjust if the budget feels tight:**
- Reduce radius if you're covering too much area
- Cut display network (it rarely converts for emergency services)
- Set a max CPC cap — I usually start at $30 and adjust based on data
- Pause ads during hours you can't respond (if you don't run 24/7)
- Focus on highest-intent keywords and ignore the expensive brand terms

I worked with a Kansas City business that started at $800/month. That was too low — they got maybe one lead every two days. We bumped it to $1,200 and the volume hit a consistent 2–3 leads daily. The client was nervous about the increase. I showed them the math: $400 more in ad spend, $6,000 more in booked work. They stopped being nervous.

Google Ads is not an expense. It's a variable cost of customer acquisition. If one lead costs you $35 and the average job is worth $1,800, you can afford a lot of leads.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do I know Google Ads will work for my specific city?**
You don't until you test it. But here's what I can tell you: I've set up campaigns in 40+ US markets from San Antonio to Syracuse. Every single one generated leads within the first week when the account was built correctly. The difference is cost per lead — it might be $18 in one city and $55 in another. Either way, if you're getting calls and booking jobs, the math works. Start with $500 and two weeks. You'll have your answer.

**Q: What if someone clicks my ad but isn't in my service area?**
That's why you use location targeting AND location extensions AND negative location targets. Set your targeting to "people in your target location" not "people who show interest in your target location." Google gives you both options — pick the stricter one. Then add your specific zip codes. Then add the neighboring counties as exclusions. You can still get occasional stray clicks, but with proper settings, it becomes noise rather than a budget drain.

**Q: Should I pause ads at night and on weekends?**
Only if you don't answer your phone. If someone's pipe bursts at 2 AM and your ad shows up but nobody answers, you just paid $30 to annoy someone who will never call you again. If you have call forwarding to an after-hours answering service, keep the ads running. If you're a one-person operation who sleeps, pause ads from 10 PM to 7 AM. Your budget will go further during daylight hours when you can actually respond.

**Q: How do I compete with big national franchises like Servpro?**
You compete on response time and local reputation. Your ad can say "10-minute response in Austin" — they can't, because their dispatch might be regional. Your ad can say "locally owned, 47 five-star reviews on Google" — that builds trust. You can also bid on their brand name as a keyword. When someone searches "Servpro Austin," your ad can appear saying "Need help faster? Local response in under 30 minutes." I've seen this strategy steal 20–30% of competitor traffic.

**Q: Can I do this myself or do I need to hire someone?**
You can do it yourself if you have the time and willingness to learn. The setup takes a few hours. The optimization is ongoing — checking search terms, adjusting bids, testing ad copy, reviewing call recordings. Most business owners I've worked with start with good intentions and then stop logging in after two weeks because they're busy running their business. That's when the campaigns drift and costs creep up. If you have the time to spend 2–3 hours per week on your ads, do it yourself. If you don't, pay someone who will.

**Q: What's the biggest waste of money you see in restoration Google Ads?**
One thing: sending traffic to a homepage that looks like a brochure. A homeowner standing in water doesn't care about your company history or your community involvement. They care about one thing: can you get someone here in the next hour? If your landing page doesn't answer that question in under two seconds, you're paying for clicks that will never become customers.
