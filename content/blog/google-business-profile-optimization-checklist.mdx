## Section 1: Basic Information

Start with the fundamentals. These need to be 100% correct and consistent with how your business appears everywhere else online (website, Yelp, Facebook, etc.).

- Business name matches your real-world name exactly (no keyword stuffing like "Mike's Plumbing — Best Plumber in Chicago")
- Primary category is as specific as possible (not just "Restaurant" but "Coffee Shop" or "Espresso Bar")
- Add all relevant secondary categories
- Address is correct and formatted consistently (Street vs St, Suite vs Ste)
- Phone number is local (not a toll-free number)
- Website URL is correct and goes to a relevant page
- Hours are current and complete, including holiday hours
- Service area is set correctly if you serve customers at their location

## Section 2: Business Description

You get 750 characters. Use them well.

- Naturally includes your primary keyword (e.g., "coffee shop in [neighborhood]")
- Describes what makes you different, not just what you do
- Mentions key services or specialties
- Includes your city/neighborhood naturally
- Does not include URLs, promotional language, or keyword stuffing
- Is written for humans first, search engines second

## Section 3: Services and Products

This section is chronically underused. Every service you add is another keyword signal to Google.

- Add every service you offer with a title and description
- Include the specific service name as customers would search for it (e.g., "Balayage" not just "Hair Colouring")
- Add products if applicable (coffee drinks, retail products, etc.)
- Include prices where possible — it builds trust and improves conversion

## Section 4: Photos and Videos

Businesses with photos get 42% more direction requests. This matters.

- Cover photo: high quality, represents your best work or space
- Logo: clear, correctly sized
- Interior photos: show the atmosphere (at least 5)
- Exterior photos: help customers find you (include signage, parking)
- Team photos: builds trust and humanises your business
- Work/product photos: before/after, food, services in action
- New photos added regularly (aim for weekly)
- No stock photos — authenticity wins

## Section 5: Reviews

Reviews are the single highest-impact factor for local pack ranking after proximity.

- Minimum 10 reviews to start ranking competitively
- Average rating 4.2+ (below this hurts conversion significantly)
- Recent reviews — recency matters as much as volume
- Reviews contain keywords naturally (customers mentioning your services and location)
- You respond to every review — positive AND negative
- Response time under 24 hours
- Active system for requesting reviews from happy customers

## Section 6: Google Posts

Posts show on your profile and signal active management to Google.

- At least one post per week
- Mix of post types: offers, events, updates, products
- Each post has a clear call-to-action
- Images are high quality and correctly sized
- Posts are timely and relevant (seasonal, promotional, newsworthy)

## Section 7: Q&A

Most businesses ignore this section completely. That's an opportunity.

- Seed your own Q&A with the questions customers actually ask
- Answer every question promptly
- Include natural keywords in your answers
- Monitor for and remove inappropriate or incorrect Q&A

## Section 8: Attributes

Attributes are the checkboxes that appear on your profile and in search filters. Getting these right helps you appear in filtered searches.

- Select all applicable service options (dine-in, takeaway, delivery, etc.)
- Add accessibility features if applicable
- Add payment methods accepted
- Add any relevant health and safety attributes
- For specific niches: add pet-friendly, outdoor seating, WiFi, etc.

## The Ongoing Maintenance Schedule

A one-time optimisation decays over time. Set up a recurring schedule:

Weekly: Add a Google Post, respond to any new reviews.

Monthly: Add fresh photos, check hours are current, review Q&A for new questions.

Quarterly: Full audit — check all information is accurate, review competitor profiles for new optimization opportunities, analyse your insights data.

## How to Measure Progress

GBP Insights (in your dashboard) shows:
- Search queries that led to your profile
- Impressions (how many times your profile appeared)
- Click-throughs (website clicks, calls, directions)
- Photo views

Track these monthly. A properly optimised profile should show steady improvement in impressions and actions over 3–6 months. If numbers are flat after 3 months of consistent effort, that's a signal to look at your citation consistency and on-page SEO, which are the next levers to pull.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Stuffing Keywords Into Your Business Name

A plumbing company in Austin, Texas — let’s call them “Mike’s Plumbing — Best Plumber in Austin — Affordable Drain Cleaning” — came to me after their Google Business Profile stopped appearing in the local pack for their own brand name. They’d been on page one for “plumber Austin” but suddenly dropped to page four. The problem? Google’s algorithm finally penalized the keyword-stuffed business name. The business name was supposed to be just “Mike’s Plumbing,” but someone in marketing decided adding every possible search term would boost rankings. It worked for about six months, then backfired hard.

**The fix:** I helped them submit a name change request through Google Business Profile support, reverting to “Mike’s Plumbing” exactly as it appeared on their storefront, business license, and website. Then we added the keyword “plumber” naturally in the description and used secondary categories like “Drain Cleaning Service” and “Water Heater Repair.” We also created Google Posts about drain cleaning and water heater services, which gave Google the context it needed without polluting the name.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, their profile was reinstated and ranking. Over the next three months, calls increased by 40%, translating to roughly $2,000 in additional monthly revenue from emergency plumbing calls. Mike himself told me, “I should have never listened to the guy who said to stuff the name. Cost me two months of leads.”

**What to do instead:** Your business name must match your real-world name exactly — no keywords, no city, no “since 1995.” If you want to rank for additional services, use secondary categories, the description field, and Google Posts. That’s where Google reads context. Sticking extra terms in your name is like wearing a sandwich board that screams “I’m desperate” — it might get attention, but not the kind you want.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring the Q&A Section (Until It Gets Ugly)

A hair salon in Nashville, Tennessee was losing walk-in appointments every week. The owner, Sarah, couldn’t figure out why. She had 4.8 stars, decent photos, and consistent hours. When I looked at her GBP, I noticed the Q&A section had three unanswered questions — and one of them was damaging. “Do they use cheap products?” (no response) and “Can I bring my dog?” (answered by someone else: “No, they have allergies” — which wasn’t even true). The worst: a competitor had left a fake question: “Is it true the owner yells at customers?” with an answer from a dummy account saying “Yes, avoid this place.” Sarah had no idea this section was open for anyone to answer.

**The fix:** First, I had Sarah claim the Q&A section by posting answers to every existing question, correcting the misinformation, and pinning a FAQ at the top. Then we set up a routine: every Monday, she checks GBP for new questions and responds within 24 hours. We also proactively posted 5–6 common questions (price range, parking, whether they use sulfate-free products) with her own answers — this prevents randoms from answering first.

**The outcome:** Within two weeks, the negative Q&A had been buried by legitimate ones. Booking requests via GBP increased by 25%, and Sarah estimated an extra $1,500 in monthly revenue from new clients who cited the Q&As as helpful. She also started getting fewer “does your salon have parking?” calls, saving her front desk about 3 hours a week.

**What to do instead:** Treat the Q&A section like your storefront window. Check it weekly. Answer every question within 24 hours. Pre-load common answers so customers don’t have to ask. If you see fake or harmful questions, flag them to Google (they’ll sometimes remove them) and respond professionally. Leaving this section unattended is like having a voicemail inbox you never listen to — you’re missing leads and letting anyone impersonate you.

### Mistake #3: Forgetting to Update Holiday Hours

A coffee shop in Portland, Oregon — let’s call them “Pioneer Roasters” — had a regular crowd of office workers and remote employees. They’d built a solid 4.7 rating and appeared in the top 3 for “coffee shop Portland.” But during the Portland Rose Festival, a week-long event that brings 10,000+ visitors to the downtown area, the shop decided to open an hour earlier and close an hour later to capture the extra foot traffic. They updated their website but forgot to update Google Business Profile. Tourists and regulars showed up at 7 AM (they were open, but Google said 8 AM) or tried to visit at 7 PM (Google said 6 PM, but they were open). The owner, James, saw a 30% drop in daily sales during the festival week compared to the previous year.

**The fix:** We set up a recurring calendar reminder for every holiday and local event. James now updates GBP hours at least two weeks before any major change. We also enabled the “Holiday hours” feature in GBP settings, which lets you set special hours for multiple dates without overwriting your regular schedule. For unplanned changes, we showed him how to use the “Temporarily closed” option and the “Special hours” override from the Google Business Profile app.

**The outcome:** The following year during the same festival, Pioneer Roasters updated their hours two weeks out. Their daily sales during the festival increased by 15% over their baseline — roughly $800 in extra revenue per week. That’s $800 because they caught the people who otherwise would have walked to the next open shop. James said, “I lost a week’s worth of profit because I forgot to click a button. Never again.”

**What to do instead:** Put a recurring monthly task on your calendar: “Check GBP hours.” Use Google’s special hours feature for all holidays, even minor ones. If you’re participating in a local event, extend your hours in GBP before the event starts. Also, if you close for a renovation or vacation, mark it as “Temporarily closed” — this protects your ranking while telling customers you’re not open, unlike leaving stale hours up which can get you flagged as permanently closed.

### Mistake #4: Using Stock Photos Instead of Real Ones

A pet groomer in Denver, Colorado — “The Pampered Pooch” — had a GBP profile with six photos: two generic dog clippers, one stock photo of a golden retriever, and three pictures of a generic storefront that wasn’t even their building. They had 22 reviews and 3.9 stars. When I asked the owner, Rachel, why she hadn’t uploaded real photos, she said, “I’m not a photographer, and I don’t have time.” The result: every time someone searched “dog groomer near me” and saw her profile, they scrolled past. Her click-through rate was below 10%.

**The fix:** We spent 45 minutes on a Tuesday afternoon taking photos with Rachel’s iPhone: front of the shop (with the actual sign), the grooming table (with a clean workspace), a before/after of a poodle, a shot of the waiting area (with treats and water bowls), and a 15-second video of a dog getting a trim. We posted all 20 images plus the video to the GBP photo section, organized into categories (interior, exterior, at work, etc.).

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, profile views increased by 50% and clicks to the website doubled. Rachel started receiving calls saying, “Your shop looks so welcoming in the photos — much better than the other groomers.” Bookings increased by 30% over two months, generating an extra $3,800 in revenue. The photos cost nothing but time. Now Rachel uploads at least one new photo per week — a happy dog, a finished groom, or a seasonal decoration.

**What to do instead:** Upload at least 20 photos that show your real space, real team, and real results. Use a smartphone — natural light beats a DSLR in a dark room. Take before/after photos of your work (especially for service businesses like salons, barbers, dentists, groomers). Add a short video tour. Update photos monthly to signal to Google that your profile is active. Stock photos signal “I don’t care,” and customers notice.

## Using GBP Insights to Decide What to Change

Most business owners open Google Business Profile insights once, stare at the numbers, and close it. That’s like getting a full lab report and throwing it in the trash. The insights tab tells you exactly what’s working and what’s broken — if you know where to look.

For a gym in Chicago, I used insights to diagnose a problem. Their “Search queries” showed that 70% of searches were for “yoga studio Chicago” and “Pilates Chicago,” but their primary category was “Gym.” They had 0 photos related to yoga. No wonder people clicked through and left — they found a weight room, not a yoga mat. By changing the primary category to “Yoga Studio” and adding secondary categories for Pilates and barre, their visibility doubled. Photos of the yoga studio replaced the treadmills. Within 8 weeks, calls from GBP increased by 35%, resulting in about $1,200 in new memberships per month.

The key metrics to watch:
- **Search queries:** What terms people use to find you. If you’re showing up for terms that don’t match your actual services, adjust categories and description.
- **Where customers find you:** “Direct” (searching your name) vs. “Discovery” (finding you through category or keyword). If discovery is low, you’re not showing up for enough relevant searches — add secondary categories and post more often.
- **Photo views vs. other views:** If your profile gets 500 views but only 20 photo views, your photos aren’t compelling. The fix: add a new batch of high-quality photos every two weeks and test different styles (close-ups, wide shots, action shots).
- **Direction requests vs. phone calls vs. website clicks:** If direction requests are high but calls are low, you’re attracting people who need to find your physical location, but they aren’t calling ahead. That’s fine for a coffee shop or retail. But if calls are important (plumber, dentist), and direction requests are high while calls are low, your primary action button might be wrong — check that your phone number is prominent and the “Call” button is visible.

I check insights for every client once a month. I look for changes of more than 20% month-over-month. If something drops, I trace it to a specific action (did we change hours? Did a competitor open nearby?). If something jumps, I try to replicate it. This is the difference between guessing and knowing. Most guides skip this part — don’t.

## How to Handle Negative Reviews Without Losing Your Cool

I’m going to be direct: you cannot delete negative reviews. Google only removes them if they violate their policies (fake, spam, hate speech, or off-topic). “The customer was rude” is not a policy violation. “The customer left a 1-star review because they had a bad hair day” is not getting removed. So stop hoping for deletion and start thinking about strategy.

A dentist in NYC had a patient leave a 1-star review claiming the dentist “pushed unnecessary procedures” and “charged too much.” The dentist’s instinct was to fire back a defensive reply explaining why the patient was wrong. I stopped her. Instead, we wrote a response that acknowledged the concern without admitting fault: “We’re sorry to hear about your experience. We take all feedback seriously. We’d like to invite you to contact us directly so we can review your treatment plan and billing in detail. Our goal is always transparent care.” Then we encouraged happy patients (about 30 of them) to leave fresh reviews over the next two weeks. The negative review stayed, but it was now buried under a sea of 5-stars, and the dentist’s reply showed future patients she was reasonable.

The rule: never argue in the response. Never call out the reviewer by name in a negative way. Thank them, apologize for their experience (not for doing something wrong), and move the conversation offline. A calm, professional reply can actually increase your rating in the eyes of a reader — they see you as responsive and reasonable. Then focus on getting more positive reviews to dilute the volume.

I’ve seen a single negative review on a profile with 10+ recent positive ones barely affect click-through rates. On a profile with 3 reviews total, one bad review can tank conversions by 20%. The math is simple: keep asking for reviews every week. Use a tool like Mailchimp or a simple text message template to send a review request after every completed service. For service businesses using Square or Booksy, you can automate this — Square’s review request feature sends a link after payment. A pet groomer in Denver using Square saw her review count jump from 12 to 68 in 3 months just by turning on the automated request.

If you believe a review is fake (competitor or former employee), you can report it to Google. They’ll sometimes remove it if there’s evidence (e.g., the reviewer never actually visited your business). But don’t count on it. Assume every review is permanent and plan your strategy around volume and professionalism, not removal.

## Integrating Your GBP with Your Booking or POS System

Many business owners run their GBP profile and their booking system in two separate worlds. A hair salon uses Booksy for appointments, Square for payments, and Google for discovery — but none of them talk to each other. The result: a customer finds the salon on Google, clicks “Book,” and ends up on a generic landing page that doesn’t match the service they saw in the GBP post. Or they call to book, the front desk writes it on paper, and the online calendar stays empty. It’s a mess.

The fix is to connect your GBP with your booking and POS tools. Here’s how it works for three common small-business setups:

- **Square:** Square’s Appointments feature can sync with GBP by adding the booking URL directly to your GBP profile. When you create a Google Post about a special offer, you link it to a Square booking page pre-filled with the exact service. A bakery in Austin tested this: they posted “Free cupcake with your first class” and linked to Square Appointments for cake decorating classes. Within a week, they booked 8 new classes worth $320 in revenue directly from the GBP link — they’d never tracked that before.

- **Booksy:** Booksy has a direct integration to add a “Book online” button to your GBP. You don’t need a separate website. A nail salon in Nashville flipped this on and saw booking requests via GBP increase by 40% — their phone didn’t ring as much, but the online bookings doubled. Less time on the phone, more money in the till.

- **Mailchimp:** Not a booking system, but integration for follow-ups. When someone books via GBP link, you can capture their email (if your booking system asks for it) and add them to a Mailchimp segment for rebooking reminders. A fitness studio in Chicago did this: after booking a class through GBP, new clients received a 3-email sequence (thank you, reminder, rebooking offer). They saw a 25% repeat booking rate within 30 days, worth roughly $500 in additional monthly revenue.

The mistake most owners make: they treat GBP as a static listing and their booking system as a separate piece of software. They’re the same funnel. Link your appointment URL in the “Booking” section of GBP. If you use Google’s own booking provider (like Wix, Squarespace, or one of the integrated partners), set it up so the “Book” button on your profile goes directly to a live calendar. Test it yourself: search your business, click the button, and see if you can book in under three taps. If it takes more than that, you’re losing customers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: If I claim my Google listing, will Google start calling me every day to sell me ads?**  
No. Google Business Profile is a free service. You’ll only get promotional emails if you sign up for Google Ads separately. However, Google sometimes sends notifications about new features or policy changes. Those are useful, not salesy. If you start getting calls from a “Google representative” telling you your listing needs urgent verification and asking for payment, that’s a scam. Hang up. Real Google support doesn’t cold call small businesses.

**Q: Can I delete bad reviews? I’m pretty sure a competitor left a fake one.**  
You cannot delete reviews yourself. Google will remove reviews that violate their policies — threats, fake names, off-topic rants, or reviews from people who never visited. You can flag a review for removal, but the process is slow and inconsistent. I’ve seen legitimate fakes removed within a week, and real reviews stay up after false flags. Your best bet is to respond professionally and then pile on genuine positive reviews to bury the bad one. If the review is clearly fake (e.g., the person claims you’re in a different city), you can also report it through the Google Business Profile support tool.

**Q: How many reviews do I need to show up in the top 3 for my service in my area?**  
There’s no magic number. I’ve seen a pizzeria with 30 reviews and 4.9 stars outrank a competitor with 200 reviews and 4.3 stars. Google considers review velocity (how fast you get new reviews), response rate, photo count, and relevance to the search query. A rule of thumb: aim for at least 20 reviews with an average above 4.0. Then focus on getting 2–3 new reviews per week consistently. That momentum signals Google your business is active and trusted. If you’re stuck at 15 reviews for six months, you’ll fall behind newer businesses that are getting fresh reviews weekly.

**Q: Should I post on Google every day? I don’t have time for that.**  
No, and you don’t need to. Posting once a week is sufficient to keep your profile active. The key is consistency, not frequency. Once a week for 12 weeks will outperform ten posts in one week followed by radio silence. Each post should have a clear call-to-action: “Book an appointment,” “Call for a quote,” or “Visit us this weekend.” I tell clients to set aside 20 minutes each Monday morning to write one post for the week. Use a photo you already took on your phone. You’re done. If you need to post about a seasonal offer or event, add one more. But daily posting is overkill for most small businesses — you’ll burn out and the posts will get repetitive.

**Q: My business doesn’t have a physical store — I’m a mobile plumber. Do I need to show my home address on Google?**  
No, and you probably shouldn’t. For service-area businesses (plumbers, dog walkers, electricians, mobile groomers), Google lets you hide your address and define a service area (up to 20 locations or a radius). You’ll disappear from the map pin, but you’ll still show up in the local pack when someone searches for your service in your area. This is a common mistake: mobile plumbers in Denver showed their home address on GBP, then got customers showing up at their front door. Set your profile as “Service area business,” enter the cities or zip codes you serve, and keep your address hidden. That way you’re local without becoming a drop-in destination.

**Q: If I change my business name to include a keyword like “best plumber Chicago,” will Google bump me up?**  
It might for a few months, but then Google will penalize you. I’ve seen this pattern repeat across clients in Austin, Nashville, and Portland. Google’s guidelines explicitly say your business name must reflect your real-world name. Keyword-stuffed names get flagged, and once flagged, you can lose your listing entirely or have it suspended. The short-term gain is not worth the long-term headache. Instead, put those keywords in your description, your categories, and your posts. It’s safer and works just as well — plus you won’t look spammy to customers reading your name.
