## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake #1: Setting Up Your Google Business Profile with the Wrong Category

A dentist in Austin, Texas came to me after spending six months frustrated with their local search performance. They'd invested $1,200/month in a local marketing agency, run Facebook ads, and even printed postcards. New patient calls were flat. When I pulled up their Google Business Profile, the primary category was set to "Dentist." Sounds right, right? Except their practice focused entirely on cosmetic dentistry — veneers, bonding, smile makeovers. The secondary categories were empty.

Here's the fix: Google's algorithm weights your primary category heavily. "Dentist" is too broad. If you do implants, set "Dental Implants Provider" as primary. If you do orthodontics, set "Orthodontist." If you're a general dentist who does a bit of everything, use "Dentist" but fill all 10 secondary categories with specific services: "Cosmetic Dentist," "Emergency Dental Service," "Teeth Whitening Service," etc.

The outcome for the Austin practice: Within 8 weeks, their GBP impressions jumped 340%. They started appearing for "veneers Austin" instead of just "dentist Austin." New patient calls went from 12 per month to 31. Average new patient value was about $650. That's an additional $12,350/month in revenue from a category fix that took 10 minutes.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Name, Address, Phone Number (NAP) Consistency

A pediatric dentist in Nashville ran a clean practice, great reviews, nice website. But their new patient bookings had plateaued at around 45 per month for two years. I ran a local citation audit. Turns out, when they moved offices three years ago, they updated Google but not Yelp, not Healthgrades, not the local chamber of commerce directory, not their Facebook page, and not a dozen dental-specific directories.

Google saw six different addresses for this practice. Their local ranking was tanked. The practice was essentially invisible for searches within 3 miles, where 62% of patients come from.

The fix: I spent two hours with a tool called BrightLocal — $29/month — to audit all citations. Then we manually updated every directory. For the ones that didn't allow edits, we claimed the listing and flagged it as closed under the old address.

The outcome: Within 60 days, their average ranking for "pediatric dentist Nashville" moved from page 3, position 4 to page 1, position 3. Monthly new patient bookings increased to 68. At $900 per new patient (pediatric cleanings, exams, and follow-up treatments), that's an additional $20,700 per month. The time investment: about 4 hours total. Cost: $29 for the tool.

### Mistake #3: Treating Reviews Like a Set-It-and-Forget-It Task

A general dentist in Portland, Oregon had 47 reviews on Google, average rating 4.8. Respectable. But they hadn't responded to a single one in over a year. Worse, they had stopped asking for reviews entirely after hitting that 47 mark. Their new patient flow had been slowly declining — about 2-3 fewer per month for six months straight.

Here's the data most guides skip: Google's algorithm factors recency and frequency of reviews into local pack rankings. A practice with 200 reviews from 2020 ranks below a practice with 30 reviews from this quarter. Additionally, responding to reviews signals active management, which Google rewards.

The fix: We implemented a three-step system. First, they bought a simple tool called Broadly ($199/month) that automates review requests via text after appointments. Second, the office manager spent 10 minutes each morning responding to every new review — thanking positive ones, addressing negative ones with a genuine offer to resolve the issue offline. Third, we added a review request link to their post-appointment email, which they sent through Mailchimp at no extra cost.

The outcome: In three months, they accumulated 82 new reviews. Their local pack ranking for "dentist Portland" went from position 7 to position 3. New patient calls increased from 28/month to 44/month. At $550 average revenue per new patient, that's an extra $8,800/month. The cost: $199/month for the tool plus 10 minutes of staff time per day.

### Mistake #4: Building a Website That Forgets Mobile Users

A cosmetic dentist in Denver spent $8,000 on a beautiful new website. Full-screen hero images. Slow-loading animations. Text in images that screen readers couldn't parse. On a desktop, it was stunning. On a phone — the device 68% of their traffic came from — it took 11 seconds to load and required constant pinching and zooming.

The fix wasn't a rebuild. We compressed images using TinyPNG (free), removed two unnecessary JavaScript libraries, simplified the font stack, and swapped the image-heavy hero for a clean text-and-button layout that loaded in under 2 seconds. We also added click-to-call buttons on every service page.

The outcome: Bounce rate dropped from 74% to 41%. Form submissions increased 160%. Phone calls from the website went from 8 per week to 23 per week. The "dentist Denver" keyword ranking actually improved two positions, because Google prioritizes fast mobile sites in local search results. Cost of the fix: maybe 6 hours of a developer's time at $75/hour. That's $450.

## How to Structure Your Service Pages for Local Search Traffic

Most dental websites organize service pages the way dentists think about dentistry — by procedure type. "Root Canals." "Crowns." "Fillings." That's fine for existing patients. But new patients don't search that way. They search for problems.

A dentist in Chicago was getting zero traffic for "tooth pain emergency." Turns out, their website had no page for that term. They had a "Services" page with a paragraph about root canals, but no dedicated page answering "what to do if your tooth hurts right now."

Here's the fix that works across every city I've tested:

Create one page per patient problem, not per procedure. For example:
- "Emergency Tooth Extraction in [City]" 
- "How to Fix a Chipped Tooth in [City]"
- "Tooth Pain Relief in [City] — What to Do Before Your Appointment"
- "Same-Day Crowns in [City]: What to Expect"
- "Invisalign vs Braces in [City]: Cost Comparison 2024"

Each page needs: the problem in the headline, what the patient should do, what the procedure involves (in plain language), cost range, and a clear next step (call button, booking link, or form). Include the city name in the H1, the meta title, and at least twice in the body text. But don't stuff it — write for humans.

The Chicago dentist created six of these pages. Within 90 days, they ranked on page 1 for "tooth pain [city]" and "emergency dentist [city]." Those two pages alone drove 45 new patient calls per month. The pages were written by their office manager in about two hours each. No copywriter needed.

Tools to use: Google Search Console (free) to see what people are actually typing to find your site. Ahrefs or Semrush for keyword research if you want to get fancy, but honestly, just ask your patients what they Googled before calling. Write that down. Build pages for those terms.

## Using Local Paid Search to Fill Gaps While Organic SEO Builds

Organic SEO takes time. Anybody who promises you page-one rankings in two weeks is selling something you don't want to buy. But you can't pause your business while waiting for Google to notice you.

A dentist in San Diego needed new patients immediately. Their organic presence was weak, and they were losing ground to a DSO (dental service organization) that had opened down the street. I set up a Google Ads campaign focused entirely on local high-intent keywords: "dentist open Saturday San Diego," "emergency tooth extraction San Diego," "same day crown San Diego."

Budget: $800/month. We excluded broad terms like "dentist" because that money burns fast. We focused on 12 specific service terms and set a radius of 5 miles around the practice.

The first month: 22 clicks, cost $620, 8 form fills, 12 phone calls. That's 20 leads. At a 40% close rate (typical for emergency and same-day services), that's 8 new patients. Average revenue per new patient: $480. Total revenue from those patients: $3,840. Return on ad spend: 6.2x in month one.

But here's the part most guides skip: the Google Ads data told us which services people actually searched for. "Emergency extraction" got 3x the clicks of "cosmetic bonding." That information shaped their organic content strategy. We built the website pages for high-search procedures first, not what the dentist thought was most important.

If you're on a tight budget, start with $500/month. Use Google's "Maximize Clicks" bid strategy initially, then switch to "Target CPA" once you have 30+ conversions of data. Track calls with call forwarding (Google Ads offers this free) so you know which keywords actually ring your phone, not just which ones get clicks.

Other tools: Booksy integrates with Google Ads for booking directly from the ad. Square Appointments can track online booking sources. Yelp Ads work for dentists in competitive markets but start at $300/month minimum and the quality of leads varies wildly. Test one platform before layering on another.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I really need to spend money on local SEO, or can I do it myself?**

You can absolutely do the basics yourself. Claim your Google Business Profile. Ask every patient for a review. Make sure your name, address, and phone number match everywhere online. Those three things will get you 60% of the way there. The paid part comes in when you want to skip the 6-month wait for organic rankings, or when you're in a competitive city where every other dentist has already done the basics. My advice: do the free stuff first. Track results for 90 days. If you're not seeing movement, then consider a consultant or paid tools.

**Q: How long does it take to see results from local SEO?**

Honest answer: 3 to 6 months for meaningful organic ranking changes. Faster if you're in a small town with little competition. Slower if you're in downtown Chicago or Manhattan. Paid search gives you results in 48 hours. The mistake people make is giving up on organic after two months. Google doesn't trust a brand-new site or profile. It needs to see consistent activity — new reviews, updated posts, fresh content — over time. Patience is the strategy.

**Q: What if I get a bad review from a patient I can't please?**

Respond publicly within 24 hours. Acknowledge the issue. Apologize for their experience, even if you think they're wrong. Do NOT argue, do NOT blame the patient, do NOT post their medical details. Say something like: "We're sorry your experience wasn't what we aim for. Please contact us directly at [phone or email] so we can make this right." Prospective patients read your responses more than they read the actual review. A gracious, professional reply to a bad review builds more trust than 20 five-star reviews with no response.

**Q: Should I pay for Yelp or Angi leads?**

Yelp Ads: test with $300/month for two months. Track every lead. In my experience, Yelp leads for dentists are lower quality than Google leads — more price-shoppers, less likely to book. But in some markets (coastal cities, affluent suburbs), Yelp drives solid results. Angi (formerly Angie's List): not worth it for dentists in most markets. The platform skews toward home services. You'll pay for clicks from people looking for plumbers. Skip it unless you have budget to burn.

**Q: How often should I post on my Google Business Profile?**

Once a week minimum. Post offers, new services, team introductions, patient education tips, before/after photos (with consent). Google rewards fresh content. A practice that posts weekly gets 3-4x more GBP views than one that posts monthly. You can schedule posts through the GBP dashboard or use a tool like OneLocal or Chatmeter. But honestly, the free dashboard works fine. Set a reminder on your phone for Tuesday mornings. Take 10 minutes. Write a post. Add a photo. Done.

**Q: My website was built by a national dental marketing company. Do I need to start over?**

Not necessarily. Most national dental websites share the same problem: generic content with your city name swapped in. If your site ranks, keep it. But audit these three things: (1) Does the site load in under 3 seconds on mobile? Test with Google's PageSpeed Insights. (2) Does each service page have unique content, not just "We offer [procedure] in [city]" repeated? (3) Is your phone number clickable on every page? If the answer to any of those is no, you don't need a rebuild. You need edits. A local freelancer can fix these issues for $500-$1,000. That's cheaper than a $5,000 rebuild.
