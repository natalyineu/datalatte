## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Choosing the Wrong Google Business Profile Category

A studio owner in Austin, Texas called me after six months of getting almost no leads from Google. He was running a BJJ and Muay Thai gym. Somebody told him to pick "Fitness Center" as his primary category because it had more search volume. That's technically true — "fitness center" gets more monthly searches than "martial arts school." But it's also useless because nobody searching for "fitness center" wants to get choked in a gi.

What happened: He was competing against Anytime Fitness and Planet Fitness for those searches. His listing showed up for people looking for treadmills and dumbbells. The people who actually wanted martial arts training couldn't find him because Google didn't know what he was.

**The fix:** I changed his primary category to "Martial Arts School" and added "Jujitsu School" and "Kickboxing School" as secondary categories. We also rewrote his business description to start with the exact services he offered — "Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu classes in Austin" — within the first 30 characters.

**The outcome:** Within three weeks, his GMB profile impressions increased by 140%. His calls from Google went from 4 per week to 18 per week. He spent $0 on this fix. The only cost was the hour it took me to explain why "fitness center" was killing him.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Duplicate Listings

A woman who ran a children's karate studio in Denver had no idea she had three separate Yelp pages, two Facebook pages with different addresses, and an old listing on a defunct directory from 2016 that listed her phone number as disconnected. She wondered why potential customers kept telling her they "couldn't find" her studio.

Duplicate listings confuse Google. When the algorithm sees the same business name at slightly different addresses or with different phone numbers, it doesn't know which one is real. Sometimes it splits the ranking signal across all of them. Sometimes it picks the wrong one and ignores the correct listing entirely.

**The fix:** We spent four hours doing a full citation audit using BrightLocal. Found 14 duplicates across directories like Yelp, Yellowpages, Superpages, and a local Denver business association site. We claimed, merged, or deleted every duplicate. Then we made sure the correct NAP (name, address, phone) was identical on every single platform.

**The outcome:** Her organic calls increased by 35% over two months. More importantly, customers stopped showing up at her old address from 2016. The cost was $35 for the BrightLocal trial month and four hours of my time. She estimated that one duplicate listing alone was costing her roughly $1,200 per month in lost trials.

### Mistake 3: Not Tracking Phone Calls

A Muay Thai gym in Portland was spending $500/month on Google Ads. The owner told me it "wasn't working." When I asked how he knew, he said not many people were clicking the ad. I checked his Google Ads dashboard — he had a 4% click-through rate and was paying $4.50 per click. That's actually solid for local martial arts. The problem was he had no way to track what happened after the click.

He was sending ad traffic to his website's contact page. People were calling the number on that page. But he had no call tracking. He thought the calls were coming from his Yelp listing or word of mouth. He was about to cancel the ads entirely.

**The fix:** We installed a call tracking number using CallRail ($30/month). We set it up so the number on his website only appeared when someone came from a Google Ad. We also added a simple form with a "How did you hear about us?" dropdown.

**The outcome:** Turns out his Google Ads were driving 22 calls per month. Of those, 9 turned into booked intro sessions. At an average first-month revenue of $180 per intro student (one month of classes plus gi purchase), those ads were generating $1,620 in revenue against $500 in ad spend. A 3.2x return. He was one week away from killing a profitable campaign because he didn't know how to measure it.

### Mistake 4: Writing a Generic Business Description

A jiu-jitsu studio in Nashville had a GMB description that read: "We are a martial arts studio dedicated to helping people of all ages achieve their fitness goals through quality instruction and a supportive environment." That sentence could describe literally any gym in America. It said nothing about jiu-jitsu. It said nothing about Nashville. It said nothing about why somebody should pick them over the five other BJJ gyms within three miles.

Google uses your GMB description to match your listing against search queries. If you type "no-gi jiu-jitsu Nashville" and the studio's description doesn't include "no-gi" or "Nashville," you're making Google work harder to find you. Sometimes it won't bother.

**The fix:** We rewrote it to: "Nashville's premier no-gi jiu-jitsu school. Morning and evening classes six days a week. First week free. Located at 123 Main Street, two blocks from the 5 Points intersection." Specific. Local. Actionable.

**The outcome:** Within 30 days, his ranking for "no-gi jiu-jitsu Nashville" went from page 4 to the top 3 local pack. Organic site traffic from GMB increased by 65%. The rewrite took me 15 minutes.

## How Much Should You Actually Spend on Local Ads?

I've managed local ad budgets from $300/month to $50,000/month. The number that works for a martial arts studio depends on your city, your competition, and how fast you want results. But here are some real numbers from actual clients.

### Google Ads for Martial Arts: What Works

In Chicago, a boxing gym was spending $1,800/month on broad keywords like "boxing classes Chicago." Their cost per click was $6.50. They were getting leads, but each lead cost $75. That's too expensive unless you're selling high-ticket private training.

**What we changed:** We narrowed the keywords to "beginner boxing classes Chicago" and "boxing gym near me for adults." We added negative keywords like "UFC," "MMA," and "professional fighting." We set a location radius of 5 miles instead of the entire Chicago metro. The cost per click dropped to $3.20. Cost per lead dropped to $28.

**The sweet spot:** For most martial arts studios in mid-sized US cities, I recommend starting at $500-$800/month on Google Ads. Target location-based keywords with a 5-mile radius. Use phrase match and exact match only — broad match will waste your money. Track conversions with a call tracking number and a "Book Now" button that goes to a specific landing page.

### The Yelp Trap (And When It Actually Works)

Yelp sales reps will tell you that you need to spend $300-$500/month to show up at the top of Yelp searches. In my experience, that's true — but it's rarely worth it for martial arts studios.

I had a client in San Diego who spent $400/month on Yelp ads for six months. He got exactly 11 leads. Two converted. That's $2,400 in ad spend for maybe $600 in first-month revenue. Terrible ROI.

But here's the exception: If your studio is in a tourist-heavy area or a neighborhood where people specifically use Yelp to find services, it can work. A studio in Manhattan Beach, California spent $200/month on Yelp and was getting consistent bookings from tourists who wanted a drop-in class while on vacation. For them, it worked because their audience was already on Yelp.

**My rule:** Test Yelp for two months at $150/month. If you don't get at least 3x return on ad spend, pull the money and put it into Google Ads or Facebook.

### Facebook and Instagram for Local Reach

This is where I see the most wasted money. Studio owners post a photo of their logo and write "Come train with us!" and then wonder why nobody shows up.

**What actually works:** Run ads that feature a specific student transformation or a video of a class in action. Target people within 10 miles who are interested in "fitness," "self-defense," or "karate." Make the offer specific: "First week free + free gi" or "$49 for your first month."

A studio in Phoenix ran Facebook ads for 90 days at $300/month. They targeted parents of kids aged 5-12. The ad showed a video of a kid breaking a board for the first time. The caption was: "Your kid wants to feel strong. We can help." They got 47 leads, 23 trial bookings, and 12 new members. At $150/month average revenue per kid membership, that's $1,800/month in recurring revenue from a $900 ad spend.

**The tool that matters:** Use Facebook's local awareness ad objective. It's built for exactly this — getting people to walk through your door.

## Building Local Backlinks That Actually Move the Ranking Needle

Most local SEO guides tell you to get links from "high-authority directories." That's vague and mostly useless. Here's what actually works for martial arts studios.

### Sponsor Local Events

A karate studio in Cleveland started sponsoring the local elementary school's fall festival. Cost: $250 for a banner and a $100 donation. In return, they got a link from the school district's website and the PTA's newsletter. That link from a .edu domain was worth more than 50 directory links.

**Why it works:** Google trusts local educational and government websites. A single link from your city's parks and recreation department or a local school can move your local pack ranking more than anything on your GMB profile.

### Trade Links With Non-Competing Local Businesses

A BJJ gym in Denver partnered with a physical therapy clinic down the street. They wrote a blog post for the clinic's website about "Why BJJ Athletes Get Injured (And How PT Helps)" and included a link back to the gym. The clinic wrote a post for the gym's blog about "Warm-Up Drills to Prevent Injury." That's a legitimate, reciprocal link between two non-competing businesses.

**The result:** Both sites saw a modest ranking boost for local search terms. More importantly, the PT clinic started referring injured athletes to the gym for rehab, and the gym started sending injured students to the clinic. Both businesses got new customers.

### Get Listed on Local Business Roundups

Local news sites and blogs often publish "Best Martial Arts Studios in [City]" articles. Find them. Reach out to the author. If you're not included, ask why. If your studio is legitimately good, they'll often add you for free.

A studio in Nashville got listed on a local lifestyle blog's "Best Gyms in Nashville" article. That one link drove 200+ referral visits and 5 new student inquiries. It took 10 minutes to send the email.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long until I see results from local SEO?**

If you fix the obvious mistakes — wrong GMB category, duplicate listings, bad description — you can see improvements in 2-4 weeks. Ranking for competitive terms takes 3-6 months. If somebody promises you page 1 rankings in 30 days, they're lying or they're targeting keywords nobody searches for.

**Q: Do I really need to respond to every Google review?**

Yes, but you don't need to write a novel. "Thanks for the kind words, Sarah. Glad your son is loving the kids' program" takes 15 seconds. Responding to reviews signals to Google that your listing is active. It also helps with conversion — people read reviews and the responses. If they see a negative review and the owner responded professionally, they'll still come in.

**Q: Should I pay for Google Business Profile posts?**

You don't have to. GMB posts are free. Post once a week — a photo of a class, a special offer, a student achievement. They show up in your listing and give people a reason to engage. I've seen posts about summer camps drive 30+ clicks each. Zero cost.

**Q: What about Bing? Do I need to optimize for it?**

Bing has about 6% market share in the US. It's not nothing, but it's not worth spending hours on. Claim your listing on Bing Places (it's free), make sure your info is correct, and move on. Don't let anyone sell you a "Bing optimization" package.

**Q: My competitor is on page 1 and I'm not. Can I ever catch up?**

Yes, if you're willing to be patient and do the work. Most of the time, the competitor got there first and hasn't been maintaining their listing. I've overtaken bigger, older studios by fixing fundamentals — clean citations, good photos, responding to reviews, and getting 2-3 solid local backlinks. It's not magic. It's just doing the things they stopped doing.

**Q: How much should I pay for local SEO services?**

For a legitimate local SEO agency or freelancer who will actually work on your account, expect $500-$2,000/month. If someone offers you "local SEO" for $99/month, they're running a script and sending you a PDF report nobody reads. Ask them: "What's your process for building local backlinks?" and "How do you find and fix duplicate listings?" If they can't answer clearly, walk away.
