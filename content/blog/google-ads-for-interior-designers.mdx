## How do I find renovation clients with Google Ads?

The first step is to target the intent that signals a homeowner is ready to remodel.
Use keywords like "kitchen remodel designer," "mid‑century modern living room," and "home office layout."

- **Geotarget** your city and neighboring towns (e.g., Austin, TX and Round Rock).
- **Schedule ads** for 9 am‑5 pm when homeowners browse design inspiration.
- **Add ad extensions** for phone calls and sitelinks to your portfolio page.

Example: BrightSpace Interiors in Austin set a $500 weekly budget, targeted "kitchen remodel designer Austin," and booked 8 new consultations in the first two weeks.

Pair this with a **Google Business Profile** boost so your ads appear with a map pin, increasing local trust. Our [Google Business Profile optimization](/services/google-business-profile) can handle that for you.

## What budget should I set for my first campaign?

Start small, test fast. Allocate $15‑$25 per day for a **30‑day pilot**.

- **$450‑$750** total spend gives enough data to see which keywords actually convert.
- Expect a **Cost per Lead (CPL)** of $60‑$80 for design services, based on industry averages.

## How to turn clicks into booked consultations?

A click is only valuable when it becomes a phone call or calendar booking.

1. **Dedicated landing page** – One headline, a short portfolio carousel, and a clear "Book a Free 30‑min Design Call" button.
2. **Instant call button** – Enable call‑only extensions for mobile users; most homeowners call within minutes of seeing an ad.
3. **Lead form** – Keep fields to name, phone, and a one‑line project description. Use a $10‑$15 incentive (e.g., "Free style board") to boost submissions.

## How to measure success and avoid wasted spend?

Track three core metrics:

- **Cost per Lead (CPL)** – Aim for &lt;$80 initially.
- **Lead‑to‑Client rate** – For interior designers, 20‑30 % of qualified leads convert to a paid project.
- **Return on Ad Spend (ROAS)** – Target at least 3 × the ad spend within 90 days.

Set up **conversion tracking** for phone calls (via call‑only ads) and form submissions. Use the **Google Ads "Search Terms" report** to add negative keywords like "DIY" or "free" that attract the wrong audience.

## Common Mistakes (And What to Do Instead)

### Mistake 1: Targeting "Interior Designer" Instead of "I Need a Kitchen"

I watched a designer in Nashville burn through $2,800 in six weeks. She targeted keywords like "interior design services Nashville" and "best interior designer Tennessee." Her click-through rate was fine. Her conversion rate was 0.4%. She got exactly one phone call, and the caller asked if she did commercial office build-outs. She does residential only.

Here's what happened: people searching "interior designer" are browsing inspiration, comparing portfolios, or researching pricing for six months from now. They're not renovating. The intent is informational, not transactional.

**The fix:** She rebuilt the campaign around "contractor-ready" keywords: "kitchen remodel contractor Nashville," "bathroom renovation designer," "open concept floor plan architect." She added negative keywords for "commercial," "office," "staging," and "virtual design."

**The outcome:** In week one with the new keywords, she spent $340 and got two consultation bookings worth a combined $8,200 in projected project fees. Her cost per lead dropped from $280 to $68. She stopped burning money three weeks earlier than I usually see people catch this.

### Mistake 2: Running Ads 24/7 and Wasting 40% of Your Budget

A Portland OR designer called me frustrated. She was spending $900 a month on Google Ads, getting clicks, but the phone never rang during business hours. I pulled her data. Seventy-three percent of her clicks happened between 8 pm and 2 am. She was paying for people who were doom-scrolling Pinterest in bed.

Small business owners in home services forget that renovation decisions are made differently than buying a t-shirt. People don't call a designer at 11 pm to schedule a consultation. They click an ad, browse your portfolio, and maybe — maybe — fill out a form. But the conversion happens the next day during working hours, after they've talked to their spouse.

**The fix:** She scheduled ads for 8 am to 6 pm, Monday through Friday, with a two-hour window on Saturday mornings. She also turned on call-only ads during those hours, so mobile users saw a "tap to call" button instead of a landing page.

**The outcome:** Her monthly spend dropped to $540. Her lead volume stayed the same because the late-night clicks weren't converting anyway. Effective cost per lead dropped by 38%. She used the saved $360 to run a retargeting campaign for people who visited her portfolio but didn't call.

### Mistake 3: Sending All Clicks to a Generic Homepage

A Chicago designer was running ads for "luxury kitchen remodel" but sending traffic to her homepage. Her homepage had a hero image of a living room. Not a kitchen. A living room with a beige sofa and a throw blanket.

The disconnect was obvious to anyone who looked at the data for more than thirty seconds. Her ad copy said, "Award-winning kitchen designs in Chicago." The landing page said, "Welcome to our design studio, serving the greater Chicago area since 2012." No kitchen photos above the fold. No call to action about scheduling a kitchen consultation. Her bounce rate was 81%.

**The fix:** She created a dedicated landing page for each service. Kitchen remodels got kitchen photos, a list of what the process looks like, and a form that asked "What room are you remodeling?" instead of "Tell us about your project." Bathroom remodels got a different page. Basement conversions got another.

**The outcome:** Landing page bounce rate went from 81% to 44%. Conversion rate on the kitchen page hit 6.2%. She spent $400 building three pages on Squarespace and recovered the cost in the first week of leads. One of those leads turned into a $14,000 full-kitchen project.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Negative Keywords Until You've Wasted $1,000

An interior designer in Denver was getting clicks from people searching "interior design salary," "how to become an interior designer," and "interior design courses online." She was paying $4.50 per click for high school students doing career research.

She didn't know this was happening because she was looking at aggregate data. "I'm getting clicks," she said. "The traffic looks good." The traffic was good — for someone who wanted to waste money on people who will never hire a designer.

**The fix:** I walked her through the Search Terms Report in Google Ads. We added 47 negative keywords in fifteen minutes: "salary," "career," "degree," "online," "remote," "DIY," "cost," "free," "template," "software," "school," "certification," "jobs," "hiring," "internship," "salary range," "become," "training," "course," "class." We also added negatives for "staging" and "rental" because she doesn't do either.

**The outcome:** Her click-through rate stayed the same. Her wasted spend dropped from roughly $320/month to under $40. She reallocated that $280 into a retargeting campaign that brought in two booked consultations within ten days.

## How to Use Google Local Services Ads Alongside Search Campaigns

If you're an interior designer running only traditional Google Ads, you're missing the second-best channel Google offers. Local Services Ads (LSAs) are the pay-per-lead listings that show up at the very top of search results with the little green checkmark and "Google Guaranteed" badge. They work differently than search ads, and for home renovation businesses, they often outperform everything else.

Here's the setup. You pay per qualified lead, not per click. If someone calls you from an LSA and it's a spam call or a wrong number, Google refunds you. That alone is worth testing because anyone who's run search ads knows how much budget gets eaten by accidental taps and tire-kickers.

A designer in Austin ran a side-by-side test. She spent $800 on search ads and $600 on LSAs in the same month. The search ads generated 12 leads at $67 each. The LSAs generated 10 leads at $60 each. But here's the difference: six of the LSA leads booked consultations within a week. Only three of the search ad leads did. The LSA leads were higher intent because customers see the "Google Guaranteed" badge and trust you before they click.

The catch is availability. LSAs require you to pass a background check, have proper business licensing, and maintain a minimum response time. If you're a solo designer who takes two days to return calls, LSAs will hurt your ranking. You need to answer calls within 24 hours and ideally within a few hours.

The smart move is to run both campaigns in parallel. Use search ads for evergreen brand awareness and specific service keywords like "kitchen remodel Austin TX." Use LSAs to capture the high-intent "I need someone now" crowd. Budget split: start with 60% search, 40% LSAs. Adjust based on which channel gives you better cost per booked consultation, not cost per click or cost per lead.

One warning: LSAs work best for services where the customer needs to hire someone locally. Virtual interior design consultations — where you work remotely with clients in other states — won't perform well on LSAs because the algorithm favors local intent. Save those for your search campaigns with national or multi-state targeting.

## Retargeting: The Tool Most Designers Skip (And Regret It)

Here's a number that made me order a second coffee I did not need: 97% of people who visit your website will leave without contacting you. They're browsing. Comparing. Thinking about it. They liked your portfolio photo of that mid-century kitchen in Minneapolis, but they're not ready to commit.

Most interior designers look at their Google Ads dashboard, see a 3% conversion rate, and say, "That's fine." It's not fine. It's leaving money sitting on the table.

Retargeting is showing ads to people who already visited your site but didn't convert. You've already paid to get them there. The click is free now. You just need a gentle reminder.

A designer in Nashville — the same one who wasted $2,800 on the wrong keywords — added a retargeting campaign using the Google Ads remarketing tag. She set it to show ads to anyone who visited her portfolio page but didn't click the "Book a Consultation" button. The ads were simple: a photo of her best kitchen renovation with text saying "Your kitchen could look like this. Free consultation."

**The cost:** $180 over two weeks.
**The result:** Four people who had visited her site and left came back and booked consultations. Two of them signed contracts worth $11,400 combined.

You need three things to make retargeting work:
- The Google Ads remarketing tag installed on your entire site. If you use Squarespace, Wix, or WordPress, this takes ten minutes.
- A specific audience segment for "portfolio page visitors only" — don't retarget everyone who hit your homepage. They might have landed there by accident.
- A dedicated landing page that looks different from what they already saw. Same portfolio photos, different layout, more emphasis on the free consultation offer.

**Frequency capping matters here.** Show your retargeting ad to the same person more than three times in a week and they'll feel stalked. Set it to twice a week maximum. If they don't click after 14 days, remove them from the audience and move on. They're not ready.

You can also retarget via email if you collect website visitors' emails through a lead magnet — a "Renovation Budget Guide" PDF, for example. Import that list into Mailchimp and send a three-email sequence: day one with the guide, day three with a case study, day seven with a consultation offer. The designer in Nashville added this sequence and saw 11% of those email recipients book a call. That's a higher conversion rate than her Google Ads campaign had on its best day.

## How to Combine Google Ads with Yelp and Houzz Without Wasting Money

I need to say something uncomfortable: Google Ads alone won't fill your pipeline if you're an interior designer who works with homeowners. Homeowners search on multiple platforms. They check Google. They check Yelp. They check Houzz. They ask their neighbor. If you're only on Google, you're invisible to the segment of your market that starts their search on Yelp.

But you can't throw money at every platform and hope. You need a specific playbook.

**Yelp:** Yelp Ads are expensive relative to Google. In Chicago, interior design keywords on Yelp run $8–$15 per click. That's brutal. The way to make Yelp work is to skip the ads and focus on profile optimization. Claim your Yelp Business Page. Make sure your address, phone number, and service areas are correct. Respond to every review — positive and negative — within 48 hours. Get 10+ reviews with photos. That alone will push you above 80% of competitors in your city.

A designer in Denver spent $0 on Yelp Ads but invested three hours responding to reviews and asking past clients to leave reviews with photos. Her organic Yelp impressions went up 200% in six weeks. She got three leads from Yelp in month two. No ad spend.

**Houzz:** Houzz is the platform where homeowners go when they're in the dreaming phase, not the hiring phase. That's valuable for awareness but terrible for direct conversion. Instead of running Houzz Ads, which can cost $300–$600 per month with mediocre targeting, use Houzz Pro's free profile to showcase your portfolio and collect leads through the "Book a Consultation" button. Respond to Houzz messages within two hours.

**Mailchimp integration:** Here's where the platforms connect. Export your Google Ads leads, your Yelp inquiries, and your Houzz inquiries into a single spreadsheet. Upload that list to Mailchimp and tag leads by source. Send a monthly newsletter with one before-and-after renovation project. Include a testimonial. End with "Book your free 30-minute consultation" button.

A designer in Portland ran this combined approach for four months. Google Ads generated 18 leads. Yelp (organic only) generated 7 leads. Houzz generated 4 leads. Mailchimp email sequences generated 5 leads from past website visitors who hadn't booked yet but were on her email list from a lead magnet. Total: 34 leads. Cost per lead averaged $52 across all channels. Google Ads alone was $68. The diversification didn't just add volume — it lowered her average cost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I'm a solo interior designer. Do I need to hire someone to run Google Ads?**

No, but you need to commit to learning the basics or accept that you'll waste your first $1,000–$2,000 figuring it out. The setup I described in this article — keyword targeting, ad scheduling, negative keywords, landing pages — takes about four hours to implement. If you can't spare four hours, hire a freelancer for a one-time setup. Expect to pay $300–$500 for a campaign build and $150–$300/month for management if you want ongoing help. Do not sign a contract with an agency that charges you a percentage of ad spend. That's a conflict of interest — they make more money when you spend more, not when you save.

**Q: How long until I see results from Google Ads?**

If you've set up your campaign correctly with high-intent keywords, tight geo-targeting, and a dedicated landing page, you should see your first lead within 3–7 days. It might not be a qualified lead — people click ads by accident — so give it two weeks before you judge the campaign. If you've spent $400+ with zero leads after two weeks, something is broken. Either your keywords are too broad, your landing page isn't converting, or your budget is too low to compete in your market.

**Q: What's the minimum budget I should start with?**

For a small city or suburb, $300 per month ($10/day) is the floor. For a major metro like NYC, Chicago, or Los Angeles, you need $750–$1,000 per month to compete. The reason: cost per click is higher in big cities. A click on "kitchen remodel NYC" can run $12–$18. If your daily budget is $15, you get one click per day. That's not enough data to optimize. At $30/day, you get 2–3 clicks daily and can start making adjustments within a week.

**Q: Should I advertise all my services or just one?**

Start with one service — the one that generates the highest average project fee. If your average kitchen remodel project is $18,000 and your average living room refresh is $4,000, advertise the kitchen remodel. You can expand to other services once the first campaign is profitable. Trying to advertise everything at once dilutes your ad copy, confuses your landing page, and wastes budget.

**Q: What if I'm getting clicks but no calls or form submissions?**

Two problems. First, check your landing page. Open it on your phone. Is the "Book a Consultation" button visible without scrolling? If I have to scroll past three paragraphs and a hero image of a living room to find your contact form, you're losing 60% of potential leads. Second, check your call tracking. A designer in Austin thought she was getting zero leads. I checked her Google Ads call tracking. She was getting calls. She just wasn't answering them because she was in design meetings with her ringer off. Set up call forwarding to your cell phone and keep it on during business hours. Or hire a virtual receptionist for $8/hour.

**Q: Can I target homeowners in neighboring cities without raising my budget?**

Yes, but carefully. If you're based in Portland OR but want clients in Lake Oswego and Beaverton, create separate ad groups for each city so you can see which one performs better. Start with a budget split: 60% on your primary city, 20% on each secondary city. After 30 days, shift budget toward whichever cities generate leads at the lowest cost. Do not target an entire metropolitan area with one ad group. You'll waste money on clicks from neighborhoods where you don't have pricing authority or travel time kills your margin.
